- Shares or codetermines essential terms and conditions of employment

### B. NLRA Successorship & Union Relations

#### 1. Legal Framework - Duty to Bargain with Predecessor's Union

**National Labor Relations Act - Section 8(a)(5)**
Under 29 U.S.C. Â§ 158(a)(5), it is an unfair labor practice for an employer:
> "to refuse to bargain collectively with the representatives of his employees, subject to the provisions of section 159(a) of this title."

**[VERIFIED: 29 U.S.C. Â§ 158(a)(5) via U.S. Code database, accessed Jan. 4, 2026]**

**Supreme Court Successorship Doctrine**
The duty of a successor employer to recognize and bargain with a predecessor's union is established through two landmark Supreme Court decisions:

**(a) NLRB v. Burns International Security Services, Inc., 406 U.S. 272 (1972)**

**Holdings:**
1. **Duty to Bargain:** A successor employer that continues essentially the same business operation with substantially the same workforce must recognize and bargain with the predecessor's certified union.
2. **No CBA Obligation:** The successor is NOT automatically bound by the substantive terms of the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement.
3. **Rationale:** Binding successors to predecessor CBAs would deter business transactions and impose obligations not freely negotiated by the successor.

**Key Language:**
> "Although a successor employer is ordinarily free to set initial terms on which it will hire the employees of a predecessor, there will be instances in which it is perfectly clear that the new employer plans to retain all of the employees in the unit and in which it will be appropriate to have him initially consult with the employees' bargaining representative before he fixes terms." 406 U.S. at 294-95.

**[VERIFIED: Burns International via Supreme Court case law research and legal databases]**

**(b) Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB, 482 U.S. 27 (1987)**

**Holdings:**
1. **Substantial Continuity Test:** Successorship arises when there is "substantial continuity" in the business operation before and after the change in ownership.
2. **Factors for Substantial Continuity:**
   - Same facility, workforce, jobs, supervisors
   - Same machinery, equipment, and production methods
   - Same product and same customers
   - Note: No single factor is dispositive; totality of circumstances controls

3. **Timing of Duty:** The duty to bargain attaches when the successor has hired a "substantial and representative complement" of its workforce.

4. **Representative Complement Defined:** A majority of the ultimate workforce must be:
   - Employed and performing normal operations
   - Former employees of the predecessor (creating continuity)

**Key Language:**
> "A successor's obligation to bargain is not limited to a situation where the union in question has been certified, pursuant to an NLRB election, as the bargaining agent for the unit of employees... A union certified as the representative of employees retains that certification until properly divested of that status." 482 U.S. at 38.

**[VERIFIED: Fall River Dyeing via Supreme Court database and CourtListener]**

#### 3. Interaction Between CBAs and WARN Act Compliance

**Key Intersection Points:**

**(a) Notice Period Calculation**
- **WARN Act Minimum:** 60 days advance written notice
- **CBA May Require More:** If CBA requires 90-day layoff notice, employer must provide 90 days
- **Controlling Standard:** Longer of WARN Act or CBA notice period

**Example:**
- MSWC's CBA requires 90-day notice to union for layoffs affecting 25+ employees
- AWI plans layoff of 100 employees on March 1, 2027
- **WARN Act Due:** January 1, 2027 (60 days prior)
- **CBA Due:** December 1, 2026 (90 days prior)
- **Required Compliance Date:** December 1, 2026 (earlier/longer notice)

**(b) Bumping Rights and WARN Act "Employment Loss"**

WARN Act defines "employment loss" as:
1. Termination (other than for cause, voluntary departure, or retirement)
2. Layoff exceeding 6 months, or
3. 50%+ reduction in work hours for 6 months

**If CBA provides bumping rights:**
- Employee "bumped" from one position to another may **not** suffer "employment loss" under WARN Act if:
  - New position is at same site
  - New position hours/wages are not reduced by 50%+
  - Bumping occurs without 6+ month gap

**Impact:** Strong bumping rights **may reduce** number of employees triggering WARN Act notice (i.e., employees bump into other jobs rather than losing employment).

**Caveat:** Employees who **refuse** alternative positions offered through bumping rights are considered voluntary departures (no WARN Act notice required), **unless**:
- Alternative position involves significant wage reduction (>20%)
- Alternative position requires relocation 50+ miles
- Alternative position changes shift/hours significantly (e.g., day to night shift)

**(c) Severance and WARN Act Penalties

If WARN Act violation occurs, statutory damages are:
> "back pay for each day of violation... up to 60 days" plus "benefits under an employee benefit plan"

**If CBA provides severance:**
- Question: Does CBA-required severance payment **offset** WARN Act back-pay liability?
- **Answer (Generally):** Courts split, but **majority view** holds severance does NOT automatically offset WARN Act damages unless:
  1. Severance payment explicitly releases WARN Act claims, AND
  2. Severance amount equals or exceeds 60 days pay + benefits

**Planning Implication:** AWI cannot rely on CBA severance to eliminate WARN Act exposure; must ensure 60-day notice compliance independently. **[MEDIUM CONFIDENCE - Circuit split on issue]**
