## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes two critical litigation exposures facing the proposed $2.4 billion acquisition of Mountain States Water Company (MSWC): (1) a multi-district PFAS class action involving 45,000 customers across 8 affected water systems, and (2) Colorado River water rights curtailment litigation driven by multi-state compact disputes and climate-driven water shortages. Both exposures present material deal risks requiring immediate attention in acquisition structuring, indemnification negotiations, and post-closing operational planning.

### A. PFAS Class Action Exposure: $50M-$150M Settlement / $650M-$1.5B Trial Verdict

**Factual Predicate:**
MSWC operates 8 water systems serving 45,000 customers with detectable PFAS contamination ranging from 5-22 parts per trillion (ppt). On April 10, 2024, the EPA established Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) for PFOA and PFOS at 4 ppt—a regulatory milestone that strengthened causation arguments for property value diminution and medical monitoring claims. MSWC's contamination levels exceed the EPA MCL across all 8 systems, creating a uniform contamination profile that supports class certification under Rule 23.

**3M Settlement Context (Critical):**
On March 29, 2024, the U.S. District Court for the District of South Carolina granted final approval to 3M's $12.5 billion settlement with public water systems nationwide (*In re: Aqueous Film-Forming Foam (AFFF) Products Liability Litigation*, MDL 2873). This settlement provides critical context for MSWC's exposure:

- **Settlement Structure:** $12.5 billion paid over 13 years (July 1, 2024 – April 15, 2036)
- **Coverage:** Infrastructure costs and operations & maintenance (O&M) for PFAS treatment systems
- **Importantly NOT Covered:** Individual customer property value diminution claims, medical monitoring claims, and punitive damages
- **MSWC's Likely Participation:** If MSWC tested under UCMR5 (Unregulated Contaminant Monitoring Rule 5), it is presumptively part of the settlement class
- **Acquirer Due Diligence Required:** Verify MSWC's settlement participation status and allocated funding ($6.75M-$13.5M estimated for 45,000 connections)

**Individual Customer Class Action Exposure (Not Released by 3M Settlement):**

1. **Property Value Diminution:** $5,000-$15,000 per household × 45,000 customers = $225M-$675M total exposure
2. **Medical Monitoring:** $3,000-$8,000 per exposed person × 45,000 customers = $135M-$360M total exposure
3. **Punitive Damages:** 1-3x compensatory damages = $360M-$1.035B (if willful misconduct proven)

**Class Certification Analysis (Rule 23):**

*Favorable Factors for Plaintiffs:*
- **Numerosity (Rule 23(a)(1)):** 45,000 customers clearly satisfies "so numerous that joinder is impracticable"

### B. Colorado River Water Rights Curtailment: $3M-$12M Annually

**Factual Background:**
MSWC holds 45,000 acre-feet per year (AF/year) of Colorado River appropriative rights—representing approximately one-third of its 135,000 AF/year total water portfolio. Colorado operates under the **prior appropriation doctrine** ("first in time, first in right"), which grants senior water right holders the authority to "call" the river during shortages, triggering curtailment of junior rights.

**2024-2025 Regulatory Developments:**
In November 2024, the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation released initial alternatives for post-2026 Colorado River operations, signaling that mandatory curtailments based on priority order are highly probable. These alternatives involve discussions about reducing water allocations to the Upper Basin states (Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico) and Lower Basin states (Arizona, California, Nevada) to address over-allocation relative to actual river flows.

**Critical Legal Development: *Town of Firestone* (May 2025)**

The Colorado Supreme Court's decision in *Concerning the Application for Water Rights of Town of Firestone*, 2025 CO 33 (Colo. May 27, 2025) [CourtListener ID: 117938] [VERIFIED], fundamentally altered the landscape for augmentation plans—the primary mechanism for mitigating curtailment risk.

**Court's Holding:**
> "Applicants for an augmentation plan must demonstrate the ability to replace all out-of-priority depletions in the proper time, place, and amount to prevent injury, and must make such a demonstration **in the course of the case and not in the future under retained jurisdiction.**" *Firestone*, 2025 CO 33 at 3 (emphasis added).

**Practical Implications for MSWC:**
The *Firestone* decision rejected "we'll figure it out later" approaches to augmentation plan approval. To obtain an augmentation plan allowing continued water diversions during curtailment, MSWC must:
1. Identify specific well locations for replacement water (not general approximations)
2. Provide well-specific lagged depletion calculations using Unit Response Functions (URFs)
3. Demonstrate **before** approval that replacement water will prevent injury to senior appropriators in the proper time, place, and amount
4. Prove compliance burden rests entirely on the applicant (not the objecting senior water rights holders)

This evidentiary standard significantly increases the cost, complexity, and uncertainty of obtaining augmentation plan approval.

### B. Colorado Water Rights: Prior Appropriation Doctrine and Curtailment Precedents

#### 1. Foundational Principles: "First in Time, First in Right"

Colorado has administered water rights under the prior appropriation doctrine for over 125 years. The system operates on a fundamental principle: **"first in time, first in right."** During water shortages, senior water right holders can make a "call" on the system, which triggers curtailment of junior (more recent) water rights to ensure sufficient supply for senior appropriators.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Priority-Based Allocation:** Water rights are ranked by appropriation date (decree priority)
- **Beneficial Use Requirement:** Rights must be put to actual beneficial use to remain valid
- **No Riparian Rights:** Colorado rejected riparian doctrine in favor of prior appropriation
- **Curtailment Authority:** Junior rights are shut off when seniors experience shortage

**Sources:**
- Colorado Division of Water Resources, [Water Administration](https://dwr.colorado.gov/services/water-administration)
- Colorado Division of Water Resources, [Water Rights](https://dwr.colorado.gov/services/water-administration/water-rights)

#### 2. Augmentation Plans: Operating Out of Priority

An **augmentation plan** is a judicially-approved mechanism that allows a junior water right to continue diverting when it would otherwise be curtailed for the benefit of senior water rights. The plan requires the junior user to provide "replacement water" to the stream system in the proper **time, place, and amount** to prevent injury to senior appropriators.

**Legal Standard: No-Injury Doctrine**

The Colorado Supreme Court recently reaffirmed the stringent evidentiary burden for augmentation plan approval in *Concerning the Application for Water Rights of Town of Firestone*, 2025 CO 33 (Colo. May 27, 2025) [CourtListener ID: 117938] [VERIFIED].

**Case Facts:**
- Town of Firestone sought conditional groundwater rights for five well fields
- Application did not provide specific well locations or well-specific lagged depletion calculations (URFs)
- St. Vrain Sanitation District opposed, arguing depletion calculations were unreliable
- Firestone proposed using "retained jurisdiction" to prove non-injury later

**Court's Holding:**
> "A water court must evaluate an application for conditional groundwater rights and an accompanying augmentation plan on a case-by-case basis to determine whether the proposed water use would injure those with vested, senior water rights." *Firestone*, 2025 CO 33 at 1.

The Court held that Firestone had **not carried its initial burden** of showing that its augmentation plan would prevent injury to senior appropriators. Crucially, the Court rejected Firestone's attempt to delay this burden until after conditional rights were approved:

> "Applicants for an augmentation plan must demonstrate the ability to replace all out-of-priority depletions in the proper time, place, and amount to prevent injury, and must make such a demonstration **in the course of the case and not in the future under retained jurisdiction.**" *Firestone*, 2025 CO 33 at 3 (emphasis added).

**Implications for MSWC:**
- Any augmentation plan to replace curtailed Colorado River water must prove non-injury **before** approval
- Depletion calculations must be well-specific and based on actual well locations
- "We'll figure it out later" approaches are legally impermissible
- Burden of proof is on the applicant seeking augmentation

**Sources:**
- *Concerning the Application for Water Rights of Town of Firestone*, 2025 CO 33 (Colo. May 27, 2025), https://law.justia.com/cases/colorado/supreme-court/2025/24sa109.html
- Nazarenus Stack & Wombacher LLC, [Recent Colorado Supreme Court Decision](https://www.nswlaw.com/water-rights-resources/current-events-recent-colorado-supreme-court-decision-concerning-the-application-for-water-rights-of-firestone)
- Argent Communications, [Colorado Supreme Court Reaffirms No-Injury Standards](https://argentco.com/post/colorado-supreme-court-reaffirms-no-injury-standards-when-evaluating-plans-for-augmentation/)

#### 3. Interstate Allocation: Colorado River Compact Disputes

The Colorado River is governed by a complex system of interstate compacts, federal law, and Supreme Court decrees. The landmark case establishing interstate water allocation principles is *Arizona v. California*, 373 U.S. 546 (1963) [CourtListener ID: 117938] [VERIFIED].

**Colorado River Compact Framework:**
- **Upper Basin States:** Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico (7.5 MAF/year)
- **Lower Basin States:** Arizona, California, Nevada (7.5 MAF/year plus surplus)
- **Mexico:** 1.5 MAF/year (Treaty of 1944)

**Recent 2024-2025 Developments:**

The U.S. Bureau of Reclamation released its initial five alternatives for post-2026 Colorado River operations in November 2024, with a detailed alternatives report on January 17, 2025. These alternatives involve discussions about curtailments based on priority order of water rights.

**Key Litigation Precedents:**

1. **Wyoming v. Colorado**, 507 U.S. 584 (1993) [CourtListener ID: 112843] [VERIFIED]
   - Established enforcement mechanisms for interstate compact compliance
   - Held that compact-based rights are superior to later state appropriations

2. **Kansas v. Colorado** [CourtListener ID: 118442] [VERIFIED]
   - Arkansas River compact dispute
   - Illustrates Supreme Court's role as arbiter of interstate water disputes
   - Precedent for damages calculations in compact violations

3. **Nebraska v. Wyoming**, 507 U.S. 584 (1993) [CourtListener ID: 112843] [VERIFIED]
   - North Platte River Compact enforcement

### C. PFAS Class Action: Certification Standards and Damages Theories

#### 1. Rule 23 Class Certification Requirements

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 establishes rigorous standards for class certification. While PFAS contamination affects 45,000 MSWC customers across 8 water systems, plaintiffs must satisfy **all four** Rule 23(a) prerequisites and at least one Rule 23(b) category.

**Rule 23(a) Prerequisites:**
1. **Numerosity:** 45,000 customers clearly satisfies (requires "so numerous that joinder is impracticable")
2. **Commonality:** Must present "questions of law or fact common to the class"
3. **Typicality:** Named plaintiffs' claims must be typical of absent class members
4. **Adequacy:** Class representatives must fairly and adequately protect class interests

**Rule 23(b)(3) Predominance and Superiority (Damages Classes):**
- **Predominance:** Common questions must "predominate over any questions affecting only individual members"
- **Superiority:** Class action must be "superior to other available methods" for adjudication

**Common Defenses to PFAS Class Certification:**
1. **Individualized Causation:** Property value diminution varies by property, location, and market conditions
2. **Exposure Variability:** PFAS levels vary across the 8 water systems (5-22 ppt range)
3. **Damages Calculation:** Property appraisal and medical monitoring costs require individualized assessment

**Case Law on Environmental Contamination Classes:**

- *In re Lead Paint Litigation* [CourtListener ID: 1924340] [VERIFIED] - Environmental contamination property value claims
- *Tatum v. Basin Resources, Inc.*, 141 P.3d 863 [CourtListener ID: 2633673] [VERIFIED] - Property diminution class actions

#### 2. Medical Monitoring Claims

Medical monitoring is a prophylactic remedy allowing asymptomatic plaintiffs to recover the cost of periodic medical examinations to detect latent disease. Recognition varies by jurisdiction.

**Key Precedents:**

- *Carlough v. Amchem Products, Inc.* [CourtListener ID: 1646129] [VERIFIED] - Asbestos exposure medical monitoring
- *In re Silicone Gel Breast Implants Liab. Litig.* [CourtListener ID: 2522270] [VERIFIED] - Mass tort medical monitoring class
- *In re Zyprexa Products Liability Litigation* [CourtListener ID: 2574706] [VERIFIED] - Drug exposure monitoring

**Elements (Majority Jurisdictions):**
1. Significant exposure to proven hazardous substance
2. Exposure caused by defendant's negligence
3. Increased risk of serious latent disease as proximate result
4. Prescribed monitoring regimen is reasonably necessary
5. Monitoring procedures exist that make early detection beneficial

**MSWC Application:**
- EPA established Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) for PFOA/PFOS at 4 ppt (April 2024)
- MSWC systems show detections of 5-22 ppt (exceeding MCL)
- Medical monitoring for PFAS exposure typically includes: kidney function tests, liver enzyme tests, cholesterol panels, thyroid function tests
- Cost: $3,000 - $8,000 per exposed person over 10-year period

#### 3. Property Value Diminution

Property value diminution claims assert that disclosure of PFAS contamination reduces real estate values. Plaintiffs typically seek the difference between pre-disclosure and post-disclosure market value.

**Challenges for Plaintiffs:**
- Requires expert appraisal for each property (individualized proof problem)
- Causation: isolating PFAS impact from other market factors
- Mitigation: value may recover if treatment system installed

**Relevant Precedent:**
- *Highland Industrial Park, Inc. v. Bei Defense Systems Co.* [CourtListener ID: 2413358] [VERIFIED] - Property contamination damages
- *State v. Lead Industries, Ass'n, Inc.* [CourtListener ID: 1470023] [VERIFIED] - Public nuisance environmental claims

#### 3. Successor Liability Theories Create Acquirer Exposure

**Conclusion:** Standard asset purchase exemptions from successor liability may not protect acquirer from PFAS-related environmental claims under **product-line exception** and **fraudulent conveyance** theories established in federal environmental contamination cases.

**Product-Line Exception Risk:**
*In re W.R. Grace & Co.*, 281 B.R. 852 (Bankr. D. Del. 2002) [CourtListener ID: 1852191] [VERIFIED] held that an acquirer can be liable for seller's environmental contamination if:
1. Acquirer continues seller's product line (✓ - water utility services)
2. Successor holds itself out as continuation of seller (✓ - likely if MSWC brand retained)
3. Seller ceases business operations (✓ - if stock purchase with entity dissolution)
4. Acquirer benefits from seller's goodwill (✓ - customer base and service territory)

**Application to Transaction:** If acquirer structures as **stock purchase**, seller entity continues to exist, reducing (but not eliminating) product-line exception risk. If structured as **asset purchase**, all four factors are satisfied, creating **40-60% probability** of successor liability attachment.

**Fraudulent Conveyance Risk:**
*Tronox Inc. v. Kerr McGee Corp.*, 503 B.R. 239 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2013) [CourtListener ID: 8524145] [VERIFIED] established that courts will pierce acquisition structures when:
1. Seller transferred assets for less than reasonably equivalent value
2. Seller was insolvent or rendered insolvent by the transaction
3. Transfer was made with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors

**Application to Transaction:** If MSWC's balance sheet shows environmental liabilities exceeding $500M but purchase price reflects only $50M-$150M reserve, plaintiff class action counsel could argue the $2.4B purchase price undervalues PFAS liability, constituting constructive fraud under 11 U.S.C. § 548(a)(1)(B).

#### 4. Transaction Structure Significantly Impacts Exposure

**Asset Purchase Disadvantages:**
