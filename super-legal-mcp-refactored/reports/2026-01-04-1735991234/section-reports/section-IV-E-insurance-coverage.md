## IV.E. INSURANCE COVERAGE FOR PFAS LIABILITIES

### A. Legal Framework

This section analyzes insurance coverage for PFAS-related liabilities arising from the pending class action litigation (*Customers v. Mountain States Water Company*, Denver County District Court) involving 45,000 customers across eight MSWC water systems with PFAS contamination ranging from 5-22 parts per trillion (ppt), exceeding the EPA Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) of 4 ppt established in April 2024. The analysis focuses on whether Zurich Insurance Company has a duty to defend and indemnify MSWC under a $50 million Commercial General Liability (CGL) policy containing a standard ISO absolute pollution exclusion, and whether MSWC has a viable statutory bad faith claim under Colorado law if Zurich unreasonably denies coverage.

#### 1. Duty to Defend — Broader Than Duty to Indemnify

Under California law, which applies to multi-state insurance coverage disputes through choice-of-law principles, an insurer's duty to defend arises whenever there is "potential for coverage" under the policy, even if the insurer ultimately has no duty to indemnify. *Gray v. Zurich Ins. Co.*, 65 Cal. 2d 263, 275, 419 P.2d 168 (1966).[1] The duty to defend is broader than the duty to indemnify and constitutes a "primary obligation" of the insurer distinct from indemnification. *Montrose Chem. Corp. v. Superior Court*, 6 Cal. 4th 287, 295, 861 P.2d 1153 (1993).[2]

Insurers must defend any suit that "potentially seeks damages within the coverage of the policy," and exclusionary clauses must be "conspicuous, plain and clear" to relieve the insurer of its defense obligation. *MacKinnon v. Truck Ins. Exch.*, 31 Cal. 4th 635, 647, 73 P.3d 1205 (2003).[3] The eight-corners rule limits the duty-to-defend inquiry to the complaint's allegations compared against the policy terms, without resort to extrinsic evidence. However, if extrinsic facts establish conclusively that no potential for coverage exists, the duty to defend may be defeated. *Montrose*, 6 Cal. 4th at 295.

**Application to PFAS Contamination:** The class action complaint alleges bodily injury (medical monitoring for PFAS exposure-related health risks) and property damage (diminution in property values due to PFAS contamination in water supply). Both bodily injury and property damage are insuring agreements under standard CGL policies. Although Zurich's policy contains an absolute pollution exclusion, the existence of potentially covered claims triggers the duty to defend under the *Gray* "potential for coverage" standard. The pollution exclusion may ultimately bar indemnity coverage, but it does not eliminate the duty to defend where the complaint raises even a single issue potentially within coverage.

#### 2. Pollution Exclusion — ISO Absolute Exclusion Language

The standard ISO Form CG 00 01 12 07 (2007 edition) pollution exclusion provides: "This insurance does not apply to 'bodily injury' or 'property damage' arising out of the actual, alleged or threatened discharge, dispersal, seepage, migration, release or escape of 'pollutants.'" The exclusion defines "pollutants" as "any solid, liquid, gaseous or thermal irritant or contaminant, including smoke, vapor, soot, fumes, acids, alkalis, chemicals and waste."[4]

Post-1986 absolute pollution exclusions removed the "sudden and accidental" exception from the 1973 CGL standard pollution exclusion, earning the name "absolute pollution exclusion" due to the absence of a fortuitous event exception.[5] Courts have strictly construed pollution exclusions against insurers, particularly in cases involving ordinary negligence involving toxic chemicals rather than intentional industrial pollution. *MacKinnon*, 31 Cal. 4th at 647 (pollution exclusion does not "plainly and clearly exclude ordinary acts of negligence involving toxic chemicals such as pesticides").

**PFAS as "Pollutant":** PFAS chemicals (perfluorooctanoic acid or PFOA, perfluorooctanesulfonic acid or PFOS) are synthetic chemical contaminants fitting the literal definition of "pollutant" under the ISO exclusion. The EPA designated PFAS as hazardous substances under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA) in 2024 and established MCLs of 4 ppt for PFOA and PFOS.[6] The CERCLA designation strengthens insurer arguments that PFAS falls squarely within the pollution exclusion.

#### 3. Third-Party Contamination Source Doctrine

MSWC did not discharge, spill, or release PFAS into the environment. PFAS contamination originated from third-party sources: (1) firefighting foam (aqueous film-forming foam or AFFF) used at airports and military installations, (2) industrial discharge from upstream manufacturers, and (3) other legacy contamination sources. MSWC's role was passive distribution of contaminated water through its public water system without knowledge or intent to pollute.

This factual distinction is critical. Some courts have held that pollution exclusions target the insured's own pollution-causing conduct (dumping, discharging, spilling), not passive transmission of third-party contamination. While no Colorado Supreme Court precedent directly addresses this issue, California courts have suggested that policy language must establish a reasonable expectation that coverage will be excluded for ordinary business conduct. *MacKinnon*, 31 Cal. 4th at 647.

**Analogous Precedent:** In *Wolverine World Wide, Inc. v. American Ins. Co.*, No. 1:19-cv-00346 (W.D. Mich. 2021), a federal district court held that Travelers Insurance was required to provide a defense despite a pollution exclusion where contamination involved passive distribution of PFAS-contaminated products rather than active discharge or intentional pollution.[7] Although *Wolverine* involved a "sudden and accidental" qualified pollution exclusion (different from Zurich's absolute exclusion), the court's reasoning regarding passive versus active contamination supports MSWC's argument for defense coverage.

**Distinguishable Precedent:** In *Tonoga, Inc. v. New Hampshire Ins. Co.*, 201 A.D.3d 68 (N.Y. App. Div. 2022), the New York Appellate Division held insurers had no duty to defend or indemnify where PFAS was "improperly dumped and spilled over many years."[8] The court found pollution was not abrupt or unintentional, precluding the "sudden and accidental" exception to the pollution exclusion. *Tonoga* is distinguishable from MSWC's situation: in *Tonoga*, the insured actively dumped and spilled PFAS, whereas MSWC passively distributed contaminated water from third-party sources without dumping or spilling conduct.

#### 4. Colorado Statutory Bad Faith Insurance Law

Colorado Revised Statutes § 10-3-1115 prohibits insurers from "unreasonably delay[ing] or deny[ing] payment of a claim for benefits owed to or on behalf of any first-party claimant." Section 10-3-1116 provides remedies: "A first-party claimant... whose claim for payment of benefits has been unreasonably delayed or denied may bring an action in district court to recover reasonable attorney fees and court costs and two times the covered benefit." C.R.S. §§ 10-3-1115, 10-3-1116 (2021).[9]

Colorado statutory bad faith imposes a lower burden of proof than common law bad faith. Under the statutory standard, an insurer's delay or denial is unreasonable if "without reasonable basis," even if the claim is "fairly debatable." Colorado courts have held that an insurer's obligation to defend is a "covered benefit" under the statutory bad faith statute. Thus, if Zurich unreasonably denies duty to defend, MSWC may recover statutory remedies (double damages, attorney fees) in addition to reimbursement of defense costs.

**Unreasonable Denial Standard:** If Zurich denies coverage based solely on pollution exclusion without investigating MSWC's third-party contamination source defense, the denial may be unreasonable under the statutory standard. A reasonable investigation would include: (1) reviewing PFAS source tracing studies identifying upstream contamination sources (airports, military bases, industrial discharge), (2) analyzing MSWC's operational history to confirm MSWC did not discharge or spill PFAS, and (3) consulting coverage counsel regarding *Wolverine* precedent supporting passive distributor coverage.

**Genuine Coverage Dispute Defense:** Zurich may defend against bad faith claim by establishing a "genuine coverage dispute" regarding pollution exclusion applicability. If the pollution exclusion facially applies to PFAS contamination and Colorado law does not clearly resolve the third-party source defense, Zurich's reliance on the exclusion may have reasonable basis, insulating Zurich from bad faith liability.

#### 5. Cumis Counsel — Independent Counsel Rights

California Civil Code § 2860 provides: "When a policy of insurance imposes a duty to defend and a conflict of interest arises, the insurer must provide independent counsel to represent the insured unless the insured expressly waives this right in writing." Cal. Civ. Code § 2860 (2024).[10] The statute derives from *San Diego Fed. Credit Union v. Cumis Ins. Soc'y*, 162 Cal. App. 3d 358 (1984), which established that when an insurer defends under reservation of rights on a coverage issue that can be controlled by defense counsel, the insured is entitled to independent counsel selected by the insured and paid by the insurer.

If Zurich agrees to defend MSWC under reservation of rights (reserving the right to deny indemnity based on pollution exclusion), a conflict of interest arises. Defense counsel hired by Zurich faces a potential conflict: counsel's loyalty to Zurich (payer of fees) may conflict with the insured's interest in avoiding admissions that could prejudice coverage. For example, defense counsel might seek to characterize MSWC as knowing about PFAS contamination for years or as actively "discharging" contaminated water—admissions that could bar coverage under the pollution exclusion while potentially limiting liability in the underlying class action.

MSWC selects Cumis counsel (not Zurich); Zurich pays counsel's fees at rates Zurich pays its own defense counsel for similar environmental/mass tort litigation (typically $400-$600/hour for partners, $250-$400/hour for associates). Total defense costs through trial: $2 million to $5 million.[11]

#### 6. Multi-Layer Coverage Allocation

If MSWC maintains excess or umbrella insurance layers above Zurich's $50 million primary CGL policy, multi-layer allocation rules determine how available coverage is accessed. Two competing allocation methodologies exist: (1) **vertical exhaustion**, where excess policy for a given policy year is triggered once the underlying primary policy for the same year is exhausted, allowing the insured to access each excess layer sequentially; and (2) **horizontal exhaustion**, where all primary policies across all triggered policy years must be exhausted before any excess coverage contributes.

California adopted vertical exhaustion in *Montrose Chem. Corp. v. Superior Court*, 10 Cal. 5th 900 (2020),[12] holding that excess policies are triggered sequentially once underlying primary is exhausted. If MSWC's excess policies are governed by California law or "follow form" language incorporating California primary policy terms, vertical exhaustion applies, maximizing available coverage.

For long-tail environmental exposures like PFAS contamination (contamination likely present for years or decades before detection), courts apply "continuous trigger" doctrine, activating policies from multiple years. If PFAS contamination spans 2015-2025, policies from all ten years may be triggered, increasing total available coverage but complicating allocation across years.

#### B.4 Uninsurable Environmental Compliance Costs (Q3, Q4, Q9) (FLAGGED)

**Conclusion:** Lead service line replacement ($1.4 billion to $1.95 billion), PFAS treatment capital ($190 million NPV), and infrastructure backlog ($69.7 million NPV) represent **$1.66 billion to $2.21 billion in uninsured regulatory compliance costs**. CGL insurance policies cover tort/litigation losses (third-party bodily injury and property damage), not capital expenditures or regulatory compliance costs. These exposures are properly classified as regulatory capital decisions, not covered perils under MSWC's CGL policy. **Exposure:** $1.66B-$2.21B uninsured (Q3: $1.4B-$1.95B lead lines; Q4: $190M PFAS treatment NPV; Q9: $69.7M infrastructure backlog NPV). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Insurance covers tort/litigation losses, not capital/regulatory compliance costs; established insurance law principle].

**Rule:** CGL insurance policies provide coverage for third-party bodily injury and property damage claims arising from the insured's operations or products. Standard CGL policies do not cover first-party property damage (insured's own remediation costs), capital expenditures, or regulatory compliance costs. The insuring agreement in ISO Form CG 00 01 states: "We will pay those sums that the insured becomes legally obligated to pay as damages because of 'bodily injury' or 'property damage' to which this insurance applies." Coverage is limited to sums the insured becomes "legally obligated to pay"—i.e., tort liability to third parties—not voluntary business expenditures or regulatory mandates.[22]

**Explanation:** Courts have consistently held that CGL insurance does not cover the cost of repairing or replacing the insured's own property or infrastructure, even if the repairs are mandated by regulatory authorities. In environmental contamination cases, CGL policies may cover third-party claims for bodily injury or property damage (e.g., neighboring properties affected by contamination) but do not cover the insured's costs of remediating its own property or upgrading its facilities to comply with new regulations.

For example, if a water utility faces tort liability for distributing contaminated water that causes bodily injury to customers or diminishes property values, a CGL policy may provide coverage (subject to applicable exclusions). However, if the same utility must install treatment systems to comply with EPA regulations, those capital expenditures are not covered under the CGL policy because they are not "damages" the insured is "legally obligated to pay" to third parties—they are business expenses incurred to comply with regulatory requirements.

Environmental Impairment Liability (EIL) or Pollution Legal Liability (PLL) policies are specialized insurance products designed to cover environmental remediation costs and regulatory compliance expenses. Unlike CGL policies with pollution exclusions, EIL/PLL policies affirmatively cover pollution exposures, including first-party cleanup costs. However, modern EIL/PLL policies (2023-2024) increasingly contain PFAS-specific exclusions due to emerging PFAS liability exposure. Older EIL policies (pre-2023) may lack PFAS exclusions and could provide coverage.[23]

**Application:** Here, three major environmental compliance exposures are not covered under MSWC's CGL policy:

**Q3 — Lead Service Line Replacement ($1.4B-$1.95B):** EPA's Lead and Copper Rule Improvements (LCRI) mandate full replacement of 148,000 lead service lines within 10 years (November 2027-November 2037). This regulatory requirement imposes capital expenditures on MSWC to replace utility-owned and customer-owned service lines. These costs are not "damages" the insured is "legally obligated to pay as damages because of bodily injury or property damage"—they are infrastructure capital expenditures mandated by federal regulation. CGL insurance does not cover such costs.

**Q4 — PFAS Treatment Capital ($190M NPV):** EPA's PFAS National Primary Drinking Water Regulation establishes individual MCLs of 4 ppt for PFOA and PFOS, requiring MSWC to install treatment systems (granular activated carbon, ion exchange, or reverse osmosis) at eight water systems. The $50 million to $80 million capital cost and $4 million to $6 million annual O&M cost (totaling $150 million to $230 million NPV over 20 years) are regulatory compliance costs, not tort damages. CGL insurance does not cover such costs.

**Q9 — Infrastructure Backlog ($69.7M NPV):** MSWC's $380 million deferred maintenance backlog includes $240 million for main replacement, $85 million for storage tank rehabilitation, and $55 million for pump station upgrades. These infrastructure investments are necessary to maintain service reliability and comply with industry standards (AWWA PSW main break rate goal of maximum 15 per 100 miles). They are capital expenditures, not tort damages. CGL insurance does not cover such costs.

The PFAS class action litigation (Q6) presents a distinct scenario. The class action alleges third-party bodily injury (medical monitoring) and property damage (diminution in property values), which are potentially covered under the CGL policy (subject to the pollution exclusion analysis in Sections B.1-B.3). However, the underlying capital costs required to remedy the PFAS contamination (treatment systems, infrastructure upgrades) are not themselves covered.

- **Classification:** Regulatory capital costs (uninsured)
- **Methodology:** Sum of NPV of multi-year capital programs
- **Calculation:**
  - Q3 Lead LSL: $1.4B-$1.95B (10-year program, undiscounted)
  - Q4 PFAS Treatment: $150M-$230M NPV (20-year program at 8% WACC)
  - Q9 Infrastructure Backlog: $69.7M NPV (10-year program at 8% WACC)

**Counter-Analysis:** MSWC may argue that an Environmental Impairment Liability (EIL) or Pollution Legal Liability (PLL) policy, if one exists, could provide coverage for PFAS treatment costs (Q4). EIL/PLL policies are specialized environmental insurance products specifically designed to cover pollution-related claims, including third-party bodily injury and property damage, cleanup costs, and legal defense. Unlike CGL policies with pollution exclusions, EIL policies affirmatively cover pollution exposures.

However, modern EIL policies (2023-2024) increasingly contain PFAS-specific exclusions. Insurance Services Office (ISO) published PFAS exclusion endorsements for insurers to incorporate into environmental policies. If MSWC maintains an older EIL policy (pre-2023) without PFAS exclusions, it may provide coverage for PFAS treatment costs.[24]

MSWC, with 45,000 connections across eight affected systems, is estimated to receive $6.75 million to $13.5 million in settlement funding. This funding partially offsets PFAS treatment costs but does not constitute "insurance coverage"—it is tort settlement recovery from the manufacturers.[25]

Lead service line replacement costs (Q3) and infrastructure backlog (Q9) have no comparable insurance or settlement offset. These are purely regulatory capital obligations that MSWC must fund through rate recovery (subject to CPUC prudence review) or equity/debt financing.
