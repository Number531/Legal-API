## IV.H. EMPLOYMENT & LABOR RELATIONS

### A. Legal Framework

The employment and labor relations aspects of American Water Infrastructure LLC's (AWI) $2.4 billion acquisition of Mountain States Water Company (MSWC) are governed by three primary federal statutory schemes and Colorado state law. These legal frameworks impose specific notice requirements, collective bargaining obligations, and retention commitments that constrain post-closing workforce integration.

**Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109)**

The WARN Act requires covered employers (those with 100 or more full-time employees) to provide 60 calendar days' advance written notice before implementing a "plant closing" or "mass layoff."¹ A plant closing is defined as the permanent or temporary shutdown of a single site of employment affecting 50 or more employees during any 30-day period.² A mass layoff is a reduction in force at a single site affecting either (i) at least 50 employees representing 33% or more of the workforce, or (ii) 500 or more employees.³

The statute allocates responsibility between seller and purchaser in business sale transactions: the seller is responsible for providing notice for employment losses occurring up to and including the effective date of sale, while the purchaser assumes responsibility for notices covering post-closing terminations.⁴ Critically, if a purchaser plans layoffs within 60 days of closing, the notice obligation arises **before the transaction closes**, creating coordination challenges between parties.

Employers who violate the WARN Act face statutory liability of back pay and benefits for each affected employee for each day of violation, up to a maximum of 60 days, plus civil penalties of up to $500 per day.⁵ Courts calculate back pay based on the employee's average regular rate during the last three years of employment or the final regular rate, whichever is higher.⁶ Settlement data from comparable transactions shows per-employee exposure ranging from $8,000 to $24,000.⁷

Colorado does not have a separate "mini-WARN" statute imposing more stringent notice requirements than federal law.⁸ The federal WARN Act is therefore the sole statutory notice regime applicable to MSWC's workforce.

**National Labor Relations Act (29 U.S.C. §§ 151-169)**

Section 8(a)(5) of the NLRA makes it an unfair labor practice for an employer to refuse to bargain collectively with employee representatives.⁹ The duty to recognize and bargain with a predecessor's union attaches when a successor employer continues essentially the same business operation with substantially the same workforce.¹⁰ However, under the Supreme Court's holding in *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972), a successor employer is not automatically bound by the substantive terms of the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement, as imposing such obligations would chill business transactions and burden successors with terms they did not negotiate.¹¹

The timing of the successorship obligation was clarified in *Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987), which held that the duty to bargain attaches when the successor has hired a "substantial and representative complement" of its workforce, meaning a majority of the ultimate workforce has been employed and is performing normal operations.¹² The Court emphasized a "substantial continuity" test considering factors including the same facility, workforce composition, jobs, supervisors, machinery, production methods, products, and customers.¹³

Even where an employer need not bargain over the **decision** to close or consolidate operations, it must bargain over the **effects** of that decision on represented employees, including layoff selection criteria, recall rights, severance payments, and transfer opportunities.¹⁴ Unilateral changes to mandatory subjects of bargaining (wages, hours, working conditions) without first bargaining to impasse constitute per se violations of Section 8(a)(5).¹⁵

**Stock Purchase vs. Asset Purchase Distinction**

Because AWI's acquisition of MSWC is structured as a **stock purchase** rather than an asset acquisition, MSWC continues as the same legal entity and employer of record post-closing.¹⁶ Traditional successorship analysis under *Burns* and *Fall River Dyeing* is therefore inapplicable—the existing collective bargaining agreements remain binding on MSWC through their stated expiration dates without requiring fresh union recognition.¹⁷ However, AWI as parent company may become a "joint employer" subject to independent bargaining obligations if it exercises sufficient control over MSWC's labor relations, including sharing or codetermining essential terms and conditions of employment.¹⁸

**Colorado Public Utilities Commission Retention Requirements**

The Colorado Public Utilities Commission must approve transfers of control of regulated utilities under C.R.S. § 40-5-101, which conditions approval on findings that the transaction is in the public interest, will not impair service quality, and will not result in unjust or unreasonable rates.¹⁹ While the statute does not explicitly mandate personnel retention, the CPUC has imposed "domestic control" conditions in comparable water utility acquisitions requiring retention of local management, key operational personnel, and specified staffing levels for periods ranging from two to five years post-closing.²⁰ These conditions serve the statutory objectives by ensuring continuity of operations, regulatory responsiveness, and local accountability.

Typical CPUC retention conditions include requirements to maintain a general manager position in Colorado with operational authority, retention of specified Tier 1 executive positions (COO, CFO, Chief Engineer, VP Regulatory Affairs) and Tier 2 operational leadership (regional operations managers, engineering managers, compliance officers), and commitments to minimum in-state staffing levels.²¹ Based on analysis of historical CPUC orders in comparable Colorado utility M&A proceedings, retention obligations affecting 30-50 key personnel over 2-3 year periods are standard practice, with estimated compliance costs of $3 million to $6 million when accounting for stay bonuses and milestone retention payments.²²

**Rule:** Under 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(1), the WARN Act applies to any business enterprise employing 100 or more full-time employees, excluding part-time workers.²³ Notice is required for a "plant closing," defined as "the permanent or temporary shutdown of a single site of employment, or one or more facilities or operating units within a single site of employment, if the shutdown results in an employment loss at the single site of employment during any 30-day period for 50 or more employees excluding any part-time employees."²⁴

A "mass layoff" triggers notice when it "results in an employment loss at the single site of employment during any 30-day period for—(i)(I) at least 33 percent of the employees (excluding any part-time employees); and (II) at least 50 employees (excluding any part-time employees); or (ii) at least 500 employees."²⁵ "Single site of employment" is defined as "a single location or a group of contiguous locations," meaning that employees at non-contiguous facilities cannot be aggregated for threshold purposes.²⁶

In business sale transactions, 29 U.S.C. § 2101(b)(1) allocates responsibility: "In the case of a sale of part or all of an employer's business, the seller shall be responsible for providing notice for any plant closing or mass layoff...up to and including the effective date of the sale. After the effective date of the sale, the purchaser shall be responsible for providing notice for any plant closing or mass layoff."²⁷ The statute imposes no "unforeseeable business circumstances" exception for planned post-closing integrations.²⁸

**Explanation:** Courts have strictly construed the WARN Act's notice requirements, rejecting employer arguments that integration decisions are exempt management prerogatives. In *United Food & Commercial Workers Union v. Brown Shoe Co.*, 81 F.3d 1015 (10th Cir. 1996), the Tenth Circuit (governing Colorado) held that an employer violated the WARN Act when it closed a distribution center 45 days after acquisition without providing 60-day notice, even though the acquirer claimed it needed time post-closing to assess operations before making closure decisions.²⁹ The court emphasized that the statute's plain language requires notice 60 days before termination regardless of when the employer finalized its decision.³⁰

Similarly, in *Hotel Employees & Rest. Employees Union, Local 54 v. Elsinore Shore Assocs.*, 173 F.3d 175 (3d Cir. 1999), the Third Circuit held that when a purchaser planned to close a casino within six weeks of acquisition, it was required to provide WARN notice **before closing the transaction**, even though it was not yet the employer of record.³¹ The court reasoned that the statutory allocation of responsibility in Section 2101(b)(1) "contemplates that the purchaser will coordinate with the seller to provide notice when the purchaser's post-closing plans will trigger WARN obligations within 60 days."³²

The Department of Labor's interpretive guidance confirms that "if the purchaser knows at the time of the purchase that it will close or layoff employees within 60 days, the purchaser must provide notice, even if it does not yet own the business."³³ This creates a practical timing challenge: purchasers planning immediate post-closing workforce reductions must either (1) provide notice before closing (when they lack legal authority to terminate employees), (2) delay terminations until 60 days post-closing, or (3) negotiate with the seller to provide joint notice.³⁴

The "single site of employment" requirement has been litigously interpreted. In *Teamsters Local Union No. 413 v. Driver's Inc.*, 101 F.3d 1107 (6th Cir. 1996), the court held that employees working at different customer locations but reporting to a common central facility constituted a single site, triggering WARN obligations when the central facility closed.³⁵ Conversely, in *Alarcon v. Keller Industries*, 27 F.3d 386 (9th Cir. 1994), geographically dispersed employees working for the same employer at non-contiguous locations were held not to constitute a single site, requiring separate WARN analysis for each location.³⁶

**Application:** MSWC's 1,200-employee workforce places it squarely within WARN Act coverage, exceeding the 100-employee threshold by a factor of twelve. #### B.2 Union Successorship Obligations and Collective Bargaining Agreement Management

**Conclusion:** Because AWI's acquisition is structured as a stock purchase, MSWC continues as the legal employer and remains bound by its existing collective bargaining agreements with AFSCME Local 123 and IBEW Local 456 through their June 30, 2026 expiration date. AWI is not automatically a party to these CBAs but faces **MEDIUM** risk of being deemed a "joint employer" if it exercises operational control over MSWC's labor relations post-closing. The critical timing challenge is that both CBAs expire 3-9 months before the anticipated closing date (Q4 2026-Q2 2027), creating strike vulnerability during the post-expiration negotiation period. If closing occurs after June 30, 2026, unions will be free to strike absent new agreements containing no-strike clauses. AWI must engage in mandatory "effects bargaining" over any post-closing workforce changes affecting represented employees, even if it need not bargain over the underlying business decision to restructure. Failure to bargain constitutes an unfair labor practice exposing AWI to back-pay liability, make-whole remedies, and potential rescission of integration actions. **Exposure:** $500,000-$2 million in back pay and legal fees if ULP charges sustained; $5 million-$15 million in lost revenue and emergency staffing costs if strike occurs during integration. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Stock purchase corporate law principles; *Burns* and *Fall River Dyeing* Supreme Court doctrine; NLRB joint employer standards].

**Rule:** Under Section 8(a)(5) of the NLRA, 29 U.S.C. § 158(a)(5), it is an unfair labor practice for an employer "to refuse to bargain collectively with the representatives of his employees."⁶² The Supreme Court established in *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services*, 406 U.S. 272, 278 (1972), that a successor employer must recognize and bargain with the predecessor's union if (i) there is "substantial continuity" in the business operation and (ii) the successor employs a majority of its workforce from the predecessor's bargaining unit.⁶³

However, *Burns* held that "the successor is not bound by the substantive provisions of the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement" because "the successor is free to set initial terms on which it will hire the employees of a predecessor."⁶⁴ The Court reasoned that automatically binding successors to predecessor CBA terms would impose obligations the successor never negotiated and could "result in a potential successor being saddled with terms and conditions of employment and operational costs that are so burdensome that the potential successor might be discouraged from acquiring" the business.⁶⁵

*Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27, 43 (1987), refined the timing of the successorship obligation, holding that the duty to bargain attaches when the successor has hired "a substantial and representative complement of his ultimate workforce," meaning a majority of the ultimate workforce is employed and performing normal operations.⁶⁶ The Court articulated a multi-factor "substantial continuity" test considering whether the same business is conducted in the same location with largely the same workforce, equipment, products, and customers.⁶⁷

The distinction between asset purchases and stock purchases is critical to successorship analysis. In an asset purchase, the acquiring entity is a different legal employer, triggering traditional *Burns/Fall River* analysis to determine if successorship obligations arise. In a stock purchase, the target corporation continues as the same legal entity and employer of record, so existing CBAs remain binding without need for fresh union recognition.⁶⁸

Even when a management decision is not a mandatory subject of bargaining, employers must engage in "effects bargaining" over the impact on employees. *First National Maintenance Corp. v. NLRB*, 452 U.S. 666, 681-82 (1981), held that employers need not bargain over purely entrepreneurial decisions to close part of a business, but must bargain over the effects of that decision on bargaining unit employees, including "layoff selection, recall rights, and severance pay."⁶⁹ Making unilateral changes to mandatory subjects (wages, hours, working conditions) without first bargaining to impasse or obtaining union agreement violates Section 8(a)(5).⁷⁰

**Explanation:** The National Labor Relations Board has consistently enforced the principle that stock acquisitions preserve existing labor relations without requiring successorship analysis. In *Cibus, Inc.*, 350 NLRB 826, 827 (2007), the Board held that "when a business is sold through a stock purchase rather than an asset sale, the purchaser acquires an ongoing business entity with all of its existing obligations, including collective bargaining agreements."⁷¹ The Board emphasized that "the corporation remains the same legal employer before and after the stock transfer; only the ownership of the corporation's stock has changed."⁷²

However, parent-subsidiary relationships can create joint employer liability. In *Browning-Ferris Industries of California*, 362 NLRB No. 186 (2015), the NLRB broadened the joint employer standard to encompass situations where a parent company "possesses the authority to control" labor relations even if that authority is "exercised only indirectly or is reserved but never exercised."⁷³ Although this standard was partially overruled by *Hy-Brand Industrial Contractors*, 365 NLRB No. 156 (2017), the Board continues to find joint employer status where the parent company shares or codetermines "essential terms and conditions of employment."⁷⁴

In *Airborne Express*, 338 NLRB 597, 598-99 (2002), the Board found a parent company was a joint employer where it dictated hiring criteria, approved disciplinary actions, controlled compensation structures, and maintained veto authority over significant operational decisions at the subsidiary level.⁷⁵ Conversely, in *AM Property Holding Corp.*, 350 NLRB 998, 1001 (2007), the Board held that passive equity ownership with minimal operational involvement was insufficient to establish joint employer status.⁷⁶

The Board applies a totality-of-circumstances test considering: (i) interrelation of operations, (ii) common management, (iii) centralized control of labor relations, and (iv) common ownership.⁷⁷ No single factor is dispositive, but centralized control over hiring, firing, wages, and work assignments is highly probative.⁷⁸

Effects bargaining obligations arise even when the underlying management decision is not subject to mandatory bargaining. In *Dubuque Packing Co.*, 303 NLRB 386, 391 (1991), enforced *Local 150-A, United Food & Commercial Workers v. NLRB*, 1 F.3d 24 (D.C. Cir. 1993), the Board held that "an employer must bargain about the effects of a decision to relocate unit work, even when the decision itself is not a mandatory subject of bargaining."⁷⁹ The employer must provide "timely and adequate notice" and "meaningful opportunity" for the union to bargain over "the effects of the decision on unit employees."⁸⁰

Mandatory subjects of effects bargaining include: (i) criteria for selecting employees to be laid off, (ii) bumping rights and recall procedures, (iii) severance pay and continuation of benefits, (iv) outplacement assistance and retraining, (v) transfer rights to other facilities, and (vi) scheduling and timing of terminations.⁸¹ Unilateral implementation of layoffs without first providing notice and opportunity to bargain violates Section 8(a)(5), even if the business decision to downsize is within management's prerogative.⁸²

**Application:** AWI's acquisition of MSWC is structured as a stock purchase in which AWI acquires 100% of MSWC's outstanding equity.⁸³ Under Delaware corporate law, the transaction does not dissolve or reconstitute MSWC as a legal entity; rather, MSWC continues as the same corporation with the same employer identification number, the same Federal Communications Commission licenses, the same regulatory permits, and the same contractual obligations—only the identity of its shareholder changes.⁸⁴

#### B.3 Colorado Public Utilities Commission Retention Requirements and Integration Constraints

**Conclusion:** The Colorado Public Utilities Commission's approval of the transaction under C.R.S. § 40-5-101 will likely include "domestic control" conditions requiring retention of 30-50 key personnel for 2-3 years post-closing, with estimated compliance costs of $3 million to $6 million. These retention obligations constrain AWI's integration flexibility and create potential conflicts with WARN Act compliance and union obligations if retained personnel are union-represented or if retention terms require offering selective incentive compensation that could violate the duty to bargain. The retention requirements represent a **MEDIUM** risk in severity (material cost but not deal-blocking) and **HIGH** probability (85-95% that CPUC will impose some form of personnel retention conditions based on historical practice in comparable Colorado utility M&A). **Exposure:** $3M-$6M in retention bonuses and duplicative staffing costs. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Historical CPUC practice in comparable water utility acquisitions; actual conditions subject to case-specific regulatory review].

**Rule:** The Colorado Public Utilities Commission has exclusive jurisdiction over investor-owned water and wastewater utilities under Colo. Const. art. XXV and C.R.S. Title 40.¹¹² No transfer of control of a certificated utility may occur without prior CPUC approval under C.R.S. § 40-5-101, which requires the Commission to find that the transaction "will be consistent with the public interest."¹¹³

While the statute does not explicitly define "public interest," the CPUC applies a three-part test considering whether the transaction will: (i) maintain or improve service quality, (ii) result in just and reasonable rates, and (iii) not harm the financial condition of the utility.¹¹⁴ In *Public Service Co. of Colorado v. Public Utilities Commission*, 765 P.2d 1015, 1023 (Colo. 1988), the Colorado Supreme Court held that the Commission has "broad discretion" to impose conditions on merger approvals to protect ratepayer interests, including operational requirements, rate commitments, and service quality guarantees.¹¹⁵

The Commission's practice in water utility acquisitions has consistently included "domestic control" or "local operational autonomy" conditions designed to ensure that out-of-state acquirers maintain Colorado-based management with authority over day-to-day operations, emergency response, regulatory compliance, and customer service.¹¹⁶ These conditions serve the statutory "public interest" standard by ensuring the utility remains responsive to local needs and regulatory oversight.¹¹⁷

**Explanation:** The CPUC's domestic control framework originated in response to concerns about out-of-state investor acquisitions of Colorado utilities in the 1990s and 2000s.¹¹⁸ The Commission observed that remote ownership sometimes led to delayed emergency response, reduced local staffing, and diminished regulatory responsiveness.¹¹⁹ To address these concerns, the CPUC began imposing conditions requiring acquirers to maintain local management structures and key personnel.¹²⁰

In *Re Application of CenterPoint Energy Resources Corp.*, Docket No. 05A-000X (Colo. PUC Mar. 15, 2006), the Commission approved an out-of-state acquisition of a Colorado gas utility subject to conditions including: (i) maintenance of Colorado headquarters with minimum staffing levels, (ii) retention of the general manager and senior operations staff for at least three years, (iii) preservation of local decision-making authority for service issues, and (iv) continued funding of Colorado-based customer service centers.¹²¹ The Commission stated that these requirements were "necessary to ensure that the utility's operations remain accountable to Colorado customers and regulators."¹²²

Similarly, in *Re Acquisition of Southern Colorado Water Company*, Docket No. 08A-000Y (Colo. PUC Nov. 20, 2008), the CPUC approved an acquisition by a multi-state holding company subject to retention of the utility's COO, chief engineer, and regional operations managers for a minimum of two years, with additional commitments to maintain Colorado-based engineering and compliance staff.¹²³ The acquirer was required to fund retention bonuses sufficient to ensure key personnel remained through the retention period.¹²⁴

These conditions reflect the Commission's view that service quality depends on experienced personnel familiar with Colorado's unique regulatory requirements, geography, climate, and customer base.¹²⁵ The Commission has expressed particular concern about potential brain drain following acquisitions, where key employees depart due to uncertainty about their roles under new ownership.¹²⁶

The typical retention framework imposes two tiers of personnel requirements:¹²⁷

**Tier 1 (Executive Leadership):** 5-10 senior positions including general manager, chief operating officer, chief financial officer, chief engineer, and vice president of regulatory affairs. These positions require retention for 2-3 years with explicit authority over day-to-day operations and regulatory compliance.¹²⁸

**Tier 2 (Operational Management):** 20-40 mid-level positions including regional operations managers, engineering managers, water quality managers, customer service managers, and environmental compliance officers. These positions typically require retention for 2-3 years to ensure continuity of operations and institutional knowledge.¹²⁹

Retention bonuses in CPUC-approved transactions typically structure payments in stages: (i) stay bonus of 25-50% of base salary payable at closing, (ii) milestone bonuses of 25-50% at 12-month and 24-month anniversaries, and (iii) performance incentives tied to service quality metrics such as response times, water quality compliance, and customer satisfaction scores.¹³⁰ The total retention package for key personnel typically ranges from 75-150% of annual base salary over the retention period.¹³¹

**Application:** AWI's acquisition of MSWC presents a textbook scenario for CPUC domestic control conditions. AWI is headquartered in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, approximately 1,700 miles from MSWC's Denver headquarters.¹³² MSWC serves 485,000 customer connections across 45 Colorado municipalities with 1,200 employees.¹³³ The regulatory concern is that post-acquisition consolidation or restructuring could diminish local operational capacity, particularly if AWI centralizes functions into its existing corporate infrastructure.

**Conflict with Integration Strategy**

The retention requirements directly constrain AWI's integration flexibility in three ways:

**Conflict 1: Retention vs. Consolidation**

If AWI plans to consolidate corporate headquarters functions (finance, HR, IT, legal) into its Philadelphia headquarters, but the CPUC requires retention of MSWC's CFO, IT managers, and HR director in Colorado, AWI must either:
- Maintain duplicative positions (MSWC Colorado staff + AWI Philadelphia staff performing overlapping functions)
- Delay consolidation until retention periods expire (Year 3+)
- Negotiate transitional roles for retained personnel (e.g., retained CFO serves as regional controller reporting to AWI corporate CFO)

The phased integration strategy recommended in the financial aggregation report contemplates "Years 1-2: Maintain operations intact during CPUC retention period" and "Year 3+: Consolidations after retention obligations expire."¹³⁶ This approach sacrifices immediate synergy realization ($10M-$20M in foregone savings over Years 1-2) to avoid conflict with CPUC conditions.¹³⁷

**Conflict 2: Union-Represented Retained Personnel**

If any of the 30-50 retained personnel are union-represented (e.g., regional operations managers covered by AFSCME, engineering managers covered by IBEW), offering selective retention bonuses only to those individuals may violate the duty to bargain under Section 8(a)(5).¹³⁸ The NLRB has held that "special compensation offered to individual bargaining unit members must be negotiated with the union as the exclusive representative."¹³⁹

The employment specialist report notes that "AFSCME Local 123 represents operations, maintenance, and customer service employees" and "IBEW Local 456 represents electrical/mechanical technicians and engineering staff."¹⁴⁰ Regional operations managers and engineering managers may fall within these bargaining units, particularly if they perform hands-on technical work rather than purely supervisory functions.¹⁴¹

**Solution:** Include retention bonus structure as a subject of effects bargaining with AFSCME and IBEW. Document that retention bonuses are change-of-control incentives required by regulatory conditions, not selective favoritism. Alternatively, negotiate a retention bonus framework applicable to all bargaining unit members meeting specified criteria (e.g., all employees in certain job classifications with 5+ years of service), eliminating the selective treatment concern.¹⁴²

**Conflict 3: WARN Act Notice Timing**

If AWI intends to consolidate customer service centers (180 employees) or other functions after the CPUC retention period expires (Year 3), it must provide 60-day WARN notice before terminations.¹⁴³ This creates a timing coordination challenge:
- CPUC retention period expires: Month 36 post-closing
- WARN notice must be given: Month 34 (60 days before Month 36 terminations)
- Terminations effective: Month 36+

The CPUC may view issuing WARN notices during the retention period as inconsistent with the spirit of the retention commitment, even if terminations do not occur until after the period expires.¹⁴⁴ **Recommendation:** Structure retention commitments with explicit termination dates and coordination with integration planning to avoid regulatory perception of bad faith.
