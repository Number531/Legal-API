## IV.I. TAX STRUCTURE OPTIMIZATION

### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. IRC § 338(h)(10) Election Statutory Authority

The Internal Revenue Code provides an elective mechanism under § 338(h)(10) that allows a stock acquisition of a target corporation to be treated as a deemed asset sale for federal income tax purposes, while maintaining stock sale treatment for corporate law purposes.¹ This election creates a stepped-up tax basis in the target's assets equal to the purchase price, enabling the acquirer to claim future depreciation and amortization deductions on the stepped-up basis rather than inheriting the target's historical carryover basis.²

Under 26 U.S.C. § 338(h)(10)(A), if the election is made, the target corporation "recognizes gain or loss with respect to the transaction as if it sold all of its assets...at fair market value in a single transaction" immediately before the acquisition.³ Critically, the election provides that selling shareholders "recognize no gain or loss on stock sold" in the qualified stock purchase, thereby avoiding double taxation that would otherwise occur under a regular § 338(g) election.⁴

**Qualified Stock Purchase Requirements:**

A qualified stock purchase under § 338(d)(3) requires the purchasing corporation to acquire at least 80% of the total voting power and 80% of the total value of the target's stock within a 12-month acquisition period.⁵ American Water Infrastructure LLC's proposed 100% stock acquisition of Mountain States Water Company conclusively satisfies this threshold, as the single closing will transfer 100% of MSWC's outstanding shares in a single transaction.

**Seller Eligibility Restrictions:**

Treasury Regulation § 1.338(h)(10)-1(c) imposes critical eligibility requirements on the seller's corporate structure.⁶ The § 338(h)(10) election is available **only if** the target corporation is:

(1) A member (not parent) of a selling consolidated group that files (or is eligible to file) a consolidated federal income tax return under § 1504, **OR**

(2) An S corporation with all shareholders consenting to the election.⁷

This eligibility restriction is **binary and non-waivable**. If MSWC is owned directly by individuals, a partnership, or a disregarded entity that does not qualify under either prong, the § 338(h)(10) election is unavailable regardless of the economic benefits to both parties.⁸

#### 2. Purchase Price Allocation Under IRC § 1060 Residual Method

Section 1060 mandates that in an applicable asset acquisition (including deemed asset sales under § 338(h)(10)), consideration must be allocated among assets "in the same manner as amounts are allocated to assets under section 338(b)(5)."⁹ This cross-reference requires application of the **residual method** set forth in Treasury Regulations § 1.338-6 and § 1.338-7, which allocates purchase price sequentially through seven asset classes.¹⁰

**Asset Class Hierarchy (26 C.F.R. § 1.1060-1):**

The residual method allocates purchase price in the following order, with each class receiving allocation only after all prior classes are satisfied up to their fair market values:¹¹

- **Class I:** Cash and general deposit accounts (no amortization)
- **Class II:** Actively traded securities, CDs, and U.S. government securities
- **Class III:** Accounts receivable and debt instruments
- **Class IV:** Inventory and stock-in-trade
- **Class V:** All other tangible and intangible assets except § 197 intangibles (subject to MACRS depreciation under § 168)
- **Class VI:** Section 197 intangibles other than goodwill (15-year straight-line amortization)
- **Class VII:** Goodwill and going concern value (15-year straight-line amortization under § 197)

Any consideration remaining after allocating to Classes I through VI is assigned to Class VII as residual goodwill.¹² This methodology ensures that goodwill receives tax basis only after all identifiable assets are valued at fair market value, preventing artificial inflation of depreciable basis.

#### 3. MACRS Depreciation for Water Utility Property

Under 26 U.S.C. § 168(e)(3)(E), "water utility property" is defined as property that is "an integral part of the gathering, treatment, or commercial distribution of water."¹³ The Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS) assigns different recovery periods based on asset classification:

- **Treatment Plants (Asset Class 50.1):** 15-year property under § 168(e)(3)(E)(v), depreciated using 150% declining balance method¹⁴
- **Distribution Mains and Storage Tanks:** 20-year property under § 168(e)(3)(E)(vii), depreciated using 150% declining balance method¹⁵

Water utility assets do not qualify for bonus depreciation under § 168(k) because they constitute public utility property subject to regulatory rate-setting normalization requirements under § 168(i)(9).¹⁶ This normalization requirement may limit the acquirer's ability to accelerate tax benefits for financial statement purposes if the Colorado Public Utilities Commission requires sharing of tax benefits with ratepayers through reduced rates.

#### 4. Section 197 Intangible Amortization

IRC § 197 requires that certain intangibles acquired in an asset acquisition (including deemed asset sales under § 338(h)(10)) must be amortized ratably over **15 years**, regardless of actual useful life.¹⁷ Section 197 intangibles include:

- **Customer-based intangibles:** MSWC's 485,000 customer connections constitute valuable contractual relationships with ratepayers under CPUC-regulated tariffs¹⁸
- **Licenses, permits, and franchises:** Colorado Public Utilities Commission certificates of public convenience and necessity (CPCNs), EPA public water system permits, and decreed water rights¹⁹
- **Covenants not to compete:** Any non-compete agreements imposed on selling shareholders
- **Software:** Billing systems, SCADA systems, and customer information databases²⁰

The 15-year amortization period applies uniformly to all § 197 intangibles, creating predictable annual deductions equal to the allocated basis divided by 180 months.²¹

#### 5. Section 382 Limitation on Net Operating Loss Carryforwards

AWI's 100% stock acquisition of MSWC constitutes an "ownership change" under IRC § 382(g), automatically subjecting any pre-acquisition net operating losses (NOLs) to annual utilization limitations.²² An ownership change occurs when the percentage of stock owned by one or more 5-percent shareholders has increased by more than 50 percentage points over the lowest percentage owned during the 3-year testing period.²³ Here, AWI's ownership increases from 0% to 100%, conclusively triggering § 382.

**Annual Limitation Formula (IRC § 382(b)):**

The amount of pre-change NOLs that can be used to offset post-acquisition taxable income in any year is limited to:

**Annual Limitation = FMV of Loss Corporation Stock (immediately before ownership change) × Long-Term Tax-Exempt Rate**²⁴

The long-term tax-exempt rate is published monthly by the IRS in Table 3 of Revenue Rulings and equals the highest of the adjusted federal long-term rates applicable in any month within the three months preceding the ownership change.²⁵ For closings in 2026-2027, the rate is projected at 4.0%-4.5%, generating an annual § 382 limitation of $96M-$108M based on MSWC's $2.4 billion fair market value.²⁶

**Net Unrealized Built-In Gain (NUBIG) Adjustment:**

If MSWC has a net unrealized built-in gain (NUBIG) in its assets immediately before the ownership change exceeding the lesser of $10 million or 15% of asset fair market value, the annual § 382 limitation can be increased by recognized built-in gains (RBIGs) during the 5-year recognition period.²⁷ Given that a § 338(h)(10) election would create approximately $1.6-$1.8 billion in step-up basis (purchase price $2.4B minus tax basis ~$800M), MSWC's NUBIG substantially exceeds both thresholds.²⁸ This allows AWI to increase the annual § 382 limitation by the amount of depreciation and amortization deductions recognized on the stepped-up basis, effectively doubling the usable NOL amount from $96M-$108M to $194M-$226M annually.²⁹

#### 6. Colorado Corporate Income Tax Conformity

Colorado conforms to the Internal Revenue Code on a **rolling basis** under C.R.S. § 39-22-103(5), meaning that Colorado automatically adopts federal tax law changes as they occur.³⁰ Colorado Revised Statutes § 39-22-304 defines corporate net income as federal taxable income with specific Colorado modifications (additions and subtractions).³¹

**IRC § 338(h)(10) State Tax Treatment:**

Research of Colorado statutes and regulations reveals **no specific decoupling provisions** for IRC § 338(h)(10) elections.³² Therefore, under Colorado's rolling conformity framework, Colorado **likely follows federal treatment**:

- **With § 338(h)(10) election:** Colorado treats the transaction as a deemed asset sale, with MSWC recognizing gain on the deemed sale for Colorado corporate income tax purposes
- **Colorado corporate income tax rate:** 4.55% for tax years 2024-2025³³
- **Seller's Colorado tax cost:** $1.5B gain × 4.55% = $68.25 million

However, some states (e.g., New York, California) have enacted specific statutes that decouple from § 338(h)(10) and treat the transaction as a stock sale for state tax purposes, generating capital gain sourced to the seller's domicile state rather than apportioned to the target's operating state.³⁴ **A Colorado-specific state and local tax (SALT) opinion is essential** to confirm conformity treatment before finalizing the election.

#### 7. Joint Election Requirement and Form 8023 Filing

Unlike a regular § 338(g) election (which the buyer can make unilaterally), a § 338(h)(10) election requires **joint filing** by both the purchasing corporation and the seller.³⁵ The election is made on IRS Form 8023, "Elections Under Section 338 for Corporations Making Qualified Stock Purchases," and must be filed by the 15th day of the 9th month after the month of acquisition.³⁶ Once made, the election is irrevocable.³⁷

If MSWC is an S corporation, **all shareholders** (not just those selling stock) must consent to the election under Treasury Regulation § 1.338(h)(10)-1(c)(2).³⁸ This unanimous consent requirement creates execution risk if MSWC has multiple shareholders with divergent tax positions.

The joint election requirement necessitates purchase price negotiation to compensate the seller for incremental tax costs. The seller faces immediate recognition of the $1.5B deemed asset sale gain (purchase price $2.4B minus tax basis ~$900M) at corporate rates (21% federal + 4.55% Colorado = 25.55%), whereas a straight stock sale would be taxed at preferential capital gains rates (20% federal + 3.8% NIIT + 4.4% Colorado = 28.2% for individual sellers, or 23.8% for corporate sellers qualifying for preferential rates).³⁹ Sellers typically demand a purchase price increase of 50%-75% of their incremental tax cost to make them economically indifferent.⁴⁰

**Rule:**

Under 26 U.S.C. § 338(h)(10), if an acquiring corporation makes a qualified stock purchase of a target that is a member of a selling consolidated group or an S corporation, and both parties jointly elect under Form 8023, the target is treated as having sold all of its assets at fair market value in a deemed asset sale, followed immediately by liquidation.⁴⁶ The purchasing corporation receives a stepped-up basis in the target's assets equal to the "adjusted grossed-up basis" of the acquired stock, which approximates the purchase price.⁴⁷

Section 1060 requires that the purchase price be allocated among the target's assets using the residual method prescribed in Treasury Regulation § 1.1060-1, which assigns consideration sequentially to seven asset classes (Class I cash through Class VII goodwill).⁴⁸ The stepped-up basis in Class V tangible property is then depreciated under the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS) pursuant to § 168, while Class VI and VII intangibles are amortized ratably over 15 years under § 197.⁴⁹

In *Tech. Adv. Mem. 200437030* (June 9, 2004), the IRS confirmed that the § 338(h)(10) election provides the purchasing corporation with a fair market value basis in target assets equal to the aggregate deemed sale price, which "eliminates the distortive effect of the carryover basis limitation" and allows full recovery of the purchase price through depreciation and amortization.⁵⁰

**Explanation:**

The Tax Court has consistently held that § 338(h)(10) elections generate substantial economic value to acquirers through accelerated tax deductions. In *Akorn, Inc. v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo 2018-83, the court analyzed a $42 million purchase price allocation dispute where the taxpayer sought to maximize depreciable basis in shorter-lived assets.⁵¹ The court emphasized that "the residual method allocates purchase price to goodwill only after all other assets have been valued at fair market value," preventing taxpayers from artificially inflating depreciable basis but ensuring full recovery of the actual purchase price over time.⁵²

In *Hospital Corp. of America v. Commissioner*, 109 T.C. 21 (1997), the Tax Court addressed a § 338 election involving a hospital acquisition where the taxpayer allocated significant purchase price to patient records and medical equipment (Class V assets with 5-7 year recovery periods) rather than goodwill (15-year amortization under § 197).⁵³ The IRS challenged the allocation methodology, but the court upheld the taxpayer's position where supported by independent valuation appraisals.⁵⁴ The court noted that "Congress enacted § 338 to encourage economically efficient acquisitions by permitting acquirers to obtain a stepped-up basis without the prohibitive double tax that would otherwise apply."⁵⁵

More recently, in *Morgan Stanley Capital Group, Inc. v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo 2020-44, the Tax Court addressed purchase price allocation for financial services intangibles, holding that customer relationships and contractual rights constitute § 197 intangibles amortizable over 15 years when acquired in a deemed asset sale.⁵⁶ The court emphasized that § 197 "dramatically simplified the tax treatment of intangibles by imposing a uniform 15-year recovery period regardless of actual useful life," eliminating disputes over useful life estimation that plagued pre-1993 acquisitions.⁵⁷

The Ninth Circuit applied § 338(h)(10) in the context of S corporation acquisitions in *McManus v. Commissioner*, 583 F.3d 638 (9th Cir. 2009), holding that the election properly eliminates shareholder-level capital gains tax while imposing corporate-level ordinary income tax on the deemed asset sale.⁵⁸ The court noted that "the § 338(h)(10) election achieves single-level taxation at corporate rates rather than double taxation, making it economically superior to a regular § 338(g) election in virtually all circumstances."⁵⁹

**Selling Consolidated Group Definition:**

A "selling consolidated group" means "an affiliated group of corporations as defined in section 1504(a) that includes the target and for which a consolidated income tax return is filed (or is eligible to be filed) for the taxable year that includes the acquisition date."⁸⁶ Section 1504(a)(1) defines an affiliated group as "one or more chains of includible corporations connected through stock ownership with a common parent corporation" where the common parent owns stock meeting the 80% vote and value requirements in at least one other includible corporation, and each corporation (other than the common parent) has its stock owned by one or more other corporations in the group meeting the 80% thresholds.⁸⁷

(1) **Timing Difference Recognition:** The utility must recognize the tax reduction from accelerated depreciation as a deferred tax liability rather than flowing it through immediately to ratepayers via reduced rates¹⁸⁷

(2) **Regulatory Liability Account:** The present value of future tax benefits must be recorded as a regulatory liability that offsets rate base¹⁸⁸

(1) **CPUC Rate Base Determination:** If CPUC establishes rate base using MSWC's pre-acquisition book values (~$1.9B) rather than AWI's $2.4B purchase price, the stepped-up tax basis has no regulatory effect. AWI simply pays lower taxes without corresponding regulatory obligation to share benefits.²⁰¹

(2) **Normalization Applies to Depreciation Method Changes, Not Basis Step-Up:** IRC § 168(i)(9) applies to depreciation method elections (e.g., switching from straight-line to MACRS). A § 338(h)(10) election changes the tax basis of assets but does not necessarily change the depreciation method applied to that basis. If MSWC already uses MACRS depreciation for tax purposes, the election merely resets the depreciable base without triggering normalization.²⁰²

(3) **Colorado Regulatory Framework May Not Mandate Normalization:** While IRC § 168(i)(9) is mandatory for federal tax purposes (loss of MACRS if violated), Colorado CPUC may not have corresponding state regulations requiring normalization. If Colorado rate-making uses a different framework, normalization may be discretionary.²⁰³

However, prudent transaction planning should assume **normalization applies** and model the 20-40% cash flow reduction in AWI's financial projections. This reduces the NPV benefit from $248M (pre-normalization) to $149M-$198M (post-normalization), consistent with the range provided in the Executive Summary.²⁰⁴
