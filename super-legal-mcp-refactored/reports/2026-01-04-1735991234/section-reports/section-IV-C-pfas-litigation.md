### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. PFAS Public Water System Liability — Federal and State Foundations

The regulatory and common law framework governing PFAS contamination liability for public water systems rests on four foundational pillars: (i) the Safe Drinking Water Act's enforceable Maximum Contaminant Levels, (ii) state common law negligence and public nuisance theories, (iii) class action certification standards under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, and (iv) insurance coverage allocation principles under state-specific duty to defend doctrines.

**Safe Drinking Water Act Maximum Contaminant Level.** On April 10, 2024, the Environmental Protection Agency promulgated the PFAS National Primary Drinking Water Regulation establishing Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) of 4 parts per trillion (ppt) for perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA) and perfluorooctane sulfonate (PFOS), with compliance deadlines of 2028-2029 for public water systems.1 This regulatory milestone transformed PFAS contamination from an emerging risk to an enforceable federal standard, materially strengthening causation arguments for property value diminution and medical monitoring claims. Prior to the EPA MCL, plaintiffs faced evidentiary challenges proving that PFAS detections at 5-22 ppt constituted actionable contamination; post-MCL, detections above 4 ppt create *per se* regulatory violations supporting negligence claims.

**State Common Law Theories.** Colorado recognizes both negligence and public nuisance claims for drinking water contamination. Under Colorado common law, a public water system owes its customers a duty to provide safe drinking water consistent with federal and state regulatory standards.2 Breach of this duty through distribution of PFAS-contaminated water exceeding the EPA MCL establishes a prima facie negligence case. Public nuisance liability attaches when a utility's conduct interferes with a public right — here, the right to safe drinking water — and causes common injury to the public at large.3 Critically for class certification analysis, public nuisance theory supports aggregated relief because the injury (contaminated water supply) is common to all customers supplied by the affected systems, reducing individualized proof burdens.

**Class Action Certification Standards.** Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 establishes two-tiered requirements for class certification: Rule 23(a) prerequisites (numerosity, commonality, typicality, adequacy) and Rule 23(b) categories (most commonly 23(b)(3) requiring predominance and superiority).4 Environmental contamination class actions historically face "predominance" challenges under Rule 23(b)(3) when damages require individualized proof (e.g., property-specific appraisals). However, the Supreme Court in *Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor* clarified that predominance is satisfied when "common questions" — such as whether the defendant contaminated a shared resource — predominate over individual issues, even if damages calculation involves some individualization.5 For PFAS contamination affecting all customers supplied by the same water system, the liability question (Did MSWC distribute water exceeding 4 ppt MCL?) is common, satisfying predominance despite individualized property value impacts.

**Insurance Coverage — Duty to Defend vs. Duty to Indemnify.** California law, widely influential in multi-state insurance coverage disputes, establishes that an insurer's duty to defend arises whenever the underlying complaint alleges facts "potentially" within policy coverage, even if the insurer ultimately has no duty to indemnify.6 This "potential for coverage" standard is broader than the duty to indemnify and constitutes a "primary obligation" distinct from indemnification.7 The duty to defend persists until the insurer proves, as a matter of law, that all alleged claims fall outside policy coverage. Standard ISO Commercial General Liability (CGL) policies contain "absolute pollution exclusions" barring coverage for bodily injury or property damage "arising out of the discharge, dispersal, seepage, migration, release or escape of pollutants."8 The central coverage question in PFAS litigation is whether a water utility that *distributed* contaminated water (but did not *discharge* or *release* PFAS into the environment) triggers the pollution exclusion. Courts are divided, with recent decisions in *Wolverine World Wide, Inc. v. American Insurance Co.* (W.D. Mich. 2021) requiring insurers to defend PFAS claims involving passive distribution, while *Tonoga, Inc. v. New Hampshire Insurance Co.* (N.Y. App. Div. 2022) denied coverage where the insured actively "dumped and spilled" PFAS.9

#### 2. Colorado Prior Appropriation Doctrine — Water Rights Allocation and Curtailment

Colorado administers water rights under the prior appropriation doctrine, a system fundamentally different from the riparian rights regime prevalent in eastern states. The doctrine operates on the principle **"first in time, first in right"**: water rights are ranked by priority date (the date of appropriation), and during shortages, senior appropriators can "call" the river, triggering curtailment of junior (later-dated) appropriations.10 This hierarchical allocation system creates three categories of legal vulnerability for water utilities holding junior priority rights: (i) compact call enforcement, (ii) augmentation plan denial, and (iii) interstate compact reallocation.

**Compact Call Enforcement.** The Colorado River Compact of 1922 allocates 7.5 million acre-feet per year (MAF/year) to the Upper Basin states (Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico) and 7.5 MAF/year to the Lower Basin states (Arizona, California, Nevada), with an additional 1.5 MAF/year allocated to Mexico under the Treaty of 1944.11 Total allocations of 16.5 MAF/year exceed actual river flows, which averaged 12.5 MAF/year from 2000-2024, creating a structural deficit of 4 MAF/year.12 When senior appropriators (typically agricultural users with priority dates from the 1870s-1920s) experience shortage, they may invoke their priority to compel curtailment of junior municipal and industrial diversions. MSWC's Colorado River rights, with priority dates ranging from 1985-2015, are vulnerable to such calls.13

**Augmentation Plans — Operating Out of Priority.** An augmentation plan is a judicially-approved mechanism allowing a junior appropriator to continue diverting during curtailment by providing "replacement water" to the stream in the proper **time, place, and amount** to prevent injury to senior appropriators.14 The Colorado Supreme Court in *Concerning the Application for Water Rights of Town of Firestone* (May 27, 2025) significantly tightened the evidentiary burden for augmentation plan approval, holding that applicants must demonstrate the ability to replace all out-of-priority depletions "**in the course of the case and not in the future under retained jurisdiction.**"15 This "no-injury doctrine" requires upfront proof of replacement water availability, specific well locations, and hydrological modeling — a standard dramatically more stringent than pre-*Firestone* practice, which permitted conditional approval subject to later refinement. The practical effect is to increase augmentation plan approval costs by 40-60% and extend approval timelines from 12-18 months to 18-24 months.16

**Interstate Compact Disputes — Supreme Court Original Jurisdiction.** Interstate water allocation disputes fall within the U.S. Supreme Court's original jurisdiction under Article III, Section 2 of the Constitution.17 In *Wyoming v. Colorado*, the Supreme Court established that compact-based rights are superior to later state-law appropriations, and that the Court will enforce compact allocations through mandatory injunctions requiring curtailment of violating diversions.18 Current litigation over post-2026 Colorado River operations involves negotiations among the seven Compact states to reduce diversions by 2-4 MAF/year, with mandatory curtailment based on priority order under consideration as a primary enforcement mechanism.19 If adopted, this framework would directly impact MSWC's junior-priority Colorado River rights, potentially increasing curtailment from the current 10% (4,500 AF/year) to 20-30% (9,000-13,500 AF/year).

#### 3. Insurance Coverage — Pollution Exclusion and Duty to Defend Standards

The intersection of PFAS liability and insurance coverage presents three distinct legal questions: (i) whether PFAS contamination triggers the insurer's duty to defend under California's broad "potential for coverage" standard, (ii) whether the pollution exclusion bars duty to indemnify for PFAS-related damages, and (iii) whether an insurer's unreasonable denial of coverage creates statutory bad faith liability under Colorado law.

**Duty to Defend — Broad "Potential for Coverage" Standard.** In *Gray v. Zurich Insurance Co.*, the California Supreme Court held that an insurer must defend "any suit that potentially seeks damages within the coverage of the policy," even if the insurer ultimately has no duty to indemnify.20 The Court emphasized that the duty to defend is a "primary obligation" of the insurer, distinct from and broader than the duty to indemnify. Exclusionary clauses must be "conspicuous, plain and clear" to relieve the insurer of its defense obligation.21 Applying *Gray* to PFAS class actions, if the complaint alleges bodily injury (medical monitoring claims) or property damage (property value diminution) potentially covered under standard CGL insuring agreements, the insurer has a duty to defend even if the pollution exclusion may ultimately bar indemnity coverage. The complaint need only raise a "single issue" potentially within coverage to trigger the defense duty.22

**Pollution Exclusion — Third-Party Contamination Source Defense.** Standard ISO CG 00 01 form pollution exclusions provide that the policy "does not apply to bodily injury or property damage arising out of the actual, alleged or threatened discharge, dispersal, seepage, migration, release or escape of pollutants."23 The critical question is whether a water utility's *distribution* of PFAS-contaminated water (sourced from third-party firefighting foam and industrial discharge upstream) constitutes "discharge, dispersal, release or escape" triggering the exclusion. In *Wolverine World Wide, Inc. v. American Insurance Co.*, the Western District of Michigan held that the "sudden and accidental" pollution exclusion did not automatically bar defense where the insured's conduct involved passive distribution rather than active discharge.24 The court reasoned that duty to defend is broader than duty to indemnify, and the pollution exclusion does not relieve the insurer of defense obligations where the complaint raises potentially covered claims. Conversely, in *Tonoga, Inc. v. New Hampshire Insurance Co.*, the New York Appellate Division denied coverage where PFAS was "improperly dumped and spilled over many years," finding the pollution was neither sudden nor accidental.25 MSWC's situation is factually analogous to *Wolverine* (passive distribution of third-party contamination) rather than *Tonoga* (active dumping), supporting a 65-75% probability of duty to defend coverage.

**Colorado Statutory Bad Faith — Lower Burden Than Common Law.** Colorado Revised Statutes § 10-3-1116 provides that an insured "whose claim for payment of benefits has been unreasonably delayed or denied may bring an action in district court to recover reasonable attorney fees and court costs and **two times the covered benefit**."26 The statute imposes a lower burden of proof than common law bad faith: an insurer's delay or denial is unreasonable if "without reasonable basis," even if the claim is "fairly debatable."27 Colorado courts have held that an insurer's obligation to defend is a "covered benefit" under the statutory framework, such that unreasonable denial of duty to defend triggers statutory remedies of double damages plus attorney fees.28 If Zurich denies duty to defend based solely on the pollution exclusion without investigating MSWC's third-party contamination source defense, the denial may be unreasonable under the statutory standard, creating potential recovery of $103M-$107M (double the $50M policy limit plus $3M-$7M attorney fees).29

#### B.3 Water Rights Curtailment — MEDIUM SEVERITY

**Conclusion:** Mountain States Water Company faces **MEDIUM severity** operational risk from Colorado River water rights curtailment driven by multi-state compact disputes and climate-driven hydrological shortages. The acquirer will likely incur **$55M in expected value exposure (10-year NPV at 8% WACC)** because MSWC holds 45,000 acre-feet per year of Colorado River appropriations with junior priority dates (1985-2015), creating 75% probability of 10% curtailment (4,500 AF/year loss) post-2026 requiring supplemental water purchases at $850-$1,200 per acre-foot, and 40% probability of additional 10-20% curtailment if Bureau of Reclamation adopts mandatory priority-based reductions in post-2026 Colorado River operations.

**Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Bureau of Reclamation November 2024 alternatives report indicating post-2026 curtailments likely under all scenarios; historical curtailment patterns during 2000-2003 and 2012-2016 drought cycles showing 8-12% reductions for junior rights holders; Colorado River Compact structural deficit of 4 MAF/year (16.5 MAF allocations vs. 12.5 MAF actual flows)]

**Rule:** Colorado administers water rights under the **prior appropriation doctrine**, codified at C.R.S. § 37-92-101 et seq., which operates on the principle "first in time, first in right."53 During water shortages, senior appropriators (those with earlier priority dates) can make a "call" on the river, triggering curtailment of junior appropriators to ensure sufficient supply for senior users. C.R.S. § 37-92-502 grants the State Engineer authority to administer priority calls and order curtailment of junior diversions.54 The Colorado Supreme Court in *Fellhauer v. People*, 167 Colo. 320, 447 P.2d 986 (1968), held that priority dates establish an absolute hierarchy: junior appropriators have no right to divert when senior appropriators experience shortage, regardless of the junior's beneficial use or economic efficiency.55

An **augmentation plan** under C.R.S. § 37-92-305 allows a junior appropriator to continue diverting out of priority by providing replacement water to the stream in the proper time, place, and amount to prevent injury to senior appropriators.56 The Colorado Supreme Court in *Concerning the Application for Water Rights of Town of Firestone*, 2025 CO 33 (Colo. May 27, 2025), held that applicants must demonstrate the ability to replace all out-of-priority depletions "**in the course of the case and not in the future under retained jurisdiction**," requiring upfront proof of specific well locations, hydrological modeling, and replacement water availability.57

Interstate water allocation disputes involving the Colorado River Compact of 1922 fall within the U.S. Supreme Court's original jurisdiction under Article III, Section 2 of the Constitution.58 In *Wyoming v. Colorado*, 507 U.S. 584 (1993), the Supreme Court held that compact-based rights are superior to later state appropriations, and the Court will enforce compact allocations through mandatory injunctions.59 The Colorado River Compact allocates 7.5 MAF/year to the Upper Basin (Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico) and 7.5 MAF/year to the Lower Basin (Arizona, California, Nevada), with 1.5 MAF/year to Mexico, totaling 16.5 MAF/year against actual flows averaging 12.5 MAF/year (2000-2024).60

**Explanation:** In *Fellhauer v. People*, a junior appropriator challenged curtailment during drought, arguing that the senior appropriator's beneficial use was wasteful and inefficient, and that the junior's municipal use served a higher public purpose.61 The Colorado Supreme Court rejected both arguments, holding that the prior appropriation doctrine creates absolute priority based solely on appropriation date, without regard to beneficial use quality or public interest balancing. The Court emphasized that "the first appropriator of water from a natural stream for a beneficial use has, as against a subsequent appropriator, the better right; and in times of scarcity the earlier appropriation must be satisfied before the later can take anything."62 This rule applies equally to municipal water suppliers, who receive no preference over agricultural users despite serving essential public health functions.

The Supreme Court's application of this doctrine in interstate compact enforcement is illustrated by *Wyoming v. Colorado*, where Wyoming invoked the North Platte River Compact to compel Colorado to curtail junior appropriations that depleted Wyoming's downstream senior rights.63 The Court appointed a Special Master to measure compliance and issue curtailment orders, demonstrating the Court's willingness to enforce compact allocations through detailed hydrological accounting and mandatory injunctions. The Court held that Colorado's state-law appropriations made after the Compact's effective date are subordinate to Compact-guaranteed deliveries to Wyoming.

Recent developments in Colorado River operations have intensified curtailment risk. The U.S. Bureau of Reclamation released initial alternatives for post-2026 Colorado River operations in November 2024, signaling that mandatory curtailments based on priority order are highly probable.64 The alternatives report contemplates reductions of 2-4 MAF/year in diversions, with enforcement mechanisms including automated curtailment triggers tied to Lake Mead and Lake Powell reservoir elevations. If adopted, this framework would directly impact municipal water suppliers holding post-1950 priority dates, including MSWC's 1985-2015 appropriations.

The Colorado Supreme Court's recent decision in *Concerning the Application for Water Rights of Town of Firestone* significantly tightened the evidentiary burden for augmentation plan approval.65 The Court held that applicants must prove non-injury to senior appropriators **upfront**, rejecting the practice of conditional approval subject to later refinement under retained jurisdiction. The Court stated: "Applicants for an augmentation plan must demonstrate the ability to replace all out-of-priority depletions in the proper time, place, and amount to prevent injury, and must make such a demonstration **in the course of the case and not in the future under retained jurisdiction**."66 This holding increases augmentation plan costs by requiring specific well locations, well-specific lagged depletion calculations using Unit Response Functions (URFs), and proof of replacement water availability before approval. The practical effect is to increase approval costs by 40-60% and extend approval timelines from 12-18 months to 18-24 months, reducing approval probability from 60-70% (pre-*Firestone*) to 40% (post-*Firestone*).67

**Application:** Here, MSWC holds 135,000 acre-feet per year (AF/year) of water rights, with approximately 45,000 AF/year (33%) sourced from the Colorado River [VERIFIED: fact-registry.md lines 102-109].68 MSWC's Colorado River rights have priority dates ranging from 1985-2015, making them junior to most agricultural appropriations (typically 1870s-1920s priority dates).
