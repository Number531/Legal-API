## IV.G. COMMERCIAL CONTRACTS & INFRASTRUCTURE OBLIGATIONS

### A. Legal Framework

This section analyzes commercial contract implications and infrastructure maintenance obligations arising from American Water Infrastructure LLC's (AWI) proposed $2.4 billion acquisition of Mountain States Water Company (MSWC). The analysis addresses two distinct but interrelated legal issues: (1) whether MSWC's $380 million deferred infrastructure maintenance backlog constitutes imprudent utility conduct under Colorado's statutory framework for public utilities, exposing the company to regulatory disallowance risk in future rate cases; and (2) whether material commercial contracts contain change of control provisions that may affect transaction execution or impose consent burdens on the acquirer.

#### 1. Colorado Statutory Framework for Adequate Utility Service

Colorado Revised Statutes § 40-3-101(2) imposes a non-discretionary statutory obligation on all public utilities operating within the state to maintain infrastructure at a level that ensures adequate service to the public. The statute provides:

> "Every public utility shall furnish, provide, and maintain such service, instrumentalities, equipment, and facilities as shall promote the safety, health, comfort, and convenience of its patrons, employees, and the public, and as shall in all respects be adequate, efficient, just, and reasonable."¹

This statutory language creates four critical obligations: (1) the duty to "maintain" (an ongoing obligation, not one-time compliance); (2) service must be "adequate" (meeting minimum performance standards); (3) service must be "efficient" (not wasteful of resources or capital); and (4) service must be "just and reasonable" (balancing utility financial viability with customer protection). The Colorado Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) possesses statutory authority under C.R.S. § 40-3-101(2) to establish performance standards, prescribe rules for service adequacy, and order utilities to provide necessary equipment and facilities to meet these requirements.²

The CPUC must balance "the needs of Colorado customers for safe and reliable utility services at reasonable rates and the needs of utility service providers to earn a reasonable profit and to sustain a reliable utility infrastructure throughout Colorado."³ This balancing test is not a safe harbor for deferred maintenance—the Commission cannot allow utilities to indefinitely postpone infrastructure replacement to preserve short-term rate affordability if doing so compromises long-term system reliability and safety. Courts have consistently held that financial constraints alone do not excuse a utility from its statutory obligation to provide adequate service under state public utility statutes.⁴

#### 2. Prudent Investment Standard for Infrastructure Capital

The prudent investment test, applied universally in utility rate regulation, determines whether costs incurred by a utility are recoverable through customer rates. The Colorado standard asks: "Were costs reasonable at the time they were incurred, and given the circumstances and what was known or knowable at the time?"⁵ For infrastructure maintenance expenditures, the prudency inquiry examines:

- **Reasonableness at the time of decision:** Did utility management have information suggesting replacement was necessary?
- **Management judgment quality:** Did the utility exercise reasonable business judgment in capital planning?
- **Used and useful test:** Does the investment provide actual service to customers (versus speculative or excess capacity)?

The "used and useful" test is generally satisfied for aging infrastructure replacement—1,200 miles of 80-100 year old cast iron mains are unquestionably providing active service to MSWC's 485,000 customers. The critical issue becomes whether *deferring replacement* beyond the infrastructure's useful life constitutes imprudent management. Regulatory precedent establishes that utilities may be found imprudent when:

**Factors Indicating Imprudence:**
- Deteriorating performance metrics (main break rates exceeding industry benchmarks by 40-82%)
- Escalating future costs due to deferral (emergency repairs, water loss, property damage claims compounding replacement costs)
- Safety and health risks to the public (aging infrastructure creates contamination risks and service interruption hazards)
- Lack of systematic capital planning (absence of asset management framework consistent with EPA best practices)
- Unjustified delay (financial constraints without evidence of rate case attempts or alternative funding sources)

State public utility commissions possess authority to disallow recovery of costs that result from imprudent management decisions, including systematic failure to maintain infrastructure.⁶ The disallowance percentage typically ranges from 10-25% of imprudent costs, with the percentage reflecting the severity of management failure and the extent to which ratepayers should be protected from bearing costs that prudent management could have avoided.

#### 3. Federal Asset Management Framework

The America's Water Infrastructure Act of 2018 (AWIA) Section 2012 requires state drinking water programs to incorporate asset management into capacity development strategies using a five-core-question framework:⁷

1. **What is the current state of the utility's assets?** (Comprehensive inventory and condition assessment)
2. **What is the utility's required sustainable level-of-service?** (Customer expectations and regulatory requirements)
3. **Which assets are critical to sustained performance?** (Risk-based prioritization for replacement)
4. **What are the utility's best minimum life-cycle cost capital improvement plan and operating strategies?** (Long-term capital planning with cost-benefit analysis)
5. **What is the best long-term funding strategy?** (Rate case planning, federal/state grants, financing mechanisms)

While EPA asset management guidance is voluntary and not a mandatory Safe Drinking Water Act requirement, state public utility commissions increasingly reference EPA best practices when evaluating whether utility infrastructure management decisions were "prudent" under state rate case standards.⁸ Failure to implement basic asset management principles—as evidenced by deteriorating performance metrics and accumulating deferred maintenance backlogs—may support regulatory findings of imprudent management.

#### 4. Industry Performance Benchmarks

The American Water Works Association (AWWA) establishes industry performance standards widely recognized by utility regulators. The AWWA Partnership for Safe Water program sets optimization goals for distribution system infrastructure, including a maximum of 15 main breaks per 100 miles of distribution pipeline annually.⁹ Industry benchmarking data from Utah State University's 2023 comprehensive study establishes a national average main break rate of 11.1 breaks per 100 miles across all pipe materials, with cast iron infrastructure averaging 28.6 breaks per 100 miles due to age and material degradation.¹⁰

Water utilities typically invest 4-5% of rate base annually for infrastructure replacement and system improvements, with capital expenditure-to-depreciation ratios (CapEx/D&A) averaging 3.02x to 3.12x over five-year periods.¹¹ Approximately 70% of utility capital investment is typically earmarked for replacement capex (versus growth or expansion projects), reflecting the ongoing need to maintain aging infrastructure systems.¹² These industry benchmarks provide objective standards against which regulators evaluate whether a utility's infrastructure investment patterns constitute prudent management.

#### 5. Change of Control Provisions in Commercial Contracts

Water utility acquisitions implicate three categories of commercial contracts: wholesale water supply agreements, purchased water contracts, and supplier service agreements. Each category presents distinct legal considerations:

**Wholesale Water Agreements:** Utilities with wholesale customers (municipalities, water districts, or large industrial users) typically operate under long-term supply agreements containing change of control provisions. The legal distinction between "direct" and "direct or indirect" change of control language is critical. Courts have held that "direct ownership interests" language refers to the contracting entity itself, not parent-company-level ownership changes, as established in energy utility precedent from *Laredo Ridge Wind v. Nebraska Public Power District*.¹³ If MSWC's wholesale agreements use "direct" language without "indirect" modifiers, AWI's stock acquisition may not trigger consent requirements as a matter of contract interpretation.

**Assignment versus Change of Control:** UCC § 9-406 generally overrides anti-assignment provisions for payment rights, but utility service contracts involve ongoing performance obligations (water delivery, pressure maintenance, quality standards) that fall outside the UCC safe harbor, making anti-assignment clauses generally enforceable.¹⁴ Stock purchases are not "assignments" because the same legal entity remains party to the contract; however, many utility contracts include "deemed assignment" clauses treating change of control as an assignment event requiring consent.

**Force Majeure Provisions:** Purchased water contracts typically contain force majeure clauses addressing drought, curtailment orders, and facility failures. Standard language provides: "Water made available to customers is subject to reduction by reason of water shortage, drought, emergencies, or by malfunctioning or rehabilitation of facilities."¹⁵ Minimum annual purchase quantity (MAQ) obligations are typically waived during shortage periods if available water is less than contractually specified minimums, with pro rata curtailment allocation being the most common method for municipal and investor-owned utility systems.¹⁶

#### B.2 Material Contract Review — Wholesale Agreements and Supplier Consents (LOW SEVERITY)

**Conclusion:** MSWC's commercial contracts present **LOW** severity risk, with estimated aggregate exposure of $3.8M-$8.2M affecting four contract categories requiring change of control consents or presenting termination risk. The primary exposure consists of wholesale water agreement consent requirements (affecting 2,800 customer connections and $7M annual revenue) with 70-80% probability that consent provisions exist, but only 15-20% probability of customer terminations or consent refusals. **Exposure (Annual):** $1.05M-$2.1M worst-case revenue loss if 15-30% of wholesale customers terminate; **Exposure (One-Time):** $2.75M-$6.1M for supplier contract renegotiation, letter of credit requirements, and purchased water cost increases. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Typical water utility contract terms from industry practice; actual MSWC contracts not reviewed, requiring data room verification].

**Rule:** Commercial contracts commonly contain anti-assignment provisions restricting transfer of contract rights and obligations without counterparty consent. The Uniform Commercial Code § 9-406 overrides anti-assignment clauses for payment rights (accounts receivable), but performance contracts requiring ongoing service delivery remain subject to contractual assignment restrictions.³⁰ Change of control provisions in utility contracts typically distinguish between "direct" ownership changes (affecting the contracting entity itself) and "direct or indirect" changes (affecting both entity and parent-level ownership).³¹ Stock acquisitions do not constitute "assignments" because the contracting entity's legal identity continues unchanged, but "deemed assignment" clauses may treat ownership changes as assignment events requiring consent.³²

**Explanation:** Energy utility precedent provides interpretive guidance for change of control provisions in infrastructure service contracts. In *Laredo Ridge Wind v. Nebraska Public Power District*, a federal court considered power purchase agreements requiring written consent for transfer of a majority of "direct ownership interests."³³ The court held that this language referred to ownership of the contracting entity itself, not parent-company-level ownership transfers, reasoning that parties intending to restrict parent-level transactions would use broader "direct or indirect" formulations. This narrow construction favors acquirers by limiting consent requirements to entity-level changes.

However, wholesale water supply agreements often contain broader change of control definitions due to the critical infrastructure nature of water service. Regulatory precedent shows that municipalities and water districts serving as wholesale customers have legitimate concerns about acquirer creditworthiness, operational capability, and long-term service reliability—particularly when acquisitions involve private equity backing or foreign investor participation.³⁴ Courts have upheld change of control consent requirements as reasonable contractual provisions protecting customer interests in continued service quality and financial stability.³⁵

The key distinction is between ministerial consent provisions (requiring notice and consent unless objection is unreasonable) versus substantive approval rights (permitting consent denial for legitimate business reasons). Ministerial consent clauses typically result in consent grants within 30-60 days absent specific creditworthiness or capability concerns. Substantive approval rights permit broader evaluation of whether the transaction serves the customer's interests, potentially creating negotiation dynamics around pricing, term extensions, or service level modifications.

**Application:** MSWC operates 2,800 master-metered connections serving small municipalities and water districts, generating $7M annual wholesale revenue.³⁶ While actual wholesale agreements are not available in public filings (requiring data room review), industry practice suggests 70-80% likelihood that these agreements contain change of control provisions, divided approximately as follows:

| Provision Type | Estimated % of Contracts | Consent Required | Risk Level |
|----------------|-------------------------|------------------|------------|
| "Direct" change of control only | 30-40% | No (per *Laredo Ridge Wind*) | LOW |
| "Direct or indirect" change of control | 40-50% | Yes | MEDIUM |
| No change of control provision | 10-20% | No | NONE |
| Notice-only requirement | 10-15% | No (notice sufficient) | LOW |

For contracts requiring consent (estimated 40-50% of wholesale agreements, representing $2.8M-$3.5M annual revenue), the probability of consent denial or customer termination is relatively low (15-20%) because:

3. **Regulatory Oversight:** Colorado PUC approval process provides wholesale customers with statutory intervention rights (C.R.S. § 40-5-101 change of control proceedings), enabling customers to impose conditions protecting their interests without contract termination.³⁷
