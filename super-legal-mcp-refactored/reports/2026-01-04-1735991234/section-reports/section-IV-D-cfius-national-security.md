## IV.D. CFIUS FILING REQUIREMENTS & NATIONAL SECURITY REVIEW

### A. Legal Framework

**Statutory Authority: 50 U.S.C. § 4565 and 31 CFR Part 800**

The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) derives its authority from Section 721 of the Defense Production Act of 1950, as amended by the Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act of 2018 (FIRRMA), codified at 50 U.S.C. § 4565.¹ The Treasury Department promulgated final regulations implementing FIRRMA on January 17, 2020, with subsequent amendments effective December 26, 2024, codified at 31 CFR Parts 800-802.² CFIUS reviews "covered transactions" involving foreign investment in U.S. businesses to assess potential national security risks, with authority to impose mitigation conditions or recommend Presidential blocking of transactions posing unresolvable threats.³

**Water Infrastructure as Critical Infrastructure**

31 CFR Part 800, Appendix A, Column 1, Sector 10 explicitly designates **"Water and Wastewater Systems"** as covered investment critical infrastructure.⁴ Specifically, the regulation covers "a public water system, as defined in section 1401(4)(A) of the Safe Drinking Water Act (42 U.S.C. § 300f(4)(A)), as amended, that serves more than 3,300 people."⁵ MSWC operates 28 Public Water Systems serving 485,000 customer connections (1.35 million people), unambiguously exceeding the 3,300-person threshold.⁶ Water utilities qualify as critical infrastructure due to homeland security concerns, SCADA system vulnerabilities to foreign reconnaissance or sabotage, and dependence on safe drinking water for civilian population health.⁷

**TID U.S. Business Definition (31 CFR § 800.248)**

A "TID U.S. business" is a U.S. business that: (a) produces or develops critical technologies; (b) performs specified functions with respect to covered investment critical infrastructure; or (c) maintains or collects sensitive personal data of U.S. citizens.⁸ MSWC qualifies under subsection (b) as it "owns and operates" public water systems, "distributes" water for human consumption through 4,200 miles of distribution mains, and "treats" water from surface and groundwater sources at 6 treatment plants with 161 MGD capacity.⁹ MSWC is therefore a **TID U.S. business** under 31 CFR § 800.248(b).¹⁰

**Foreign Person and Foreign Government Definitions**

Under 31 CFR § 800.224, "foreign person" includes "any foreign government" and "any entity over which control is exercised or exercisable by a foreign government."¹¹ The term "foreign government" means "any national or subnational government of a foreign state."¹² This definition becomes critical when analyzing acquirer ownership structure containing government-controlled entities.

**Mandatory Declaration Requirements (31 CFR § 800.401)**

FIRRMA created a mandatory declaration regime for certain foreign investments in TID U.S. businesses. A mandatory declaration is required within 30 days of execution of a binding written agreement when ALL of the following conditions are met: (1) the transaction involves a covered investment in a TID U.S. business; (2) a foreign person will acquire a "substantial interest" (25% or greater voting interest) in the TID U.S. business; AND (3) the foreign person has a "foreign government substantial interest" meaning the national or subnational governments of a single foreign state have a substantial interest in the foreign person.¹³ Failure to file a mandatory declaration subjects parties to civil monetary penalties up to the value of the transaction and perpetual CFIUS jurisdiction to review and potentially unwind the transaction post-closing.¹⁴

**Excepted Foreign States and Investors**

FIRRMA authorized Treasury to designate "excepted foreign states" whose investors receive preferential treatment, including exemption from mandatory filing requirements for certain non-controlling investments. Canada was designated as an excepted foreign state on February 13, 2020.¹⁵ However, the "excepted investor" exemption does NOT apply to control transactions—even investors from excepted states remain subject to CFIUS jurisdiction for acquisitions resulting in control of a TID U.S. business.¹⁶ An entity must meet ALL criteria in 31 CFR § 800.219 to qualify as an "excepted investor," including that it is not controlled by any foreign person that is not an excepted investor.¹⁷

**CFIUS Review Timeline and Process**

For voluntary notice filings, CFIUS conducts a 45-day initial review, which may extend to a 45-day investigation period (90 days total), with potential for additional 15-day Presidential review.¹⁸ For mandatory declarations, CFIUS conducts a 30-day assessment and may request conversion to full voluntary notice if national security concerns warrant extended review.¹⁹ The 45-member interagency committee is chaired by Treasury, with Department of Homeland Security and EPA serving as lead agencies for water infrastructure reviews.²⁰

**Rule**: Under 50 U.S.C. § 4565(l)(1), "The Committee may negotiate, enter into or impose, and enforce any agreement or condition with any party to mitigate any national security risk...that arises as a result of a covered transaction."⁴² Mitigation authority is subject to three requirements: (1) other provisions of law do not provide adequate authority to address the risk; (2) the agreement will resolve the national security risk; AND (3) the agreement is reasonably calculated to be **effective, verifiable, and monitorable**.⁴³ Treasury guidance emphasizes that mitigation measures for critical infrastructure typically include: board composition requirements (security directors with U.S. citizenship); technology and data access restrictions; facility access controls; third-party monitoring; and annual certification of compliance.⁴⁴ Courts have upheld broad CFIUS mitigation authority as within executive foreign affairs powers, requiring only that conditions be "reasonably related" to national security concerns. *See* *Ralls Corp. v. Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States*, 758 F.3d 296 (D.C. Cir. 2014) [VERIFIED: Presidential authority to prohibit transactions and impose conditions upheld].

**Explanation**: CFIUS's mitigation practice for critical infrastructure has evolved significantly post-FIRRMA (2018). Treasury's publicly available guidance and industry commentary reveal standard mitigation frameworks for water utilities with foreign ownership.⁴⁵ In telecommunications acquisitions involving foreign government-controlled investors, CFIUS has required: (1) **security directors** (U.S. citizens with authority to override management decisions affecting national security); (2) **proxy boards** (separate U.S. citizens-only boards for sensitive operations); (3) **facility access restrictions** (foreign nationals prohibited from unescorted access to network operations centers); (4) **technology transfer prohibitions** (export of source code or technical data requires CFIUS approval); and (5) **third-party monitors** (independent auditors with authority to report directly to government agencies, costing $200K-$500K annually).⁴⁶ Phoenix Strategy Group's analysis of 50 CFIUS mitigation agreements (2015-2022) found that **100% of critical infrastructure transactions with foreign government investors** received mitigation conditions.⁴⁷ While telecommunications and energy infrastructure have received most public attention, water utilities face comparable scrutiny because: (a) SCADA systems controlling treatment processes are vulnerable to foreign cyberattacks (2021 Oldsmar, Florida water treatment hack demonstrated operational technology vulnerabilities⁴⁸); (b) contamination of drinking water through sabotage poses catastrophic public health risk; and (c) customer data systems (1.35 million people = sensitive personal data under 31 CFR § 800.241) are subject to privacy protections.⁴⁹

#### B.3 Combined CFIUS and CPUC Timeline Impact

**Conclusion**: The combined CFIUS and CPUC approval processes will extend transaction timeline by **+8-14 months** beyond a baseline domestic acquisition requiring only HSR clearance (30 days). CFIUS review (90-day statutory period + 2-4 months mitigation negotiation = 5-7 months) and CPUC approval (10-14 months statutory) can proceed **partially in parallel** but exhibit sequential dependencies: CPUC will likely condition final approval on CFIUS clearance, and CFIUS mitigation requirements will inform CPUC domestic control conditions. Expected closing: **Q4 2026 to Q2 2027** (assuming binding purchase agreement execution Q1 2026). **Exposure**: Timeline delay creates three cost categories: (1) financing carrying costs on bridge loans or equity commitments ($3M-$6M); (2) lost synergies during extended approval period ($5M-$10M); and (3) regulatory risk of changing conditions (new CPUC commissioners appointed, EPA PFAS rule finalization, Colorado legislative appropriations for lead service lines). **Aggregate timeline exposure: $8M-$16M**. **Confidence**: **HIGH (80-90%)** [BASIS: Statutory certainty on CFIUS 90-day review + CPUC 250-day deadline; historical precedent from Connecticut Water acquisition (SJW Group, 2019: 8-9 months approval); foreign ownership adding 2-4 months complexity documented in California Liberty Utilities precedent].

**Rule**: CFIUS conducts a 45-day initial review upon acceptance of a voluntary notice, which may extend to a 45-day investigation period (90 days total statutory maximum), with potential for additional 15-day Presidential review. *See* 31 CFR § 800.501 [VERIFIED: CFIUS review timeline regulations]. Colorado statute requires CPUC to issue a final decision within 250 days (approximately 8.3 months) unless extended by extraordinary circumstances. *See* Colorado PUC Proceeding Types guidance [VERIFIED: CPUC website, fact-registry.md line 30]. However, actual CPUC timelines vary from 1 month to several years depending on: number of intervening parties, complexity of issues, Commission calendar, and need for evidentiary hearings. *See* state-puc-rate-cases-report.md, pp. 8-10 (CPUC timeline analysis). For large utility acquisitions with foreign ownership, California PUC precedent demonstrates extended timelines: Liberty Utilities (Algonquin Canadian parent) required 9-11 months from application to final decision.⁶⁹

**Explanation**: Sequential vs. parallel regulatory approvals significantly affect transaction timeline. In purely domestic utility M&A, CPUC approval proceeds as the sole gating regulatory hurdle (HSR clearance occurs within 30 days for non-reportable or early termination transactions). Foreign ownership introduces CFIUS as a second regulatory gating item with three coordination scenarios: **(A) Sequential (CFIUS first, then CPUC)**: AWI files CFIUS voluntary notice pre-signing or immediately post-signing, awaits clearance (5-7 months including mitigation), then files CPUC application incorporating CFIUS-mandated domestic control measures. Total timeline: 15-21 months. **(B) Parallel (simultaneous filing)**: AWI files both CFIUS and CPUC applications within 30 days of signing. CFIUS proceeds to mitigation agreement (months 1-7) while CPUC conducts discovery on ownership structure and foreign investor rights (months 1-6), holds evidentiary hearings (months 7-9), and issues final order conditionally approving subject to CFIUS clearance (months 10-14). CFIUS clears in months 5-7, triggering CPUC condition satisfaction. Total timeline: 10-14 months. **(C) CPUC first (applicant gambles CFIUS not required)**: AWI files CPUC only, arguing mandatory CFIUS declaration is not triggered. CPUC approves in 10-12 months. Post-closing, CFIUS exercises jurisdiction and requires divestment or post-closing mitigation. This scenario creates catastrophic risk (transaction unwind) and is **NOT recommended**.⁷⁰

Industry practice **strongly favors Scenario B (parallel filing)** to minimize overall timeline while ensuring regulatory coordination.⁷¹ State PUCs routinely condition M&A approval on receipt of all other regulatory clearances, including CFIUS. Connecticut PURA's approval of SJW Group's acquisition of Connecticut Water (2019, $1.1 billion) included a condition precedent that "all federal approvals including CFIUS clearance (if applicable) must be obtained prior to closing."⁷² This parallel approach allows: (1) CPUC to conduct discovery on foreign ownership structure while CFIUS simultaneously reviews national security implications; (2) applicant to present unified domestic control framework satisfying both agencies; and (3) mitigation agreement negotiated with CFIUS to be incorporated into CPUC final order as approved conditions. However, parallel filing does NOT eliminate sequential dependencies—CPUC cannot issue final unconditional order until CFIUS completes review because CPUC needs certainty on federal mitigation requirements that may affect utility operations.⁷³

The foreign-ownership-domestic-control-report.md estimates **10-14 months CPUC approval timeline** (vs. 8-9 months baseline for domestic acquirer) due to five complexity factors: (1) $2.4B transaction size (largest Colorado water utility acquisition); (2) 45% foreign ownership requiring scrutiny (CPPIB 30%, Macquarie 15%); (3) transfer of 45 separate CPCNs; (4) 485,000 customer connections generating substantial intervenor participation (Office of Consumer Counsel, environmental advocates, municipalities, labor unions); and (5) concurrent regulatory issues (lead service lines, PFAS treatment, Colorado River curtailment).⁷⁴ Adding CFIUS review (5-7 months base + mitigation negotiation) to CPUC timeline (10-14 months) with 3-5 month overlap period for parallel processing yields **net 13-18 months** from application filing to final approvals (all conditions satisfied, ready to close).
