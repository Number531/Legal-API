### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Colorado Public Utilities Commission Jurisdiction Over Water Utilities

The Colorado Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) exercises comprehensive regulatory authority over investor-owned water utilities operating within Colorado under C.R.S. Title 40 (Public Utilities). This jurisdiction encompasses rate regulation, service quality standards, certificate authority, and change-of-control approvals for utilities serving Colorado customers.

**Statutory Foundation:** C.R.S. § 40-5-101 et seq. establishes the CPUC's authority to regulate "public utilities," defined to include "every common carrier, pipeline corporation, gas corporation, electrical corporation, telephone corporation, telegraph corporation, and water corporation" operating within Colorado.¹ Mountain States Water Company, as a Delaware corporation operating 28 Public Water Systems serving 485,000 customer connections (1.35 million people) across 45 Colorado municipalities, unequivocally falls within this statutory definition.²

**Certificate of Public Convenience and Necessity (CPCN) Requirements:** Under C.R.S. § 40-5-101, no public utility may begin construction or operation without first obtaining a certificate of public convenience and necessity from the CPUC finding that "the present or future public convenience and necessity require or will require such construction."³ MSWC currently holds 45 separate CPCNs authorizing water service across its Colorado Front Range service territory (Denver metro, Colorado Springs, Fort Collins, Boulder).⁴

**Change-of-Control Approval Requirements:** Colorado law mandates CPUC pre-approval for any transaction that would result in: (1) transfer of a certificate of public convenience and necessity, (2) transfer or obtaining a controlling interest in any utility (whether through asset transfer, stock transfer, merger, or other business combination), or (3) transfer of utility assets outside the normal course of business.⁵ This approval requirement is codified in 4 CCR 723-3-3104, which requires utilities seeking change-of-control approval to file a formal application demonstrating that the proposed transaction serves the public interest.⁶

**Public Interest Standard:** The CPUC's regulatory objectives under C.R.S. § 40-3-102 require maintaining "just and reasonable" rates for residential and business consumers consistent with: (1) minimum standards of service, (2) safety requirements, (3) economic viability of the utility, and (4) environmental protection.⁷ In evaluating change-of-control applications, the CPUC applies a "not detrimental" standard, assessing whether the transaction would adversely affect ratepayers, service quality, or the utility's financial capacity to provide safe and adequate service.⁸

#### 2. Statutory Approval Timeline and Procedural Requirements

**250-Day Statutory Deadline:** Colorado statute requires the CPUC to issue a final decision within 250 days (approximately 8-8.5 months) from the date an application is deemed complete, unless extraordinary circumstances warrant extension.⁹ This timeline includes: (1) public notice and intervention period (30-45 days), (2) discovery phase (60-90 days), (3) evidentiary hearings (30-60 days), and (4) deliberation and final order issuance (30-45 days).¹⁰

**Procedural Steps:**
- **Application Filing:** Utility files comprehensive application including: transaction structure, financial pro formas, public interest showing, proposed rate commitments, and environmental/operational impact analysis
- **Public Notice:** CPUC distributes notice to all customers, municipalities, and interested parties
- **Intervention Period:** 30-day window for intervention motions from entities with "justified financial or tangible interest"¹¹
- **Discovery:** Parties exchange information requests and testimony
- **Evidentiary Hearing:** Written and oral testimony before administrative law judge or full Commission
- **Final Order:** Commission issues decision with findings of fact, conclusions of law, and conditions of approval (if granted)

**Extension Factors:** Proceedings extending beyond the 250-day statutory deadline typically involve: (1) significant opposition from intervenors (Office of Consumer Counsel, municipal governments, environmental groups), (2) complex financial structures requiring extensive discovery, (3) novel regulatory issues, or (4) coordination with other regulatory approvals (e.g., CFIUS review for foreign ownership).¹²

#### 4. Colorado-Specific Rate Case Standards: Prudent Investment Rule

Colorado applies the "prudent investment test" to determine whether capital expenditures qualify for inclusion in rate base and recovery through customer rates. Under this test, the CPUC examines whether costs were: (1) reasonable at the time incurred, and (2) made given circumstances known or knowable at the time of investment.²⁰

**Disallowance Authority:** When a utility fails to demonstrate prudent decision-making in CPUC hearings, the facility or capital investment being reviewed is disallowed from rate base, meaning the utility cannot recover those costs through customer rates and must absorb the expense.²¹ Additional grounds for disallowance include: (1) excess capacity not justified by forecasted demand, and (2) costs exceeding CPUC-established caps or prudency thresholds.²²

**"Used and Useful" Standard:** Capital investments must be "used and useful" in providing utility service to qualify for rate recovery. This standard creates significant risk for customer-side infrastructure investments (such as customer-owned lead service line replacement) where the utility does not own the asset but seeks to recover costs through rates.²³

Statutory 250-day (8.3-month) deadline will likely be extended by 60-180 days due to: (1) $2.4B transaction size (largest Colorado water utility M&A), (2) 45% foreign ownership complexity requiring CFIUS coordination, (3) transfer of 45 separate CPCNs, (4) 485,000 customer base generating significant public interest and intervenor participation, and (5) concurrent regulatory issues (lead service lines, PFAS treatment, Colorado River curtailment). **Probability:** 70% chance approval occurs within 10-14 months; 25% chance extension to 15-18 months; 5% chance approval denied or indefinitely delayed. **Exposure:** Each quarter of delay beyond Q4 2026 costs **$8M-$12M** in financing carrying costs and lost operational synergies. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Colorado statutory 250-day baseline; Connecticut Water 8-month precedent; California Liberty Utilities 10-month timeline with foreign ownership].

**Rule:** C.R.S. § 40-6-109.5 requires the CPUC to issue a final decision within 250 days from the date an application is deemed complete, unless the Commission determines that extraordinary circumstances necessitate extension.⁶² This timeline is consistent with procedural due process requirements under Colorado Administrative Procedure Act (APA), C.R.S. § 24-4-101 et seq., which mandates reasonable opportunity for parties to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses.⁶³

**Explanation:** Utility change-of-control proceedings vary significantly in duration based on complexity factors. Simple transactions involving small customer bases (<10,000 connections), domestic acquirers, and no material rate impacts can be completed within 6-9 months.⁶⁴ Conversely, complex transactions involving foreign ownership, significant capital programs, or substantial intervenor opposition routinely extend to 12-18 months.⁶⁵

Connecticut Water's 8-month approval timeline (January 2019 filing → September 2019 approval) provides a useful baseline, but that transaction involved: (1) a 100% domestic acquirer (SJW Group), (2) smaller customer base (136,000 vs. MSWC's 485,000), and (3) no concurrent federal regulatory review (no CFIUS).⁶⁶ California's Liberty Utilities approval required approximately 10 months despite involving only a small electric utility (CalPeco), reflecting the additional time burden imposed by foreign ownership due diligence.⁶⁷
