## IV.G. Reinsurance Agreements

### A. Legal Framework

The acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) implicates material reinsurance agreements totaling $515 million in ceded reserves across three traditional reinsurers: Munich Re North America ($240 million yearly renewable term), Swiss Re Life & Health America ($180 million coinsurance), and Reinsurance Group of America ($95 million modified coinsurance). These treaties present consent and recapture risks that, if realized, could materially impair LLIC's Risk-Based Capital (RBC) position and require costly replacement coverage in constrained markets.

**Nebraska Credit for Reinsurance Framework**

Nebraska law governs the statutory accounting treatment of reinsurance ceded by LLIC, a Nebraska-domiciled insurer. Under **Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-416.06(2)**, a ceding insurer may take full reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to an assuming insurer "licensed to transact insurance or reinsurance in this state."¹ All three traditional reinsurers—Munich Re North America (Delaware-domiciled), Swiss Re Life & Health America (Connecticut-domiciled), and RGA Reinsurance Company (Missouri-domiciled)—operate as **admitted/licensed** reinsurers in Nebraska, qualifying LLIC for full statutory reserve credit without collateral requirements.²

This contrasts sharply with the Vermont captive reinsurer Liberty Re Ltd., which operates as an **unauthorized** alien reinsurer requiring $850 million in letters of credit from Barclays to secure Nebraska reserve credit under § 44-416.06(4).³ The traditional reinsurers' admitted status provides maximum regulatory certainty: reserve credit is automatic, subject only to the reinsurers' financial solvency and treaty compliance.⁴

**NAIC Model Law Compliance**

Nebraska has adopted the National Association of Insurance Commissioners' **Credit for Reinsurance Model Law (#785)** and **Model Regulation (#786)**, as revised in 2019 and implemented in 56 U.S. jurisdictions.⁵ The Model Law establishes a tiered framework for reserve credit:

1. **Licensed/Accredited Reinsurers (Tier 1):** Full credit without collateral for reinsurers licensed in the ceding insurer's state or accredited by the domiciliary regulator (Munich Re, Swiss Re, and RGA qualify).⁶
2. **Certified Reinsurers (Tier 2):** Graduated collateral requirements (0%-100%) based on credit ratings for unauthorized alien reinsurers.⁷
3. **Reciprocal Jurisdictions (Tier 3):** Reduced collateral for reinsurers domiciled in qualified non-U.S. jurisdictions (EU, UK, Switzerland, Japan, Bermuda as of 2024).⁸

All three traditional reinsurers bypass Tiers 2 and 3 entirely due to their U.S. admitted status, eliminating collateral posting, trust account funding, and "cut-through" endorsement requirements that burden unauthorized reinsurers.⁹

**Change of Control Consent Requirements**

Reinsurance treaties in the U.S. life insurance industry universally contain **change of control provisions** requiring the reinsurer's consent before the ceding insurer may assign the treaty to a successor entity or undergo ownership change exceeding specified thresholds (typically 50%-80% voting control transfer).¹⁰ These provisions serve three reinsurer interests:

1. **Counterparty Credit Risk:** Reinsurers assess the ceding insurer's financial strength, underwriting discipline, claims administration quality, and regulatory compliance when pricing treaties. A change of control introduces uncertainty about whether the acquirer will maintain these standards.¹¹
2. **Relationship Continuity:** Life reinsurance often involves decades-long relationships with embedded institutional knowledge (underwriting guidelines, claims protocols, actuarial assumptions). Acquirer-driven operational changes may disrupt this continuity.¹²
3. **Regulatory Stability:** Reinsurers monitor the ceding insurer's regulatory standing (RBC ratios, market conduct examinations, product approvals). Acquirers with weak regulatory track records or aggressive growth strategies may increase risk.¹³

**Recapture Rights**

If a reinsurer withholds consent to a change of control, the treaty typically grants the reinsurer a **recapture right**—the ability to terminate the treaty and return ceded reserves, liabilities, and related assets to the ceding insurer within 60-180 days.¹⁴ Recapture triggers three adverse consequences for the ceding insurer:

1. **Reserve Reassumption:** The ceding insurer's statutory liabilities increase by the amount of recaptured reserves (Swiss Re $180M + RGA $95M = $275M; Munich Re's YRT treaty holds no reserves).¹⁵
2. **RBC Capital Strain:** Reassuming mortality, lapse, and investment risks increases the RBC C2 (insurance risk) component, requiring higher Authorized Control Level (ACL) capital and reducing the RBC ratio.¹⁶
3. **Replacement Cost:** Alternative reinsurance in the market typically costs 5-15% more than incumbent treaties due to anti-selection risk, transaction complexity, and post-COVID mortality deterioration.¹⁷

**Standard of Review for Consent Decisions**

Reinsurance treaties vary in the contractual standard governing consent decisions:

- **"Not Unreasonably Withheld" (Moderate Standard):** Yearly renewable term (YRT) treaties often include this language, imposing an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.¹⁸ Courts applying this standard examine whether the reinsurer's concerns are objectively reasonable (e.g., acquirer's financial instability, regulatory violations) or pretextual (e.g., seeking to renegotiate pricing).¹⁹
- **"Sole and Absolute Discretion" (Strict Standard):** Coinsurance treaties frequently grant reinsurers unfettered discretion to approve or deny consent.²⁰ This language eliminates the reasonableness inquiry, allowing reinsurers to condition consent on enhanced economic terms (reduced experience refunds, dividend covenants, reporting requirements) or exercise recapture for any reason.²¹

The distinction is material: acquirers facing "absolute discretion" clauses have weaker negotiating leverage and higher recapture risk, often requiring upfront concessions (RBC covenants, investment restrictions, management retention commitments) to secure consent.²²
