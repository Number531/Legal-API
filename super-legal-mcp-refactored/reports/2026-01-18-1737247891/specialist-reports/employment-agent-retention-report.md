#### 2. Nebraska/California Non-Compete Void

**Legal Framework by Key State:**

**California (Largest Market)**:
- Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code ¬ß 16600: "every contract by which anyone is restrained from engaging in a lawful profession, trade, or business of any kind is to that extent void"
- **SB-699** (effective Jan 1, 2024): Expands California's non-compete ban to **contracts signed outside California** governing California residents
- **Creates private right of action**: Employees can sue employers for attempting to enforce void non-competes
- **Impact**: LLIC cannot enforce non-competes against California-based agents, even if agent agreement signed in Nebraska

**Nebraska (Domicile State)**:
- Nebraska case law: Non-competes "generally unenforceable" in employment context, viewed as impermissible restraint on trade
- **Exception**: Nebraska courts **will uphold non-solicitation agreements** if narrowly drafted
- **Non-solicit enforceability requirement**: Restricts only solicitation of clients/accounts with whom agent "actually did business and had personal contact"
- **No blue-penciling**: If non-compete unreasonable, entire covenant unenforceable (court cannot modify to make reasonable)

**Texas (Major Market)**:
- Tex. Bus. & Com. Code ¬ß 15.50: Non-competes enforceable if (1) ancillary to enforceable agreement, (2) supported by consideration, (3) reasonable in time/geography/scope
- Texas courts **routinely enforce** non-competes meeting reasonableness test
- Typical reasonable terms: 1-2 year duration, geographic limit to agent's territory, scope limited to life insurance sales

**New York (Major Market)**:
- Current law: Case-by-case reasonableness analysis
- **Pending legislation**: Senate Bill would ban non-competes except for individuals earning $500K+ annually (currently in committee)
- Non-solicitation agreements generally enforceable if reasonable

**Practical Enforcement Strategy:**

| Agent Tier | California Agents | Nebraska Agents | Texas/Florida Agents | Recommended Approach |
|------------|------------------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Top 50 Producers** | Do not enforce non-compete; monitor for trade secret misappropriation | Enforce non-solicits only; seek injunction if customer solicitation | Full enforcement of non-compete and non-solicit if 1-2 year duration | Selective enforcement ($50K-$150K legal cost per injunction) |
| **Mid-Level Agents** | Do not enforce | Cease-and-desist letter only | Cease-and-desist; pursue injunction if systematic customer theft | Limited enforcement |
| **Lower-Volume Agents** | Do not enforce | Do not enforce | Do not enforce | No enforcement (not cost-effective) |

**Trade Secret Alternative:**
Rather than non-competes, LLIC should protect confidential information (customer lists, pricing, underwriting guidelines) under **Uniform Trade Secrets Act** (adopted by most states):
- Classify customer lists as trade secrets
- Implement access controls (password protection, need-to-know)
- Upon agent departure, demand return of all confidential information
- Monitor for use of LLIC customer lists at new employer
- Advantages: No reasonableness test; protects against anyone (not just former employees); damages include actual losses + unjust enrichment

#### 1. Change of Control Severance Agreements: Single vs. Double Trigger

Change of control agreements outline pay and benefits an executive will receive in the event of a change in company ownership, commonly known as "golden parachutes."¬π These agreements typically utilize either single-trigger or double-trigger mechanisms:

- **Single Trigger**: Payment triggered solely by change of control; executive may voluntarily resign and demand payment¬≤
- **Double Trigger** (more common): Payment requires both (1) change of control AND (2) qualifying termination (without cause or with good reason)¬≥

**Market Practice (2023 Study):**

Over 99% of companies providing change-in-control cash severance condition payment upon a "qualifying termination of employment" within a specified protection period following a change in control (double trigger).‚Å¥ Key statistics:

- 100% define qualifying termination as termination without "cause" or for "good reason"
- 71% define protection period as 24 months following change in control
- Most companies prefer double-trigger structures to avoid large payouts triggered solely by ownership change‚Åµ

#### 2. IRC ¬ß 280G Golden Parachute Payment Analysis

**Statutory Framework:**

IRC ¬ß 280G was enacted in the 1980s to curb abusive executive compensation practices when executives negotiated "golden parachute" clauses so lucrative that acquisitions would be crippled by amounts owed to outgoing leadership.‚Å∂ 26 U.S.C. ¬ß 280G, https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/280G.

**Key Definitions:**

**Parachute Payment:** Any payment in the nature of compensation to a "disqualified individual" if the aggregate present value of payments contingent on change of control equals or exceeds **3 times the base amount**.‚Å∑

**Excess Parachute Payment:** The amount exceeding the base amount allocated to the parachute payment.‚Å∏

**Base Amount:** Average annual compensation includible in the individual's gross income for the 5 taxable years ending before the year of the change of control.‚Åπ

**Disqualified Individual:** Officers, shareholders owning >1%, or highly compensated individuals (top 1% by compensation, maximum 250 employees) during the 12 months preceding closing.¬π‚Å∞

**Tax Consequences:**

1. **Employee Impact**: 20% excise tax on all excess parachute payments (in addition to ordinary income taxes)¬π¬π
2. **Employer Impact**: Corporation cannot take tax deduction for excess parachute payments¬π¬≤

**Types of Parachute Payments (Inclusive List):**

Parachute payments take many forms including bonuses, retention bonuses, accelerated vesting of stock options, restricted stock, other equity compensation, fringe benefits, severance payments, **life insurance**, and post-change-in-control arrangements.¬π¬≥

**Example Calculation:**

"Nebraska law is **not kind to noncompetes** and they are generally **unenforceable** in the employee context."‚Å∂‚Åµ

"Nebraska courts, with very limited exception, will **not enforce non-competition agreements**, viewing them as an impermissible restraint on trade."‚Å∂‚Å∂

**However**: Nebraska courts **will uphold employee nonsolicitation agreements** if properly and narrowly drafted.‚Å∂‚Å∑

**Nebraska Non-Solicit Requirements:**
Enforceable only if restricts former employee from working for or soliciting former employer's clients or accounts with whom former employee **"actually did business and had personal contact"** while employed.‚Å∂‚Å∏

**Nebraska Non-Compete (If Enforceable):**
Supreme Court of Nebraska established three rules for enforceability:‚Å∂‚Åπ
1. Reasonably necessary to protect employer's legitimate business interest
2. Reasonable in time, territory, and scope
3. Not unduly burdensome on employee or injurious to public

**Critical**: Nebraska does **not permit blue penciling** (modifying unreasonable provisions). If unreasonable, entire covenant unenforceable.‚Å∑‚Å∞

**NEW YORK ‚Äî EVOLVING (Major Market)**

Governor Hochul **vetoed** proposed law banning non-competes in 2023, but indicated support for bill allowing non-competes for high earners.‚Å∑¬π

New York Senate Bill (currently in committee) would ban non-competes except for individuals earning average $500,000+ per year.‚Å∑¬≤

**Current Law**: New York courts enforce non-competes on case-by-case basis evaluating reasonableness in scope, duration, geographic area.

**TEXAS ‚Äî ENFORCEABLE (Major Market)**

Texas law makes non-competes enforceable if:‚Å∑¬≥
1. Ancillary to otherwise enforceable agreement
2. Supported by valid consideration
3. **Reasonable** in time, geographic scope, and activities restrained

Texas courts **routinely enforce noncompetes** meeting these criteria.‚Å∑‚Å¥ Tex. Bus. & Com. Code ¬ß 15.50.

**Reasonableness Standards:**
- Duration: 1-2 years typical; 3+ years may be unreasonable
- Geography: Tied to territory where employee worked or employer does business
- Scope: Limited to type of work employee performed

**Tier 1 ‚Äî Top 50 Producers:**

**Tier 3 ‚Äî Lower-Volume Agents:**

#### 5. Trade Secret Protection (Alternative to Non-Competes)

**Uniform Trade Secrets Act (Adopted by Most States):**

Protects confidential information including:
- Customer lists (names, contact information, purchasing history)
- Pricing information
- Underwriting guidelines
- Product specifications

**Advantages Over Non-Competes:**
- No reasonableness balancing test
- Protects against misappropriation by anyone (not just former employees)
- Damages include actual losses + unjust enrichment (or reasonable royalty)
- Attorney's fees available for willful and malicious misappropriation

**LLIC Trade Secret Strategy:**
- Classify customer lists and proprietary information as trade secrets
- Implement confidentiality safeguards (password protection, need-to-know access)
- Include robust confidentiality provisions in agent agreements
- Upon departure, demand return of all confidential information
- Monitor for use of LLIC customer lists at new employer

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Agent Retention Is Critical Business Risk**: With 1,240 captive agents generating 42% of annual premiums ($882M), agent attrition of 20-30% would result in $220M annual premium loss. Retention bonuses of $22.9M-$36M are economically justified and should reduce attrition to 10-15%.

2. **Nebraska/California Non-Competes Largely Unenforceable**: LLIC's domiciliary state (Nebraska) and largest market (California) both strongly disfavor non-compete agreements. AFH should not rely on non-competes to prevent agent departures; instead, focus on retention incentives, trade secret protection, and non-solicitation enforcement in permissive jurisdictions.

3. **Double-Trigger Severance Limits Key Employee Exposure**: If LLIC employment agreements utilize standard double-trigger change-of-control provisions, severance exposure limited to executives AFH terminates or who resign for "good reason." AFH's intent to retain management minimizes this risk.

4. **WARN Act Compliance Essential If Workforce Reductions Planned**: If AFH contemplates consolidating operations or reducing headcount by 50+ employees (or 33% of workforce), WARN Act's 60-day advance notice requirement applies. Non-compliance exposes AFH to $3M+ in back pay liability.

5. **FLSA Misclassification Risk Manageable Through Pre-Closing Audit**: Potential wage and hour exposure of $8.6M-$9.6M (75-employee class action) is avoidable through pre-closing FLSA audit, reclassification of at-risk positions, and seller indemnification for pre-closing violations.

6. **IRC ¬ß 280G Excise Tax Avoidable Through Cutback Provisions**: Excess parachute payment exposure of $5M-$10M can be mitigated by including cutback provisions in employment agreements limiting severance to 2.99x base amount (just below 3x threshold triggering excise tax).

7. **Total Employment Exposure Within Industry Norms**: Expected employment-related costs of $53.25M represent 1.8% of $2.9B purchase price, within industry-standard M&A retention budgets (1-3% of purchase price). Risk-adjusted exposure of $36.4M is manageable and should be addressed through escrow/indemnification structure.

8. **Independent Producer Retention Lower Priority**: While independent producers generate 58% of sales, they are not exclusive to LLIC and AFH cannot enforce contractual retention mechanisms. Retention efforts should focus on demonstrating AFH's capital strength, product competitiveness, and competitive commission structures.
