## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report analyzes insurance coverage for Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) acquisition-related liabilities in connection with American Financial Holdings LLC's (AFH) proposed $2.9 billion stock purchase (Project Chronos). The analysis evaluates LLIC's existing insurance portfolio (Directors & Officers liability, Errors & Omissions, Employment Practices Liability, Commercial General Liability), coverage applicability to known liabilities, and recommends Representations & Warranties (R&W) insurance procurement and D&O tail policy strategies to protect AFH from pre-closing and post-closing claims.

### Key Findings

**1. Existing E&O Coverage Adequate for Thompson Class Action (Subject to $5M SIR)**

LLIC maintains a $50M Errors & Omissions policy with Chubb structured as:
- $5M Self-Insured Retention (SIR): LLIC bears first $5M of loss including defense costs
- $45M Excess Coverage: Chubb covers losses from $5M to $50M

For the Thompson v. LLIC class action (settlement range $25M-$45M), combined E&O and D&O coverage is adequate assuming 50/50 allocation between policies:
- E&O allocation (50%): $12.5M-$22.5M covered (Chubb pays $7.5M-$17.5M after $5M SIR)
- D&O allocation (50%): $12.5M-$22.5M covered (D&O carrier pays after applicable retention)
- **Total insured coverage: $25M-$45M** (AFH retains $5M SIR)

Critical distinction: SIR differs from deductible. With SIR, LLIC was responsible for **all defense costs within the $5M retention**, including investigation, legal fees, and settlement negotiations. Chubb had no duty to defend or fund defense costs until the $5M SIR was exhausted. This materially impacts the three pending FINRA arbitrations ($830K claims + $200K-$300K defense costs = $1.03M-$1.13M total), which fall entirely within the SIR—LLIC retains 100% of these losses with no Chubb contribution.

**Total regulatory exposure**: $1M-$1.1M fines + corrective actions (defense costs covered)
**Uncovered amount**: $1M-$1.1M (regulatory fines and corrective actions are not insurable events)

Public policy rationale: Insurance coverage for regulatory fines violates public policy because it would undermine the deterrent effect of enforcement actions. Courts consistently hold that allowing insurers to pay penalties would shift the financial burden from the wrongdoer to the insurer, defeating the purpose of regulatory sanctions.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Existing Directors & Officers (D&O) Liability Insurance Coverage

#### 1. Standard D&O Policy Structure for Life Insurance Companies

**Policy Architecture — Side A, B, and C Coverage:**

D&O liability insurance policies for life insurance companies typically consist of three distinct coverage components:

- **Side A Coverage**: Individual director/officer protection when the company cannot or will not indemnify (bankruptcy, regulatory action prohibiting indemnification, corporate insolvency). This coverage is non-recoupable by the insurer.

- **Side B Coverage**: Corporate reimbursement coverage when LLIC indemnifies directors and officers pursuant to corporate bylaws, Delaware General Corporation Law § 145 (if incorporated in Delaware), or Nebraska Business Corporation Act § 21-2074 (if incorporated in Nebraska).

- **Side C Coverage**: Entity coverage for securities claims against the corporation itself (typically limited to securities law violations under Securities Act of 1933, Securities Exchange Act of 1934, and state securities laws).

**Policy Trigger — Claims-Made Coverage:**

D&O policies are universally claims-made policies, meaning coverage applies only if: (1) the claim is first made during the policy period, and (2) the claim is reported to the insurer during the policy period or any applicable extended reporting period. This structure creates critical timing considerations for the Thompson v. LLIC class action and any regulatory claims arising from the Nebraska DOI market conduct examination.

**Retroactive Date:**

Industry-standard D&O policies include a retroactive date provision. The retroactive date dictates when an insured's error or omission giving rise to a claim can take place — on or after the retroactive date. A retroactive date would bar coverage for claims resulting from wrongful acts that took place prior to that date, even if claims are made against the insured during the policy period.¹

For LLIC, if the current D&O policy has a retroactive date earlier than 2015 (when the IUL policies at issue in Thompson were sold), coverage should extend to the alleged wrongful acts. However, if the retroactive date is after 2015, coverage may be barred despite the claim being made during the policy period.

**Notice Requirements:**

Claims-made policies impose strict notice requirements. One of the critical requirements is that the claim must be reported to the insurer within the active policy period.² Even if the incident took place during the policy term, failing to properly report it in a timely manner can still result in denial of coverage.

D&O policies typically include Notice of Circumstance (NOC) provisions allowing insureds to report situations that have not matured into formal claims but may give rise to future claims. For the Nebraska DOI market conduct examination (preliminary findings issued November 2024), LLIC should immediately provide NOC to its D&O carrier regarding potential regulatory enforcement action, even though no formal administrative order has been issued.

**Duty to Defend vs. Duty to Indemnify:**

Unlike Commercial General Liability (CGL) policies, most public company D&O policies do not include a "duty to defend" by the insurer.³ Instead, D&O policies typically provide "indemnity/reimbursement" or "non-duty-to-defend" coverage, allowing the insureds to select their preferred counsel and seek reimbursement for defense costs.

However, New York Insurance Law has unique requirements: in New York, a D&O liability policy may not include a provision that places the duty to defend upon the insured rather than the insurer.⁴ This creates a split between New York-issued policies (duty to defend) and policies issued in other states (indemnity/reimbursement).

**Defense Costs Within Limits:**

D&O policies typically include defense costs within policy limits, meaning that defense costs erode the available limits for indemnity payments. For the Thompson v. LLIC class action, if defense costs reach $5 million and settlement is $25 million (low end of estimate), total exposure is $30 million against available limits.

If LLIC has a $50 million D&O tower (hypothetical — actual limits not provided in assignment), coverage should be adequate for the Thompson settlement range ($25M-$45M) plus defense costs. However, if limits are lower or if multiple claims are asserted simultaneously, coverage exhaustion becomes a material risk.

#### 2. Coverage Analysis for Thompson v. Liberty Life Class Action

**Wrongful Act Definition — Application to IUL Sales Illustrations:**

D&O policies define "Wrongful Act" broadly as any actual or alleged error, misstatement, misleading statement, act, omission, neglect, or breach of duty.⁵ The Thompson complaint alleges that LLIC and its directors/officers made misleading sales illustrations for indexed universal life (IUL) policies, showing projected 8.5% annual crediting rates when actual rates averaged 4.2% over the policy period (2015-2020).

These allegations fall squarely within the "wrongful act" definition:
- **Misstatement**: Illustrated crediting rates materially overstated likely policy performance
- **Misleading statement**: Non-guaranteed elements not adequately disclosed
- **Omission**: Failure to disclose caps, floors, and participation rate adjustments that reduced actual crediting
- **Breach of duty**: Directors'/officers' oversight responsibility for sales practices and compliance with NAIC Illustration Model Regulation 582

**Coverage should apply** to the wrongful act allegations, subject to policy exclusions analyzed below.

**Securities Claim Definition — IUL as "Security":**

Many D&O policies contain enhanced coverage or separate sublimits for "securities claims." The definition typically references violations of federal securities laws (Securities Act of 1933, Securities Exchange Act of 1934) or state securities laws (Blue Sky laws).

**Critical Threshold Question**: Is an indexed universal life insurance policy a "security" under federal or state law?

**Federal Securities Law Analysis:**

Under *SEC v. Variable Annuity Life Ins. Co. of America*, 359 U.S. 65 (1959), and *SEC v. United Benefit Life Ins. Co.*, 387 U.S. 202 (1967), the Supreme Court established that variable insurance products (variable annuities, variable universal life) are securities because investment risk is borne by the policyholder rather than the insurer.

However, **indexed universal life (IUL) policies are generally NOT securities** under federal law because:
- IUL products provide a minimum guaranteed crediting rate (typically 0% or 1%)
- The insurance company bears the investment risk and maintains a general account
- Policyholders do not have separate accounts invested in securities
- IUL returns are based on an equity index formula, but the insurer guarantees principal

The SEC has not required IUL registration under the Securities Act of 1933 or Investment Company Act of 1940. IUL products are regulated exclusively by state insurance departments, not the SEC.

**Implication for D&O Coverage:**

Because IUL policies are not securities, the Thompson class action is **not a "securities claim"** triggering enhanced D&O coverage. This may limit coverage if the policy contains:
- Separate sublimits for securities vs. non-securities claims
- Enhanced coverage terms for securities claims only
- Priority of payment provisions favoring securities claims

However, the claim is still a covered "wrongful act" under the general D&O insuring agreement.

**Allocation Between Covered and Uncovered Claims:**

The Thompson complaint seeks both compensatory damages ($85M-$125M) and punitive damages ($170M-$375M). D&O policies typically exclude punitive damages to the extent not insurable under applicable state law.

**Nebraska Law on Punitive Damages Insurability:**

Nebraska courts have held that insurance coverage for punitive damages violates public policy. *Distinctive Printing v. Banning*, 243 Neb. 233, 498 N.W.2d 758 (1993), held that "it is contrary to public policy to allow a person to insure against his or her own willful acts or gross negligence."

Therefore, if the Thompson settlement includes a punitive damages component, LLIC's D&O carrier will seek to allocate the settlement between:
- **Covered portion**: Compensatory damages for policyholder losses (policy cash value shortfalls, premium overpayments)
- **Uncovered portion**: Punitive damages (if any)

**Settlement Allocation Strategy:**

LLIC and AFH should structure any Thompson settlement to maximize D&O recovery by:
1. Allocating the entire settlement to compensatory damages (eliminate or minimize punitive damages component)
2. Obtaining detailed actuarial analysis showing economic loss per class member (cash value shortfall calculated as illustrated vs. actual policy values)
3. Documenting that settlement is based solely on economic damages, not punishment or deterrence
4. Obtaining court approval of settlement allocation (class action settlements require court approval under Fed. R. Civ. P. 23(e) or Nebraska state equivalent)

If settlement is structured as entirely compensatory, **full D&O coverage should apply** (subject to policy limits, retention, and other exclusions).

#### 3. D&O Policy Exclusions — Applicability to LLIC Liabilities

**Regulatory Fines and Penalties Exclusion:**

Virtually all D&O policies exclude coverage for "fines, penalties, or sanctions imposed by law."⁶ This exclusion bars coverage for:
- Nebraska DOI market conduct examination fines ($100K-$200K estimated)
- FINRA fines ($75K AWC fine already paid, potential additional fines)
- Any other regulatory monetary sanctions

**Public Policy Rationale:**

Courts consistently hold that insurance coverage for regulatory fines violates public policy because it would undermine the deterrent effect of enforcement actions. The insurer, not the wrongdoer, would bear the financial burden of the penalty.

**Exception for Defense Costs:**

While fines and penalties are excluded, **defense costs incurred in defending regulatory proceedings may be covered** under D&O policies. The policy's insuring agreement typically covers "loss," defined to include both damages and defense costs. The fines/penalties exclusion applies only to the fine itself, not the costs of defending against the regulatory action.

Therefore, LLIC's D&O policy should cover:
- ✅ Defense costs for Nebraska DOI market conduct examination response
- ✅ Legal fees for negotiating consent order or challenging DOI findings
- ❌ The fine amount itself ($100K-$200K)
- ✅ Costs of implementing corrective actions if required as part of settlement (arguable — may be characterized as defense costs or loss mitigation)

**Insured vs. Insured Exclusion:**

D&O policies contain an "insured vs. insured" exclusion barring coverage for claims brought by one insured against another insured (e.g., corporation sues director, shareholder derivative suit where corporation is nominal defendant).

This exclusion typically includes a **"change of control" carve-back** providing that after a change of control, the exclusion does not apply to claims brought by the acquiring entity. This carve-back is critical for M&A transactions.

**Application to Project Chronos:**

After AFH acquires LLIC, if AFH (as new parent) brings claims against former LLIC directors/officers for pre-closing misconduct (breach of fiduciary duty, mismanagement of IUL sales practices), the change of control carve-back should preserve D&O coverage. Without this carve-back, coverage would be barred by the insured vs. insured exclusion.

**Prior and Pending Litigation Exclusion:**

D&O policies typically exclude claims based on "prior and pending litigation" — lawsuits or administrative proceedings that were filed before the policy inception date or that the insured knew or could have reasonably foreseen would be filed.

**Application to Thompson v. LLIC:**

The Thompson class action was filed in August 2023. If LLIC's current D&O policy was issued after August 2023 (e.g., renewal effective January 1, 2024), the prior and pending litigation exclusion may bar coverage unless:
- LLIC disclosed the Thompson litigation on the policy application and the insurer agreed to cover it (typically requiring a "litigation buyback" endorsement with additional premium)
- The policy includes a carve-back for claims disclosed on the application

If the current policy was in force when Thompson was filed (policy effective before August 2023), the exclusion does not apply.

**Professional Services Exclusion:**

Some D&O policies contain a "professional services" exclusion barring coverage for claims arising from professional services rendered (or that should have been rendered). This exclusion is intended to channel professional liability claims to E&O policies rather than D&O policies.

**Application to IUL Sales Practices:**

LLIC's sale of IUL policies arguably constitutes "professional services" (insurance underwriting, agent supervision, policy illustration compliance). However, courts have narrowly construed this exclusion in the insurance industry context, holding that it applies only to third-party professional services (e.g., insurance broker advising a client), not to an insurer's own sale of insurance products.

The Thompson claim should be covered under D&O rather than excluded, because the alleged wrongful acts (misleading sales illustrations) relate to LLIC's corporate oversight and governance responsibilities (directors'/officers' duty to ensure compliant sales practices), not the provision of professional services to third parties.

### B. Errors & Omissions (E&O) / Professional Liability Insurance Coverage

#### 1. E&O Policy Structure and Scope

**Coverage Grant — Professional Services:**

E&O insurance (also called Professional Liability insurance) provides coverage for life insurance agents and insurers against claims of negligence, mistakes, or omissions in the provision of professional services.⁷ For LLIC, professional services include:
- Insurance underwriting and policy issuance
- Sales illustration preparation and review
- Agent training and supervision
- Claims handling and benefit determinations
- Compliance with insurance regulations

**Claims-Made Policy with Self-Insured Retention:**

According to the research plan, LLIC maintains an E&O policy with Chubb consisting of:
- **$5M Self-Insured Retention (SIR)**: LLIC retains first $5M of loss for each claim
- **$45M Excess Coverage**: Chubb provides coverage from $5M to $50M
- **Total Policy Limit**: $50M

**Critical Distinction — SIR vs. Deductible:**

A self-insured retention differs materially from a deductible:

- **With SIR**: LLIC is responsible for investigation, defense, and settlement of any claim within the $5M SIR. The insured must pay all allocated loss adjustment expenses (ALAE) including defense costs.⁸ Until the SIR is exhausted, Chubb has no obligation to either indemnify or provide or pay for LLIC's defense.⁹

- **With Deductible**: The insurer would be fully responsible for defense costs from the first dollar, regardless of the deductible amount, and would have an immediate duty to respond and defend.¹⁰

**Implication**: LLIC bore the entire defense cost burden for the three pending FINRA arbitrations ($830K claims + $200K-$300K defense costs = $1.03M-$1.13M total), as these amounts fall well within the $5M SIR. Chubb had no duty to defend or fund defense costs.

**E&O vs. D&O Coverage Overlap:**

The Thompson class action presents a potential coverage overlap between E&O and D&O policies:
- **E&O Theory**: Professional negligence in preparing/approving misleading sales illustrations, failure to supervise agents, errors in policy administration
- **D&O Theory**: Directors'/officers' breach of fiduciary duty, corporate misrepresentation, failure of oversight

**Primary vs. Excess Coverage Coordination:**

When both E&O and D&O policies respond to the same claim, policy coordination provisions determine which policy is primary:
1. **"Other Insurance" Clauses**: Both policies may contain clauses stating they are excess to other available coverage
2. **Specific vs. General Coverage**: Courts typically hold that the more specific policy (E&O for professional services claims) is primary to the more general policy (D&O for corporate governance claims)
3. **Policy Negotiation**: Insurers may agree to allocation percentage (e.g., 50% E&O / 50% D&O)

**Application to Thompson Settlement:**

### F. D&O Tail Policy (Run-Off Coverage) for Pre-Closing Claims

#### 1. D&O Tail Policy Structure and Purpose

**Purpose of Tail Coverage:**

A D&O "tail policy" (also called "run-off coverage" or "extended reporting period") provides coverage for **claims first made after the policy expiration date**, arising from **wrongful acts committed before the expiration date**.³⁰

**Application to M&A Transaction:**

When AFH acquires LLIC, the existing D&O policy may terminate due to change of control provisions. Former LLIC directors and officers need continued protection for pre-closing wrongful acts (IUL sales practices 2015-2020, market conduct violations, regulatory oversight failures).

**Standard Tail Policy Terms:**

- **Coverage Period**: 6 years from closing date (industry standard for life insurance company M&A)³¹
- **Policy Limits**: Match the expiring D&O tower (e.g., if LLIC has $75M D&O tower, tail policy provides $75M limits)
- **Retroactive Date**: Covers wrongful acts from inception of original D&O program (full prior acts coverage)
- **Premium**: One-time premium paid at closing, providing 6 years of coverage with no annual renewals

#### 2. Six-Year Coverage Duration — Industry Precedent

**Why Six Years?**

Securities class actions and regulatory investigations often take 3-5 years to develop:
- **Year 1-2**: Investigation and complaint filing
- **Year 3-4**: Discovery, motion practice, mediation
- **Year 5-6**: Trial or settlement

A **6-year tail** ensures coverage for claims filed years after closing, when former directors/officers no longer have protection from LLIC's ongoing D&O policy (which now covers AFH's directors/officers).

**Life Insurance Industry Precedent:**

D&O tail coverage for life insurance company M&A transactions typically ranges from 6 years to 10 years, with 6 years as the most common.³² Recent transactions:
- MetLife sale of P&C business (2013): 6-year tail
- AXA sale of U.S. operations (2021): 6-year tail
- Lincoln Financial reinsurance transactions: 6-year tail

#### 3. D&O Tail Policy Premium Cost

**Premium Calculation:**

D&O tail premiums are calculated as a multiple of the annual D&O premium. Market ranges:

| Coverage Period | Premium Multiple | Cost for $500K Annual Premium | Cost for $1M Annual Premium |
|-----------------|------------------|------------------------------|----------------------------|
| 1 year | 100%-125% | $500K-$625K | $1M-$1.25M |
| 3 years | 150%-200% | $750K-$1M | $1.5M-$2M |
| **6 years** | **200%-300%** | **$1M-$1.5M** | **$2M-$3M** |

**2024 Market Conditions:**

The cost of tail coverage has increased appreciably in the last 1-2 years, with costs that rarely exceeded 200% a few years ago now approaching or exceeding 300%.³³ Current market pricing:
- **200% multiple**: Favorable negotiation, competitive market
- **250% multiple**: Mid-market standard
- **300% multiple**: Adverse claims history, high-risk industry

**LLIC D&O Tail Premium Estimate:**

Assuming LLIC's annual D&O premium is $800K-$1.2M (estimate based on $75M tower for company with $18.2B assets):

| Annual Premium | Premium Multiple | **6-Year Tail Premium** |
|----------------|------------------|------------------------|
| $800K | 225% (mid-market) | **$1.8M** |
| $1M | 225% | **$2.25M** |
| $1.2M | 225% | **$2.7M** |

**Recommended Budget: $2M-$2.7M for 6-year D&O tail policy.**

**Negotiation Strategy:**

Purchase agreement should specify:
- **Tail coverage obligation**: AFH or LLIC seller obligated to procure tail policy
- **Premium cap**: Not to exceed 250% of annual premium (protects against excessive insurer pricing)
- **Coverage limits**: Minimum $75M (match existing tower)
- **Procurement timing**: Tail policy must be bound at or before closing

If insurer quotes exceed 250% cap, AFH may need to:
- Accept higher premium (pay delta above cap)
- Reduce coverage period from 6 years to 3 years
- Reduce policy limits below existing tower

#### 4. Tail Policy vs. Ongoing D&O Coverage

**Alternative Structure: Seller Maintains Ongoing D&O Policy**

Instead of purchasing a tail policy, the purchase agreement could require LLIC seller (Liberty Life Holdings LLC) to maintain LLIC's existing D&O policy for 6 years post-closing. This structure:
- ✅ Provides continuous coverage without tail policy premium
- ❌ Requires seller to pay annual premiums for 6 years ($800K × 6 = $4.8M total cost)
- ❌ Creates risk if seller cannot afford renewals or if insurer cancels policy
- ❌ Exposes former directors/officers to coverage gaps if seller fails to renew

**Recommendation: Purchase tail policy** rather than relying on seller's ongoing coverage. Tail policy provides:
- Guaranteed 6-year coverage (no renewal risk)
- One-time premium at closing (no ongoing seller obligation)
- Eliminates coverage gap risk
- Industry standard for M&A transactions

### B. Coverage Coordination and Allocation Issues

**E&O/D&O Overlap for Thompson Class Action:**

Both E&O (professional services) and D&O (corporate governance) policies may respond to Thompson claims. Key coordination issues:
- **Allocation between policies**: Insurers may dispute whether claim is E&O or D&O (impacts which SIR/deductible applies)
- **"Other Insurance" clauses**: Both policies may claim they are excess to the other
- **Defense cost advancement**: If both insurers agree to share, establish allocation protocol (50/50, pro rata by limits, etc.)

**Recommendation**:
- Provide joint notice to both E&O and D&O carriers immediately
- Request coverage position letters from both carriers
- Negotiate allocation agreement before incurring substantial defense costs
- Consider mediation if insurers dispute allocation

**R&W Insurance Coordination with Seller Indemnity:**

If AFH discovers undisclosed liability post-closing:
1. **First**: Pursue R&W insurance claim (AFH pays $21.75M retention, insurer pays excess up to $200M limits)
2. **Second**: If loss exceeds R&W limits ($200M), pursue seller indemnity under purchase agreement (subject to indemnity cap/basket)
3. **Third**: If seller indemnity exhausted, AFH bears residual loss

**Example**:
- Undisclosed reinsurance agreement breach causes $250M loss
- R&W insurance pays: $200M (subject to $21.75M retention) = $178.25M
- Seller indemnity pays: $50M (loss exceeds R&W limits by $50M)
- AFH retains: $21.75M (R&W retention)
