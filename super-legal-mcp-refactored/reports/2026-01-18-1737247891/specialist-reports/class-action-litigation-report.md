### D. Indexed Universal Life (IUL) Product Structure

Indexed Universal Life insurance is a permanent life insurance product where the cash value component earns interest based on the performance of a market index (typically the S&P 500, Dow Jones, or Nasdaq) rather than being directly invested in securities or earning a fixed rate.

**Key Product Features:**

1. **Index Crediting Mechanism:**
   - Cash value is **not** directly invested in the stock market
   - Interest is credited using a formula defined by the insurer based on index performance
   - Annual point-to-point calculation compares index value at policy anniversary

2. **Cap Rate:**
   - Maximum interest rate that can be credited in any given year
   - Typical caps range from 8-12% for S&P 500-linked strategies as of 2024
   - Example: If S&P 500 returns 15% but cap is 10%, policyholder receives 10% credit

3. **Floor Rate:**
   - Minimum interest rate guaranteed, typically 0%
   - Protects against negative returns from index declines
   - Example: If S&P 500 declines -20%, policyholder receives 0% (no loss to cash value from market decline)

4. **Participation Rate:**
   - Percentage of index gains applied to cash value
   - Can be less than, equal to, or greater than 100%
   - Example: 100% participation rate means full index gains (up to cap) credited; 80% participation rate means 80% of index gains credited

5. **Cost of Insurance (COI) Charges:**
   - Monthly deductions from cash value for mortality costs
   - Based on insured's age, health class, policy face amount
   - Can increase over time based on insurer's experience or contractual provisions

**Illustration Requirements:**

Under NAIC Model Regulation 582 (Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation), adopted by Nebraska and most states:

- **Basic Illustration:** Must show both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements at the same durations
- **Guaranteed Elements:** Premiums, benefits, values, credits, charges that are guaranteed and determined at policy issue
- **Non-Guaranteed Elements:** Premiums, benefits, values, credits, charges that are not guaranteed or determined at policy issue (e.g., current cap rates, participation rates, COI charges, credited interest rates)
- **Disclosure Requirement:** Non-guaranteed elements must be clearly labeled as "not guaranteed" and shown alongside guaranteed values
- **Illustrated Rate:** Cannot exceed certain prescribed maximums; must show hypothetical scenarios

### E. Alleged Deceptive Marketing Practices (Thompson Case)

According to the transaction materials, the Thompson complaint alleges the following misconduct by LLIC in marketing and selling indexed universal life policies between 2015-2020:

**Primary Allegations:**

1. **Overstated Projected Returns:**
   - Sales illustrations allegedly showed hypothetical credited interest rates of 8.5% annually
   - Actual performance: IUL policies credited average of 4.2% annually (2015-2023)
   - Shortfall: 4.3 percentage points below illustrated projections
   - Result: Cash values significantly lower than illustrated; policies at risk of lapsing

2. **Inadequate Disclosure of Non-Guaranteed Elements:**
   - Illustrations allegedly failed to adequately disclose that cap rates, participation rates, and floors could be changed by LLIC
   - Policyholders allegedly led to believe illustrated rates were more certain than contractually guaranteed
   - Fine print disclosures allegedly insufficient to overcome misleading headline projections

3. **Failure to Disclose Cost of Insurance (COI) Increase Risk:**
   - Policies contain provisions allowing LLIC to increase COI charges
   - Allegations claim agents did not explain that COI increases could require additional premiums to keep policy in force
   - Higher COI charges combined with lower credited interest = cash value erosion

4. **Unsuitable Sales:**
   - Allegations that IUL policies were sold to individuals for whom they were unsuitable given age, income, risk tolerance, or financial sophistication
   - Particular concern with sales to older individuals (ages 60-65) where policy may not perform as illustrated before life expectancy

**Damages Claimed:**

- **Compensatory Damages:** $85 million to $125 million representing:
  - Difference between illustrated cash values and actual cash values (expectation damages)

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Legal Framework for IUL Deceptive Marketing Claims

#### 1. Nebraska Consumer Protection Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. Â§ 59-1601 et seq.)

The Nebraska Consumer Protection Act prohibits unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce. Neb. Rev. Stat. Â§ 59-1602 states: "Unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce shall be unlawful."

**Application to Insurance:**

The Act explicitly applies to insurance-related transactions. Neb. Rev. Stat. Â§ 59-1602 provides that "actions and transactions prohibited or regulated under the laws administered by the Director of Insurance shall be subject to section 59-1602." This means insurance misrepresentation and deceptive practices fall under the Consumer Protection Act's framework with enforcement through the Nebraska Department of Insurance.

**Elements for Liability:**

To be actionable under the Nebraska Consumer Protection Act, the plaintiff must show:
1. An unfair or deceptive act or practice
2. In the conduct of trade or commerce
3. That has an impact upon the public interest

**Relevance to Thompson Case:**

The allegations that LLIC used misleading sales illustrations overstating projected returns (8.5% illustrated vs. 4.2% actual) and failing to adequately disclose non-guaranteed elements would constitute "deceptive acts or practices" if proven. A class of 5,000-8,000 policyholders would satisfy the "impact upon the public interest" requirement, as Nebraska courts have found that systematic misconduct affecting numerous consumers meets this threshold.

#### 2. Common Law Fraud and Misrepresentation (Nebraska)

**Elements of Common Law Fraud (Nebraska):**

Under Nebraska case law, to establish fraud, a plaintiff must prove:
1. A representation was made
2. The representation was false
3. When made, the representation was known to be false or made without knowledge of its truth or falseness
4. The representation was made with the intention that the plaintiff should rely upon it
5. The plaintiff reasonably relied on the representation
6. The plaintiff suffered damage as a result

*See* State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Duel, 324 Neb. 567, 882 N.W.2d 407 (2016).

**Negligent Misrepresentation:**

Nebraska also recognizes claims for negligent misrepresentation, which require:
1. Information supplied in the course of the defendant's business
2. The defendant failed to exercise reasonable care in obtaining or communicating the information
3. The plaintiff justifiably relied on the information
4. The plaintiff suffered pecuniary loss

*See* Hawkins Constr. Co. v. Beatrice Dev., 265 Neb. 109, 655 N.W.2d 234 (2003).

**Insurance-Specific Context:**

In Nebraska, to defeat recovery on the ground of fraudulent representations, an insurance company must prove that representations were untrue, were made by the insured knowingly with fraudulent intent to deceive, and were material to the risk and relied upon by the defendant. An insurer may rescind an insurance policy when the insured has knowingly made a material misrepresentation, but the same principles apply in reverse when insurers allegedly misrepresent terms to policyholders.

**Application to Thompson Case:**

Plaintiffs allege that LLIC's sales illustrations showing 8.5% projected crediting rates constituted false representations about expected policy performance. LLIC's defense would likely argue that:
- Illustrations included disclaimers that projections were hypothetical and not guaranteed
- Policy contracts clearly stated that cap rates, participation rates, and COI charges were subject to change
- Industry-standard illustrations under NAIC Model 582 inherently involve projections subject to variability

The outcome would depend on whether a reasonable policyholder, reviewing the entire illustration package (including disclosures), would be misled about the likelihood of achieving illustrated values.

#### 3. Breach of Contract Claims

**Theory:**

Policyholders may allege that LLIC breached the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing by:
- Reducing cap rates or participation rates from illustrated levels
- Increasing COI charges beyond reasonable expectations created by illustrations
- Administering policies in a manner inconsistent with reasonable expectations

**Nebraska Law on Insurance Contracts:**

Nebraska follows general principles of contract interpretation for insurance policies:
- Ambiguous terms construed against the insurer (contra proferentem)
- Reasonable expectations of the insured given effect where policy language is unclear
- Express contractual provisions control over pre-contract representations (parol evidence rule), but fraud or misrepresentation can void this protection

**Challenges for Plaintiffs:**

IUL policy contracts typically include express provisions stating:
- Non-guaranteed elements (cap rates, participation rates, COI charges) may be changed at insurer's discretion subject to contractual limitations
- Illustrations are hypothetical and not guaranteed
- Only guaranteed elements are contractually binding

Defense would likely argue that plaintiffs cannot claim breach of contract for changes to non-guaranteed elements that the contract expressly permits LLIC to modify.

#### 4. NAIC Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation 582

**Purpose and Adoption:**

NAIC Model Regulation 582 provides rules for life insurance policy illustrations to protect consumers and foster consumer education. The regulation prescribes standards for illustration formats and specifies required disclosures. Nebraska has adopted Model 582 (verify through Nebraska Administrative Code Title 210, Chapter 70 or equivalent).

**Key Requirements:**

1. **Types of Illustrations:**
   - **Basic Illustration:** Used in marketing, shows both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements
   - **Supplemental Illustration:** Additional scenarios beyond basic illustration
   - **In-Force Illustration:** Provided annually to show current policy status

2. **Guaranteed vs. Non-Guaranteed Elements:**
   - **Guaranteed Elements:** Premiums, benefits, values, credits, charges that are guaranteed and determined at issue
   - **Non-Guaranteed Elements:** Premiums, benefits, values, credits, charges that are not guaranteed or determined at issue

3. **Display Requirements:**
   - Non-guaranteed elements must be shown at the same durations as corresponding guaranteed elements
   - If no guaranteed benefit exists at a duration where non-guaranteed benefit is shown, a zero must be displayed
   - Clear labeling distinguishing guaranteed from non-guaranteed values required

4. **Illustrated Rate Limits:**
   - Non-guaranteed elements cannot be illustrated using rates that exceed regulatory maximums
   - Insurers must use disciplined current scale that reflects anticipated experience

**Compliance as Affirmative Defense:**

If LLIC's illustrations complied with NAIC Model 582 and Nebraska's implementing regulations, LLIC may assert regulatory compliance as an affirmative defense. However:
- Regulatory compliance does not provide absolute immunity from fraud claims if overall presentation was materially misleading
- Compliance with minimum regulatory standards may be insufficient if disclosures were technically present but designed to obscure material information (fine print, buried in lengthy documents)

**Relevance to Thompson Case:**

Plaintiffs likely argue that even if LLIC's illustrations technically complied with Model 582 format requirements, the overall presentation was deceptive because:
- Illustrated 8.5% rates created unreasonable expectations despite disclaimers
- Agents orally misrepresented the likelihood of achieving illustrated values during sales process
- Actual experience (4.2% average crediting) was so far below illustrated rates that illustrations were misleading

Discovery of LLIC's illustration development process, rate-setting methodology, and agent training materials will be critical to assessing these claims.

### B. Class Certification Analysis Under Federal Rule 23

#### 1. Rule 23(a) Requirements (Prerequisites)

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(a) requires that a class representative prove four elements:

**(1) Numerosity (Rule 23(a)(1)):**

"The class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable."

**Analysis:** With 5,000-8,000 putative class members, numerosity is easily satisfied. Courts routinely find numerosity met with classes exceeding 40 members; here, even the low-end estimate (5,000) is 125 times that threshold.

**Conclusion:** Numerosity requirement **SATISFIED**.

**(2) Commonality (Rule 23(a)(2)):**

"There are questions of law or fact common to the class."

**Supreme Court Standard (Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.S. 338 (2011)):**

Commonality requires that class members' claims "depend upon a common contention" such that "determination of its truth or falsity will resolve an issue that is central to the validity of each one of the claims in one stroke." *Id.* at 350. What matters is "the capacity of a classwide proceeding to generate common answers apt to drive the resolution of the litigation." *Id.*

**Common Questions in Thompson Case:**

- Did LLIC use a standardized IUL sales illustration format showing 8.5% projected crediting rates for policies sold 2015-2020?
- Did LLIC's standard illustration adequately disclose that cap rates, participation rates, and COI charges were non-guaranteed and subject to change?
- Did LLIC train agents using standardized materials that reinforced or contradicted illustration disclosures?
- Were the 8.5% projected rates materially misleading under Nebraska Consumer Protection Act and common law fraud standards?

**LLIC's Likely Arguments Against Commonality:**

- Individual reliance: Each policyholder's reliance on illustrations was individual and subjective (some may have read disclaimers carefully, others may not have)
- Suitability varies: Whether IUL was suitable depends on individual financial circumstances, age, income, investment objectives
- Damages vary: Cash value shortfall differs for each policy based on premium payment history, policy duration, individual cap/participation rates

**Counter-Arguments (Plaintiffs):**

- State Farm precedent: In similar universal life class actions (e.g., Rogowski v. State Farm, $325M settlement), courts found commonality where insurer used uniform methodology for setting charges/credits
- Proof of uniform practices: If LLIC can show standard illustration format, standard agent training, and uniform cap rate adjustments applied to all class policies, common evidence establishes liability
- Damages calculable on classwide basis: Expert can calculate illustrated vs. actual cash value for each policy using uniform methodology

**Assessment:** Commonality is **LIKELY SATISFIED** if plaintiffs can demonstrate through discovery that LLIC used standardized illustration practices and uniformly applied rate-setting methodologies. Individual reliance issues typically go to predominance (Rule 23(b)(3)), not commonality.

**(3) Typicality (Rule 23(a)(3)):**

"The claims or defenses of the representative parties are typical of the claims or defenses of the class."

**Analysis:**

Named plaintiff Thompson must have purchased an IUL policy during the class period (2015-2020), received a sales illustration showing 8.5% projected crediting, and experienced lower actual performance. If Thompson's circumstances match the typical class member experience, typicality is satisfied.

**Potential Issues:**

- If Thompson had unique circumstances (e.g., unusually large policy, special underwriting, agent relationship), LLIC may challenge typicality
- If Thompson's policy performed significantly better or worse than average class member, representativeness may be questioned

**Assessment:** Typicality **LIKELY SATISFIED** assuming Thompson is representative of the typical IUL purchaser in the class period.

**(4) Adequacy of Representation (Rule 23(a)(4)):**

"The representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class."

**Two-Part Test:**

1. Adequate legal representation: Class counsel must be experienced and qualified
2. No conflicts of interest: Representative plaintiff's interests must align with class members' interests

**Analysis:**

- Assuming competent, experienced class action counsel representing Thompson, legal adequacy should be satisfied
- No apparent conflicts between Thompson and other class members (all seek recovery for allegedly misleading illustrations)

**Assessment:** Adequacy **LIKELY SATISFIED**.

#### 2. Rule 23(b)(3) Requirements (Predominance and Superiority)

For a class seeking money damages (as opposed to injunctive relief), plaintiffs must also satisfy Rule 23(b)(3):

**(1) Predominance:**

"Questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members."

**Supreme Court Standard:**

Predominance is a "far more demanding" requirement than Rule 23(a) commonality. *Comcast Corp. v. Behrend*, 569 U.S. 27, 34 (2013). Plaintiffs must show that "common questions" are "more prevalent or important" than individual ones. The inquiry focuses on whether classwide litigation "will generate common answers apt to drive the resolution of the litigation."

**Common Issues That Predominate:**

1. **Liability Issues:**
   - Whether LLIC's standard IUL illustrations were materially misleading (uniform evidence)
   - Whether LLIC violated NAIC Model 582 or Nebraska Consumer Protection Act (uniform legal standard)
   - Whether LLIC's business practices (standard illustration format, agent training, rate-setting) constituted deceptive practices

2. **Causation (Classwide Presumption):**
   - In fraud cases involving standardized misrepresentations to a large group, courts may apply a rebuttable presumption of classwide reliance (similar to fraud-on-the-market theory in securities cases, though less established in insurance context)
   - If all class members received materially similar illustrations, inference of reliance may be appropriate

**Individual Issues That Could Defeat Predominance:**

1. **Individual Reliance:**
   - Did each policyholder actually read and rely on the illustration?
   - Did individual agents make different oral representations?
   - Did some policyholders have independent financial advisors who reviewed illustrations?

2. **Individual Damages:**
   - Each policyholder's damage calculation requires individual computation based on premiums paid, actual cash value, illustrated cash value, policy duration
   - However, if a uniform damages formula can be applied (e.g., Expert calculates illustrated vs. actual cash value delta for each policy using standardized methodology), individualized damages do not defeat predominance

3. **Suitability/Causation:**
   - Some policyholders may have suffered cash value shortfalls due to non-payment of premiums rather than misleading illustrations
   - Some may have affirmatively chosen IUL after understanding risks
   - Some may have switched from other products (replacement analysis)

**Case Law on Insurance Illustration Class Actions:**

- **Transamerica Thompson Settlement ($88M):** Similar universal life case involving COI increases; class certified on common issues of whether increases were improper under contract terms
- **Gerber Life ($700M certified class, 2024):** Judge Karas (S.D.N.Y.) certified nationwide class for deceptive marketing of life insurance policies, finding predominance satisfied despite individual damages calculations

**LLIC's Strongest Argument Against Predominance:**

Individual reliance is outcome-determinative in fraud cases. Each policyholder's decision to purchase was influenced by unique factors: agent relationship, personal financial situation, comparison shopping, review of policy contract. Requiring mini-trials on 5,000-8,000 individual reliance questions defeats the efficiency rationale for class certification.

**Plaintiffs' Counter-Arguments:**

- Presumption of reliance: Where defendant makes uniform material misrepresentations to a large group, classwide inference of reliance is appropriate; defendant can rebut by showing individual class members knew the truth or did not rely
- *Affiliated Ute Citizens v. United States*, 406 U.S. 128 (1972): In omission cases (failure to disclose material facts), reliance may be presumed

### C. Settlement Valuation and Precedent Analysis

#### 1. Damages Calculation Methodologies

**Potential Damages Theories:**

**(A) Expectation Damages (Benefit of the Bargain):**

Policyholders expected cash value growth at illustrated 8.5% rates. Actual performance averaged 4.2%. Difference between illustrated and actual cash values = expectation damages.

**Calculation Formula:**
- Illustrated Cash Value at Current Duration (based on 8.5% crediting)
- MINUS Actual Cash Value (based on actual crediting rates)
- EQUALS Per-Policy Damages

**Example (hypothetical policy):**
- Premium: $10,000/year for 8 years = $80,000 total premiums
- Illustrated cash value (Year 8, 8.5% crediting): $95,000
- Actual cash value (Year 8, 4.2% crediting): $68,000
- Expectation damages: $27,000

**For 5,000-8,000 class members:**
- If average per-policy damages = $15,000 to $20,000
- Total class damages: $75M to $160M

**(B) Premium Refund (Rescission/Restitution):**

Alternative remedy: Rescind policies ab initio (as if never issued) and refund all premiums paid, less death benefit protection costs.

**Calculation Formula:**
- Total Premiums Paid
- MINUS Cost of Term Life Insurance for Equivalent Coverage (fair value of death benefit protection actually provided)
- EQUALS Refundable Premiums

**Example:**
- Premiums paid: $80,000
- Equivalent term insurance cost for 8 years: $15,000
- Refundable amount: $65,000

**Challenge:** This approach requires surrendering policies, which many policyholders may not want (especially if older and uninsurable, or if death benefit still needed).

**(C) Cash Value Shortfall (Limited Damages):**

More conservative measure: Compensate only for out-of-pocket loss (premiums paid minus current cash value), capped at amounts needed to keep policies in force.

**Calculation:**
- Premiums Paid: $80,000
- Current Cash Value: $68,000
- Shortfall: $12,000

This approach understates harm because it ignores forgone investment returns if premiums had been invested elsewhere.

**(D) "Top-Up" Damages (Make-Whole):**

Calculate amount needed to restore each policy to illustrated cash value trajectory.

**Example:**
- Shortfall between illustrated and actual cash value: $27,000
- Additional premium injection needed to achieve illustrated value: $27,000 + time value of money
- Class member can choose: (1) receive cash payment of $27,000, or (2) receive credit to policy cash value of $27,000

This is most attractive to class members who want to keep policies in force.

### A. Successor Liability for Pre-Closing Litigation

#### 1. Stock Purchase = Automatic Successor Liability

**Transaction Structure:**

American Financial Holdings LLC is acquiring 100% of the stock of Liberty Life Insurance Company in a **stock purchase transaction** (not an asset purchase). This distinction is critical for successor liability analysis.

**General Rule:**

In a **stock sale**, the successor buys a business outright, effectively stepping into the shoes of the acquisition target by not only acquiring its assets but also assuming any liabilities. The general rule is that an asset acquisition cuts off successor liability, while a merger results in the successor entity assuming the liabilities of the target company.

**Application to Thompson Litigation:**

- American Financial Holdings will own LLIC as a wholly-owned subsidiary
- LLIC continues to exist as a legal entity after the transaction
- All pre-closing liabilities of LLIC (including the Thompson class action) automatically transfer to the post-acquisition entity
- American Financial Holdings, as sole shareholder, bears economic responsibility for LLIC's liabilities through its equity investment

**No Avoidance of Successor Liability:**

Unlike asset purchases where buyers can selectively assume liabilities, stock purchases provide no mechanism to avoid pre-existing litigation claims. The Thompson class action will continue against LLIC post-closing, and any settlement or judgment will be paid by LLIC (now owned by American Financial Holdings).

#### 2. Nebraska Law on Successor Liability

**Continued Enterprise Doctrine:**

Under Nebraska common law and general corporate law principles applicable in Nebraska, corporations that continue the enterprise of the predecessor may be held liable for the predecessor's obligations, particularly where:
- Same business operations continue
- Same management or workforce retained
- Continuity of ownership or control
- Assumption of liabilities by agreement or operation of law

**Stock Purchase Analysis:**

In this case, successor liability is not based on the "continued enterprise" exception (which typically applies to asset purchases) but rather on the fundamental principle that LLIC remains the same legal entity before and after the acquisition. The stock purchase merely changes ownership; it does not create a new legal entity or terminate the existing entity's liabilities.

**Nebraska Statutory Framework:**

Nebraska's corporation law (Neb. Rev. Stat. Â§ 21-2,101 et seq., Nebraska Model Business Corporation Act) recognizes that stock sales do not affect the corporation's separate legal status. LLIC's liabilities are corporate obligations that survive changes in share ownership.

**Conclusion:** Successor liability is **CERTAIN (100% probability)** under Nebraska law for stock purchase transactions. American Financial Holdings will inherit the Thompson class action litigation exposure.

### C. Material Adverse Effect (MAE) Analysis

#### 1. MAE Clause Standard (M&A Transactions)

**Definition:**

Material Adverse Effect (MAE) or Material Adverse Change (MAC) clauses are common in merger and acquisition agreements. They allow a buyer to opt out of a deal if some significant negative change occurs to the target company prior to closing.

**Legal Standard (Delaware and Most Jurisdictions):**

Many courts have held that, to invoke MAE clauses, buyers must demonstrate adverse changes in the target company that are consequential to the company's long-term earning power. This has proven a difficult standard to meet. Parties seeking to invoke MAC outs bear a "heavy burden" to demonstrate that an MAE has occurred.

**Delaware Precedent:**

As of 2008, Delaware courts had never found a material adverse effect to have occurred in the context of a merger agreement. The landmark case *Akorn v. Fresenius* (Del. Ch. 2018) was the **first** Delaware case where a buyer successfully established an MAE, involving a dramatic deterioration in the target's business and regulatory compliance.

**Materiality Thresholds (Recent Case Law):**

- **20% Decline in Equity Value:** Vice Chancellor Laster suggested that a 20% decline in equity value would be sufficient to constitute an MAE, and courts have agreed that a reduction in equity value of 20% or more would be material
- **10-15% Decline:** A reduction of more than 15% **might** be material depending on context
- **Qualitative Factors:** Courts also consider whether adverse change affects long-term earnings, not merely short-term fluctuations

**Federal Rule 23(a) - Prerequisites to Class Action**
> "One or more members of a class may sue or be sued as representative parties on behalf of all members only if: (1) the class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable; (2) there are questions of law or fact common to the class; (3) the claims or defenses of the representative parties are typical of the claims or defenses of the class; and (4) the representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class."

**Federal Rule 23(b)(3) - Predominance and Superiority**
> "A class action may be maintained if Rule 23(a) is satisfied and if: ... (3) the court finds that the questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members, and that a class action is superior to other available methods for fairly and efficiently adjudicating the controversy."
