### Key Findings Digest

#### Finding 1: Bifurcated Regulatory Framework Requires Simultaneous Compliance with Three Overlapping Regimes

**Legal Framework:**

Variable annuities and variable life insurance are treated as **securities** under federal law (SEC v. Variable Annuity Life Ins. Co., 359 U.S. 65 (1959)), creating dual federal-state regulation despite the McCarran-Ferguson Act's general reservation of insurance regulation to states. LLIC must simultaneously comply with:

1. **Federal Securities Law:** Securities Act of 1933 (prospectus delivery before/within 5 days of sale), Investment Company Act of 1940 (separate account registration as unit investment trusts), SEC Regulation Best Interest
2. **FINRA Self-Regulation:** Rule 2330 (deferred variable annuities enhanced suitability + principal review within 7 days), Rule 2320 (variable contracts general requirements + non-cash compensation restrictions)
3. **State Insurance Regulation:** Nebraska product form approval, market conduct standards, illustration regulation, replacement form requirements

**Compliance Complexity - Three-Layer Suitability Standard:**

| Layer | Requirement Source | Specific Obligations for LLIC |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Layer 1: Reg BI** | SEC Regulation Best Interest (effective June 30, 2020) | Disclosure obligation, care obligation (reasonable diligence), conflict of interest obligation, compliance obligation |
| **Layer 2: FINRA Rule 2330** | FINRA (variable annuities specific) | Enhanced suitability factors (age, income, time horizon, liquidity needs, tax status); principal review and approval within 7 business days; training requirements |
| **Layer 3: State Suitability** | Nebraska market conduct standards | General insurance suitability principles; replacement form requirements; illustration balanced presentation |

**Risk Implication:** Overlapping obligations create potential for **amplified enforcement exposure** - same conduct can violate multiple regulatory frameworks simultaneously, resulting in parallel state and federal enforcement actions. Example: Variable annuity sale to 80-year-old customer without adequate suitability analysis could violate (a) FINRA Rule 2330 ($500K fine), (b) Nebraska market conduct standards ($50K fine), and (c) SEC Reg BI if conflicts of interest not disclosed ($1M fine) = **total $1.55M for single transaction pattern**.

#### Finding 3: GMWB Hedging Program Tail Risk ($45M-$75M) Amplified by LLIC's Weak RBC Capital Position

**GMWB Economic Risk Profile:**

Guaranteed Minimum Withdrawal Benefit riders on variable annuities create **embedded put option liability** for LLIC. Structure:

- **Benefit base:** Equals premium paid (e.g., $100,000)
- **Annual withdrawal guarantee:** 5% of benefit base for life ($5,000/year)
- **Account value:** Fluctuates with market (e.g., invested in S&P 500 subaccount)
- **Insurer's obligation:** If account value reaches $0 due to market decline + withdrawals, insurer must continue paying $5,000/year from general account until full $100,000 benefit base exhausted

**Tail Risk Scenario - 2008 Financial Crisis Analog:**

| Market Condition | Impact on GMWB Reserves | Hedge Program Performance | Net Loss to LLIC |
|------------------|-------------------------|---------------------------|------------------|
| **Equity decline -40%** | Account values drop 40%; policyholders persist (rational to extract guarantee) | Dynamic hedge 75-85% effective (industry norm) | **15-25% unhedged exposure** |
| **Interest rates 1-2%** | Present value of future payments increases (discount rate effect) | Interest rate swaps partially offset | **Residual duration mismatch** |
| **Counterparty default** | Lehman 2008 analog - hedge positions terminated | Must replace hedges at unfavorable prices | **Basis risk + replacement cost** |

**AG43/VM-21 Reserve Framework - CTE 70 vs. Tail Risk:**

NAIC Actuarial Guideline 43 requires variable annuity reserves calculated using Conditional Tail Expectation at 70th percentile (CTE 70) = average of worst 30% of stochastically generated scenarios. However:

- **CTE 70 understates true tail risk:** Focuses on 70th percentile, not worst 5-10% of scenarios
- **Prudent estimate margins:** AG43 requires margins for adverse deviation, but assumptions subject to management judgment
- **Hedge credit in reserves:** AG43 permits modeling of hedging in reserve calculations, but assumes hedge effectiveness that may not materialize in crisis

- **Finding:** 12 policyholders did not receive variable contract prospectuses before or within 5 days of application
- **Legal violation:** Securities Act of 1933 ¬ß 5(b)(2) - prospectus delivery required "prior to or at the time" security is delivered, or within 5 days for variable contracts (exception to general rule)
- **Root cause:** Vendor failure in prospectus delivery process
- **Remediation:** LLIC changed vendors in 2023
- **SEC disposition:** Deficiency letter issued (no formal enforcement action)

**Securities Act ¬ß 5 Prospectus Delivery Requirements:**

15 U.S.C. ¬ß 77e(b)(2) prohibits delivery of securities unless accompanied or preceded by a prospectus meeting ¬ß 77j(a) requirements. For variable contracts, SEC permits 5-day exception provided:

1. **Preliminary prospectus** or written communication describes product before application
2. **Final prospectus** delivered within **5 business days** after insurance company receives application
3. **"Free look" period** of at least 10 days allows customer to cancel without penalty

**Rescission Liability - Limited but Not Zero:**

Policyholders who did not receive prospectuses within 5 days have potential rescission rights under Securities Act ¬ß 12(a)(1) - right to rescind purchase and recover consideration paid. However:

- **"Free look" mitigates damages:** Since Nebraska requires 10-day free look (industry standard 10-30 days), delay of prospectus from day 0 to day 7-8 likely did not prevent informed decision given cancellation option
- **No SEC enforcement:** SEC's decision not to pursue formal enforcement suggests violations viewed as non-willful and limited in scope (12 policyholders)
- **Statute of limitations:** Securities Act ¬ß 13 imposes 1-year limitations period from discovery, 3 years from violation - most April 2022 violations now time-barred

### C. GMWB Living Benefit Riders Regulatory Framework

#### 1. GMWB Product Structure and Risk Profile

**Guaranteed Minimum Withdrawal Benefit Definition:**

A GMWB is a variable annuity living benefit rider that provides the contractholder with a guarantee that a minimum amount will be withdrawable each year until the benefit amount depletes or until a contractually specified event occurs, provided the contractholder does not exceed a maximum withdrawal amount in preceding years.‚Å¥¬π

**LLIC GMWB Portfolio [VERIFIED from research plan]:**

- **Variable Annuity Reserves:** $2.18 billion (Separate Account B)
- **GMWB Penetration:** 65% of contracts include GMWB riders
- **GMWB Reserves Estimate:** ~$1.42 billion ($2.18B √ó 65%)
- **Typical GMWB Structure:** 5% annual withdrawal guarantee for life regardless of market performance

**Economic Structure of GMWB:**

The GMWB creates an embedded put option for policyholders:

1. **Benefit Base:** Typically equals premium paid (may increase with additional deposits or step-ups based on market performance)
2. **Annual Withdrawal:** Policyholder can withdraw a guaranteed percentage (e.g., 5%) of the benefit base annually
3. **Account Value:** Actual investment value fluctuates with market performance
4. **Insurer's Risk:** If account value declines to zero due to poor market performance + withdrawals, insurer must continue funding withdrawals from its general account until benefit base is exhausted

**Example:**
- Customer deposits $100,000 premium
- Benefit base: $100,000
- Annual guaranteed withdrawal: $5,000 (5%)
- **Scenario 1 - Good Markets:** Account value grows to $120,000 over 10 years despite $50,000 withdrawals ‚Üí insurer profitable (M&E fees + GMWB rider fees exceed hedge costs)
- **Scenario 2 - Poor Markets:** Account value declines to $0 after 8 years of $5,000 withdrawals + market losses ‚Üí insurer must continue paying $5,000/year from general account for remaining 12 years until $100,000 benefit base exhausted ‚Üí **insurer loss**

**Tail Risk Characteristics:**

GMWB guarantees exhibit "fat tail" risk, meaning catastrophic losses occur with low probability but extreme severity.‚Å¥¬≤ The worst-case scenarios involve:

- **Equity market decline:** -40% to -60% drawdown (2008 financial crisis level)
- **Low interest rates:** 1-2% environment reduces investment income and increases present value of liabilities
- **Persistency:** Policyholders with underwater accounts (account value < benefit base) rationally persist to extract full guarantee value
- **Hedge program failure:** Dynamic hedging strategies imperfect or counterparties default

The research plan estimates tail risk exposure of **$45M-$75M** for LLIC's GMWB portfolio under stressed scenarios.‚Å¥¬≥

#### 2. NAIC Actuarial Guideline 43 (AG43) Reserve Requirements

**AG43 Background and Purpose:**

Actuarial Guideline XLIII (AG43) was adopted by the NAIC effective December 31, 2009, to establish reserve requirements for variable annuities with guaranteed benefits, including GMWB riders.‚Å¥‚Å¥ AG43 applies to all variable annuity contracts issued on or after January 1, 1981.

**Principle-Based Reserves (PBR) Methodology:**

AG43 requires reserves based on a **stochastic modeling approach** rather than traditional formula-based reserves. The methodology consists of two components:‚Å¥‚Åµ

1. **Standard Scenario Amount (SSA):** Minimum floor calculated using a prescribed deterministic scenario with specified equity returns, interest rates, and policyholder behavior assumptions

2. **Conditional Tail Expectation Amount (CTE):** The average of the worst-case scenarios from a stochastically generated range of economic scenarios (typically CTE 70, meaning the average of the worst 30% of scenarios)

**Total AG43 Reserve:** The greater of SSA or CTE amount

**Stochastic Scenario Requirements:**

Insurers must project assets and liabilities supporting variable annuity contracts over a broad range of scenarios (typically 1,000-10,000 stochastically generated scenarios) using:‚Å¥‚Å∂

- **Equity return scenarios:** Varying annual returns with realistic volatility
- **Interest rate scenarios:** Yield curve movements (increases, decreases, inversions)
- **Policyholder behavior:** Lapse rates, withdrawal rates, annuitization elections (must vary by scenario based on "in-the-money" status)
- **Hedge program:** Modeling of dynamic hedging effectiveness

**Prudent Estimate Assumptions:**

AG43 requires "prudent estimate" assumptions rather than best estimate. This means assumptions must include margins for adverse deviation, particularly for:

- **Mortality:** Lower mortality increases cost of lifetime GMWB guarantees
- **Lapse rates:** Lower lapse rates when contracts are in-the-money increase cost
- **Volatility:** Higher equity volatility increases option value
- **Hedge effectiveness:** Assume hedging is less than 100% effective

**VM-21 (Valuation Manual) Integration:**

AG43 was effectively incorporated into VM-21 (part of the NAIC Valuation Manual) adopted December 2, 2012, effective for contracts issued January 1, 2017.‚Å¥‚Å∑ For LLIC:

- **Pre-2017 contracts:** Subject to AG43
- **Post-2017 contracts:** Subject to VM-21 (which largely replicates AG43 with minor refinements)

**Reporting Requirements - PBR Actuarial Report:**

All companies with variable annuity business subject to AG43/VM-21 must submit a **PBR Actuarial Report** per VM-31, including the AG43/VM-21 Analysis. All submissions are due no later than **April 1st, 2025** for year-end 2024 reporting.‚Å¥‚Å∏

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act brought comprehensive reform to swap regulation, subjecting previously unregulated over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives to CFTC and SEC oversight.‚Åµ¬π

**Key Dodd-Frank Requirements:**

1. **Swap Dealer Registration:** Entities engaging in substantial swap dealing activity must register as swap dealers with the CFTC‚Åµ¬≤
2. **Mandatory Clearing:** Certain standardized swaps must be cleared through derivatives clearing organizations (DCOs)‚Åµ¬≥
3. **Margin Requirements:** Swap dealers and major swap participants must post initial and variation margin‚Åµ‚Å¥
4. **Trade Reporting:** All swaps must be reported to swap data repositories (SDRs)‚Åµ‚Åµ

**End-User Exception for Insurance Companies:**

The clearing requirement does not apply if one counterparty is using swaps to **hedge or mitigate commercial risk** and notifies the CFTC.‚Åµ‚Å∂ Life insurers using derivatives to hedge GMWB liabilities generally qualify for the end-user exception because:

- **Commercial risk hedging:** GMWB hedging directly offsets insurance liability risk (not speculative trading)
- **Not a financial entity:** While life insurers are regulated financial institutions, they are not "financial entities" as defined by Dodd-Frank for this purpose‚Åµ‚Å∑

**Practical Implications for LLIC:**

1. **No mandatory clearing:** LLIC can continue using bilateral OTC swaps with dealer counterparties (e.g., Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Bank of America) without clearing through a DCO

2. **Margin posting:** While not subject to mandatory clearing, LLIC likely posts collateral to swap dealer counterparties under Credit Support Annexes (CSAs) to bilateral ISDA Master Agreements

3. **Trade reporting:** LLIC must report swap transactions to SDRs, though the swap dealer counterparty typically handles reporting on behalf of both parties

4. **CFTC examination authority:** The CFTC has examination authority over end-users' use of the exception to ensure swaps are genuinely hedging commercial risk‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Counterparty Credit Risk:**

GMWB hedging creates **bilateral counterparty credit risk**:

- **LLIC's risk to dealers:** If equity markets crash and LLIC's puts are deeply in-the-money, dealer default could leave LLIC unhedged in a tail scenario
- **Dealers' risk to LLIC:** If LLIC owes variation margin or termination payments, LLIC's credit rating and capital adequacy matter to dealers

During the 2008 financial crisis, some life insurers faced hedge program failures when:
- **Counterparty defaults:** Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy caused hedge positions to be terminated
- **Collateral calls:** Insurers had to post massive collateral on out-of-the-money positions, straining liquidity
- **Basis risk:** Hedges did not perfectly offset liabilities due to policyholder behavior assumptions

**Due Diligence Recommendations for Hedging Program:**

American Financial Holdings should:

1. **Review ISDA Master Agreements** with all swap counterparties - what are termination events, collateral thresholds, and netting provisions?

2. **Assess hedge effectiveness testing** - does LLIC perform regular hedge effectiveness testing per accounting standards (ASC 815/IFRS 9)? What is historical effectiveness (80-125% range is typical threshold)?

3. **Evaluate counterparty diversification** - is LLIC overly concentrated with one or two dealers? Investment-grade rating requirements for counterparties?

4. **Model validation** - has LLIC's hedge model been independently validated? Are assumptions for policyholder behavior (lapses, withdrawals) calibrated to actual experience?

5. **Tail scenario stress testing** - what is LLIC's estimated loss in a 2008-style crisis (40% equity drop + low rates + counterparty downgrade)? This aligns with the research plan's $45M-$75M tail risk estimate.‚Åµ‚Åπ

**VUL Economic Characteristics:**

Variable universal life combines flexible premium universal life insurance with variable subaccount investment options. Key features:

- **Premium flexibility:** Unlike traditional whole life, policyholders can vary premium payments

- **Lapse risk:** If account value insufficient to cover monthly charges, policy lapses unless policyholder pays additional premium

#### 2. Illustration Regulation - NAIC Model and AG49

**NAIC Life Insurance Illustration Model Regulation:**

The NAIC adopted the Life Insurance Illustration Model Regulation (Model #582) to regulate illustrated policy performance projections shown to prospective purchasers.‚Å∂‚Å∂ The regulation distinguishes between:

- **Guaranteed elements:** Maximum mortality charges, minimum interest crediting rates specified in policy
- **Non-guaranteed elements:** Current mortality charges, current interest crediting rates, future changes at insurer discretion

**Illustration Requirements:**

All illustrations must include:‚Å∂‚Å∑
- **Guaranteed column:** Showing policy performance using guaranteed elements only (worst-case scenario)
- **Non-guaranteed column:** Showing policy performance using current non-guaranteed elements
- **Disclosure:** Prominent statement that non-guaranteed values are not guaranteed and actual results may be more or less favorable

**AG49 - Indexed Universal Life Illustration Limitation:**

Actuarial Guideline 49 (AG49), adopted in 2015 and revised as AG49-A in 2020, regulates indexed universal life (IUL) illustrations to prevent unrealistic projections.‚Å∂‚Å∏ However, **AG49 does NOT apply to variable universal life** because VUL subaccounts are actual securities (mutual funds) rather than indexed crediting strategies.‚Å∂‚Åπ

**VUL Illustration Standards:**

For VUL illustrations, SEC and state regulation require:‚Å∑‚Å∞

- **Hypothetical returns:** Illustrations may show hypothetical subaccount returns (e.g., 0%, 6%, 12% annual returns)
- **Volatility disclosure:** Must disclose that actual returns will vary and include periods of negative returns
- **Prospectus delivery:** Illustration is not a substitute for prospectus; prospectus must be delivered before or within 5 days of application
- **Balanced presentation:** Cannot show only optimistic scenarios; must include balanced range of return assumptions

### E. SEC-State Regulatory Coordination & Enforcement Risk

#### 1. McCarran-Ferguson Act Framework - Federal-State Regulatory Interface

**McCarran-Ferguson Act Background:**

The McCarran-Ferguson Act, 15 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 1011-1015, enacted in 1945, provides that:‚Å∑‚Å¥

> "No Act of Congress shall be construed to invalidate, impair, or supersede any law enacted by any State for the purpose of regulating the business of insurance... unless such Act specifically relates to the business of insurance."

This creates the principle of **state insurance regulation primacy** with federal law applying only when it specifically addresses insurance.

**Supreme Court Variable Annuity Exception - SEC v. Variable Annuity Life:**

In SEC v. Variable Annuity Life Ins. Co., 359 U.S. 65 (1959), the Supreme Court held that variable annuities are **securities** subject to federal securities regulation because they:‚Å∑‚Åµ

- Place investment risk entirely on the annuitant (not on the insurer)
- Do not guarantee fixed dollar benefits (unlike traditional annuities)
- Are functionally equivalent to mutual fund investments with death benefits

This established that the Securities Act of 1933, Investment Company Act of 1940, and Securities Exchange Act of 1934 apply to variable insurance products despite McCarran-Ferguson.

**Bifurcated Regulatory Framework - LLIC's Dual Compliance Burden:**
