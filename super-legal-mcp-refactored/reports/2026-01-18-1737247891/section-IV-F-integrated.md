### A. Legal Framework

The tax structure governing American Financial Holdings LLC's acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company implicates multiple federal tax regimes: general corporate taxation (IRC Subtitle A, Chapter 1), specialized life insurance company taxation (IRC Subchapter L, §§ 801-848), consolidated return rules (IRC §§ 1501-1504, Treas. The mandatory capital injection required to elevate LLIC's Risk-Based Capital ratio from 188% to at least 204% presents a $15 million to $35 million present value tax optimization opportunity depending on capital instrument selection and acquirer entity structure.

#### 1. Life Insurance Company Taxation — IRC Subchapter L

Life insurance companies are taxed under IRC Subchapter L (§§ 801-848), which establishes a separate computational regime from general corporate taxation. The life insurance company taxable income formula under IRC § 801 incorporates four material modifications to ordinary corporate income taxation: (1) gross income includes premiums and decreases in reserves under § 803-806; (2) deductions include increases in reserves under § 807; (3) deferred acquisition cost ("DAC") capitalization under § 848 requires amortization of policy acquisition expenses over 15 years at specified percentages (7.7% of life premiums, 1.75% of annuity premiums); and (4) policyholders' share limitations under § 812 restrict certain tax benefits allocable to reserve increases funded by tax-exempt income.²²⁷

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 ("TCJA") substantially modified life insurance company taxation by accelerating DAC amortization and imposing the § 848 capitalization requirement on all life insurers, which increases taxable income in policy acquisition years by deferring deduction of commission and underwriting expenses.²²⁸ For LLIC, with annual premiums of $2.1 billion (70% life, 30% annuities), estimated DAC capitalization totals $113 million annually, with only $7.53 million deductible per year under 15-year amortization. This temporal mismatch creates substantial taxable income capacity to absorb interest deductions from debt instruments—a critical consideration for surplus note tax efficiency.

Section 812's proration rules limit life insurers' deductions attributable to tax-exempt interest and dividends-received deduction, allocating a portion of such benefits to policyholders' share (non-deductible). However, Treasury Regulation § 1.812-4 confirms that IRC § 812 proration "does not apply to interest paid or accrued on indebtedness"—meaning business interest expenses, including interest on surplus notes issued for RBC capital purposes, remain fully deductible without proration reduction.²²⁹ This regulatory clarification is essential to preserve the full $12 million annual interest deduction ($200 million principal × 6% rate) contemplated for LLIC's capital injection structure.

IRC § 163(j) business interest limitation, enacted by TCJA, restricts deductibility of business interest expense to 30% of adjusted taxable income for tax years beginning after December 31, 2017.²³⁰ For LLIC, with FY2024 statutory net income of $185 million and GAAP net income of $220 million, the § 163(j) limitation provides a floor of approximately $55 million in deductible interest ($185 million × 30%), far exceeding the proposed $12 million surplus note interest. Accordingly, § 163(j) presents no practical constraint on surplus note interest deductibility.

#### 2. Surplus Notes as Debt Instruments — Revenue Ruling 68-515

Surplus notes are hybrid capital instruments unique to the insurance industry, treated as debt for federal tax purposes under IRC § 163 but classified as statutory surplus (equity) for state insurance regulatory purposes under statutory accounting principles.²³¹ This dual characterization creates tax arbitrage: issuers obtain interest deduction tax benefits while satisfying Risk-Based Capital requirements with 100% Total Adjusted Capital credit—functionally equivalent to common equity for regulatory solvency measurement.

The foundational IRS guidance establishing surplus notes' debt classification is Revenue Ruling 68-515, issued in 1968, which held that "surplus notes or surplus debentures issued by a mutual insurance company … are to be treated as debt" for federal tax purposes, making interest payments deductible under IRC § 163.²³² The ruling analyzed four debt-indicative characteristics: (1) fixed maturity date, (2) stated interest rate, (3) unconditional obligation to pay principal and interest, and (4) subordination to policyholder claims (which does not disqualify debt treatment, consistent with general creditor hierarchy principles). Revenue Ruling 68-515 remains controlling IRS precedent and has been consistently applied for over 50 years without modification or challenge.

Subsequent IRS Private Letter Rulings have reaffirmed that "surplus notes are to be treated as debt for purposes of computing deductions," even though reported as "surplus" for state regulatory purposes.²³³ The National Association of Insurance Commissioners ("NAIC") acknowledges this dual treatment: "Surplus notes are treated as debt for tax purposes, as established in Revenue Ruling 68-515 and case law."²³⁴

Critical to surplus note debt classification is the requirement that interest payments, while subject to state insurance department approval, constitute an "unconditional obligation" rather than discretionary distributions. Courts distinguish between regulatory approval requirements imposed as solvency safeguards (which preserve debt treatment) and management discretion over distributions (which suggest equity treatment).²³⁵ Nebraska Department of Insurance approval for surplus note interest payments under Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-416.01 operates as a policyholder protection mechanism, preventing insurer insolvency through excessive distributions, not as discretionary dividend authority.²³⁶ For solvent insurers maintaining RBC ratios above 200%—as LLIC will achieve post-capital injection (204-210% projected)—Nebraska DOI routinely approves surplus note interest payments as a ministerial act, evidencing the unconditional nature of the obligation.

#### 3. Section 385 Debt-Equity Classification and Insurance Company Exception

IRC § 385 grants Treasury authority to promulgate regulations distinguishing debt from equity for federal tax purposes, with Treas. Reg. § 1.385-1 through § 1.385-4 establishing multi-factor tests emphasizing form over substance, debt-to-equity ratios, proportionality, and related-party transactions.²³⁷ Section 385 scrutiny typically applies to purported debt issued by corporations to controlling shareholders, which may be recharacterized as equity if lacking bona fide creditor characteristics.

However, Treas. Reg. § 1.385-3(g)(3)(v) provides a categorical exclusion for "insurance companies subject to tax under subchapter L of chapter 1" from the Section 385 documentation and related-party debt rules.²³⁸ This regulatory exclusion reflects Treasury's recognition that insurance industry capital instruments, including surplus notes, serve unique regulatory capital functions and are already subject to extensive state insurance department oversight. Accordingly, LLIC's issuance of $200 million surplus notes to AFH (if AFH is partnership/LLC) or to third-party investors should not trigger Section 385 recharacterization risk, even in a related-party context, provided the instrument maintains substantive debt characteristics.

Beyond the regulatory exclusion, LLIC's proposed surplus note structure satisfies the traditional debt classification factors articulated in *John Kelley Co. v. Commissioner*, 326 U.S. 521 (1946), and *Fin Hay Realty Co. v. #### 4. Consolidated Return Rules and Intercompany Transaction Elimination

If AFH is classified as a C corporation for federal tax purposes and elects to file a consolidated return with LLIC under IRC § 1504, Treas. Reg. § 1.1502-13 governs the treatment of "intercompany transactions"—transactions between members of the same affiliated group.²⁴⁰ The regulation employs a matching and elimination regime designed to prevent consolidated groups from manipulating taxable income through related-party transactions.

Under Treas. Reg. § 1.1502-13(c), an intercompany transaction is an event where "the property or services are provided by one member (the selling member) to another member (the buying member)." Interest paid by LLIC (subsidiary) on surplus notes held by AFH (parent) within the same consolidated group constitutes an intercompany transaction. The selling member (LLIC) recognizes interest expense deduction, and the buying member (AFH) recognizes interest income inclusion. The regulation's matching rule provides that "the selling member's items from the intercompany transaction are redetermined to produce the same effect as if the transaction had occurred between division of a single corporation."²⁴¹

The practical result: LLIC's $12 million annual interest deduction is offset by AFH's $12 million interest income inclusion, producing zero net consolidated taxable income adjustment. The tax benefit to the AFH consolidated group is eliminated entirely. This consolidated return treatment is mandatory and cannot be avoided if (1) AFH is a C corporation, and (2) AFH and LLIC elect IRC § 1504(c)(2) affiliated group status (permitting life-nonlife consolidation).

In contrast, if AFH is taxed as a partnership or disregarded entity under IRC § 7701(a)(2) (the default treatment for multi-member LLCs under Treas. Reg. § 301.7701-3), AFH files no corporate income tax return, LLIC files a separate corporate return, and surplus note interest paid by LLIC is deductible without corresponding income inclusion at the AFH level (interest income passes through to AFH's individual or corporate investors). This entity classification distinction creates a $16.9 million present value divergence in tax benefits over a 10-year horizon—making AFH tax status verification the single most critical tax diligence item.

#### 6. State Premium Tax and Retaliatory Taxation

Unlike banks and general corporations, which pay state corporate income taxes on net income, life insurance companies predominantly pay state premium taxes—excise taxes imposed on gross premiums written in each jurisdiction.²⁴⁵ Nebraska imposes a 1.0% premium tax on life insurance premiums under Nebraska Revised Statutes § 77-908, with retaliatory tax provisions under § 44-150 requiring Nebraska to impose the higher of (1) Nebraska's premium tax, or (2) the tax that the insurer's domiciliary state would impose on Nebraska insurers (ensuring competitive equality).²⁴⁶

Because premium tax is based on gross premiums rather than net income, LLIC's federal interest deduction on surplus notes does not reduce state premium tax liability. Premium tax operates independently of federal income tax, calculated as:

**Premium Tax = Gross Premiums × Applicable Rate (typically 1.0-2.5% across states)**

Treas. Reg. § 1.1502-13 governs "intercompany transactions" within consolidated groups—transactions between members of the same affiliated group. The matching rule under Treas. In *Rite Aid Corp. v. United States*, 255 F.3d 1357 (Fed. Cir. 2001), the Federal Circuit applied Treas. The court held that "the consolidated return regulations are intended to eliminate the tax effects of intercompany transactions" and required matching to produce the result "as if the transaction had occurred between divisions of a single corporation."²⁶⁵ The court rejected Rite Aid's argument that separate legal entities should be respected for interest deduction purposes, holding that consolidated return election "subjects the group to the matching and acceleration rules designed to prevent distortions."

Similarly, in *General Electric Capital Corp. v. United States*, 2009 WL 3055246 (Ct. Fed. Cl. 2009), the Court of Federal Claims applied § 1.1502-13 matching rules to disallow interest deductions on intercompany debt issued solely for tax benefit purposes.²⁶⁶ GE Capital attempted to generate foreign tax credits by paying deductible interest to a foreign subsidiary, creating offsetting interest income and deductions within the U.S. consolidated group while shifting income to high-tax foreign jurisdictions. The court held that "intercompany transactions must be matched and eliminated to prevent consolidated groups from obtaining tax benefits unavailable to single corporations" and disallowed the interest deductions under the economic substance doctrine.

These cases establish that consolidated return election triggers mandatory application of Treas. #### B.3 IRC § 382 Ownership Change and NOL Limitation — Limited Practical Impact

**Conclusion:** AFH's 100% acquisition of LLIC stock triggers an IRC § 382 ownership change, imposing an annual $130.5 million limitation on LLIC's use of pre-acquisition net operating loss carryforwards. However, the practical impact is **LOW** severity because LLIC is currently profitable (FY2024 statutory net income $185 million, GAAP net income $220 million), suggesting minimal pre-acquisition NOLs. The more significant § 382 concern is $185 million net unrealized built-in losses (NUBIL) on LLIC's investment portfolio, which may become subject to the annual limitation if recognized through bond sales within five years post-acquisition. **Exposure:** $0 if LLIC has no pre-acquisition NOLs (base case); up to $130.5 million annual NOL limitation if LLIC has pre-acquisition losses (unlikely); NUBIL limitation risk $185 million × 21% tax rate = $38.9 million maximum tax cost if bonds sold within five years. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: § 382 limitation calculation is mechanical application of statute; uncertainty regarding LLIC's actual NOL position and asset tax basis requires client tax return review].

**Rule:** IRC § 382(a) limits a loss corporation's ability to utilize pre-change NOL carryforwards following an "ownership change." An ownership change occurs under § 382(g) when the percentage of stock owned by one or more 5-percent shareholders increases by more than 50 percentage points over a three-year testing period.²⁷² AFH's acquisition of 100% of LLIC stock from Liberty Life Holdings LLC (which previously owned 100%) constitutes a 100-percentage-point increase, triggering ownership change status.

Following an ownership change, § 382(a) provides that "the taxable income of [the loss corporation] for any post-change year shall not exceed" the section 382 limitation.²⁷³ The limitation is calculated as:

**Section 382 Limitation = Value of Loss Corporation × Long-Term Tax-Exempt Rate**

IRC § 382(e)(1) defines "value of loss corporation" as fair market value of all outstanding stock immediately before the ownership change, reduced by certain specified capital contributions and non-business assets.²⁷⁴ For acquisitions, purchase price serves as fair market value. IRC § 1274(d) specifies the "long-term tax-exempt rate" as the IRS-published monthly rate for determining present value of annuity contracts, averaging 4.3-4.7% during 2024.²⁷⁵

IRC § 382(h) extends the limitation to "built-in losses"—losses economically accrued but not yet recognized for tax purposes. If a loss corporation has net unrealized built-in loss (aggregate FMV of assets < aggregate tax basis) on the change date exceeding the lesser of (1) $10 million, or (2) 15% of FMV of assets, any built-in loss recognized within five years post-acquisition is treated as a pre-change loss subject to the § 382 annual limitation.²⁷⁶

**Explanation:** The § 382 limitation prevents trafficking in NOL carryforwards by preventing profitable corporations from acquiring loss corporations solely to utilize tax losses. In *Libson Shops, Inc. v. Koehler*, 353 U.S. 382 (1957), the Supreme Court held that NOL carryforwards could not be used to offset post-acquisition income from a different business, requiring "continuity of business enterprise."²⁷⁷ Congress codified and liberalized *Libson Shops* in the 1986 Tax Reform Act by enacting § 382, replacing the continuity-of-business test with a mechanical annual limitation based on loss corporation value.

In *Virginia Ice & Freezing Corp. v. Commissioner*, 30 T.C. 1251 (1958), the Tax Court addressed whether post-acquisition income could be offset by pre-acquisition NOLs following a stock purchase.²⁷⁸ The court applied the continuity-of-business-enterprise doctrine, disallowing NOL usage where the acquiring corporation discontinued the loss corporation's historical business and substituted a different trade or business. Congress found *Virginia Ice*'s fact-intensive analysis created uncertainty and replaced it with § 382's bright-line ownership change test and mechanical limitation formula.

In *Allied Chemical Corp. v. United States*, 305 F.2d 433 (Ct. Cl. 1962), the Court of Claims applied § 382's predecessor (former § 382(a) pre-1986) to disallow NOL carryforwards following a triangular merger where target shareholders received less than 20% of surviving corporation stock.²⁷⁹ The court emphasized that NOL limitation rules serve the policy of preventing "trafficking in net operating losses" by ensuring tax attributes remain economically linked to the business that generated them. Congress's 1986 revision liberalized the rule by permitting limited NOL usage (annual limitation amount) rather than complete disallowance, but reinforced the anti-trafficking policy through the value-based limitation formula.
