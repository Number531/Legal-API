## A. Legal Framework

### 1. NAIC Risk-Based Capital System

The NAIC Risk-Based Capital (RBC) for Insurers Model Act establishes a minimum capital framework for life insurance companies based on four risk categories: C1 (asset risk), C2 (insurance risk), C3 (interest rate risk), and C4 (business risk).¹ The system uses a formulaic approach to calculate the Authorized Control Level (ACL), representing the minimum capital threshold below which regulators may seize the insurer.² The RBC ratio compares an insurer's Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) to the ACL, with regulatory intervention triggered at specified action levels.³

| Action Level | RBC Ratio Threshold | Regulatory Response | Legal Authority |
|--------------|---------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Company Action Level (CAL)** | 200% | Insurer must file RBC Plan identifying conditions causing deficiency and proposing corrective actions | NAIC Model Act § 3(B)(2) |
| **Regulatory Action Level (RAL)** | 150% | Commissioner issues Corrective Order requiring specific actions within specified timeframes | NAIC Model Act § 3(B)(3) |
| **Authorized Control Level (ACL)** | 100% | Commissioner authorized (but not required) to place insurer under regulatory control | NAIC Model Act § 3(B)(4) |
| **Mandatory Control Level (MCL)** | 70% | Commissioner must place insurer under regulatory control | NAIC Model Act § 3(B)(1) |

Under Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-6008, Nebraska adopted the NAIC RBC framework with the same action level triggers.⁵ The Nebraska statute incorporates the NAIC's annual RBC instructions by reference, requiring insurers to calculate and report RBC ratios using standardized formulas updated annually.⁶

**Total Adjusted Capital (TAC)** consists of statutory capital and surplus plus certain supplementary items, reduced by non-admitted assets and other regulatory adjustments.⁷ TAC is calculated using Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP), which differ materially from GAAP through more conservative treatment of assets (non-admission of certain intangibles and deferred acquisition costs) and liabilities (stronger reserve requirements).⁸ Liberty Life's TAC of $1.85B reflects these SAP adjustments and represents the capital available to absorb losses under regulatory accounting.⁹

### 2. Statutory Accounting Principles

Statutory Accounting Principles govern insurance company financial reporting to state regulators and differ fundamentally from Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) used for investor reporting. SAP prioritizes **policyholder protection** over matching revenues and expenses, resulting in more conservative capital measurements.

**Key SAP vs. GAAP Differences:**

| Accounting Item | SAP Treatment | GAAP Treatment | Capital Impact |
|----------------|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| Deferred Acquisition Costs (DAC) | Expensed immediately | Capitalized and amortized | SAP reduces surplus by $250M-$350M |
| Furniture & Equipment | Non-admitted (excluded from assets) | Capitalized at depreciated value | SAP reduces assets by $15M-$25M |
| Certain Intangibles | Non-admitted unless specifically approved | Recognized at fair value | Variable by asset type |
| Investment Reserves | Asset Valuation Reserve (AVR) required | Fair value through OCI | SAP reduces surplus by $50M-$100M |
| Deferred Tax Assets | Limited admission (35% cap) | Fully recognized if realizable | SAP reduces assets by $30M-$50M |

The practical effect of SAP's conservatism is that Liberty Life reported $1.85B statutory surplus (SAP) versus $2.35B shareholders' equity (GAAP) for FY2024—a $500M difference.⁹ This $500M gap represents regulatory "cushion" that absorbs losses before impacting GAAP equity but cannot be counted toward RBC capital requirements.

**Non-Admitted Assets** are excluded from TAC calculations despite economic value. Under NAIC SSAP No. 4, non-admitted assets include: uncollectible premium receivables (>90 days overdue), certain deferred tax assets exceeding limitations, unapproved software, and furniture/fixtures/equipment.⁸ Liberty Life's $175M in non-admitted assets (primarily DAC-related) are excluded from the $1.85B TAC calculation despite appearing on the balance sheet.

NAIC Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48), adopted in 2015 to address "shadow insurance" concerns, establishes minimum security requirements for captive reinsurance of policies with guaranteed living benefits or term life insurance:³⁶

1. **Primary Security:** Cash, U.S. Treasuries, or other highly liquid instruments posted to secure at least the statutory reserves³⁶
2. **Other Security:** Parental guarantees or letters of credit may substitute for Primary Security only if the guarantor has net worth at least 2-3 times the guaranteed amount and is independently creditworthy³⁷

The Nebraska Department of Insurance has historically applied a **2-3× net worth coverage requirement** for parental guarantees supporting captive reserve credit, based on 2015 guidance addressing captive adequacy concerns.³⁸ This standard emerged from federal and state regulatory concerns about "shadow insurance"—the practice of ceding substantial reserves to thinly capitalized offshore captives backed by parental guarantees that provide illusory protection.³⁹,⁴⁰

AG48's purpose is "addressing regulatory concerns about captive reinsurance structures that provide reserve relief without transferring meaningful risk," particularly structures where captive assets are inadequate and parental guarantees rest on circular ownership.⁴¹

**Explanation:** Nebraska precedent demonstrates strict enforcement of captive collateralization requirements in change-of-control contexts. Similarly, in *In re American Republic Insurance Co.*, NE DOI Order No. 2019-03, the Nebraska DOI disallowed reserve credit for a Cayman Islands captive where the parental guarantor's net worth was $115M supporting $185M of ceded reserves (0.62× coverage).⁴³ The DOI stated: "A guarantee from an entity whose net worth is less than the guaranteed amount provides no meaningful protection and defeats the purpose of the reserve adequacy requirements."

**Counter-Analysis:** Liberty Life may argue that the Vermont captive structure was approved by Vermont DFR and has been in place for 7+ years without Nebraska DOI objection, creating "grandfathering" protection under AG48 ¶ 7.⁵² Additionally, Liberty Life might contend that the Nebraska DOI lacks authority to retroactively impose enhanced collateralization requirements on existing structures.

**Rule:** Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-2127(2) authorizes the Nebraska DOI Commissioner to approve, conditionally approve, or disapprove a Form A application based on the statutory criteria analyzed above (financial condition, trustworthiness, competence, acquisition plans, financial soundness).¹³ The statute grants the Commissioner discretion to "impose such conditions as are reasonable and necessary for the protection of policyholders."

Nebraska precedent demonstrates that "conditional approval" for private equity acquisitions of life insurers routinely includes:⁵⁵

1. **Enhanced Capital Requirements:** Capital injections 20-40% above RBC Plan minimum
2. **Extended Dividend Restrictions:** 36-60 month restrictions on extraordinary dividends
3. **Management Retention:** 3-5 year employment agreements for CEO, CFO, Chief Actuary

5. **Investment Restrictions:** Limitations on below-investment-grade allocations, private equity, derivatives
6. **Related-Party Transaction Restrictions:** Prior approval required for material transactions with AFH affiliates

**Explanation:** The NAIC Financial Analysis Handbook establishes guidelines for Form A review timelines, noting that median approval time for complex multi-state life insurance acquisitions is 9-12 months from initial filing to final multi-state clearance.⁵⁶ For private equity acquirers, the timeline is typically at the upper end due to enhanced scrutiny of source of funds, business plans, and exit strategies.⁵⁷

Nebraska DOI practice involves multi-stage review:⁵⁸

1. **Preliminary Completeness Review (15-30 days):** Initial review of Form A application for completeness; information requests for deficiencies
2. **Financial Analysis (45-60 days):** Actuarial review of pro forma projections, stress testing, RBC sensitivity analysis
3. **Background Investigation (30-45 days):** FBI background checks, prior regulatory inquiries, litigation searches for all key individuals
4. **Public Comment Period (30 days):** Publication of application summary; opportunity for public comment
5. **Commissioner Review and Decision (30-60 days):** Final deliberations; drafting of approval order with conditions

Nebraska DOI precedent demonstrates that acquisitions involving RBC-deficient insurers receive heightened scrutiny and almost uniformly result in conditional rather than unconditional approval. ### E.3 Deal Structure Recommendations

**1. Three-Tier Capital Injection Structure**

To balance regulatory approval certainty with transaction economics, structure the RBC capital injection in three tiers:

| Tier | Amount | Timing | Instrument | Regulatory Purpose |
|------|--------|--------|------------|-------------------|
| **Tier 1: Committed** | $150M | At Closing | Surplus notes (6%, 30-year) | Satisfies RBC Plan minimum; achieves 204% RBC ratio |
| **Tier 2: Conditional** | $70M | Closing + 90 days | Surplus notes (triggered if NE DOI conditions require) | Addresses potential DOI enhanced capital requirement ($220M total) |
| **Tier 3: Vermont Contingent** | $150M | Activated if captive recapture | Equity or surplus notes (at AFH election) | Protects against economic recapture scenario requiring additional buffer |

**Total Maximum Commitment:** $370M (covers moderate scenarios through Tier 2; Scenario C full captive disallowance would require additional capital beyond this structure)

**Rationale:** Tier structure provides regulatory certainty (Tier 1 committed) while managing AFH's capital deployment risk (Tiers 2-3 conditional).

**2. Purchase Price Adjustment for Excess Capital**

Implement symmetric purchase price adjustment mechanism:

- **Baseline Capital Assumption:** $220M (upper RBC Plan scenario)
- **Adjustment Mechanism:** For every dollar of regulatory capital required above $220M (up to $300M threshold), purchase price reduces by $1.00
- **Example:** If Nebraska DOI requires $275M total capital, purchase price reduces by $55M ($275M - $220M)
- **Cap:** Adjustment limited to $80M ($300M capital requirement); above $300M triggers Material Adverse Regulatory Condition walk-away right

**Rationale:** Shares regulatory capital risk between parties; Seller retains upside if capital requirements below $220M; AFH protected if requirements exceed baseline assumption.

**3. Vermont Captive Remediation Options**

Provide Seller with three remediation options to address Vermont captive risk pre-Closing:

| Option | Mechanism | Seller Cost | Impact on Purchase Price | AFH Risk Reduction |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-------------------------|-------------------|
| **A: Recapitalize Holdings** | Seller injects $650M equity into Liberty Life Holdings to achieve 2.0× net worth coverage ($930M net worth ÷ $730M guarantee) | $650M | None (Seller bears cost) | Eliminates 10-15% disallowance risk |
| **B: Replace Guarantee** | Seller obtains third-party LOC for $730M from AA-rated bank replacing parental guarantee | $18M-$22M annual fee | Seller seeks $100M price increase to offset 5-year NPV cost | Reduces disallowance risk to <5% |
| **C: Economic Recapture** | Seller voluntarily recaptures $200M-$300M reserves from Vermont captive to LLIC statutory books pre-Closing | $0 cash cost (accounting change) | $200M-$300M reduction (reduced deal value due to lower LLIC surplus) | Partially mitigates risk but doesn't eliminate it |

**Recommendation:** Structure SPA to give Seller 60 days post-signing to elect Option A or B; if Seller does not remediate, proceed with Vermont Captive Escrow structure ($150M escrow with 72-month release) and purchase price adjustment reflecting captive risk.

**4. Form A Closing Date Flexibility**

Build regulatory approval timeline flexibility into Outside Date provisions:

- **Initial Outside Date:** 9 months after SPA execution (Q3 2025 if SPA signed in Q4 2024 or Q1 2025)
- **Automatic Extension 1:** Additional 60 days if Form A applications filed but not yet approved
- **Automatic Extension 2:** Additional 30 days if Nebraska DOI issues conditional approval requiring pre-closing remediation steps (e.g., Thompson settlement, Vermont captive confirmation)
- **Break-Up Fee:** If transaction terminates due to regulatory disapproval (not Material Adverse Regulatory Condition), no break-up fee; if Buyer walks based on Material Adverse Regulatory Condition, Buyer pays Seller $25M break-up fee; if Seller walks due to regulatory approval delay, Seller pays Buyer $40M reverse break-up fee

**Rationale:** Aligns incentives for both parties to cooperate in regulatory approval process while protecting AFH from extended regulatory review consuming deal economics.
