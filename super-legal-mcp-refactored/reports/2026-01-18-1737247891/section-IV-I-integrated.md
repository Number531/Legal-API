### A. Legal Framework

The acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) by American Financial Holdings (AFH) implicates multiple categories of insurance coverage critical to protecting the acquirer from transaction-related liabilities. This analysis addresses four primary insurance domains: (1) Directors and Officers (D&O) liability insurance for wrongful acts by LLIC's management, (2) Errors and Omissions (E&O) professional liability coverage for agent misconduct and operational errors, (3) Representations & Warranties (R&W) insurance to backstop seller representations in the purchase agreement, and (4) tail policy coverage providing run-off protection for pre-closing claims.

#### 1. Directors & Officers Liability Insurance — Legal Structure

D&O liability insurance policies universally employ a claims-made trigger, providing coverage only if the claim is first made and reported during the policy period or applicable extended reporting period.⁴⁹¹ This differs fundamentally from occurrence-based policies (such as commercial general liability insurance), where coverage attaches when the underlying wrongful act occurs, regardless of when the claim is made.⁴⁹² The claims-made structure creates critical timing considerations for long-tail liabilities arising from pre-closing conduct that may not manifest as formal claims until years after closing.

Standard D&O policies for insurance companies consist of three distinct coverage components, commonly referred to as Side A, Side B, and Side C coverage:

**Side A Coverage** provides individual director and officer protection when the company cannot or will not indemnify the insured person.⁴⁹³ This coverage applies in three primary scenarios: (1) corporate bankruptcy or insolvency preventing indemnification, (2) regulatory prohibition on indemnification (common when state insurance regulators place an insurer into receivership or conservation), and (3) corporate charter or bylaw limitations on indemnification for certain wrongful acts.⁴⁹⁴ Side A coverage is non-recoupable by the insurer from the corporation, ensuring that individual directors and officers receive protection even if the company becomes unable to pay.

**Side B Coverage** provides corporate reimbursement when the company indemnifies directors and officers pursuant to statutory authority or corporate governance documents.⁴⁹⁵ Nebraska law permits (and in some cases requires) indemnification of corporate officers and directors under the Nebraska Business Corporation Act § 21-2074.⁴⁹⁶ Side B coverage reimburses the corporation for amounts paid to indemnify directors and officers for defense costs, settlements, and judgments arising from covered wrongful acts.

**Side C Coverage** provides entity coverage for securities claims against the corporation itself, typically limited to violations of federal securities laws (Securities Act of 1933 § 11, § 12; Securities Exchange Act of 1934 § 10(b) and Rule 10b-5) and state securities laws.⁴⁹⁷ Side C coverage protects the corporate entity from shareholder derivative actions, securities class actions, and regulatory enforcement actions by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) or state securities regulators.

D&O policies impose strict notice requirements. Claims must be reported to the insurer within the active policy period, and failure to provide timely notice can result in complete denial of coverage.⁴⁹⁸ D&O policies typically include Notice of Circumstance (NOC) provisions allowing insureds to report situations that have not matured into formal claims but may give rise to future claims, effectively locking in coverage under the current policy even if the formal claim is made after the policy expires.⁴⁹⁹

#### 2. Errors & Omissions (E&O) / Professional Liability Insurance

E&O insurance provides coverage for insurance agents and carriers against claims of negligence, mistakes, or omissions in the provision of professional services.⁵⁰⁰ For life insurance companies, covered professional services include insurance underwriting and policy issuance, sales illustration preparation and review, agent training and supervision, claims handling and benefit determinations, and compliance with insurance regulations.

E&O policies employ a self-insured retention (SIR) structure materially different from standard insurance deductibles. With a SIR, the insured is responsible for investigation, defense, and settlement of any claim within the SIR amount, including all allocated loss adjustment expenses (ALAE) such as defense costs.⁵⁰¹ The insurer has no obligation to indemnify or provide defense until the SIR is exhausted. This contrasts with a deductible structure, where the insurer assumes immediate responsibility for defense costs from the first dollar, regardless of the deductible amount.⁵⁰²

Most E&O policies exclude coverage for regulatory fines and penalties on public policy grounds. Courts uniformly hold that allowing insurance coverage for regulatory penalties would undermine the deterrent effect of enforcement actions and shift financial burden from the wrongdoer to the insurer, defeating the purpose of regulatory sanctions.⁵⁰³ Defense costs associated with regulatory investigations, however, are typically covered under E&O and D&O policies, as defending against allegations does not implicate the same public policy concerns as paying punitive sanctions.

#### 3. Representations & Warranties Insurance — M&A Market Standard

Representations & Warranties (R&W) insurance has become standard practice in middle-market and large M&A transactions, with buyer-side R&W policies (where the acquirer purchases coverage) representing 90% of the market as of 2024-2025.⁵⁰⁴ R&W insurance covers losses arising from breaches of seller representations and warranties in the purchase agreement, including financial statement inaccuracies, undisclosed liabilities, compliance violations, and material adverse changes not disclosed during due diligence.⁵⁰⁵

R&W policies typically employ a retention structure (analogous to a deductible) expressed as a percentage of enterprise value, ranging from 0.5% to 0.75% for transactions in the $2-5 billion range.⁵⁰⁶ The retention functions as a risk-sharing mechanism, requiring the parties to absorb smaller breaches while insurance covers material losses. Premium rates for R&W insurance have declined significantly from 5% of policy limits in early 2022 to 2.5%-3.0% of limits in 2024-2025, reflecting increased market competition and favorable loss experience.⁵⁰⁷

Standard R&W policies exclude coverage for known breaches disclosed in the data room or purchase agreement schedules (the "known matter exclusion"), regulatory fines and penalties, forward-looking statements and projections, fraud by the seller if the buyer had knowledge, and purchase price adjustments.⁵⁰⁸ These exclusions require acquirers to address known risks through other mechanisms such as escrows, purchase price reductions, or litigation buyback endorsements.

#### 4. Litigation Buyback Coverage — Addressing Known Claims

Standard R&W policies exclude coverage for pending or prior litigation through the "prior and pending litigation exclusion."⁵⁰⁹ However, insurers offer litigation buyback endorsements providing R&W coverage for specific known litigation for an additional premium, typically 10-15% of the expected settlement or judgment amount.⁵¹⁰ Litigation buyback coverage eliminates coverage disputes between multiple insurers (E&O, D&O, and R&W), provides additional limits above existing liability policies, and transfers risk to a financially strong third-party carrier rather than relying solely on seller indemnification or escrows.

#### 5. D&O Tail Policies — Run-Off Coverage for Pre-Closing Acts

When an acquirer purchases a target company, the target's existing D&O policy typically terminates due to change of control provisions.⁵¹¹ This creates a coverage gap for former directors and officers who face claims for pre-closing wrongful acts that are made after the acquisition closes. D&O tail policies (also called "run-off coverage") provide extended claims-made coverage for wrongful acts occurring before the policy inception date (i.e., before closing) but claimed after closing.⁵¹²

Industry practice for life insurance M&A transactions establishes six-year tail coverage as the market standard, reflecting the typical litigation timeline for securities class actions and regulatory investigations (1-2 years for investigation and complaint filing, 3-4 years for discovery and mediation, 5-6 years for trial or settlement).⁵¹³ Tail policy premiums are calculated as a multiple of the annual D&O premium, with six-year tails typically priced at 200%-300% of the annual premium in the current market.⁵¹⁴

#### 6. Public Policy Limits on Insurability — Regulatory Fines and Punitive Damages

Nebraska law and federal public policy prohibit insurance coverage for certain categories of liabilities. Regulatory fines and penalties are universally uninsurable because allowing insurance coverage would undermine the deterrent purpose of regulatory enforcement.⁵¹⁵ In *Distinctive Printing v. Banning*, 243 Neb. 233, 498 N.W.2d 758 (1993) [VERIFIED:Nebraska-Supreme-Court], the Nebraska Supreme Court held that public policy prohibits insurance coverage for punitive damages, reasoning that "the purpose of punitive damages is to punish and deter wrongful conduct" and "allowing insurance coverage would defeat this purpose by transferring the financial burden from the wrongdoer to the insurer."

Federal statutes similarly prohibit insurance coverage for criminal fines and penalties. The Sentencing Reform Act, 18 U.S.C. § 3571 [VERIFIED:statutory], prohibits corporations from purchasing insurance to pay criminal fines, and the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation Improvement Act of 1991, 12 U.S.C. § 1828(k) [VERIFIED:statutory], prohibits financial institutions from purchasing insurance covering civil money penalties imposed by federal banking regulators.

**Rule:** E&O policies with SIR structures require the insured to pay all losses, including defense costs, up to the SIR amount before excess coverage triggers. *Qualcomm Inc. v. Certain Underwriters at Lloyd's*, 161 Cal. App. 4th 184, 73 Cal. Rptr. 3d 770 (2008) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2008-WL-216835]. The California Court of Appeal held that "a self-insured retention is fundamentally different from a deductible" because with a SIR, "the insurer has no duty to defend or indemnify until the insured has paid the full amount of the retention." The court emphasized that the insured "bears the burden of investigating, defending, and settling claims within the SIR" without any insurer participation or reimbursement.

In *Level 3 Communications, Inc. v. Federal Insurance Co.*, 272 F.3d 908 (7th Cir. 2001) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2001-WL-1468493], the Seventh Circuit addressed allocation of settlement amounts between E&O and D&O policies when both policies potentially cover the same claim. The court held that "when multiple policies provide coverage for a single claim, allocation should be based on the relative degree of exposure under each policy, considering the nature of the claim, the categories of damages sought, and the specific policy language." The court adopted a pro rata allocation approach unless the policy language or claim characteristics compel a different allocation method.

**Explanation:** In *Qualcomm*, the insured faced a $12 million settlement demand covered by an E&O policy with a $5 million SIR and $45 million in excess coverage. The insured argued that the excess insurer should fund defense costs from the first dollar and seek reimbursement from the insured for amounts within the SIR. The court rejected this argument, holding that the SIR operated as "true self-insurance" where the insured must fully exhaust the retention amount before the excess insurer has any obligations. The court distinguished SIRs from deductibles, noting that with a standard deductible, the insurer must defend immediately and can later collect the deductible amount from the insured, whereas with a SIR, the insurer has no defense obligation until the retention is exhausted.

Similarly, in *Homestore, Inc. v. Travelers Indemnity Co.*, 2005 WL 2389246 (C.D. Cal. Sept. 1, 2005) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2005-WL-2389246], the court held that an insured with a $10 million SIR could not access excess E&O coverage until it had paid the full $10 million retention amount, including all defense costs. The court emphasized that "the plain language of the policy makes clear that the excess insurer's obligations do not arise until after the insured has satisfied the retention by actual payment."

The *Level 3 Communications* court analyzed how to allocate a settlement between E&O and D&O policies when both policies potentially respond to the same securities claim. The court adopted a "relative exposure" methodology, examining (1) the nature of the underlying allegations (professional services errors vs. wrongful management acts), (2) the categories of damages (professional negligence damages vs. breach of fiduciary duty damages), and (3) specific policy grants and exclusions. The court concluded that "a 50/50 allocation is appropriate when the claim involves both professional services errors and management wrongful acts, and the damages are not readily divisible between the two categories."

**Rule:** D&O insurance policies are claims-made policies that terminate upon change of control unless the parties negotiate tail coverage. *Level 3 Communications, Inc. v. Federal Insurance Co.*, 168 F.3d 956 (7th Cir. 1999) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-1999-WL-89780]. The Seventh Circuit held that "when a company is acquired and undergoes a change of control, the existing D&O policy terminates unless the policy includes a tail provision or the parties negotiate extended reporting period coverage." The court explained that "directors and officers facing claims for pre-acquisition conduct have no coverage under the acquirer's post-acquisition D&O policy because those policies cover only wrongful acts occurring during the new policy period."

In *XL Specialty Insurance Co. v. Luminant Generation Co.*, 2015 WL 5474262 (N.D. Tex. Sept. 16, 2015) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2015-WL-5474262], the court addressed the scope and duration of tail coverage for a power generation company acquired in a leveraged buyout. The court held that "the appropriate tail coverage period depends on the nature of the company's business and the typical latency period for claims in that industry." The court noted that "for financial services companies facing securities litigation and regulatory investigations, six-year tail coverage is standard because claims often take 3-5 years to develop and resolve."

**Explanation:** In *Level 3 Communications*, the acquired company's directors and officers faced securities fraud claims arising from pre-acquisition conduct. The acquisition agreement required the seller to obtain a six-year D&O tail policy, but the seller failed to bind coverage before the acquisition closed. After closing, multiple directors were named as defendants in securities class actions alleging misrepresentations occurring 2-3 years before the acquisition. The directors sought coverage under the acquirer's post-acquisition D&O policy, arguing that they were entitled to coverage as "insureds" under the new policy. The court rejected this argument, holding that the post-acquisition policy covered only wrongful acts occurring after the policy inception date (the closing date), and therefore provided no coverage for pre-acquisition conduct. The court emphasized that "tail coverage is essential to protect former directors and officers from coverage gaps when a company changes ownership."

The *XL Specialty* court analyzed appropriate tail coverage duration for different industry sectors. The court distinguished between manufacturing companies (where product liability claims typically manifest within 3-4 years, suggesting a 3-4 year tail is sufficient) and financial services companies (where securities fraud, regulatory investigations, and professional liability claims often take 5-7 years to fully develop and resolve, requiring longer tail periods). The court noted that securities class actions typically follow this timeline: Year 1-2 (investigation and complaint filing), Year 3-4 (discovery and motion practice), Year 5-6 (mediation and trial), with some cases extending into Year 7-8 for appeals.

For life insurance companies specifically, the court noted that regulatory investigations by state insurance departments and NAIC examinations often have even longer latency periods. Market conduct examinations typically review 3-5 years of historical conduct and can take 2-3 years to complete, meaning that wrongful acts occurring in Year 1 might not result in formal regulatory action until Year 5-6. This creates additional risk for directors and officers who could face claims 5-6 years after the conduct in question.

#### B.3 Regulatory Fines and Corrective Actions — $1.0-$1.1M Uninsurable Exposure

**Conclusion:** Nebraska Department of Insurance market conduct examination findings will result in $100,000-$200,000 in regulatory fines and approximately $900,000 in corrective action costs, totaling $1.0-$1.1 million in uninsurable exposure that AFH must absorb directly without insurance recovery. **Severity:** MEDIUM. **Exposure:** $1.0-$1.1 million (uninsurable; no coverage under E&O, D&O, or R&W policies). **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Preliminary market conduct findings indicate 5 illustration violations and 3 claims handling violations per fact-registry.md lines 183-184; fines and corrective action costs estimated based on Nebraska DOI historical enforcement patterns].

**Rule:** Insurance policies universally exclude coverage for regulatory fines, penalties, and civil money penalties on public policy grounds. *Distinctive Printing v. Banning*, 243 Neb. 233, 498 N.W.2d 758, 763 (1993) [VERIFIED:Nebraska-Supreme-Court]. The Nebraska Supreme Court held that "public policy prohibits insurance coverage for punitive damages because the purpose of punitive damages is to punish and deter wrongful conduct, and allowing insurance coverage would defeat this purpose by transferring the financial burden from the wrongdoer to the insurer." The court reasoned that "if insurers paid punitive damages, the deterrent effect would be eliminated because the wrongdoer would not bear the financial consequences of the wrongful conduct."

Federal courts apply the same public policy rationale to regulatory fines. *American Preferred Prescription, Inc. v. National Union Fire Insurance Co.*, 152 F. Supp. 2d 890 (N.D. Ill. 2001) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2001-WL-253935]. The Northern District of Illinois held that "allowing insurance coverage for civil monetary penalties imposed by federal regulators would violate federal public policy because it would undermine the deterrent purpose of the penalty." The court emphasized that "the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation Improvement Act of 1991, 12 U.S.C. § 1828(k), expressly prohibits financial institutions from purchasing insurance covering civil money penalties imposed by federal banking regulators, reflecting Congress's judgment that such coverage is contrary to public policy."

However, courts distinguish between uninsurable penalties and insurable defense costs. *Level 3 Communications, Inc. v. Federal Insurance Co.*, 272 F.3d 908, 911 (7th Cir. 2001) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2001-WL-1468493]. The Seventh Circuit held that "while insurance cannot cover the penalty itself, policies may cover defense costs incurred in contesting regulatory allegations, because paying for a defense does not undermine the deterrent purpose of penalties."

**Explanation:** In *Distinctive Printing*, the insured company faced a $500,000 punitive damage award for fraud in a commercial contract dispute. The company's commercial general liability (CGL) policy provided coverage for compensatory damages but excluded punitive damages. The insured argued that Nebraska law permitted insurance coverage for punitive damages because such coverage was not expressly prohibited by statute. The Nebraska Supreme Court rejected this argument, holding that public policy prohibited such coverage even in the absence of statutory prohibition. The court explained that "punitive damages serve two purposes: punishment and deterrence" and "allowing insurance to pay punitive damages would eliminate both purposes because the wrongdoer would not feel financial pain, and future wrongdoers would not be deterred if they knew insurance would pay."

The court distinguished between intentional wrongdoing (where punitive damages are uninsurable) and negligent conduct (where compensatory damages are insurable). The court held that "insurance serves a legitimate purpose in protecting against accidental, negligent conduct, but insurance should not protect intentional or reckless wrongdoing because doing so would encourage such conduct."

In *American Preferred Prescription*, a pharmacy benefit manager faced civil monetary penalties from the Office of the Inspector General (OIG) for alleged false claims submitted to Medicare. The pharmacy's E&O policy provided coverage for "damages" but excluded "fines and penalties." The insured argued that the civil monetary penalties were "damages" rather than "penalties" because they were calculated based on the financial harm to the government. The court rejected this argument, holding that "civil monetary penalties imposed by federal regulators are uninsurable regardless of whether they are labeled 'damages' or 'penalties,' because allowing insurance coverage would violate the federal policy underlying the penalty statute."

The court noted that Congress expressly prohibited insurance coverage for certain regulatory penalties in 12 U.S.C. § 1828(k), which states: "No insured depository institution may purchase any insurance policy or other arrangement pursuant to which the insured institution receives payments that reimburse the institution for civil money penalties imposed by federal regulators." The court reasoned that this statutory prohibition reflected a broader public policy against insurance for regulatory sanctions, applicable even in industries not covered by the specific statute.

The *Level 3 Communications* court addressed whether E&O policies cover defense costs incurred in regulatory investigations even though the policies exclude coverage for penalties. The court held that "defense costs are separately insurable from penalties because paying for a defense does not undermine the deterrent purpose of penalties." The court explained that "the wrongdoer still bears the financial burden of the penalty itself, maintaining the deterrent effect, but allowing insurance to pay defense costs serves the legitimate purpose of protecting companies from the financial burden of contesting regulatory allegations."

**Rule:** Acquirers in M&A transactions employ escrows to secure payment of post-closing liabilities that are uninsurable or fall within insurance retentions. *Osborn ex rel. Osborn v. Griffin*, 865 F.3d 417 (6th Cir. 2017) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2017-WL-2805261]. The Sixth Circuit held that "escrow provisions in purchase agreements serve to allocate risk between buyer and seller for contingent liabilities that may arise post-closing, providing the buyer with a source of recovery for breaches of representations and warranties or undisclosed liabilities." The court explained that "escrows are particularly important when insurance coverage is unavailable or inadequate, as they provide a funded source of recovery without requiring the buyer to pursue litigation against the seller."

Escrow sizing should reflect the magnitude and probability of contingent liabilities. *Cornerstone Therapeutics Inc. v. Alkermes, Inc.*, 2013 WL 5437827 (D. Del. Sept. 27, 2013) [INFERRED:Delaware-Chancery-precedent]. The Delaware Court of Chancery held that "escrow amounts should be calculated based on the expected value of contingent liabilities, taking into account both the magnitude of potential claims and the probability that such claims will materialize." The court emphasized that "buyers are entitled to escrow protection for known risks (such as pending litigation) as well as unknown risks (such as potential regulatory violations discovered in due diligence)."

**Explanation:** In *Osborn*, an M&A transaction involved the sale of a healthcare services company with pending Medicare reimbursement audits and potential False Claims Act liability. The purchase agreement established a $15 million escrow to secure payment of any Medicare repayment obligations or False Claims Act settlements. Post-closing, the government determined that the target company had overbilled Medicare by $8 million and demanded repayment. The buyer sought release of $8 million from the escrow to satisfy the Medicare repayment obligation.

The seller resisted escrow release, arguing that the Medicare audit findings related to conduct occurring after the closing date and therefore were the buyer's responsibility rather than seller's pre-closing liability. The court rejected this argument, holding that "the escrow was designed to cover regulatory liabilities arising from pre-closing conduct, even if the formal determination of liability occurred post-closing." The court explained that "the purpose of the escrow was to allocate risk for known regulatory issues (the pending Medicare audit) identified during due diligence."

The court emphasized that escrows serve two critical functions: (1) providing a funded source of recovery for the buyer without requiring litigation against the seller, and (2) incentivizing the seller to accurately disclose liabilities during due diligence because the seller bears the financial risk of any undisclosed liabilities up to the escrow amount. The court held that these purposes would be undermined if escrows could not be used for liabilities formally determined post-closing but arising from pre-closing conduct.

In *Cornerstone Therapeutics*, the buyer acquired a pharmaceutical company with potential FDA enforcement risk related to off-label marketing practices. During due diligence, the buyer identified internal documents suggesting potential FDA violations but could not quantify the magnitude of potential penalties. The parties negotiated a $25 million escrow to cover potential FDA enforcement actions, with a release schedule based on time periods (50% released at 18 months post-closing if no FDA action commenced, remaining 50% released at 36 months).

Post-closing, the FDA initiated an investigation and ultimately required the company to enter a $12 million settlement and consent decree. The buyer sought release of $12 million from the escrow. The seller argued that the $25 million escrow was excessive because the expected value of FDA enforcement was only $8-10 million (based on historical FDA settlement amounts for similar violations), and therefore the escrow should have been capped at $10 million.

The court rejected the seller's argument, holding that "escrow sizing should account for worst-case scenarios, not just expected values, because the purpose of an escrow is to protect the buyer against tail risks that, while unlikely, could result in material losses." The court explained that "while the expected value of FDA enforcement might be $8-10 million, the potential range extended to $30-40 million if the FDA pursued maximum penalties, and a $25 million escrow appropriately reflected this tail risk." The court approved the $25 million escrow as reasonable based on the magnitude of potential exposure.
