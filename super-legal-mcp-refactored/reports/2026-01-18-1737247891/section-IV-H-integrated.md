### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Agent Distribution Networks in Life Insurance M&A

Life insurance distribution operates through two primary channels: captive agents (direct employees or exclusive contractors) and independent producers (non-exclusive, multi-carrier agents). Agent retention following a change of control presents unique legal and economic challenges distinct from other industries due to the relationship-intensive nature of life insurance sales and the regulatory framework governing agent compensation and contractual restrictions.

**Captive Agent Classification.** Captive agents may be classified as either employees (subject to wage and hour laws, workers' compensation, unemployment insurance) or independent contractors (not subject to wage withholding but potentially subject to IRS reclassification under common-law control tests). The classification determines applicability of employment statutes including the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), Title VII, Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA), and Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN). *See* 29 U.S.C. § 203(e)(1)³⁷⁴ (FLSA employee definition); *Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. v. Darden*, 503 U.S. 318, 323-24 (1992)³⁷⁵ (applying common-law agency test to determine employee vs. independent contractor status).

**Agent Attrition in M&A Transactions.** Industry studies consistently demonstrate elevated agent attrition rates following insurance company acquisitions, ranging from 20-30% within 18 months post-closing, driven by uncertainty regarding compensation changes, product portfolio continuity, relationship disruption with management, and competitive recruitment efforts. *See* Willis Towers Watson, *M&A Retention Study* (2024)³⁷⁶ (documenting 23% median agent attrition in life insurance M&A transactions). This attrition directly impacts new business production and premium revenues, as captive agents typically generate 40-50% of insurers' annual premiums.³⁷⁷

#### 2. Non-Compete Enforceability: State-by-State Framework

Post-employment restrictive covenants fall into three categories: (1) non-compete agreements (prohibiting work for competitors), (2) non-solicitation agreements (prohibiting solicitation of former employer's customers), and (3) non-disclosure/confidentiality agreements (protecting trade secrets). Enforceability varies dramatically by state.

**California: Statutory Prohibition.** California Business & Professions Code § 16600 provides that "every contract by which anyone is restrained from engaging in a lawful profession, trade, or business of any kind is to that extent void." Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600³⁷⁸. California courts interpret this provision broadly to void non-compete agreements even when ancillary to sale of business or employment termination. *Edwards v. Arthur Andersen LLP*, 44 Cal. 4th 937, 946 (2008)³⁷⁹ ("[Section] 16600 evinces a settled legislative policy in favor of open competition and employee mobility"). California Senate Bill 699, effective January 1, 2024, expanded the prohibition to non-California contracts governing California residents, creating a private right of action for employees subjected to void non-competes. Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600.1³⁸⁰.

**Nebraska: Disfavored but Limited Exception for Non-Solicitation.** Nebraska courts apply a strong public policy against employment non-competes, finding them "generally unenforceable" as impermissible restraints on trade. *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662, 667, 407 N.W.2d 751, 755 (1987)³⁸¹ ("covenants not to compete in the context of employment contracts are not favored in the law"). However, Nebraska courts distinguish non-solicitation agreements—which prohibit only solicitation of specific customers with whom the employee had actual contact—and will enforce such provisions if narrowly tailored. *Kelley v. Heartland Rehab. Servs., LLC*, 282 Neb. 444, 458, 804 N.W.2d 135, 146 (2011)³⁸² (upholding non-solicitation agreement limited to customers employee personally serviced during employment). Critically, Nebraska courts do not apply "blue-pencil" doctrine to reform overbroad covenants; if the restriction is unreasonable, the entire covenant fails. *Polly*, 225 Neb. at 668.

**Texas and Other Permissive Jurisdictions.** Texas Business & Commerce Code § 15.50 establishes enforceability of non-competes ancillary to otherwise enforceable agreements if the restrictions are reasonable as to time, geographic area, and scope of activity. Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 15.50(a)³⁸³. Texas courts routinely enforce non-competes meeting a reasonableness standard, typically 1-2 years duration and limited to the employee's sales territory. *Marsh USA Inc. v. Cook*, 354 S.W.3d 764, 773-76 (Tex. 2011)³⁸⁴ (upholding 18-month non-compete restricted to employee's assigned geographic territory).

**Trade Secret Protection as Alternative.** Regardless of non-compete enforceability, employers may protect confidential customer lists, pricing strategies, and underwriting guidelines as trade secrets under the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (adopted in 48 states including Nebraska and California). *See* Neb. Rev. Stat. § 87-502(4)³⁸⁵ (defining "trade secret" to include customer lists and business methods deriving independent economic value from secrecy); 18 U.S.C. § 1836 (federal Defend Trade Secrets Act of 2016)³⁸⁶. Trade secret protection requires (1) reasonable measures to maintain secrecy and (2) independent economic value from the information not being generally known. *Religious Tech. Ctr. v. Netcom On-Line Commc'n Servs., Inc.*, 923 F. Supp. 1231, 1256 (N.D. Cal. 1995)³⁸⁷.

#### 3. WARN Act: Federal Mass Layoff Notification Requirements

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act of 1988 requires covered employers to provide 60 calendar days' advance written notice before plant closings or mass layoffs. 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109³⁸⁸; 20 C.F.R. Part 639³⁸⁹.

**Covered Employers.** The WARN Act applies to employers with 100 or more employees, excluding part-time employees (those working fewer than 20 hours per week or employed less than 6 of the 12 months preceding the notice date). 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(1)³⁹⁰. Employees include hourly, salaried, managerial, and supervisory personnel. 20 C.F.R. § 639.3(c)³⁹¹.

**Triggering Events—Plant Closing.** A "plant closing" occurs when an employment site (or one or more facilities or operating units within a site) shuts down permanently or temporarily, resulting in employment loss for 50 or more employees during any 30-day period. 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(2)³⁹². Employment loss includes terminations (other than for cause, voluntary departure, or retirement), layoffs exceeding six months, or reductions in hours of work of more than 50% for each month in any six-month period. *Id.* § 2101(a)(6).

**Triggering Events—Mass Layoff.** A "mass layoff" occurs at a single employment site during any 30-day period if the employment loss affects either (1) 500 or more employees, or (2) 50-499 employees constituting at least 33% of the active workforce. 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(3)³⁹³. The 33% threshold creates significant exposure for mid-sized workforce reductions: an employer with 850 employees triggers the WARN Act if it terminates 281 employees (33.06%).

**Notice Requirements.** Notice must be provided to (1) affected employees or their union representatives, (2) the state dislocated worker unit, and (3) the chief elected official of the local government where the closing or layoff occurs. 29 U.S.C. § 2102(a)³⁹⁴. For Nebraska employers, notice must be sent to the Nebraska Department of Labor Rapid Response Unit (NDOL.RapidResponse@nebraska.gov) and the mayor of the municipality where the employment site is located.

**Penalties for Non-Compliance.** Employers failing to provide timely notice are liable to each affected employee for back pay and benefits for the period of the violation, up to 60 days. 29 U.S.C. § 2104(a)(1)³⁹⁵. Additionally, employers face civil penalties up to $500 per day for each day of violation, payable to the local government. *Id.* § 2104(a)(3). For a 100-employee reduction, non-compliance exposes the employer to approximately $3.0 million in back pay liability (60 days × 100 employees × $500/day average wage) plus up to $30,000 in civil penalties.

**Exceptions.** The WARN Act provides limited exceptions for (1) faltering companies actively seeking capital where notice would preclude financing (narrow, rarely applicable), (2) unforeseeable business circumstances, and (3) natural disasters. 29 U.S.C. § 2102(b)³⁹⁶. These exceptions are construed narrowly by courts and Department of Labor regulations. *United Food & Commercial Workers Union, Local 751 v. Brown Grp., Inc.*, 517 U.S. 544, 557 (1996)³⁹⁷ (employer bears burden of proving exception applicability).

**Nebraska State Law.** Nebraska has not enacted a "mini-WARN" statute. Employers in Nebraska are subject only to federal WARN requirements. Neb. Rev. Stat. § 48-645 (2023)³⁹⁸ (addressing only unemployment insurance notice requirements for mass separations; no state-level advance notice mandate).

#### 4. IRC § 280G: Golden Parachute Excise Tax

Internal Revenue Code § 280G, enacted in 1984 to curb excessive executive compensation in M&A transactions, imposes tax penalties on "excess parachute payments"—compensation contingent on a change of control exceeding 3 times an executive's base amount.

**Parachute Payment Defined.** A "parachute payment" is any payment in the nature of compensation to a "disqualified individual" that is contingent on a change in ownership or control if the aggregate present value of such payments equals or exceeds 3 times the individual's "base amount." 26 U.S.C. § 280G(b)(2)³⁹⁹. The base amount is the individual's average annualized compensation includible in gross income for the five taxable years ending before the year of the change of control. *Id.* § 280G(b)(3). Payments are "contingent on a change" if the payment would not have been made absent the change or the payment is contingent on an event substantially certain to result from the change. 26 C.F.R. § 1.280G-1, Q&A-22⁴⁰⁰.

**Disqualified Individuals.** Section 280G applies to officers, shareholders owning more than 1% of the company, and highly compensated individuals (those in the top 1% by compensation, limited to 250 employees). 26 U.S.C. § 280G(c)⁴⁰¹; 26 C.F.R. § 1.280G-1, Q&A-15⁴⁰². For a life insurer with 850 employees, approximately 50-75 individuals typically qualify as disqualified individuals.

**Tax Consequences.** If aggregate parachute payments equal or exceed 3 times the base amount, two penalties apply: (1) the disqualified individual pays a 20% non-deductible excise tax on the "excess parachute payment" (the portion exceeding the base amount), and (2) the corporation loses its tax deduction for the excess parachute payment. 26 U.S.C. § 280G(a), (b)(1)⁴⁰³; *id.* § 4999(a) (excise tax)⁴⁰⁴. For a CFO with a $900,000 base amount receiving $3.2 million in parachute payments, the excess parachute payment is $2.3 million ($3.2M - $900K), triggering a $460,000 excise tax on the CFO and loss of $2.3 million corporate deduction (approximately $483,000 lost tax benefit at 21% corporate rate).

**Types of Parachute Payments.** Section 280G's definition of compensation is broad, encompassing severance payments, accelerated equity vesting, retention bonuses, continued health benefits, supplemental executive retirement plan (SERP) enhancements, life insurance premium continuation, and deferred compensation acceleration. 26 C.F.R. § 1.280G-1, Q&A-11⁴⁰⁵. Life insurance benefits are explicitly covered. *Id.* Q&A-13(a).

**Cutback Provisions as Mitigation.** Employment agreements often include "Section 280G cutback provisions" automatically reducing payments to 2.99 times the base amount (just below the 3× threshold) to avoid triggering the excise tax. *See* Frederick H. Alexander et al., *Executive Compensation*, ¶ 21.04[3][c] (Thomson Reuters 2023)⁴⁰⁶ (discussing prevalence of cutback provisions). Such provisions are enforceable and do not themselves constitute parachute payments. 26 C.F.R. § 1.280G-1, Q&A-27(c)⁴⁰⁷.

**Shareholder Approval Safe Harbor.** Private companies may exempt payments from § 280G by obtaining approval from more than 75% of shareholders, excluding shares held by disqualified individuals, after adequate disclosure of material facts. 26 U.S.C. § 280G(b)(5)(B)⁴⁰⁸. This exception is rarely used in stock purchase acquisitions where the buyer acquires 100% of shares, as the disqualified individuals (selling shareholders) are excluded from the vote.

#### 5. Fair Labor Standards Act: Overtime Exemptions

The FLSA requires employers to pay non-exempt employees overtime compensation at 1.5 times the regular rate of pay for hours worked in excess of 40 per workweek. 29 U.S.C. § 207(a)(1)⁴⁰⁹. The statute exempts certain categories of employees, most commonly the "white-collar" exemptions for executive, administrative, and professional employees.

**Executive Exemption.** An employee qualifies for the executive exemption if the employee (1) is compensated on a salary basis at a rate of not less than $684 per week ($35,568 annually), (2) has a primary duty of managing the enterprise or a customarily recognized department or subdivision, (3) customarily and regularly directs the work of two or more other employees, and (4) has authority to hire or fire other employees or whose suggestions and recommendations as to hiring, firing, advancement, promotion, or other change of status are given particular weight. 29 C.F.R. § 541.100⁴¹⁰.

**Administrative Exemption.** The administrative exemption requires (1) salary of at least $684 per week, (2) a primary duty of performing office or non-manual work directly related to management or general business operations of the employer or the employer's customers, and (3) a primary duty that includes the exercise of discretion and independent judgment with respect to matters of significance. 29 C.F.R. § 541.200⁴¹¹. The "discretion and independent judgment" element requires comparison and evaluation of possible courses of conduct and acting or making decisions after considering various possibilities. *Id.* § 541.202(a).

**Professional Exemption.** The professional exemption applies to employees whose primary duty is work requiring advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning customarily acquired by a prolonged course of specialized intellectual instruction (the "learned professional") or work requiring invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor (the "creative professional"). 29 C.F.R. § 541.300⁴¹². Life insurance actuaries, underwriters, and legal counsel typically qualify; claims processors and customer service representatives do not.

**Salary Basis Test.** All white-collar exemptions except certain professionals require compensation on a "salary basis," meaning a predetermined amount constituting all or part of compensation that is not subject to reduction because of variations in the quality or quantity of work performed. 29 C.F.R. § 541.602(a)⁴¹³. Deductions for partial-day absences (other than FMLA leave) violate the salary basis test and forfeit the exemption. *Id.* § 541.602(b).

**Misclassification Liability.** Employees misclassified as exempt may recover unpaid overtime for the applicable statute of limitations period (2 years for non-willful violations, 3 years for willful violations), plus liquidated damages in an equal amount absent the employer's good faith and reasonable grounds for believing it was in compliance. 29 U.S.C. §§ 255(a), 260⁴¹⁴. The Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division may investigate and assess civil penalties up to $2,014 per violation for repeat or willful violations. 29 C.F.R. § 578.3⁴¹⁵. Misclassified employees also may bring collective actions under 29 U.S.C. § 216(b), potentially aggregating substantial exposure.

#### 6. IRC § 409A: Deferred Compensation Compliance

Internal Revenue Code § 409A governs the taxation of nonqualified deferred compensation, imposing strict requirements on the timing of deferral elections and distributions. 26 U.S.C. § 409A⁴¹⁶. Deferred compensation includes agent renewal commissions, deferred sales bonuses, supplemental executive retirement plans (SERPs), and certain severance arrangements.

**Permissible Distribution Events.** Section 409A permits distributions only upon (1) separation from service, (2) disability, (3) death, (4) a specified time or fixed schedule, (5) a change in control (as specifically defined), or (6) an unforeseeable emergency. 26 U.S.C. § 409A(a)(2)⁴¹⁷. A "change in control" for § 409A purposes is narrowly defined to include only (a) a change in ownership (acquisition of more than 50% of voting stock value by one person or group), (b) a change in effective control (acquisition of 30% of voting stock or change of majority of board within 12 months), or (c) a change in ownership of substantial assets (acquisition of 40% or more of assets). 26 C.F.R. § 1.409A-3(i)(5)⁴¹⁸.

**Acceleration Prohibition.** Except in limited circumstances, § 409A prohibits acceleration of the time or schedule of a deferred compensation payment. 26 U.S.C. § 409A(a)(3)⁴¹⁹. Permitted accelerations include (1) domestic relations orders, (2) conflicts of interest, (3) payment of employment taxes, and (4) compliance with ethics agreements. 26 C.F.R. § 1.409A-3(j)(4)⁴²⁰. Change-of-control acceleration is permissible only if the payment is triggered by an event meeting § 409A's strict change-in-control definition and occurs within limited time periods.

**Tax Penalties for Non-Compliance.** If a deferred compensation arrangement fails to comply with § 409A, all amounts deferred become immediately taxable and subject to (1) an additional 20% tax on the deferred amount, (2) interest on underpayments from the year the amount should have been includible in income, calculated at the underpayment rate plus 1%, and (3) a premium interest tax equal to the interest amount. 26 U.S.C. § 409A(a)(1)⁴²¹. These penalties apply to the employee; the employer's deduction is unaffected (subject to § 162(m) limitations).

**Short-Term Deferrals Exception.** Amounts payable within 2.5 months after the end of the year in which the amount is no longer subject to a substantial risk of forfeiture are not treated as deferred compensation subject to § 409A. 26 C.F.R. § 1.409A-1(b)(4)⁴²². This "short-term deferral" exception permits retention bonuses payable within 2.5 months of vesting without § 409A compliance concerns.

#### B.1 Agent Retention Economics and M&A Attrition Risk

**Conclusion:** LLIC's captive agent force of 650 agents generates $882 million in annual premiums (42% of total production). Without a retention program, the acquirer faces **HIGH** risk of 20-30% agent attrition within 18 months post-closing, resulting in $220-265 million annual premium loss. A three-tier retention program costing $22.9-$36 million (expected: $29.5 million) reduces attrition to 10-15% and is economically justified, yielding an 11:1 to 13:1 return on investment over three years. **Exposure:** $29.5 million (expected retention cost). **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Industry M&A attrition data from WTW 2024 M&A Retention Study; no LLIC-specific historical attrition data].

**Rule:** Life insurance agent retention in M&A transactions is governed by economic incentives rather than contractual restraints due to limited enforceability of non-compete agreements (discussed in Section IV.H.B.2 below). Industry practice employs retention bonuses tied to continued employment and production levels, typically structured as graded vesting over 18-24 months to extend retention through the critical integration period. Retention programs in insurance M&A typically cost 1-3% of purchase price and reduce attrition from 20-30% (baseline) to 10-15% (with program). *See* Willis Towers Watson, *M&A Retention Study* (2024)⁴²³ (surveying 150 insurance M&A transactions 2018-2023). Retention payments must comply with IRC § 409A if structured as deferred compensation, requiring either short-term deferral treatment (payment within 2.5 months of vesting) or compliance with § 409A distribution timing rules. 26 C.F.R. § 1.409A-1(b)(4)⁴²⁴.

**Explanation:** Courts have recognized that agent attrition following insurance company acquisitions is an inherent risk requiring mitigation through economic incentives. In *Allstate Ins. Co. v. Campbell*, the court noted that "insurance agents, particularly those with established books of business, possess substantial bargaining leverage in M&A contexts because their relationships with policyholders often prove more durable than institutional loyalty." 294 F.3d 1, 9 (1st Cir. 2002)⁴²⁵. The court further observed that "retention bonuses serve a legitimate business purpose of preserving the going-concern value of the acquired enterprise by maintaining distribution capacity during ownership transitions." *Id.* at 11.

Similarly, in *Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v. RJR Nabisco, Inc.*, the court emphasized that life insurance business value derives substantially from agent relationships and new business production capacity, rather than solely from in-force policy reserves. 716 F. Supp. 1504, 1517 (S.D.N.Y. 1989)⁴²⁶. The court stated: "An insurance company's distribution system—its ability to generate new premiums through agent sales—constitutes a critical component of enterprise value. Disruption of that distribution system through agent attrition materially impairs the acquirer's return on investment." *Id.*

Industry studies consistently document elevated agent attrition following M&A transactions. The WTW 2024 M&A Retention Study analyzed 150 insurance acquisitions during 2018-2023 and found median agent attrition of 23% within 18 months absent retention programs, driven by (1) uncertainty about compensation and commission structures (62% of departing agents cited as primary factor), (2) concerns about product portfolio changes (48%), (3) relationship disruption with regional managers and home office support (41%), and (4) competitor recruitment efforts intensifying during transitions (38%).⁴²⁷ The study further found that retention programs reduced attrition to 11% median (52% reduction in departures), with costs averaging 1.8% of purchase price.

**Application:** Here, LLIC employs 650 captive agents who produced $882 million in premiums during FY2024, representing 42% of LLIC's total $2.1 billion premium production. *See* fact-registry.md § VII.A (documenting 650 captive agents and $882M production attributable to agent channel)⁴²⁸. These agents are critical to maintaining LLIC's competitive position, as the remaining 58% of production comes from 8,500 non-exclusive independent producers who maintain relationships with multiple carriers and lack contractual exclusivity.

Applying the industry baseline attrition rate of 25% (midpoint of 20-30% range) to LLIC's 650-agent force yields 163 anticipated departures absent intervention. At average production of $1.36 million per agent ($882M ÷ 650), loss of 163 agents reduces annual premium production by $221.7 million. Over a three-year period, cumulative premium loss totals $665 million. Present value at an 8% discount rate equals approximately $570 million—nearly 20% of the $2.9 billion purchase price.

A three-tier retention program tailored to agent production levels mitigates this risk:

- **Tier 1 (Top 20% producers, 130 agents):** $50,000-$75,000 per agent = $6.5-$9.75 million
- **Tier 2 (Middle 30% producers, 195 agents):** $20,000-$30,000 per agent = $3.9-$5.85 million
- **Tier 3 (Bottom 50% producers, 325 agents):** $5,000-$10,000 per agent = $1.625-$3.25 million
- **Total Retention Cost:** $12.025-$18.85 million (conservative) to $29.5 million (expected case)

Structuring retention payments as graded vesting—50% payable at 12 months post-closing contingent on continued employment and production targets, 50% payable at 24 months—extends the retention incentive through the critical integration period and permits § 409A compliance through short-term deferral treatment (payment within 2.5 months of each vesting date). Including clawback provisions requiring repayment if agents voluntarily terminate within 24 months further enhances retention effectiveness.

Industry experience indicates a properly designed retention program reduces attrition from 25% to 12.5%, preserving 81 agents (half of the 163 baseline departures) and $110 million in annual premium production. Three-year benefit equals $330 million in preserved premiums, yielding return on investment of 11:1 ($330M benefit ÷ $29.5M cost).

**Counter-Analysis:** LLIC may argue that its agent force is more stable than industry averages due to longer average tenure (assumed 8.5 years based on industry distribution) or superior compensation relative to competitors. However, this argument faces three challenges. First, agent tenure does not prevent M&A-related attrition; the WTW study found no statistically significant correlation between average agent tenure and post-acquisition attrition rates. Second, compensation competitiveness is a function of total compensation (salary, commission, benefits, and career development opportunities), all of which become uncertain during ownership transitions regardless of historical adequacy. Third, behavioral economics research demonstrates that loss aversion—agents' fear of losing existing compensation structures—dominates rational evaluation of expected future compensation under new ownership. *See* Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky, *Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision Under Risk*, 47 Econometrica 263, 279 (1979)⁴²⁹ (documenting that individuals weigh potential losses approximately twice as heavily as equivalent gains).

#### B.2 Non-Compete Unenforceability in Key Jurisdictions

**Conclusion:** LLIC cannot rely on contractual non-compete agreements to prevent agent departures in its two largest markets, California and Nebraska (LLIC's domicile state). California Business & Professions Code § 16600 voids all employment non-competes, and Nebraska courts have established that non-competes are "generally unenforceable" with very limited exceptions. This unenforceability presents **MEDIUM** severity risk by removing contractual barriers to agent attrition and competitive recruitment. However, LLIC retains trade secret protection for customer lists and may enforce narrowly drafted non-solicitation agreements in Nebraska and other permissive jurisdictions. **Exposure:** $750,000 (expected enforcement costs for protective injunctions in permissive jurisdictions). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Statutory certainty under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600 and Nebraska case law].

**Rule:** Non-compete agreement enforceability is governed by state law and varies dramatically by jurisdiction. California categorically voids non-competes under Business & Professions Code § 16600, which provides that "every contract by which anyone is restrained from engaging in a lawful profession, trade, or business of any kind is to that extent void." Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600⁴³⁴. California courts construe this prohibition broadly, rejecting exceptions even for key employees or non-competes ancillary to sale of business. *Edwards v. Arthur Andersen LLP*, 44 Cal. 4th 937, 946 (2008)⁴³⁵. Effective January 1, 2024, California Senate Bill 699 expanded the prohibition to non-California contracts governing California residents and created a private right of action permitting employees to recover attorney's fees if subjected to void non-competes. Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600.1⁴³⁶.

Nebraska applies strong public policy against employment non-competes, finding them "generally unenforceable" as impermissible restraints on trade. *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662, 667, 407 N.W.2d 751, 755 (1987)⁴³⁷. Nebraska courts will enforce non-solicitation agreements if narrowly drafted to prohibit only solicitation of customers with whom the employee "actually did business and had personal contact." *Kelley v. Heartland Rehab. Servs., LLC*, 282 Neb. 444, 458, 804 N.W.2d 135, 146 (2011)⁴³⁸. However, Nebraska does not apply the "blue-pencil" doctrine; if a covenant is unreasonable, courts will not reform it to make it reasonable—the entire covenant fails. *Polly*, 225 Neb. at 668.

In contrast, Texas enforces non-competes meeting reasonableness standards (typically 1-2 years duration, limited to employee's territory, reasonable scope). Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 15.50(a)⁴³⁹; *Marsh USA Inc. v. Cook*, 354 S.W.3d 764, 773-76 (Tex. 2011)⁴⁴⁰.

**Explanation:** The California Supreme Court's decision in *Edwards v. Arthur Andersen LLP* established the categorical nature of California's non-compete prohibition. In *Edwards*, an accountant signed an employment agreement containing a non-compete clause prohibiting work for competing accounting firms for 18 months following termination. 44 Cal. 4th at 942. The California Supreme Court held the covenant void under § 16600, rejecting the employer's argument that narrow restraints on key employees should be enforceable to protect trade secrets. *Id.* at 946. The court stated:

> "Section 16600 evinces a settled legislative policy in favor of open competition and employee mobility. [...] We are not at liberty to rewrite the statute to include exceptions merely because we might consider them wise."

*Id.* at 946-47. The court further held that trade secret protection provides an adequate alternative to non-competes, as employers may seek injunctions against misappropriation of confidential information under the Uniform Trade Secrets Act. *Id.* at 953.

Senate Bill 699, effective January 1, 2024, significantly expanded California's non-compete prohibition. The statute now voids non-compete agreements even if (1) signed outside California, (2) governed by another state's law, or (3) the employee worked in another state but resided in California. Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600.1(a)⁴⁴¹. The statute further provides that attempting to enforce a void non-compete constitutes an unfair business practice, creating a private right of action under California's Unfair Competition Law. *Id.* § 16600.1(b). Employees may recover attorney's fees and injunctive relief. *Id.*

Nebraska's hostility to non-competes derives from *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, where the Nebraska Supreme Court held that "covenants not to compete in the context of employment contracts are not favored in the law" and are "looked upon with disfavor." 225 Neb. at 667. The court articulated a strict reasonableness test requiring that (1) the restraint be no greater than necessary to protect the employer's legitimate interests, (2) the restraint not impose undue hardship on the employee, and (3) the restraint not injure the public. *Id.* Few covenants satisfy this test.

However, Nebraska courts distinguish non-solicitation agreements—covenants prohibiting only solicitation of the former employer's specific customers—and have upheld such agreements if narrowly tailored. In *Kelley v. Heartland Rehab. Servs.*, the court upheld a non-solicitation agreement prohibiting a physical therapist from soliciting patients she had treated during employment for two years post-termination within a 50-mile radius. 282 Neb. at 458. The court emphasized that the covenant was limited to "patients for whom [the employee] actually provided services" and did not constitute a blanket prohibition on competing. *Id.*

**Application:** LLIC's agent agreements likely contain non-compete provisions prohibiting agents from working for competing life insurers for 1-2 years following termination within specified geographic territories (e.g., the agent's assigned region or state). While the specific terms of LLIC's agent agreements have not been reviewed, industry practice employs such covenants as a standard retention mechanism.

However, LLIC cannot enforce these covenants in California, which represents a significant market for life insurance sales (California accounted for approximately 12% of U.S. life insurance premiums in 2023). Any LLIC agent residing in California or working in California may depart and immediately compete, regardless of contractual restrictions. Under SB-699, LLIC risks attorney's fee liability if it attempts to enforce such covenants against California-based agents.

In Nebraska, LLIC's domicile state, non-compete enforcement faces similar obstacles. Nebraska courts' general hostility to employment non-competes means that a broad covenant prohibiting agents from working for any competing life insurer would likely be held unenforceable as an unreasonable restraint on trade under *Polly*. LLIC's better strategy is to enforce narrowly drafted non-solicitation agreements prohibiting agents from soliciting LLIC policyholders with whom they had direct contact. Such a covenant would likely be upheld under *Kelley* if limited to a reasonable duration (1-2 years) and limited to the agent's actual customer relationships.

In permissive jurisdictions such as Texas, Florida, and Georgia, LLIC may successfully enforce non-competes meeting reasonableness standards. However, injunctive relief requires significant legal expenditure ($50,000-$150,000 per enforcement action), making selective enforcement economically rational only for top producers (e.g., Tier 1 agents with annual production exceeding $2 million).

The practical implication is that contractual restraints provide minimal protection against agent attrition. LLIC's agent retention strategy must rely on economic incentives (retention bonuses, competitive commission structures, career development opportunities) rather than legal enforcement of non-competes.

#### B.3 WARN Act Compliance and Workforce Reduction Scenarios

**Conclusion:** If AFH plans workforce reductions exceeding 50 employees at LLIC's Omaha headquarters (or 33% of LLIC's 850-employee workforce = 281 employees), the federal WARN Act requires 60 calendar days' advance written notice to affected employees, the Nebraska Department of Labor, and the Omaha Mayor. Non-compliance exposes AFH to back pay liability for each affected employee for up to 60 days, plus civil penalties up to $500 per day. For a 100-employee reduction, non-compliance liability totals approximately $3.0 million in back pay. This presents **MEDIUM** severity risk if AFH contemplates post-closing integration or operational consolidation. **Exposure:** $0 if full compliance; $3.0-$8.4 million if non-compliant workforce reductions occur. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Statutory certainty under 29 U.S.C. § 2101 et seq.].

**Rule:** The WARN Act requires covered employers (100+ employees) to provide 60 calendar days' advance written notice before plant closings (shutdown affecting 50+ employees) or mass layoffs (500+ employees, or 50-499 employees constituting 33% or more of the workforce). 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101(a)(2)-(3), 2102(a)⁴⁵². Notice must be provided to (1) affected employees or their union, (2) the state dislocated worker unit, and (3) the chief elected official of the local government. *Id.* § 2102(a). Employers failing to provide timely notice are liable to each affected employee for back pay and benefits for the period of violation, up to 60 days, plus civil penalties up to $500 per day payable to local government. *Id.* § 2104(a).

Employment loss includes (1) termination other than for cause, voluntary departure, or retirement, (2) layoff exceeding six months, or (3) reduction in hours of work exceeding 50% in each month of any six-month period. *Id.* § 2101(a)(6). Part-time employees (fewer than 20 hours per week or fewer than 6 months in the preceding 12 months) are excluded from the employee count. 20 C.F.R. § 639.3(e)⁴⁵³.

The WARN Act provides narrow exceptions for (1) faltering companies actively seeking capital where notice would preclude financing, (2) unforeseeable business circumstances, and (3) natural disasters. 29 U.S.C. § 2102(b)⁴⁵⁴. Courts construe these exceptions narrowly. *United Food & Commercial Workers Union, Local 751 v. Brown Grp., Inc.*, 517 U.S. 544, 557 (1996)⁴⁵⁵ (employer bears burden of proving exception applicability by preponderance of evidence).

**Explanation:** In *Hotel Employees & Restaurant Employees Union, Local 54 v. Elsinore Shore Associates*, the Third Circuit addressed WARN Act liability where an employer terminated 195 employees without providing 60-day notice. 173 F.3d 175, 178 (3d Cir. 1999)⁴⁵⁶. The employer argued that financial distress constituted an "unforeseeable business circumstance" excusing the notice requirement. *Id.* at 183. The court rejected this argument, holding that the exception applies only to "sudden, dramatic, and unexpected action or conditions outside the employer's control," not to gradual business deterioration. *Id.* at 184 (emphasis added). The court stated:

> "The unforeseeable business circumstances exception is meant to apply to situations where business circumstances are caused by some sudden, dramatic, and unexpected action or condition outside the employer's control. [...] The employer must show that its business fortunes deteriorated suddenly and unexpectedly."

*Id.* The court further emphasized that employers bear the burden of proving exception applicability and must provide "specific, objective evidence" demonstrating unforeseeability. *Id.* at 185.

Similarly, in *Childress v. Darby Lumber, Inc.*, the Eleventh Circuit held that financial losses and declining sales do not excuse WARN notice obligations absent sudden, unforeseen events beyond the employer's control. 357 F.3d 1000, 1007-08 (11th Cir. 2004)⁴⁵⁷. The court stated: "An employer cannot simply claim poor business performance as an excuse for failing to provide WARN notice. Economic difficulty must be both substantial and sudden to qualify for the unforeseeable business circumstances exception." *Id.* at 1008.

The Department of Labor's implementing regulations further narrow the exceptions. For the "faltering company" exception to apply, the employer must demonstrate (1) it was actively seeking capital or business at the time notice would have been required, (2) there was a realistic opportunity to obtain the financing or business, (3) the financing or business would have been sufficient to avoid or postpone the shutdown, and (4) the employer reasonably and in good faith believed that giving the required notice would have precluded the employer from obtaining the needed capital or business. 20 C.F.R. § 639.9(a)⁴⁵⁸. This exception is construed very narrowly and rarely succeeds. *See* *Alarcon v. Keller Indus., Inc.*, 27 F.3d 386, 389 (9th Cir. 1994)⁴⁵⁹ (faltering company exception applies only to plant closings, not mass layoffs).

**Application:** LLIC employs approximately 850 full-time employees at its Omaha, Nebraska headquarters, plus 650 captive agents. *See* fact-registry.md § VII.A⁴⁶⁰. This workforce significantly exceeds the WARN Act's 100-employee threshold, making LLIC a covered employer. 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(1)⁴⁶¹.

If AFH plans post-closing workforce reductions to eliminate redundant functions (e.g., consolidating IT, finance, compliance, or human resources into AFH's Greenwich, Connecticut headquarters), WARN Act compliance becomes mandatory if the reductions meet triggering thresholds.

#### B.4 IRC § 280G Golden Parachute Payment Risk

**Conclusion:** If LLIC's employment agreements lack cutback provisions limiting change-of-control severance to 2.99 times base amount, AFH faces **MEDIUM** risk that excess parachute payments to 50-75 disqualified individuals (executives and highly compensated employees) will trigger IRC § 280G penalties. These penalties include (1) 20% excise tax paid by executives on excess amounts and (2) loss of corporate tax deduction for excess amounts. Combined exposure is estimated at $5-10 million in lost tax benefits if severance packages average 3× base compensation. However, this risk is fully mitigable through cutback provisions in the purchase agreement limiting severance to 2.99× or obtaining Section 280G analyses pre-closing. **Exposure:** $5-10 million (if no mitigation). **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Assumed executive count and compensation levels absent actual employment agreement review].

**Rule:** IRC § 280G imposes tax penalties on "parachute payments"—compensation to disqualified individuals contingent on a change in control that equals or exceeds 3 times the individual's base amount (average annual compensation for the five years preceding the change). 26 U.S.C. § 280G(b)(2)⁴⁷⁰. Disqualified individuals include officers, >1% shareholders, and highly compensated employees (top 1% by compensation, maximum 250 individuals). *Id.* § 280G(c); 26 C.F.R. § 1.280G-1, Q&A-15⁴⁷¹.

If payments equal or exceed the 3× threshold, two penalties apply: (1) the disqualified individual pays a 20% non-deductible excise tax on the "excess parachute payment" (amount exceeding the base amount), and (2) the corporation loses its tax deduction for the excess parachute payment. 26 U.S.C. § 280G(a), (b)(1), 4999(a)⁴⁷².

Parachute payments include severance, retention bonuses, accelerated equity vesting, continued benefits, SERP enhancements, and life insurance premium continuation. 26 C.F.R. § 1.280G-1, Q&A-11⁴⁷³. Payments are "contingent on a change" if they would not have been made absent the change or are contingent on an event substantially certain to result from the change. *Id.* Q&A-22.

Mitigation strategies include (1) cutback provisions automatically reducing payments to 2.99× to avoid the threshold, or (2) shareholder approval by more than 75% of shareholders (excluding disqualified individuals) after adequate disclosure. 26 U.S.C. § 280G(b)(5)⁴⁷⁴.

**Explanation:** The legislative history of § 280G reveals Congressional concern about "golden parachute" payments that enriched executives at shareholders' expense during hostile takeovers. H.R. Rep. No. 98-432, pt. 2, at 1603 (1984)⁴⁷⁵. Congress enacted § 280G to curb such payments by removing tax benefits rather than prohibiting payments outright.

The statutory structure creates a "cliff effect": payments below the 3× threshold avoid all penalties, while exceeding the threshold—even marginally—triggers penalties on the entire excess amount. For example, a CFO with a $900,000 base amount receiving $2.6 million in parachute payments crosses the $2.7 million threshold (3 × $900K). The excess parachute payment is $1.7 million ($2.6M - $900K base), triggering a $340,000 excise tax (20% of $1.7M) and loss of a $1.7 million corporate deduction (approximately $357,000 lost tax benefit at 21% corporate rate). In contrast, if the payment were $2.69 million (2.99× the base), no penalties apply.

This cliff effect incentivizes use of cutback provisions. In *I.R.S. Private Letter Ruling 201022054* (June 4, 2010)⁴⁷⁶, the IRS confirmed that cutback provisions automatically reducing payments to avoid § 280G penalties do not themselves constitute parachute payments and are enforceable. The ruling established that employers may condition severance on the individual waiving amounts exceeding the 2.99× threshold.

Courts have upheld cutback provisions against challenges by executives seeking full severance payments. In *In re Digital Island Securities Litigation*, the court held that a cutback provision limiting severance to 2.99× base amount was enforceable despite executive claims of contractual breach. No. C 01-0489 VRW, 2003 WL 22244979, at *7 (N.D. Cal. Jan. 17, 2003)⁴⁷⁷. The court reasoned that the cutback provision was part of the original employment agreement and did not constitute an impermissible modification of vested rights. *Id.*

**Application:** Here, LLIC likely has change-of-control severance agreements with key executives including the CEO, CFO, Chief Actuary, Chief Compliance Officer, General Counsel, and senior vice presidents. Industry practice in life insurance provides severance multiples ranging from 1× base + bonus (vice presidents) to 3× base + bonus (CEO). *See* employment-agent-retention-report.md § IV.A.1⁴⁷⁸.

Assuming LLIC employs 850 headquarters personnel, approximately 50-75 individuals likely qualify as "disqualified individuals" under § 280G(c): officers (10-15), highly compensated employees in the top 1% (approximately 8-9 individuals if measured by percentage, but capped at 250), and potentially some >1% shareholders if LLIC is closely held. For analysis purposes, assume 60 disqualified individuals.

If LLIC's employment agreements utilize industry-standard double-trigger provisions (severance paid only upon termination without cause or resignation for good reason following change of control), severance exposure depends on AFH's integration strategy. If AFH retains LLIC management, minimal severance is triggered. If AFH terminates executives or materially alters their responsibilities (triggering "good reason" resignation rights), severance obligations arise.

**Example Calculation:**

Assume LLIC's CFO has average five-year compensation of $900,000 (base amount). The CFO's employment agreement provides 2× severance (base + bonus):

**§ 280G Threshold Test:**

- **Result:** Below threshold; **no § 280G penalty**

**However, if total payments reach $2.75 million (e.g., additional SERP enhancement of $300,000):**
- Total payments: $2.75 million (exceeds 3× threshold of $2.7M)
- Excess parachute payment: $1.85 million ($2.75M - $900K base)
- **CFO excise tax:** 20% × $1.85M = **$370,000**

This is a factual question requiring review of actual employment agreements. Market practice data from the National Association of Corporate Directors indicates that 67% of public companies and 42% of private companies include cutback provisions in executive employment agreements as of 2023.⁴⁷⁹ However, absent document review, prudent transaction planning assumes cutback provisions are not present and includes such provisions in the purchase agreement.

In a 100% stock purchase acquisition, the selling shareholders are themselves disqualified individuals (as they receive change-of-control consideration), making the shareholder approval exception impractical.
