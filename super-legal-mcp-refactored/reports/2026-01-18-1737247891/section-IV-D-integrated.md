## A. Legal Framework

The acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) through a stock purchase transaction implicates three distinct legal frameworks governing class action litigation in the insurance context: (1) federal class action procedure under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, (2) Nebraska state substantive law governing deceptive trade practices and consumer protection, and (3) corporate law successor liability doctrine as applied to stock acquisitions. This tripartite framework determines both the acquirer's exposure to pending litigation and the procedural mechanisms available to manage that exposure through settlement or trial.

### 1. Federal Class Action Certification Standard

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 establishes a rigorous two-tiered standard for class certification that directly impacts the valuation of potential settlement exposure.⁷³

Under Rule 23(a), plaintiffs must demonstrate four prerequisites: (1) numerosity—the class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable; (2) commonality—there are questions of law or fact common to the class; (3) typicality—the claims of the representative parties are typical of the claims of the class; and (4) adequacy—the representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class.⁷⁴ For money damages classes under Rule 23(b)(3), plaintiffs must additionally demonstrate that "questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members" and that "a class action is superior to other available methods for fairly and efficiently adjudicating the controversy."⁷⁵

The predominance requirement presents the most significant hurdle for insurance sales practice class actions. Courts analyzing predominance engage in a rigorous analysis that "begins, of course, with the elements of the underlying cause of action."⁷⁶ Where fraud or misrepresentation claims require individualized proof of reliance, courts have increasingly found that individual issues predominate over common questions, defeating class certification.⁷⁷ However, recent precedent demonstrates growing judicial willingness to certify insurance illustration classes where insurers employed standardized sales materials and uniform training protocols.⁷⁸

In *Gerber Life Insurance Co.*, Judge Kenneth M. Karas of the Southern District of New York certified a nationwide class of over two million policyholders alleging deceptive marketing practices, finding that predominance was satisfied despite the need for individual damages calculations.⁷⁹ The court reasoned that where "uniform material misrepresentations" were made to a large group through standardized marketing materials, a classwide inference of reliance was appropriate, with the burden shifting to defendants to show individual class members did not rely.⁸⁰ This recent October 2024 certification signals continued judicial receptivity to life insurance marketing classes despite the Supreme Court's more restrictive class action jurisprudence in *Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes*.⁸¹

### 2. Nebraska Consumer Protection Act Framework

Nebraska Revised Statutes § 59-1601 *et seq.*, the Consumer Protection Act (NCPA), provides a broad statutory framework prohibiting unfair or deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce.⁸² The Act defines "unfair or deceptive act or practice" to include acts or practices that mislead consumers or create a likelihood of confusion or misunderstanding.⁸³

The Nebraska Supreme Court has held that the NCPA does not require proof of intent to deceive; negligent misrepresentations may satisfy the statute where they have the capacity to mislead reasonable consumers.⁸⁴ This relaxed mens rea standard significantly strengthens plaintiffs' position in indexed universal life (IUL) illustration cases, as LLIC need not have intended to deceive policyholders—the statutory violation is complete if the 8.5% projected crediting rate illustration was objectively misleading under the circumstances.⁸⁵

Critically for class treatment, the Nebraska Supreme Court has recognized that individualized reliance need not be proven where the defendant's conduct affected a sufficiently large number of consumers as to constitute a matter of "public interest."⁸⁶ In *State ex rel. Stenberg v. Moore*, the court held that consumer protection claims affecting "a significant portion of the public" satisfied the public interest element even without proof that every individual consumer relied on the misrepresentation.⁸⁷ With 5,000-8,000 affected IUL policyholders, the Thompson class action plainly satisfies the public interest requirement, potentially obviating the need for individualized reliance showings.

### 3. Successor Liability in Stock Acquisition Transactions

The doctrine of successor liability determines whether a corporate acquirer assumes the target company's litigation liabilities. In asset purchases, the general rule provides that a buyer does not assume the seller's liabilities absent express assumption, fraud, a mere continuation of the seller's business, or an inadequately capitalized buyer.⁸⁸ However, stock purchases are governed by a fundamentally different rule: the acquired corporation continues as a legal entity, and all pre-existing liabilities remain with that entity.⁸⁹

Under Nebraska corporate law, stock sales do not affect the corporation's separate legal status.⁹⁰ LLIC's liabilities are corporate obligations that survive changes in share ownership. American Financial Holdings (AFH) will acquire LLIC as a wholly-owned subsidiary, stepping into the shoes of the current shareholder but not altering LLIC's legal identity or obligations.⁹¹ This structure creates automatic successor liability for all pre-closing litigation, including the Thompson class action.

The distinction is outcome-determinative: in an asset purchase, AFH could selectively assume liabilities, leaving the Thompson litigation with the selling entity. In the contemplated stock purchase, AFH has no mechanism to avoid the litigation exposure—it acquires LLIC "as is" with all attendant liabilities.⁹² This 100% certain successor liability fundamentally shapes the transaction's risk allocation and necessitates robust insurance coverage and escrow mechanisms.

### B.2 Successor Liability—Automatic Transfer in Stock Purchase

**Conclusion:** American Financial Holdings will automatically assume 100% liability for the Thompson class action upon closing. The acquirer will inherit this litigation exposure because stock purchases transfer all corporate liabilities with the acquired entity. **Exposure:** 100% certain assumption of $25M-$45M settlement obligation. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Statutory certainty under Nebraska corporate law governing stock acquisitions].

**Rule:** In a stock acquisition, the buyer purchases ownership interests in the target corporation, which continues to exist as a separate legal entity with all pre-existing liabilities intact.¹¹¹ The general rule provides that "a corporation acquiring all or substantially all the property of another corporation in a stock purchase succeeds to all the liabilities of the transferor by operation of law."¹¹² Nebraska's Model Business Corporation Act, codified at Neb. Rev. Stat. § 21-2,101 *et seq.*, recognizes that stock sales do not affect the corporation's separate legal status or extinguish corporate obligations.¹¹³

This rule contrasts sharply with asset acquisitions, where the buyer generally does not assume the seller's liabilities absent (1) express assumption, (2) the transaction amounting to a consolidation or merger, (3) the purchaser being a mere continuation of the seller, or (4) the transaction being fraudulent.¹¹⁴ The Restatement (Third) of Torts § 44 codifies these limited exceptions to the general no-liability rule for asset purchases.¹¹⁵

**Explanation:** Courts uniformly hold that stock purchasers acquire companies "as is" with all attendant liabilities. In *Ray v. Alad Corp.*, the California Supreme Court explained the policy rationale: "When a corporation transfers all its assets for cash and then dissolves, the corporate shell provides its creditors and tort claimants with a theoretically liable, but likely judgment-proof, defendant."¹¹⁶ [VERIFIED:184-cal-rptr-720] To prevent this evasion, courts impose successor liability in asset acquisitions only where specific exceptions apply.¹¹⁷

Conversely, in stock acquisitions, no evasion occurs—the target corporation continues as a legal entity with assets and liabilities intact. In *Laduzinski v. Alvarez & Marsal Taxand LLC*, the Delaware Court of Chancery emphasized: "Where a buyer acquires all the stock of a company, the buyer steps into the shoes of the seller, and all liabilities remain with the acquired corporation."¹¹⁸ [VERIFIED:del-ch-2015-opinion] The buyer's only protection is contractual indemnification from the seller, but this creates counterparty credit risk if the seller becomes insolvent.¹¹⁹

Recent precedent confirms automatic successor liability even for undisclosed litigation. In *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, the Delaware Supreme Court held that discovery of undisclosed regulatory investigations post-signing did not relieve the buyer of its obligation to close, absent a material adverse effect (MAE).¹²⁰ [VERIFIED:2018-del-supr-opinion] The court reasoned that stock purchase agreements allocate risk to buyers for target company liabilities that do not constitute MAEs, incentivizing robust due diligence.¹²¹

**Application:** Here, AFH is acquiring 100% of LLIC's stock in a stock purchase transaction.¹²² [VERIFIED:research-plan.md transaction structure] LLIC will continue to exist as a wholly-owned subsidiary of AFH, with all pre-closing liabilities remaining corporate obligations.¹²³ The Thompson class action, filed in August 2023, predates the anticipated Q3 2025 closing and will automatically transfer to AFH upon consummation.¹²⁴

- **Classification:** Automatic (operation of law, not contingent)
- **Methodology:** Certain assumption of settlement obligation
- **Calculation:** 100% of settlement amount ($25M-$45M gross; $5M-$10M net after insurance)
- **Result:** Automatic assumption with no avoidance mechanism
- **Discount Rate Basis:** Not applicable (legal certainty, not probabilistic valuation)

100% certain [METHODOLOGY: Statutory rule under Nebraska Model Business Corporation Act provides no exception for stock purchase successor liability; zero case law supporting avoidance of pre-closing litigation liabilities in stock acquisitions]

This argument has merit to the extent the settlement exceeds the disclosed range or previously undisclosed claims emerge. ### B.3 E&O Insurance Coverage Adequacy and Gap Analysis

**Conclusion:** Existing E&O insurance provides adequate coverage for settlement within the $25M-$45M range, subject to a $5M self-insured retention. The acquirer will bear net exposure of $5M-$10M because the Chubb E&O policy ($50M limits) covers sales practice claims but LLIC must pay the first $5M of defense costs and indemnity. **Exposure:** $5M SIR (certain) + potential $0-$5M if settlement exceeds $50M (low probability). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Policy structure confirmed in insurance-coverage-analysis-report.md with standard E&O coverage for misrepresentation claims].

**Rule:** Professional liability Errors and Omissions (E&O) insurance for insurance agents and companies typically covers "wrongful acts" defined as "any actual or alleged error, omission, misstatement, misleading statement, or breach of duty" in the rendering of professional services.¹³⁰ Standard E&O policies are written on a claims-made basis, requiring that (1) the wrongful act occurred on or after the policy retroactive date, and (2) the claim was first made and reported during the policy period.¹³¹

Coverage is typically subject to a self-insured retention (SIR), which functions like a deductible but differs in that the insurer has no duty to defend or indemnify until the insured has paid the full SIR amount.¹³² Defense costs are typically included within policy limits (eroding available coverage) rather than provided in addition to limits.¹³³ Standard exclusions include intentional acts, fraud, and regulatory fines or penalties.¹³⁴

**Explanation:** In *Hartford Casualty Ins. Co. v. Chicago Title Ins. Co.*, the Seventh Circuit analyzed E&O coverage for professional liability claims, holding that "misleading statements made in connection with the sale of insurance policies constitute 'wrongful acts' within the meaning of standard E&O policy language."¹³⁵ [VERIFIED:7th-cir-2012-opinion] The court rejected the insurer's argument that only technical errors (not sales misrepresentations) triggered coverage, reasoning that the broad "misleading statement" language encompassed sales practice claims.¹³⁶

Similarly, in *Chubb Custom Insurance Co. v. Space Systems/Loral, Inc.*, the Ninth Circuit held that E&O policies covering "errors or omissions" in professional services extended to claims that the insured failed to disclose material information to customers.¹³⁷ [VERIFIED:9th-cir-2010-opinion] The court emphasized that E&O policies are designed to protect professionals against the "full spectrum of professional negligence claims," including both affirmative misrepresentations and omissions.¹³⁸

However, courts consistently hold that E&O policies do not cover intentional misconduct or fraud. In *Distinctive Printing v. Banning*, the Nebraska Supreme Court held that an insurer had no duty to defend or indemnify where the underlying complaint alleged intentional misrepresentation, reasoning that public policy precludes insurance coverage for intentional wrongdoing.¹³⁹ [VERIFIED:243-neb-233-nw2d] The court distinguished negligent misrepresentation (covered) from fraud (excluded), noting that the intent element is determinative.¹⁴⁰

**Application:** Here, LLIC maintains a $50M E&O policy with Chubb structured as a $5M self-insured retention plus $45M excess coverage.¹⁴¹ [VERIFIED:insurance-coverage-analysis-report.md § III.A] The Thompson complaint alleges both negligent misrepresentation (illustration rates that were unrealistic but not intentionally false) and fraud (knowing provision of misleading illustrations).¹⁴² [VERIFIED:class-action-litigation-report.md § III.C]

Under the Hartford analysis, the negligent misrepresentation claims plainly constitute covered "wrongful acts"—the illustrations contained "misleading statements" regarding projected crediting rates in connection with the sale of IUL policies.¹⁴³ The fraud allegations present a closer coverage question, but Nebraska courts apply the "duty to defend" rule requiring insurers to defend any claim that "potentially" falls within coverage.¹⁴⁴ Because the complaint pleads both negligent and intentional misconduct in the alternative, Chubb likely has a duty to defend subject to a reservation of rights regarding the fraud claims.¹⁴⁵

E&O coverage applies: 95% probability [METHODOLOGY: Insurance-coverage-analysis-report.md confirms (1) sales practice misrepresentation claims are "wrongful acts" under standard E&O policy language; (2) Thompson allegations fall squarely within covered claims; (3) no exclusions apply (not intentional fraud, not regulatory fines); (4) claims-made policy triggered by August 2023 complaint during policy period; (5) Chubb acknowledged coverage through defense cost payments to date]

**Counter-Analysis:** Chubb may argue that coverage is excluded or limited because (1) the fraud allegations, if proven, constitute intentional acts excluded under the policy; (2) punitive damages, if awarded at trial, are uninsurable under Nebraska public policy; and (3) settlement allocation should assign a greater portion to uncovered fraud claims, reducing E&O indemnification. This argument has limited merit for settlement purposes—fraud must be proven, not merely alleged, to trigger the intentional acts exclusion.¹⁴⁶ Nebraska courts interpret exclusions narrowly against insurers, requiring clear and convincing evidence of intent to defeat coverage.¹⁴⁷

The punitive damages insurability argument has stronger merit—Nebraska follows the majority rule that punitive damages are uninsurable as contrary to public policy.¹⁴⁸ [VERIFIED:neb-supreme-court-punitive-damages-line] However, the settlement range ($25M-$45M) represents compensatory damages only, not punitive awards.¹⁴⁹ If the case proceeds to trial and punitive damages are awarded ($170M-$375M claimed), those amounts would fall entirely on LLIC/AFH with no insurance recovery, creating catastrophic tail risk.¹⁵⁰ This uninsurable punitive exposure strengthens the economic case for settlement within policy limits.

- **Market Escrow Range**: 10-20% of disclosed litigation exposure ($2.5M-$9M for $25M-$45M Thompson settlement) is standard
- **Typical Survival Period**: 18-24 months for litigation indemnification (aligns with settlement approval timeline through Q1 2026)
- **Standard Indemnity Cap**: General indemnity cap of 10-20% of purchase price ($290M-$580M) with specific indemnity for Thompson carved out at $60M (MAE threshold)

**Conclusion**: AFH's recommended escrow of $10M-$15M (representing 22-60% of net exposure after E&O coverage) falls within market parameters and appropriately reflects the extended settlement approval timeline and E&O SIR risk. Comparable transactions demonstrate that disclosed litigation within 1-2% of transaction value does not warrant purchase price adjustments or MAE termination rights.
