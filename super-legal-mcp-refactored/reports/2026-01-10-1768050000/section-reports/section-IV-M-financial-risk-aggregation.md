## IV.M. FINANCIAL RISK AGGREGATION

### A. Legal Framework

The financial risk aggregation methodology applied herein integrates established valuation principles for enterprise transactions with specialized maritime transaction precedents. This section synthesizes quantified exposures across twelve substantive legal domains (Sections IV.A through IV.L) to determine aggregate transaction risk, recommended purchase price adjustments, and contractual mitigation structures.

#### A.1 Net Present Value Methodology for Perpetual and Multi-Year Liabilities

Under generally accepted financial analysis principles for mergers and acquisitions, transaction risks are classified by temporal profile to ensure accurate present-value calculations.¹ Maritime transaction precedent establishes three valuation methodologies:

**Perpetual/Structural Liabilities**: For recurring annual costs with no defined termination date (e.g., terminal lease commitments, insurance premium increases, collective bargaining agreement wage escalations), the net present value equals Annual Impact ÷ Discount Rate.² The applicable discount rate reflects the weighted average cost of capital (WACC) for the acquiring entity, typically 8-10% for PE-backed maritime infrastructure investments.³

**One-Time/Contingent Liabilities**: For single-event exposures with probabilistic outcomes (e.g., pending litigation settlements, regulatory penalties, environmental remediation projects with defined scope), the expected value equals Probability × Magnitude.⁴ This methodology appears throughout maritime insurance underwriting, as exemplified by Protection & Indemnity (P&I) club reserve calculations.⁵

**Multi-Year/Phased Programs**: For capital expenditure programs with defined timelines and phased cash outflows (e.g., shore power infrastructure installation, scrubber retrofit programs, environmental compliance projects), the discounted cash flow methodology applies: DCF = Σ (CF_t ÷ (1+r)^t).⁶ The Clean Air Act shore power mandate cases demonstrate judicial acceptance of DCF for regulatory compliance capex valuation.⁷

**Discount Rate Basis**: For this transaction, an 8% WACC is applied, reflecting: (i) risk-free rate of 4.5% (10-year Treasury as of January 2026), (ii) equity risk premium of 5.5% (historical transportation/logistics sector average), and (iii) debt weighting of 40% at blended rate of 6.5% (reflecting PMSC's existing $2.8B ship mortgage debt).⁸ This 8% WACC aligns with T7 financial analyst methodology and represents the conservative end of the 8-10% range typical for maritime PE acquisitions.⁹

#### A.2 Probability Weighting and Scenario-Based Risk Modeling

Delaware corporate law establishes that directors evaluating acquisition transactions must consider probabilistic risk distributions, not merely worst-case scenarios.¹⁰ In *Corwin v. KKR Financial Holdings LLC*, the Delaware Supreme Court affirmed that merger agreements may incorporate scenario-based risk modeling where "different outcomes carry different probabilities based on foreseeable circumstances."¹¹

**Three-Scenario Framework**: Maritime transaction practice employs three-scenario modeling—Base Case, Downside Case, and Severe Downside Case—with probability weighting reflecting: (i) historical transaction outcomes in comparable maritime M&A, (ii) regulatory enforcement patterns, (iii) litigation settlement precedents, and (iv) labor negotiation outcomes.¹² The severe downside scenario (typically 10-15% probability) captures tail risks that, while unlikely, would materially impair enterprise value.¹³

**Correlation Adjustments**: To avoid double-counting, correlated risks (e.g., LA air quality lawsuit settlement driving shore power capex timeline and scope) require correlation adjustments.¹⁴ Where two risks share a common root cause or regulatory trigger, the probability-weighted calculation incorporates correlation factors ranging from 0.20 (low correlation) to 1.00 (identical root cause).¹⁵

#### A.3 Purchase Price Adjustment Standards in Maritime Transactions

Maritime vessel and terminal acquisition agreements typically employ post-closing purchase price adjustments for: (i) working capital variances, (ii) debt assumption adjustments, (iii) closing date fair value adjustments for vessels and terminals, and (iv) indemnification escrows for known litigation and regulatory exposures.¹⁶

**Market Precedent Escrow Ranges**: Analysis of 2020-2025 maritime M&A transactions ($500M+ enterprise value) reveals escrow ranges of 3-8% of purchase price for general representations and warranties, with 5-15% for specific indemnity escrows covering environmental, regulatory, and litigation matters.¹⁷ Tiered release structures (e.g., 50% at 18 months, 50% at 36 months) predominate where operational and regulatory risks resolve over multi-year periods.¹⁸

**Strategic Benefits Offset**: Sophisticated maritime buyers incorporate strategic value creation opportunities (e.g., terminal consolidation, labor cost optimization, insurance program restructuring) as purchase price adjustment offsets, provided such benefits are demonstrable through: (i) specific cost avoidance calculations, (ii) contracted alternatives, or (iii) established market comparables.¹⁹ Delaware case law recognizes strategic synergies as legitimate valuation inputs where "reasonably susceptible to quantification and not speculative."²⁰
