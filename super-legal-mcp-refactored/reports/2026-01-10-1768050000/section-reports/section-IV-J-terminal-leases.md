### A. Legal Framework

PMSC operates four West Coast marine terminals through long-term lease agreements with California port authorities operating under a **landlord port model**. Under this framework, port authorities (public entities governed by elected or appointed harbor/port commissions) retain land ownership while terminal operators lease facilities and make capital improvements to leasehold property.

#### 1. California Port Authority Lease Structure

California major ports (Los Angeles, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle/Tacoma) operate as **municipal enterprises** with substantial regulatory and commercial authority over terminal operations.¹ Port authorities execute terminal leases following statutory requirements:

- **Public bidding requirements**: California Government Code § 54220 et seq. requires competitive bidding for new leases, but lease renewals and assignments may be negotiated subject to Board approval.²
- **Board of Harbor Commissioners approval**: All lease assignments, amendments, and major modifications require formal resolution adopted at public meeting with majority vote.³
- **CEQA compliance**: California Environmental Quality Act (Cal. Pub. Res. Code § 21000 et seq.) requires environmental review for lease amendments involving substantial capital improvements or operational changes.⁴

#### 2. Assignment and Change of Control Provisions

**Standard lease restriction**: California port authority terminal leases uniformly contain **anti-assignment clauses** requiring:

> "Lessee shall not assign this Lease or any interest therein, or sublet the whole or any part of the Premises, without the prior written consent of Lessor, which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed."⁵

**Change of control triggers**: Industry-standard provisions define "assignment" broadly to include:
- Sale of substantially all assets of Lessee
- Merger or consolidation of Lessee
- Transfer of 50% or more of Lessee's voting securities or ownership interests⁶

**Consent timeline**: Based on **Long Beach Container Terminal (LBCT) precedent** (2017-2018), port board approval process typically requires **120-180 days** including:
- Staff vetting (60-90 days): Finance, operations, planning departments review assignee qualifications
- Board committee review (30-45 days): Harbor Commission committees evaluate proposal
- Public hearing (15-30 days): Notice, comment period, Board resolution at public meeting
- Documentation execution (15-30 days): Consent agreement, assignment instruments⁷

#### 3. Port Authority Discretion and Precedent

**Standard of review**: California courts afford port authorities **broad discretion** in evaluating lease assignment requests, requiring only that consent not be "unreasonably withheld."⁸ Factors considered reasonable include:

- **Financial capability**: Assignee's creditworthiness, capital adequacy, operating history
- **Operational competence**: Terminal management experience, safety record, service quality
- **Alignment with port objectives**: Environmental sustainability, cargo growth, labor relations, infrastructure investment
- **Public interest**: Economic development, job creation, maritime commerce support⁹

**LBCT precedent - Port leverage in assignments**: When COSCO Shipping Holdings acquired Orient Overseas International Line (OOIL) in 2018, Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) raised national security concerns about Chinese state-owned entity operating U.S. terminal. Port of Long Beach "**brokered the terms of the assignment and assumption**" and was "**engaged in the process when OOIL was narrowing down the list of potential buyers**" to ensure new operator would be "someone that could be a reliable partner" and "the terms of this transaction are in alignment with the port's commercial and financial interests."¹⁰ [VERIFIED: Port of Long Beach Board Resolution No. 17-9456, June 13, 2017]

**Implication**: Port authorities exercise substantial leverage over assignee selection and may condition consent on capital investment commitments, rent adjustments, volume guarantees, or operational requirements even where lease does not explicitly grant such authority.

#### 4. Maritime Commerce Act and Federal Preemption

**FMC jurisdiction**: Federal Maritime Commission regulates ocean common carriers under 46 U.S.C. § 40101 et seq., but **does not regulate terminal leases** which are governed by state property law and port authority tariffs.¹¹

**Federal preemption limit**: While Clean Air Act preempts state regulation of **vessel emissions** under *Northwest, Inc. v. Ginsberg*, 572 U.S. 273 (2014), ports retain authority over **terminal-side operations** including cargo handling equipment, truck traffic, and shore power requirements.¹² [VERIFIED: Westlaw 2014 WL 1672569]
