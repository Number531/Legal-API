### A. Legal Framework

The International Longshore and Warehouse Union (ILWU) Pacific Coast Longshore Agreement governs labor relations for all West Coast terminal operators through the Pacific Maritime Association (PMA), a multi-employer bargaining unit representing terminal operators and shipping companies at 30 U.S. West Coast ports. The current coastwide agreement, ratified August 2023, runs from July 1, 2022 through July 1, 2028, covering approximately 22,000 dockworkers system-wide, including PMSC's 3,200 ILWU-represented longshoremen across four terminals (Los Angeles, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle).¹

The National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), 29 U.S.C. § 151 *et seq.*, establishes the legal framework governing labor relations in the acquisition context. Under NLRA § 8(a)(5), employers must "bargain collectively with the representatives of [their] employees."² The Supreme Court's successor employer doctrine, articulated in *NLRB v. Burns Int'l Security Services*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972), and refined in *Fall River Dyeing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987), creates mandatory bargaining obligations for acquirers who continue the seller's business operations with substantially the same workforce.³

**Key statutory and contractual provisions:**

**NLRA § 8(a)(5) Bargaining Obligation:** An employer commits an unfair labor practice if it refuses "to bargain collectively with the representatives of [its] employees, subject to the provisions of section [159(a)] of this title." 29 U.S.C. § 158(a)(5).⁴ The duty to bargain attaches when the employer hires a "substantial and representative complement" of the predecessor's unionized workforce, typically defined as a majority.⁵

**ILWU-PMA Pacific Coast Agreement (2022-2028):** The six-year contract provides ILWU members with a 32% wage increase over the contract term, escalating average full-time registered longshoreman compensation from $197,514 (2022 baseline) to approximately $260,000 by 2029, plus a $70 million one-time "hero bonus" for pandemic service.⁶ The agreement grants PMA members the right to introduce automation and new technologies in exchange for assigning maintenance and repair work to ILWU members, a framework established in the 2008 agreement and carried forward in subsequent contracts.⁷

**PMA Membership Requirement:** To operate West Coast terminals with ILWU labor, Global Logistics Partners must join the Pacific Maritime Association, which bargains collectively on behalf of all member terminal operators. Individual operators cannot negotiate separate agreements with the ILWU; all are bound by the coastwide PMA-ILWU Master Agreement.⁸ PMA membership application typically requires 30-60 days for approval.⁹

**Taft-Hartley Act Emergency Provisions:** The Labor Management Relations Act of 1947, 29 U.S.C. § 176, authorizes the President to seek an 80-day injunction against strikes or lockouts that "imperil the national health or safety."¹⁰ This provision was invoked during the 2002 West Coast port lockout, establishing precedent for federal intervention in prolonged ILWU-PMA disputes.¹¹

#### B.1 Global Logistics Partners Will Be Deemed Successor Employer Under NLRA with Mandatory Duty to Recognize and Bargain with ILWU

**Conclusion:** Global Logistics Partners LLC will be deemed a **successor employer** under the National Labor Relations Act with a mandatory duty to recognize the ILWU as the bargaining representative for PMSC's 3,200 longshoremen and to bargain in good faith over terms and conditions of employment. This obligation attaches immediately upon acquiring a "substantial and representative complement" of PMSC's ILWU workforce—realistically, substantially all 3,200 workers—because West Coast terminal operations cannot function without ILWU-represented labor. **Severity: MEDIUM.** **Exposure:** No direct financial liability, but failure to recognize successor obligations constitutes an unfair labor practice under NLRA § 8(a)(5), subjecting the acquirer to National Labor Relations Board proceedings, cease-and-desist orders, and potential back-pay liability for unilateral changes to employment terms. **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: Statutory certainty under *Burns*/*Fall River Dyeing* precedent; substantial continuity and majority workforce tests unquestionably met].

**Rule:** Under the NLRA successor employer doctrine, an acquiring company assumes bargaining obligations when it satisfies two elements: (1) **substantial continuity** between the predecessor's and successor's business operations, and (2) the successor employs a **majority** of its workforce from the predecessor's bargaining unit. *Fall River Dyeing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27, 43 (1987).¹² The Supreme Court emphasized that "the focus is on whether there is 'substantial continuity' between the enterprises," examining factors including continuity in: (a) the business operations, (b) the work force, (c) the jobs, (d) supervision, (e) working conditions, (f) machinery and equipment, and (g) customers. *Id.* at 43.¹³

The successor employer's duty to bargain arises when it has hired a "substantial and representative complement" of its workforce, meaning enough employees "to carry on the normal operations" and "represent a substantial percentage of the predecessor's employees." *Id.* at 52 n.7.¹⁴ Critically, under *Burns*, the successor is **not automatically bound** by the substantive terms of the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement, unless the successor makes "perfectly clear" that it intends to retain all employees without changing terms and conditions of employment. *NLRB v. Burns Int'l Security Servs.*, 406 U.S. 272, 294-95 (1972).¹⁵

**Explanation:** In *Fall River Dyeing*, the Supreme Court found successorship where Sterlingwear purchased substantially all assets of Fall River Dyeing and continued the same fabric dyeing operations with 53 of Fall River's 55 former employees (96% continuity) plus 52 new hires, for a total of 107 employees of whom 49.5% were former Fall River workers. 482 U.S. at 34.¹⁶ The Court held that hiring a majority of the predecessor's employees in key jobs was dispositive: "In short, the focus is on whether the majority of the successor's employees were employed by the predecessor." *Id.* at 48.¹⁷ The Court rejected the employer's argument that successorship should be determined solely at the moment of takeover, holding instead that the appropriate measuring point is when the successor reaches a "substantial and representative complement" of its workforce. *Id.* at 50-51.¹⁸

The Ninth Circuit applied this framework in *NLRB v. Foodway of El Paso*, 496 F.2d 117 (9th Cir. 1974), finding successorship where the new operator of a grocery store retained 22 of 40 former employees (55%) and continued substantially the same operations with minimal changes to product lines or store layout.¹⁹ Similarly, in *Stardyne, Inc. v. NLRB*, 41 F.3d 141, 144 (3rd Cir. 1994), the Third Circuit found successorship where the acquirer initially employed only 10 of the predecessor's 22 bargaining unit members (45%), but planned to hire additional former employees as business ramped up, demonstrating an intent to achieve a representative complement.²⁰

Courts have consistently held that continuity of the *business operations* is more important than continuity of *ownership*. In *Howard Johnson Co. v. Detroit Local Joint Executive Board*, 417 U.S. 249, 263 (1974), the Supreme Court found **no successorship** where the new operator retained only 9 of 53 former employees (17%), concluding that "there was no substantial continuity of identity in the business enterprise before and after the sale."²¹ This negative precedent confirms that majority workforce retention is essential.

The Board has clarified that the successor's duty to bargain does not arise until it has hired a substantial and representative complement. *Spruce Up Corp.*, 209 NLRB 194, 195 (1974).²² This allows the successor to set initial terms and conditions of employment *before* hiring the bulk of its workforce, provided it does not give employees the impression that all will be retained under existing terms (the "perfectly clear" exception). *Burns*, 406 U.S. at 294-95.²³

**Application:** Here, Global Logistics Partners unquestionably satisfies both prongs of the *Fall River Dyeing* test. **Substantial continuity** exists because the acquirer will continue PMSC's terminal operations in unchanged form: (1) **same terminals** under existing Port leases at Los Angeles (Pier 300), Long Beach (Pier J), Oakland (Berth 24-26), and Seattle (Terminal 46); (2) **same services**—containerized cargo handling, stevedoring, marshalling, gate operations; (3) **same equipment**—rubber-tired gantry cranes, reach stackers, chassis, container handling equipment at each facility; (4) **same customers** via assumption of 850 FMC service contracts covering Asia-to-West Coast trade lanes; (5) **same workforce composition**—3,200 ILWU longshoremen performing identical job classifications (crane operators, lashers, clerks, mechanics); and (6) **same operational footprint**—5.6M TEU annual capacity with 4.7M TEU throughput (FY2024) across the four-terminal network.²⁴

The facts here present even stronger continuity than *Fall River Dyeing*, where the successor reduced the workforce from 105 to 55 employees and changed product focus. 482 U.S. at 34.²⁵ Global Logistics Partners acquires PMSC's terminal operations *wholesale*, with no reduction in terminal count, capacity, or service offerings.

**Majority workforce retention** is effectively certain. Global Logistics Partners cannot operate West Coast terminals without ILWU-represented labor because: (1) the ILWU represents **all longshoremen** at every major West Coast port through the coastwide PMA-ILWU agreement; (2) **no non-union alternative** exists—attempting to operate with non-union labor would trigger immediate coastwide ILWU strike under the PMA mutual aid pact; (3) **dispatching through ILWU hiring halls** is required for all longshore positions under the Master Agreement; and (4) **specialized terminal knowledge**—PMSC's 3,200 longshoremen possess terminal-specific knowledge of gate systems, crane operations, vessel stowage plans, and customer requirements that cannot be readily replicated with new hires.²⁶

The employment-labor specialist report confirms operational necessity: "Terminal operations require the same 3,200 ILWU longshoremen; mass termination not feasible."²⁷ PMSC's ILWU workforce represents 37.6% of the company's 8,500 total employees, a proportion far exceeding the 49.5% predecessor-employee ratio in *Fall River Dyeing* that the Supreme Court found sufficient. 482 U.S. at 48.²⁸

Global Logistics Partners will hire a "substantial and representative complement" immediately upon closing because terminal operations require full ILWU crews from day one. Unlike *Stardyne*, where the successor gradually ramped up employment, 41 F.3d at 144,²⁹ or *Spruce Up*, where hiring occurred over several months, 209 NLRB at 195,³⁰ PMSC's terminals generate $947M annual revenue ($2.59M daily) and cannot suspend operations during a transition period.³¹ The acquirer will retain 95-100% of PMSC's ILWU workforce on closing.

#### B.2 ILWU Strike or Slowdown During 2027 Negotiations Presents 40-50% Probability of Revenue Disruptions with $75M-$233M Gross Exposure

**Conclusion:** The ILWU Pacific Coast Longshore Agreement expires July 1, 2028, with negotiations expected to commence in 2027 (12-18 months before expiration per historical pattern). Based on analysis of the three most recent contract cycles—2002 PMA lockout (10 days, $10B U.S. economic loss), 2014-2015 slowdown (9 months of work-to-rule disruptions), and 2022-2023 negotiations (13 months with rolling terminal shutdowns)—there is **40-50% probability** of slowdowns or work disruptions during 2027 negotiations and **20-30% probability** of a full work stoppage exceeding 30 days. **Severity: HIGH.** **Exposure:** $75M-$233M gross revenue loss for PMSC's four terminals, assuming 30-day to 90-day disruption duration. **Probability-weighted exposure: $76.3M** (calculated as 40% × $77.7M + 10% × $233M). **Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH** [METHODOLOGY: Historical precedent analysis of three contract cycles spanning 23 years; ILWU's 50-year pattern of avoiding full strikes in favor of slowdowns; federal intervention precedent via Taft-Hartley 2002].

**Rule:** The NLRA protects employee rights to strike under § 7, which guarantees employees the right "to engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection." 29 U.S.C. § 157.³⁸ Section 13 of the NLRA preserves the right to strike: "Nothing in this subchapter, except as specifically provided for herein, shall be construed so as either to interfere with or impede or diminish in any way the right to strike." 29 U.S.C. § 163.³⁹ However, the Taft-Hartley Act § 206 authorizes presidential intervention when a strike "will imperil the national health or safety," permitting the President to seek an injunction for up to 80 days. 29 U.S.C. § 176.⁴⁰

Unions retain the tactical right to employ "work to the rule" slowdowns—strictly complying with all safety protocols, work rules, and contract provisions in a manner that reduces productivity—as long as they do not constitute a *partial strike* violating contractual no-strike clauses. The NLRB distinguishes between protected slowdowns and unprotected partial strikes based on whether employees continue to perform *all* contractual duties. *NLRB v. Insurance Agents' Int'l Union*, 361 U.S. 477, 488-90 (1960) (union's on-the-job conduct, though disruptive, was protected activity because employees continued to perform job duties).⁴¹

**Explanation:** The 2002 West Coast port labor dispute provides the clearest precedent for federal intervention. On September 27, 2002, the Pacific Maritime Association **locked out** ILWU workers at all 29 West Coast ports, alleging that the union was engaged in a coordinated slowdown through strict enforcement of safety rules and reduced crane productivity. The 10-day lockout cost the U.S. economy an estimated $10 billion (~$900M per day).⁴² President George W. Bush invoked the Taft-Hartley Act on October 7, 2002, directing the Attorney General to seek an injunction ordering ports reopened. The U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California granted the injunction on October 8, 2002, and the Ninth Circuit upheld it, finding that the port closure "imperiled the national health and safety" by disrupting military shipments, agricultural exports, and manufacturing supply chains.⁴³

The 2014-2015 contract negotiation followed a different pattern. When the ILWU-PMA agreement expired July 1, 2014, the parties continued negotiations while ILWU members worked without a contract for **nine months**. Rather than a formal strike, the ILWU employed **work-to-rule tactics**—loading and unloading containers at dramatically reduced speeds, strictly enforcing meal and break periods, and conducting detailed safety inspections before operating equipment.⁴⁴ West Coast ports experienced months of congestion, with vessels anchored offshore waiting for berths and chassis shortages crippling inland transportation. Labor Secretary Tom Perez personally mediated negotiations in San Francisco for weeks, ultimately brokering a tentative agreement in February 2015.⁴⁵ Significantly, **no Taft-Hartley intervention occurred** despite the prolonged disruption, suggesting that slowdowns fall below the threshold for federal emergency action.

The 2022-2023 contract cycle demonstrated the ILWU's continued reliance on tactical disruptions short of full strikes. The agreement expired July 1, 2022, but negotiations extended for **13 months** until the August 2023 ratification.⁴⁶ During this period, the ILWU: (1) conducted **24-hour terminal shutdowns** at the Port of Oakland in June 2023, citing "safety concerns" that idled all container operations;⁴⁷ (2) eliminated **staggered lunch breaks** at LA/Long Beach terminals in March 2023, effectively adding two hours of closure time daily when all workers took lunch simultaneously;⁴⁸ and (3) coordinated **crane operator "sick-outs"** that reduced gate capacity by 30-40% on peak shipping days.⁴⁹ Crucially, these disruptions occurred **without violating the no-strike clause** of the expired agreement because employees continued to report for scheduled shifts—they simply enforced contractual work rules in a manner that maximized operational disruption.

Comparative analysis of East Coast and Gulf Coast port labor relations is instructive. The International Longshoremen's Association (ILA), which represents dockworkers at East Coast and Gulf ports, conducted a three-day strike in October 2024 over automation provisions in contract negotiations, shutting down 36 ports from Maine to Texas.⁵⁰ However, the ILA historically engages in shorter, more targeted strikes compared to the ILWU's preference for prolonged slowdowns.⁵¹ The difference reflects the ILWU's strategic calculation that extended slowdowns are more effective than brief strikes in extracting concessions, while remaining below the threshold that triggers Taft-Hartley intervention.

**Application:** PMSC's exposure to ILWU labor disruptions during 2027 negotiations is **high** because: (1) the four West Coast terminals (LA, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle) generate **$947M annual revenue** ($2.59M daily), representing 29.6% of PMSC's total $3,200M enterprise revenue;⁵² (2) all four terminals are **simultaneously vulnerable** to coastwide ILWU actions coordinated by the Pacific Coast District;⁵³ (3) the 2022-2028 contract's **automation provisions remain contentious**, with the ILWU stating it is "militant and resolute in our fight against automation" despite agreeing to automation frameworks in prior contracts;⁵⁴ and (4) three automated terminals in the LA/Long Beach complex (LBCT, TraPac LA, APM Terminals Pier 400) provide concrete evidence of job losses, with an ILWU-funded study documenting **572 FTE jobs eliminated annually** at LBCT and TraPac during 2020-2021.⁵⁵

#### B.3 ILWU Wage Escalation of $200M-$226M (2024-2028) Is Contractually Certain and Should Be Treated as Baseline Operating Cost, Not Contingent Transaction Risk

**Conclusion:** The 2022-2028 ILWU-PMA Pacific Coast Longshore Agreement mandates a **32% wage increase** over the six-year contract term, escalating PMSC's aggregate ILWU compensation from $632M-$704M (2024 baseline) to $832M-$930M (2028), for a cumulative increase of **$200M-$226M**. This wage escalation is **contractually certain** under the executed collective bargaining agreement and is **not contingent** on the transaction's consummation. **Severity: MEDIUM (financial impact but not transaction risk).** **Exposure:** $200M-$226M cumulative increase in ILWU labor costs over 2024-2028, representing $40M-$45M average annual increase. **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: Contractual certainty; 32% wage schedule explicitly enumerated in 2022-2028 ILWU-PMA agreement, verified by Crawford Compensation Study showing 2022 baseline of $197,514 average annual earnings].

**Rule:** Collective bargaining agreements are contracts governed by ordinary principles of contract law, subject to the NLRA's overlay requiring employers to honor agreements negotiated in good faith. 29 U.S.C. § 158(d) prohibits employers from unilaterally changing terms and conditions of employment covered by a collective bargaining agreement during the agreement's term.⁷⁰ Courts enforce CBA wage schedules as contractual obligations. *United Steelworkers v. Warrior & Gulf Navigation Co.*, 363 U.S. 574, 580 (1960) ("A collective bargaining agreement is an effort to erect a system of industrial self-government.").⁷¹

When an acquirer assumes an existing collective bargaining agreement—either by becoming a "perfectly clear successor" under *Burns* or by explicit contractual assumption—the acquirer steps into the seller's shoes and becomes bound by all substantive terms, including wage schedules and benefit contributions. *John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. Livingston*, 376 U.S. 543, 550-51 (1964).⁷²

**Explanation:** The Supreme Court in *John Wiley* addressed whether a merger obligated the surviving company to arbitrate grievances under the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement. 376 U.S. at 545-46.⁷³ The Court held that when "one company has succeeded another," the successor may be required to arbitrate under the predecessor's agreement, particularly where there is "substantial continuity of operation and identity of the work force." *Id.* at 551.⁷⁴ The Court emphasized that "the impressive policy considerations favoring arbitration" in labor relations require successors to honor dispute resolution mechanisms in assumed CBAs. *Id.* at 549.⁷⁵

In *Burns*, the Supreme Court distinguished between successor employers' duty to **bargain** (mandatory) and successor employers' duty to **assume existing CBA terms** (discretionary unless "perfectly clear"). 406 U.S. at 284-85.⁷⁶ However, the Court acknowledged that successors who *elect* to assume existing agreements—either expressly or by conduct leading employees to believe terms will continue unchanged—become fully bound by those terms. *Id.* at 294-95.⁷⁷

Lower courts have applied this principle to wage escalation clauses. In *NLRB v. Superior Sprinkler, Inc.*, 583 F.2d 469, 472 (9th Cir. 1978), the Ninth Circuit enforced a predecessor's CBA wage schedule against a successor who continued the same business operations with the same workforce but attempted to freeze wages at pre-contract levels.⁷⁸ The court held that by hiring the predecessor's employees and continuing operations without clearly communicating new terms, the successor became a "perfectly clear" successor bound by the existing wage schedule. *Id.* at 472-73.⁷⁹

Similarly, in *Foodway of El Paso*, the Ninth Circuit found that a successor grocery store operator who retained 55% of the predecessor's workforce and continued substantially the same operations was obligated to recognize the union and could not unilaterally reduce wages below CBA levels. 496 F.2d at 119-20.⁸⁰

**Application:** Global Logistics Partners faces **no discretion** on the $200M-$226M wage escalation because: (1) the escalation is **contractually locked in** under the 2022-2028 ILWU-PMA agreement for all PMA member employers operating West Coast terminals;⁸¹ (2) the acquirer must **join the PMA** to operate with ILWU labor, automatically binding it to the coastwide agreement;⁸² (3) the acquirer **cannot negotiate individual wage rates** separate from the PMA-ILWU master agreement—all West Coast terminal operators pay identical hourly rates by job classification;⁸³ and (4) attempting to deviate from the agreement would constitute an NLRA § 8(a)(5) refusal to bargain in good faith and trigger immediate ILWU strike authorization.⁸⁴

1. **Opening Position**: $75M escrow (29-day revenue coverage) held through July 1, 2029 (first anniversary of 2028 contract expiration), 50/50 tiered release upon contract ratification and 12-month post-ratification. Labor disruption indemnity cap at $75M.

3. **Walk-Away**: $40M escrow minimum (15-day revenue coverage). Below $40M, ILWU strike risk becomes unacceptable without alternative mitigation (e.g., $100M purchase price reduction in lieu of escrow, or seller-funded labor dispute insurance policy for 36 months post-closing).

4. **Leverage Points**:
   - Historical precedent: 100% of recent contract cycles included disruptions, making seller's "no strike in 50 years" argument factually misleading.
   - Timing risk: 2027 negotiations occur 12-18 months post-closing, during integration when PMSC most vulnerable to customer defections.
   - Asymmetric risk: Seller exits deal and avoids 2027 negotiations entirely; buyer bears full downside of labor disputes without seller continuing involvement.
   - Comparable transactions: SSA Marine/Carrix deal ($40M escrow for labor risk) and other maritime M&A establish market precedent for labor escrows in ILWU-represented terminal acquisitions.

**Response Playbook:**

- **If seller argues strike risk <20%**: Counter with analysis of three consecutive contract cycles with disruptions (2002 lockout, 2014-15 slowdown, 2022-23 rolling shutdowns), demonstrating 100% historical frequency. "We need assurance the contract is working in practice, not just ratified on paper."

- **If seller refuses escrow entirely**: Propose purchase price reduction alternative: "$75M escrow or $100M purchase price reduction—you choose. We're not closing this deal bearing 100% of 2027 strike risk with zero seller participation." Explain that escrow with tiered release gives seller upside (recovers funds if no strike) whereas purchase price reduction is permanent.

- **If seller proposes labor risk insurance instead of escrow**: Agree to split premium cost 50/50 if seller obtains quote for $75M labor disruption coverage from London marine insurance market. Likely no viable insurance product exists (strikes by insured's own employees universally excluded), which demonstrates need for escrow as only practical risk allocation mechanism.
