### A. Legal Framework

The acquisition of Pacific Maritime Services Corporation (PMSC) by Global Logistics Partners LLC requires navigating complex maritime finance law governing $2.8 billion in preferred ship mortgages secured by PMSC's 22-vessel fleet. This transaction implicates three core federal statutory regimes: (1) the change-of-control consent requirement under 46 U.S.C. § 31321(d), (2) maritime lien priority under 46 U.S.C. § 31341, and (3) the interplay between vessel financing and regulatory compliance obligations under Jones Act cabotage rules.

#### Ship Mortgage Recording and Preferred Status

A "preferred ship mortgage" is a maritime security interest recorded with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center under 46 U.S.C. § 31321.¹ To achieve preferred status, the mortgage must: (1) cover a documented vessel of at least five net tons, (2) be executed by the vessel owner, (3) secure a loan made in good faith and without notice of adverse claims, and (4) be recorded before competing interests arise.² Preferred ship mortgages grant lenders in rem jurisdiction against the vessel itself—the cornerstone of ship finance, enabling vessel arrest and judicial sale if the borrower defaults.³

**Change of Control Provisions - 46 U.S.C. § 31321(d):**

Federal maritime law does not statutorily mandate lender consent for ownership changes. However, **virtually all commercial ship mortgage agreements contain contractual covenants requiring borrower notification and lender consent for any "change of control" transaction**, defined as transfer of more than 50% equity ownership, change of management control, or alteration of vessel operations.⁴ While not codified in § 31321, these covenants are universally enforceable under contract law and represent industry-standard practice in ship finance.⁵

**Commercial Rationale for Consent Requirements:**

Ship mortgage lenders require change-of-control consent for three core reasons: (1) **credit risk reassessment**—lenders underwrite loans based on specific borrower management expertise, operating history, and financial strength; (2) **regulatory compliance verification**—new owners may have different risk tolerance for Jones Act compliance, environmental violations, or safety standards; and (3) **repricing opportunity**—change of control constitutes a material event permitting lenders to demand higher interest rates reflecting current market conditions or refuse consent entirely (forcing refinancing at prevailing rates).⁶

**Consequences of Proceeding Without Consent:**

If PMSC and Global Logistics Partners close the transaction without obtaining required lender consent, consequences include: (1) **immediate acceleration**—lenders may declare entire $2.8 billion principal balance immediately due and payable; (2) **default interest**—penalty rates (typically +2-5% over contract rate) apply from date of breach; (3) **cross-default cascade**—if one loan agreement triggers default, cross-default provisions in other financing agreements (working capital facilities, terminal leases with financial covenants) may cascade across PMSC's capital structure; and (4) **vessel arrest**—lenders may invoke in rem jurisdiction, arresting vessels to secure debt repayment.⁷

#### Maritime Lien Priority Hierarchy

Federal maritime law establishes a strict hierarchy of claims against vessel assets under 46 U.S.C. § 31341, with profound implications for mortgage lender security:

**Tier 1 - Super-Priority Maritime Liens (Superior to Mortgages):**⁸
1. Crew wages, master salaries, and vessel employee compensation
2. Salvage claims (vessel rescue operations)
3. Maritime tort claims (personal injury, property damage including Section 905(b) longshoreman injuries)
4. General average contributions (proportionate cargo loss-sharing)
5. Necessaries (fuel, repairs, provisions supplied to vessel)

**Tier 2 - Preferred Ship Mortgages:**
PMSC's $2.8 billion debt secured by 22 vessels falls into this category, subordinate to all Tier 1 liens.⁹

**Tier 3 - General Maritime Liens and Unsecured Creditors:**
All other claims against vessel assets.

**Critical Principle:**
"Maritime liens for crew wages, salvage, and certain tort claims rank ABOVE preferred ship mortgages."¹⁰ This means that if a vessel is arrested and sold at judicial auction, Tier 1 lienholders are paid first from sale proceeds, with mortgage lenders receiving only residual amounts. This super-priority exists because crew members and tort victims are **involuntary creditors** who did not negotiate contractual protections, whereas mortgage lenders are sophisticated commercial parties who voluntarily assumed subordination risk.¹¹

**Policy Rationale:**

The Supreme Court has explained that maritime lien super-priority protects: (1) seafarers who work aboard vessels and cannot easily abandon employment if unpaid; (2) salvors who risk their own vessels to rescue ships in distress; and (3) injured third parties (longshoremen, passengers, cargo owners) who have no contractual relationship with the vessel owner.¹² Courts have consistently held that "those who contribute to a vessel's preservation through labor or rescue services should be paid before financiers who merely extended credit."¹³

**Impact on PMSC's $2.8B Mortgage Security:**

If Martinez v. PMSC results in a $5 million judgment (within the $2M-$5M expected verdict range), Martinez obtains a Tier 1 maritime tort lien superior to PMSC's preferred mortgages.¹⁴ In a hypothetical forced sale scenario: assume the injury occurred aboard a container vessel valued at $186 million (average per-vessel value of PMSC's 22-vessel fleet worth $4.1 billion); judicial auction achieves $150 million (typical 20% discount below fair market value due to forced-sale conditions); distribution of proceeds would allocate $5 million to Martinez (Tier 1) first, leaving $145 million for the preferred mortgage lender (Tier 2).¹⁵ If the mortgage loan secured by this specific vessel totaled $186 million (loan-to-value ratio of 100%), the lender suffers a $41 million shortfall ($186M loan minus $145M recovery).

This subordination risk is a core concern for ship mortgage lenders reviewing the PMSC acquisition. Unresolved tort liabilities like Martinez (trial scheduled Q3 2025, verdict reasonably likely by closing date February 28, 2026) erode vessel collateral value, reducing lender recovery in bankruptcy or foreclosure scenarios. Lenders may condition consent on Martinez case settlement or establishment of escrow to neutralize the contingent lien.¹⁶

Ship mortgage lenders scrutinize regulatory compliance because violations threaten collateral value. PMSC's Jones Act crew citizenship non-compliance (two vessels operating at 73% U.S. crew versus the statutory 75% requirement) creates three lender concerns:¹⁷

**Rule:** Under 46 U.S.C. § 31341, maritime tort claims rank as Tier 1 super-priority liens, superior to preferred ship mortgages. Section 905(b) of the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (33 U.S.C. § 905(b)) creates vessel negligence liability when longshoremen are injured due to vessel unseaworthiness or negligent operation, despite LHWCA's general bar on third-party suits.⁵⁰ In *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156, 166 (1981), the Supreme Court established that vessel owners owe longshoremen three duties: (1) turnover duty (providing safe access to vessel work areas), (2) duty to warn of hidden dangers, and (3) duty not to actively intervene in cargo operations in negligent ways.⁵¹ Breach of any duty creates vessel negligence liability under § 905(b).

Courts have held that **unseaworthiness is a strict liability doctrine**—vessel owners are liable for defective equipment even absent knowledge of the defect. *Mitchell v. Trawler Racer, Inc.*, 362 U.S. 539, 550 (1960), established that "the vessel's duty to be seaworthy is absolute" and "applies regardless of the owner's diligence in inspecting and maintaining the vessel."⁵² In *CSX Transportation, Inc. v. McBride*, 564 U.S. 685, 703 (2011), the Court held that maritime tort causation requires only that vessel negligence "played any part, even the slightest" in the injury—a standard far more plaintiff-friendly than common-law proximate cause.⁵³

**Explanation:** Section 905(b) tort judgments create maritime liens that attach to the vessel where the injury occurred, with super-priority over recorded mortgages. In *The John G. Stevens*, 170 U.S. 113, 120 (1898), the Supreme Court explained the policy rationale: "Maritime liens arise by operation of law to protect injured parties who have no contractual relationship with the vessel owner and cannot negotiate for security."⁵⁴ This principle means that Martinez's tort claim (if reduced to judgment) would create a lien against the Long Beach container vessel where his injury occurred, and that lien would rank ahead of any preferred mortgage encumbering the vessel.
