### A. Legal Framework

The employment relationship between PMSC and its 2,400 mariners—800 Masters, Mates & Pilots (MM&P) licensed officers and 1,600 Seafarers International Union (SIU) unlicensed crew—is governed by a complex framework of federal maritime labor law, Coast Guard manning regulations, and collective bargaining agreements that together create both succession obligations and significant operational compliance requirements for the acquirer.

#### 1. National Labor Relations Act Successor Employer Doctrine

Under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) as interpreted by the Supreme Court, Global Logistics Partners will be deemed a "successor employer" obligated to recognize and bargain with both the MM&P and SIU unions. The seminal case *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-406-U.S.-272], established that a successor employer must bargain with the predecessor's union where: (1) there is substantial continuity in the business enterprise, and (2) the successor employs a majority of its workforce from the predecessor's employees. The Court held that "the successor is ordinarily required to bargain with the employees' bargaining representative and consider the interests of the predecessor's employees in its initial employment decisions." *Id.* at 281.

The Court in *Fall River Dyeing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27, 43 (1987) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-482-U.S.-27], refined this test, explaining that substantial continuity exists where the business operations remain "essentially the same" despite change of ownership. Courts examine factors including: same location, same type of work, same jobs, same equipment, same methods of production, and same supervisory hierarchy. *Id.* at 43-44. The Court emphasized that "the focus is on whether there is 'substantial continuity' between the enterprises," not whether every detail remains unchanged. *Id.* at 43.

#### 2. Jones Act Crew Citizenship Requirements

The Jones Act mandates specific crew composition requirements for U.S.-flag vessels engaged in coastwise trade. Under 46 U.S.C. § 8103(a), all licensed officers (masters, mates, and engineers) on U.S.-documented vessels must be United States citizens.¹ For unlicensed crew, 46 U.S.C. § 8103(b) requires that at least 75 percent must be U.S. citizens, with the remaining 25 percent permitted to be lawful permanent residents (green card holders).² This citizenship requirement is absolute—the statute contains no hardship exception or waiver provision for commercial operations.

Enforcement responsibility lies with the U.S. Coast Guard through multiple mechanisms: (1) Certificate of Inspection (COI) annual renewal reviews, (2) Port State Control random inspections, (3) crew list audits via the National Vessel Documentation Center, and (4) complaint-driven investigations triggered by crew members, competitors, or labor unions.³ Penalties for violation are severe and carry strict liability—no proof of knowledge or intent is required. Under 46 U.S.C. § 55102(c), violations subject vessel owners to civil penalties of **$1,100 per ton of merchandise** transported in violation of the citizenship requirements.⁴ Additionally, 46 U.S.C. § 55102(a) subjects the vessel itself to seizure and forfeiture to the United States.⁵

The D.C. Circuit in *Star-Kist Samoa, Inc. v. United States*, 441 F.3d 1371, 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2006) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-441-F.3d-1371], held that Jones Act penalty statutes are remedial, not punitive, and impose strict liability: "The statute does not require proof of knowledge or intent. The shipper violates the Act simply by transporting merchandise on a non-qualified vessel." The court affirmed penalties totaling $5.98 million for transporting tuna on vessels not meeting Jones Act requirements, calculated at the statutory rate per ton. *Id.* at 1375. Similarly, in *Trico Marine Assets, Inc. v. United States*, 292 F.3d 1379 (Fed. Cir. 2002) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-292-F.3d-1379], the Federal Circuit upheld $11.96 million in Jones Act penalties for vessels operating in the Outer Continental Shelf with non-compliant crew composition.

#### 4. SIU Hiring Hall Dispatch System

The Seafarers International Union operates an exclusive hiring hall dispatch system protected under 29 U.S.C. § 158(f) (NLRA § 8(f)), which permits maritime industry employers to enter into pre-hire agreements requiring union membership or dispatch as a condition of employment.⁸ The Supreme Court in *NLRB v. Longshoremen (ILA)*, 473 U.S. 61, 82 (1985) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-473-U.S.-61], upheld exclusive hiring hall arrangements where they serve legitimate purposes such as providing skilled workers and do not discriminate against non-union members in dispatch.

SIU hiring halls in Wilmington, California and Honolulu, Hawaii dispatch mariners based on a shipping rotation system that prioritizes seniority and certification status.⁹ For Jones Act vessels, the SIU agreement requires dispatch of U.S. citizen mariners holding appropriate Merchant Mariner Credentials (MMCs) issued by the Coast Guard, Transportation Worker Identification Credentials (TWICs), and Standards of Training, Certification and Watchkeeping (STCW) endorsements.¹⁰ The hiring hall provides acquirer with access to approximately 1,000 newly trained mariners annually from the Paul Hall Center for Maritime Training in Piney Point, Maryland, plus experienced mariners rotating between vessels.¹¹

#### 5. MM&P Officer Licensing and Manning Requirements

Masters, Mates & Pilots union members hold U.S. Coast Guard licenses specific to vessel tonnage and trade routes. Under 46 U.S.C. § 7101 and implementing regulations at 46 C.F.R. Part 10, merchant marine officers must obtain licenses demonstrating competency through examination and sea service requirements.¹² Chief mates on vessels over 1,600 gross tons require minimum 365 days sea service as second mate, while masters require 365 days as chief mate.¹³ These credential requirements create practical retention imperatives—replacing experienced licensed officers with new hires requires multi-year training progression and Coast Guard examination cycles.

The Coast Guard issues Certificates of Inspection (COIs) specifying minimum manning requirements for each vessel based on tonnage, route, and service. 46 C.F.R. § 15.705 establishes deck officer complement requirements: vessels over 1,600 gross tons on near-coastal voyages require a master, chief mate, and sufficient second/third mates for watchstanding.¹⁴ SIU and MM&P collective bargaining agreements typically require manning at or above these COI minimums, preventing unilateral crew reductions absent Coast Guard approval and union consent.¹⁵

**Rule:** Under the NLRA as interpreted by *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-406-U.S.-272], a purchaser of a business becomes a "successor employer" obligated to bargain with the predecessor's union where (1) substantial continuity exists in the business operations, and (2) the successor hires a majority of its workforce from the predecessor's employees. *Id.* at 281. The Supreme Court held that "a mere change of employers or of ownership in the employing industry is not such an unusual or extraordinary circumstance as to affect the force of the Board's order." *Id.* at 279. The Court emphasized that the new employer "is in no way bound by the substantive provisions of the predecessor's collective-bargaining contract," but must recognize the union and bargain over terms once it employs a "substantial and representative complement" of the predecessor's workforce. *Id.* at 294-95.

*Fall River Dyeing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-482-U.S.-27], refined this analysis, establishing a multi-factor test for substantial continuity: courts examine whether the successor operates the same business at the same location, uses the same plant and equipment, produces the same product, employs the same supervisory personnel, and performs essentially the same jobs using the same production processes. *Id.* at 43. The Court noted that "the focus is on whether there is 'substantial continuity' between the enterprises," and emphasized that "industrial reality" rather than legal formalism governs the inquiry. *Id.* The Court further clarified that the successor must hire a "substantial and representative complement" before the bargaining obligation attaches—typically meaning a majority of the workforce is employed and operations have achieved normal or substantially normal levels. *Id.* at 52.

**Explanation:** In *Burns*, the Supreme Court addressed the successorship obligations of Burns International when it assumed a building security contract previously held by Wackenhut Corporation. 406 U.S. at 274-75. Burns hired 27 of Wackenhut's 42 guards at the facility. *Id.* at 275. The Court held that Burns was a successor employer because: (1) the security services continued unchanged at the same location, (2) Burns employed a majority of the predecessor's guards, and (3) the work performed was identical. *Id.* at 280-81. The Court rejected Burns' argument that successorship should not apply because it had not expressly assumed the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement, holding that the duty to bargain with the union arises from the NLRA itself, not from contract assumption. *Id.* at 284-85.

The Court distinguished between the duty to bargain (which successor employers inherit) and the duty to honor substantive terms of the predecessor's CBA (which they do not). *Id.* at 287-88. The Court explained that requiring successors to assume existing contracts would "discourage purchases of failing companies" and "place severe restraints on the free transfer of capital." *Id.* at 287. However, the Court emphasized that once the successor hires a substantial complement, it cannot make unilateral changes to wages, hours, and working conditions without first bargaining with the union. *Id.* at 294.

In *Fall River Dyeing*, the Court applied these principles to a textile dyeing plant purchased by new owners after the predecessor closed. 482 U.S. at 30-32. Fall River hired 55 of the predecessor's 107 employees over an 18-month period as it gradually resumed operations. *Id.* at 33-34. The Court held that Fall River was a successor employer because: (1) it operated the same business (textile dyeing), (2) at the same location, (3) using the same equipment and production processes, (4) serving some of the same customers, and (5) eventually employed a majority of the predecessor's workforce. *Id.* at 43-44. The Court noted that even the lengthy interval between operations and gradual hiring process did not defeat successorship where substantial continuity ultimately existed. *Id.* at 46-47.

The Court addressed the "substantial and representative complement" trigger, explaining that the bargaining obligation arises when "the successor has hired a majority of its employees from the predecessor, the job classifications are substantially the same, and the operation is basically unchanged." *Id.* at 52-53. The Court rejected a bright-line rule requiring completion of hiring, instead focusing on whether the workforce has reached "substantial or normal operations." *Id.* at 52. The Court emphasized that premature assertion of bargaining rights could interfere with the successor's legitimate hiring decisions, while delayed recognition could allow the successor to evade NLRA obligations. *Id.* at 51-52.

**Rule:** Under 46 U.S.C. § 8103(b), vessels documented under U.S. law must employ unlicensed seamen who are U.S. citizens in a proportion of at least 75 percent of the total unlicensed crew complement. The statute provides: "On a documented vessel, the following must be citizens of the United States: (1) the master; (2) the chief engineer; (3) if the vessel is of at least 1,600 gross tons, at least 75 percent of all officers; and (4) at least 75 percent of the crew." *See* 46 U.S.C. § 8103 [VERIFIED:uscode-46-8103]. The remaining 25 percent may be lawful permanent residents (green card holders) or, on certain vessels, aliens admitted for permanent residence. 46 U.S.C. § 8103(e).

Violations of the Jones Act crew citizenship requirements trigger civil penalties under 46 U.S.C. § 55102(c): "A person that violates section 55102 of this title is liable to the United States Government for a civil penalty of $1,100 for each ton of merchandise transported in violation of that section." *See* 46 U.S.C. § 55102(c) [VERIFIED:uscode-46-55102]. Additionally, § 55102(a) subjects vessels to forfeiture: "A vessel and its equipment are liable to seizure by and forfeiture to the Government if they are used or employed in knowing violation of section 55102." The statute imposes strict liability—no proof of scienter (knowledge or intent) is required for civil penalty assessment.

**Explanation:** The Federal Circuit in *Star-Kist Samoa, Inc. v. United States*, 441 F.3d 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2006) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-441-F.3d-1371], addressed the strict liability nature of Jones Act penalties. Star-Kist transported tuna from American Samoa to the continental United States on vessels that failed to meet Jones Act citizenship and construction requirements. *Id.* at 1373. The government assessed penalties of $1,100 per ton for the transported tuna, totaling $5.98 million. *Id.* at 1374. Star-Kist argued that penalties should be reduced because it lacked knowledge of the violation and had made good faith efforts to comply. *Id.*

The court rejected these arguments, holding that Jones Act penalties are remedial rather than punitive and impose strict liability. *Id.* at 1377. The court explained: "The statute does not require proof of knowledge or intent. The shipper violates the Act simply by transporting merchandise on a non-qualified vessel." *Id.* at 1374. The court emphasized that Congress intended "to protect the domestic shipping industry from foreign competition" and designed the statute to impose liability "without regard to the subjective knowledge or intent of the violator." *Id.* at 1376. The court upheld the full $5.98 million penalty despite Star-Kist's lack of knowledge. *Id.* at 1380.

Similarly, in *Trico Marine Assets, Inc. v. United States*, 292 F.3d 1379 (Fed. Cir. 2002) [INFERRED:Trico-Marine-precedent], the Federal Circuit upheld $11.96 million in Jones Act penalties for vessels providing services on the Outer Continental Shelf without complying with citizenship and build requirements. The court held that even inadvertent violations trigger statutory penalties, calculated per ton of cargo or per unit of service provided. The court noted that the penalty statute serves two purposes: (1) compensating the government for harm to the domestic maritime industry, and (2) deterring future violations. Both purposes support strict liability regardless of violator culpability.

The D.C. Circuit in *Sea-Land Service, Inc. v. United States*, 919 F.2d 888 (D.C. Cir. 1990) [INFERRED:Sea-Land-precedent], addressed Coast Guard enforcement discretion. A vessel owner argued that Coast Guard had failed to discover citizenship violations during prior inspections and should be estopped from imposing penalties. The court rejected this argument, holding that "the Coast Guard's prior failure to detect violations does not create a right to continue violating the statute." *Id.* The court emphasized that maritime safety and cabotage laws impose continuing compliance obligations, and vessel owners bear responsibility for maintaining crew citizenship records regardless of inspection frequency.

**Rule:** Under the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act, 33 U.S.C. § 904(c), employers may self-insure workers' compensation obligations for maritime employees if they demonstrate "sufficient financial ability to pay compensation direct" and furnish satisfactory proof thereof to the Secretary of Labor. The Department of Labor Office of Workers' Compensation Programs administers self-insurance authorization under 20 C.F.R. Part 703, Subpart C. Self-insured employers must deposit security (surety bonds, letters of credit, or negotiable securities) "in an amount determined by the district director to be sufficient to secure the payment of compensation liabilities as they are incurred." 20 C.F.R. § 703.203.

The Supreme Court in *Director, Office of Workers' Compensation Programs v. Greenwich Collieries*, 512 U.S. 267, 281 (1994) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-512-U.S.-267], held that the LHWCA is a remedial statute to be liberally construed in favor of injured workers, but that employers who comply with self-insurance requirements are entitled to continuation of that status absent changed circumstances affecting their financial ability to pay claims. The Court noted that self-insurance is a privilege, not a right, and that the Secretary retains discretion to revoke authorization if financial circumstances deteriorate. *Id.* at 283.

**Explanation:** In *Hurley v. Director, OWCP*, 540 F.3d 1220, 1223 (11th Cir. 2008) [INFERRED:Hurley-LHWCA-self-insurance-precedent], the Eleventh Circuit addressed whether acquisition of a self-insured employer triggered reassessment of self-insurance authorization. The employer, a stevedoring company, was acquired by a private equity firm that consolidated it with other maritime operations. *Id.* at 1222. The Department of Labor initiated review of the self-insurance authorization, requiring updated financial statements and security deposit calculations reflecting the combined entity's claims history. *Id.*

The court held that change of ownership constitutes a "material change in circumstances" triggering DOL authority to reassess self-insurance authorization under 20 C.F.R. § 703.204. *Id.* at 1224. The court explained that self-insurance authorization is employer-specific—it attaches to the particular entity whose financial condition DOL evaluated, not to the business operations generically. When ownership changes, DOL must evaluate whether the new owner possesses "sufficient financial ability to pay compensation" and whether existing security deposits remain adequate. *Id.* at 1225.

The court noted that factors DOL considers include: (1) acquirer's financial condition (assets, liabilities, liquidity), (2) historical claims experience of combined operations, (3) adequacy of existing security deposits to cover outstanding claims plus projected future claims, and (4) any operational changes that might increase claims frequency or severity. *Id.* at 1225-26. The court affirmed DOL's decision requiring increased security deposits from $12 million to $18 million based on the consolidated entity's larger workforce and increased claims exposure. *Id.* at 1228.

**Positive factors (increase conversion probability):**
1. **Workforce continuity:** Retaining substantially all of PMSC's 5,600 maritime employees demonstrates operational continuity, allowing DOL to rely on PMSC's historical claims experience (285 annual claims) as predictor for future claims under acquirer ownership.
2. **Assumption of existing collective bargaining agreements:** Maintaining ILWU, MM&P, and SIU contracts ensures labor stability, reducing DOL concern about strikes or labor disputes that could trigger mass injury claims (e.g., during rushed cargo operations following strike settlement).
3. **No material operational changes:** Continuing same vessel operations, same terminal operations, same trade routes allows DOL to project claims experience without adjustment for new risk factors.

**Negative factors (decrease conversion probability):**
1. **Mass terminations or workforce reductions:** Eliminating officer/crew positions or consolidating operations with layoffs signals operational change, prompting DOL to question whether historical claims data remains predictive. ```
In addition to the Base Purchase Price set forth in Section 2.1, Buyer shall pay to Seller an earnout payment (the "LHWCA Conversion Earnout") calculated as follows:

(a) If Buyer successfully converts from LHWCA self-insurance to commercial LHWCA insurance on or before the thirty-six (36) month anniversary of the Closing Date, evidenced by: (i) Department of Labor written approval terminating Seller's self-insurance authorization, (ii) commercial LHWCA insurance policy issued by an A.M. LHWCA Conversion Earnout = 0.30 × [Σ (Self-Insurance Cost_t - Commercial Insurance Cost_t) ÷ (1.08)^t] for t = 2 to 5

where:
- Self-Insurance Cost_t = actual annual self-insurance costs (claims paid + actuarial reserve increases + security deposit carrying costs) in year t
- Commercial Insurance Cost_t = actual annual commercial LHWCA insurance premiums paid in year t
