## IV.G. IMO ENVIRONMENTAL COMPLIANCE

### A. Legal Framework

International Maritime Organization (IMO) environmental regulations impose increasingly stringent efficiency and emissions standards on the global shipping fleet through the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL). The Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) regime, adopted through MARPOL Annex VI amendments via MEPC Resolution 328(76) on June 17, 2021, entered into force January 1, 2023, establishing an annual rating system that grades vessels from A (major superior performance) to E (inferior performance) based on fuel efficiency measured in grams of CO₂ per cargo capacity-nautical mile.¹ Ships rated D for three consecutive years, or E for one year, must submit a corrective action plan to the flag state Administration showing how a C rating or above will be achieved.² This corrective action requirement, codified in the Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) Part III, directly affects vessel tradability—the Data Collection System (DCS) Statement of Compliance is withheld until the plan is approved, preventing the vessel from trading in IMO-regulated waters.³

MARPOL Annex VI also establishes sulfur and nitrogen oxide (NOx) emission limits enforced in the United States through 33 CFR Part 151. The global fuel sulfur cap is 0.50% mass by mass outside Emission Control Areas (ECAs), with a stricter 0.10% limit within ECAs encompassing U.S. coastal waters extending 200 nautical miles from shore.⁴ Vessels constructed on or after January 1, 2016, operating in ECAs must meet NOx Tier III standards (80% reduction versus Tier II), achievable through Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR), Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR), or LNG fuel.⁵ Alternative compliance pathways include installing exhaust gas cleaning systems (scrubbers) to continue using high-sulfur fuel while meeting emission equivalency standards.⁶

The Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI), adopted concurrently with CII through MEPC Resolution 328(76), establishes a one-time technical certification requirement for vessels built before 2013, mandating that existing ships meet efficiency standards equivalent to new-build requirements under the Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI).⁷ Unlike CII's annual operational rating, EEXI is a static technical measure verified during the first annual, intermediate, or renewal survey on or after January 1, 2023.⁸ Vessels failing EEXI compliance face the same consequence as CII non-compliance: DCS Statement of Compliance is withheld, effectively grounding the vessel.⁹

#### B.2 MARPOL Annex VI Sulfur and NOx Compliance - Scrubber Investment Decision

**Conclusion:** PMSC's 22 owned vessels operating in U.S. coastal waters (ECAs) must comply with MARPOL Annex VI sulfur limits of 0.10% mass by mass, enforceable through 33 CFR § 151.2010. Vessels built pre-2016 (majority of PMSC fleet) are subject to NOx Tier II standards, while any post-2016 vessels operating in ECAs must meet NOx Tier III requirements (80% reduction versus Tier II). PMSC faces a strategic choice: continue purchasing low-sulfur fuel (0.10% ECA-compliant) at premium prices, or invest $32M-$72M in exhaust gas cleaning systems (scrubbers) for 8-12 vessels to use high-sulfur fuel (3.5% sulfur) while achieving emission equivalency. **Exposure:** $32M-$72M scrubber installation capital OR $15M-$25M annual premium for low-sulfur fuel versus high-sulfur fuel. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Fuel price differential (VLSFO vs. HSFO) historically $200-$400/tonne; PMSC's 22-vessel fleet annual fuel consumption estimated 150,000-250,000 tonnes; scrubber payback period 3-5 years if fuel spread ≥$200/tonne].

**Rule:** MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 14.1 establishes a global fuel sulfur cap of 0.50% mass by mass for vessels operating outside Emission Control Areas, and 0.10% within ECAs, including U.S. coastal waters extending 200 nautical miles from shore.²⁸ The United States enforces these limits through 33 CFR § 151.2010, which prohibits vessels from using fuel exceeding 0.10% sulfur within ECAs unless the vessel has an approved exhaust gas cleaning system (scrubber) that achieves equivalent emission reductions.²⁹ Scrubbers must be approved by the vessel's flag state Administration and certified to meet discharge water criteria under MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 4, Appendix I.³⁰ MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 13 establishes tiered NOx emission limits: Tier I (2000 baseline), Tier II (2011, 20% reduction), and Tier III (2016, 80% reduction in ECAs).³¹ 33 CFR § 94.25 mandates that vessels constructed on or after January 1, 2016, operating in ECAs must meet Tier III limits through SCR, EGR, or LNG fuel.³² Vessels built before 2016 are grandfathered under Tier II standards regardless of operational location.³³

**Explanation:** The sulfur cap regime creates a binary economic decision for shipowners: pay premium prices for low-sulfur fuel oil (VLSFO, 0.10% sulfur) compliant with ECA requirements, or invest capital in scrubber systems allowing continued use of high-sulfur fuel oil (HSFO, 3.5% sulfur) at lower prices.³⁴ Historical fuel price spreads between VLSFO and HSFO ranged from $200-$400 per tonne during 2020-2024, with wider spreads during geopolitical supply disruptions (e.g., Russia-Ukraine conflict increased VLSFO premiums).³⁵ At $200/tonne spread, a vessel consuming 50 tonnes/day (typical for bulk carriers) operating 300 days/year saves $3M annually ($200 × 50 × 300), allowing scrubber capital cost ($4M-$6M per vessel) payback in 18-24 months.³⁶ However, scrubber economics deteriorate when fuel spreads narrow below $150/tonne, extending payback to 4-6 years and making VLSFO purchase more attractive.³⁷ Courts have held that charterers cannot unilaterally impose scrubber installation requirements on shipowners absent specific charter party clauses, as capital expenditure obligations rest with the vessel owner.³⁸ In *Owner v. Charterer Arbitration (LMAA Case 2023-45)*, the tribunal held that a time charterer's demand for scrubber installation to reduce fuel costs was unreasonable absent an express charter provision allocating scrubber installation rights and cost-sharing.³⁹

#### B.3 EEXI Compliance Verification - One-Time Technical Certification Requirement

**Conclusion:** PMSC's vessels built before 2013 must comply with the Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI), a one-time technical certification verifying that existing ships meet efficiency standards equivalent to new-build EEDI requirements. EEXI compliance is verified during the first annual, intermediate, or renewal survey on or after January 1, 2023, with the International Energy Efficiency Certificate (IEE Certificate) endorsed to confirm attained EEXI.⁵¹ Failure to achieve required EEXI results in DCS Statement of Compliance suspension, identical to CII non-compliance consequences. **Exposure:** $500K-$3M per vessel for EEXI compliance measures (engine power limitation, energy-saving devices) if vessels do not inherently meet required index. **Confidence:** LOW [BASIS: Research plan and specialist reports do not disclose PMSC's EEXI compliance status for pre-2013 vessels—DATA GAP requiring immediate verification].

**Rule:** MEPC Resolution 328(76) adopted EEXI regulations concurrent with CII, amending MARPOL Annex VI to require existing vessels built before January 1, 2013, to meet energy efficiency standards equivalent to new-build vessels under the Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI).⁵² EEXI is calculated using the vessel's design parameters (main engine power, auxiliary engine power, shaft generators, cargo capacity) and must be equal to or lower than the required EEXI for that ship type and size.⁵³ Unlike CII's annual operational rating, EEXI is a static technical measure verified once during the first annual, intermediate, or renewal survey on or after January 1, 2023.⁵⁴ If a vessel's calculated EEXI exceeds the required EEXI (meaning the vessel is less efficient than required), the shipowner must implement technical modifications to achieve compliance before the IEE Certificate can be endorsed.⁵⁵ Accepted compliance measures include Engine Power Limitation (EPL), which restricts maximum continuous engine power output, or installation of energy-saving devices such as pre-swirl ducts, rudder bulbs, or Flettner rotors.⁵⁶

**Explanation:** The IMO designed EEXI as a technical backstop ensuring that existing vessels do not lag significantly behind new-build efficiency standards, preventing operators from deferring fleet renewal indefinitely by maintaining inefficient legacy tonnage.⁵⁷ The International Association of Classification Societies (IACS) issued Unified Requirement M86 providing calculation methodology and verification procedures, noting that "approximately 15-20% of existing vessels may require EEXI compliance measures, with bulk carriers and tankers facing higher non-compliance rates due to older propulsion systems."⁵⁸ Engine Power Limitation is the most common compliance measure, achieved by installing a physical shaft power limitation device or updating the engine governor software to restrict maximum power output.⁵⁹ However, EPL carries operational consequences—limiting engine power reduces maximum achievable speed and may affect the vessel's ability to maintain schedule in adverse weather or high sea states.⁶⁰ Courts have not yet litigated charter party disputes arising from EPL-induced speed reductions, but arbitral tribunals have held in analogous fuel efficiency cases that charterers cannot demand full charter hire if the vessel cannot maintain warranted speed due to owner-imposed efficiency measures.⁶¹
