### A. Legal Framework

The Jones Act, codified at **46 U.S.C. § 55102**, establishes the foundational requirement for coastwise trade in the United States, mandating that vessels transporting merchandise or passengers between U.S. points must meet four distinct criteria: (1) U.S.-built construction, (2) U.S. flag registration, (3) U.S. ownership (at least 75% U.S. citizen-owned), and (4) U.S. crew composition.¹ The statute serves dual purposes of national security (maintaining a robust domestic merchant marine) and economic protectionism (reserving domestic maritime commerce for U.S. vessels and mariners).²

**Crew Citizenship Requirements (46 U.S.C. § 8103):** The Jones Act's crew citizenship provisions require that the master and all licensed officers must be U.S. citizens, and **not more than 25% of the total number of unlicensed seamen may be aliens lawfully admitted to the United States for permanent residence**.³ This statutory language establishes a bright-line rule: **at least 75% of unlicensed crew must be U.S. citizens** to satisfy Jones Act compliance for coastwise trade.⁴ The Coast Guard interprets this requirement strictly, with no discretion to waive the 75% threshold for routine commercial operations.⁵

**Penalty Structure (46 U.S.C. § 55102(b)):** Violations of the Jones Act crew citizenship requirements trigger severe statutory penalties: (1) **$1,100 per ton of merchandise** transported in violation (calculated based on vessel deadweight tonnage), and (2) **forfeiture of the vessel** to the U.S. Government.⁶ Importantly, these penalties apply **per voyage** in violation, not per vessel or per year, creating catastrophic tail risk for non-compliant operators conducting multiple voyages.⁷ Courts have upheld these penalties as mandatory statutory minima, rejecting arguments that the penalty structure constitutes excessive fines under the Eighth Amendment.⁸

**Enforcement Authority:** The U.S. Coast Guard, U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP), and the Maritime Administration (MARAD) share concurrent jurisdiction to enforce Jones Act crew citizenship requirements.⁹ Enforcement typically occurs through three channels: (1) routine Port State Control (PSC) inspections, (2) casualty investigations triggered by vessel accidents or crew injuries, and (3) whistleblower complaints from displaced U.S. mariners or competitor vessels.¹⁰ The Coast Guard Authorization Act of 2024 enhanced transparency by requiring public disclosure of Jones Act penalties in the Customs Bulletin and Decisions within 14 days of issuance, including vessel name, owner, fine amount, and misconduct summary.¹¹

**Outer Continental Shelf Extension:** The Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (43 U.S.C. § 1333) extends Jones Act requirements to vessels servicing U.S. offshore installations on the OCS, including offshore wind farms.¹² This extension requires that vessels serving U.S. wind projects must satisfy all four Jones Act criteria, including U.S.-built construction—a requirement that foreign-built vessels cannot satisfy even if subsequently U.S.-flagged and crewed.¹³
