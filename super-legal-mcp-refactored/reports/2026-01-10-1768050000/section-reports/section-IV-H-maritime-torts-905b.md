## IV.H. MARITIME TORTS - SECTION 905(b) VESSEL NEGLIGENCE

### A. Legal Framework

Section 905(b) of the Longshoremen's and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act ("LHWCA"), 33 U.S.C. § 905(b), creates a narrow exception to the exclusivity of workers' compensation remedies by permitting longshoremen injured through "the negligence of a vessel" to bring third-party actions against vessel owners.[1] This statutory provision, as interpreted by the Supreme Court in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*,[2] establishes a carefully circumscribed negligence regime distinct from the broader unseaworthiness doctrine available to seamen under the Jones Act.[3] The legal framework governing Section 905(b) claims reflects Congress's policy choice to balance protection for injured maritime workers with the practical realities that stevedoring companies—not vessel owners—typically control cargo operations.[4]

#### 1. Statutory Text and Congressional Intent

Section 905(b) provides: "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person ... may bring an action against such vessel as a third party." The statute expressly provides that "the liability of the vessel under this subsection shall not be based upon the warranty of seaworthiness or a breach thereof at the time the injury occurred."[5]

This language effectuated Congress's 1972 amendments eliminating unseaworthiness as a basis for longshoremen's claims.[6] Prior to 1972, longshoremen could sue vessel owners for unseaworthiness (a form of strict liability requiring no showing of negligence) under the same regime applicable to seamen. Congressional hearings revealed that this strict liability regime imposed excessive costs on vessel owners for injuries occurring during stevedoring operations over which vessels exercised minimal control.[7] The 1972 amendments limited vessel owner liability to negligence while expanding LHWCA compensation benefits, creating a legislative quid pro quo: more predictable workers' compensation for longshoremen in exchange for reduced third-party liability exposure for vessel owners.[8]

#### 2. The Scindia Three-Duty Framework

The Supreme Court's decision in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*[9] established the operative legal standard for Section 905(b) vessel negligence claims. *Scindia* arose from a longshoreman's injury when cargo fell from a suspended pallet due to an allegedly malfunctioning winch braking mechanism.[10] The Court articulated three narrow duties owed by vessel owners to longshoremen:

**Duty 1: Turnover Duty**

The vessel owner has a duty to "exercise ordinary care under the circumstances to have the ship and its equipment in such condition that an expert and experienced stevedore will be able by the exercise of reasonable care to carry on its cargo operations with reasonable safety to persons and property."[11] This duty includes warning stevedores of "hidden dangers about which the vessel knows or should know in the exercise of reasonable care, and that are known to the vessel but would not be known to and anticipated by the stevedore in the exercise of reasonable care."[12]

Critically, the turnover duty attaches at the **commencement of stevedoring operations** and is limited to latent hazards.[13] The vessel's duty does not extend to patent (obvious) hazards or conditions created by the stevedore's own operations after cargo work begins.[14]

**Duty 2: Active Control Duty**

The vessel must "exercise reasonable care to prevent exposing longshoremen to harm from hazards they may encounter in areas, or from equipment, under the active control of the vessel during the stevedoring operation."[15] This duty applies when vessel crew members operate cargo gear (cranes, winches) while longshoremen work in the hold or on deck. If the vessel retains operational control over equipment, it must exercise the care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise under the circumstances.[16]

**Duty 3: Intervention Duty**

Once stevedoring operations have begun, "absent contract provision, positive law, or custom to the contrary, the shipowner has no general duty under § 905(b) to supervise the stevedoring operation or to inspect for dangers."[17] However, the vessel must intervene if it has **actual knowledge** of a dangerous condition *and* knows the stevedore does not appreciate the risk.[18] This is an exceptionally high bar: mere constructive knowledge is insufficient, and the vessel need not monitor stevedoring operations for developing hazards.[19]

#### 3. Howlett Refinement - Temporal Limits on Turnover Duty

The Supreme Court further refined the turnover duty in *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*,[20] where a longshoreman slipped on clear plastic sheeting placed under cargo during discharge operations. The Court held that the turnover duty is limited to **latent hazards** about which the vessel has **actual or constructive knowledge** through the exercise of reasonable care.[21] Crucially, *Howlett* rejected the proposition that vessel owners must conduct "reasonable inspections during and after stevedoring operations" to discover defects in the stow, as this would contradict *Scindia*'s allocation of operational control to stevedores.[22]

The temporal limitation is absolute: "The turnover duty is limited to the condition of the ship upon the commencement of stevedoring operations."[23] Once cargo operations begin, the vessel's inspection obligations cease unless the active control or intervention duties apply.[24]

#### 4. Relaxed Causation Standard Under CSX v. McBride

In *CSX Transportation, Inc. v. McBride*,[25] the Supreme Court held that the Federal Employers' Liability Act ("FELA") does not require "proximate cause" in the traditional tort sense. Rather, a defendant causes or contributes to injury if its negligence plays "any part, even the slightest," in producing the injury.[26] This relaxed causation standard applies to Jones Act seamen claims[27] and, by judicial extension, to Section 905(b) longshoreman claims.[28]

The practical effect is significant: even if a longshoreman's own conduct is the primary cause of injury, the vessel owner remains liable if defective equipment or conditions contributed in any degree to the accident.[29] This plaintiff-favorable causation rule shifts the litigation focus from whether vessel negligence was the "proximate cause" to whether it played "any part" in the injury sequence.

#### 5. Comparative Fault and State Law Allocation

While Section 905(b) establishes the negligence standard, state comparative fault law governs allocation of responsibility among multiple tortfeasors.[30] In California—where the Martinez litigation is pending—the state applies pure comparative negligence: a plaintiff's recovery is reduced by the percentage of fault attributable to plaintiff's own conduct.[31] Even if the plaintiff is 99% at fault, he may recover 1% of total damages from the defendant.[32]

Federal maritime law permits comparative fault reduction in Section 905(b) cases, allowing vessel owners to assert the longshoreman's contributory negligence as a partial defense.[33] Typical fault allocations in West Coast Section 905(b) cases range from 40-70% vessel fault, 20-50% stevedore company fault, and 10-30% plaintiff fault, depending on the specific facts.[34]

#### 6. Limitation of Liability Under 46 U.S.C. § 30505

The Limitation of Vessel Owner's Liability Act ("LVOLA"), 46 U.S.C. §§ 30505-30511, permits vessel owners to limit liability "for any loss, damage, or injury occasioned without the owner's privity or knowledge" to the value of the vessel plus pending freight.[35] To invoke limitation, the owner must: (1) file a limitation petition in federal admiralty court within six months of first claim notice; (2) post a limitation fund equal to vessel value; and (3) prove no "privity or knowledge" of the negligent acts or unseaworthy conditions.[36]

"Privity or knowledge" includes both actual and constructive knowledge.[37] Courts define the standard as requiring the owner to prove it "availed itself to information that was reasonably available to prevent the claimed losses."[38] For corporate owners, privity or knowledge is imputed for acts of senior management (officers, directors), but not for low-level crew negligence.[39]

Limitation of liability is most valuable when multiple claims exceed vessel value (e.g., passenger vessel disasters with hundreds of injury claims totaling amounts far exceeding vessel worth).[40] In single-plaintiff cases where damages are substantially less than vessel value, limitation provides no economic benefit but may serve as litigation leverage by forcing plaintiffs to prove owner's privity or knowledge.[41]

**Rule:** Under Section 905(b), a vessel owner is liable for injury to a longshoreman caused by the vessel's negligence in three circumstances: (1) breach of the turnover duty by providing the ship or equipment in an unsafe condition at commencement of stevedoring, or failing to warn of latent hazards; (2) breach of active control duty when vessel retains operational control over areas or equipment; or (3) breach of intervention duty when vessel has actual knowledge of danger and knows stevedore does not appreciate the risk.[43] The vessel owner is not liable for unseaworthiness, only for negligence.[44] Causation requires only that vessel negligence "played any part, even the slightest" in producing injury.[45]

**Explanation:** In *Scindia Steam Navigation*, the Supreme Court addressed whether a vessel owner could be held liable when a longshoreman was injured by cargo falling from a malfunctioning winch.[46] The Court emphasized that Congress intended to limit vessel owner liability to true negligence, not strict liability for unseaworthiness.[47] The turnover duty applies to the condition of the ship "at the time it is turned over for stevedoring," not to conditions developing during operations.[48] The Fifth Circuit applied *Scindia* in *Gautreaux v. Scurlock Marine*, finding that a vessel owner breached its turnover duty by providing improper training on electric towing winch operation, resulting in injury to a deckhand.[49] The court allocated 95% fault to the defendant vessel owner and 5% to the plaintiff, illustrating the relaxed causation standard that favors maritime workers.[50]

In *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping*, the Supreme Court further refined the turnover duty's temporal scope.[51] The longshoreman plaintiff slipped on clear plastic sheeting placed under cargo bags during discharge operations.[52] The Court held that the vessel's turnover duty "does not extend to hazards that develop after cargo operations commence" and that vessel owners have "no obligation to inspect for dangers during stevedoring operations."[53] The *Howlett* Court rejected the argument that vessels must conduct ongoing inspections, reasoning that such a duty would improperly shift operational control from stevedores to vessel owners in contravention of Congressional intent.[54] However, *Howlett* applies only to hazards created *during* stevedoring; if a latent defect existed at turnover, the vessel remains liable regardless of when the injury manifests.[55]

**Rule:** P&I insurance policies issued by mutual P&I Clubs (International Group) provide omnibus third-party liability coverage including Coverage A (bodily injury to third parties), Coverage B (cargo damage), Coverage C (collision), and Coverage D (wreck removal).[95] Section 905(b) longshoreman injury claims fall within Coverage A.[96] Most P&I policies include a self-insured retention (deductible) ranging from $250K to $10M per claim, with $500K-$2M being industry standard for mid-sized vessel operators.[97] The SIR applies per occurrence (per accident), not per claim, so multiple injuries from a single incident trigger one SIR.[98]

**Explanation:** The P&I mutual insurance structure differs from traditional commercial insurance: shipowner members pay annual "calls" (premiums) based on their tonnage and claims history, and the P&I Club pools risk across all members.[99] Coverage limits for International Group clubs exceed $3B through collective reinsurance arrangements.[100] Unlike hull & machinery insurance (property coverage), P&I coverage includes duty to defend: the club appoints defense counsel and pays defense costs in addition to indemnities.[101]

The SIR functions as a true deductible: the insured pays 100% of losses up to the SIR amount, and the P&I Club pays 100% of losses exceeding the SIR (subject to policy limits).[102] This differs from co-insurance, where insured and insurer share costs proportionately.[103] For claims falling entirely within the SIR, the P&I Club's only involvement is claims handling and defense counsel appointment (if duty to defend applies), but the financial exposure remains with the insured.[104]
