If vessel is arrested and sold at judicial auction, Tier 1 lienholders (Martinez's $3.5M tort lien, crew wage claims, salvage claims) are paid FIRST from sale proceeds, THEN Tier 2 preferred mortgage lenders receive balance. This super-priority protects involuntary creditors (crew who labor aboard vessels, injured third parties) over voluntary creditors (sophisticated lenders who underwrite vessel finance with knowledge of maritime operations).

### B. SECTION 905(b) VESSEL NEGLIGENCE - MARTINEZ V. PMSC CASE ANALYSIS

#### 1. Statutory Framework - 33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b)

**Primary Citation:** 33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b) (Longshoremen's and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act)

**Statutory Text:** Section 905(b) provides that a longshoreman injured "by the negligence of a vessel" may bring an action against the vessel as a third party, and "the liability of the vessel under this subsection shall not be based upon the warranty of seaworthiness."Â¹â´

**Key Distinction from Jones Act (Seamen):**
- **Seamen** (Jones Act): Can sue for unseaworthiness (strict liability) AND negligence
- **Longshoremen** (LHWCA Â§ 905(b)): Can sue ONLY for negligence (unseaworthiness eliminated by 1972 Amendments)

**Policy Rationale:**
Congress eliminated unseaworthiness as a basis for longshoremen claims in 1972 to reduce vessel owner liability, recognizing that stevedores (not vessel owners) control cargo operations.

#### 2. Scindia Duties - Supreme Court Framework

**Controlling Precedent:** *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981)Â¹âµ

**Facts:** Longshoreman Santos injured while loading cargo aboard M/V Jalaratna when cargo fell from suspended pallet due to allegedly malfunctioning winch braking mechanism.

**Supreme Court Holding:**
The Court established that vessel-owner liability under Â§ 905(b) sounds only in negligence and articulated **three narrow duties**:

**1. Turnover Duty:**
"The shipowner has a duty regarding the ship's gear, equipment, tools, and work space to be used in stevedoring operations, and must at least warn the stevedore of hidden dangers that would have been known to the shipowner through reasonable care."Â¹â¶

- Duty applies at the **commencement** of stevedoring operations
- Vessel must provide ship in reasonably safe condition OR warn of latent hazards
- Duty limited to hazards known or that should be known through reasonable care

**2. Active Control Duty:**
Vessel owner must "exercise reasonable care to prevent injuries to longshoremen in areas that remain under the active control of the vessel."Â¹â·

- Applies when vessel retains operational control during stevedoring
- Example: Vessel crew operates cargo winches/cranes while longshoremen work below

**3. Duty to Intervene:**
"Once stevedore cargo operations have begun, absent contract provision, positive law, or custom to the contrary, the shipowner has no general duty under Â§ 905(b) to supervise or inspect to discover dangerous conditions that develop within the stevedore's assigned operations."Â¹â¸

- Vessel must intervene ONLY if it has actual knowledge of danger AND knows stevedore does not appreciate risk
- High bar: vessel not required to monitor stevedore's work for defects

**Significance:** Scindia sharply limits vessel owner liability, shifting primary responsibility to stevedoring companies.

#### 3. Howlett Refinement - Turnover Duty Limitations

**Controlling Precedent:** *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*, 512 U.S. 92 (1994)Â¹â¹

**Facts:** Longshoreman Howlett slipped and fell on sheet of clear plastic placed under bags of cocoa beans during cargo discharge operations.

**Supreme Court Holding:**
"The turnover duty to warn pursuant to Â§ 5(b) is limited to **latent hazards** about which the vessel has **actual knowledge** or should know through the exercise of reasonable care."Â²â°

**Critical Limitation:**
"The Court declined Howlett's contention that a vessel must make reasonable inspections **during and after** stevedoring operations to discover defects in the stow, as this contradicts the principles underlying Scindia Steam."Â²Â¹

**Practical Effect:**
- Vessel's turnover duty ends once stevedoring begins
- No ongoing inspection obligation
- Vessel not liable for hazards created by stevedore's operations (unless active control or intervention duty triggered)

#### 4. CSX Transportation v. McBride - Relaxed Causation Standard

**Controlling Precedent:** *CSX Transportation, Inc. v. McBride*, 564 U.S. 685 (2011)Â²Â²

**Issue:** Whether FELA (and by incorporation, Jones Act) requires "proximate cause" or a more relaxed causation standard.

**Supreme Court Holding (5-4 decision, Justice Ginsburg):**
"The Federal Employers Liability Act does not incorporate the proximate cause standards developed in non-statutory common-law tort cases; rather, a railroad causes or contributes to an employee's injury if the railroad's negligence plays **any part, even the slightest**, in producing the injury for which damages are sought."Â²Â³

**Application to Section 905(b):**
While McBride directly addressed FELA/Jones Act (seamen), courts have extended the relaxed causation standard to Â§ 905(b) longshoreman claims:
- Plaintiff need not prove vessel negligence was "proximate cause"
- Sufficient if vessel negligence played "any part, even the slightest" in injury

### C. MARITIME LIEN PRIORITY - $2.8B SHIP MORTGAGE SECURITY

#### 1. Statutory Framework - 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31341

**Primary Citation:** 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31341 - Maritime lien priority

**Statutory Hierarchy (in order of priority):**

The statute establishes that certain maritime liens have **super-priority** over preferred ship mortgages:

**Tier 1 - Highest Priority (Superior to Mortgages):**
1. **Preferred maritime liens** under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31326:
   - Wages of crew, master, and other persons employed on vessel
   - Salvage claims (vessel rescue operations)
   - Maritime tort claims (personal injury, property damage)
   - General average contributions (cargo loss-sharing)
   - Necessaries (fuel, repairs, provisions supplied to vessel)

**Tier 2 - Preferred Ship Mortgages:**
2. **Preferred ship mortgages** recorded under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31321
   - PMSC's $2.8B debt (4 lenders) falls into this category
   - "Preferred" means recorded with U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center
   - Secured by vessel itself (in rem jurisdiction)

**Tier 3 - General Maritime Liens:**
3. Other maritime liens (non-preferred)
4. General unsecured creditors

**Critical Principle:**
"Maritime liens for crew wages, salvage, and certain tort claims rank ABOVE preferred ship mortgages." This means if a vessel is arrested and sold at judicial auction, lienholders in Tier 1 are paid FIRST from sale proceeds, THEN mortgage lenders in Tier 2.

#### 2. Policy Rationale for Lien Super-Priority

**Historical Maritime Law Principle:**
Maritime liens exist to protect:
1. **Crew members** who work aboard vessel (cannot easily leave if unpaid, unlike land-based employees)
2. **Salvors** who risk their own vessels to rescue ships in distress
3. **Injured third parties** (longshoremen, passengers, cargo owners) who have no contractual relationship with vessel owner

**Why Liens Outrank Mortgages:**
- Mortgage lenders voluntarily extend credit with knowledge of vessel operations (sophisticated parties)
- Crew and tort victims are **involuntary creditors** who did not negotiate terms
- Equity principle: Those who contribute to vessel's preservation (crew labor, salvage) should be paid before financiers

#### 3. Limitation of Liability - 46 U.S.C. Â§ 30505

**Statutory Framework:**
The Limitation of Vessel Owner's Liability Act (LVOLA), 46 U.S.C. Â§Â§ 30505-30511, allows vessel owner to limit liability "for damage or injury, occasioned without the owner's privity or knowledge to the value of the vessel or the owner's interest in the vessel."Â²â¸

**Requirements for Limitation:**
1. **File limitation petition** in federal admiralty court within 6 months of first claim notice
2. **Post limitation fund** equal to vessel value plus pending freight
3. **Prove no privity or knowledge** of negligent acts or unseaworthy conditions

**Privity or Knowledge Standard:**
- "Includes actual or constructive knowledge"Â²â¹
- Owner must prove it "availed itself to information that was reasonably available to prevent the claimed losses"
- Corporate owner: Privity imputed for acts of senior management (officers, directors) NOT low-level crew

**Burden of Proof:**
- **Initial burden on claimant:** Prove negligence/unseaworthiness
- **Burden shifts to vessel owner:** Prove no privity or knowledge
- If owner successful: Liability limited to vessel value
- If owner unsuccessful: Full liability for all claims

**Outcomes:**
- **Limitation granted:** All claimants receive pro rata distribution of vessel value
- **Limitation denied:** Claimants recover full damages from vessel owner's general assets

### D. CLEAN AIR ACT PREEMPTION - LA AIR QUALITY LAWSUIT

#### 1. Engine Manufacturers Association v. South Coast AQMD - Controlling Precedent

**Controlling Precedent:** *Engine Manufacturers Ass'n v. South Coast Air Quality Management District*, 541 U.S. 246 (2004)Â³Â¹

**Facts:**
South Coast Air Quality Management District (SCAQMD) enacted six Fleet Rules prohibiting purchase or lease by public/private fleet operators of vehicles not complying with SCAQMD's emission requirements (more stringent than federal Clean Air Act standards).

**Issue:**
Are SCAQMD's Fleet Rules preempted by Â§ 209(a) of the federal Clean Air Act, which prohibits state/local adoption or enforcement of any "standard relating to the control of emissions from new motor vehicles or new motor vehicle engines"?

**Supreme Court Holding (8-1 decision, Justice Scalia):**
"The Supreme Court rejected the argument that the Rules escaped preemption under Â§ 209(a) because they address the **purchase** of vehicles, rather than their manufacture or sale."Â³Â²

**Key Principle:**
"**Standards themselves are separate from enforcement techniques**... standard-enforcement efforts that are proscribed by Â§ 209 can be directed to manufacturers or purchasers."Â³Â³

**Effect:**
State/local regulations that **indirectly** regulate vehicle emissions (by mandating fleet operators purchase only low-emission vehicles) are preempted, just as direct manufacturer regulations would be.

**Remand:**
Court remanded for lower courts to determine whether specific aspects of Fleet Rules were preempted (acknowledged some provisions might survive if they regulate fleet operations, not emission standards).

#### 2. Clean Air Act Preemption - Vessel Emissions vs. Terminal Operations

**Critical Distinction:**

**PREEMPTED (Federal Exclusive Authority):**
- **Ocean-going vessel emissions** (main engines, auxiliary engines, hotelling emissions while at berth)
- Clean Air Act Â§ 209(a) preempts state/local regulation of vessel emission standards
- EPA has exclusive authority under Clean Air Act Â§ 213 (marine engines)

**NOT PREEMPTED (State/Local Authority):**
- **Terminal-side operations** (trucks, cargo handling equipment, shore power infrastructure)
- **Land-based sources** (even if serving maritime commerce)
- **Economic regulations** (requiring terminals to provide zero-emission infrastructure)

**Why the Distinction Matters:**
LA air quality lawsuit likely seeks to regulate BOTH vessel emissions (preempted) AND terminal operations (not preempted). PMSC's defense must identify which demands fall on each side of the line.
