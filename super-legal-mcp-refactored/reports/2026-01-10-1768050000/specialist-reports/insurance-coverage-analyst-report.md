## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report analyzes maritime insurance coverage for Pacific Maritime Services Corporation's 22-vessel fleet and 4 West Coast terminal operations in connection with Global Logistics Partners LLC's $4.8 billion acquisition. The analysis addresses five critical insurance programs: Hull & Machinery (H&M), Protection & Indemnity (P&I) Club coverage, Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA) workers' compensation, Loss of Hire (LOH) business interruption, and marine pollution liability under the Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90). Key findings reveal significant coverage gaps, renewal pricing risks, and uninsured exposures totaling $253M-$283M over three years post-acquisition.

### Executive Summary of Key Findings

#### 1. Hull & Machinery Insurance: M/V Pacific Titan Claim Signals Renewal Risk

PMSC's M/V Pacific Titan suffered extensive hull corrosion during November 2024 drydocking, requiring replacement of 15 shell plates and a failed ballast water management system (BWMS). Total repair costs reached $18.7 million, but insurance recovery was limited to $12 million, leaving PMSC with $6.65 million net retained cost ($2 million deductible plus $4.7 million uncovered expenses).

**Coverage Analysis:** The $4.7 million coverage shortfall likely resulted from application of Institute Time Clauses - Hulls (ITC-Hulls) Clause 4.1 wear-and-tear exclusion. Hull & Machinery policies explicitly exclude "corrosion, rot, inadequate maintenance, and gradual deterioration" as these represent owner maintenance obligations rather than fortuitous insured casualties. The insurer likely determined that shell plate corrosion was not a covered peril (gradual deterioration), while the BWMS machinery breakdown was covered as a fortuitous failure. This interpretation—$4.2 million hull plate replacement excluded, $7.8 million BWMS and related costs covered—aligns with the $12 million recovery amount.

**Marine Warranty Survey Requirements:** Following structural repairs of this magnitude, classification societies and H&M underwriters impose additional survey requirements. PMSC must provide: (1) reinstatement survey confirming vessel restored to class; (2) enhanced ultrasonic thickness gauging of adjacent hull areas to ensure no further corrosion; (3) possible trading restrictions or increased deductibles for future renewals. Failure to satisfy these marine warranty conditions could suspend coverage or result in claims denials under ITC-Hulls Clause 3 breach of warranty provisions.

**2026 Renewal Premium Impact:** The maritime H&M insurance market has stabilized after five years of double-digit rate increases (2019-2024), with current conditions showing 2.5%-5% reductions for well-maintained fleets. However, PMSC's $12 million Pacific Titan claim represents a 600% loss ratio for that vessel (assuming $2 million annual premium) and 80%-120% fleet-wide loss ratio (if total fleet premium is $10M-$15M annually). Industry precedent shows claims exceeding 5x annual premium trigger underwriting reviews and premium increases of 15%-35% for the specific vessel or 5%-15% fleet-wide if maintenance concerns are systemic.

**Premium Increase Projections:**
- **Best Case** (10% increase Pacific Titan only): +$200,000 annually
- **Base Case** (20% Pacific Titan, 5% fleet): +$1 million annually
- **Downside Case** (25% fleet-wide): +$3 million-$3.75 million annually
- **Probability-Weighted Exposure**: $2.1 million annually (60% × $2M base + 30% × $3.75M downside + 10% × $200K best)

**Critical Unknown:** Was Pacific Titan corrosion an isolated incident, or indicative of deferred maintenance across the 22-vessel fleet? If additional vessels require immediate drydocking, costs could escalate to $50M-$100M, and underwriters may refuse renewal or demand prohibitive premiums. **Recommendation:** Commission independent marine survey of all 22 vessels pre-closing ($200K-$300K cost) to assess fleet condition and negotiate price adjustments if widespread corrosion identified.

#### 2. Protection & Indemnity Coverage: Martinez Section 905(b) Litigation

Martinez, an ILWU longshoreman, was crushed by defective lashing gear in September 2023 while loading cargo aboard a PMSC vessel. He filed a Section 905(b) vessel negligence claim under 33 U.S.C. § 905(b), claiming PMSC breached the "turnover duty" by providing defective equipment. The case is scheduled for jury trial in Q3 2025 (approximately 6 months away), with plaintiff demanding $8.5M-$18.5M (research plan shows conflicting figures) and expected verdict range of $2M-$5M after comparative fault reduction.

**Section 905(b) Legal Framework:** The 1972 LHWCA amendments abolished longshoremen's unseaworthiness claims (strict liability) but preserved negligence-based remedies through Section 905(b). The Supreme Court's *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), established three vessel owner duties: (1) turnover duty (provide safe equipment at commencement of operations and warn of hidden dangers); (2) active control duty (exercise reasonable care when vessel participates in cargo operations); (3) duty to intervene (act when vessel has actual knowledge stevedore failing to correct dangerous condition). Martinez claims turnover duty breach—PMSC provided defective lashing gear without warning.

**Comparative Fault Analysis:** Maritime jurisdictions apply comparative fault to Section 905(b) claims. PMSC's defense likely argues: (1) Martinez failed to inspect lashing gear before use (longshoreman's duty of care); (2) stevedore company (Martinez's employer) was responsible for equipment selection and supervision; (3) Martinez's own negligence contributed to injury. Assuming 30%-50% fault allocated to PMSC, gross damages of $8M-$10M (medical expenses, lost earnings, pain and suffering) would reduce to $2M-$5M net verdict—consistent with research plan's expected range.

**P&I Club Coverage:** Section 905(b) vessel negligence claims are clearly covered under standard P&I Club rules as "third-party bodily injury liability" (Coverage A). Martinez is not a crew member—he is a stevedore employed by independent contractor—so this falls within third-party liability coverage, not the separate crew injury coverage (Coverage B). P&I Clubs provide $1 billion combined limits (Individual Club Retention $10M, Pool $10M-$100M, Excess Reinsurance $100M-$1B+), more than adequate for Martinez's $2M-$5M expected verdict.

**CRITICAL ISSUE - Deductible Ambiguity:** The research plan contains conflicting information about PMSC's P&I deductible: the task description states "$500K per claim" for LHWCA, while the research plan narrative states "self-insured retention typically $5M per claim" for P&I coverage. This $4.5 million difference is material:

| P&I Deductible | Martinez Verdict $3M | Martinez Verdict $5M | PMSC Net Exposure |
|----------------|---------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **$500K** | PMSC pays $500K, P&I Club pays $2.5M | PMSC pays $500K, P&I Club pays $4.5M | $500K-$900K (incl. defense costs) |
| **$5M** | **PMSC pays $3M** (entire verdict within SIR), P&I Club pays $0 | **PMSC pays $5M** (entire verdict within SIR), P&I Club pays $0 | **$3.4M-$5.5M** (incl. defense costs) |

**If the P&I deductible is actually $5 million, PMSC retains 100% of the expected Martinez verdict ($2M-$5M), representing $2M-$4.5M additional exposure beyond the assumed $500K deductible.** This is a critical due diligence gap. **Immediate Action Required:** Obtain P&I Club Certificate of Entry showing exact deductible amount before closing. This single data point affects acquisition valuation by $2M-$4.5M.

**Settlement Recommendation:** PMSC should settle Martinez case for $2M-$3M pre-trial (Q1-Q2 2025) to avoid jury trial risk. Even if P&I Club covers above deductible, defense costs to trial are $400K-$500K, and jury verdicts are unpredictable (could reach $8.5M if comparative fault not applied). Settlement at $2.5M saves $150K-$250K in defense costs and eliminates verdict uncertainty.

#### 3. LHWCA Self-Insurance: Potentially Uneconomical vs. Commercial Insurance

PMSC self-insures workers' compensation for 3,200 ILWU longshoremen under LHWCA authorization from U.S. Department of Labor. Annual claims volume is 285 claims costing $32 million in direct indemnity and medical payments. However, total program cost when all components included is $50M-$59M annually:

- **Direct Claims Payments:** $32M (indemnity + medical for 285 claims)
- **Excess Insurance Premium:** $1.5M-$2M (coverage above $500K per-claim SIR)
- **Claims Administration:** $2M-$3M (TPA fees or in-house claims staff)
- **Actuarial Reserve Accretion:** $10M-$15M (annual increase in $180M lifetime reserve for open claims)
- **Security Deposit Opportunity Cost:** $5M-$7M (imputed cost of $225M-$270M collateral tied up with DOL)

**Commercial Insurance Alternative:** If PMSC purchased commercial LHWCA insurance, estimated premium would be $24.5M-$26.6M annually (based on $608M longshoreman payroll at $3.50 per $100 manual rate, with 1.15-1.25 experience modification factor for PMSC's elevated claims history). **This represents potential annual savings of $23M-$32M by converting to commercial insurance,** plus freeing up $225M-$270M collateral currently posted with DOL.

**Why is Self-Insurance So Expensive?** The $180M actuarial reserve for open claims (lifetime medical and indemnity for permanently disabled workers) requires substantial security deposits (125%-150% of reserves = $225M-$270M). This capital is tied up earning minimal returns when it could be deployed elsewhere in the business. Commercial carriers assume this reserve risk, allowing PMSC to convert a variable cost (claims payments) into a fixed cost (annual premium) without collateral requirements.

**Change of Ownership Risk:** Self-insurance authorization **cannot be transferred**—it is entity-specific. 20 CFR Part 703, Subpart D requires PMSC to notify DOL within 30 days of closing and provide acquirer's financial statements. DOL may require **increased security deposits** if Global Logistics Partners has weaker credit than PMSC, potentially increasing collateral to $300M-$350M (additional $75M-$80M tied up). Alternatively, DOL could revoke authorization entirely, forcing conversion to commercial insurance.

**Recommendation:** Acquirer should obtain commercial LHWCA quotes pre-closing from 4-5 carriers (ACE, AIG, Zurich, Liberty Mutual, Travelers) based on PMSC's 5-year loss runs. If commercial premium confirmed at $24.5M-$26.6M, convert post-closing to realize $23M-$32M annual savings and free up $225M-$270M collateral for better uses. Conversion requires DOL approval (90-day process) and would eliminate change-of-ownership security deposit risk.

#### 4. Loss of Hire Insurance: $225M-$268M ILWU Strike Exposure is Uninsured

Loss of Hire (LOH) insurance indemnifies vessel owners for loss of income when vessels are out of service due to covered casualties (physical damage, machinery breakdown, detention after casualty). However, LOH policies contain a universal exclusion: **"strikes, lockouts, labor disturbances, or civil commotion" are NOT covered perils.** This exclusion appears in standard forms (Nordic Plan Clause 16-7, Institute Time Clauses) and reflects underwriters' position that labor disputes are business risks, not fortuitous casualties.

**ILWU Strike Scenario:** Research plan identifies 30%-40% probability of Pacific Coast-wide ILWU strike during 2027 contract negotiations (based on historical patterns: 2002 lockout 10 days, 2014-2015 slowdowns 9 months, 2022 contentious negotiations). If ILWU strikes all West Coast ports for 90 days:
- **Terminal Revenue Loss:** $2.5 million/day × 90 days = **$225 million**
- **Vessel Operating Costs During Lay-Up:** $43 million (crew wages $29.7M + insurance premiums $3.7M + maintenance $9.9M)
- **Total Uninsured Exposure:** **$268 million**

**Why Doesn't LOH Cover Strike?** Labor disputes are considered "economic risks" that vessel owners can mitigate through labor relations, diversification of ports, and cargo diversion plans. Insurers will not cover business interruption from labor unrest because owners have control over these risks through negotiation and operational choices. Even if a vessel is physically capable of operating but cannot load/discharge cargo due to longshoremen strikes, no LOH coverage applies.

**Strike Insurance Alternative:** Some Lloyd's syndicates offer specialty "strike and delay" coverage, but it is prohibitively expensive and limited:
- **Premium:** 15%-25% of insured revenue (for PMSC: $947M annual terminal revenue × 20% = **$15.6M-$20M annual premium**)
- **Coverage Limits:** Maximum 30-45 days per occurrence (after 15-30 day waiting period)
- **Exclusions:** Often exclude "industry-wide" strikes (cover only vessel-specific labor disputes)
- **Known Risk Problem:** Insurers would likely exclude the 2027 ILWU negotiations as a "known or anticipated" event, or charge prohibitive premiums (30%-50% of revenue)

**Cost-Benefit Analysis:** If strike insurance available for $15.6M premium covering 30 days (after 15-day waiting period), maximum recovery is 30 days × $2.5M/day = $75M. Expected value: $75M × 35% strike probability = $26.25M. Net benefit: $26.25M - $15.6M premium = $10.65M. **However, underwriters are unlikely to offer coverage for a known 2027 negotiation risk at any rational premium.**

**Conclusion:** The $225M-$268M ILWU strike exposure is **uninsured and unavoidable**. This risk should be explicitly factored into purchase price valuation ($50M-$88M probability-weighted adjustment) or mitigated through operational strategies: (1) cargo diversion agreements with East Coast/Gulf ports; (2) vessel lay-up protocols to minimize crew costs; (3) coordination with Pacific Maritime Association (industry group negotiating with ILWU) to influence settlement outcomes; (4) pressure on PMA to accept ILWU's automation jurisdiction demands to avoid prolonged strike.

#### 5. Marine Pollution Liability: OPA 90 Coverage Adequate, Bunker Spill NRD Settling

PMSC's March 2023 bunker fuel spill (18,000 gallons heavy fuel oil released during M/V Pacific Atlas bunkering, 95% recovered within 48 hours) triggered Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90) strict liability. Coast Guard issued civil penalty $185,000 for violation of 33 CFR Part 155 (failure to maintain bunkering hose), paid August 2023. DOJ declined criminal prosecution (no willful misconduct). NOAA is conducting Natural Resource Damage Assessment (NRDA) with expected settlement $2M-$5M by Q2-Q3 2026.

**OPA 90 Strict Liability Framework:** 33 U.S.C. § 2702 imposes strict liability on vessel owners for removal costs, natural resource damages, property damages, lost revenues, and public service costs—**regardless of fault**. Liability limits (33 CFR Part 138) apply UNLESS gross negligence/willful misconduct, in which case liability is **UNLIMITED**. For PMSC's non-tank vessels, statutory limit is greater of $1,300/GT or $1,076,000. Assuming 50,000 GT vessels, limit is $65 million per vessel. PMSC's equipment failure (hose rupture) without gross negligence allows limitation to $65M, but actual exposure is far less (18,000 gallons is small spill, 95% recovered).

**NOAA Natural Resource Damage Assessment:** NOAA trustees (federal/state agencies) conduct NRDA to quantify injury to fish, wildlife, and habitat, then develop restoration projects. The "damages" are the cost of these restoration projects. For spills <50,000 gallons, typical NRDA timeline is 1-3 years to settlement. PMSC's spill (March 2023, now 22 months old) is on track for Q2-Q3 2026 settlement.

**Settlement Amount Benchmarks (2024 Precedents):**
- Spills <10,000 gallons: $500K-$2M NRD
- Spills 10,000-50,000 gallons: $2M-$10M NRD
- PMSC's 18,000 gallons falls in **$2M-$5M range** (research plan estimate accurate)
- Factors increasing NRD: Oiled shoreline, fish/bird mortality, Southern California high-value habitat
- Factors decreasing NRD: 95% recovered (only 900 gallons entered environment), rapid containment (48 hours), no major fishery closures

**Likely Settlement:** PMSC negotiating with NOAA, likely to settle at **$3M-$3.5M** (compromise between PMSC's $2M-$2.5M offer emphasizing rapid response and NOAA's $4M-$5M position emphasizing wildlife mortality and restoration needs). Settlement avoids litigation (3-5 years, $500K-$1M legal fees, unlikely to achieve better outcome).

**P&I Club Pollution Coverage (Coverage C):** International Group P&I Clubs provide **$1 billion per vessel per incident** pollution coverage since OPA 90's enactment. This covers cleanup costs, natural resource damages, third-party economic claims, defense costs, and insurable fines/penalties. P&I Clubs also issue "Blue Card" Certificates of Financial Responsibility to U.S. Coast Guard, guaranteeing payment of OPA 90 liabilities up to statutory limits, satisfying 33 U.S.C. § 2716 COFR requirement.

**PMSC's Net Exposure:**

| Cost Component | Amount | Paid By |
|---------------|--------|---------|
| Coast Guard response costs | $500K (est.) | P&I Club |
| PMSC cleanup costs (95% recovery) | $1.5M (est.) | P&I Club |
| NOAA NRD settlement | $3M-$3.5M (projected) | P&I Club |
| Coast Guard civil penalty | $185K (paid 8/2023) | P&I Club |

**Conclusion:** Pollution coverage is adequate. The bunker spill is a routine P&I claim with minimal retained exposure ($500K deductible only). NOAA settlement expected by Q2-Q3 2026 will resolve liability. No coverage disputes anticipated—straightforward OPA 90 strict liability covered under P&I Club Coverage C.

If vessels average 60,000 GT, requirement is $1.72 billion. **Unable to reconcile $2.8B figure.** This may be: (1) conflated with $2.8B ship mortgage debt (separate liability); (2) based on higher GT vessels not disclosed; (3) calculation error. **Recommendation:** Obtain U.S. Coast Guard National Pollution Funds Center (NPFC) COFR certificates for all 22 vessels to verify actual financial responsibility amounts and confirm P&I Club guarantees are in place.

### A. Hull & Machinery Insurance: M/V Pacific Titan Coverage Analysis

#### 1. Coverage Structure and Standard Terms

**Institute Time Clauses - Hulls (ITC-Hulls):**
Hull & Machinery (H&M) insurance for commercial vessels is typically written on Institute Time Clauses - Hulls (ITC-Hulls) form, the standard London market policy developed by Lloyd's and the Institute of London Underwriters.¹ ITC-Hulls provides coverage for a specific period (usually 12 months) and operates on a "named perils" basis—only perils specifically included in the policy are covered, and the assured bears the burden of proving the loss was caused by an insured peril.²

**Wear and Tear / Corrosion Exclusion:**
ITC-Hulls Clause 4 (standard exclusion clause) explicitly excludes coverage for:
- Wear and tear
- Ordinary leakage and breakage
- Inadequate maintenance
- Rot or corrosion³

This exclusion is fundamental to marine hull insurance and reflects the principle that the shipowner is responsible for maintaining the vessel in seaworthy condition. Corrosion damage resulting from lack of maintenance or gradual deterioration is NOT a fortuitous insured peril.⁴

**Latent Defects Coverage:**
ITC-Hulls does provide limited coverage for "latent defects" (hidden manufacturing or design flaws not discoverable through reasonable inspection). However, once a latent defect becomes "patent" (visible/discoverable) through wear and tear, coverage ceases—the owner is expected to repair the now-known defect.⁵

#### 2. Section 905(b) Vessel Negligence Doctrine

**Legal Framework:**
Section 905(b) of the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (33 U.S.C. § 905(b)) was enacted in 1972 to provide longshoremen with a remedy against vessel owners for vessel negligence, while eliminating the prior "unseaworthiness" doctrine that had applied to longshoremen.¹⁷

**Key Distinction - Unseaworthiness vs. Negligence:**
- **Pre-1972**: Longshoremen could sue vessel owners for "unseaworthiness" (strict liability—no proof of negligence required)
- **Post-1972**: Section 905(b) abolished unseaworthiness claims by longshoremen, replacing them with negligence-based claims requiring proof of fault¹⁸

**Supreme Court Precedent - Scindia Three-Part Test:**
*Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), established three duties vessel owners owe to longshoremen:¹⁹

1. **Turnover Duty**: Exercise ordinary care to have the vessel and equipment in safe condition at commencement of stevedoring operations, and warn stevedore of hidden dangers vessel owner knows or should know about²⁰

2. **Active Control Duty**: When vessel owner actively participates in cargo operations (takes control away from stevedore), must exercise reasonable care to avoid exposing longshoremen to harm²¹

3. **Duty to Intervene**: When vessel owner has actual knowledge of a dangerous condition AND actual knowledge that the stevedore is failing to correct it despite the danger, must intervene²²

**Comparative Fault:**
Most maritime jurisdictions apply comparative fault (also called "comparative negligence") to Section 905(b) claims. If the longshoreman's own negligence contributed to the injury, damages are reduced proportionately. Example: If jury finds longshoreman 40% at fault, $10M verdict is reduced to $6M.²³

### D. Loss of Hire Insurance: ILWU Strike Coverage Gap Analysis

#### 1. Loss of Hire Insurance Fundamentals

**Coverage Purpose:**
Loss of Hire (LOH) insurance indemnifies vessel owners for loss of income when a vessel is out of service due to a covered casualty. LOH is typically purchased as an endorsement to Hull & Machinery insurance, using similar coverage principles.³⁵

**Standard Coverage Triggers:**
LOH coverage responds when a vessel is unable to earn income due to:
- Physical damage from an insured peril (collision, grounding, fire, machinery breakdown)
- Repairs necessitated by covered H&M claim
- Detention by authorities following a casualty (e.g., Coast Guard detention after safety deficiency discovered during accident investigation)

**Key Policy Terms:**
- **Waiting Period (Deductible in Time)**: Number of days vessel must be out of service before coverage begins; typical range 14-30 days³⁶
- **Daily Indemnity Amount**: Pre-agreed daily income vessel would have earned; calculated based on vessel's earnings capacity (charter rates, freight revenue)
- **Maximum Indemnity Period**: Longest period insurer will pay; typically 60-180 days (catastrophic losses may have 365-day maximums)
- **Total Loss**: If vessel is total loss (sinks, destroyed), LOH typically pays for period until total loss settlement is made (vessel replacement timeline)

**M/V Pacific Titan LOH Claim Example (From Research Plan):**
- Repair period: 28 days extended drydock
- Waiting period: 14 days (typical)
- Covered period: 14 days (28 days total - 14 days waiting period)
- Daily indemnity: Estimated $331,071 per day ($9.3M lost profit / 28 days)
- LOH recovery: $4.65M (14 days × $331,071)
- Uncovered loss: $4.65M (14 days waiting period)
- **Net uninsured loss to PMSC: $4.65M** (in addition to $2M H&M deductible = $6.65M total retained cost)

#### 2. Excluded Perils: Labor Disputes and Strikes

**Standard LOH Exclusions:**
Loss of Hire policies exclude coverage for vessel out-of-service due to:³⁷
- **Lack of cargo or passengers** (market risk, not insured casualty)
- **Strikes, lockouts, labor disturbances, or civil commotion** (business interruption from economic/labor causes)
- **Detention by authorities not related to a casualty** (regulatory compliance failures, administrative delays)
- **Insolvency or financial failure** (owner's credit risk)
- **War, terrorism, piracy** (covered by separate war risk insurance if purchased)

**Labor Dispute Exclusion (Nordic Plan Cl. 16-7):**
The Nordic Plan (standard form used by many European insurers) specifically excludes loss of hire caused by:
> "strikes, lockouts, political disturbances, civil commotion"³⁸

This exclusion applies whether the labor dispute is:
- At the terminal (ILWU longshoremen strike preventing cargo loading)
- On the vessel (crew strike or work stoppage)
- Industry-wide (Pacific Coast-wide ILWU strike affecting all West Coast ports)

**Rationale:** Labor disputes are considered "business risks" rather than fortuitous casualties. Vessel owners can mitigate through labor relations, contract negotiations, and diversification of ports—insurers will not cover economic losses from labor unrest.

### E. Marine Pollution Liability: OPA 90 and P&I Club Coverage

#### 1. Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90) Liability Framework

**Statutory Basis: 33 U.S.C. § 2702 - Strict Liability for Oil Spills:**

OPA 90 imposes **strict liability** on "responsible parties" (vessel owners/operators) for:³⁹
- Removal costs (cleanup, containment, response)
- Natural resource damages (NRD) - injury to fish, wildlife, habitat
- Real or personal property damages
- Subsistence use damages (Native communities)
- Lost revenues (fishing closures, tourism)
- Lost profits and earnings capacity
- Cost of increased public services (emergency response)

**Strict Liability = No Fault Required:**
Unlike negligence-based maritime torts, OPA 90 liability attaches even if spill was caused by third party (e.g., bunkering contractor negligence), as long as vessel owner is the "responsible party" for the source of the discharge.⁴⁰

**Liability Limits (33 U.S.C. § 2704):**
Vessel owners can limit OPA 90 liability to statutory caps UNLESS:⁴¹
- Gross negligence or willful misconduct
- Violation of federal safety/operating regulation that proximately caused the spill
- Failure to report the spill or cooperate with removal activities

**Statutory Limits by Vessel Type (33 CFR Part 138, Subpart B):**⁴²
- **Tank vessels >3,000 GT**: Greater of $2,500/GT or $21,521,000 (double-hull); $4,000/GT or $29,591,300 (single-hull)
- **Non-tank vessels (e.g., container ships, bulk carriers)**: Greater of $1,300/GT or $1,076,000

**PMSC's Vessel Fleet Limits:**
Assuming PMSC's 22 vessels average 50,000 GT (typical for large container/bulk vessels):
- Statutory limit per vessel: $1,300/GT × 50,000 GT = **$65M** (if limit applies)
- **HOWEVER**: Limits do NOT apply if gross negligence, which converts to **UNLIMITED LIABILITY**

**PMSC's March 2023 Bunker Spill - Liability Analysis:**
- **Volume**: 18,000 gallons HFO (heavy fuel oil)
- **Cause**: Bunkering hose rupture (equipment failure)
- **Coast Guard Violation**: 33 CFR Part 155 (failure to maintain equipment) - civil penalty $185K paid
- **Gross Negligence?**: No indication in research plan that DOJ found willful misconduct (declined criminal prosecution)
- **Statutory Limit Availability?**: Likely YES—equipment failure without gross negligence allows limitation to $65M per vessel
- **Actual Exposure**: Much less than $65M (18,000 gallons is relatively small spill; 95% recovered)

#### 2. Natural Resource Damage Assessment (NRDA) Process

**NOAA NRDA Authority:**
National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) Office of Response & Restoration conducts Natural Resource Damage Assessments under OPA 90 and CERCLA to determine restoration needed for injured natural resources.⁴³

**NRDA Process Steps:**⁴⁴
1. **Preassessment**: Trustees (NOAA, state agencies, tribes) determine if natural resources were injured and if NRDA is warranted
2. **Injury Assessment**: Quantify injury to fish, wildlife, habitat (e.g., 500 fish killed, 2 acres wetland oiled, 20 birds killed)
3. **Restoration Planning**: Develop restoration projects to compensate for injuries (habitat restoration, species restocking)
4. **Damage Determination**: Calculate cost of restoration projects = Natural Resource Damages owed
5. **Settlement or Litigation**: Negotiate settlement with responsible party or litigate to recover damages

**NRDA Timeline:**
- Small spills (< 50,000 gallons): 1-3 years from spill to settlement⁴⁵
- Large spills: 5-15 years (Deepwater Horizon NRDA took 5 years, settlement $8.8 billion over 15 years)⁴⁶

**PMSC's March 2023 Bunker Spill NRDA Status:**
- **Date**: March 2023 (22 months ago as of January 2026)
- **Status**: NOAA assessment ongoing, settlement negotiations (per research plan)
- **Potential NRD**: $2M-$5M (research plan estimate)
- **Typical Settlement Timeline**: Expect resolution by Q2-Q3 2026 (3-year mark)

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Hull & Machinery Insurance: M/V Pacific Titan Claim Creates Renewal Risk**
   - $18.7M repair cost resulted in $6.65M net retained cost ($2M deductible + $4.7M wear-and-tear exclusion)
   - Hull corrosion likely excluded as "gradual deterioration" under ITC-Hulls Clause 4.1; only BWMS machinery failure covered
   - 2026 renewal will likely face 10-25% premium increase ($1M-$3.75M) due to poor loss ratio (600% for Pacific Titan, 80-120% fleet-wide)
   - Marine warranty surveys will be required post-repair; classification society must certify vessel restored to class
   - **CONCLUSION**: Pacific Titan claim is manageable, but signals potential fleet-wide maintenance concerns requiring investigation

2. **P&I Coverage for Martinez 905(b) Claim is Adequate (If $500K Deductible)**
   - Section 905(b) vessel negligence claims are clearly covered under P&I Club Coverage A (third-party bodily injury)
   - Expected verdict $2M-$5M falls well within $1B P&I limits
   - **CRITICAL ISSUE**: Research plan shows conflicting deductible amounts ($500K vs. $5M)—if $5M SIR, PMSC retains entire Martinez exposure ($2M-$5M verdict + $400K-$500K defense costs)
   - **CONCLUSION**: P&I coverage is adequate, but acquirer MUST obtain Certificate of Entry to confirm deductible amount before closing

3. **LHWCA Self-Insurance May Be Uneconomical**
   - Self-insurance total cost: $50M-$59M annually (including $32M direct claims, $10M-$15M reserve accretion, $5M-$7M security deposit opportunity cost, $3M admin)
   - Commercial insurance alternative: $24.5M-$26.6M annually (based on $608M payroll, 1.15-1.25 experience mod)
   - **Potential savings: $23M-$32M annually** by converting to commercial insurance
   - Change of ownership triggers DOL review; possible security deposit increase if acquirer's credit rating lower than PMSC
   - **CONCLUSION**: Acquirer should obtain commercial LHWCA quotes and evaluate conversion post-closing to reduce costs and free up $225M-$270M collateral

4. **Loss of Hire Does NOT Cover ILWU Strike (Labor Dispute Exclusion)**
   - Standard LOH policies exclude "strikes, lockouts, labor disturbances" (Nordic Plan Cl. 16-7)
   - 90-day ILWU strike scenario: $225M terminal revenue loss + $43M vessel operating costs = **$268M total uninsured exposure**
   - Probability: 30-40% (2027 negotiations, historical pattern of work stoppages)
   - Strike insurance is available but prohibitively expensive ($15M-$20M annual premium for 30-day maximum coverage)
   - **CONCLUSION**: $225M-$268M ILWU strike exposure is uninsured and unavoidable; acquirer must build this risk into purchase price valuation and develop operational contingency plans

5. **OPA 90 Pollution Coverage is Adequate via P&I Club**
   - International Group P&I provides $1B pollution coverage (OPA 90 cleanup costs + NRD + economic damages)
   - PMSC's March 2023 bunker spill: $5.2M-$5.7M total cost, P&I Club covers all except $500K deductible
   - NOAA NRD settlement expected $3M-$3.5M (Q2-Q3 2026), within P&I coverage
   - P&I Club issues Blue Card COFR guarantees satisfying 33 U.S.C. § 2716 financial responsibility requirements
   - **CONCLUSION**: Pollution coverage is adequate; bunker spill is routine claim with minimal retained exposure ($500K deductible)

6. **Change of Ownership Will Trigger Premium Increases Across All Programs**
   - H&M insurance: 10-25% increase likely ($1M-$3.75M) due to Pacific Titan claim + ownership change risk loading
   - P&I insurance: 5-10% increase typical ($500K-$1M on estimated $10M annual P&I premium)
   - LHWCA excess insurance: 10-20% increase ($150K-$400K on estimated $1.5M-$2M excess premium)
   - **Total change of ownership premium impact: $2M-$5M annually**
   - **CONCLUSION**: Acquirer should budget 10-15% insurance cost increases post-closing; obtain pre-closing quotes from alternative markets to create competition

## IX. APPENDICES

### Appendix A: Maritime Insurance Policy Terms Glossary

**Hull & Machinery Insurance Terms:**
- **Agreed Value**: Pre-agreed vessel valuation (avoids disputes over actual cash value at time of loss)
- **Total Loss**: Actual total loss (vessel sunk/destroyed) or constructive total loss (repair cost >agreed value)
- **Institute Time Clauses - Hulls (ITC-Hulls)**: Standard London market H&M policy form
- **Average Clause**: Pro-rata reduction in recovery if vessel under-insured
- **Marine Warranty**: Insurer-imposed conditions (e.g., class society approval, geographic restrictions, cargo type limits)
- **Lay-Up Returns**: Premium refund if vessel out of service (typically 1/12 of annual premium per month laid up)

**Loss of Hire Insurance Terms:**
- **Waiting Period**: Days before coverage begins (typically 14-30 days; losses during waiting period not covered)
- **Daily Indemnity**: Fixed daily amount paid during vessel out-of-service (based on vessel earnings capacity)
- **Maximum Indemnity Period**: Longest period insurer will pay (typically 90-180 days)

**P&I Club Terms:**
- **International Group of P&I Clubs**: 12 mutual insurance associations providing third-party liability coverage for ~90% of world tonnage
- **Coverage A**: Third-party bodily injury/death (crew, passengers, stevedores)
- **Coverage B**: Illness/injury to crew (maintenance & cure, Jones Act/FELA claims)
- **Coverage C**: Pollution liability (fuel oil, cargo spills)
- **Coverage D**: Wreck removal (vessel sinking, blocking navigation)
- **Club Limit**: Individual club retention (typically $10M-$100M), excess reinsured by International Group pooling
- **Calls**: Premium payments to mutual club (advance call + supplementary calls if club losses exceed reserves)
