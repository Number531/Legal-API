## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. LA Air Quality Lawsuit - Clean Air Act Preemption Analysis

#### 1. Clean Air Act Preemption Doctrine - *Engine Mfrs. Ass'n v. South Coast AQMD*, 541 U.S. 246 (2004)

**Holding:** The Supreme Court held that Section 209 of the Clean Air Act (42 U.S.C. § 7543) preempts state and local regulations that constitute "standards relating to the control of emissions from new motor vehicles or new motor vehicle engines." *Engine Mfrs. Ass'n v. South Coast Air Quality Management Dist.*, 541 U.S. 246, 255 (2004).

**Key Principles:**
- **Express Preemption:** 42 U.S.C. § 7543(a) prohibits any state or political subdivision from adopting or enforcing "any standard relating to the control of emissions from new motor vehicles."
- **Standard vs. Enforcement Mechanism:** The Court rejected the argument that regulations "escape preemption" because they address the *purchase* of vehicles rather than their manufacture or sale. "[S]tandard-enforcement efforts that are proscribed by § 209 can be directed to manufacturers or purchasers." *Id.* at 255.
- **California Waiver Exception:** Only California can obtain a waiver under 42 U.S.C. § 7543(b) to adopt standards more stringent than federal requirements, provided the state adopted standards prior to March 30, 1966. *Id.* at 253.

**Application to Ocean-Going Vessels:**
While *Engine Mfrs.* addressed motor vehicles, the preemption principles extend to ocean-going vessels under MARPOL Annex VI implementation. The Clean Air Act preempts state and local regulation of **vessel emissions**, but does **NOT** preempt regulation of:
1. **Terminal-side operations:** Diesel trucks, cargo handling equipment (CHE), and shore-side infrastructure
2. **Fuel usage requirements:** California's Ocean-Going Vessel Fuel Regulation (requiring 0.1% sulfur fuel within 24 nm of California baseline)
3. **At-berth shore power mandates:** CARB's 2020 At Berth Regulation requiring vessels to use shore power or CARB-approved emission control strategies

**Federal vs. State Jurisdiction - Critical Distinction:**

| Emission Source | Federal Jurisdiction (Preempted) | State Jurisdiction (NOT Preempted) |
|----------------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Vessel auxiliary engines** | ✓ (MARPOL Annex VI, EPA 40 CFR Part 1043) | ✗ |
| **Vessel hotelling emissions** | ✓ (at-sea and in-port operations) | ✗ (but shore power mandate allowed) |
| **Diesel drayage trucks** | Federal standards (EPA Tier 4) | ✓ (CARB can require ZEV trucks) |
| **Cargo handling equipment** | Federal nonroad engine standards | ✓ (CARB CHE regulation) |
| **Shore power infrastructure** | ✗ | ✓ (state/local land-use authority) |

#### 1. OPA 90 Strict Liability Framework (33 U.S.C. § 2702)

**Liability Standard:**
Under 33 U.S.C. § 2702(a), the "responsible party" for a vessel is strictly liable for:
1. **Removal costs** incurred by the United States, a state, or an Indian tribe for actions to remove or mitigate a discharge
2. **Damages** for injury to, or economic losses resulting from destruction of, natural resources

**Vessel Owner Liability:**
"Responsible party" means, in the case of a vessel, any person owning, operating, or demising by charter the vessel. 33 U.S.C. § 2701(32)(A).

**Limits of Liability:**
For vessels other than tank vessels, liability is limited to the greater of:
- **$1,100 per gross ton**, or
- **$939,800** (adjusted for inflation to **$1,214,700** as of 2023)

33 U.S.C. § 2704(a)(2); [Federal Register Vol. 88, No. 73 (Apr. 14, 2023)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/04/14/2023-07931/oil-spill-financial-responsibility-adjustment-of-the-limit-of-liability-for-offshore-facilities).

**Exception - Limits Not Applicable:**
Liability limits do NOT apply if the discharge was caused by:
- Gross negligence or willful misconduct, or
- Violation of an applicable federal safety, construction, or operating regulation

33 U.S.C. § 2704(c)(1).

#### 2. NOAA Natural Resource Damage Assessment Methodology (43 CFR Part 11, 15 CFR Part 990)

**Regulatory Framework:**
- **DOI Regulations:** 43 CFR Part 11 (CERCLA NRDAs)
- **NOAA Regulations:** 15 CFR Part 990 (OPA 90 NRDAs - **supersedes 43 CFR Part 11 for oil spills**)

**Three-Phase Assessment Process (43 CFR § 11.14):**

1. **Preassessment Phase** (§ 11.23):
   - Determine whether injury to natural resources occurred
   - Identify potentially responsible parties
   - Notify PRPs of potential NRDA

2. **Assessment Plan Phase** (§ 11.30-11.38):
   - Develop Injury Assessment Plan
   - Quantify injuries and baseline resource conditions
   - Estimate recovery time and interim losses

3. **Post-Assessment Phase** (§ 11.80-11.84):
   - Determine damages (restoration costs + compensable value)
   - Develop Restoration and Compensation Determination Plan
   - Demand payment or initiate litigation

**Damage Measure (43 CFR § 11.35(b)):**
"The measure of damages is the cost of restoring, rehabilitating, replacing, and/or acquiring the equivalent of the injured natural resources and the services those resources provided, plus the compensable value, if any, of all or part of the services lost to the public for the time period between the discharge or release and the completion of the restoration, rehabilitation, replacement, and/or acquisition of equivalent of those resources and services (known as interim lost use)."

**Cost Recovery Methodology:**
NRD = **Primary Restoration** + **Compensatory Restoration** + **Assessment Costs**

Where:
- **Primary Restoration:** Cost to return injured resources to baseline condition (cleanup, rehabilitation)
- **Compensatory Restoration:** Cost to compensate public for interim loss of services (e.g., habitat enhancement, species restoration projects)
- **Assessment Costs:** Reasonable costs of conducting NRDA (field studies, modeling, expert fees)

#### 2. Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) Rating System (IMO MEPC.328(76))

**Regulatory Basis:**
IMO adopted amendments to MARPOL Annex VI through Resolution MEPC.328(76) (June 17, 2021), establishing mandatory annual carbon intensity ratings from January 1, 2023.

**CII Calculation:**
CII = (CO₂ emissions in grams) / (deadweight tonnage × nautical miles)

Expressed in **gCO₂ per dwt-nm**, CII measures vessel efficiency of CO₂ emitted per unit of cargo transported.

**Rating System:**
Vessels receive annual ratings (A = best, E = worst) compared to a reference line and reduction factors:

| Rating | Performance | Required Action |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **A** | Major superior | None |
| **B** | Minor superior | None |
| **C** | Moderate (acceptable) | None |
| **D** | Minor inferior | **SEEMP Part III** corrective action plan if **3 consecutive years** |
| **E** | Inferior | **SEEMP Part III** corrective action plan **immediately** (1 year) |

**SEEMP Part III Requirements (for D/E-rated vessels):**
- CII calculation methodology
- Required CII values over next 3 years
- Implementation plan for achieving required CII
- Procedures for self-evaluation and improvement
- **Must be approved by flag state Administration or Recognized Organization**

[Sources: [IMO MEPC.328(76) Resolution](https://wwwcdn.imo.org/localresources/en/KnowledgeCentre/IndexofIMOResolutions/MEPCDocuments/MEPC.328(76).pdf), [Lloyd's Register CII Overview](https://www.lr.org/en/services/statutory-compliance/marpol-international-convention-for-the-prevention-of-pollution/carbon-intensity-indicator/)]

#### 2. Exhaust Gas Cleaning Systems (Scrubbers)

**Technology Overview:**
Scrubbers remove sulfur oxides (SOx) from vessel exhaust, allowing use of higher-sulfur fuel (IFO 380, 3.5% sulfur) instead of low-sulfur marine gas oil (MGO, 0.5%/0.1% sulfur).

**Capital Costs:**

**Operational Economics:**

| Factor | Open-Loop Scrubber | Closed-Loop Scrubber | Hybrid Scrubber |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| **Capital cost** | Lowest ($2M-$4M) | Medium ($3M-$5M) | Highest ($4M-$6M) |
| **Fuel cost savings** | $2,600/day (VLCC in ECA) | $2,600/day | $2,600/day |
| **Annual savings** | ~$950K/vessel | ~$950K/vessel | ~$950K/vessel |
| **Payback period** | 2-4 years | 3-5 years | 4-6 years |
| **Regulatory risk** | High (discharge bans in sensitive waters) | Low (no discharge) | Medium (operational flexibility) |

**Breakeven Analysis:**
Scrubbers become economically compelling when:
- Vessel burns **>4,000 MT fuel in ECA annually** (breakeven)
- Optimal: **>7,500 MT fuel in ECA annually**
- A US West Coast containership burning 9,600 MT in ECA saves ~$2.6M annually

**Regulatory Challenges:**
- **Port discharge bans:** Singapore, California (partial), EU ports increasingly banning open-loop scrubber discharge
- **Sludge disposal:** Closed-loop scrubbers generate hazardous waste requiring disposal
- **Future-proofing:** Scrubbers do NOT reduce CO₂ or NOx (only SOx) → Limited value for GHG regulations

**PMSC Application:**
- **22-vessel fleet:** If average fuel consumption 3,000-5,000 MT/year in ECA

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **LA Air Quality Lawsuit - Partial Preemption Defense Available**

   The Clean Air Act expressly preempts state and local regulation of **vessel emissions** (42 U.S.C. § 7543; *Engine Mfrs. Ass'n v. South Coast AQMD*, 541 U.S. 246 (2004)), but does NOT preempt regulation of **terminal-side operations** (diesel trucks, cargo handling equipment, shore power infrastructure). PMSC's motion to dismiss should argue that City's demands related to "vessel hotelling emissions" are preempted, while conceding state authority over CHE/truck emissions.

   **Settlement projection:** $125M-$175M (40-55% of $315M demand) with phased 5-year compliance timeline, based on:
   - SSA Containers precedent ($2.5M for CHE non-compliance)
   - China Shipping terminal settlement ($50M for pollution mitigation)
   - PMSC's existing 40% CHE electrification (exceeds CAAP 25% voluntary target)
   - EPA Clean Ports Program grants available (30-40% funding offset)

2. **Bunker Fuel Spill - NOAA NRD Settlement Range $5M-$8M**

   OPA 90 imposes strict liability on vessel owners (33 U.S.C. § 2702), with damages calculated under NOAA's Natural Resource Damage Assessment methodology (15 CFR Part 990). PMSC's March/October 2023 spill (18,500 gallons HFO, 95% recovered) likely results in **$5M-$8M NRD** based on:
   - Cosco Busan precedent: $837/gallon all-in settlement ($608/gallon NRD only)
   - PMSC's superior recovery rate (95% vs. Cosco Busan's slower response)
   - Lower wildlife impact (Long Beach commercial harbor vs. SF Bay sensitive habitat)
   - Coast Guard finding of maintenance failure (potential gross negligence → no liability cap)

   California OSPRA penalties ($100K-$250K) likely already resolved in Coast Guard's $185K civil penalty.

3. **IMO CII Compliance - Retrofit Economically Superior to Scrapping**

   PMSC's 2 D-rated bulk carriers require corrective action under IMO MEPC.328(76) to avoid commercial disadvantage (charter party rejections, rate discounts). **Retrofit strategy ($1.7M-$4.3M total) achieves 15-25% CII improvement** through:
   - Voyage optimization software ($100K-$300K)
   - Engine tuning ($100K-$400K)
   - Hull coating/propeller upgrades ($600K-$1.6M)
   - Energy-saving devices ($1M-$3M)

   This is economically superior to scrapping ($46M-$74M write-down) and avoids $12M/year revenue loss from speed reduction.

4. **MARPOL Annex VI Compliance - Fleet-Wide Audit Required**

   EPA and Coast Guard jointly enforce MARPOL Annex VI violations with penalties of **$25K/day per violation** (33 U.S.C. § 1908(b)). PMSC's 22-vessel fleet must verify:
   - NOx Tier III compliance (vessels built ≥2016 in ECA)
   - Sulfur 0.1% ECA / 0.5% global compliance
   - No carriage of non-compliant fuel without scrubber (carriage ban since March 1, 2020)

   **Recommendation:** Conduct internal MARPOL audit pre-closing to identify/remediate violations before EPA inspection.

5. **Vessel Emission Reduction Roadmap - $380M-$473M Near-Term Investment**

   PMSC faces significant capital requirements for environmental compliance over 5-year horizon:
   - **Shore power infrastructure:** $346M-$397M (mitigated by EPA grants, phased installation)
   - **CII improvement (2 vessels):** $1.7M-$4.3M
   - **Scrubbers (8-12 vessels):** $32M-$72M (3-6 year payback via fuel savings)

   **Operational savings offset:** $27M-$38M/year (shore power fuel savings $16.5M-$22M + scrubber savings $7.6M-$11.4M + voyage optimization $3M-$5M)

   **Net 15-year payback:** 12-18 years (before grants), reduced to **8-12 years** with EPA Clean Ports Program funding (30-40% of shore power capex).
