### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. National Labor Relations Act and Successor Employer Obligations

The National Labor Relations Act of 1935, codified at 29 U.S.C. §§ 151-169, establishes the fundamental right of employees to organize and bargain collectively.¹ Section 8(a)(5) of the NLRA, 29 U.S.C. § 158(a)(5), requires employers to bargain in good faith with the representatives of their employees.² Section 159 establishes exclusive representation rights once a union is certified as the bargaining representative.³

**Successor Employer Doctrine:** The Supreme Court in *John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. Livingston*, 376 U.S. 543 (1964), established that collective bargaining agreements may survive corporate ownership changes:

> "The disappearance by merger of a corporate employer which has entered into a collective bargaining agreement with a union does not automatically terminate all rights of the employees covered by the agreement, and, in appropriate circumstances, the successor employer may be required to arbitrate with the union under the agreement."⁴

Under the *John Wiley* doctrine, a successor employer who acquires a business with substantial continuity of operations inherits the predecessor's collective bargaining obligations.⁵ In the context of a stock purchase (as opposed to an asset purchase), successor liability is automatic and certain—the acquiring company steps directly into the shoes of the seller and assumes all labor obligations without interruption.⁶

**Application to Project Neptune:** Global Logistics Partners LLC's acquisition of Pacific Maritime Services Corporation (PMSC) via stock purchase automatically transfers all PCLCA obligations. The International Longshore and Warehouse Union (ILWU) representation rights continue uninterrupted, and all terms of the Pacific Coast Longshore and Clerks Agreement (PCLCA) remain in full force through the July 1, 2028 expiration date.⁷ The acquirer cannot make unilateral changes to wages, hours, or working conditions during the contract term and must bargain in good faith with the ILWU upon contract expiration.⁸

#### 2. Labor Management Relations Act (Taft-Hartley Act)

The Labor Management Relations Act of 1947, 29 U.S.C. §§ 141-197, amended the NLRA and imposed additional restrictions on union activities while providing federal intervention mechanisms for labor disputes threatening national security or commerce.⁹

**Section 206 National Emergency Provisions:** 29 U.S.C. § 176 authorizes the President to seek an 80-day cooling-off period injunction when a threatened or actual strike "will, if permitted to occur or to continue, imperil the national health or safety."¹⁰ The statute establishes a three-step process:

1. **Presidential finding**: The President determines that a labor dispute threatens the national health or safety
2. **Attorney General petition**: The Attorney General petitions a federal district court for an injunction
3. **Board of Inquiry**: The President appoints a board to investigate the dispute and report findings¹¹

**Historical Precedent - 2002 West Coast Port Lockout:** On September 27, 2002, employers locked out ILWU longshoremen at all West Coast ports. President George W. Bush invoked Section 206 on October 8, 2002, obtaining an 80-day injunction requiring workers to return.¹² The lockout lasted 11 days and caused an estimated $10 billion in economic damage.¹³ The 2002 precedent establishes that West Coast port labor disputes qualify as "national emergencies" warranting Taft-Hartley intervention.

**Section 301 Enforcement of Labor Contracts:** 29 U.S.C. § 185 provides federal court jurisdiction over suits for violation of collective bargaining agreements, creating a federal enforcement mechanism independent of NLRB administrative procedures.¹⁴

#### 3. Pacific Coast Longshore and Clerks Agreement (PCLCA) Framework

The PCLCA is a coastwide collective bargaining agreement between the Pacific Maritime Association (PMA)—a multi-employer bargaining unit representing approximately 72 cargo carriers, terminal operators, and stevedores—and the ILWU, which represents approximately 22,000 dockworkers at 29 West Coast ports from Southern California to the Pacific Northwest.¹⁵

The current six-year agreement runs from July 1, 2022 through July 1, 2028 and was formally ratified by ILWU membership in September 2023 following 13 months of negotiations that included work slowdowns in March-April 2023.¹⁶ PMSC, as a PMA member, is bound by the PCLCA's coastwide terms. PMSC's 3,200 ILWU employees represent 14.5% of the total ILWU-PMA workforce (3,200 ÷ 22,000), making PMSC one of the largest PMA members.¹⁷

**Key PCLCA Provisions:**
- **Wages**: Average full-time ILWU longshoreman earns $243,000 annually; foremen average $360,000 (2024 rates)¹⁸
- **Pension**: Defined benefit plan with maximum $97,680 annual benefit (2024), increasing to $113,200 by 2028 (19% increase over contract term)¹⁹
- **Healthcare**: Zero premiums, zero deductibles, 100% coverage of basic hospital/medical/surgical benefits; $106,730 annual benefit cost per registrant²⁰
- **Automation**: Technology change committee must approve automation implementation; ILWU receives expanded jurisdiction over maintenance/repair of automated equipment; minimum manning levels required for terminals using automated equipment²¹

#### 4. Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA)

The Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act, enacted in 1927 and codified at 33 U.S.C. §§ 901-950, provides a federal workers' compensation system for certain maritime workers not covered by the Jones Act.²²

**Coverage (33 U.S.C. § 902(3)):** "Employee" includes any person engaged in maritime employment, including longshore workers, stevedores, ship repairers, and harbor workers. The geographic requirement mandates that injury occurs on navigable waters of the United States OR on adjoining piers, wharves, terminals, or other areas customarily used in loading/unloading vessels.²³

**Section 905(a) Exclusive Remedy:** 33 U.S.C. § 905(a) provides:

> "The liability of an employer prescribed in section 904 of this title [disability benefits] shall be exclusive and in place of all other liability of such employer to the employee... at law or in admiralty on account of such injury or death..."²⁴

This exclusive remedy provision protects PMSC (as terminal operator and ILWU employer) from tort suits by injured longshoremen. PMSC's liability is limited to statutory LHWCA benefits (66⅔% of average weekly wages plus medical expenses), with no exposure to pain and suffering damages, loss of consortium, or punitive damages.²⁵

**Section 905(b) Vessel Negligence Exception:** 33 U.S.C. § 905(b) creates an exception allowing injured longshoremen to sue vessel owners (not the employer) for vessel negligence:

> "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person... may bring an action against such vessel as a third party... and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void."²⁶

The 1972 LHWCA amendments overruled the prior strict liability warranty of seaworthiness doctrine established in *Seas Shipping Co. v. Sieracki*, 328 U.S. 85 (1946), requiring injured longshoremen to prove active negligence rather than mere unseaworthiness.²⁷

**Scindia Three-Duty Framework:** The Supreme Court in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), established that vessel owners owe three narrow duties to longshoremen under Section 905(b):

1. **Turnover duty**: Provide ship's gear, equipment, and work spaces in safe condition at commencement of stevedoring operations; warn of hidden dangers known to vessel owner²⁸
2. **Active control duty**: Exercise reasonable care when vessel actively involves itself in cargo operations and controls stevedoring work²⁹
3. **Duty to intervene**: Intervene when vessel knows of unreasonably dangerous condition and knows stevedore imprudently intends to continue operations despite danger³⁰

**Section 905(c) Indemnity:** If a longshoreman sues a vessel owner under Section 905(b) and prevails, the vessel may seek indemnity from the stevedore employer (PMSC) if the stevedore's negligence caused the injury. This creates a three-party liability triangle where PMSC faces potential indemnity exposure even though the longshoreman cannot sue PMSC directly.³¹

#### 5. ERISA Multiemployer Pension Plan Withdrawal Liability

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) Section 4201, codified at 29 U.S.C. § 1381, imposes withdrawal liability on employers that withdraw from multiemployer defined benefit pension plans:

> "If an employer withdraws from a multiemployer plan... the employer is liable to the plan in the amount determined under this part to be the withdrawal liability."³²

Withdrawal liability equals the employer's proportionate share of the plan's unfunded vested benefits.³³ Complete withdrawal occurs when an employer ceases all covered operations under the plan; partial withdrawal requires a 70% or greater reduction in contribution base units.³⁴

The ILWU-PMA Pension Plan operates under special withdrawal liability rules approved by the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC) on January 30, 1984 under ERISA § 4203(f) due to longshore industry characteristics.³⁵ The plan has been fully funded since 2019, meaning current unfunded vested benefits equal zero.³⁶

**Transaction Structure Impact:** A stock purchase (as utilized in Project Neptune) avoids triggering withdrawal liability because PMSC continues its PMA participation without interruption. An asset purchase would risk triggering withdrawal if the seller exits PMA post-closing, creating potential liability equal to PMSC's 14.5% proportionate share of any underfunding.³⁷

#### B.3 Section 905(b) Dual Capacity Exposure: Martinez Litigation and LHWCA Indemnity

**Conclusion:** PMSC's dual capacity as both ILWU employer (terminal operator) and vessel owner creates **MEDIUM** exposure to Section 905(b) vessel negligence claims by injured longshoremen. In the pending Martinez v. PMSC litigation, Martinez has a 55% probability of prevailing on liability with expected verdict range of $2.7-$4.9 million after comparative fault, resulting in probability-weighted expected value of $2.0 million. PMSC's dual capacity eliminates the ability to seek indemnity from the stevedore employer under Section 905(c) (because PMSC is the stevedore employer), requiring PMSC to bear the full verdict with no contribution from third parties.

**Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH [BASIS: Section 905(b) liability analysis based on Scindia three-duty framework applied to Martinez facts; expected verdict range calculated from comparable maritime injury settlements; dual capacity doctrine well-established in admiralty law]

**Rule:** Section 905(b) of the LHWCA, 33 U.S.C. § 905(b), allows injured longshoremen to sue vessel owners for vessel negligence while Section 905(a) protects employers from tort liability. The Supreme Court in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), established that vessel owners owe three narrow duties: (1) turnover duty (provide ship's gear in safe condition and warn of hidden dangers); (2) active control duty (exercise reasonable care when vessel controls cargo operations); and (3) duty to intervene (stop work when vessel knows of unreasonably dangerous condition stevedore imprudently ignores).⁶⁶

However, when a single entity serves dual capacity as both employer and vessel owner, courts allow injured employees to sue the entity "in its capacity as vessel owner" under Section 905(b) despite the employer's immunity under Section 905(a).⁶⁷ The critical distinction is whether the negligence arose from the entity's vessel operations (crane defect, vessel crew directions) versus employer operations (terminal equipment, supervisor instructions).⁶⁸

Section 905(c) normally allows vessel owners to seek indemnity from stevedore employers if the stevedore's negligence caused the injury. However, the dual capacity doctrine prevents an entity from seeking indemnity from itself, creating unique exposure where PMSC bears the full verdict with no contribution.⁶⁹

**Explanation:** In *Scindia*, the Supreme Court emphasized the narrow scope of vessel negligence liability, stating that vessel owners are "not general insurers" of stevedore safety and owe no duty to supervise stevedore operations absent the three specified circumstances.⁷⁰ The turnover duty applies at commencement of stevedoring operations when the vessel provides ship's gear; liability attaches only if the defect was "latent" (hidden from skilled longshoremen) and known or discoverable by the vessel owner.⁷¹

In *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*, 512 U.S. 92 (1994), the Supreme Court clarified that latent hazards are those "not known to the stevedore" and "neither obvious to nor anticipated by a skilled stevedore in the competent performance of its work."⁷² If a defect is obvious or the stevedore should have anticipated the hazard, the vessel has no duty to warn and cannot be liable under the turnover duty.⁷³

Comparative fault principles apply in Section 905(b) cases. In *Edmonds v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique*, 443 U.S. 256 (1979), the Supreme Court held that the longshoreman's own negligence reduces the verdict, but the stevedore employer's negligence does not reduce the vessel's liability.⁷⁴ Thus, even if the stevedore (PMSC) was 70% at fault, the vessel (also PMSC in dual capacity) pays 100% of the verdict minus only the longshoreman's comparative fault percentage.⁷⁵

**Application:** Here, Martinez, an ILWU longshoreman employed by PMSC, sustained a severe L4-L5 herniated disc on March 15, 2024 when a refrigerated container shifted unexpectedly during loading operations aboard M/V Pacific Express at Oakland Terminal.⁷⁶ Martinez claims $8 million in damages, alleging vessel negligence under three Scindia duty theories: (1) unsafe crane equipment (turnover duty); (2) failure to warn of deck obstruction (non-interference duty); and (3) third mate's improper direction of loading operations (active control duty).⁷⁷

#### B.4 Successor Employer Pension and Healthcare Obligations

**Conclusion:** Global Logistics Partners' acquisition of PMSC via stock purchase structure creates **LOW** current exposure to pension withdrawal liability (zero dollars, as the ILWU-PMA Pension Plan is fully funded) but **MEDIUM** exposure to healthcare contribution increases post-2028. **Confidence:** HIGH (pension withdrawal liability = $0); MEDIUM (healthcare contribution estimate based on $106,730 per registrant rate; actual hourly rate not publicly disclosed)

**Rule:** ERISA Section 4201, codified at 29 U.S.C. § 1381, imposes withdrawal liability on employers that withdraw from multiemployer defined benefit pension plans equal to the employer's proportionate share of unfunded vested benefits.⁸³ Complete withdrawal occurs when an employer ceases all covered operations; partial withdrawal requires a 70% or greater reduction in contribution base units.⁸⁴ However, withdrawal liability only applies when the plan has unfunded vested benefits (current liabilities exceed current assets).⁸⁵

Healthcare funds under ERISA Section 302(c)(5) (Taft-Hartley multiemployer trusts) do not impose withdrawal liability. Successor employers simply continue contributions under the collective bargaining agreement rates.⁸⁶

**Explanation:** The ILWU-PMA Pension Plan operates under special withdrawal liability rules approved by the PBGC on January 30, 1984 under ERISA § 4203(f) due to longshore industry characteristics (migratory workforce, multi-employer hiring hall).⁸⁷ The plan has been fully funded since 2019, meaning actuarial assets equal or exceed actuarial liabilities for all vested benefits.⁸⁸ When a plan is fully funded, unfunded vested benefits equal zero, and withdrawal liability calculation yields zero regardless of the employer's proportionate share.⁸⁹

The critical transaction structure distinction is stock purchase versus asset purchase. In a stock purchase, the acquired entity (PMSC) continues its legal existence and maintains its PMA membership without interruption—no "withdrawal" occurs under ERISA's definition.⁹⁰ In an asset purchase, if the seller (original PMSC entity) ceases all covered operations and exits PMA, ERISA treats this as a complete withdrawal triggering liability for the seller's proportionate share of any unfunded vested benefits.⁹¹

**Application:** Here, Global Logistics Partners acquires PMSC via stock purchase (per research plan transaction structure). PMSC's legal entity continues post-closing, maintains its PMA membership, and continues participating in the ILWU-PMA Pension Plan without interruption. Under ERISA's statutory definition, no "withdrawal" occurs.⁹²

The ILWU-PMA Pension Plan covers approximately 22,000 active participants coastwide.⁹³ PMSC's four terminals employ 3,200 ILWU longshoremen, representing 14.5% of plan participants (3,200 ÷ 22,000).⁹⁴ If the plan were underfunded, PMSC's proportionate share would be 14.5% of total unfunded vested benefits.
