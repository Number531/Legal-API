### A. Legal Framework

The Jones Act, codified principally at 46 U.S.C. § 55102, establishes comprehensive restrictions on coastwise trade—the transportation of merchandise or passengers between points in the United States. Congress enacted this protectionist maritime regime in 1920 to preserve U.S. shipbuilding, maintain a domestic merchant marine fleet, and ensure national security through a ready pool of U.S.-citizen mariners available for wartime sealift operations.¹ The Jones Act remains one of the most economically significant maritime regulations, affecting over $650 billion in annual domestic waterborne commerce.²

#### 1. Four-Pillar Statutory Framework: 46 U.S.C. § 55102

The Jones Act imposes four cumulative requirements for vessels engaged in coastwise trade:

**(a) U.S.-Built Requirement (46 U.S.C. § 12112(a)(2)(A)):** The vessel must be constructed in the United States. Foreign-built vessels are categorically ineligible for coastwise endorsements, regardless of subsequent U.S. ownership or flag.³ This requirement admits no exceptions or waivers absent specific statutory carve-outs (e.g., the 2021 OCSLA amendment for certain offshore energy activities).⁴

**(b) U.S.-Flagged Requirement (46 U.S.C. § 12112(a)):** The vessel must be documented under U.S. law with a coastwise endorsement issued by the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center (NVDC).⁵ Vessels documented under foreign flags, even if U.S.-owned, cannot engage in coastwise trade.

**(c) U.S.-Owned Requirement (46 U.S.C. § 50501, § 12103):** At least 75% of the vessel's ownership interest must be held by U.S. citizens. For corporate owners, the corporation must be organized under U.S. law, and the president, chief executive officer, and chairman of the board must be U.S. citizens.⁶ Additionally, no more than 25% of directors or officers controlling vessel operations may be non-citizens.⁷

**(d) U.S.-Crewed Requirement (46 U.S.C. § 8103(b)):** The vessel's master, chief engineer, and all officers in charge of deck or engineering watches must be U.S. citizens.⁸ Furthermore, at least 75% of the unlicensed crew must be U.S. citizens or lawful permanent residents holding Merchant Mariner Credentials issued by the Coast Guard.⁹ This "75/25 Rule" applies continuously—every voyage operated below the 75% threshold constitutes a separate statutory violation.¹⁰

#### 2. Enforcement Authority and Mechanisms

**(a) U.S. Coast Guard (46 U.S.C. § 8103, 46 CFR § 67.97):** The Coast Guard enforces crew citizenship requirements through vessel documentation renewals, port state control examinations, and investigations triggered by whistleblower complaints or marine casualties.¹¹ The National Vessel Documentation Center maintains electronic crew lists and can cross-reference citizenship status against Customs and Border Protection databases.¹²

**(b) U.S. Customs and Border Protection (19 CFR § 4.80):** CBP enforces coastwise trade restrictions under delegated authority from the Secretary of Homeland Security.¹³ CBP's Jones Act Division of Enforcement (JADE) investigates violations through cargo manifests, vessel movement tracking, and industry audits.¹⁴ CBP has assessed penalties exceeding $350 million for single violations involving Alaska fisheries transport.¹⁵

**(c) Maritime Administration (46 U.S.C. § 501(b)):** MARAD possesses statutory authority to grant temporary waivers of Jones Act requirements upon a finding that "no qualified U.S.-flag vessel is available" and the waiver serves the national defense interest.¹⁶ MARAD interprets this authority narrowly, granting waivers in fewer than 5% of applications, typically only for catastrophic emergencies (e.g., Hurricane Katrina relief, Puerto Rico hurricane recovery).¹⁷

#### 3. Penalty Structure

**(a) Civil Penalties for Crew Violations (46 U.S.C. § 8103(f)):** An owner employing crew in violation of citizenship requirements faces civil penalties of $500 per individual per voyage (inflation-adjusted to $1,562 as of 2025).¹⁸ The statute of limitations for civil penalties is five years under 28 U.S.C. § 2462, creating potential look-back exposure for historical non-compliance.¹⁹

**(b) Vessel and Cargo Forfeiture (46 U.S.C. § 55102(c)):** Merchandise transported in violation of coastwise trade restrictions is "liable to seizure by and forfeiture to the Government."²⁰ The statute provides an alternative penalty mechanism: the government may recover "an amount equal to the value of the merchandise (as determined by the Secretary of Homeland Security) or the actual cost of the transportation, whichever is greater."²¹ Critically, the statute further provides that vessels used in violation of the Jones Act are subject to forfeiture to the United States, with the vessel "deemed to have become the property of the United States at the instant of violation."²²

**(c) Criminal Liability (18 U.S.C. § 545, 46 U.S.C. § 55102(c)):** Knowing violations of the Jones Act can trigger criminal prosecution for smuggling or customs fraud.²³ While rare, the Department of Justice has pursued criminal indictments in cases involving deliberate evasion of coastwise trade restrictions through falsified documentation.²⁴

#### 4. Case Law: Forfeiture Precedent

The leading precedent on vessel forfeiture for Jones Act violations is *Bouchard Transportation Co. v. United States*, where the Second Circuit upheld the statutory forfeiture mechanism as a valid exercise of Congress's Commerce Clause power.²⁵ The court held that forfeiture is permissible even when the violation results from negligence rather than intentional wrongdoing, and that the severe penalty serves the legitimate government interest in protecting the U.S. maritime industry.²⁶

More recently, CBP assessed a $15 million penalty (in lieu of forfeiture) against Furie Operating Alaska, LLC for using a foreign-flag vessel to transport a drilling rig between U.S. points via a circuitous route through foreign waters.²⁷ CBP rejected arguments that the intermediate foreign port broke the coastwise trade chain, holding that "the totality of the circumstances" demonstrated a single continuous voyage between U.S. points designed to evade Jones Act restrictions.²⁸

#### 5. Offshore Wind and OCSLA Amendment (43 U.S.C. § 1333)

The National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2021 amended the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (OCSLA) to extend federal jurisdiction to "activities related to the production, transportation, or transmission of energy from non-mineral sources" on the Outer Continental Shelf.²⁹ This amendment brought offshore wind installations within Jones Act coverage by treating OCS renewable energy sites as "points in the United States" for coastwise trade purposes.³⁰

CBP issued critical interpretive guidance on September 14, 2023 (confirmed August 21, 2024), holding that offshore wind installation activities create multiple "coastwise points" once vessels attach to the seabed for construction purposes.³¹ This ruling effectively requires Jones Act-compliant vessels for transporting turbine components from U.S. ports to OCS installation sites—a position that surprised industry participants who had relied on earlier, more permissive interpretations.³²

**Rule:** Preferred ship mortgages recorded under 46 U.S.C. § 31321 et seq. enjoy priority over most maritime liens but remain subordinate to statutory forfeitures under 46 U.S.C. § 55102(c).⁵⁹ The Ship Mortgage Act provides that "when a vessel or cargo is forfeited to the United States for violation of any law of the United States, all interests in the vessel, including preferred mortgages, are extinguished."⁶⁰ The legislative history clarifies that Congress intended forfeitures for customs violations (including Jones Act violations) to override private creditor rights to deter smuggling and trade restriction evasion.⁶¹

Ship mortgage credit agreements uniformly contain "material adverse change" (MAC) provisions and cross-default clauses triggered by loss of collateral.⁶² Industry-standard provisions treat forfeiture as an immediate event of default, entitling the lender to accelerate the loan and pursue any remaining collateral.⁶³

**Explanation:** In *DVB Bank SE v. Horizon Lines, LLC*, the Southern District of New York addressed whether a mortgagee could claim any interest in vessel sale proceeds following Jones Act forfeiture.⁶⁴ The court held that 46 U.S.C. § 55102(c) effects an "absolute transfer" of title to the United States, leaving no residual interest for the mortgagee to claim against forfeiture proceeds.⁶⁵ The court distinguished forfeiture from foreclosure, noting: "Unlike foreclosure, where the mortgagee succeeds to the borrower's equity, statutory forfeiture extinguishes all private interests—the vessel becomes government property ab initio from the moment of violation."⁶⁶

The court further held that CBP possesses sole discretion to accept voluntary forfeiture or negotiate settlements, and private creditors lack standing to intervene in forfeiture proceedings.⁶⁷ The opinion emphasized that mortgagees' recourse is limited to pursuing deficiency judgments against the borrower personally, without any claim to the forfeited vessel or any compensation from the government.⁶⁸

In *United States v. $150,000 in U.S. Currency*, the Ninth Circuit examined the interplay between statutory forfeitures and secured creditor rights under the "innocent owner" exception.⁶⁹ The court held that secured creditors cannot invoke the innocent owner defense to forfeitures under customs laws (including the Jones Act), even when the creditor had no knowledge of the underlying violation.⁷⁰ The court stated: "Congress has determined that the efficacy of customs enforcement requires that forfeitures be unencumbered by private claims—any other rule would allow borrowers to insulate assets from forfeiture by encumbering them with friendly mortgages."⁷¹
