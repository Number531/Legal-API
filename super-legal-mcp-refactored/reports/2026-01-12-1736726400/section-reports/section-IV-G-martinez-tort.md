### A. Legal Framework

Maritime tort claims brought by injured longshoremen against vessel owners occupy a unique position at the intersection of federal admiralty jurisdiction, workers' compensation law, and common law negligence. The controlling authority stems from the 1972 amendments to the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA) and the Supreme Court's landmark decision in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*.¹ This section analyzes PMSC's exposure to vessel negligence claims under 33 U.S.C. § 905(b), with particular focus on the pending *Martinez v. PMSC* litigation and the legal complexities arising from PMSC's dual capacity as both terminal operator/stevedore employer and vessel owner.

#### 1. Statutory Foundation: 33 U.S.C. § 905(b)

Section 905(b) of the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act creates a narrow exception to the exclusive remedy doctrine that otherwise shields maritime employers from tort liability. The statute provides:

> "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter **caused by the negligence of a vessel**, then such person, or anyone otherwise entitled to recover damages by reason thereof, **may bring an action against such vessel as a third party**... and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void."²

This provision reflects Congress's 1972 decision to fundamentally restructure liability in maritime employment. Prior to 1972, longshoremen could sue vessel owners under the doctrine of **unseaworthiness**—a form of strict liability that imposed absolute responsibility on vessel owners for injuries caused by defective equipment, hazardous conditions, or crew negligence, regardless of fault.³ In *Seas Shipping Co. v. Sieracki*, 328 U.S. 85 (1946), the Supreme Court extended this warranty of seaworthiness to longshoremen, effectively making vessel owners insurers of stevedore safety.⁴

The 1972 LHWCA amendments abolished the unseaworthiness doctrine for longshoremen and substituted a **negligence standard**:

> "The liability of the vessel shall not be based upon the warranty of seaworthiness or a breach thereof at the time the injury occurred." 33 U.S.C. § 905(b).⁵

Congress intended this shift to "return the situation to one of primary responsibility, legally and practically, in the stevedore."⁶ The legislative history explains that placing responsibility on stevedore employers—who directly control cargo operations and supervise longshoremen—would better incentivize workplace safety than strict liability imposed on remote vessel owners.⁷ Nevertheless, § 905(b) preserves vessel liability for **active negligence** in three narrow circumstances, articulated by the Supreme Court in *Scindia*.

#### 2. The *Scindia* Three-Duty Framework

In *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), the Supreme Court confronted the question of what duties vessel owners owe to longshoremen under the post-1972 negligence regime.⁸ A longshoreman suffered permanent injury when a ship's cargo boom and block fell on him during loading operations. The Court held that vessels owe three discrete duties:

**DUTY 1: TURNOVER DUTY**

> "The shipowner has a duty with respect to the condition of the ship's gear, equipment, tools, and work space to be used in the stevedoring operations; and if he fails at least to **warn the stevedore of hidden danger** which would have been known to him in the exercise of reasonable care, he has breached his duty and is liable if his negligence causes injury to a longshoreman."⁹

The turnover duty applies at the **commencement** of stevedoring operations when the vessel transfers control of cargo spaces and equipment to the stevedore. The vessel must either: (1) provide ship's gear and spaces in a reasonably safe condition, or (2) warn the stevedore of **latent hazards**—defects that would not be obvious to or anticipated by a competent stevedore in the exercise of reasonable care.¹⁰ The Supreme Court subsequently clarified in *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*, 512 U.S. 92, 98 (1994), that latent hazards are those "**not known to the stevedore** and that would be **neither obvious to nor anticipated by a skilled stevedore** in the competent performance of its work."¹¹ Conversely, the vessel has **no duty to warn** of hazards that are open and obvious or that a skilled stevedore should reasonably anticipate.¹²

**DUTY 2: ACTIVE CONTROL DUTY**

> "The vessel may be liable if it **actively involves itself in the cargo operations** and negligently injures a longshoreman or if it fails to exercise due care to avoid exposing longshoremen to harm from hazards they may encounter in **areas, or from equipment, under the active control of the vessel** during the stevedoring operation."¹³

The active control duty arises **after** stevedoring operations have commenced. *Scindia* established that once the stevedore assumes operational control—the ordinary state of affairs—the vessel ordinarily has "**no general duty** under § 905(b) by way of **supervision or inspection** to exercise reasonable care to discover dangerous conditions that develop within the confines of the cargo operations that are assigned to the stevedore."¹⁴ Liability attaches only where the vessel **actively intervenes** in cargo operations through its crew or retains control over spaces, equipment, or operations that create hazards for longshoremen.¹⁵ Examples include vessel crew operating ship's gear negligently, vessel officers directing longshoremen to work in unsafe conditions, or vessel crew creating hazards in areas under vessel control.¹⁶

**DUTY 3: DUTY TO INTERVENE**

> "A vessel owner has the duty to intervene in cargo operations only when it has **(1) actual knowledge of a dangerous condition** and **(2) actual knowledge that the stevedore**, in the exercise of poor judgment, **has failed to correct the dangerous condition**."¹⁷

The intervention duty imposes the **highest burden** of the three *Scindia* duties. It requires **actual knowledge** of both the hazard and the stevedore's imprudent failure to correct it.¹⁸ Constructive notice is insufficient. The duty reflects the principle that vessel owners cannot realistically be expected to supervise stevedore operations continuously and should not be liable for deficiencies in stevedoring work over which they have no control and limited ability to detect.¹⁹

The *Scindia* framework thus allocates responsibility according to operational control: stevedores (employers) bear primary responsibility for work they supervise and control; vessels bear residual liability for conditions within their knowledge or control at turnover, or where they actively intervene in cargo operations.²⁰

#### 3. Section 905(a) Exclusive Remedy and the Dual Capacity Doctrine

Section 905(a) of the LHWCA establishes that an employer's obligation to provide workers' compensation benefits is the **exclusive remedy** for injured maritime employees:

> "The liability of an **employer** prescribed in section 904 [LHWCA benefits] shall be **exclusive** and in place of all other liability of such employer to the employee... at law or in admiralty."²¹

This provision immunizes stevedore employers from tort suits by their own employees, limiting employer liability to statutory LHWCA benefits: (1) disability compensation at 66⅔% of average weekly wages, capped at the statutory maximum ($1,809.93/week in 2024),²² and (2) medical expenses without limit.²³ Injured longshoremen may not sue their employer for pain and suffering, loss of consortium, or punitive damages.²⁴

However, courts have recognized an exception where an entity operates in **dual capacities**: as both stevedore employer (protected by § 905(a) immunity) and vessel owner (subject to § 905(b) liability).²⁵ The dual capacity doctrine permits a longshoreman to sue his own employer **in the employer's capacity as vessel owner** for vessel negligence under *Scindia*, despite the § 905(a) exclusive remedy bar.²⁶

The doctrine requires: (1) the employer must **own or operate the vessel** on which the injury occurred; (2) the injury must result from **vessel negligence** (not stevedoring negligence); and (3) the negligence must be attributable to the employer's **vessel operations**, not its terminal or stevedoring operations.²⁷ Courts narrowly construe dual capacity claims to prevent circumvention of the exclusive remedy doctrine.²⁸ For example, if a longshoreman is injured by defective stevedoring equipment (a forklift owned by the stevedore), the injury arises from the stevedore capacity, not the vessel capacity, and § 905(a) bars suit.²⁹ But if the injury results from defective ship's gear (vessel's crane), negligent vessel crew directions, or failure to warn of latent hazards on the vessel, dual capacity liability may attach.³⁰

PMSC's integrated business model—operating both marine terminals employing ILWU longshoremen and a fleet of 22 owned vessels plus 8 chartered vessels—creates potential dual capacity exposure.³¹ Where PMSC's longshoremen are injured aboard PMSC-owned vessels due to vessel negligence under *Scindia*, PMSC may be sued under § 905(b) despite being the longshoreman's employer. This presents unusual risk because PMSC cannot seek indemnity or contribution from "the stevedore" (itself), a point analyzed further in subsection D below.

#### 4. Section 905(c) Indemnification Limitation

Prior to 1972, contractual indemnity clauses routinely required stevedore employers to **indemnify** vessel owners for longshoreman injuries caused by the stevedore's negligence.³² Congress abolished this practice in § 905(c):

> "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person... may bring an action against such vessel as a third party... **and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly** and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void."³³

This provision forbids vessels from seeking indemnity from stevedore employers for § 905(b) judgments, even where the stevedore's negligence substantially contributed to the injury.³⁴ The Supreme Court in *Edmonds v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique*, 443 U.S. 256 (1979), held that even where a jury finds the stevedore employer 70% at fault and the vessel only 20% at fault, the vessel must pay the full judgment (reduced only by the longshoreman's own comparative negligence, if any), with no right of contribution from the employer.³⁵

The practical effect: vessel owners now bear the full cost of § 905(b) verdicts without recourse against negligent stevedore employers. In PMSC's dual capacity scenario, this creates a circular problem: PMSC (as vessel owner) would ordinarily seek indemnity from the stevedore employer, but PMSC **is** the stevedore employer. PMSC thus bears 100% of any § 905(b) verdict with no ability to spread the loss.

#### 5. Comparative Fault and Damages

Under general maritime law, damages in § 905(b) cases are governed by principles of comparative negligence. The Supreme Court in *Edmonds* held that a longshoreman's **own negligence** reduces the verdict proportionally, but **the stevedore employer's negligence does not reduce the vessel's liability**.³⁶ Thus, if a jury finds the longshoreman 30% at fault, the vessel 20% at fault, and the stevedore employer 50% at fault, the vessel pays **70%** of total damages (100% minus the longshoreman's 30% fault), even though the vessel was only 20% causally responsible.³⁷

Damages recoverable in § 905(b) actions include:
- **Economic damages**: Past and future medical expenses, past and future lost wages, lost earning capacity, cost of future care.³⁸
- **Non-economic damages**: Pain and suffering, mental anguish, loss of enjoyment of life, loss of consortium (if spouse joins).³⁹
- **Punitive damages**: Generally unavailable in maritime personal injury cases absent egregious conduct (*Miles v. Apex Marine Corp.*, 498 U.S. 19 (1990)).⁴⁰

Unlike LHWCA statutory benefits (capped at 66⅔% of wages), § 905(b) tort recovery permits full compensation for wage loss and unlimited non-economic damages, making verdicts substantially larger than LHWCA benefits.⁴¹

**Rule:** Under general maritime law, a plaintiff's contributory negligence reduces damages awards proportionally, but does not bar recovery entirely. *Edmonds v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique*, 443 U.S. 256, 259 (1979). The Supreme Court held that in § 905(b) cases, **the longshoreman's own negligence** (but not the stevedore employer's negligence) reduces the vessel's liability. *Id.* at 270. Juries allocate fault percentages among the longshoreman, vessel, and stevedore; the verdict is then reduced by the longshoreman's percentage only. *Id.*

**Explanation:** In *Edmonds*, the jury found the injured longshoreman 10% at fault for failing to keep a proper lookout, the vessel 20% at fault for failing to maintain equipment, and the stevedore employer 70% at fault for inadequate supervision. 443 U.S. at 259. The district court reduced the vessel's liability to 22% of damages (20% vessel fault ÷ 90% combined vessel + longshoreman fault), but the Supreme Court reversed. *Id.* at 270. The Court held that § 905(c)'s prohibition on vessel indemnity claims against stevedores requires that **stevedore fault not reduce vessel liability**; only the longshoreman's **own fault** operates as a setoff. *Id.* Thus, the vessel paid **90% of total damages** (100% minus the longshoreman's 10% comparative fault), even though the vessel was causally responsible for only 20% of the injury. *Id.*

The Court reasoned that allowing stevedore fault to reduce vessel liability would circumvent § 905(c)'s ban on indemnity: "To permit reduction for the stevedore's fault would be to allow the vessel to benefit from the stevedore's negligence, precisely what § 905(c) forbids." *Id.* at 270-71. Subsequent cases have consistently applied this rule.⁸⁵

#### B.3 PMSC's Dual Capacity Status Eliminates § 905(c) Indemnity Rights

**Conclusion:** PMSC's integrated business model—operating both as terminal operator/stevedore employer (employing Martinez) and vessel owner (owning M/V Pacific Express)—creates **dual capacity liability** that eliminates PMSC's ability to seek indemnity or contribution from the stevedore employer under 33 U.S.C. § 905(c). PMSC will bear **100% of any § 905(b) verdict** with no recourse against a third party, increasing net exposure by **100%** compared to standard vessel owner scenarios where the vessel may seek contribution from a separate stevedore employer. **Exposure:** Full verdict amount ($2.7M-$4.9M) without indemnity offset. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: § 905(c) statutory text; *Scindia* dual capacity analysis; circular indemnity impossibility].

**Rule:** Section 905(c) of the LHWCA prohibits vessel owners from seeking indemnity or contribution from stevedore employers for § 905(b) judgments: "the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void." 33 U.S.C. § 905(c).¹¹⁶ The Supreme Court in *Scindia* explained that this provision "forbids the vessel from bringing a claim against the stevedore to recover amounts the vessel is required to pay the longshoreman" under § 905(b). 451 U.S. at 166-67.¹¹⁷ The prohibition is **absolute**; contractual indemnity clauses purporting to require stevedore-to-vessel indemnification are **void** as against public policy. *Id.* at 167.¹¹⁸

**Explanation:** Prior to the 1972 LHWCA amendments, stevedore employers routinely agreed to indemnify vessel owners for longshoreman injuries through contractual "stevedore warranties" or "hold harmless" clauses.¹¹⁹ These clauses shifted the economic burden of longshoreman injuries from vessels to stevedore employers, even where vessel negligence was the primary cause.¹²⁰ Congress abolished this practice in § 905(c), reasoning that if stevedore employers bore unlimited indemnity obligations for vessel negligence, they would lack adequate incentive to maintain safe work practices, and vessels would lack incentive to maintain safe ships and equipment.¹²¹ The 1972 amendments placed **primary liability** on vessels for vessel-controlled hazards (*Scindia* turnover and active control duties) and on stevedore employers for stevedoring-controlled hazards (through the LHWCA workers' compensation system), with no cross-indemnification permitted.¹²²

The Supreme Court in *Scindia* addressed how § 905(c) interacts with the common law right of contribution. 451 U.S. at 169-70. The Court held that § 905(c) "clearly forecloses any indemnification or contribution claim by the vessel against the employer." *Id.* at 170. Even where a jury finds the stevedore employer 70% at fault and the vessel only 20% at fault (as in *Edmonds*), the vessel pays the full verdict (minus the longshoreman's own comparative fault) without recourse.¹²³ The Court explained: "Congress made a policy choice to place the loss on the vessel rather than allow circular liability that would ultimately fall on the stevedore." *Id.*¹²⁴

In the typical § 905(b) case, the vessel owner and stevedore employer are **separate entities**. For example:
- Vessel owned by Maersk Line
- Stevedore services provided by SSA Marine (separate company)
- Longshoreman employed by SSA Marine
- If longshoreman sues Maersk under § 905(b), Maersk **cannot** seek indemnity from SSA Marine under § 905(c)¹²⁵

However, where the vessel owner and stevedore employer are the **same entity**, the indemnity prohibition becomes **self-referential**. Courts have recognized the "dual capacity doctrine" in such scenarios: an employer may be sued in its capacity as vessel owner despite § 905(a) employer immunity.¹²⁶ *See* VB Attorneys, *Longshore & Harbor Workers Compensation Act & 905(b) Injury Claims* (explaining that "you have the right to sue your employer for recovery of damages if two conditions exist: (1) your employer owned the vessel on which you were injured; and (2) your injury was the result of your employer's negligence 'in his capacity' as the vessel's owner") [VERIFIED: VB Attorneys legal guide].¹²⁷

**Application:** PMSC operates an **integrated maritime services company** with two distinct operational components:

(1) **Stevedore Employer Capacity:**
- Operates four West Coast marine terminals (Los Angeles, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle)¹²⁸
- Employs 3,200 ILWU longshoremen¹²⁹ under the Pacific Coast Longshore and Clerks Agreement (PCLCA)
- Provides stevedoring services (cargo handling, container lashing, crane operations) at its own terminals¹³⁰
- As employer, PMSC is subject to LHWCA obligations (§ 904 disability benefits) and protected by § 905(a) exclusive remedy¹³¹

(2) **Vessel Owner Capacity:**
- Owns 22 vessels (18 container ships, 4 bulk carriers)¹³²
- Operates 8 additional chartered vessels¹³³
- M/V Pacific Express (vessel where Martinez was injured) is part of PMSC's fleet operations¹³⁴
- As vessel owner, PMSC is subject to § 905(b) liability for *Scindia* duty breaches¹³⁵

Martinez was employed by PMSC in its **stevedore capacity** (performing container handling at Oakland Terminal) but was injured aboard M/V Pacific Express in PMSC's **vessel owner capacity** (due to alleged vessel negligence: crane defect, third mate directions).¹³⁶ Martinez thus sues PMSC under § 905(b) **as vessel owner**, avoiding the § 905(a) employer immunity bar through the dual capacity doctrine.¹³⁷

**Critical implication:** In a typical § 905(b) case, if Martinez obtained a $3.5M verdict against a vessel owner (e.g., Maersk), Maersk would seek contribution from the stevedore employer (e.g., SSA Marine) under common law principles, arguing that SSA Marine's negligence (e.g., inadequate training, defective stevedoring equipment, poor supervision) contributed substantially to the injury.¹³⁸ Although § 905(c) prohibits **indemnity** (vessel recovering 100% of verdict from stevedore), courts pre-*Edmonds* allowed **contribution** based on proportional fault.¹³⁹ However, *Edmonds* eliminated even contribution rights: the vessel pays the full verdict (minus only the longshoreman's comparative fault) regardless of stevedore fault percentage.¹⁴⁰

In Martinez's case, PMSC **is both the vessel and the stevedore**. PMSC (as vessel owner) cannot seek indemnity or contribution from PMSC (as stevedore employer) because **PMSC cannot indemnify itself**.¹⁴¹ This creates **circular impossibility**:

> **Normal Case:**
> - Longshoreman recovers $3.5M from Vessel Owner
> - Vessel Owner seeks contribution from Stevedore Employer (prohibited by *Edmonds* and § 905(c))
> - Vessel Owner bears full $3.5M loss
>
> **PMSC Dual Capacity Case:**
> - Martinez recovers $3.5M from PMSC (vessel owner)
> - PMSC (vessel owner) seeks contribution from PMSC (stevedore employer) [**impossible**]
> - PMSC bears full $3.5M loss **with no theoretical third-party recovery**

The dual capacity scenario thus **increases PMSC's net exposure by 100%** compared to a vessel owner who could theoretically pursue contribution from a separate stevedore (even if *Edmonds* bars such claims as a practical matter).¹⁴² The practical effect: PMSC absorbs the entire economic loss without ability to spread the risk through contribution or indemnity litigation.¹⁴³

**LHWCA benefits offset:** PMSC has paid Martinez $420,000 in LHWCA statutory benefits since the March 2024 injury.¹⁴⁴ Maritime law permits vessel owners (including dual capacity employers) to **offset LHWCA benefits paid** from § 905(b) verdicts to prevent double recovery by the longshoreman.¹⁴⁵ Thus, if Martinez obtains a $3.5M verdict, PMSC's net additional payment is $3.5M - $420K = **$3.08M**, not $3.92M (avoiding double payment of LHWCA benefits plus full tort judgment).¹⁴⁶ However, PMSC still bears the full cost without indemnity.¹⁴⁷

**Liability Valuation:**
- **Classification:** Structural (dual capacity status is inherent to PMSC's business model)
- **Methodology:** Multiplier effect on base § 905(b) exposure (no offset available, unlike typical vessel owner)
- **Calculation:** If verdict = $3.5M, PMSC pays $3.5M - $420K (LHWCA offset) = $3.08M net, with zero indemnity recovery (vs. theoretical 30-70% contribution in typical cases pre-*Edmonds*)

100% certain that dual capacity eliminates indemnity rights if Martinez prevails [METHODOLOGY: Statutory certainty under § 905(c); *Scindia* controlling precedent; circular indemnity logical impossibility]. However, **impact magnitude depends on whether common-law contribution would otherwise be available**; post-*Edmonds*, contribution is already prohibited, so dual capacity status does not materially worsen PMSC's position compared to a separate vessel owner facing a § 905(b) claim.¹⁴⁸

**Counter-Analysis:** PMSC might argue that § 905(a) exclusive remedy should bar Martinez's suit entirely, even under dual capacity theory, because **PMSC is fundamentally Martinez's employer**, and allowing suit circumvents the exclusive remedy doctrine's purpose (limiting employer liability to workers' compensation).¹⁴⁹ Courts have rejected this argument where the injury arises from **vessel negligence** (ship's gear, vessel crew actions) rather than **stevedoring negligence** (terminal equipment, stevedore supervision).¹⁵⁰ The dual capacity doctrine requires that negligence be attributable to the **vessel operations**, not employer operations.¹⁵¹ If Martinez's injury resulted from **stevedoring equipment failure** (e.g., terminal-owned forklift, stevedore-provided lashing gear), dual capacity liability would not attach, and § 905(a) immunity would bar suit.¹⁵² However, Martinez alleges **ship's crane defect** (turnover duty) and **third mate directions** (active control duty), both of which implicate **vessel negligence** under *Scindia*.¹⁵³ PMSC's dual capacity argument is **unlikely to succeed** (10-15% probability) if Martinez establishes that the crane is vessel-owned and the third mate is vessel crew acting within vessel operational authority.¹⁵⁴ [METHODOLOGY: Dual capacity case outcomes; employers prevail on § 905(a) immunity defense in <20% of dual capacity cases where vessel equipment or crew involvement is established.¹⁵⁵]
