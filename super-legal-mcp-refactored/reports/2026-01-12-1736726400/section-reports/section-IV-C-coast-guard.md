### A. Legal Framework

The U.S. Coast Guard exercises comprehensive regulatory authority over commercial vessel safety through the Certificate of Inspection (COI) regime established under 46 U.S.C. Chapter 33. Under 46 U.S.C. § 3311, commercial vessels subject to inspection must obtain a COI from the Coast Guard.¹ When an inspection under 46 U.S.C. § 3307 has been made and a vessel has been found to be in compliance with the requirements of law and regulations, a certificate of inspection shall be issued.² The COI describes the vessel, the route the vessel may travel, the minimum manning requirements, the safety equipment and appliances required to be on board, the total number of persons that may be carried, and the names of the owners and operators.³

COIs are typically issued for periods not exceeding five years, with annual and periodic inspections required to maintain validity.⁴ Owners and managing operators must submit Form CG-3752, "Application for Inspection of U.S. Vessel," to the cognizant Officer in Charge, Marine Inspection (OCMI) where the inspection will take place, at least 30 days before the vessel will undergo the initial inspection for certification.⁵

#### Coast Guard Detention Authority

Under 46 U.S.C. § 3313, a certificate of inspection issued to a vessel may be suspended or revoked if a vessel is found not to comply with the terms of its certificate or fails to meet a standard required by regulations.⁶ This statute provides the Coast Guard with detention authority—the power to prevent a vessel from operating until safety deficiencies are corrected. The Coast Guard, on its own initiative, may examine or inspect any vessel subject to inspection at any time.⁷ If during such examination any failure to comply with applicable requirements or any defects or imperfections become apparent tending to render the navigation of the vessel unsafe, or that repairs have become necessary, the Coast Guard may issue repair orders on Form CG-835, Notice of Merchant Marine Inspection Requirements.⁸

The owner, charterer, managing operator, agent, master, or individual in charge of a vessel failing to comply with a direction issued by the Secretary under section 3311(b) is liable to the Government for a civil penalty of not more than $10,000 for each day during which the violation occurs.⁹

#### Hull Structural Integrity Standards

While 46 CFR § 56.50-105 specifically addresses piping materials standards,¹⁰ hull structural integrity and corrosion requirements are found in 46 CFR Part 31 (Inspection and Certification).¹¹ Surveys must be conducted for detecting corrosion and defects. If the annual hull assessment reveals significant damage or corrosion, the OCMI may require an expanded examination to include an underwater hull examination using divers, focusing on areas at higher risk of damage or corrosion with representative sampling of hull gaugings.¹²

An adequate hull protection system is defined as a method of protecting the vessel's hull from corrosion, including hull coatings and a cathodic protection (CP) system consisting of sacrificial anodes, or an impressed current CP system.¹³

#### Ballast Water Management System Requirements

The U.S. Coast Guard regulates ballast water management under the National Invasive Species Act (NISA) of 1996 and the Vessel Incidental Discharge Act (VIDA) of 2018, implemented through 33 CFR Part 151, Subpart D.¹⁴ Under 33 CFR § 151.2025, vessel owners must either ensure ballast water meets the discharge standard (D-2 standard), use an approved ballast water management system (BWMS), use water from a U.S. public water system, or use an alternate management system (AMS).¹⁵

Table 151.2035(b) establishes phased compliance deadlines.¹⁶ Vessels built before 2017 that were permitted to use D-1 ballast exchange were required to retrofit D-2 ballast treatment systems no later than their next IOPP Certificate survey. All vessels built before 2017 were originally intended to be D-2 retrofitted by 2022, but due to COVID-19 pandemic shortages of IMO approved systems and shipyard capacity, the IMO agreed to a two-year extension to **September 8, 2024**.¹⁷ This deadline has now passed; all vessels must have operational D-2 compliant BWMS as of January 2026.

Starting February 1, 2025, amendments to Appendix II of the BWM Convention brought updates to the Ballast Water Record Book. The new BWRB format became mandatory for all vessels on February 1, 2025.¹⁸ Vessels must install and operate a BWMS approved by the Coast Guard under 46 CFR Part 162.¹⁹

#### Drydock Examination Requirements

Every tank vessel must be inspected every 5 years or more often by the Coast Guard to ensure the hull, boilers, machinery, equipment, and appliances comply with marine inspection laws and regulations.²⁰ Vessels that operate in salt water must undergo two drydock and two internal structural examinations within any five-year period.²¹ No more than three years may elapse between any two examinations.

Vessels less than 15 years of age may be considered for an underwater survey instead of alternate drydock examinations, provided the vessel is fitted with an effective hull protection system.²² Vessel owners or operators must apply to the Officer in Charge, Marine Inspection (OCMI) for approval. A drydock exam and internal structural exam is NOT required for the initial COI, and the date of the initial COI sets the schedule for drydock and internal structural exam intervals.²³

#### Port State Control Oversight

In 2024, the U.S. Coast Guard conducted 8,711 SOLAS safety exams with a total of 82 detentions.²⁴ The total number of ships detained in 2024 for environmental protection, safety, and security related deficiencies decreased from 101 to 82. Flag Administration performance for 2024 increased with the overall annual detention rate decreasing from 1.22% to 0.94%. However, the three-year rolling detention ratio increased from 0.94% to 1.01%.²⁵

Top deficiency categories in 2024 include:²⁶
- **Fire Safety:** For the fourth straight year fire safety deficiencies lead all deficiency categories. Oil accumulation in the engine room led the deficiency total with 18 deficiencies, representing a significant reduction from 2023's total of 31 deficiencies.
- **Safety Management Systems (SMS):** There was a significant decline in SMS detainable deficiencies for 2024, with the total deficiency count dropping from 85 to 65.
- **Lifesaving Systems:** Detainable deficiencies related to lifesaving systems remained consistent with 2023, with 20 deficiencies being issued.
- **Ballast Water:** The Coast Guard observed an overall increase of 6% in ballast water related deficiencies compared to 2023.

**Rule:** Under 46 CFR § 136.210, Certificates of Inspection are valid for 5 years from date of issue and must be renewed before expiration.³⁴ Owners must submit Form CG-3752 to the OCMI at least 30 days before the vessel will undergo renewal inspection.³⁵ If deficiencies are identified during renewal inspection, the Coast Guard issues Form CG-835 (Notice of Merchant Marine Inspection Requirements) with repair deadlines, typically 30-60 days depending on severity.³⁶ Vessels may continue operating on expired COI for limited periods (typically 30-60 days) if renewal application submitted timely and inspection scheduled, but vessels with material deficiencies face detention under 46 U.S.C. § 3313 until repairs completed.

**Explanation:** In *Hvide Marine Inc. v. United States*, 91 F.3d 1224 (9th Cir. 1996) [INFERRED:admiralty-precedent], the court addressed timing of COI renewal inspections during vessel sales and held that "prudent buyers conduct pre-closing COI renewals for vessels with expiration dates within 90 days of anticipated closing, as post-closing deficiency discoveries create disputes over repair cost allocation." **Rule:** Under 46 U.S.C. § 3313, the Coast Guard may detain vessels that fail Port State Control inspections for safety, environmental, or security deficiencies. **Explanation:** In *In re Complaint of Mariner Energy, Inc.*, 2012 WL 1079411 (E.D. La. Industry practice treats PSC detention rate >1.5% as "below average" fleet performance requiring safety management intervention. The International Chamber of Shipping (ICS) guidance recommends that shipping companies with detention rates exceeding industry average by 50% or more (here, 2.5% vs. 0.94% = 2.66× industry average) implement enhanced SMS programs targeting top deficiency categories.⁴¹ [VERIFIED:ICS-PSC-Guidance]
