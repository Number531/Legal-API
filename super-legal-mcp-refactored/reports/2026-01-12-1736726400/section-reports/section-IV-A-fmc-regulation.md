### A. Legal Framework

The Federal Maritime Commission (FMC) derives its regulatory authority over ocean common carriers from the Shipping Act of 1984, as amended by the Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (Pub. L. 117-146, 136 Stat. 1369), codified at 46 U.S.C. Chapter 401.¹ The Act establishes comprehensive regulation of oceanborne transportation in U.S. foreign commerce, including carrier licensing, tariff and service contract requirements, detention and demurrage practices, and carrier agreements.²

#### 1. Ocean Common Carrier Classification

The Shipping Act defines an "ocean common carrier" as "a vessel-operating common carrier that operates, for all or part of its common carrier service, a liner vessel in foreign commerce."³ This definition distinguishes Vessel-Operating Common Carriers (VOCCs) from Non-Vessel-Operating Common Carriers (NVOCCs). VOCCs operate at least one vessel in the foreign commerce of the United States and are subject to the Shipping Act's requirements regarding tariffs, service contracts, and agreement filing.⁴ NVOCCs, by contrast, function as ocean transportation intermediaries and are subject to separate licensing and bonding requirements under 46 CFR Part 515.⁵

Pacific Maritime Services Corporation (PMSC) operates 22 owned vessels and 8 chartered vessels in liner service between Asia and U.S. West Coast ports.⁶ PMSC is therefore properly classified as a VOCC, not an NVOCC. This classification is critical to determining regulatory requirements for the proposed acquisition by Global Logistics Partners LLC.

#### 2. Change of Ownership Requirements for VOCCs

**Critical Finding:** Unlike NVOCCs, which must file amended Form FMC-18 within 30 days of ownership changes,⁷ VOCCs do **not** require FMC pre-approval for change of ownership transactions. The task delegation referenced "OCC License No. 024587" and cited "46 CFR § 514" for license transfer requirements. This reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of FMC regulatory structure:

- **46 CFR Part 514** (Tariffs and Service Contracts) is an outdated regulation from the 1990s that does not establish, and never established, a "license transfer" approval process for VOCCs.⁸
- **VOCCs are not "licensed"** in the same manner as NVOCCs. VOCCs must comply with operational requirements (tariff publication, service contract filing, agreement filing) but do not obtain a specific "OCC license" from the FMC.⁹
- **No pre-approval requirement exists** for VOCC ownership changes absent government control triggering "controlled carrier" status under 46 CFR § 565.4.¹⁰

The only FMC notification requirement triggered by a change of ownership applies if the transaction results in **government ownership or control** of the carrier. Section 565.4 provides: "Whenever the operation, control or ownership of an ocean common carrier is transferred resulting in a majority portion of the interest of that ocean common carrier being owned or controlled in any manner by a government, the ocean common carrier shall immediately send written notification of the details of the change to the Secretary of the Commission."¹¹ A "controlled carrier" is defined as an ocean common carrier "directly or indirectly owned or controlled by a government," where government ownership exists if a majority interest is owned/controlled by a government, government agency, or government-controlled person, or if the government has the right to appoint or disapprove a majority of directors or the chief executive officer.¹²

Global Logistics Partners LLC is a private equity-backed transportation and logistics investor organized in New York.¹³ The acquisition therefore **does not trigger controlled carrier notification** requirements under 46 CFR Part 565. Even if controlled carrier status applied, notification is **post-closing** ("immediately send written notification" after the change occurs), not a pre-approval requirement.¹⁴

**Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Statutory certainty under 46 U.S.C. Chapter 401 and 46 CFR Parts 515, 565; FMC Industry Advisory on VOCC requirements]

#### 3. Service Contract Framework

Ocean common carriers may enter into service contracts with shippers under 46 U.S.C. § 40502.¹⁵ Each service contract must be filed confidentially with the FMC, and a concise Essential Terms Publication (ETP) containing origin/destination port ranges, commodity descriptions, minimum volume commitments, and service contract duration must be published and made available to the general public in tariff format.¹⁶ PMSC has 850 service contracts filed confidentially with the FMC.¹⁷

The Shipping Act contains **no provision requiring re-filing or FMC approval of service contract assignments** following merger and acquisition transactions. Service contracts remain valid and enforceable post-acquisition, subject to the contract terms themselves regarding assignability.¹⁸

#### 4. Detention and Demurrage Regulation

On May 18, 2020, the FMC issued its Interpretive Rule on Demurrage and Detention Under the Shipping Act (46 CFR Part 545, Docket No. 19-05).¹⁹ The rule addresses whether detention and demurrage (D&D) practices violate 46 U.S.C. § 41102(c), which prohibits ocean common carriers from engaging in unjust or unreasonable practices.²⁰

The FMC's central standard is whether D&D charges serve their "intended primary purposes as financial incentives to promote freight fluidity."²¹ Section 545.5 identifies four key factors for determining unreasonable practices:

1. **Empty Container Return:** Practices imposing detention when empty containers **cannot be returned** due to circumstances beyond the shipper's control are presumptively unreasonable.²²
2. **Cargo Availability:** D&D charges must relate to cargo **availability for retrieval**.²³
3. **Notice of Cargo Availability:** The FMC examines type, timing, format, and recipient of cargo availability notices.²⁴
4. **Policies and Transparency:** D&D policies must be accessible, clear, and include dispute resolution procedures.²⁵

The Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (OSRA), signed June 16, 2022, significantly strengthened FMC enforcement authority. For D&D charges, **carriers bear the burden of proof** to establish reasonableness.²⁶ Previously, complainants bore the burden. Section 41109(a)(1), as amended, authorizes the FMC to "assess a civil penalty" or "in addition to, or in lieu of a civil penalty . . . order a refund of money."²⁷

Effective May 28, 2024, D&D invoices must include: clear container identification, timeframe at issue, detailed billed amounts, point of contact for questions, and a **certified statement** that charges comply with FMC rules and that the carrier's performance did not cause or contribute to the charges.²⁸ Invoices issued after May 28, 2024, that fail to meet these requirements subject carriers to FMC enforcement.²⁹

#### 5. Vessel-Sharing Agreements

Vessel-Sharing Agreements (VSAs) are cooperative arrangements among ocean carriers to share space on cargo vessels along specified trade routes.³⁰ VSAs differ from full alliances in that VSAs are route-specific slot exchanges where each carrier maintains operational independence, while alliances involve global cooperation across multiple routes.³¹ VSAs are filed with the FMC under 46 U.S.C. § 40303 (agreements among ocean common carriers) and become effective 45 days after filing or 30 days after Federal Register publication, whichever is later.³²

**Rule:** Under 46 U.S.C. § 40502(a), "an individual ocean common carrier or an agreement between or among ocean common carriers may enter into a service contract with one or more shippers."⁴² [VERIFIED:https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/46/40502] Each contract must be "filed confidentially with the Federal Maritime Commission."⁴³ While the full contract remains confidential, carriers must publish Essential Terms Publications (ETPs) containing origin/destination port ranges, commodity descriptions, minimum volume commitments, and service contract duration.⁴⁴

The Shipping Act does **not address assignment or novation** of service contracts upon carrier ownership changes.⁴⁵ Therefore, assignability depends on: (1) express contract language (anti-assignment clauses), (2) state contract law governing the agreement, and (3) maritime law principles for contracts of carriage.

**Explanation:** General contract law principles distinguish between assignment (transfer of contract rights/duties to successor without counterparty consent, permitted if contract silent) and novation (three-party agreement creating new contract, releasing original party, requiring counterparty consent).⁴⁶ [INFERRED:Restatement-Second-Contracts-317-328] In the maritime context, courts have generally upheld the assignability of service contracts to successor carriers absent express contractual prohibitions. *See, e.g.*, principles applied in commercial shipping M&A transactions where service contracts transferred automatically to acquiring carriers. [ASSUMED:industry-standard-maritime-M&A-practice]

However, many ocean carrier service contracts include change of control provisions. Typical provisions state: "Carrier may assign this Agreement to any successor-in-interest resulting from merger, acquisition, or reorganization without Shipper consent." [ASSUMED:industry-standard-service-contract-terms] Some contracts, particularly those negotiated by large shippers with market leverage, require shipper consent for assignment or include MFN clauses requiring the carrier to offer the same rates/terms to similarly situated shippers.

**Application:** Here, PMSC has 850 service contracts filed with the FMC.⁴⁷ Without access to the actual service contracts, this analysis assumes **standard commercial terms** permitting assignment to successors. However, several risk factors exist:

#### B.4 Post-Closing D&D Tariff Revision: Strategic Compliance Decision

**Conclusion:** PMSC has the option to revise its D&D tariff post-closing to increase free time from 4 days to 6 days, aligning with industry standards. **Rule:** Section 545.5 of 46 CFR requires D&D charges to serve the "incentive principle" by promoting freight fluidity.⁶⁶ The FMC's Interpretive Rule does not prescribe specific free time periods (e.g., "5 days minimum"), but the agency evaluates whether the free time provided is sufficient to allow shippers a reasonable opportunity to retrieve cargo or return empty containers.⁶⁷ Industry practice during 2020-2024 converged on **5-7 days free time** as a reasonable standard, particularly during port congestion when chassis availability and terminal appointments were constrained.⁶⁸ [ASSUMED:industry-standard-D&D-free-time-5-7-days]

**Explanation:** PMSC's current D&D tariff provides 4 days free time before charges commence at $150/day per TEU (days 5-10), increasing to $250/TEU (days 11-20) and $400/TEU (day 21+).⁶⁹ If PMSC revises its tariff to **6 days free time**, the effect is to eliminate charges for days 5-6, reducing D&D revenue by 33% (assuming linear distribution of charges across days 5-10).

**Application:** Here, PMSC's estimated annual D&D revenue (based on 2M TEU annual volume, 15% containers incurring D&D charges, average 6 days detention at $150/day) is **$27M annually**.⁷⁰ A revised tariff with 6-day free time eliminates charges for days 5-6, resulting in **$9M annual revenue loss** (2M TEU × 15% incidence × 2 days eliminated × $150/day = $9M). Net D&D revenue post-revision: **$18M** (33% reduction).

#### B.5 Vessel-Sharing Alliance (TPSA) Termination Risk Upon Change of Control

**Conclusion:** PMSC participates in the Trans-Pacific Slot Alliance (TPSA) with 3 partner carriers. VSAs typically include change-of-control termination provisions allowing partners to exit if a party undergoes M&A affecting >50% ownership. TPSA termination presents **MEDIUM** risk because loss of slot exchange capacity reduces PMSC's competitive service offerings. **Exposure:** $5M-$10M (20% probability 1-2 partners terminate), $15M-$22M (10% probability all 3 partners terminate); probability-weighted expected value **$3.35M**. **Confidence:** LOW-MEDIUM [BASIS: Industry practice suggests low termination rate for PE financial buyer acquisitions; actual risk depends on TPSA agreement terms not reviewed]

**Rule:** Vessel-Sharing Agreements (VSAs) are cooperative arrangements among ocean carriers to share space on cargo vessels along specified trade routes.⁷² [VERIFIED:https://www.shippingandfreightresource.com/what-is-a-vessel-sharing-agreement-vsa-in-shipping/] VSAs differ from full alliances: VSAs are route-specific slot exchanges where each carrier maintains operational independence, while alliances involve global cooperation across multiple routes.⁷³ [VERIFIED:https://www.eezyimport.com/glossary/vessel-sharing-agreement-vsa/]

VSAs are filed with the FMC under 46 U.S.C. § 40303 (agreements among ocean common carriers) and become effective 45 days after filing or 30 days after Federal Register publication, whichever is later.⁷⁴ [VERIFIED:https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-46/chapter-IV/subchapter-B/part-535] While the actual TPSA agreement is not available for review, industry-standard VSAs include: (1) notice termination (90-180 days' notice without cause), and (2) change of control termination (immediate termination right if a VSA partner undergoes M&A transaction affecting >50% ownership).⁷⁵ [ASSUMED:industry-standard-VSA-change-of-control-termination-provisions]

**Explanation:** The rationale for change-of-control termination rights is that VSA partners may not wish to cooperate with new ownership, particularly if the acquirer is a PE-backed entity with cost-cutting focus or if the acquirer is a strategic competitor. However, PE acquisitions of ocean carriers historically have **lower VSA termination rates** than strategic acquisitions because PE buyers typically maintain operational continuity and do not integrate the target into a competing carrier network. [INFERRED:maritime-M&A-precedent-PE-vs-strategic-VSA-termination-rates]

In precedent transactions, carriers have addressed VSA change-of-control risk through: (1) pre-closing notification to VSA partners emphasizing operational continuity, (2) negotiating amendments to VSA agreement waiving change-of-control termination rights for the specific transaction, and (3) identifying replacement VSA partners or charter vessel capacity if terminations occur. [ASSUMED:VSA-mitigation-strategies-precedent-maritime-M&A]

**Application:** Here, PMSC participates in the Trans-Pacific Slot Alliance (TPSA) with 3 partner carriers.⁷⁶ Typical arrangements include: slot exchange (each carrier provides X slots per sailing on its vessels to partner carriers), revenue allocation (pro-rata based on actual cargo carried), and operational coordination (schedule coordination, terminal sharing, equipment interchange).

If TPSA partners exercise change-of-control termination rights, PMSC loses access to approximately 30-40% additional slot capacity on routes where partners provide vessels. This reduces PMSC's service frequency and increases reliance on PMSC's own 22-vessel fleet, potentially necessitating reduced sailings on affected routes. The estimated revenue impact of losing TPSA slot exchange benefits is **$15M-$22M annually** (based on typical VSA contribution to carrier economics).⁷⁷ [ASSUMED:VSA-revenue-contribution-15M-22M-annually]

**Liability Valuation:**
- **Classification:** Perpetual (ongoing annual revenue reduction if TPSA terminates)
- **Methodology:** Expected Value (probability-weighted scenario analysis) + NPV for perpetual scenarios
- **Calculation:**
  - Scenario 1: 70% probability no TPSA termination (Global Logistics Partners is financial buyer, not strategic competitor) = $0
  - Scenario 2: 20% probability 1-2 partners terminate = $5M-$10M annual revenue loss (mid-point $7.5M; NPV = $7.5M ÷ 0.08 = $93.75M)
  - Scenario 3: 10% probability all 3 partners terminate = $15M-$22M annual revenue loss (mid-point $18.5M; NPV = $18.5M ÷ 0.08 = $231.25M)
  - Expected Value: (0.70 × $0) + (0.20 × $7.5M) + (0.10 × $18.5M) = **$3.35M annual expected exposure**
  - NPV of Expected Exposure: $3.35M ÷ 0.08 = **$41.9M**
- **Result:** $3.35M annual expected value; $41.9M NPV at 8% discount rate
- **Discount Rate Basis:** 8% WACC [ASSUMED:PE-acquirer-target-WACC-8pct]

**Probability Assessment:**
70% probability no termination, 20% probability partial termination (1-2 partners), 10% probability full termination (all 3 partners) [METHODOLOGY: Expert judgment based on industry practice for PE financial buyer acquisitions; assumes standard VSA change-of-control provisions; actual probability depends on TPSA agreement terms and partner relationships]

**Counter-Analysis:** PMSC may argue that TPSA termination risk is overstated because VSA partners have strong economic incentives to maintain cooperation: terminating TPSA reduces their own slot capacity on trans-Pacific routes where PMSC provides vessels. This argument has merit, particularly if TPSA is a balanced slot exchange where each carrier contributes comparable capacity. However, if TPSA is imbalanced (e.g., PMSC receives more slots from partners than it provides), partners may view termination as cost-saving.

Global Logistics Partners may argue that the 70% no-termination probability is conservative, citing precedent PE acquisitions of ocean carriers where VSAs continued post-closing. For example, [precedent transaction examples if available]. [ASSUMED:precedent-maritime-M&A-VSA-continuity] However, without access to the actual TPSA agreement, uncertainty regarding termination notice periods and partner consent requirements supports the assigned probabilities.
