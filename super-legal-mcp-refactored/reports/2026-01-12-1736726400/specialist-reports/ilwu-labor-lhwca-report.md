**5. Successor Employer Obligations Under Federal Labor Law**

**John Wiley & Sons Doctrine:**
The Supreme Court in *John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. Livingston*, 376 U.S. 543 (1964), established that collective bargaining agreements survive corporate ownership changes:

> "The disappearance by merger of a corporate employer which has entered into a collective bargaining agreement with a union does not automatically terminate all rights of the employees covered by the agreement, and, in appropriate circumstances, the successor employer may be required to arbitrate with the union under the agreement."

**Application:**
- Global Logistics Partners (successor employer) **automatically inherits PCLCA obligations** through July 1, 2028
- NLRA ¬ß 8(a)(5) requires good faith bargaining with ILWU upon contract expiration (July 1, 2028)
- No unilateral changes to wages, hours, or working conditions permitted during contract term
- PMA membership must transfer to Global Logistics Partners within 30 days post-closing

**Transaction Structure Impact:**
- **Stock purchase** (research plan indicates this structure): Successor liability automatic and certain
- **Asset purchase**: Successor liability depends on "substantial continuity" test (same business, same workforce); risk of withdrawal liability if PMSC exits PMA

**Recommendation**: Stock purchase structure strongly preferred to avoid pension withdrawal liability and ensure seamless labor relations continuity.

**6. LHWCA Exclusive Remedy Protects PMSC from Tort Suits**

The Longshoremen's and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (33 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 901-950) provides federal workers' compensation for maritime employees including longshoremen.

**Section 905(a) Exclusive Remedy:**
> "The liability of an employer prescribed in section 904 [disability benefits] shall be exclusive and in place of all other liability of such employer to the employee... at law or in admiralty."

**Effect:**
- Injured longshoremen **cannot sue PMSC** (their employer) in tort
- PMSC's liability limited to **statutory LHWCA benefits** (66‚Öî% of wages + medical expenses)
- No exposure to pain/suffering damages, loss of consortium, or punitive damages against PMSC

**PMSC's LHWCA Exposure:**
- 285 claims annually [VERIFIED: Research Plan]
- $32M annual benefit payments
- $180M actuarial reserve for open claims (5.6 years of coverage at current rate)

**Section 905(b) Vessel Negligence Exception:**
33 U.S.C. ¬ß 905(b) creates exception allowing injured longshoremen to sue **vessel owners** (not PMSC) for vessel negligence. This creates indirect exposure for PMSC through **indemnity claims** under ¬ß 905(c) if PMSC's negligence (e.g., inadequate training, defective equipment) contributed to longshoreman's injury.

**Scindia Three-Duty Framework:**
*Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981) established vessel owners owe three narrow duties to longshoremen:
1. **Turnover duty**: Provide ship's gear/equipment in safe condition, warn of hidden dangers
2. **Active control duty**: Exercise reasonable care when vessel actively controls cargo operations
3. **Duty to intervene**: Intervene when vessel knows of unreasonably dangerous condition stevedore imprudently ignores

**PMSC Indemnity Risk:**
If longshoreman sues vessel owner under ¬ß 905(b) and prevails, vessel may seek **indemnity from PMSC** under ¬ß 905(c) if PMSC's negligence caused injury. Martinez v. PMSC case (research plan fact pattern: $8.5M claimed, trial Q3 2025, expected verdict $2M-$5M) illustrates this exposure.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Legal Framework for Labor Relations

#### 1. National Labor Relations Act (NLRA)

The National Labor Relations Act of 1935, codified at 29 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 151-169, establishes the fundamental right of employees to organize and bargain collectively. Key provisions relevant to PMSC's acquisition:

- **29 U.S.C. ¬ß 158(a)(5)**: Requires employers to bargain in good faith with representatives of their employees [VERIFIED: Cornell LII]
- **29 U.S.C. ¬ß 159**: Establishes exclusive representation rights once a union is certified
- **Successor employer doctrine**: Change of ownership does not automatically terminate union representation or collective bargaining obligations

The NLRA protects the right of employees to engage in "concerted activities" including strikes, subject to certain limitations under the Labor Management Relations Act.¬π

#### 2. Labor Management Relations Act (LMRA) - Taft-Hartley Act

The Labor Management Relations Act of 1947, 29 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 141-197, amended the NLRA and imposes additional restrictions on union activities:

- **29 U.S.C. ¬ß 176**: Authorizes the President to seek an 80-day cooling-off period injunction in national emergency disputes
- **29 U.S.C. ¬ß 185 (Section 301)**: Provides federal court jurisdiction over labor contract disputes
- **29 U.S.C. ¬ß 158(d)**: Requires 60-day notice before strike or contract modification

The Taft-Hartley Act was invoked during the 2002 West Coast port lockout, when President George W. Bush obtained an 80-day injunction requiring longshoremen to return to work.¬≤

#### 3. Norris-LaGuardia Act

The Norris-LaGuardia Act of 1932, 29 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 101-115, limits federal court authority to issue injunctions in labor disputes:

- **29 U.S.C. ¬ß 104**: Restricts injunctions against strikes except in narrow circumstances
- **29 U.S.C. ¬ß 107**: Establishes procedural requirements for labor injunctions

This Act generally prevents employers from obtaining injunctions to stop strikes, except for the Taft-Hartley "national emergency" exception.¬≥

### D. Successor Employer Obligations Under Federal Labor Law

#### 1. John Wiley & Sons Doctrine - CBA Survival

The Supreme Court established in *John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. Livingston*, 376 U.S. 543 (1964), that collective bargaining agreements may survive changes in corporate ownership:

**Holding:**
"The disappearance by merger of a corporate employer which has entered into a collective bargaining agreement with a union does not automatically terminate all rights of the employees covered by the agreement, and, in appropriate circumstances, the successor employer may be required to arbitrate with the union under the agreement."¬≥‚Åµ

**Application to PMSC Acquisition:**
- Global Logistics Partners LLC acquires PMSC (stock purchase structure assumed)
- PCLCA does not terminate upon ownership change
- Successor employer (Global Logistics Partners) inherits PMA membership obligations
- ILWU representation rights continue uninterrupted
- All PCLCA terms remain in effect through July 1, 2028 expiration

**PMA Notification Requirements:**
- PMA will notify ILWU of ownership change within 30 days post-closing (standard PMA procedure)
- Global Logistics Partners must execute PMA membership agreement
- No re-negotiation of PCLCA terms (locked in through 2028)

**NLRA Section 8(a)(5) Bargaining Obligation:**
Under 29 U.S.C. ¬ß 158(a)(5), successor employers must:
1. Recognize the union as exclusive bargaining representative
2. Bargain in good faith upon contract expiration
3. Refrain from unilateral changes to wages, hours, or working conditions during contract term¬≥‚Å∂

**Transaction Structure Impact:**
- **Stock purchase**: Successor liability clear (Global Logistics Partners steps into PMSC's shoes)
- **Asset purchase**: More complex; successor liability depends on "substantial continuity" test (same business, same workforce, same supervisors)¬≥‚Å∑

Given research plan indicates "stock purchase/merger," successor liability for PCLCA obligations is **certain**.

#### 2. ERISA Multiemployer Pension Plan Withdrawal Liability

**Statutory Framework:**
The Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) ¬ß 4201, codified at 29 U.S.C. ¬ß 1381, imposes withdrawal liability on employers that withdraw from multiemployer defined benefit pension plans:

> "If an employer withdraws from a multiemployer plan... the employer is liable to the plan in the amount determined under this part to be the withdrawal liability."¬≥‚Å∏

**ILWU-PMA Pension Plan Status:**
- Multiemployer defined benefit plan established pursuant to collective bargaining¬≥‚Åπ
- Governed by special withdrawal liability rules approved by PBGC on January 30, 1984 under ERISA ¬ß 4203(f)‚Å¥‚Å∞
- Plan has operated under modified withdrawal liability rules since 1984 due to longshore industry characteristics‚Å¥¬π
- Plan status: **Fully funded since 2019** (per PMA disclosures)‚Å¥¬≤

**Withdrawal Liability Trigger Events:**
- **Complete withdrawal**: Employer ceases all covered operations under the plan (exits PMA entirely)
- **Partial withdrawal**: 70% or greater reduction in contribution base units‚Å¥¬≥
- **Asset sale**: If structured as asset purchase (rather than stock purchase), seller may trigger withdrawal

**Calculation of Withdrawal Liability:**
Withdrawal liability equals the employer's proportionate share of the plan's unfunded vested benefits:

Withdrawal Liability = (Unfunded Vested Benefits) √ó (Employer's Contribution Base Units √∑ Total Contribution Base Units)

### E. Longshoremen's and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA) Framework

**CRITICAL: This section provides the legal foundation for T7 case-law-analyst's Section 905(b) vessel negligence analysis of Martinez v. PMSC.**

#### 1. LHWCA Statutory Overview

The Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act, enacted in 1927 and codified at 33 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 901-950, provides a federal workers' compensation system for certain maritime workers not covered by the Jones Act.‚Åµ‚Å∞

**Coverage (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 902(3)):**
- "Employee" includes any person engaged in maritime employment
- Maritime employment includes longshore workers, stevedores, ship repairers, and harbor workers
- Geographic requirement: Injury occurs on navigable waters of the United States OR on adjoining piers, wharves, terminals, or other areas customarily used in loading/unloading vessels‚Åµ¬π

**PMSC's LHWCA Exposure:**
- 3,200 ILWU longshoremen employed at four West Coast terminals [VERIFIED: Research Plan]
- All terminal work meets LHWCA coverage requirements (container handling on wharves/terminals adjacent to navigable waters)
- Average 285 LHWCA claims annually (per research plan fact pattern)
- Annual LHWCA benefit payments: $32M
- Actuarial reserve for open claims: $180M‚Åµ¬≤

#### 2. LHWCA Benefits Structure vs. Tort Damages

**LHWCA Statutory Benefits (33 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 907-909):**

**Disability Compensation (¬ß 908):**
- Temporary total disability: 66‚Öî% of average weekly wages (subject to statutory maximum)
- Permanent total disability: 66‚Öî% of average weekly wages for life
- Permanent partial disability: Scheduled benefits based on body part (e.g., arm loss = 312 weeks compensation)‚Åµ¬≥
- 2024 maximum weekly compensation: $1,809.93 (adjusted annually for national average weekly wage)‚Åµ‚Å¥

**Medical Benefits (¬ß 907):**
- All reasonable and necessary medical expenses
- Unlimited duration if causally related to injury
- Employer selects initial treating physician; employee may change after 30 days‚Åµ‚Åµ

**Death Benefits (¬ß 909):**
- Surviving spouse: 50% of deceased's average weekly wages
- Each dependent child: 16‚Öî% (up to maximum 66‚Öî% total for widow + children)
- Burial expenses: $3,000 maximum‚Åµ‚Å∂

**Contrast with Tort Damages:**

| Category | LHWCA Benefits | Tort Damages (¬ß 905(b) Vessel Suit) |
|----------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Wage Loss** | 66‚Öî% of wages (capped) | 100% of lost wages (uncapped) |
| **Medical Expenses** | 100% reasonable/necessary | 100% past and future medical |
| **Pain & Suffering** | **NOT AVAILABLE** | **Unlimited compensatory damages** |
| **Loss of Consortium** | **NOT AVAILABLE** | **Available to spouse** |
| **Punitive Damages** | **NOT AVAILABLE** | **Available if gross negligence** |
| **Attorney Fees** | 20% of benefits (if contested) | 33-40% contingency fee standard |

**Example Comparison (Catastrophic Injury):**
- **LHWCA benefits**: $1,809.93/week √ó 52 weeks √ó 30 years (life expectancy) = $2.82M + medical expenses
- **Tort verdict (¬ß 905(b) case)**: $5M past/future medical + $10M lost wages + $15M pain/suffering + $5M loss of consortium = **$35M total** (before comparative fault reduction)

This disparity creates strong incentive for injured longshoremen to pursue ¬ß 905(b) vessel negligence claims against vessel owners rather than accepting LHWCA benefits alone.

#### 3. LHWCA Exclusive Remedy Doctrine (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 905(a))

**Statutory Text:**
> "The liability of an employer prescribed in section 904 of this title [disability benefits] shall be exclusive and in place of all other liability of such employer to the employee... **at law or in admiralty** on account of such injury or death..."‚Åµ‚Å∑

**Exclusive Remedy Effect:**
- Injured longshoremen **cannot sue their employer** (terminal operator) in tort
- LHWCA benefits are the **sole remedy** against the employer
- Exception: Intentional torts (not applicable to negligence)‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Application to PMSC:**
- PMSC, as terminal operator and ILWU employer, is **immune from tort suits** by injured longshoremen
- Martinez (injured longshoreman in research plan fact pattern) **cannot sue PMSC** for negligence
- PMSC's liability limited to LHWCA statutory benefits ($420K paid to Martinez per fact pattern)

**Policy Rationale:**
Exclusive remedy rule balances:
1. **Employee benefit**: Guaranteed compensation without proving fault (strict liability)
2. **Employer benefit**: Immunity from unlimited tort damages‚Åµ‚Åπ

#### 4. Section 905(b) Vessel Negligence Exception

**Statutory Text (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 905(b)):**
> "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter **caused by the negligence of a vessel**, then such person... **may bring an action against such vessel** as a third party... and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void."‚Å∂‚Å∞

**Critical Distinction:**
- ¬ß 905(a) protects **terminal operators/employers** (like PMSC) from tort suits
- ¬ß 905(b) allows suits against **vessel owners/operators** for vessel negligence
- "Vessel" = ship engaged in navigation, not fixed structures like piers‚Å∂¬π

**1972 Amendment - Legislative History:**
Prior to 1972, longshoremen could sue vessel owners under **warranty of seaworthiness** (strict liability). *Seas Shipping Co. v. Sieracki*, 328 U.S. 85 (1946) established this doctrine.‚Å∂¬≤

The 1972 LHWCA amendments **overruled Sieracki**, requiring injured longshoremen to prove **active negligence** rather than strict liability:

> "The liability of the vessel shall not be based upon the warranty of seaworthiness or a breach thereof at the time the injury occurred."‚Å∂¬≥

**Effect:** Section 905(b) now requires **negligence**, not seaworthiness breach.

#### 5. Scindia Three-Duty Framework for Vessel Negligence

The Supreme Court in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), established that vessel owners owe three **narrow duties** to longshoremen under ¬ß 905(b):‚Å∂‚Å¥

**Duty 1: Turnover Duty**
- Vessel must provide ship's gear, equipment, and work spaces **in safe condition** at commencement of stevedoring operations
- Duty to **warn of hidden dangers** known to vessel owner (or should be known with reasonable care)
- Breach: Defective lashing gear, broken ladder, corroded deck plates **not disclosed** to stevedore‚Å∂‚Åµ

**Example:**
> "If a ship's winch is defective and the vessel owner fails to warn the stevedore, and a longshoreman is injured when the winch fails, vessel owner liable under turnover duty."

**Duty 2: Active Control Duty**
- Vessel liable if it **actively involves itself** in cargo operations and negligently injures longshoreman
- Applies when vessel crew **exercises control** over stevedoring work (normally stevedore controls operations)
- Breach: Vessel crew operates crane negligently, vessel directs longshoremen to use unsafe method‚Å∂‚Å∂

**Example:**
> "If vessel's chief mate orders longshoremen to work in hold during heavy weather, and injury results, vessel liable under active control duty."

**Duty 3: Duty to Intervene**
- Vessel must **intervene** when it:
  1. **Knows** of unreasonably dangerous condition developed during operations, AND
  2. **Knows** stevedore imprudently intends to continue operations despite danger, AND
  3. Stevedore cannot be relied upon to protect its workers‚Å∂‚Å∑

**Example:**
> "If vessel officer observes longshoremen working under improperly secured container that is visibly unstable, and fails to stop work, vessel liable under duty to intervene."

**Scindia's Narrow Scope:**
The Court emphasized that vessel negligence liability is **limited** to these three duties. Vessel is **not liable** for:
- Stevedore's negligence in conducting operations
- Obvious hazards apparent to longshoremen
- Conditions entirely within stevedore's control‚Å∂‚Å∏

**Burden of Proof:**
Plaintiff longshoreman must prove:
1. Vessel owed one of three *Scindia* duties
2. Vessel breached that duty
3. Breach proximately caused injury‚Å∂‚Åπ

#### 6. Comparative Fault and Damages Allocation

**Comparative Negligence (General Maritime Law):**
Even if vessel is negligent under ¬ß 905(b), damages are reduced by plaintiff's comparative fault percentage.‚Å∑‚Å∞

**Example (Martinez Case from Research Plan):**
- Total damages: $8.5M claimed
- Jury finds vessel 60% at fault (failed to maintain lashing gear - turnover duty breach)
- Jury finds Martinez 40% at fault (failed to inspect gear before use)
- Verdict: $8.5M √ó 60% = **$5.1M vessel liability**

**LHWCA Benefits Offset:**
- Martinez already received $420K LHWCA benefits from PMSC
- Vessel owner entitled to **credit** for LHWCA benefits (no double recovery)‚Å∑¬π
- Net recovery from vessel: $5.1M - $420K = $4.68M

**PMSC Indemnity Claim (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 905(c)):**
If vessel is found liable under ¬ß 905(b), vessel may seek **indemnity** from stevedore (PMSC) if stevedore's negligence caused injury:
- Vessel pays injured longshoreman tort judgment
- Vessel sues PMSC for indemnity under ¬ß 905(c)
- PMSC liable if its negligence (e.g., inadequate supervision) caused injury‚Å∑¬≤

**Three-Party Liability Triangle:**
1. **Longshoreman** sues **vessel** under ¬ß 905(b) (tort damages)
2. **Longshoreman** receives LHWCA benefits from **employer** (PMSC) - mandatory
3. **Vessel** sues **employer** (PMSC) for indemnity under ¬ß 905(c) - if employer negligence caused injury

This creates complex litigation with PMSC potentially liable for indemnity even though longshoreman cannot sue PMSC directly.

#### 7. LHWCA Claims Administration

**Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP):**
- U.S. Department of Labor administers LHWCA claims
- Regulations: 20 C.F.R. Part 702‚Å∑¬≥
- Administrative Law Judges (ALJs) adjudicate disputed claims
- Appeals to Benefits Review Board, then to federal courts (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 921)

**Claim Process:**
1. Employer receives notice of injury (Form LS-201)
2. Employer/insurance carrier pays or controverts claim (Form LS-207)
3. If disputed, claimant requests informal conference or formal hearing
4. ALJ hearing and decision
5. Benefits Review Board review
6. U.S. Court of Appeals (if substantial evidence standard not met)‚Å∑‚Å¥

**PMSC Claims Volume:**
- 285 claims annually (per research plan)
- $32M annual benefit payments
- $180M actuarial reserve for open claims
- Claims administration costs: Estimated $3-5M annually (adjusters, medical reviews, ALJ hearings)

**Insurance Coverage:**
Employers must secure LHWCA coverage through:
- Private insurance carrier, OR
- Self-insurance (requires OWCP approval and financial guarantees)‚Å∑‚Åµ

PMSC likely self-insured or uses large deductible policy given size of operation.
