## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Legal Framework for Port Terminal Leases

#### 1. California Harbors and Navigation Code

Port terminal leases in California are governed by the California Harbors and Navigation Code (H&NC), which establishes the legal framework for port authorities operating as public entities.¬≤ The Code defines "harbor agency" as any state or local agency, city, county, city and county, harbor district, port district, harbor improvement district, river port district, small craft harbor district, or other public district, entity, board, commission, or agency that operates a port or harbor in California.¬≥

**Key Statutory Provisions:**

- **Public Trust Doctrine**: California port authorities hold tidelands in trust for public purposes, including maritime commerce.‚Å¥ This public trust status constrains port authorities' discretion in lease negotiations‚Äîthey must ensure that lease terms serve the public interest and generate fair market value returns for publicly-owned port assets.

- **Port Commission Authority**: Port commissions have statutory authority to lease port facilities to private terminal operators.‚Åµ California H&NC Division 8 (Harbor and Port Districts, ¬ß¬ß 5800-7340) grants port commissions broad powers to "lease or rent wharves, warehouses, and other structures" for terms not exceeding 50 years.‚Å∂

- **Fair Market Value Requirements**: While California law does not mandate specific rent formulas, port authorities must comply with general principles of municipal finance requiring that public assets be leased at fair market value.‚Å∑ This requirement protects against claims that port commissioners are "giving away" public assets at below-market rates.

**Judicial Interpretation:**
California courts have held that port authorities, as public entities managing state tidelands, owe fiduciary duties to maximize public benefits while ensuring maritime commerce remains economically viable.‚Å∏ This creates tension in lease negotiations: ports must extract maximum rent without rendering terminals economically unviable.

#### 2. Port Authority Lease Structures

West Coast port terminal leases typically follow a standard structure:

**Rent Components:**
- **Base Rent (per acre)**: Fixed annual payment based on terminal acreage, escalating annually by CPI or fixed percentage
- **Percentage Rent (revenue share)**: Additional rent calculated as percentage of gross terminal revenues above threshold amount
- **Capital Investment Requirements**: Tenant-funded infrastructure improvements (cranes, container handling equipment, rail spurs, paving, utilities)

**Standard Lease Terms:**
- **Initial Term**: 15-40 years (longer terms justified by larger capital investments)
- **Renewal Options**: 1-2 renewal options of 10-20 years each, typically at "fair market value" rent to be negotiated
- **Reversion of Improvements**: Upon lease termination, tenant-funded improvements (cranes, rail, paving) revert to port without compensation‚Åπ
- **Assignment/Change of Control**: Requires port authority consent; ports retain discretion to approve or deny tenant changes¬π‚Å∞

#### 3. Terminal Operating Agreements (TOAs) with Ocean Carriers

In addition to port lease agreements, terminal operators like PMSC enter into Terminal Operating Agreements (TOAs) with ocean carriers (shipping lines) that commit cargo volumes to the terminal.¬π‚Å¥ These TOAs create a dual-layer contractual structure:

**TOA Key Provisions:**
- **Minimum Volume Commitments (MVCs)**: Carriers commit to deliver minimum annual TEU volumes; terminal commits to service level guarantees (vessel turn times, gate hours, equipment availability)
- **Terminal Handling Charges (THC)**: Fees charged to carriers for container lifts (ship-to-shore crane moves), gate transactions, storage/demurrage
- **Assignment and Change of Control**: TOAs typically require carrier consent for terminal operator changes, particularly for vessel-sharing alliance partners¬π‚Åµ

**M&A Implications:**
If PMSC's ownership changes (acquisition by Global Logistics Partners LLC), ocean carrier counterparties to TOAs may have contractual rights to:
1. Consent to the assignment of the TOA to the new owner
2. Renegotiate THC rates or service levels
3. Terminate the TOA and shift cargo to competing terminals

**Federal Regulatory Overlay:**
TOAs between terminal operators and ocean common carriers are subject to Federal Maritime Commission (FMC) oversight under the Shipping Act of 1984.¬π‚Å∂ However, FMC regulations specifically exempt certain terminal arrangements from the definition of "agreements" requiring FMC filing, including arrangements that convey rights to operate terminal facilities "by means of a lease, license, permit, assignment, land rental, or similar other arrangement for the use of marine terminal facilities."¬π‚Å∑

### B. Oakland Terminal Lease - Detailed Terms and Renewal Negotiation

#### 1. Current Lease Terms (2007-2026)

**Lease Parties:**
- **Lessor**: Port of Oakland (California public entity, tidelands trustee)
- **Lessee**: PMSC Terminal Operations LLC (PMSC subsidiary)

**Lease Premises:**
- **Location**: 50-acre container terminal, Berths 55-58, Outer Harbor, Oakland, California
- **Improvements**: PMSC has invested $180M in capital infrastructure:
  - 8 ship-to-shore (STS) gantry cranes (4 Super Post-Panamax, 4 Panamax)¬≤‚Å¥
  - Automated container stacking system (42 automated stacking cranes)
  - 60,000 TEU on-terminal container storage capacity
  - 12-acre refrigerated ("reefer") container yard with 850 plug-in positions
  - Rail spur connecting to Union Pacific mainline (8 working tracks)
  - Terminal operating system (TOS) IT infrastructure

**Lease Term:**
- **Original term**: 20 years (executed January 1, 2007; expires December 31, 2026)
- **Renewal option**: 1 √ó 10-year renewal option (exercisable by December 31, 2025‚Äî12 months before expiration)
- **Renewal rent**: Subject to negotiation; lease requires Port to offer "fair market value" rent

**Current Rent Structure:**
- **Base rent**: $28M/year ($560,000/acre/year)
- **Percentage rent**: 2% of gross terminal revenues exceeding $200M/year
  - FY2024 revenues: $225M
  - Percentage rent: ($225M - $200M) √ó 2% = $500,000
  - **Total FY2024 rent**: $28.5M

**Capital Investment Reversion:**
The lease provides that upon termination or non-renewal, all tenant-funded improvements‚Äîincluding cranes, stacking equipment, rail infrastructure, and paving‚Äîrevert to the Port of Oakland without compensation to PMSC.¬≤‚Åµ This standard provision creates significant sunk-cost leverage for the Port in renewal negotiations, as PMSC's $180M capital investment is effectively non-portable.

#### 2. Port of Oakland Renewal Proposal (September 2024)

The Port of Oakland submitted its renewal proposal on September 15, 2024:

**Proposed Rent:**
- **Base rent**: $42M/year ($840,000/acre/year)‚Äîa 50% increase from current $28M
- **Percentage rent**: 3% of gross revenues exceeding $200M (increased from 2%)
  - At FY2024 revenues ($225M): ($225M - $200M) √ó 3% = $750,000
  - **Total proposed rent**: $42.75M/year

**Capital Investment Requirement:**
- Port requires PMSC to invest $25M in terminal electrification infrastructure within 5 years:
  - Shore power (alternative marine power/AMP) for vessel cold-ironing (3 berths)
  - Electric container handling equipment (CHE) replacement (42 diesel RTGs ‚Üí battery-electric)
  - Electric truck charging infrastructure (50 Level 3 DC fast chargers)

**Lease Term:**
- 10-year renewal (2027-2036)
- No additional renewal option (terminal reverts to Port in 2036)

**Port's Justification:**
1. **Comparable terminal rents**: Port of Los Angeles and Port of Long Beach terminals average $800K-$900K/acre in base rent¬≤‚Å∂
2. **Rent stagnation**: Oakland has not increased PMSC's base rent since 2015 (10+ years without adjustment)
3. **Infrastructure investment**: Port claims it has deferred $120M in wharf/berth maintenance to accommodate PMSC's low rent
4. **Environmental compliance**: Shore power and electrification requirements align with California Air Resources Board (CARB) mandates for 2030 zero-emission targets

#### 3. PMSC Counteroffer (October 2024)

PMSC submitted its counteroffer on October 20, 2024:

**Proposed Rent:**
- **Base rent**: $33M/year ($660,000/acre/year)‚Äîan 18% increase from current $28M
- **Percentage rent**: Maintain 2% of gross revenues exceeding $200M
  - At FY2024 revenues: $500,000 (unchanged)
  - **Total proposed rent**: $33.5M/year

**PMSC's Justification for $33M:**
- **Inflation adjustment**: $28M base rent in 2015, adjusted for 10 years of cumulative inflation (CPI-U 2015-2024: ~18%), yields $33M
- **Below LA/Long Beach comparables**: PMSC acknowledges Oakland rent is below LA/Long Beach, but notes:
  - Oakland terminal has lower productivity (25 container moves/hour vs. 30+ moves/hour at LA/Long Beach automated terminals)
  - Oakland serves different cargo mix (40% agricultural exports vs. LA/Long Beach 90% manufactured imports)‚Äîagricultural exports generate lower revenue per TEU
  - Oakland lacks deep-water access for ultra-large container vessels (ULCVs)‚ÄîOakland draft is 50 feet vs. LA/Long Beach 53-55 feet

**Capital Investment Counteroffer:**
- PMSC offers $15M terminal electrification investment (vs. Port's $25M demand):
  - Shore power for 2 berths (vs. Port's 3 berths)
  - Electric CHE replacement phased over 10 years (vs. Port's 5 years)
  - Truck charging infrastructure contingent on truck fleet electrification adoption rates

**Lease Term Counteroffer:**
- 15-year renewal (2027-2041) with 1 √ó 10-year renewal option (potential extension to 2051)
- Longer term justified by capital investment amortization (electrification payback period 12-15 years)¬≤‚Å∑

#### 4. Port Response and Competitive Pressure (November 2024)

On November 8, 2024, the Port of Oakland formally rejected PMSC's counteroffer and disclosed significant competitive pressure:

**Port's Position:**
- **Rent demand unchanged**: Port reiterated $42M/year base rent requirement
- **Competitive bids**: Port disclosed that Mediterranean Shipping Company (MSC)‚Äîthe world's largest container carrier‚Äîsubmitted an unsolicited proposal at $44M/year for the same 50-acre terminal¬≤‚Å∏
- **RFP threat**: Port stated that if PMSC does not accept $42M by **March 31, 2025**, the Port will issue a formal Request for Proposals (RFP) to solicit competitive bids from other terminal operators, including MSC and Evergreen Marine

**Port's Leverage Points:**
1. **Alternative tenants**: MSC's $44M unsolicited bid demonstrates market demand exceeds PMSC's $33M offer
2. **Capital investment reversion**: PMSC's $180M invested capital reverts to Port if lease terminates‚ÄîPort can offer this infrastructure to MSC without requiring MSC to replicate the investment
3. **Political support**: Oakland Port Commission is under pressure from City of Oakland to maximize port revenues to fund infrastructure repairs (Port's operating budget faces $85M deficit through 2028)

**PMSC's Counterleverages:**
1. **Sunk capital investment**: $180M invested, non-portable‚Äîwalking away results in total loss
2. **Cargo base loyalty**: PMSC's integrated shipping/terminal model brings PMSC-owned vessel cargo to Oakland terminal (180,000 TEU/year, 40% of terminal volume)‚Äîif PMSC exits, this cargo shifts to LA/Long Beach
3. **Agricultural export specialization**: Oakland terminal's reefer capacity and agricultural export expertise serve California Central Valley (almonds, walnuts, hay, meat, dairy)¬≤‚Åπ‚ÄîMSC is primarily an import-focused carrier and may not maintain Oakland's agricultural export franchise
4. **ILWU labor continuity**: PMSC has strong relationship with International Longshore and Warehouse Union (ILWU) Local 10 (Oakland longshoremen)‚Äîterminal operator change risks labor disputes

#### 5. Strategic Significance of Oakland Terminal to PMSC

**Cargo Profile:**
Oakland terminal handles a unique cargo mix compared to PMSC's other West Coast terminals:

| Cargo Type | Oakland % | LA/Long Beach/Seattle % |
|------------|-----------|------------------------|
| Agricultural exports (reefer) | 40% | 8-12% |
| Manufactured imports | 35% | 70-80% |

**Key Observation:** Oakland's 40% agricultural export concentration makes the terminal strategically valuable but economically vulnerable. Agricultural exports generate lower revenue per TEU ($400-$450/TEU) than manufactured imports ($550-$650/TEU) due to:
- Lower storage charges (agricultural shippers demand fast vessel loading to preserve product quality)
- Seasonal volume fluctuations (almond harvest August-October, walnut harvest September-November)
- Equipment imbalance (exports require returning empty containers from Asia, increasing handling costs)

**Port of Oakland's Agricultural Export Dominance:**
The Port of Oakland is the **#1 U.S. port for refrigerated (reefer) agricultural exports** for seven consecutive years,¬≥‚Å∞ handling 235,899 TEUs of agricultural commodities valued at $8.5B in 2024.¬≥¬π Ninety percent (90%) of Northern California agricultural products destined for Asia transit through Oakland terminals.¬≥¬≤

**Implications for Walk-Away Analysis:**
If PMSC exits Oakland, **60% of the terminal's 450,000 TEU volume (270,000 TEU) cannot be economically redeployed** to LA/Long Beach due to:
1. **Geographic mismatch**: California Central Valley agricultural shippers are located 90-150 miles from Oakland vs. 400+ miles from LA/Long Beach‚Äîtrucking costs increase $200-$300/container
2. **Reefer capacity constraints**: LA/Long Beach terminals have limited reefer plug-in capacity (prioritize manufactured import volumes)
3. **Vessel service patterns**: Oakland-Asia direct vessel services optimized for export cargo; LA/Long Beach services prioritize import cargo

#### 2. Scenario Analysis: Rent Levels and Economic Viability

**Walk-Away Threshold Determination:**

The industry benchmark for container terminal EBITDA margins is 20-25%.¬≥¬≥ PMSC's corporate investment policy requires a minimum 20% EBITDA margin for terminal operations to justify capital at risk.

| Scenario | Annual Rent | EBITDA | EBITDA Margin | ROI on Capital¬≥‚Å¥ | Viability Assessment |
|----------|-------------|--------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Current (baseline)** | $28M | $55M | 24.4% | 30.6% (3.3-year payback) | ‚úì **Highly profitable** |

| **Mid-range** | $36M | $47M | 20.9% | 26.1% (3.8-year payback) | ‚ö† **Marginal** (at industry benchmark floor) |

**Key Findings:**

1. **PMSC's stated walk-away threshold of $33M** is economically rational: it preserves 22.2% EBITDA margin (above 20% benchmark) and maintains a 3.6-year payback on the $180M capital invested.

2. **Port's $42M demand** reduces EBITDA margin to 18.2%, falling **below the 20% industry benchmark**. At this rent level, the Oakland terminal would be the **worst-performing asset in PMSC's portfolio** (LA 27.6%, Long Beach 27.0%, Seattle 24.8%).

3. **Maximum economically justifiable rent is $38M**, which yields exactly 20% EBITDA margin ($45M EBITDA on $225M revenue). Any rent above $38M produces sub-benchmark returns.

4. **MSC's $44M unsolicited bid** reflects a different business model: MSC is a vertically integrated carrier that would use Oakland as a **captive terminal for MSC-owned cargo** (potentially 350,000-400,000 TEU of MSC's own containers). This allows MSC to accept lower terminal-level margins because it captures shipping line profits upstream. PMSC, by contrast, relies on third-party carrier TOAs for 60% of Oakland volume‚Äîmaking PMSC more sensitive to terminal-level profitability.

#### 3. Alternative Scenario 1: Redeploy to LA/Long Beach Excess Capacity

**LA/Long Beach Capacity Analysis:**

PMSC operates two terminals in the LA/Long Beach port complex:
- **LA terminal**: 80 acres, 760,000 TEU capacity, FY2024 throughput 650,000 TEU ‚Üí **110,000 TEU spare capacity (14% utilization)**
- **Long Beach terminal**: 60 acres, 630,000 TEU capacity, FY2024 throughput 560,000 TEU ‚Üí **70,000 TEU spare capacity (11% utilization)**
- **Combined spare capacity**: 180,000 TEU/year available

**Redeployment Feasibility:**

Of Oakland's 450,000 TEU annual volume:
- **180,000 TEU (40%) can be redeployed** to LA/Long Beach spare capacity:
  - 110,000 TEU to LA terminal
  - 70,000 TEU to Long Beach terminal
  - These are primarily **manufactured import containers** destined for Southern California (Los Angeles metro, Inland Empire distribution centers)‚Äîminimal trucking cost penalty

- **270,000 TEU (60%) cannot be economically redeployed**:
  - **Agricultural export containers** (180,000 TEU): California Central Valley shippers cannot absorb $200-$300/container trucking cost increase to reach LA/Long Beach vs. Oakland
  - **Empty container repositioning** (90,000 TEU): Empty containers used to balance trade lanes; moving empty handling from Oakland to LA/Long Beach adds deadhead trucking costs without revenue

**Financial Impact of Partial Redeployment:**

| Item | Calculation | Amount |

| **Revenue lost (unrecoverable 270K TEU)** | 270,000 TEU √ó $500/TEU | **-$135M/year** |

**Key Observation:** Walking away from Oakland results in a **$64.5M to $78.5M annual EBITDA loss** depending on the counterfactual rent avoided. However, this must be compared to the alternative of accepting uneconomic rent terms:

**Comparative Analysis:**

| Option | Oakland Terminal EBITDA | Net Portfolio Impact |
|--------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| **Accept $28M (current rent)** | $55M | Baseline (no change) |

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Oakland terminal lease renewal is the most material commercial contract issue in the PMSC acquisition**, representing potential transaction value impact of $20M-$433M depending on lease outcome.

2. **PMSC's walk-away threshold of $33M rent is economically rational** based on:
   - Preserves 22.2% EBITDA margin (above 20% industry benchmark)
   - Maintains 3.6-year payback on $180M capital invested
   - Inflation-adjusted rent increase (18%) from 2015 baseline

3. **Port of Oakland's $42M rent demand is economically unjustified for PMSC** because:
   - Reduces EBITDA margin to 18.2% (below 20% benchmark)
   - Oakland terminal becomes worst-performing asset in PMSC's portfolio
   - Walking away (net -$64.5M impact) is worse than accepting, but accepting $42M violates corporate investment return requirements

4. **Maximum economically justifiable rent for PMSC is $38M** (yields 20% EBITDA margin floor). Any rent above $38M produces sub-benchmark returns.

5. **MSC's $44M unsolicited bid reflects different business model** (vertically integrated carrier using Oakland as captive terminal for MSC-controlled cargo). PMSC cannot match MSC's bid economics due to reliance on third-party carrier TOAs for 60% of Oakland volume.

6. **Walking away from Oakland results in $64.5M-$78.5M annual EBITDA loss** (net of savings), primarily driven by:
   - $135M revenue loss from 270,000 TEU that cannot be redeployed (agricultural exports, empty containers)
   - Only 180,000 TEU (40%) can be absorbed by LA/Long Beach spare capacity

7. **LA, Long Beach, and Seattle terminal leases present lower risk** with leases secure through 2029-2042 and strong terminal financial performance (EBITDA margins 24.8%-27.6%).

8. **100% geographic concentration in West Coast terminals creates systemic risk** from ILWU labor strikes, California environmental mandates, and West Coast port congestion.
