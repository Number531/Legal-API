### Key Findings

**1. No FMC License Transfer Approval Required (Critical Regulatory Finding)**

The task delegation referenced "OCC License No. 024587" and cited "46 CFR ¬ß 514" for license transfer requirements. This research identifies a **critical misunderstanding of FMC regulatory framework**:

- **PMSC is a Vessel-Operating Common Carrier (VOCC)**, not an Ocean Transportation Intermediary (OTI) requiring FMC licensing. VOCCs operate their own vessels (PMSC operates 22 owned + 8 chartered vessels) and are **not subject to OTI licensing** under 46 CFR Part 515.

- **46 CFR Part 514** is an outdated regulation (1990s) governing tariffs and service contracts, since superseded. It does not, and never did, establish a "license transfer" approval process for VOCCs.

- **Change of control notification** is required only if the transaction results in **government ownership/control** of the carrier (46 CFR ¬ß 565.4 - controlled carrier notification). Global Logistics Partners LLC is a private equity-backed entity, not a government-owned enterprise. Therefore, **no FMC notification is required**, even post-closing.

**Conclusion:** The acquisition proceeds without FMC license transfer approval, notification, or waiting period. This eliminates the anticipated "60-90 day delay" risk referenced in the task delegation.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. REGULATORY FRAMEWORK: SHIPPING ACT OF 1984 AND FMC JURISDICTION

#### 1. Statutory Authority

The Federal Maritime Commission derives its regulatory authority over ocean common carriers from the Shipping Act of 1984, as amended, codified at 46 U.S.C. Chapter 401.‚Å∑ The Act establishes comprehensive regulation of oceanborne transportation in U.S. foreign commerce.‚Å∏

**Ocean Common Carrier Definition:**
46 U.S.C. ¬ß 40102(6) defines an "ocean common carrier" as:
> "a vessel-operating common carrier that operates, for all or part of its common carrier service, a liner vessel in foreign commerce."‚Åπ

**VOCC vs. NVOCC Distinction (Critical for License Transfer Analysis):**
Organizations offering common carriage that **operate at least one vessel** in the foreign commerce of the United States are classified as Vessel-Operating Common Carriers (VOCCs).¬π‚Å∞ In contrast, Non-Vessel-Operating Common Carriers (NVOCCs) function as ocean transportation intermediaries subject to licensing under 46 CFR Part 515.¬π¬π

**PMSC Status: VOCC, Not OTI**
PMSC operates 22 owned vessels and 8 chartered vessels in liner service between Asia and U.S. West Coast ports.¬π¬≤ Therefore, PMSC is properly classified as a **vessel-operating common carrier (VOCC)**, not an ocean transportation intermediary requiring FMC OTI licensing under 46 CFR Part 515.

**CRITICAL FINDING #1:** The task delegation referred to "OCC License No. 024587" and cited "46 CFR ¬ß 514" for license transfer requirements. However:
- **46 CFR Part 514 (Tariffs and Service Contracts) was superseded in the 1990s** and no longer governs license transfers.¬π¬≥
- **VOCCs like PMSC do not require FMC "licensing"** in the same manner as NVOCCs/OTIs. VOCCs must comply with Shipping Act provisions (tariff publication, service contract filing, agreement filing) but do not obtain a specific "OCC license" from the FMC.¬π‚Å¥
- **Change of control/ownership for VOCCs does NOT require FMC pre-approval** under current regulations.¬π‚Åµ

#### 2. FMC Notification Requirements for Ownership Changes

**Controlled Carrier Notification (46 CFR ¬ß 565.4):**
The only FMC notification requirement triggered by a change of ownership applies if the transaction results in **government ownership or control** of the carrier:

> "Whenever the operation, control or ownership of an ocean common carrier is transferred resulting in a majority portion of the interest of that ocean common carrier being owned or controlled in any manner by a government, the ocean common carrier shall immediately send written notification of the details of the change to the Secretary of the Commission."¬π‚Å∂

**Definition of "Controlled Carrier":**
46 CFR ¬ß 565.2 defines "controlled carrier" as an ocean common carrier that is "directly or indirectly owned or controlled by a government."¬π‚Å∑ Government ownership exists where:
- A majority interest is owned/controlled by a government, government agency, or government-controlled person; OR
- The government has the right to appoint or disapprove a majority of directors or the chief executive officer.¬π‚Å∏

**CRITICAL FINDING #2:** Global Logistics Partners LLC is a private equity-backed entity, not a government-owned enterprise.¬π‚Åπ Therefore, the acquisition **does NOT trigger controlled carrier notification requirements** under 46 CFR Part 565.

**Post-Closing Notification (If Applicable):**
Even for controlled carrier status changes, notification is **post-closing** ("immediately send written notification" after the change occurs), not a pre-approval requirement.¬≤‚Å∞ The FMC does not operate a "license transfer" approval process for VOCCs.

### C. DETENTION & DEMURRAGE COMPLAINTS: 46 CFR PART 545 ANALYSIS

#### 1. Regulatory Framework: FMC D&D Interpretive Rule (May 2020)

On May 18, 2020, the FMC issued its **Interpretive Rule on Demurrage and Detention Under the Shipping Act** (46 CFR Part 545, Docket No. 19-05).¬≥‚Å∞ The rule provides guidance on determining whether D&D practices violate 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 41102(c) (prohibition on unjust and unreasonable practices).¬≥¬π

**The Incentive Principle:**
The FMC's central standard is whether D&D charges serve their "intended primary purposes as financial incentives to promote freight fluidity."¬≥¬≤

**Key Factors for Unreasonable Practices (46 CFR ¬ß 545.5):**

1. **Empty Container Return:** Practices imposing detention when empty containers **cannot be returned** are presumptively unreasonable.¬≥¬≥
   - Example: Carrier imposes detention charges when its own terminal refuses to accept empty returns due to congestion.

2. **Cargo Availability:** D&D charges must relate to cargo **availability for retrieval**.¬≥‚Å¥
   - Example: Demurrage begins before shipper receives notice of cargo availability = unreasonable.

3. **Notice of Cargo Availability:** FMC examines type, timing, format, and recipient of cargo availability notices.¬≥‚Åµ
   - Inadequate notice (e.g., email to wrong party, 24-hour notice for complex retrieval) = unreasonable D&D.

4. **Policies and Transparency:** D&D policies must be accessible, clear, and include dispute resolution procedures.¬≥‚Å∂
   - Vague tariff terms, hidden policies, or refusal to consider disputes = unreasonable.

#### 2. Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (OSRA) Enhancements

On June 16, 2022, President Biden signed OSRA into law (Pub. L. 117-146), significantly strengthening FMC enforcement authority:¬≥‚Å∑

**Burden of Proof Shift:**
For D&D charges, **carriers bear the burden of proof** to establish reasonableness.¬≥‚Å∏ Previously, complainants bore the burden.

**FMC Authority to Order Refunds:**
46 U.S.C. ¬ß 41109(a)(1), as amended, authorizes the FMC to "assess a civil penalty" or "in addition to, or in lieu of a civil penalty . . . order a refund of money."¬≥‚Åπ

**Billing Requirements Final Rule (February 2024):**
Effective May 28, 2024, D&D invoices must include:‚Å¥‚Å∞
- Clear container identification
- Timeframe at issue
- Detailed billed amounts
- Point of contact for questions
- **Certified statement:** Charges comply with FMC rules, and carrier's performance did not cause/contribute to charges‚Å¥¬π

**Non-Compliance = Enforcement Action:**
Invoices issued after May 28, 2024, that fail to meet these requirements subject carriers to FMC enforcement.‚Å¥¬≤

#### 3. FMC Enforcement Precedent and Settlement Amounts

**Scale of D&D Charges (COVID-19 Period):**

**Notable Enforcement Actions:**

### D. VESSEL-SHARING ALLIANCE (TPSA) TERMINATION RISK

#### 1. VSA Framework and FMC Filing Requirements

Vessel-Sharing Agreements (VSAs) are cooperative arrangements among ocean carriers to share space on cargo vessels along specified trade routes.‚Åµ¬π VSAs differ from full alliances:
- **VSA:** Route-specific slot exchange, each carrier maintains operational independence‚Åµ¬≤
- **Alliance:** Global cooperation across multiple routes under unified terms‚Åµ¬≥

**FMC Filing Requirement:**
VSAs are filed with the FMC under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 40303 (agreements among ocean common carriers).‚Åµ‚Å¥ VSAs become effective **45 days after filing or 30 days after Federal Register publication, whichever is later**.‚Åµ‚Åµ

**TPSA Structure (Based on Typical Trans-Pacific VSAs):**
PMSC participates in the Trans-Pacific Slot Alliance (TPSA) with 3 partner carriers.‚Åµ‚Å∂ Typical arrangements include:
- Slot exchange: Each carrier provides X slots per sailing on its vessels to partner carriers
- Revenue allocation: Pro-rata based on actual cargo carried
- Operational coordination: Schedule coordination, terminal sharing, equipment interchange

#### 2. Change of Control Termination Rights

**Standard VSA Termination Provisions:**
While the actual TPSA agreement is not available for review, industry-standard VSAs include:‚Åµ‚Å∑
- **Notice termination:** 90-180 days' notice without cause
- **Change of control termination:** Immediate termination right if a VSA partner undergoes M&A transaction affecting >50% ownership
- **Rationale:** Partners may not wish to cooperate with new owner (e.g., PE-backed entity with cost-cutting focus, competitor acquires partner)

**Risk Assessment:**
If TPSA partners exercise change-of-control termination rights:
- **Slot capacity loss:** PMSC loses access to ~30-40% additional slot capacity on routes where partners provide vessels
- **Service frequency reduction:** PMSC may need to reduce sailings if it cannot fill partners' vacated slots
- **Revenue impact:** Loss of slot exchange benefit estimated at **$15M-$22M annually** (based on typical VSA contribution to carrier economics)

#### 3. Probability Analysis and Mitigation

| Scenario | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|----------|-------------|--------|------------|
| **TPSA partners do not terminate (standard PE acquisition)** | 70% | $0 | Global Logistics Partners is financial buyer, not strategic competitor |
| **1-2 partners terminate, others continue** | 20% | $5M-$10M revenue loss | Renegotiate remaining partnership, seek replacement slot partners |
| **All 3 partners terminate TPSA** | 10% | $15M-$22M revenue loss | Deploy PMSC's own vessel capacity, reduce service frequency on affected routes |

**Expected Value:** (70% √ó $0) + (20% √ó $7.5M) + (10% √ó $18.5M) = **$3.35M expected exposure**

**Mitigation Strategy:**
1. **Pre-closing partner notification:** Inform TPSA partners of transaction, emphasize continuity of operations and management.
2. **Obtain partner consents/waivers:** Negotiate amendments to TPSA agreement waiving change-of-control termination rights for this transaction.
3. **Replacement capacity:** Identify alternative VSA partners or charter vessel capacity to replace terminated slots if necessary.
