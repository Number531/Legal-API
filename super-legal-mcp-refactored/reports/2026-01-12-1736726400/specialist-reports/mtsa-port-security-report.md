## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Critical Finding: Seattle TWIC Reader "Non-Compliance" Does Not Exist

**The task materials identified Seattle terminal TWIC reader non-compliance as Critical Issue #3, with assessed penalty exposure of $2.25M-$4.5M if the November 2024 extension request is denied by USCG Sector Puget Sound. This risk assessment is INCORRECT and based on an outdated regulatory deadline.**

The U.S. Coast Guard published a final rule on **October 31, 2024** (89 Fed. Reg. 87,382), titled "TWIC-Reader Requirements; Second Delay of Effective Date," which **delays the Transportation Worker Identification Credential (TWIC) reader implementation deadline to May 8, 2029** for Risk Group A facilities (those handling Certain Dangerous Cargoes in bulk). This delay replaces the previous deadline of May 8, 2026 established in the April 17, 2023 conforming amendment (88 Fed. Reg. 23,405). The October 2024 delay was authorized by Congress through Section 11804 of the James M. Inhofe National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2023 (Pub. L. 117-263), which directed the Secretary of Homeland Security not to implement TWIC reader regulations for "covered facilities" before May 8, 2026. The Coast Guard exercised regulatory authority to further delay implementation to May 8, 2029, citing "continued challenges with integrating TWIC readers into physical access control systems" and "supply chain delays."

**For facilities NOT classified as Risk Group A**, 33 CFR ¬ß 105.255(a) allows either electronic TWIC inspection (TWIC readers) OR visual TWIC inspection (manual verification of TWIC card). This means non-Risk Group A facilities have **NO mandatory TWIC reader deadline** - visual inspection remains compliant indefinitely.

**Impact on Seattle Terminal:**

The task states Seattle terminal is subject to a December 31, 2024 TWIC reader deadline with potential $25,000/day penalties starting January 1, 2025. **This deadline does not exist in current Coast Guard regulations.** Seattle terminal's actual compliance status depends on its Risk Group classification:

### TWIC Credential Compliance for Facility Personnel: ILWU Responsibility with Facility Verification Obligation

Under 33 CFR ¬ß 105.255 and 49 CFR Part 1572, all individuals seeking unescorted access to secure areas of PMSC's 4 terminals must hold a valid TWIC and pass either electronic or visual TWIC inspection. This includes:
- 3,200 ILWU longshoremen working at the 4 terminals
- Terminal equipment operators (crane operators, CHE drivers)
- Security personnel (FSOs, security guards)
- Maintenance contractors accessing secure areas unescorted
- Truck drivers delivering/picking up containers (if entering secure areas)
- Facility management (terminal managers, operations supervisors)

**Division of Responsibility:**

Under the Pacific Coast Longshore Agreement (ILWU/PMA, 2022-2028), the International Longshore and Warehouse Union is responsible for ensuring its members maintain valid TWICs as a condition of dispatch from the hiring hall. PMSC's terminals do not bear the cost ($125.25 per TWIC, 5-year validity) or administrative burden of obtaining TWICs for ILWU longshoremen.

However, 33 CFR ¬ß 105.255(a) requires the **facility owner/operator** to ensure individuals entering secure areas hold valid TWICs and pass inspection. This means PMSC's terminals must:
1. **Verify TWIC at entry**: Check that TWIC is not expired, tampered with, or on TSA's Canceled Card List
2. **Maintain access logs**: Document TWIC inspections (electronic reader logs or manual logbooks)
3. **Deny access to invalid TWICs**: Refuse entry to individuals with expired, revoked, or missing TWICs

**Risk Exposure:**

If a Coast Guard inspection discovers un-credentialed workers in secure areas (e.g., ILWU member with expired TWIC), PMSC could receive:
- **Notice of Violation (NOV)** for failure to enforce 33 CFR ¬ß 105.255(a)
- **Civil penalty**: $5,000-$25,000 if pattern of non-compliance established
- **FSP amendment required**: Enhanced TWIC verification procedures

Based on Government Accountability Office data (GAO-08-12, 2008 study), Coast Guard conducted over 10,000 facility inspections annually with a deficiency rate of 15-20%; however, 85% of violations resulted in verbal warnings or NOVs, and **only 3% resulted in civil penalties**. This demonstrates Coast Guard's preference for cooperative compliance over punitive enforcement.

**Estimated Probability and Exposure:**

- **Penalty if pattern established**: $5,000-$25,000 (Class I or Class II civil penalty under 33 CFR ¬ß 1.07-11)
- **Probability-weighted exposure**: $1,500 ($15,000 average penalty √ó 10% probability)

**Mitigation Strategy (Post-Closing Implementation):**

- Implement daily gate log procedures documenting all TWIC inspections

### Coast Guard Enforcement Discretion: "Compliance as the Goal, Not Punishment"

The Coast Guard's civil penalty enforcement policy emphasizes cooperative compliance rather than punitive action. Coast Guard Commandant Instruction M16200.3 (Civil Penalty Procedures and Administration) establishes the framework: "The lowest level of enforcement necessary to compel compliance should be used. Commands retain the discretion to not issue a Notice of Violation (NOV) for any offense(s)...if they believe the Coast Guard's enforcement goals would be better served by pursuing a Class I Administrative Civil Penalty."

**Enforcement Progression:**
1. **Verbal Warning**: Minor violations, first offense, immediate correction (no formal documentation)
2. **Notice of Violation (NOV)**: Documented violation requiring corrective action, no monetary penalty
3. **Class I Civil Penalty**: Minor violations, penalty typically <$5,000 (no formal hearing required)
4. **Class II Civil Penalty**: Serious violations, penalty $5,000-$25,000 (requires formal hearing under 33 CFR Part 1, Subpart 1.07)

**Penalty Assessment Factors (33 CFR ¬ß 1.07-11):**

When assessing civil penalties, the Coast Guard considers: (1) nature, circumstances, extent, and gravity of the violation, (2) degree of culpability of the violator, (3) history of prior offenses, (4) ability to pay, and (5) other matters as justice may require.

**Mitigating Factors** (reduce penalty):
- **Good faith compliance efforts** (e.g., equipment ordered, installation scheduled) - **directly applicable to Seattle terminal TWIC reader situation**
- **First-time violation** (no prior MTSA deficiencies)
- **Prompt self-reporting** to Coast Guard
- **Immediate corrective action** upon discovery
- **Economic hardship** (small facility operator - not applicable to $4.8B acquisition)

**MTSA Facility Inspection Data (SAFE Port Act):**

The SAFE Port Act of 2006 requires the Coast Guard to conduct at least 2 security inspections per year for each MTSA-regulated facility (one announced, one unannounced). Inspection findings are documented in the Coast Guard's Marine Information for Safety and Law Enforcement (MISLE) database. The task states "no material deficiencies noted in last USCG FSP audits (2023)," suggesting PMSC's 4 terminals maintain good MTSA compliance posture.

**Recommendation**: Obtain MISLE database inspection history for all 4 terminals (2022-2024) to confirm SAFE Port Act 2-inspection-per-year compliance and verify absence of unresolved deficiencies before closing.

### A. Legal and Regulatory Framework

#### 1. Maritime Transportation Security Act (MTSA) - Statutory Authority

The Maritime Transportation Security Act of 2002 (MTSA), Pub. L. No. 107-295, 116 Stat. 2064 (Nov. 25, 2002), codified at 46 U.S.C. Chapter 701, established a comprehensive federal framework for maritime security in the United States.¬π The MTSA was enacted in response to concerns that international waterborne transportation could become a conduit for terrorists or unconventional delivery of weapons of mass destruction.¬≤ Congress granted the Secretary of Homeland Security, acting through the U.S. Coast Guard, authority to identify, assess, and prevent transportation security incidents in the Marine Transportation System.¬≥

**Key Statutory Provisions:**

- **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 70101** - Definitions (facility, transportation security incident, security plan)
- **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 70103** - Maritime transportation security plans (Area Maritime Security Plans, Facility Security Plans)
- **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 70116** - Civil penalties for violations: up to **$25,000 per violation, per day**‚Å¥
- **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 70119** - Coast Guard inspection authority (SAFE Port Act requires 2+ annual inspections per facility)

**Civil Penalty Authority - 33 U.S.C. ¬ß 1232(a):**

> "Any person found by the Secretary to have violated this chapter or a regulation prescribed under this chapter shall be liable to the United States Government for a civil penalty of not more than $25,000 for each violation. Each day of a continuing violation shall constitute a separate violation."‚Åµ

The Coast Guard adjusts civil penalties under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act, with adjusted amounts listed in 33 CFR ¬ß 27.3 (Table 1), applicable for assessments issued after January 2, 2025, for violations occurring after November 2, 2015.‚Å∂

#### 2. Coast Guard Facility Security Regulations - 33 CFR Part 105

The Coast Guard implemented MTSA through 33 CFR Part 105, "Maritime Security: Facilities," published as a final rule on July 1, 2003 (68 Fed. Reg. 39,240).‚Å∑ Part 105 applies to:

- Facilities subject to 33 CFR Parts 126 (waterfront facilities handling LNG), 127 (waterfront facilities handling liquefied natural gas), and 154 (oil transfer facilities)
- Facilities that receive vessels certified to carry more than 150 passengers
- Facilities that receive vessels on international voyages, including vessels solely navigating the Great Lakes‚Å∏

**Subpart Structure:**
- **Subpart A**: General provisions (applicability, definitions)
- **Subpart B**: Facility Security Requirements (security measures, TWIC requirements, MARSEC levels)
- **Subpart C**: Facility Security Assessment (FSA)
- **Subpart D**: Facility Security Plan (FSP) (development, submission, approval, amendment)‚Åπ

**Key Facility Security Personnel:**

- **Facility Security Officer (FSO)**: Individual designated by the facility owner/operator responsible for implementing and maintaining the FSP (33 CFR ¬ß 105.105(a))
- **Company Security Officer (CSO)**: Individual designated by the company to ensure FSP compliance across multiple facilities (33 CFR ¬ß 105.200(a))

### C. Coast Guard Enforcement Discretion and Civil Penalty Mitigation

#### 1. Coast Guard Enforcement Philosophy

The Coast Guard's civil penalty enforcement policy emphasizes **"compliance as the goal, not punishment."**¬≥‚Å¥ Coast Guard Commandant Instruction M16200.3 (Civil Penalty Procedures and Administration) establishes the framework:

> "The lowest level of enforcement necessary to compel compliance should be used. Commands retain the discretion to not issue a Notice of Violation (NOV) for any offense(s)...if they believe the Coast Guard's enforcement goals would be better served by pursuing a Class I Administrative Civil Penalty."¬≥‚Åµ

**Enforcement Progression:**

1. **Verbal Warning**: Minor violations, first offense, immediate correction
2. **Notice of Violation (NOV)**: Documented violation requiring corrective action, no monetary penalty
3. **Class I Civil Penalty**: Minor violations, penalty typically <$5,000
4. **Class II Civil Penalty**: Serious violations, penalty $5,000-$25,000 (requires formal hearing under 33 CFR Part 1, Subpart 1.07)¬≥‚Å∂

#### 2. Penalty Assessment Factors - 33 CFR ¬ß 1.07-11

When assessing civil penalties, the Coast Guard considers:¬≥‚Å∑

1. **Nature, circumstances, extent, and gravity** of the violation
2. **Degree of culpability** of the violator
3. **History of prior offenses**
4. **Ability to pay**
5. **Other matters as justice may require**

**Aggravating Factors** (increase penalty):
- Intentional or reckless conduct
- Pattern of violations
- Economic benefit gained from non-compliance
- Refusal to cooperate with Coast Guard inspectors

**Mitigating Factors** (reduce penalty):
- **Good faith compliance efforts** (e.g., equipment ordered, installation scheduled)
- **First-time violation** (no prior MTSA deficiencies)
- **Prompt self-reporting** to Coast Guard
- **Immediate corrective action** upon discovery
- **Economic hardship** (small facility operator)

#### 3. MTSA Facility Inspection and Enforcement Data

The SAFE Port Act of 2006 requires the Coast Guard to conduct **at least two security inspections per year** for each MTSA-regulated facility (one announced, one unannounced).¬≥‚Å∏ Inspection findings are documented in the Coast Guard's **Marine Information for Safety and Law Enforcement (MISLE)** database.¬≥‚Åπ

**GAO Report on MTSA Enforcement (2008):**

A Government Accountability Office study found:‚Å¥‚Å∞
- Coast Guard conducted **over 10,000 facility inspections annually**
- **Deficiency rate: 15-20%** of inspections identified FSP violations
- **Primary deficiencies**: Record-keeping failures, drill non-compliance, inadequate access control
- **Enforcement outcome**: 85% verbal warnings or NOVs; **only 3% resulted in civil penalties**

This data demonstrates Coast Guard's **preference for cooperative compliance** over punitive enforcement, particularly for facilities with no prior violation history.

### D. TWIC Credential Requirements for Facility Personnel

**Rationale**: **Pre-closing FSP submission** eliminates post-closing compliance uncertainty and obtains COTP approval before transaction consummates. However, this requires seller cooperation and may reveal transaction details before public announcement.

**Rationale**: Proactive inspections allow correction of any issues **before closing**, avoiding inherited compliance problems. Coast Guard views voluntary inspections favorably and may expedite post-closing FSP amendment approvals.

The following items do **not require additional investigation** because they present **no material risk** to the acquisition:

4. **ISPS Code Compliance**: PMSC's terminals receive international vessels, requiring compliance with the International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) Code. 5. **Cybersecurity Amendments to FSPs**: The Coast Guard's January 2025 final rule on cybersecurity in the marine transportation system (90 Fed. Reg. 4,210, Jan. 17, 2025) requires FSPs to address cyber risks. However, this rule became effective January 16, 2025, and facilities have 2 years to submit cyber annexes to FSPs (deadline: January 2027). This does not impact the acquisition timeline.
