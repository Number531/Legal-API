### A. Legal Framework: Federal Preferred Mortgage Regime

**46 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 31301-31330 (Ship Mortgage Act of 1920)** establishes a comprehensive federal regime granting "preferred" status to properly recorded ship mortgages. Key features:

1. **Priority Over Most Maritime Liens**: Preferred mortgages rank ahead of subsequently arising necessaries liens (fuel, repairs, supplies provided after mortgage recording). This priority is exceptional in admiralty law, which traditionally favors maritime liens securing operational necessities.

2. **Preferred Maritime Lien Exceptions**: Four categories of maritime liens rank **ahead of** preferred mortgages under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31326(b):
   - **Crew and master wages** (highest priority‚Äîpublic policy favoring vulnerable maritime workers)
   - **Salvage and general average** (salvage saves vessel for benefit of all creditors)
   - **Tort claims** (personal injury, collision, property damage from unseaworthiness or crew negligence)
   - **Pre-mortgage necessaries** (supplies/repairs provided before mortgage recorded)

3. **Bankruptcy Protection**: 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 1110 provides extraordinary creditor rights in bankruptcy. If PMSC files Chapter 11, ship mortgage lenders have 60 days to cure defaults or surrender vessels. After 60 days, the automatic stay terminates automatically, and lenders may foreclose without bankruptcy court approval‚Äîa "self-help" remedy unavailable to most secured creditors.

4. **Recording with USCG NVDC**: All 22 PMSC vessels have preferred mortgages recorded with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center, creating a federal public registry. 46 CFR Part 67, Subpart Q governs mortgage recording procedures (electronic filing via PDF email now permitted as of 2025).

**Implication for PMSC Acquisition**: The preferred mortgage framework provides lenders with robust security interests, but also creates **statutory restrictions** on vessel transfers. 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31321(c) prohibits the mortgagor from selling, transferring, or encumbering mortgaged vessels without mortgagee consent‚Äîindependent of any contractual change of control provisions. Even if the loan agreements lacked change of control clauses, this statutory consent requirement would apply.

### B. Change of Control Provisions: Contractual Consent Requirements

While the Ship Mortgage Act provides the statutory framework, **change of control provisions are contractual** terms negotiated in ship mortgage loan agreements. Industry-standard provisions treat change of control as a **mandatory prepayment event** or **consent-triggering event**, giving lenders the option to:
1. **Consent** to the change of control (typically with consent fees and covenant adjustments)
2. **Withhold consent** and demand immediate repayment
3. **Accelerate the loan** and foreclose on vessels (treating unconsented change of control as event of default)

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Legal Framework for Preferred Ship Mortgages

#### 1. Statutory Authority: Ship Mortgage Act of 1920 (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31301 et seq.)

The Ship Mortgage Act of 1920, codified at 46 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 31301-31330, establishes a comprehensive federal regime for ship mortgages that grants "preferred" status to properly recorded mortgages.¬π This preferred status gives qualifying mortgages priority over most maritime liens, subject only to limited exceptions for "preferred maritime liens" and custodia legis expenses.¬≤

**46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31301 - Key Definitions:**¬≥

- **"Preferred Mortgage"**: A mortgage that satisfies the requirements of 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31322, including proper recording with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center (NVDC). Also includes mortgages on foreign vessels executed under the laws of the foreign country where ownership is documented and registered in a public register at the port of registry or central office.

- **"Mortgagee"**: A person to whom property is mortgaged and any trustee authorized under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31328 that is designated in the trust agreement.

- **"Preferred Maritime Lien"**: A lien on a vessel that arises before a preferred mortgage was filed under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31321 for damages arising out of:
  - Maritime tort
  - Stevedore wages
  - Crew wages
  - General average
  - Salvage

**46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31322 - Requirements for Preferred Mortgage Status:**‚Å¥

A mortgage attains "preferred" status when all four statutory conditions are met:‚Åµ

1. **Vessel Eligibility**: The mortgage must cover a documented vessel or a vessel for which an application for documentation is filed.

2. **Proper Execution**: The mortgage must be executed in compliance with 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31321 (signed by or on behalf of each mortgagor, identifying mortgagees, describing the interest granted, stating the amount of obligations secured).

3. **Recording**: The mortgage must be recorded with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31321.

4. **Certificate Endorsement**: The Secretary of Homeland Security (through the U.S. Coast Guard) must endorse the vessel's certificate of documentation noting the preferred mortgage.

**Critical Timing**: A mortgage attains preferred status from the time all four conditions are met, rather than from when the vessel is finally documented.‚Å∂ Therefore, a mortgage usually attains its preferred status when the application for documentation and the instrument have been filed with the NVDC.

#### 2. USCG Vessel Documentation and Mortgage Recording (46 CFR Part 67)

**Subpart Q - Filing and Recording of Mortgages:**‚Å∑

The U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center (NVDC) administers the federal vessel documentation system and maintains the public registry of preferred ship mortgages. Mortgages and related instruments presented for filing and recording must meet all requirements of 46 CFR Part 67, Subpart O (general filing provisions) in addition to the pertinent sections of Subpart Q (mortgage-specific provisions).‚Å∏

**Electronic Filing** (as of 2025): Any instrument eligible for filing and recording may be submitted in portable document format (.pdf) as an attachment to electronic mail (e-mail) for filing at the NVDC.‚Åπ The e-mail address is obtainable from the NVDC website (https://www.dco.uscg.mil/nvdc/).

**Mortgage Recording Requirements:**¬π‚Å∞

A mortgage presented for filing and recording must:
- Be signed by or on behalf of each mortgagor
- Include the name and address of each mortgagee
- Describe the interest in the vessel granted by the mortgage
- State the amount of obligations secured by the mortgage

**Public Registry**: All recorded preferred mortgages are maintained in a public registry accessible through the NVDC, enabling prospective lienholders, purchasers, and other interested parties to ascertain the existence and priority of ship mortgages.

#### 3. Maritime Lien Priority: Preferred Mortgages vs. Maritime Liens

**The Fundamental Priority Rule**: Under U.S. admiralty law, maritime liens generally rank in inverse chronological order within their class ("last-in-time, first-in-right").¬π¬π However, the Ship Mortgage Act established a critical exception: properly recorded preferred ship mortgages rank ahead of subsequently arising maritime liens, except for specifically enumerated "preferred maritime liens."¬π¬≤

**The J.E. Rumbell, 148 U.S. 1 (1893) - Historical Foundation:**¬π¬≥

The Supreme Court in *The J.E. Rumbell*, 148 U.S. 1 (1893), addressed the tension between state-created necessaries liens and federal mortgage recording systems.¬π‚Å¥ The Court held that a lien upon a vessel for necessary supplies and repairs in her home port, given by the statute of a state and to be enforced by proceedings in rem, takes precedence of a prior mortgage recorded under the predecessor to the Ship Mortgage Act.¬π‚Åµ

This decision reflected the common law principle that necessaries liens (for repairs, supplies, and crew wages) were essential to the vessel's continued operation and thus should enjoy priority over financial encumbrances. The Ship Mortgage Act of 1920 partially reversed this rule by granting "preferred" status to properly recorded mortgages, subject to the exceptions enumerated in 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31326.

**Modern Maritime Lien Priority Ranking (Commercial Instruments and Maritime Liens Act):**¬π‚Å∂

Under current U.S. law, the following priority order applies:¬π‚Å∑

| Priority Rank | Lien Type | Legal Basis | Priority Rationale |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **1** | **Expenses of Justice** (custodia legis) | Court costs, U.S. Marshal vessel custody expenses | Not technically a lien; expenses incurred to preserve vessel for all creditors |
| **2** | **Crew and Master Wages** | 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31342(a)(1); general maritime law | Public policy: protect most vulnerable maritime workers who rely on wages for subsistence |
| **3** | **Salvage and General Average** | 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31342(a)(2); general maritime law | Salvage saves vessel for benefit of all parties; general average distributes sacrifice |
| **4** | **Tort Liens** (collision, personal injury, death) | 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31342(a)(3); general maritime law | Involuntary creditors who did not contract with vessel owner |
| **5** | **Pre-Mortgage Necessaries Liens** | 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31342(a)(4) (arising before mortgage recorded) | Necessary supplies/repairs enabling vessel operation |
| **6** | **PREFERRED SHIP MORTGAGES** | **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31322** | **Federal preference for recorded financial encumbrances** |
| **7** | **Post-Mortgage Necessaries Liens** | 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31342(a)(4) (arising after mortgage recorded) | Subordinate to preferred mortgages under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31326(b) |
| **8** | **State-Created Maritime Liens** | State statutes | Lower priority absent federal recognition |
| **9** | **Federal Statutory Liens** (e.g., customs, tax) | Various federal statutes | Generally subordinate to maritime liens |

**46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31326 - Preferred Maritime Liens Priority Over Preferred Mortgages:**¬π‚Å∏

Section 31326(b) provides that a preferred mortgage has priority over all claims against the vessel, except for the following "preferred maritime liens":
1. **Wages** of a steward, mariner, or other person employed on the vessel
2. **General average** contributions
3. **Salvage** (including contract salvage)
4. **Tort claims** for damages arising from maritime tort

**Critical Implication for PMSC Ship Mortgages**: The four lenders' $2.8 billion preferred mortgages are subordinate to crew wage liens, salvage liens, general average claims, and tort liens (including personal injury claims arising from vessel operations, such as the Martinez v. PMSC Section 905(b) case analyzed by the case-law specialist).¬π‚Åπ However, the mortgages rank ahead of subsequently arising necessaries liens (fuel, repairs, supplies provided after mortgage recording).

#### 4. Bankruptcy Protection: 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 1110 (Aircraft Equipment and Vessels)

**Special Status in Bankruptcy**: Preferred ship mortgages receive extraordinary protection in bankruptcy proceedings under 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 1110, which provides that lessors and secured creditors of vessels are not subject to the automatic stay.¬≤‚Å∞

**11 U.S.C. ¬ß 1110(a) - Limited Stay Period**:¬≤¬π
> The right of a secured party with a security interest in equipment...to take possession of such equipment in compliance with an equipment security agreement...or with applicable law...is not limited or otherwise affected by any other provision of this title or by any power of the court.

The statute requires the debtor (vessel owner in bankruptcy) to either:
- **Cure all defaults** within 60 days of the bankruptcy petition filing; OR
- **Agree to perform all obligations** under the mortgage and cure defaults; OR
- **Surrender the vessel** to the mortgagee.

If the debtor fails to act within 60 days, the automatic stay terminates automatically, and the mortgagee may foreclose on the vessel without further bankruptcy court approval.¬≤¬≤

**Strategic Implication for PMSC Acquisition**: If PMSC were to file for bankruptcy protection post-acquisition (e.g., due to ILWU strike disrupting cash flow, or refinancing failure), the four ship mortgage lenders (Bank of America, Citibank, HSBC, DVB Bank) could repossess the vessels within 60 days unless PMSC cures defaults. This "self-help" remedy makes ship mortgages significantly more creditor-friendly than typical secured loans subject to the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 362.

### B. Change of Control Provisions in Ship Mortgage Agreements

#### 1. Contractual Change of Control Restrictions

While federal maritime law (46 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 31301-31330) establishes the statutory framework for preferred ship mortgages, **change of control provisions are creatures of contract**, negotiated between shipowners (mortgagors) and lenders (mortgagees).¬≤¬≥ The Ship Mortgage Act itself does not mandate change of control consent requirements; rather, these provisions appear in the underlying loan agreements as protective covenants.

**Industry-Standard Change of Control Clauses**: Based on maritime finance market practice, ship mortgage loan agreements typically include change of control provisions that define triggering events and consent requirements.¬≤‚Å¥ In vessel financing, mandatory prepayment events commonly include:¬≤‚Åµ
- The total loss of the ship
- The sale of the ship
- **A change of control or ownership of the borrower**
- It becoming illegal for the lender to maintain the loan
- In pre-delivery financing, the non-delivery of the ship or termination of the shipbuilding contract

**Consent vs. Automatic Default**: Some change of control covenants require the borrower to obtain the lender's consent before proceeding with a transaction that could trigger a change of control, giving the lender the option to negotiate terms in advance.¬≤‚Å∂ More aggressive provisions may treat an unconsented change of control as an automatic event of default, permitting the lender to accelerate the loan and foreclose on the vessel.

#### 2. What Constitutes "Change of Control"?

**Typical Definitional Components**:¬≤‚Å∑

Change of control clauses should include clear definitions specifying the events that constitute a change of control. In the context of ship mortgage financing, "change of control" is commonly defined to include:

1. **Direct Ownership Change**: Transfer of 50% or more of the voting equity interests in the borrower (PMSC) to a third party (Global Logistics Partners LLC).

2. **Indirect Ownership Change**: A change in the ultimate beneficial ownership or control of the borrower, even if accomplished through intermediate holding companies.

3. **Board Control Change**: A change in the majority composition of the board of directors, such that the new board is not elected or approved by the original shareholders.

4. **Asset Sale**: Sale of all or substantially all assets of the borrower (though vessel-specific mortgages may carve out individual vessel sales if loan balance is paid down).

**Application to PMSC Acquisition**: The Global Logistics Partners acquisition of PMSC involves:
- **100% stock purchase** or merger transferring all equity to the PE sponsor (triggers direct ownership change)
- **Complete board replacement** with PE sponsor designees (triggers board control change)
- **Change in ultimate control** from family ownership to institutional PE sponsor (triggers indirect ownership change)

All four ship mortgage lenders (Bank of America, Citibank, HSBC, DVB Bank) will likely view this transaction as a textbook "change of control" requiring lender consent under their respective loan agreements.

#### 5. Financial Covenants: DSCR and LTV Requirements

**Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**: The DSCR measures an entity's ability to generate sufficient cash to cover its debt obligations (interest + principal payments). **Industry-Standard DSCR Requirements**:¬≥‚Å∂
- **Minimum DSCR**: 1.25x (NOI must be 25% greater than annual debt service)
- **Preferred DSCR**: 2.0x or higher for stronger safety margins
- **Monitoring**: Tested quarterly based on trailing 12-month financial statements

**PMSC's Current DSCR (FY 2024)**:
- Operating Income (EBITDA): $580 million

- **DSCR**: $580M √∑ $420M = **1.38x** ‚úì (exceeds 1.25x minimum)

**Post-Acquisition Impact**: If the acquisition adds $3.2 billion in acquisition debt at PMSC level (as suggested in the research plan), total debt service will increase significantly, potentially breaching the 1.25x DSCR covenant. Lenders may require covenant reset to accommodate higher leverage.

**Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio**: In ship finance, LTV covenants provide an early warning to lenders if vessel values decline.¬≥‚Å∑ Maritime loans typically cap at **50-60% LTV** to reduce risk.¬≥‚Å∏ Lenders require periodic vessel appraisals (usually annually or semi-annually) to test LTV covenant compliance.

**PMSC's Current LTV (as of Dec. 31, 2024)**:

- **LTV**: $2.8B √∑ $5.8B = **48.3%** ‚úì (within 50-60% range)

**LTV Covenant Monitoring**: Ship finance loans almost always have an LTV maintenance covenant, and a valuation of the ship needs to be procured on a periodic basis to test this covenant.¬≥‚Åπ If vessel values decline (e.g., due to freight market downturn, CII D-rated vessels requiring scrapping, or M/V Pacific Titan-type hull damage), PMSC may breach the LTV covenant and be required to:
- Make principal prepayments to reduce loan balance
- Pledge additional collateral (other vessels or cash)
- Accept higher interest rates to compensate for increased risk

#### 6. Preferred Mortgage Restrictions Post-Execution

Under U.S. law, after executing a preferred mortgage, the mortgagor may not incur, without consent of the mortgagee, any contractual obligations establishing a lien on the vessel‚Äîexcept a lien for stevedore wages, crew wages, general average, and salvage.‚Å¥‚Å∞ Additionally, the mortgage ensures the owner will not:‚Å¥¬π
- Leave the country without intending to return
- Abandon the vessel
- Sell, mortgage, or charter the vessel without the consent of the mortgagee

**Implication for PMSC Operations**: These statutory restrictions (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 31321(c)) supplement the contractual change of control provisions. Even if the loan agreements did not contain change of control clauses, the statutory prohibition on selling or transferring the mortgaged vessels without mortgagee consent would require lender approval for the acquisition.

### C. Refinancing Risk: If Lenders Withhold Consent

#### 1. Triggers for Consent Denial

While ship mortgage lenders generally consent to change of control transactions involving creditworthy acquirers (such as established PE sponsors), consent may be withheld in the following scenarios:‚Å¥¬≤

**Scenario 1: Acquirer Creditworthiness Concerns**
- PE sponsor lacks maritime industry experience or track record
- Acquirer's financial statements show high leverage or weak liquidity
- Pro forma DSCR post-acquisition falls below lender's minimum threshold (e.g., <1.25x)
- Acquirer is domiciled in jurisdiction with weak legal enforcement (asset recovery concerns)

**Scenario 2: Shipping Market Deterioration**
- Freight rates collapse (e.g., 2008 financial crisis, 2020 COVID-19 initial shock)
- Vessel values decline sharply, causing LTV covenant breaches across maritime lending portfolio
- Lender seeks to reduce maritime sector exposure due to internal risk management policies

**Scenario 3: Lender Strategic Exit**
- Lender is exiting maritime lending sector (e.g., DVB Bank reduced shipping exposure 2020-2024)
- Lender prefers to be repaid rather than extend credit to new borrower
- Lender views change of control as opportune moment to accelerate exit

**Scenario 4: Prepayment Demand Not Met**
- Lender conditions consent on 25% principal prepayment ($700M for Bank of America loan)
- Acquirer refuses or is unable to meet prepayment demand
- Lender withholds consent, triggering event of default

### D. Bankruptcy Remote Structures and Vessel Segregation

**Observation**: Standard ship mortgage financing often employs **single-purpose entity (SPE)** structures where each vessel (or small group of vessels) is owned by a separate LLC subsidiary, with non-recourse financing at the SPE level.‚Å¥‚Åµ This structure:
- Limits contagion risk (if one vessel is lost or impaired, lender can only pursue that SPE's assets)
- Facilitates vessel sales (buyer acquires SPE owning specific vessel, mortgage transfers with vessel)
- Protects lenders from parent company bankruptcy (SPE has independent directors, limited business purpose)

1. **Parent level** (Global Logistics Partners acquires PMSC parent holding company)

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

#### 1. Legal Framework: Preferred Ship Mortgages Provide Strong Creditor Protection

PMSC's $2.8 billion ship mortgage portfolio benefits from the federal **preferred mortgage** regime under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 31301-31330, which:
- Grants recorded ship mortgages **priority over subsequently arising necessaries liens** (fuel, repairs, supplies)
- Provides **bankruptcy protection** under 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 1110 (60-day cure period, then automatic stay terminates)
- Subordinates mortgages only to **preferred maritime liens** (crew wages, salvage, general average, tort claims arising before mortgage filing)

All 22 PMSC vessels have preferred mortgages properly recorded with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center (NVDC), providing lenders with first-priority security interests (subject only to limited preferred maritime lien exceptions).

#### 2. Change of Control Consents: Required from All Four Lenders

The Global Logistics Partners acquisition of PMSC constitutes a **"change of control"** under industry-standard ship mortgage loan agreement provisions because:
- 100% of PMSC equity transfers to PE sponsor (direct ownership change)
- Majority of board replaced with PE sponsor designees (board control change)
- Ultimate beneficial ownership shifts from family to institutional sponsor (indirect control change)

All four lenders (Bank of America, Citibank, HSBC, DVB Bank) will require **affirmative consent** before the acquisition closes. Failure to obtain consent constitutes an event of default, permitting lenders to accelerate loans and foreclose on vessels.

#### 3. Consent Fees: $7M-$10.5M Expected Cost
