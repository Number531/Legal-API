## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. SECTION 905(b) LEGAL FRAMEWORK

#### 1. Statutory Text and Purpose

**33 U.S.C. § 905(b) provides:**

> "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter **caused by the negligence of a vessel**, then such person, or anyone otherwise entitled to recover damages by reason thereof, **may bring an action against such vessel as a third party**... and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void."²

**Purpose:** Section 905(b) creates a limited exception to the LHWCA exclusive remedy doctrine (§ 905(a)), allowing injured longshoremen to sue **vessel owners** for vessel negligence, while preserving **employer immunity** for stevedore companies.³

**1972 Amendment - Negligence Standard:**

Prior to 1972, longshoremen could sue vessel owners under **warranty of seaworthiness** (strict liability). *Seas Shipping Co. v. Sieracki*, 328 U.S. 85 (1946).⁴

The 1972 LHWCA amendments **overruled Sieracki**, requiring proof of **active negligence**:

> "The liability of the vessel shall not be based upon the warranty of seaworthiness or a breach thereof at the time the injury occurred." 33 U.S.C. § 905(b).⁵

**Effect:** Longshoremen must prove **negligence**, not strict liability, shifting more responsibility to the stevedore-employer "best able to prevent injuries."⁶

---

#### 2. Scindia Three-Duty Framework

The Supreme Court in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), established that vessel owners owe **three narrow duties** to longshoremen under § 905(b):⁷

**DUTY 1: TURNOVER DUTY**

> "The shipowner has a duty with respect to the condition of the ship's gear, equipment, tools, and work space to be used in the stevedoring operations; and if he fails at least to **warn the stevedore of hidden danger** which would have been known to him in the exercise of reasonable care, he has breached his duty and is liable if his negligence causes injury to a longshoreman."⁸

**Key Elements:**
- Applies at **commencement** of stevedoring operations
- Duty to provide ship/equipment in **reasonably safe condition**
- Duty to **warn of latent (hidden) defects**
- No duty to warn of **obvious hazards** or hazards stevedore should anticipate⁹

**Latent Hazard Definition (*Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*, 512 U.S. 92 (1994)):**

> "Latent hazards are hazards that are **not known to the stevedore** and that would be **neither obvious to nor anticipated by a skilled stevedore** in the competent performance of its work."¹⁰

**Examples of Turnover Duty Breach:**
- Defective winch not disclosed to stevedore
- Broken ladder on ship's gear
- Corroded deck plates creating hidden danger¹¹

**DUTY 2: ACTIVE CONTROL DUTY**

> "The vessel may be liable if it **actively involves itself in the cargo operations** and negligently injures a longshoreman or if it fails to exercise due care to avoid exposing longshoremen to harm from hazards they may encounter in **areas, or from equipment, under the active control of the vessel** during the stevedoring operation."¹²

**Key Elements:**
- Applies **after** stevedoring operations commence
- Vessel must **actively control** cargo operations (normal practice: stevedore controls)
- Vessel crew **operates equipment** or **directs longshoremen**
- Vessel negligently creates hazards in areas under its control¹³

**Examples of Active Control Breach:**
- Vessel crew operates crane negligently
- Vessel's chief mate orders longshoremen to work in unsafe conditions
- Vessel crew closes hatch cover without warning, injuring longshoreman (*Kiwia v. M/V OSLO BULK 9*)¹⁴

**DUTY 3: DUTY TO INTERVENE**

> "A vessel owner has the duty to intervene in cargo operations only when it has **(1) actual knowledge of a dangerous condition** and **(2) actual knowledge that the stevedore**, in the exercise of poor judgment, **has failed to correct the dangerous condition**."¹⁵

**Key Elements:**
- **Highest threshold** of three duties
- Requires **actual knowledge** (not constructive notice)
- Stevedore must be "imprudently" ignoring obvious danger
- Vessel cannot be relied upon to protect workers (stevedore incompetent)¹⁶

**Example:**
- Vessel officer observes longshoremen working under visibly unstable container, fails to stop work

**Scindia's Narrow Scope:**

> "Once the stevedore's cargo operations have begun, **absent contract provision, positive law, or custom to the contrary**, the shipowner has **no general duty** under § 905(b) by way of **supervision or inspection** to exercise reasonable care to discover dangerous conditions that develop within the confines of the cargo operations that are assigned to the stevedore."¹⁷

**Effect:** Vessel owners are **not general insurers** of stevedore safety. Liability limited to three narrow duties.

### B. CRITICAL THRESHOLD ISSUE: IS PMSC A "VESSEL" OR "EMPLOYER"?

#### 1. The "Vessel" vs. "Employer" Distinction

**Section 905(b) Definition of "Vessel":**

33 U.S.C. § 905(b) creates liability for "the negligence of **a vessel**." Courts interpret "vessel" to include:
- Vessel owner
- Vessel operator
- Charterer (time or bareboat)
- Master, officers, or crew members acting within scope of employment¹⁸

**Section 905(a) Employer Immunity:**

> "The liability of an **employer** prescribed in section 904 [LHWCA benefits] shall be **exclusive** and in place of all other liability of such employer to the employee... at law or in admiralty."¹⁹

**Effect:** Employers (stevedore companies, terminal operators) are **immune** from tort suits by their employees. LHWCA benefits are the exclusive remedy.²⁰

#### 2. PMSC's Dual Role Creates Ambiguity

**PMSC's Business Model (From Research Plan Fact Pattern):**

PMSC operates as an **integrated maritime services company**:
- **Fleet Operations:** 22 owned vessels (18 container ships, 4 bulk carriers) + 8 chartered vessels²¹
- **Terminal Operations:** 4 West Coast terminals (LA, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle) employing 3,200 ILWU longshoremen²²
- **Stevedoring Services:** PMSC provides cargo handling services at its own terminals

**Critical Question:** When Martinez was injured aboard **M/V Pacific Express**, was PMSC acting as:
- **(A) Vessel owner/operator** of M/V Pacific Express? → Subject to § 905(b) liability
- **(B) Terminal operator/employer** of Martinez? → Protected by § 905(a) immunity

#### 3. The "Dual Capacity" Doctrine

**General Rule:**

Courts have recognized that an entity can be sued under § 905(b) **even if it is also the longshoreman's employer**, if the entity acts in its **separate capacity as vessel owner**:

> "You have the right to sue your employer for recovery of damages if two conditions exist: (1) your employer **owned the vessel** on which you were injured; and (2) your injury was the result of your employer's negligence **'in his capacity' as the vessel's owner**."²³

**Application:**

If PMSC **owns M/V Pacific Express** and the injury resulted from **vessel negligence** (crane defect, deck obstruction, third mate's directions), PMSC may be liable under § 905(b) despite also being Martinez's employer.

**However:** Dual capacity claims are **narrowly construed**. Courts require:
- Clear separation between employer role and vessel owner role
- Negligence attributable to vessel operations, not terminal/stevedoring operations
- Vessel's equipment or crew caused injury, not employer's equipment or supervision²⁴

##### C. Active Control Duty Breach: Third Mate Improper Directions

**Martinez's Allegation:**

- General coordination between vessel crew and stevedore (e.g., "ready to load," "all clear")

### D. COMPARATIVE FAULT ANALYSIS

#### 1. Comparative Negligence in Section 905(b) Cases

**Supreme Court Precedent: *Edmonds v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique*, 443 U.S. 256 (1979)**

The Supreme Court held that in § 905(b) cases:
- **Longshoreman's own negligence** DOES reduce the verdict
- **Stevedore employer's negligence** DOES NOT reduce the vessel's liability⁴⁰

**Key Holding:**

> "A longshoreman could recover the full amount of damages from a shipowner, **even if the stevedore's negligence contributed to the injuries**, without the damages being reduced in proportion to the stevedore's fault."⁴¹

**Edmonds Fault Allocation:**
- **10% longshoreman negligence** → verdict reduced by 10%
- **20% vessel negligence** → vessel pays 90% of damages (after longshoreman's 10% reduction)
- **70% stevedore employer negligence** → does NOT reduce vessel's liability

**Result:** Vessel liable for 90% of total damages despite being only 20% at fault.⁴²

### E. SECTION 905(c) INDEMNITY ANALYSIS

#### 1. Section 905(c) Statutory Framework

**33 U.S.C. § 905(c) provides:**

> "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person... may bring an action against such vessel as a third party... **and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly** and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void."⁴⁴

**Effect of 1972 Amendment:**

Prior to 1972, stevedore employers routinely **indemnified** vessel owners for longshoreman injuries caused by stevedore negligence.

The 1972 amendment **abolished one-way indemnity**:
- Vessel owners **cannot** seek indemnity from stevedore employers for § 905(b) verdicts
- **Exception:** **Reciprocal indemnity agreements** permitted under § 905(c) for mutual defense costs⁴⁵

#### 2. PMSC's Unique Dual Capacity Problem

**Normal § 905(b) Case:**
- Longshoreman employed by **Stevedore Company A**
- Longshoreman injured aboard vessel owned by **Vessel Owner B**
- Longshoreman sues **Vessel Owner B** under § 905(b)
- Vessel Owner B **cannot** seek indemnity from Stevedore Company A (§ 905(c) bars it)

**Martinez's Dual Capacity Case:**
- Martinez employed by **PMSC** (stevedore/terminal operator)
- Martinez injured aboard **M/V Pacific Express** (owned by PMSC)
- Martinez sues **PMSC in its capacity as vessel owner**
- **Question:** Can PMSC (as vessel owner) seek indemnity from PMSC (as stevedore employer)?

**Answer: NO (but academic)**

PMSC cannot indemnify itself. However, this creates **unusual exposure**:
- In normal cases, vessel owner pays § 905(b) verdict but can seek contribution from stevedore (if stevedore 70% at fault per *Edmonds*)
- In dual capacity cases, PMSC pays **full verdict** with **no indemnity or contribution** from anyone
- PMSC bears **both** § 905(b) tort liability **AND** LHWCA benefits already paid ($420,000)

**CRITICAL IMPLICATION:**

PMSC's total exposure = **§ 905(b) verdict + LHWCA benefits already paid**

**Example:**
- Jury awards Martinez $5,000,000 (gross damages)
- Jury finds Martinez 30% at fault → verdict reduced to $3,500,000
- PMSC pays: $3,500,000 (§ 905(b)) + $420,000 (LHWCA benefits already paid) = **$3,920,000 total**

**However:** PMSC likely entitled to **offset** (credit) for LHWCA benefits paid.

#### 3. LHWCA Benefits Offset

**General Rule:**

Maritime law allows vessel owners to **offset** LHWCA benefits paid by the employer from the § 905(b) verdict to avoid double recovery by the longshoreman.⁴⁶

**Application to Martinez:**

- PMSC (as employer) paid Martinez $420,000 in LHWCA benefits
- Martinez receives $3,500,000 verdict from PMSC (as vessel)
- PMSC entitled to **offset** the $420,000 already paid
- **Net additional payment:** $3,500,000 - $420,000 = **$3,080,000**

**Total PMSC Exposure:**
- LHWCA benefits: $420,000 (already paid)
- Additional § 905(b) payment: $3,080,000
- **Total: $3,500,000** (equivalent to the verdict amount)

**CONCLUSION:**

PMSC does **not** face double payment (LHWCA + full verdict). Offset doctrine ensures Martinez recovers total damages once, but PMSC bears the full amount.

In a normal case, vessel owner might recover 70% from stevedore (per *Edmonds* scenario). PMSC has **no such recovery** in dual capacity case.
