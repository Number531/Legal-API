## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Critical Issue: ACGME Surgery Program Probation and $2.5M Annual GME Payment Risk

Mercy Regional Health System's general surgery residency program (40 residents, 22% of total 180-resident GME enterprise) was placed on **probationary accreditation** by the Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME) in May 2024 due to **work hours violations**. During February-April 2024, surgery residents averaged **85-90 hours per week**, exceeding the mandatory **80-hour weekly limit** under ACGME Common Program Requirements. This violation affected **10 residents over 12 weeks** (120 resident-weeks of non-compliance), representing a substantial, systemic failure rather than isolated incidents.

The ACGME imposed a **12-month probation** (May 2024 - May 2025) requiring corrective action, with a follow-up site visit scheduled for **Q2 2025** to determine whether full accreditation will be restored. If the ACGME **withdraws accreditation**, the surgery program must close, resulting in:

1. **Immediate loss of $2.5 million annual Medicare GME payments** (42 C.F.R. § 413.75(b) requires ACGME accreditation for CMS payment eligibility)
2. **40 surgery residents forced to transfer** to other ACGME-accredited programs nationwide
3. **Surgical faculty departures** (estimated 30-50% attrition of 15-20 faculty within 12-24 months)
4. **Reputational harm** undermining Mercy Regional's competitive position as a teaching hospital
5. **Operational revenue loss** of $5-10 million annually from reduced surgical capacity

**Total exposure** if accreditation is withdrawn: **$63.6M-$109.5M (NPV, 10-year horizon)**, comprising $20.3M GME payment loss, $40.6M-$81.2M surgical revenue loss, and $2M-$6M program closure costs.

However, based on the **substantial corrective actions implemented** (2 additional faculty hired, electronic work hours monitoring, associate program director oversight) and **case precedent** showing successful probation exits, the probability of full accreditation restoration is assessed at **75-80%**. The probability of accreditation withdrawal is only **5%**, with a **15-20% probability** of continued probation requiring additional monitoring.

This Executive Summary provides a **self-contained analysis** enabling memorandum synthesis without requiring review of the full 40,000-word detailed report. All material findings, risk quantifications, regulatory citations, and transaction recommendations are included below.

### Key Findings Digest

#### 1. ACGME Common Program Requirements: The 80-Hour Limit and Enforcement

The ACGME's **80-hour weekly limit** (averaged over four weeks) has been the cornerstone of resident duty hours requirements since 2003. The ACGME Common Program Requirements (Residency) 2025, Section VI.A.1, mandates that clinical and educational work hours—including in-house activities, clinical work from home, and all moonlighting—must not exceed 80 hours per week when averaged over a four-week period.

**Strict Enforcement**: The ACGME has emphasized that **"non-compliance from the 80-hour rule will not be tolerated."** Review Committees issue citations where violations are identified, and repeated work hour violations correlate with lower compliance rates on the ACGME Resident/Fellow Survey, triggering probationary accreditation.

**Surgery-Specific Pattern**: Surgery programs have been identified as having **recurring problems** with work hour compliance. According to the ACGME Data Book 2022-2023, **four surgery programs** were issued probationary accreditation in the 2022-2023 academic year. Citations for surgery programs typically focus on: (1) duty hours violations, (2) service-to-education ratios (residents used for service rather than education), and (3) educational experiences in surgical subspecialties.

**Limited Exceptions**: The only exceptions to the 80-hour limit are Review Committee-approved specialty-specific rotations, which can extend to **88 hours maximum** based on sound educational rationale. These exceptions are rare and require explicit approval. No such exception was granted for Mercy Regional's surgery program.

#### 2. Mercy Regional Surgery Program Violation: February-April 2024

**ACGME Site Visit**: April 2024 (3-day visit)
- Surveyors reviewed electronic work hours logs, interviewed residents PGY2-PGY5, observed OR/ICU/trauma rotations, and assessed program curriculum.

**Deficiency**: Surgery residents PGY2-PGY5 averaged **85-90 hours/week** during February-April 2024
- **Magnitude**: 6.25-12.5% excess over the 80-hour limit
- **Scope**: 10 residents affected × 12 weeks = **120 resident-weeks of violations**
- **Pattern**: Consistent, sustained violations (not isolated incidents)

**Root Cause**:
- Surgical volume increased **15% in Q1 2024** (trauma cases, complex surgeries)
- Residents needed to cover ORs, ICU, and trauma consults
- **Insufficient staffing** to accommodate increased workload
- No compensatory adjustments to resident schedules despite volume increase

**Citation Level**: **Probationary Accreditation** (May 2024)
- Reduced from Continued Accreditation to Probationary Accreditation
- **12-month probation** (May 2024 - May 2025)
- Requirement: Correct work hours compliance by May 2025
- Consequence if not corrected: **Risk withdrawal of accreditation**

**Severity Assessment**: Probationary accreditation is a **serious adverse action** indicating the ACGME determined violations were substantial (not isolated), systemic (affecting 25% of program residents), and sustained (3-month period).

#### 3. Corrective Action Plan: June-August 2024 Implementation

Mercy Regional implemented **three concrete corrective measures** within 60-90 days of the May 2024 citation:

**A. Hired 2 Additional Surgical Faculty**
- **Cost**: $800,000 annually (2 faculty × $400K average general surgery faculty salary)
- **Function**: Distribute call coverage more evenly, reducing resident on-call frequency; faculty cover more nights/weekends; faculty supervise residents in OR, performing more cases independently
- **Impact**: Approximately 10-15% reduction in resident on-call burden, enabling compliance with 80-hour limit
- **Verifiable**: Employment records, payroll data, organizational chart

**B. Electronic Work Hours Tracking Enhancement**
- **System**: NewInnovations software
- **Features**: Residents log in/out via mobile app; automatic alerts if approaching 80-hour limit; real-time monitoring by program coordinator; weekly compliance reports generated automatically
- **Monitoring Protocol**: Program coordinator reviews weekly reports, identifies residents approaching 80-hour limit, adjusts schedules **prospectively** to prevent violations; monthly reports to program director and DIO
- **Verifiable**: System audit logs showing resident compliance rates, alert history, coordinator access logs

**C. Associate Program Director Appointed**
- **Role**: Dedicated faculty member responsible for resident well-being and compliance oversight
- **Responsibilities**: Work hours monitoring; quarterly reporting to ACGME; resident wellness checks (confidential); faculty recruitment if additional staffing needed; ACGME liaison
- **Accountability**: Reports directly to program director and designated institutional official (DIO)
- **Verifiable**: Organizational structure, ACGME correspondence, quarterly reports

**Assessment of Corrective Actions**:

These measures are **substantial and verifiable**, directly addressing the root cause (workload-to-staffing imbalance). They align with **ACGME best practices** for work hours compliance:
- Electronic monitoring with real-time alerts (industry standard)
- Faculty staffing increases to reduce resident clinical burden (recommended approach)
- Dedicated oversight role for compliance accountability (strengthens institutional commitment)

**Comparison to Successful Precedents**:
- One orthopaedic surgery program successfully moved from **16 citations to 1 citation** and achieved full accreditation through similar corrective actions (night float system, faculty hiring, electronic monitoring).
- A psychiatry residency program achieved restoration from probationary to **continued accreditation within 13 months** through comprehensive quality improvements.

#### 4. Medicare GME Payment Structure: $27M Total, $2.5M Surgery Attribution

**Legal Framework**: Medicare GME payments are authorized under **42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(h)** (Direct GME) and **42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(d)(5)(K)** (Indirect Medical Education), implemented in regulations at **42 C.F.R. §§ 413.75-413.86** (Direct GME) and **42 C.F.R. § 412.105** (IME).

**Critical Requirement for Payment**: 42 C.F.R. § 413.75(b) defines "approved program" as one accredited by the ACGME or other organizations designated by the Secretary. **Payments are only provided for residents in "approved" (ACGME-accredited) programs.** If ACGME withdraws accreditation, CMS will **immediately cease GME payments** for that program.

**A. Direct Graduate Medical Education (DGME): $15M Annually**

**Formula**: DGME Payment = PRA × Weighted FTE Residents × Hospital's Medicare Share of Patient Days

Where:
- **PRA** (Per-Resident Amount): Hospital-specific base amount (Mercy's allowable GME costs in base period ÷ number of residents in base period), inflation-adjusted annually
- **FTE Residents**: Full-Time Equivalent resident count (180 across 6 programs)
- **Medicare Share**: Proportion of patient days attributable to Medicare beneficiaries

**Calculation** (42 C.F.R. § 413.76): Hospital's updated PRA × actual FTE residents = aggregate approved amount for cost reporting period

**Costs Covered**: Resident salaries ($55K-$65K for PGY1-PGY7), faculty supervision, program administration, educational materials

**B. Indirect Medical Education (IME): $12M Annually**

**Formula** (42 C.F.R. § 412.105(d)): IME Adjustment Factor = c × [(1 + r)^0.405 - 1]

Where:
- **c** = Congressional multiplier (currently 1.35 for most hospitals)
- **r** = Hospital's ratio of FTE residents to beds
- **0.405** = Exponential power (statutory formula)

**Application**: IME adjustment factor is applied to **each Medicare DRG payment**. For example, if base DRG payment is $10,000 and IME adjustment factor is 5.89%, IME add-on is $589, for total payment of $10,589.

**Purpose**: Compensates teaching hospitals for higher operating costs due to: (1) more complex patient mix, (2) slower resident workflows (learning curve), (3) additional supervision requirements, (4) increased diagnostic testing for educational purposes.

**C. Total GME Payments and Surgery Program Attribution**

**Total Annual GME Payments**: **$27 million** ($15M Direct GME + $12M IME)

**Surgery Program Attributable Share**: **$2.5 million annually**
- 40 surgery residents / 180 total residents = 22.2%
- Surgery residents command higher per-resident costs due to: (1) longer training (5 years vs. 3 years for many specialties), (2) higher supervision requirements in OR, (3) more expensive educational resources (simulation, cadaver labs)
- Conservative estimate: $2.5M represents **direct attributable GME loss** if surgery program closes

**10-Year NPV of GME Loss** (if accreditation withdrawn): $2.5M annually × 8.11 (annuity factor at 4% discount rate) = **$20.3 million**

#### 5. 1996 Balanced Budget Act Resident Cap: 185 FTE Limit

**Statutory Authority**: Balanced Budget Act of 1997, Pub. L. 105-33, § 4623, 111 Stat. 251 (Aug. 5, 1997), codified at **42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(h)(4)(F)** (Direct GME cap) and **42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(d)(5)(B)(v)** (IME cap).

**Purpose**: Congress capped the number of FTE residents that can be counted for Medicare GME reimbursement purposes at each hospital's count as of **December 31, 1996**. The cap is based on the hospital's number of FTE residents for the most recent cost reporting period before December 31, 1997. In subsequent years, the cap is recomputed using a **three-year rolling average**.

**Mercy Regional's Resident Cap**: **185 FTE residents** (established in 1996 base year)

**Current Utilization**: **180 FTE residents** (97.3% of cap)
- Internal Medicine: 60
- Surgery: 40
- Family Medicine: 35
- OB/GYN: 20
- Pediatrics: 15
- Psychiatry: 10

**Available Capacity**: **5 FTE residents** (2.7% headroom) — minimal ability to expand GME programs beyond current levels

**Exceptions to the 1996 Cap**:

1. **Rural Track Programs (RTPs)**: Under 42 C.F.R. § 412.105(f)(1)(vii) and 42 C.F.R. § 413.79(e)(3), rural hospitals may receive IME and DGME FTE cap increases at any time for participating in training residents in new programs. Effective for cost reporting periods beginning on or after October 1, 2022 (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021, § 127), both urban and rural hospitals can receive cap increases for RTP time, provided >50% of residents' training takes place in rural areas.
   - **Mercy Regional Prospects**: **Unlikely** (Columbus, OH is urban, not rural)

2. **Section 5506 (ACA) — Closed Hospital Slot Redistribution**: When a teaching hospital closes, its GME FTE cap slots can be redistributed to other hospitals meeting certain criteria.
   - **Mercy Regional Prospects**: **Opportunistic only** (depends on another hospital closing)

3. **Section 126 (CAA 2021) — 1,000 New Slots**: Made available 1,000 additional FTE cap slots phased in at 200 slots/year beginning FY 2023, distributed nationally via competitive application (underserved areas, shortage specialties).
   - **Mercy Regional Prospects**: **Uncertain** (would require competitive application)

**Practical Conclusion**: Mercy Regional is **effectively capped at 185 FTE** for the foreseeable future.

**Impact if Surgery Program Closes**:
- Current: 180 FTE / 185 cap = 97.3% utilization
- Post-closure: 140 FTE / 185 cap = 75.7% utilization (**45 FTE slots unused**, 24.3% of cap)
- **Strategic options**: Could expand other programs or launch new programs up to 45 additional FTE
- **Practical barrier**: Reputational harm from losing surgery accreditation would significantly impair ability to attract residents/faculty to new or expanded programs

#### 6. Consequences if ACGME Accreditation is Withdrawn

If the ACGME withdraws accreditation for the surgery program following the Q2 2025 follow-up visit, the consequences are severe and multi-dimensional:

**A. Immediate Program Closure and Resident Displacement**

**40 Surgery Residents Must Transfer**: The ACGME requires the sponsoring institution to "inform affected residents/fellows as soon as possible" and either allow them to complete their education at the institution (if another ACGME-accredited program exists) or "assist them in enrolling in (an)other ACGME accredited program(s)." However, the requirement to "assist" is **vague and does not address institutional financial responsibilities**.

**Transfer Process Challenges**:
- Residents must apply for transfer to other ACGME-accredited surgery programs nationwide
- Other programs must have **available slots under their 1996 BBA resident caps**
- Disruption to residents' training timelines and career progression
- Possible geographic relocation required (family, financial hardship)
- Match violations if residents must transfer mid-training (NRMP rules)

**CMS GME Cap Transfer Rules**: Under Medicare rules, residents are not geographically restricted and can go to any hospital willing to accept them. In the case of residency program closure, the **receiving hospital must submit a request** to its Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) no later than **60 days after beginning to train the displaced residents**. CMS created a new definition of "displaced resident" to facilitate GME FTE cap transfers from hospitals that close programs to receiving hospitals.

**Critical Limitation**: FTE cap transfers are a **voluntary act** on the part of the closing hospital. Since many hospitals train residents **in excess of their FTE caps**, this rule change **does not guarantee that every displaced resident will be accompanied by a GME FTE cap slot**. Some of the 40 surgery residents may not find immediate transfer placements, requiring gap years or permanent career disruption.

**Potential Litigation Risk**: Residents may sue Mercy Regional for breach of training obligations, failure to provide promised educational opportunities, or fraudulent inducement (if probation was not disclosed during recruitment).

**B. Loss of $2.5 Million Annual GME Payments**

**Immediate CMS Action**: Because payments are **only provided for residents in "approved" (ACGME-accredited) programs** (42 C.F.R. § 413.75(b)), CMS will immediately cease GME payments for the surgery program.

**Financial Impact**:
- Direct GME loss: ~$1.5M annually (proportional share of $15M total)
- IME loss: ~$1.0M annually (reduced resident-to-bed ratio decreases IME factor hospital-wide)
- **Total annual loss**: $2.5M

**Impact on Mercy Regional Operating Income**:
- Current operating income: $95M (5.3% margin)
- Post-GME loss: $92.5M (5.1% margin)
- **2.6% reduction in operating income**

**10-Year NPV of GME Loss**: At 4% discount rate, present value of $2.5M annually over 10 years = **$20.3 million**

**C. Surgical Faculty Departures and Revenue Loss**

**Current Surgical Faculty**: Estimated 15-20 general surgery faculty (typical 2:1 resident-to-faculty ratio for 40 residents)

**Anticipated Departures**: Without a residency program:
- **Academic surgeons** recruited specifically for teaching will likely seek employment at other teaching hospitals
- Loss of **academic mission** reduces institutional prestige and appeal
- Estimated **30-50% faculty attrition** within 12-24 months post-closure

**Operational Impact**:
- Reduced surgical capacity (fewer surgeons to perform cases)
- Loss of subspecialty expertise (trauma, surgical oncology, vascular surgery, minimally invasive surgery)
- Recruitment difficulty: Academic surgeons strongly prefer teaching hospitals
- Increased locum tenens costs to backfill departing faculty ($300-500/hour vs. employed physician costs)

**Revenue Loss from Reduced Surgical Volume**:
- 30-50% faculty attrition × 15-20 faculty = 5-10 departures
- Each general surgeon generates approximately $1-2M in hospital revenue annually (OR time, facility fees, inpatient admissions, post-operative care)
- **Annual revenue loss**: $5M-$10M
- **10-year NPV**: $40.6M-$81.2M (at 4% discount rate)

**Additional Costs**:
- Recruitment/retention bonuses for remaining faculty: $500K-$1M
- Locum tenens backfill: $1M-$2M annually × 2-3 years = $2M-$6M

**D. Reputational Harm and Competitive Disadvantage**

**Loss of Teaching Hospital Prestige**: While Mercy Regional would retain its 5 other residency programs (140 residents), the **loss of a core specialty program** (surgery) signals:
- Quality concerns to patients and referring physicians
- Regulatory compliance failures
- Institutional instability

**Patient Perception**: Teaching hospitals are perceived as providing higher quality care due to evidence-based medicine emphasis, access to latest treatments/technologies, and rigorous peer review. Losing surgery residency accreditation undermines this perception.

**Physician Recruitment Impact**: Physicians (both employed and community-privileged) prefer affiliations with teaching hospitals for collegial academic environment, CME opportunities, research collaboration, and prestige. Loss of surgery accreditation provides ammunition for competitors (OhioHealth, other Columbus-area systems) in physician recruitment.

**Payer Contract Negotiations**: Commercial payers may use accreditation loss as leverage to negotiate lower reimbursement rates, arguing quality concerns justify reduced payments.

**E. Resident Transfer Assistance Costs**

**Program Closure Costs**:
- Resident transfer assistance (application fees, interview travel, relocation): **$500K-$1M** (estimated $12.5K-$25K per resident × 40 residents)
- Legal fees if residents litigate breach of contract claims: **$250K-$500K**
- Faculty retention bonuses (remaining faculty): **$500K-$1M**
- Locum tenens backfill (as noted above): **$2M-$6M** over 2-3 years

**F. Potential Impact on Other Residency Programs**

**Spillover Effects**: While the other 5 programs (internal medicine, family medicine, OB/GYN, pediatrics, psychiatry) are in good standing with continued accreditation and no citations 2020-2024, the ACGME may:
- Scrutinize other programs more closely during next accreditation cycles
- Flag institutional culture concerns (work hours compliance broadly)
- Reduce institutional accreditation confidence

**Institutional Accreditation Risk**: The ACGME conducts **institutional reviews** separate from program-specific accreditation. Loss of surgery accreditation could trigger:
- Institutional site visit to assess broader compliance
- Heightened scrutiny of Designated Institutional Official (DIO) oversight
- Potential institutional probation if systemic issues identified

**Mitigating Factors**: (1) 5 of 6 programs compliant (83% program compliance rate), (2) corrective action implemented swiftly (June-August 2024), (3) institutional commitment demonstrated (substantial financial investment), (4) no prior probations in surgery or other programs (first citation in recent history).

**Conclusion**: Institutional accreditation risk is **low** (<10% probability). Surgery program probation is treated as program-specific issue, not institutional failure.

### A. ACGME Common Program Requirements: Duty Hours Standards

#### 1. The 80-Hour Weekly Limit (ACGME Core Requirement)

The cornerstone of ACGME duty hour requirements is the **80-hour weekly limit**, which has remained a strict standard since its implementation. Clinical and educational work hours must be limited to **no more than 80 hours per week, averaged over a four-week period**, inclusive of all in-house clinical and educational activities, clinical work done from home, and all moonlighting.¹

**Specific Requirements:**

- **80-hour maximum**: Clinical work periods must not exceed 80 hours per week when averaged over four weeks.²
- **Clinical work from home counts**: All clinical work done remotely must be counted toward the 80-hour weekly maximum.³
- **Continuous duty limit**: Clinical work periods for all residents must not exceed 24 hours of continuous scheduled clinical assignments.⁴
- **One day off in seven**: Residents must receive at least one day (24 hours) off per week, averaged over four weeks.⁵
- **In-house call frequency**: No more often than every three days, averaged over four weeks.⁶

**Limited Exceptions:**

Clinical work hour exceptions may be granted by the Review Committee to support specialty-specific rotations approved by the institution, for up to a maximum of **88 hours** based on sound educational rationale.⁷ However, these exceptions are rare and require explicit Review Committee approval.

**ACGME Enforcement Stance:**

With increased flexibility in certain areas, the ACGME has emphasized that **non-compliance from the 80-hour rule will not be tolerated**.⁸ The ACGME provided increased flexibility in scheduling, but emphasized strict adherence to the 80-hour maximum to optimize patient safety, resident well-being, and education.⁹

### B. ACGME Probationary Accreditation: Definition and Consequences

#### 1. Definition of Probationary Accreditation

**Probationary Accreditation** is an **adverse action** resulting from a Review Committee's determination of **substantial non-compliance** with the applicable Foundational and/or Advanced Specialty Requirements.¹⁴

Probationary Accreditation indicates a program does not demonstrate substantial compliance with the requirements due to:
- Failure to correct previous citations, OR
- New areas of non-compliance are identified¹⁵

#### 2. Impact on Residency Recruitment

The fact that **residency applicants must be notified** that the program is on probationary accreditation **negatively impacts the ability to attract good residents**.¹⁶ This creates a cascading effect:

- Lower-quality applicant pool
- Difficulty filling residency positions
- Potential impact on program reputation
- Faculty morale concerns

#### 3. Probation Duration and Review Process

Programs may be placed on probation for failure to comply with the standards.¹⁷ While on probationary accreditation:

- The ACGME conducts follow-up site visits to assess compliance
- Interviews residents to verify corrective action implementation
- Reviews program operations, including adherence to the 80-hour work week¹⁸
- Typically allows **12 months** for corrective action before reassessment

### E. Medicare Graduate Medical Education Payments: Structure and Calculations

#### 1. Legal Framework: 42 C.F.R. §§ 413.75-413.86

Medicare GME payments are established under **Section 1886(h) of the Social Security Act**, implemented in regulations at **42 C.F.R. §§ 413.75 through 413.83**.¹⁹

**Critical Requirement for Payment:**

Payments are only provided for residents who are in **"approved" programs**, essentially those **accredited by the ACGME**.²⁰ [VERIFIED: 42 C.F.R. § 413.75(b)]

**If ACGME Accreditation is Withdrawn**: CMS will **cease GME payments** for residents in that program effective immediately upon withdrawal.²¹

#### 2. Direct Graduate Medical Education (DGME) Payments

**Statutory Authority**: 42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(h); 42 C.F.R. §§ 413.75-413.83

**Payment Formula**:²²

**DGME Payment = PRA × Weighted FTE Residents × Hospital's Medicare Share of Patient Days**

Where:
- **PRA** = Per-Resident Amount (hospital-specific, inflation-adjusted)
- **FTE Residents** = Full-Time Equivalent resident count
- **Medicare Share** = Proportion of patient days attributable to Medicare beneficiaries

**Calculation Methodology** (42 C.F.R. § 413.76):²³

The hospital's **updated per resident amount** (as determined under § 413.77) is multiplied by the **actual number of FTE residents** (as determined under § 413.79), which results in the aggregate approved amount for the cost reporting period.

**Per-Resident Amount (PRA) Determination** (42 C.F.R. § 413.77):²⁴

The PRA is hospital-specific and based on the hospital's allowable GME costs in a base period divided by its number of residents in that period. If a hospital did not previously have a PRA but begins training residents, the Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) establishes a PRA effective with the hospital's first cost reporting period.

**What Costs Are Covered** (User-Provided Data):

- Resident salaries: $55,000-$65,000 (PGY1-PGY7)
- Faculty supervision costs
- Program administration
- Educational materials and resources

**Mercy Regional DGME**: **$15 million annually** (across all 6 programs, 180 residents)

#### 3. Indirect Medical Education (IME) Adjustment

**Statutory Authority**: 42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(d)(5)(K); 42 C.F.R. § 412.105

**Purpose**: CMS makes an **additional payment** to hospitals for **indirect medical education costs** beyond direct resident training expenses. The theory is that teaching hospitals incur higher operating costs due to:
- More complex patient mix (tertiary/quaternary care)
- Slower resident workflows (learning curve)
- Additional supervision requirements
- Increased diagnostic testing for educational purposes²⁵

**IME Adjustment Formula** (42 C.F.R. § 412.105(d)):²⁶

**IME Adjustment Factor = c × [(1 + r)^0.405 - 1]**

Where:
- **c** = Multiplier set by Congress (currently 1.35 for most hospitals)
- **r** = Hospital's ratio of FTE residents to beds
- **0.405** = Exponential power (statutory formula)

**Calculation Steps**:²⁷

**Step One**: A factor representing the sum of 1.00 plus the hospital's ratio of FTE residents to beds is raised to an exponential power equal to 0.405.

**Step Two**: The factor derived from step one is reduced by 1.00, then multiplied by the Congressional multiplier (c).

**Number of Beds Determination**:²⁸

The number of beds in a hospital is determined by counting the number of **available bed days** during the cost reporting period and dividing that number by the number of days in the cost reporting period.

**Application to DRG Payments**:

The IME adjustment factor is applied to **each Medicare DRG payment**. For example:
- Base DRG payment: $10,000
- IME adjustment factor: 5.89%
- IME add-on: $589
- Total payment: $10,589

**Mercy Regional IME**: **$12 million annually** (adjustment to DRG payments across all Medicare admissions)

### G. 1996 Balanced Budget Act Resident Cap: Constraints on GME Expansion

#### 1. Statutory Framework: Pub. L. 105-33, § 4623 (Aug. 5, 1997)

**Codified at**: 42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(h)(4)(F) (Direct GME cap); 42 U.S.C. § 1395ww(d)(5)(B) (IME cap)

**Purpose**: Congress capped the number of FTE residents that can be counted for purposes of **Medicare reimbursement** at the FTE count for each hospital as of **December 31, 1996**.³⁷

**Implementation**: The cap is based on the hospital's number of FTE residents for the **most recent cost reporting period before December 31, 1997**.³⁸ In subsequent years, the cap is recomputed using a **three-year rolling average**.³⁹

**Limited Exceptions**: The Secretary of HHS has **limited authority** to make exceptions to this cap.⁴⁰

#### 2. Mercy Regional's Resident Cap: 185 FTE

**Current Cap**: 185 FTE residents (established in 1996 base year)

**Current Utilization**: 180 FTE residents (97.3% of cap)
- Internal Medicine: 60
- Surgery: 40
- Family Medicine: 35
- OB/GYN: 20
- Pediatrics: 15
- Psychiatry: 10

**Available Capacity**: 5 FTE residents (2.7% headroom)

**Implication**: Mercy Regional has **minimal ability** to expand GME programs beyond current levels without qualifying for an exception.

#### 3. Exceptions to the 1996 Resident Cap

**Rural Track Programs (RTPs)**:⁴¹

Under **42 C.F.R. § 412.105(f)(1)(vii)** and **42 C.F.R. § 413.79(e)(3)**, rural hospitals may receive IME and DGME FTE cap increases **at any time** for participating in training residents in new programs.⁴²

**Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021 Expansion**:⁴³

Effective for cost reporting periods beginning on or after **October 1, 2022**, both urban and rural hospitals can receive an increase to their DGME and IME caps for the time residents spend in an RTP, provided that:
- Greater than **50% of the residents' training takes place in rural areas**⁴⁴
- Any residency program accredited by the ACGME can participate⁴⁵

**Section 5506 (Affordable Care Act) — Redistribution of Closed Hospital Slots**:⁴⁶

When a teaching hospital **closes**, its direct GME and IME FTE resident cap slots can be **redistributed** to other hospitals that meet certain criteria, rather than being "lost."⁴⁷

#### 3. Best Practices for Ensuring Successful Follow-Up

**Electronic Monitoring Compliance**:⁵⁶

- Residents must log work hours in a **timely and accurate manner** via electronic systems on a **two-week rolling basis**⁵⁷
- Programs commonly use systems like **MedHub** or **NewInnovations** to calculate work hour violations⁵⁸
- **Program Directors monitor** resident clinical and educational work hours and adjust schedules as needed to mitigate excessive service demands and/or fatigue⁵⁹

**Corrective Action Plans When Violations Identified**:⁶⁰

When violations are identified, Program Directors must decide whether a corrective action plan is required, which may involve:
- Discussions with trainees regarding their hours worked
- Adjusting call schedules
- Providing additional training on work hour compliance
- Consulting with the GME Office for further guidance⁶¹

**Faculty Staffing Solutions**:⁶²

To achieve compliance, programs have implemented various strategies:
- **Virtually all programs increased clinical responsibilities of faculty physicians**⁶³
- Some programs replaced resident services with care by **nurse practitioners, physician assistants, or hospitalists**⁶⁴
- On-call schedules for teaching staff must be structured to ensure **supervision is readily available** to residents on duty⁶⁵

**Quarterly Reporting**:

Programs must **report work hour violations to the GME office quarterly** and articulate a process for addressing those violations.⁶⁶ Summaries of findings are combined with actual annual ACGME survey data and used to develop recommended corrective actions and monitoring plans.⁶⁷

**Faculty Monitoring Responsibilities**:⁶⁸

Faculty must **remain vigilant** of the trainees under their supervision and **frequently monitor** the residents' activities.⁶⁹

**ACGME Enforcement**:

The ACGME Board reaffirmed its **strict interpretation of the 80-hour rule**, and Review Committees are **issuing citations where violations of the 80-hour limit are identified**.⁷⁰ Repeated work hour flags correlate with **lower compliance rates** on the ACGME Resident/Fellow Survey, which leads to ACGME citations.⁷¹

All **5 other residency programs** (internal medicine, family medicine, OB/GYN, pediatrics, psychiatry) totaling 140 residents are in good standing with **continued accreditation** and **no citations 2020-2024**. #### Ongoing Compliance Recommendations (Post-Closing)

**For Buyer (National Healthcare Partners) Post-Acquisition**:

1. **Maintain Corrective Actions for Minimum 24 Months Post-Accreditation Restoration**

Even if full accreditation is restored in Q2 2025, the buyer should:
- **Retain 2 additional surgical faculty** through at least Q2 2027 (24 months post-restoration)
- **Continue electronic work hours monitoring** (NewInnovations or equivalent system)
- **Maintain Associate Program Director role** for compliance oversight

**Rationale**: ACGME typically monitors programs for 2-3 years post-restoration to ensure sustained compliance. Premature reduction in resources could trigger new citations during subsequent accreditation cycles.

2. **Quarterly ACGME Compliance Reporting to Buyer's Board**

Establish governance oversight:
- GME office submits quarterly reports to hospital board
- Include: Work hours compliance rates, resident survey results, faculty adequacy, surgical volume trends
- **Red flags**: If compliance rates drop below 95%, trigger corrective action review

3. **Annual ACGME Site Visit Preparation**

Treat every accreditation cycle as a compliance audit:
- Mock site visits 6 months before scheduled ACGME visits
- Pre-visit document review (resident files, work hours logs, curriculum documentation)
- Resident interview preparation (ensure residents comfortable discussing work hours truthfully)

4. **Surgical Volume Management**

Monitor surgical volume prospectively:
- If volume increases beyond baseline, assess faculty adequacy **prospectively**
- Hire additional faculty or implement night float system **before** residents exceed 80-hour limit
- **Preventive approach** is less costly than probation remediation

5. **DIO Institutional Oversight**

Strengthen Designated Institutional Official (DIO) oversight across all 6 programs:
- Monthly GMEC meetings with program directors
- Proactive work hours monitoring (not just reactive to violations)
- Institutional culture of resident well-being (not just compliance)

6. **Resident Wellness Initiatives**

Invest in resident well-being programs:
- Mental health resources (confidential counseling, stress management)
- Fatigue mitigation strategies (call room improvements, protected sleep time)
- Resident engagement in schedule design (input on call schedules, elective rotations)

**Rationale**: ACGME views work hours compliance as a **proxy for resident well-being**. Programs that prioritize wellness holistically (not just tracking hours) have better accreditation outcomes.⁸⁷

7. **Faculty Development for Teaching**

Invest in faculty development:
- Teaching skills training (effective supervision, feedback)
- Work-life balance for faculty (prevent burnout that leads to over-reliance on residents)
- Academic productivity support (research time, scholarly activity)

**Rationale**: High-quality faculty who are engaged in teaching reduce reliance on residents for service coverage, improving work hours compliance.⁸⁸
