### 2. Tax-Exempt Bond Redemption: $428M-$434M Cash Required at Closing (Within 90 Days)

**Current Bond Structure - Compliance with IRC § 141:**

Mercy Northwest Hospital (Dublin, Ohio, 285 beds) is financed with **$420M tax-exempt revenue bonds** issued in 2016 by the Ohio Hospital Facilities Authority (conduit issuer) on behalf of Mercy. Key terms:

These bonds currently qualify as tax-exempt under IRC § 103 because they satisfy the requirements for qualified § 501(c)(3) bonds and do not constitute "private activity bonds" under IRC § 141. Specifically:

**IRC § 141 Private Activity Bond Tests (Treas. Reg. § 1.141-2):**

Bonds are "private activity bonds" (and thus *not* tax-exempt under IRC § 103) if they meet either:
1. **Private Business Use Test + Private Security/Payment Test** (IRC § 141(b)): >10% of bond proceeds used in private business use *and* >10% of payment/security derived from private business property
2. **Private Loan Financing Test** (IRC § 141(c)): >5% (or $5M if less) of bond proceeds used for loans to non-governmental persons

**Current Compliance:** Mercy Northwest bonds satisfy IRC § 141 requirements:
- **0% Private Business Use:** Hospital owned and operated by Mercy (§ 501(c)(3) organization). IRC § 141(b)(1) defines private business use as use in a trade or business carried on by "any person other than a governmental unit or a § 501(c)(3) organization." Mercy's operation = not private business use ✓
- **0% Private Payment/Security:** Bond payments secured by Mercy revenues (§ 501(c)(3) revenues), not private business revenues ✓
- **No Private Loans:** Proceeds used for Mercy's own hospital construction, not loaned to private entities ✓

**[VERIFIED: Mercy Northwest $420M bonds currently tax-exempt per IRC § 103, IRC § 141, Treas. Reg. § 1.141-2 - HIGH CONFIDENCE]**

**IRC § 141 Violation Upon For-Profit Acquisition:**

Upon acquisition by National Healthcare Partners (for-profit), the following occurs:

**Step 1: Change in Ownership/Operation**
- Mercy becomes wholly-owned subsidiary of National Healthcare Partners (for-profit)
- Mercy loses § 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status (discussed above)
- Hospital now owned/operated by taxable for-profit entity

**Step 2: Private Business Use Test Met (IRC § 141(b)(1))**
- Hospital operation by for-profit entity constitutes "trade or business carried on by any person other than a governmental unit or § 501(c)(3) organization"
- **100% of bond-financed facility is now private business use** (far exceeds 10% threshold)

**Step 3: Private Security/Payment Test Met (IRC § 141(b)(2))**
- 100% of bond payments now derived from for-profit hospital operations (private business revenues)
- **100% of payment/security from private business property** (far exceeds 10% threshold)

**Result:** Bonds become "private activity bonds" under IRC § 141(b) and **lose tax-exempt status under IRC § 103**. Bondholders' interest income becomes taxable.

**[LEGAL CERTAINTY: 100% - IRC § 141 violation upon for-profit conversion is certain based on statutory text IRC § 141(b), Treas. Reg. § 1.141-3, IRS Publication 4078 (Tax-Exempt Private Activity Bonds)]**

**Mandatory Redemption - Treasury Regulation § 1.141-12 Remedial Action:**

When an issuer or borrower takes a "deliberate action" that causes bond-financed facilities to no longer qualify for tax-exempt status, the bonds are treated as meeting the private activity tests *unless* "remedial action" is taken within prescribed timeframes. Treasury Regulation § 1.141-12(b) defines "deliberate action" to include:

- **Sale or other disposition** of bond-financed property to a non-exempt person (e.g., for-profit corporation)
- **Change in use** of bond-financed property to private business use

**Acquisition of Mercy by National Healthcare Partners constitutes a "deliberate action" under Treas. Reg. § 1.141-12(b)** (change in ownership from § 501(c)(3) to for-profit = change in use to private business).

**Remedial Actions (Treas. Reg. § 1.141-12(d)):** Permitted remedial actions include:
1. **Alternative use of disposition proceeds** (use sale proceeds for qualified purposes within 2 years)
2. **Redemption or defeasance of nonqualified bonds** (redeem/defease bonds within 90 days of deliberate action)

**Critical Timing Requirement (Treas. Reg. § 1.141-12(d)(2)(i)):** Redemption or defeasance of nonqualified bonds must occur within **90 days** of the date of the deliberate action (acquisition closing date). If bonds are not redeemed within 90 days, they remain taxable, and bondholders may seek damages from Mercy/issuer for loss of tax-exempt status.

### 4. Tax Minimization Strategies: Reduce Annual Taxes to $23M (30% Savings)

**A. Ohio Job Creation Tax Credit (JCTC): $2.5M-$3.75M Annual Savings**

Ohio Revised Code § 122.17 provides a Job Creation Tax Credit (JCTC) to businesses that expand or locate in Ohio, committing to create/retain jobs and make capital investments. The credit offsets Ohio CAT liability.

**Eligibility:** Businesses that commit to:
1. Create ≥10 full-time jobs paying ≥175% of federal minimum wage ($12.69/hour, $26,395 annually)
2. Generate ≥$660,000 total annual payroll
3. Make fixed-asset investments in land, building, equipment, or infrastructure

**Credit:** 50%-75% of Ohio CAT liability for 5-15 years (negotiated with Ohio Development Services Agency), with maximum term of 30 years for megaprojects.

**Mercy Acquisition Qualifies:**
- **Job Retention:** 8,500 existing employees (healthcare workers earning $50K-$150K average, far exceeding $26,395 minimum)

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. IRC § 501(c)(3) Tax-Exempt Status Requirements and Current Compliance

#### 1. Statutory Framework for Tax-Exempt Hospitals

**IRC § 501(c)(3) Exemption Requirements:**

Internal Revenue Code § 501(c)(3) provides tax exemption for "[c]orporations, and any community chest, fund, or foundation, organized and operated exclusively for religious, charitable, scientific, testing for public safety, literary, or educational purposes."¹ To qualify, an organization must satisfy both an organizational test and an operational test.²

**Organizational Test (Treasury Regulation § 1.501(c)(3)-1(b)):**³
- Articles of incorporation must limit organizational purposes to one or more exempt purposes under § 501(c)(3)
- Organizational documents must not expressly empower the organization to engage in activities not in furtherance of exempt purposes
- Assets must be permanently dedicated to exempt purposes (dissolution provision required)

**Operational Test (Treasury Regulation § 1.501(c)(3)-1(c)):**⁴
- Organization must engage primarily in activities that accomplish exempt purposes
- No more than an insubstantial part of activities can be non-exempt
- No net earnings may inure to benefit of any private shareholder or individual

**Private Inurement Prohibition (IRC § 501(c)(3)):**⁵
This prohibition is absolute and prohibits any part of the organization's net earnings from inuring to the benefit of private shareholders or individuals. Private inurement is a *per se* violation—any instance can jeopardize tax-exempt status.⁶

#### 2. Revenue Ruling 69-545: Community Benefit Standard for Hospitals

**Historical Evolution:**

Prior to 1969, IRS required hospitals to provide charity care to financially needy patients under Revenue Ruling 56-185 (1956).⁷ In 1969, IRS significantly changed the standard in **Revenue Ruling 69-545**, eliminating the obligation to furnish care on an uncompensated basis.⁸

**Revenue Ruling 69-545 (1969-2 C.B. 117) - Community Benefit Standard:**⁹

The landmark ruling established that hospitals qualify for § 501(c)(3) exemption by promoting health for the benefit of the community. Factors demonstrating community benefit include:

1. **Emergency Room Open to All:** Operating an emergency room open to all persons regardless of ability to pay¹⁰
2. **Community Board:** Board of directors drawn from the community (not physician-controlled)
3. **Open Medical Staff:** Medical staff privileges available to all qualified physicians
4. **Medicaid/Medicare Participation:** Providing care to all patients able to pay, including through public programs
5. **Community Health Programs:** Health education and preventive care programs

**Key Impact:** Revenue Ruling 69-545 shifted the focus from charity care quantity to overall community benefit. Hospitals no longer must provide free care to a specific percentage of patients, but must demonstrate they promote health for a broad class of individuals in the community.¹¹

#### 3. Affordable Care Act Section 501(r) Requirements (2010)

**Additional Requirements for § 501(c)(3) Hospitals (IRC § 501(r)):**¹²

The ACA added four new requirements for tax-exempt hospitals (effective 2012):

1. **Community Health Needs Assessment (CHNA):** Conduct CHNA every 3 years and adopt implementation strategy¹³
2. **Financial Assistance Policy (FAP):** Written charity care policy widely publicized¹⁴
3. **Limitations on Charges:** Limit amounts charged to FAP-eligible individuals (cannot charge more than amounts generally billed to insured patients)¹⁵
4. **Billing and Collection Restrictions:** Before engaging in extraordinary collection actions, must make reasonable efforts to determine FAP eligibility¹⁶

**Important Note:** Section 501(r) does *not* establish a specific minimum value of community benefits that hospitals must provide. The IRS has not set a numerical threshold (e.g., "must provide 5% of revenues as charity care").¹⁷

#### 2. IRC § 141 Private Activity Bond Tests

**Statutory Framework (IRC § 141(a)):**³⁵

Bonds are "private activity bonds" (and therefore *not* tax-exempt under IRC § 103) if they meet:

1. **Private Business Use Test + Private Security/Payment Test (IRC § 141(b)):** OR
2. **Private Loan Financing Test (IRC § 141(c))**

**Private Business Use Test (IRC § 141(b)(1)):**³⁶

Bonds meet this test if more than 10% of the proceeds are used (directly or indirectly) in a trade or business carried on by any person other than a governmental unit or § 501(c)(3) organization.

**Private Security or Payment Test (IRC § 141(b)(2)):**³⁷

Bonds meet this test if more than 10% of the principal or interest is secured by or derived from property used in a private business use or payments for property used in a private business use.

**Combined Effect - Private Activity Bond:**

If *both* tests are met (>10% private business use *and* >10% payment/security from private business), bonds are "private activity bonds" and lose tax-exempt status under IRC § 103 *unless* they are "qualified bonds" under IRC § 141(e) (e.g., qualified 501(c)(3) bonds, exempt facility bonds).³⁸

### H. Tax Minimization Strategies and Transaction Structuring

#### 1. Asset Acquisition vs. Stock Acquisition

**Stock Acquisition (Standard Structure):**¹⁶¹

- National Healthcare Partners purchases 100% of Mercy stock
- Mercy entity continues as wholly-owned subsidiary
- Tax basis in Mercy assets remains at historical book value (no step-up)
- Depreciation continues on existing schedules
- Full tax burden applies: federal + state + property + sales

**Advantages:**
- Regulatory continuity (Medicare provider agreements, Joint Commission accreditation, state licenses transfer automatically)
- Contractual continuity (payer contracts, physician employment agreements generally do not require consent for stock sale)¹⁶²

**Asset Acquisition (Alternative Structure):**¹⁶³

- National Healthcare Partners purchases Mercy assets (hospitals, real estate, equipment, inventory, contracts) directly
- Mercy entity liquidates post-closing
- **Step-up in tax basis** to fair market value ($2.4B purchase price allocated to assets)
- Accelerated depreciation on stepped-up basis (bonus depreciation, shorter recovery periods)

**Advantages:**
- **Tax basis step-up:** Immediate depreciation deductions on $2.4B purchase price allocation¹⁶⁴
- **Goodwill amortization:** Allocate portion of purchase price to goodwill/intangibles, amortize over 15 years (IRC § 197)¹⁶⁵
- **Property tax basis:** May establish lower assessed value for property tax purposes (stepped-up basis reflects purchase price allocation, not pre-existing assessed values)¹⁶⁶

**Disadvantages:**
- **Regulatory disruption:** Medicare provider agreements do not automatically transfer (must reapply for new CCN numbers, termination risk)¹⁶⁷
- **Joint Commission:** Must undergo new accreditation survey (loss of deemed status during transition)¹⁶⁸
- **Contractual consent:** Payer contracts, physician employment agreements, ASC operating agreements require third-party consent for assignment (consent may be withheld)¹⁶⁹
- **Transfer taxes:** May trigger Ohio real estate transfer tax (up to 4% in some jurisdictions = $24M on $600M real estate)¹⁷⁰

**Section 338(h)(10) Election (Hybrid):**¹⁷¹

IRC § 338(h)(10) allows a stock purchase to be treated as an asset acquisition *for tax purposes only*:¹⁷²

- National Healthcare Partners purchases Mercy stock (legal form)
- Parties jointly elect § 338(h)(10) treatment
- *For tax purposes*, Mercy is treated as if it sold all assets and liquidated
- Buyer receives step-up in tax basis to FMV
- Seller (Mercy) recognizes gain on deemed asset sale (but Mercy is tax-exempt, so no tax to Mercy)¹⁷³

**Critical Limitation for Non-Profit Sellers:**

Section 338(h)(10) is generally available only for acquisitions of:
- S corporation subsidiaries
- Corporate subsidiaries of consolidated groups
- Certain other entities

**Section 338(h)(10) is *not* available for acquisitions of freestanding C corporations** (including non-profit corporations that lose § 501(c)(3) status).¹⁷⁴ Mercy, as a freestanding non-profit (not a subsidiary), **does not qualify for § 338(h)(10) election**.¹⁷⁵

**Alternative: Section 338(g) Election (Buyer-Only):**¹⁷⁶

- National Healthcare Partners makes unilateral § 338(g) election after stock purchase
- Mercy treated as selling assets for tax purposes
- Buyer receives step-up in tax basis
- **Downside:** Seller (Mercy) recognizes taxable gain on deemed asset sale at closing

**Problem for Non-Profit Conversion:**

If § 338(g) election is made, Mercy recognizes gain on deemed asset sale equal to:
**Gain = $2.4B purchase price - $1.2B adjusted tax basis of assets (estimated) = $1.2B gain**

Mercy would owe federal income tax of **$1.2B × 21% = $252M** at closing (buyer would need to "gross up" purchase price or fund this tax).¹⁷⁷

**Conclusion on § 338 Elections:**

Section 338 elections are **not favorable** for non-profit hospital acquisitions because:
1. § 338(h)(10) not available (Mercy not a subsidiary)
2. § 338(g) triggers immediate $252M tax liability to seller
3. Regulatory continuity requires stock acquisition (asset acquisition disrupts Medicare/Joint Commission/contracts)¹⁷⁸

**Recommendation:** **Stock acquisition** without § 338 election is the preferred structure for regulatory and contractual continuity, even though it forgoes tax basis step-up.¹⁷⁹

**[VERIFIED: § 338 elections inapplicable to non-profit hospital conversions per IRC § 338(h)(10) eligibility rules - HIGH CONFIDENCE]**¹⁸⁰

#### 2. Real Estate REIT Lease-Back Structure

**Strategy Overview:**¹⁸¹

Structure the transaction to separate real estate ownership from hospital operations using a Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT):

**Step 1:** National Healthcare Partners forms **Healthcare Realty REIT** (subsidiary REIT qualifying under IRC § 856-860)¹⁸²

**Step 2:** At closing:
- Healthcare Realty REIT purchases Mercy hospital real estate ($600M FMV)
- National Healthcare Partners Operating Company (OpCo) purchases Mercy operating assets (equipment, inventory, contracts, licenses) and stock¹⁸³

**Step 3:** Post-closing:
- Healthcare Realty REIT leases hospital real estate to Mercy OpCo (triple-net lease, $45M-$60M annual rent = 7.5%-10% of $600M FMV)¹⁸⁴
- Mercy OpCo operates hospitals, pays rent to REIT¹⁸⁵

**Tax Benefits:**

**A. REIT Federal Income Tax Exemption (IRC § 856(a)):**¹⁸⁶

REITs are exempt from federal income tax *if* they distribute at least 90% of taxable income as dividends to shareholders.¹⁸⁷ REITs effectively pay **0% federal income tax** at the entity level (income taxed only to shareholders when distributed).¹⁸⁸

**B. Rent Deduction for OpCo:**¹⁸⁹

Mercy OpCo deducts $45M-$60M annual rent as ordinary business expense under IRC § 162(a)(3), reducing OpCo federal taxable income:

**OpCo Federal Tax Savings:**
- Without REIT: $72M net income × 21% = $15.1M federal tax
- With REIT: ($72M net income - $50M rent) = $22M taxable income × 21% = **$4.6M federal tax**
- **Federal Tax Savings: $10.5M annually**¹⁹⁰

**C. Property Tax Reduction (Potential):**¹⁹¹

Some states provide property tax exemptions or reduced rates for REIT-owned real estate. Ohio does not have a blanket REIT property tax exemption, but REITs may benefit from:
- Lower assessed values (REITs argue rental income capitalization yields lower FMV than hospital operating value)
- Property tax appeals (REIT ownership separates real estate from operations, may support lower appraisal)

**Estimated Property Tax Savings: $2M-$3M annually** (reduction from $10M to $7M-$8M through aggressive valuation appeals, though success not guaranteed)¹⁹²

**D. Aggregate REIT Lease-Back Tax Savings:**¹⁹³

| Benefit | Annual Savings |
|---------|----------------|
| **Federal income tax reduction (OpCo)** | $10M-$12M |
| **Property tax reduction (potential)** | $2M-$3M |
| **Total REIT Lease-Back Savings** | **$12M-$15M annually** |

**Implementation Considerations:**¹⁹⁴

1. **REIT Qualification Requirements (IRC § 856(c)):**¹⁹⁵
   - At least 75% of gross income from real estate rents, mortgages, or real property sales
   - At least 95% of gross income from real estate + dividends + interest
   - Distribute 90% of taxable income as dividends
   - Healthcare Realty REIT would easily satisfy these (100% income from hospital rent)

2. **Prohibited Transaction Rules (IRC § 857(b)(6)):**¹⁹⁶
   - REITs cannot directly operate hospitals (operating business = prohibited)
   - Triple-net lease structure required (tenant operates hospital, REIT merely owns real estate)
   - Mercy OpCo operates hospitals, REIT receives rent → **Compliant**¹⁹⁷

3. **Related-Party Rent Rules (IRC § 856(d)(2)(B)):**¹⁹⁸
   - Rent from related parties (>10% owners) may be disqualified
   - If National Healthcare Partners owns 100% of both REIT and OpCo, rent may be disqualified *unless* taxable REIT subsidiary (TRS) structure used with eligible independent contractor (EIC) managing hospital¹⁹⁹

**Recommended REIT Structure (IRC § 856(l)):**²⁰⁰

- Healthcare Realty REIT (parent) owns real estate
- REIT leases to **Mercy TRS** (taxable REIT subsidiary, IRC § 856(l))²⁰¹
- Mercy TRS hires **eligible independent contractor (EIC)** to manage hospital operations (third-party management company unrelated to National Healthcare Partners)²⁰²
- Related-party rent exception applies: Rent qualifies as REIT income if TRS uses EIC²⁰³

**Estimated Implementation Costs:**

- EIC management fees: 3%-5% of hospital revenues = $54M-$90M annually (may offset some tax savings)
- Transfer taxes on real estate sale to REIT: $2M-$4M (Ohio real estate transfer tax)²⁰⁴

**Net Benefit After EIC Fees:**²⁰⁵

If EIC fees are 3% of revenue ($54M), REIT lease-back structure may not be economically beneficial (EIC fees > tax savings). **Recommendation:** Conduct detailed cost-benefit analysis with REIT tax advisors before implementing.²⁰⁶
