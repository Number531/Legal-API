## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This report analyzes Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA) compliance risks for Mercy Regional Health System's $2.4 billion acquisition by National Healthcare Partners LLC. The analysis focuses on a confirmed July 2023 EMTALA violation at Mercy East Hospital (financial inquiry before patient transfer, $50,000 civil monetary penalty paid), assesses system-wide compliance across all four Mercy hospitals (1,285 beds, 285,000 annual ED visits), and evaluates pattern violation risk that could trigger Medicare provider agreement termination—a deal-blocking scenario given Medicare represents 70% of Mercy's $1.8 billion net patient revenue.

**Key Finding:** The July 2023 violation involved business office staff asking an uninsured STEMI patient about insurance and stating "transfers can be expensive, you may owe $50,000" 35 minutes before transfer was arranged, violating 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(h)'s prohibition on delaying care to inquire about payment. While the patient ultimately received successful treatment at Mercy Regional Medical Center with no adverse medical outcome, EMTALA imposes strict liability for procedural violations regardless of patient harm (*Roberts v. Galen of Va.*, 525 U.S. 249 (1999)). CMS assessed a $50,000 penalty in August 2024, and Mercy East implemented corrective action (financial inquiry prohibition policy, staff training, monthly audits) in September 2024, with 100% compliance sustained through January 2026.

**Critical Risk:** The primary transaction risk is **pattern violations**. If due diligence uncovers 2+ additional EMTALA violations at any Mercy hospital during 2020-2024 (beyond the disclosed July 2023 incident), CMS may determine a pattern exists, escalating civil monetary penalties to $200,000-$300,000 and triggering Medicare termination proceedings. Medicare termination would eliminate $1.26 billion in annual revenue (70% of net patient revenue), rendering the hospitals non-operational and constituting a deal-blocking event. As of this report, only the single July 2023 violation has been disclosed; comprehensive due diligence is required to verify no additional violations exist.

**Exposure Summary:**

### Key Takeaways

**1. July 2023 Violation Fully Remediated but Represents Systemic Risk**

On July 15, 2023, at 11:55 PM, business office staff at Mercy East Hospital approached Jane Doe, a 35-year-old patient diagnosed with STEMI (acute heart attack), and inquired about her insurance status before transfer to Mercy Regional's cardiac catheterization lab was arranged. When Jane Doe disclosed she was uninsured ("I lost my job last month"), staff stated, "Transfers can be expensive, you may owe $50,000 for ambulance and hospital bills." Transfer was not arranged until 12:30 AM (35 minutes later), though the patient ultimately received successful treatment with no permanent cardiac damage.

CMS Region 5 investigated the incident following an anonymous complaint and issued findings in August 2024 confirming violation of **42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(h)**, which prohibits hospitals from delaying medical screening examination or treatment "in order to inquire about the individual's method of payment or insurance status." The 35-minute gap between financial inquiry (11:55 PM) and transfer arrangement (12:30 AM) constituted impermissible delay, and the "$50,000" statement "unduly discouraged" the patient in violation of CMS interpretive guidelines (State Operations Manual Appendix V). CMS assessed a $50,000 civil monetary penalty under 42 CFR § 1003.500, which Mercy East paid in August 2024.

**Corrective Action Effectiveness:** Mercy East implemented a comprehensive corrective action plan in September 2024 including: (1) financial inquiry prohibition policy (no financial discussions until patient stabilized or discharged), (2) 100% staff training completion (ED clinical, registration, business office staff), and (3) monthly chart audits (20 charts/month minimum). Monthly audits from September 2024 through January 2026 demonstrate 100% compliance—no financial inquiries documented before stabilization or discharge. CMS accepted the corrective action plan with no follow-up citations, and no Medicare provider agreement termination threat was issued.

**Systemic Concern:** The violation reflects **organizational culture risk** rather than isolated individual misconduct. Business office staff (non-clinical personnel) approached the patient before clinical team completed the transfer arrangement, suggesting inadequate training on EMTALA's financial inquiry prohibition. While corrective action at Mercy East appears effective, the critical unknown is whether the same financial counseling practices existed system-wide at Mercy Regional, Northwest, and South hospitals prior to September 2024, and whether the corrective action policy was implemented **system-wide** or only at Mercy East where the violation was discovered.

**2. Pattern Violation Risk Is Transaction-Critical but Unquantifiable Without Full Lookback**

EMTALA enforcement is **complaint-driven**, not audit-based. CMS does not conduct random EMTALA compliance audits; investigations are triggered by complaints from patients, other hospitals, or whistleblowers. Consequently, hospitals may have unreported EMTALA violations that have not yet generated CMS complaints. The July 2023 Mercy East violation was discovered only because an anonymous complaint was filed; absent that complaint, the violation would remain undisclosed.

**Pattern Violation Consequences:** CMS enforcement practice (though not formally codified) treats **2+ violations within 3 years as a pattern**, escalating sanctions:
- **First violation:** $50,000-$133,000 CMP + corrective action plan required
- **Second violation (within 3 years):** $100,000-$300,000 elevated CMP + intensified CMS monitoring
- **Third violation (within 3 years):** Medicare termination threat + 90-day termination notice

**2024-2025 Enforcement Data Analysis:** Review of HHS-OIG enforcement actions (January 2024–January 2026) reveals 20 hospitals/physicians penalized, with **Baptist Medical Center South** receiving the highest hospital penalty ($290,000 in February 2025) for **three separate violations** (approximately $97,000 per violation, elevated from typical $50,000-$66,000 baseline). This demonstrates that pattern violations drive substantially higher penalties and termination risk.

**Mercy's Pattern Risk Status:**
- **Known:** 1 violation (July 2023 Mercy East financial inquiry, $50,000 CMP paid)
- **Unknown:** Any additional EMTALA violations 2020-2024 at Mercy Regional, Northwest, East, or South?
- **If 1 additional violation found:** Total 2 violations = pattern concern, elevated CMP $100,000-$150,000 for second violation, moderate Medicare termination risk (10-15%)
- **If 2+ additional violations found:** Total 3+ violations = pattern established, CMS termination threat highly probable (30-40% risk), deal-blocking scenario

**Due Diligence Imperative:** Acquirer **must** obtain the following documents before finalizing transaction:
1. All CMS Region 5 survey reports, findings letters, EMTALA investigation correspondence (2020-2024, all 4 hospitals)
2. Ohio Department of Health patient complaint records (2020-2024, all 4 hospitals)
3. Hospital incident reports/quality reviews identifying potential EMTALA issues (2020-2024)
4. Patient complaint logs alleging financial pressure, delayed care, transfer issues (2020-2024)
5. ED transfer logs documenting all transfers out, transfers refused/delayed, reasons (2020-2024)
6. Mercy Regional transfer acceptance logs (transfers accepted, transfers refused, capacity issues, 2020-2024)

If this lookback reveals **zero additional violations**, risk is limited to the single July 2023 incident with low ongoing exposure ($25,000-$50,000 5-year post-closing risk). If additional violations are discovered, exposure escalates exponentially due to pattern consequences.

**3. Medicare Termination = Deal-Blocking Event ($1.26B Annual Revenue Loss)**

The ultimate EMTALA sanction is **termination of Medicare provider agreement** under 42 CFR § 489.53. CMS may terminate a hospital's Medicare participation for either: (1) a single egregious violation (patient death, gross negligence), or (2) a pattern of violations indicating systemic noncompliance. Termination eliminates Medicare and Medicaid reimbursement, triggers exclusion from Medicare Advantage and Medicaid managed care networks, and effectively forces hospital closure—no hospital can operate without Medicare participation.

**Mercy's Medicare Dependency:**
- **Medicare Traditional FFS:** 38% of net patient revenue = $684M annually
- **Medicare Advantage:** 10% of net patient revenue = $180M annually
- **Total Medicare:** 48% of net patient revenue = $864M annually
- **Medicaid:** 22% of net patient revenue = $396M annually
- **Combined Medicare/Medicaid:** 70% of net patient revenue = **$1.26 billion annually**

Termination of Medicare provider agreements for any of the four Mercy hospitals would eliminate this revenue stream, rendering the affected hospital(s) non-operational. For a $2.4 billion acquisition, loss of $1.26 billion in annual revenue (representing the bulk of operating cash flow) constitutes a **deal-blocking scenario**—no acquirer can operate hospitals without Medicare participation.

**Current Termination Risk Assessment:**
- **Base Case (No Pattern):** **LOW risk** (5-10%). Single July 2023 violation with accepted corrective action, no repeat violations since September 2024, no CMS termination threat issued. Standard CMS practice is to accept corrective action plans for first-time violations absent patient harm.
- **Pattern Case (2+ Violations):** **MEDIUM-HIGH risk** (30-40%). If additional violations discovered establishing pattern, CMS likely issues 90-day termination notice. Hospitals can contest termination through corrective action, but pattern violations elevate scrutiny. Termination not automatic but becomes material risk.
- **Active Proceedings:** If CMS has already issued termination notice for any hospital (not disclosed in facts provided), risk is **CRITICAL** (60-80%). Transaction should be delayed pending resolution or restructured to exclude hospital under termination threat.

**Transaction Impact Mechanisms:**
1. **Material Adverse Change (MAC):** Medicare termination satisfies MAC definition, giving buyer right to terminate purchase agreement or renegotiate price downward
2. **Closing Condition Failure:** Recommended closing condition requires valid Medicare provider agreements in good standing; termination proceedings prevent satisfaction
3. **Indemnification/Escrow:** Recommend $5 million EMTALA escrow to cover pattern violation CMPs ($200,000-$350,000) + tail risk of undiscovered liabilities; escrow released 18 months post-closing if no additional violations emerge
4. **Indemnification Cap Carve-Out:** Medicare termination indemnification should be uncapped (or capped at 50% of purchase price = $1.2B) given deal-blocking magnitude; standard 20% general indemnification cap ($480M) insufficient for Medicare termination scenario

**4. System-Wide Compliance Gaps Require Pre-Closing Remediation**

The July 2023 corrective action was implemented at **Mercy East only** (based on disclosed facts). Four critical system-wide compliance gaps require verification and remediation before closing:

**Gap #1: Financial Inquiry Policy Uniformity**
- **Issue:** Unknown whether September 2024 financial inquiry prohibition policy implemented system-wide at all 4 hospitals or only at Mercy East where violation occurred
- **Risk:** If Mercy Regional, Northwest, South continue pre-violation financial counseling practices, identical violations may occur
- **Recommendation:** Verify uniform policy adoption system-wide; if not implemented, require seller to implement at all hospitals **before closing** as closing condition

**Gap #2: On-Call Specialist Compliance (1,200 Community Physicians)**
- **Issue:** Mercy system includes 1,200 community physicians with privileges who serve on-call rotations. Under *Burditt v. U.S. Dep't of Health & Human Servs.*, 934 F.2d 1362 (5th Cir. 1991), on-call specialists cannot refuse to evaluate/treat ED patients based on insurance status or ability to pay. Unknown whether on-call physicians receive EMTALA training or have refused patients based on financial status.
- **Risk:** On-call refusals trigger **both hospital penalties** ($133,420 per violation, 2025 inflation-adjusted) **and physician penalties** ($133,420 per violation). First physician penalty under EMTALA was $20,000 against Dr. Burditt for refusing to treat pregnant patient with severe hypertension due to financial concerns.
- **Recommendation:** Verify all on-call physicians (employed + community) complete annual EMTALA training; audit on-call logs 2020-2024 for any documented refusals based on insurance status; ensure on-call agreements prohibit financial refusals

**Gap #3: Transfer Acceptance Obligations (Mercy Regional as Receiving Hospital)**
- **Issue:** Mercy Regional Medical Center serves as **receiving hospital** for cardiac, neuro, and trauma transfers from Mercy East, Northwest, and South (which lack specialized capabilities). Under 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(g), hospitals with specialized capabilities must **accept appropriate transfers** when capacity available; refusal violates EMTALA.
- **Risk:** 2024-2025 enforcement data shows receiving hospital refusal penalties are **elevated** ($150,000-$350,000 range), with Brentwood Behavioral Healthcare paying $350,000 (May 2025) and Flowers Hospital paying $150,000 (July 2025) for transfer refusal violations. Unknown whether Mercy Regional has ever refused transfers (including intra-system transfers from other Mercy hospitals) due to capacity, insurance, or other factors.
- **Recommendation:** Audit Mercy Regional transfer acceptance logs 2020-2024; verify no inappropriate refusals; implement capacity management protocols to ensure accepting hospital obligations met

**Gap #4: Proactive Monitoring (Not Just Complaint-Driven)**
- **Issue:** CMS enforcement is complaint-driven (reactive), not audit-based (proactive). Hospitals relying solely on CMS surveys to identify violations will miss incidents that don't generate complaints. Best practice is proactive internal monitoring to identify and remediate EMTALA near-miss events before CMS complaints filed.
- **Risk:** Unreported violations may exist that have not yet generated CMS complaints; violations discovered post-closing become acquirer liabilities
- **Recommendation:** Implement system-wide EMTALA compliance monitoring: monthly ED chart audits (all 4 hospitals), quarterly patient complaint review for EMTALA-related allegations, annual on-call specialist compliance review, incident reporting system for near-miss events with EMTALA whistleblower protections (42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(i))

**5. Private Equity Acquirer Revenue Cycle Risk**

National Healthcare Partners LLC (private equity-backed, for-profit) will likely implement **revenue cycle optimization** post-closing to improve collections and reduce bad debt (currently $150M annually). However, revenue cycle enhancements in emergency departments create **direct conflict with EMTALA's financial inquiry prohibition**. Common private equity revenue cycle strategies that violate EMTALA include:
- **Pre-service registration:** Requiring patients to complete financial/insurance forms before medical screening examination
- **Point-of-service deposits:** Demanding deposits or credit card authorization before treatment
- **Aggressive financial counseling:** Business office staff approaching ED patients during treatment to discuss payment options
- **Credit checks:** Running credit reports to assess patient's ability to pay before stabilization

**EMTALA Compliance Requirement:** 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(h) prohibits **all** financial inquiries before patient stabilized or discharged, except basic insurance identification concurrent with triage (name, date of birth, insurance company name) that does not delay care. Financial counseling, payment discussions, credit assessments **must** occur **after** patient stabilized/discharged, not during ED visit.

**Post-Closing Covenant Recommendation:** Purchase agreement should require acquirer to: (1) conduct EMTALA compliance review **before** implementing any revenue cycle changes in emergency departments, (2) maintain Mercy's September 2024 corrective action plan (financial inquiry prohibition) for minimum 3 years post-closing, (3) provide seller with notice/approval rights over revenue cycle changes that could implicate EMTALA (to protect seller's indemnification obligations for pre-closing conduct).

### Statutory and Regulatory Citations Summary

**Primary EMTALA Authority:**
- **42 U.S.C. § 1395dd** — Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA) statutory requirements
- **42 CFR § 489.24** — Special responsibilities of Medicare hospitals in emergency cases (regulatory implementation)
- **42 CFR § 1003.500** — Civil monetary penalties for EMTALA violations (OIG enforcement authority)
- **42 CFR § 489.53** — Termination of Medicare provider agreements (CMS termination authority)

**Key Statutory Provisions Cited:**
- **§ 1395dd(a)** — Medical screening examination (MSE) requirement
- **§ 1395dd(b)** — Stabilization requirement for emergency medical conditions
- **§ 1395dd(c)** — Restricting transfer until stabilization + appropriate transfer requirements
- **§ 1395dd(d)** — Penalties: hospital CMPs ($50K-$133K), physician CMPs ($50K-$133K), Medicare termination
- **§ 1395dd(d)(2)** — Private right of action (patients may sue for personal injury, 2-year statute of limitations)
- **§ 1395dd(e)(1)** — Emergency medical condition definition (acute symptoms, absence of care could result in serious jeopardy/impairment/dysfunction)
- **§ 1395dd(e)(3)** — Stabilization definition (treatment to assure no material deterioration during transfer)
- **§ 1395dd(g)** — Receiving hospital obligations (must accept appropriate transfers when capacity/capabilities available)
- **§ 1395dd(h)** — **Prohibition on delay to inquire about payment** (violated in July 2023 Mercy East incident)
- **§ 1395dd(i)** — Whistleblower protections (hospitals cannot retaliate against staff reporting EMTALA violations)

**Case Law (Binding Precedent):**
- ***Roberts v. Galen of Va., Inc.*, 525 U.S. 249 (1999)** — Supreme Court held EMTALA imposes strict liability; no proof of improper motive (financial discrimination, racial animus) required; patient need only prove violation occurred + personal harm + direct causation
- ***Burditt v. U.S. Dep't of Health & Human Servs.*, 934 F.2d 1362 (5th Cir. 1991)** — First physician penalty under EMTALA ($20,000 against on-call obstetrician who refused to treat pregnant patient with severe hypertension due to financial concerns); established on-call specialists cannot refuse patients based on ability to pay; rejected constitutional challenge to EMTALA (Medicare participation voluntary)

**CMS Guidance:**
- **CMS State Operations Manual, Appendix V** — Interpretive Guidelines for EMTALA (clarifies financial inquiry prohibition, "reasonable registration" exception limited to concurrent triage questions with no delay, statements about potential costs "unduly discourage" patients even if no explicit payment demand)

**2024-2025 Inflation-Adjusted Penalty Amounts:**
- **Hospital ≥100 beds:** $133,420 per violation (statutory baseline $50,000, adjusted annually under Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act)
- **Hospital <100 beds:** $66,712 per violation (statutory baseline $25,000)
- **Individual physician:** $133,420 per violation (statutory baseline $50,000)

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. EMTALA Legal Framework (42 U.S.C. § 1395dd)

#### 1. Statutory Authority and Applicability

The **Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA)**, largely codified in Section 1867 of the Social Security Act, 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd, is a federal law enacted in 1986 amid reports of hospital emergency rooms refusing to treat poor or uninsured patients ("patient dumping"). EMTALA requires hospitals, as a condition of federal Medicare funding, to provide emergency services to any individual presenting at an emergency department, irrespective of the individual's ability to pay.¹

**Applicability:**
- **Medicare-Participating Hospitals:** All hospitals with emergency departments that participate in Medicare (including critical access hospitals and, effective January 1, 2023, rural emergency hospitals)²
- **Trigger:** Individual "comes to the emergency department" and requests examination or treatment for emergency medical condition³
- **All Four Mercy Hospitals Subject:** Mercy Regional, Northwest, East, and South all maintain Medicare provider agreements with valid CCN numbers, subjecting them to EMTALA obligations⁴

**Voluntary Participation:** Hospitals voluntarily participate in Medicare by signing provider agreements; EMTALA compliance is a condition of participation. Hospitals may exit Medicare to avoid EMTALA obligations, but such exit would result in loss of Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement (approximately 70% of Mercy's patient revenue) and effective operational insolvency.⁵

#### 2. Three Core EMTALA Requirements

##### A. Medical Screening Examination (MSE) — 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(a)

**Statutory Requirement:**
> "If any individual...comes to the emergency department and a request is made on the individual's behalf for examination or treatment for a medical condition, the hospital must provide for an **appropriate medical screening examination** within the capability of the hospital's emergency department...to determine whether or not an emergency medical condition...exists."⁶

**Regulatory Implementation (42 CFR § 489.24(a)):**
- MSE must be conducted by **"qualified medical personnel"** per hospital bylaws and consistent with 42 CFR § 482.55 (emergency services condition of participation)⁷
- Examination must be **"appropriate"** — using available ancillary services (labs, imaging, consultations) to determine if emergency medical condition exists⁸
- MSE must be applied **uniformly** — hospital cannot provide different levels of screening based on patient's insurance status, race, or other discriminatory factors⁹
- MSE must not be **delayed** to inquire about insurance or payment method (see § 1395dd(h) below)

**"Appropriate" MSE Standard:**
Courts have rejected requiring hospitals to provide identical screening to all patients; instead, MSE must be "appropriate" given patient's presenting symptoms and within hospital's capabilities. However, disparate treatment based on financial status violates EMTALA.¹⁰

**Mercy East Compliance (July 2023):**
✅ **COMPLIANT** — Dr. Johnson performed timely, appropriate MSE within 20 minutes of patient arrival (EKG, labs, physical examination), diagnosed STEMI, determined emergency medical condition exists. No MSE violation.

##### B. Stabilization Requirement — 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(b)

**Statutory Requirement:**
> "If any individual...comes to a hospital and the hospital determines that the individual has an emergency medical condition, the hospital must provide either—(A) within the staff and facilities available at the hospital, for such further medical examination and such treatment as may be required to **stabilize the medical condition**, or (B) for transfer of the individual to another medical facility..."¹¹

**Key Definitions:**

**"Emergency Medical Condition" (42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(e)(1)):**
> "(A) a medical condition manifesting itself by **acute symptoms of sufficient severity** (including severe pain) such that the absence of immediate medical attention could reasonably be expected to result in—
> (i) placing the health of the individual...in serious jeopardy,
> (ii) serious impairment to bodily functions, or
> (iii) serious dysfunction of any bodily organ or part..."¹²

**Application to STEMI:** Acute myocardial infarction (STEMI) unequivocally constitutes emergency medical condition. Absence of immediate treatment (cardiac catheterization, angioplasty) places patient at imminent risk of cardiac arrest, cardiogenic shock, ventricular arrhythmias, and death. STEMI requires treatment within 90 minutes of diagnosis ("door-to-balloon time") per American College of Cardiology guidelines.¹³

**"Stabilize" (42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(e)(3)):**
> "The term 'to stabilize' means, with respect to an emergency medical condition...to provide such medical treatment of the condition as may be necessary to assure, **within reasonable medical probability**, that **no material deterioration** of the condition is likely to result from or occur during the transfer of the individual from a facility..."¹⁴

**Regulatory Definition (42 CFR § 489.24(b)):**
> Stabilization involves treatment "as may be necessary to assure, within reasonable medical probability, that no material deterioration is likely to result from or occur during the transfer from a facility." A patient may be stabilized even if the patient's medical condition remains unresolved or requires further treatment at another facility.¹⁵

**Key Principle:** **Stabilization ≠ Complete Resolution of Condition**

EMTALA does not require hospitals to provide definitive treatment if beyond their capabilities. Instead, hospitals must provide treatment **within their capabilities** to minimize risk of deterioration during transfer. For STEMI patients, sending hospital without cardiac cath lab cannot perform definitive treatment (angioplasty), but must stabilize to extent possible (oxygen, aspirin, nitroglycerin, heparin, beta-blockers, monitoring) before transfer.¹⁶

**Mercy East Compliance (July 2023):**
✅ **COMPLIANT** — Dr. Johnson provided stabilizing treatment within Mercy East's capabilities (oxygen to correct hypoxemia, aspirin/nitroglycerin/heparin per STEMI protocol, cardiac monitoring). Patient stabilized to extent possible before transfer. Definitive treatment (angioplasty) beyond Mercy East's capabilities, appropriately transferred. No stabilization violation.

##### C. Appropriate Transfer Requirements — 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(c)

**Prohibition on Unstabilized Transfer:**
> "If an individual at a hospital has an emergency medical condition which has not been stabilized...the hospital may not transfer the individual unless—(A)(i) the individual...after being informed of the hospital's obligations...and of the risk of transfer, in writing requests transfer to another medical facility, (ii) a physician...has signed a certification that...the medical benefits reasonably expected from the provision of appropriate medical treatment at another medical facility **outweigh the increased risks** to the individual..."¹⁷

**Appropriate Transfer Requirements (42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(c)(2)):**

An appropriate transfer must satisfy the following:

**(A) Sending Hospital Treatment:**
> "The transferring hospital must provide the medical treatment within its capacity which minimizes the risks to the individual's health..."¹⁸

**(B) Receiving Hospital Acceptance:**
> "The receiving facility has available space and qualified personnel for the treatment of the individual, and has **agreed to accept transfer** of the individual..."¹⁹

**(C) Medical Records Transfer:**
> "The transferring hospital sends to the receiving facility all medical records...including...records related to the emergency condition for which the individual has presented, available at the time of the transfer..."²⁰

**(D) Qualified Personnel and Equipment:**
> "The transfer is effected through **qualified personnel** and **transportation equipment**, as required, including the use of necessary and medically appropriate life support measures during the transfer."²¹

**Regulatory Detail (42 CFR § 489.24(d)):**

Transfers require:
- **Physician Certification:** Physician must sign certification stating medical benefits of transfer outweigh risks OR patient/representative signs written request after being informed of risks²²
- **Complete Medical Records:** EKG, labs, H&P, nursing notes, treatment rendered must be transmitted (electronic transmission acceptable)²³
- **Qualified Personnel:** Paramedics, nurses, or physicians as appropriate given patient's condition; basic EMTs insufficient for unstable cardiac patients requiring continuous monitoring²⁴
- **Equipment:** Cardiac monitor, defibrillator, oxygen, medications as medically appropriate during transport²⁵

|-------------|--------|----------|
| **(A) Sending hospital treatment to minimize risks** | ✅ **COMPLIANT** | Mercy East provided oxygen, aspirin, nitroglycerin, heparin (STEMI protocol within capabilities), cardiac monitoring until transfer |
| **(B) Receiving hospital acceptance** | ✅ **COMPLIANT** | Dr. Martinez (Mercy Regional interventional cardiologist) accepted transfer, cath lab available, team assembling |
| **(C) Medical records transmitted** | ✅ **COMPLIANT** | EKG, labs (troponin), H&P, treatment record transmitted electronically to Mercy Regional before patient arrival |

**CRITICAL CAVEAT:** While transfer itself appropriate, the **financial inquiry before transfer** violates separate EMTALA provision (§ 1395dd(h)), discussed below.

#### 3. Prohibition on Financial Inquiry Before Stabilization — 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(h)

**Statutory Prohibition:**
> "A participating hospital may **not delay** providing an appropriate medical screening examination required under subsection (a) or further medical examination and treatment required under subsection (b) **in order to inquire about the individual's method of payment or insurance status**."²⁶

**Regulatory Implementation (42 CFR § 489.24(h)):**
> "A participating hospital may not delay providing an appropriate medical screening examination or further medical examination and treatment in order to inquire about the individual's method of payment or insurance status."²⁷

**Limited Exception — Reasonable Registration:**
> "Hospitals may follow **reasonable registration processes** for individuals for whom examination or treatment is required by this section, including asking whether an individual is insured and, if so, what that insurance is, **as long as that inquiry does not delay screening or treatment**. Reasonable registration processes may not **unduly discourage** individuals from remaining for further evaluation."²⁸

**CMS Interpretive Guidelines (State Operations Manual Appendix V):**

CMS guidance clarifies:
- **Permissible:** Brief registration questions during triage (name, DOB, address, emergency contact, insurance company name) if **concurrent** with clinical triage, **no delay** to MSE/treatment²⁹
- **Prohibited:** Requiring completion of financial forms, credit checks, deposit/payment demands, detailed insurance verification, financial counseling **before** MSE completed or patient stabilized³⁰
- **Unduly Discouraging:** Statements that imply patient will incur large bills, may be financially responsible, or should consider refusing treatment/transfer due to cost — such statements violate EMTALA even if not explicit demands for payment³¹

**"Delay" Standard:**

Courts and CMS apply both **actual delay** and **constructive delay** standards:
- **Actual Delay:** Measurable time elapsed due to financial inquiry (e.g., 35 minutes between financial inquiry and transfer arrangement in Mercy East case)³²
- **Constructive Delay:** Patient discouraged from remaining for treatment due to financial discussion, even if patient ultimately receives care (e.g., patient told "you may owe $50,000," patient feels pressured but remains)³³

**Policy Rationale:**
EMTALA's prohibition on financial inquiry protects vulnerable populations (uninsured, underinsured, indigent) from being "dumped" or discouraged from seeking emergency care due to inability to pay. Congress recognized that financial discussions in emergency contexts create coercive pressure on patients to forego medically necessary care.³⁴

**Mercy East Violation Analysis (July 2023):**

❌ **VIOLATION CONFIRMED**

**Facts:**
- Business office staff approached Jane Doe at 11:55 PM (5 minutes after MSE completed, STEMI diagnosed)
- Asked about insurance status
- Patient disclosed lack of insurance
- Staff stated: *"Transfers can be expensive, you may owe $50,000 for ambulance and hospital bills"*
- Transfer not arranged until 12:30 AM (35 minutes later)

**Legal Analysis:**
1. **Timing:** Financial inquiry occurred **after** MSE but **before** transfer arranged (patient not yet stabilized/transferred)
2. **Delay:** 35-minute gap between financial inquiry (11:55 PM) and transfer arrangement (12:30 AM) constitutes **actual delay**
3. **Unduly Discouraging:** Statement about "$50,000" potential cost **unduly discouraged** patient, created financial coercion (even though patient ultimately agreed to transfer)
4. **No "Reasonable Registration" Exception:** Discussion conducted by **business office staff** (non-clinical), focused on **potential costs** (not mere insurance identification), occurred **after diagnosis but before stabilization/transfer complete**

**CMS Finding:**
Violation of 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(h) and 42 CFR § 489.24(h). Financial inquiry delayed transfer arrangement and unduly discouraged patient from accepting medically necessary care.

**Penalty:** $50,000 civil monetary penalty under 42 CFR § 1003.500.

**Irrelevance of Successful Outcome:**
Courts and CMS consistently hold that **patient harm not required** for EMTALA violation. Even though Jane Doe ultimately received successful treatment at Mercy Regional with no permanent injury, the **violation occurred when financial inquiry delayed/discouraged transfer**, regardless of ultimate outcome.³⁵ This principle derives from Supreme Court precedent in *Roberts v. Galen of Virginia*, 525 U.S. 249 (1999), holding EMTALA imposes strict liability without requiring proof of improper motive or patient harm.³⁶

#### 1. Civil Monetary Penalties (CMPs) — 42 CFR § 1003.500

**Hospital Penalties:**

- **Hospitals with ≥100 beds:** $133,420 per violation (statutory baseline $50,000, adjusted annually for inflation)
- **Hospitals with <100 beds:** $66,712 per violation (statutory baseline $25,000, adjusted)

**Original Statutory Amounts (1986):**

- $25,000 maximum for hospitals with <100 beds

**Mercy East Penalty (August 2024):**
- **Assessed:** $50,000 (below inflation-adjusted maximum of $66,712)

  - First EMTALA violation (no prior history)
  - No patient harm (successful treatment outcome)
  - Prompt corrective action (policy implemented September 2024, training completed)
  - Cooperation with CMS investigation

**Physician Penalties:**³⁸

Individual physicians may be penalized **$133,420 per violation** (2024-2025 inflation-adjusted; statutory baseline $50,000) if physician "knowingly" violates EMTALA or "negligently" violates EMTALA after hospital notified of deficiency.

**Key Distinction:** Physician penalties require **knowledge** or **negligence** (mens rea), whereas hospital penalties impose **strict liability** (no proof of intent required).³⁹

**Mercy East Application:**
Dr. Sarah Johnson (ED physician) **not** penalized because:
- Dr. Johnson performed appropriate MSE, stabilization, and transfer arrangement
- **Only business office staff** involved in financial inquiry violation
- Dr. Johnson had no knowledge of/participation in improper financial discussion
- EMTALA physician penalty applies to physicians who refuse screening/stabilization or authorize inappropriate transfer, not to collateral hospital staff conduct⁴⁰

**Penalty Assessment Factors (42 CFR § 1003.106):**⁴¹

OIG considers the following factors when determining CMP amount:
1. **Nature of violation:** Failure to screen (most serious) vs. transfer deficiency vs. financial inquiry (less serious but still penalized)
2. **Prior history:** Repeat violations increase penalties substantially
3. **Patient harm:** Actual injury/death increases penalties; no harm mitigates
4. **Financial condition:** Hospital's ability to pay (rarely invoked for large systems)
5. **Corrective action:** Prompt remediation reduces penalties

#### 2. Medicare Provider Agreement Termination — 42 CFR § 489.53

#### 3. Private Right of Action — 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd(d)(2)

**Statutory Provision:**⁴⁵
> "Any individual who suffers personal harm as a direct result of a participating hospital's violation of a requirement of this section may, in a civil action against the participating hospital, obtain those damages available for personal injury under the law of the State in which the hospital is located, and such equitable relief as is appropriate."

**Key Elements:**
1. **Standing:** Individual who suffered personal harm (patient or medical facility suffering financial loss from accepting "dumped" patient)⁴⁶
2. **Causation:** Personal harm must be "direct result" of EMTALA violation
3. **Damages:** State personal injury law governs damages (compensatory, lost wages, pain/suffering; punitive damages if state law permits)
4. **Statute of Limitations:** 2 years from date of violation⁴⁷

**No Improper Motive Required — *Roberts v. Galen of Virginia***⁴⁸

*Roberts v. Galen of Va., Inc.*, 525 U.S. 249 (1999), is landmark Supreme Court decision establishing that plaintiffs need **not prove improper motive** (e.g., financial discrimination, racial animus) to recover under EMTALA. EMTALA imposes **strict liability** for violations of stabilization/transfer requirements.

**Case Background:**
Patient Brenda Jean Roberts (later deceased, action by guardian) brought to Humana Hospital Audubon, diagnosed with back pain. Hospital transferred patient to University of Louisville Hospital without stabilizing. Patient alleged EMTALA violation (failure to stabilize before transfer).

Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals held plaintiff must prove hospital acted with "improper motive" (e.g., financial status, race) to establish EMTALA violation.

**Supreme Court Holding (Unanimous, Per Curiam):**⁴⁹
> "Section 1395dd(b) contains no express or implied 'improper motive' requirement. The text of § 1395dd(b) does not require an 'appropriate' stabilization, nor can it reasonably be read to require an improper motive...The court below was therefore incorrect to require proof of such a motive."

**Significance:**
- EMTALA violations do **not** require proof of discriminatory intent, financial motive, or bad faith
- Hospitals liable for objective failure to screen, stabilize, or transfer appropriately, regardless of subjective intent
- Plaintiff need only prove: (1) EMTALA violation occurred, (2) plaintiff suffered personal harm, (3) harm directly resulted from violation

#### 4. Physician Liability — *Burditt v. U.S. Department of Health & Human Services*⁵¹

**Landmark Case:** *Burditt v. U.S. Dep't of Health & Human Servs.*, 934 F.2d 1362 (5th Cir. 1991), is first physician sanctions case under EMTALA, establishing individual physician liability for on-call specialists who refuse to treat emergency patients.

**Case Facts:**
- Dr. Michael L. Burditt, on-call obstetrician, contacted about pregnant patient (Rosa Rivera) presenting to ED
- Patient's blood pressure dangerously elevated (210/130 mmHg), pregnancy at term
- Dr. Burditt expressed reluctance to treat due to patient's lack of prenatal care and inability to pay
- Dr. Burditt instructed nurses to transfer patient to charity hospital 170 miles away without stabilizing
- Patient gave birth in ambulance during transfer

**OIG Penalty:**
$20,000 civil monetary penalty against Dr. Burditt (first physician penalty under EMTALA)

**Fifth Circuit Holdings:**⁵²

1. **Emergency Medical Condition Existed:**
Expert testimony established patient's severe hypertension (210/130) placed her at high risk of seizures, stroke, heart failure, placental abruption — satisfied EMTALA emergency medical condition definition.

2. **Failure to Stabilize:**
Dr. Burditt failed to stabilize patient before transfer. Court noted: *"Every reasonable adult, let alone physician, understands that labor evolves to delivery, that high blood pressure is dangerous"* — physician's indifference demonstrated he never made required medical judgment weighing transfer risks vs. benefits.

3. **Physician Liability Standard:**
While EMTALA imposes no duties directly on physicians (statute addresses hospital obligations), physicians may be penalized for "knowingly" violating EMTALA **"with respect to"** a hospital's violation. Physicians who refuse to screen, stabilize, or authorize inappropriate transfers are liable.

4. **Constitutional Challenge Rejected:**
Dr. Burditt argued EMTALA imposed unconstitutional "taking" of his services without compensation. Fifth Circuit rejected, holding Medicare participation voluntary; physicians who accept on-call duties at Medicare-participating hospitals consent to EMTALA obligations.

**Significance for On-Call Specialists:**⁵³
- On-call physicians **cannot refuse** to evaluate/treat ED patients with emergency medical conditions based on inability to pay, insurance status, or usual treating relationship
- Most physician EMTALA penalties target on-call specialists who decline to come in or who authorize inappropriate transfers
- On-call obligations extend beyond employed physicians — community physicians with hospital privileges subject to EMTALA when on-call

**Mercy East Application:**
Dr. Johnson (employed ED physician) appropriately performed MSE, stabilization, and transfer. Business office staff (non-physicians) caused violation. **No physician liability in this case.**

However, **Mercy system-wide on-call risk:** 1,200 community physicians with privileges at Mercy hospitals serve on-call rotations. If on-call specialists refuse to evaluate patients or authorize inappropriate transfers, both hospital **and** individual physician liable for EMTALA penalties.
