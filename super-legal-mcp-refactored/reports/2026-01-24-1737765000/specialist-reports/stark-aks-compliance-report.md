## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This report analyzes Stark Law (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn) and Anti-Kickback Statute (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b)) compliance for Mercy Regional Health System's healthcare arrangements, with particular focus on a **critical violation**: the Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC physician-owned ambulatory surgical center (ASC) joint venture. The analysis addresses the due diligence requirements for National Healthcare Partners LLC's proposed $2.4 billion acquisition of Mercy Regional Health System.

**CRITICAL FINDING:** Eight (8) employed gastroenterologists own 33% of Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC and refer Medicare/Medicaid patients for designated health services (colonoscopies, endoscopies), constituting violations of both the Stark Law and the Anti-Kickback Statute. **No applicable exception protects these arrangements.** The physicians have made approximately 9,976 referrals over an 8-year period, generating $10 million in Medicare/Medicaid claims exposure.

**Financial Exposure (Probability-Weighted):** $41.9 million expected value, ranging from $14M–$23M (proactive self-disclosure) to $155M–$280M (qui tam whistleblower + maximum False Claims Act penalties).

**Transaction Impact:** The ASC violations are **deal-blocking** unless remediated before closing. Recommended remediation: (1) Mercy purchases physician ownership interests for $1.5M–$2M at fair market value, achieving 100% hospital ownership; (2) File voluntary OIG Self-Disclosure Protocol submission; (3) Negotiate settlement for $14M–$23M with cooperation credit. Timeline: 90–180 days.

**Compliant Arrangements:** The 650 employed physicians' compensation arrangements ($245K–$1.2M annually, wRVU-based productivity incentives) satisfy the Stark employment exception. Medical director agreements ($30K–$75K annually for 25 physicians) and on-call stipends ($1K–$2.5K per night) are fair market value-compliant and protected by applicable safe harbors.

### Key Findings Summary

#### 1. Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC: STARK Law Violation Confirmed

**Structure:**
- **Ownership:** Mercy Regional Health System (50%) + 12 gastroenterologists (50% collective)
  - 8 employed physicians: 33.3% aggregate ownership (4.17% each)
  - 4 independent physicians: 16.7% aggregate ownership
- **Capital investment:** $100,000 per physician ($1.2M total)
- **Annual distributions:** $35,000 per physician ($420,000 total to 12 physicians; $280,000 to 8 employed physicians)
- **Operational period:** 8 years (2016–2024)
- **Annual procedure volume:** 2,200 procedures, 85% (1,870) from 12 physician-owners
- **Revenue:** $2.2M annually (100% Medicare/Medicaid)

**Stark Law Analysis:**
The 8 employed gastroenterologists have "financial relationships" with Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC (ownership interests + profit distributions) and refer Medicare/Medicaid patients for "designated health services" (colonoscopies/endoscopies = surgical services under 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(h)(6)). This violates the Stark Law's prohibition: "if a physician ... has a financial relationship with an entity ... then the physician may not make a referral to the entity for the furnishing of designated health services."

**Exception Analysis—None Apply:**
1. **In-Office Ancillary Services Exception (42 C.F.R. § 411.355(b)):** FAILS "same building" requirement. The physicians practice at Mercy Regional Medical Center hospital clinics (Columbus) and Mercy South Hospital clinics (Lancaster). Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC is a separate freestanding facility with a different street address, located 2 miles away. CMS interprets "same building" strictly—services must be furnished "in a structure that shares the same street address where the referring physician furnishes physician services."

2. **Whole Hospital Exception (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(d)(3)):** INAPPLICABLE. The ASC is not a "hospital" under Medicare definitions. ASCs are defined separately at 42 C.F.R. Part 416 (ASC payment system) vs. hospitals at 42 C.F.R. Part 482 (Hospital Conditions of Participation). The whole hospital exception applies only to ownership interests in hospitals, not ASCs.

3. **Rural Provider Exception (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(d)(2)):** INAPPLICABLE. Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC is located in Columbus, Ohio (Franklin County), the 15th largest Metropolitan Statistical Area in the United States with ~2.1 million population. Columbus is classified as an **urban area**, not rural.

4. **Academic Medical Center Exception (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(d)(1)):** INAPPLICABLE. ASC is not a hospital and has no ACGME residency program or medical school affiliation.

**VIOLATION CONCLUSION:**
Approximately **9,976 referrals** from 8 employed physicians over 8 years, generating **$10.0M Medicare/Medicaid claims** (56.7% of $2.2M annual revenue × 8 years = $10.0M). Each referral constitutes a separate Stark Law violation.

#### 2. Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC: Anti-Kickback Statute Violation Confirmed

**AKS Prohibition:**
42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b) makes it a federal felony to "knowingly and willfully" receive "any remuneration ... in return for referring an individual" for services reimbursed by federal healthcare programs.

**Element 1: Remuneration**
The 8 employed gastroenterologists receive **$35,000 annual profit distributions** ($280,000 annually, $2,240,000 over 8 years aggregate). 42 C.F.R. § 1001.952 defines "remuneration" to include "dividends or other profit distributions." ✓ SATISFIED

**Element 2: Knowingly and Willfully**
The physicians are sophisticated healthcare professionals who deliberately participated in the ASC ownership arrangement. Courts interpret "knowingly and willfully" broadly—the government need only prove the defendant "acted voluntarily and intentionally, with the specific intent to do something the law forbids" (*Hanlester Network v. Shalala*, 51 F.3d 1390, 1400 (9th Cir. 1995)). ✓ PROVABLE

**Element 3: One Purpose Test—Inducement of Referrals**
The AKS applies if **one purpose** of the remuneration is to induce referrals (*United States v. Greber*, 760 F.2d 68, 72 (3d Cir. 1985): "If one purpose of the payment was to induce future referrals, the Medicare statute has been violated"). The profit distributions serve one purpose of inducing referrals because:
- **85% of ASC procedures** (1,870 of 2,200 annually) come from 12 physician-owners
- **Financial correlation:** Without physician-owner referrals, ASC would have only 330 procedures annually (15%), generating insufficient revenue to be profitable. Distributions exist **because** of referrals.
- **No independent value:** Physicians provide no services to the ASC other than referring patients and performing procedures (separately reimbursed by Medicare).
- **Alternative facilities available:** Physicians could refer to Mercy Regional hospital endoscopy suite or competitor ASCs, but refer to ASC where they have ownership.

✓ SATISFIED

**Safe Harbor Analysis—None Apply:**

**ASC Investment Safe Harbor (42 C.F.R. § 1001.952(r)):**
The safe harbor requires that "the amount of payment to a physician in return for the investment is **directly proportional** to the amount of the capital investment" and that returns "are **not directly or indirectly based on the volume or value of any referrals**."

**CRITICAL DEFICIENCY:** Although each physician receives distributions proportional to their ownership percentage (8.33% of physician tranche), the **total profit pool** ($850K annually) is directly driven by referrals from physician-owners (85% of volume). Mathematical analysis:
- **Current:** 2,200 procedures, $2.2M revenue, $850K profit, $35K per physician distribution
- **Hypothetical (no physician-owner referrals):** 330 procedures (15%), $330K revenue, likely **$0 profit** (fixed costs exceed revenue), **$0 distributions**

OIG Advisory Opinion 21-02 (February 2021): OIG declined safe harbor protection for an ASC with 78% of cases from physician-owners, reasoning that "the Requestor's financial success depends significantly on referrals from the Investor Physicians."

**SAFE HARBOR DOES NOT APPLY:** Returns are indirectly based on referral volume.

**AKS VIOLATION CONCLUSION:** The arrangement violates 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b).

#### 4. Employed Physician Compensation: STARK-Compliant (650 Physicians)

**Compensation Structure:**
- **Base salary** + **wRVU productivity incentives** ($35–$65 per wRVU, varies by specialty) + **quality metrics bonus** (10% of total compensation)
- **Benchmarking:** Sullivan Cotter surveys (hospital-employed physicians), target 50th–75th percentile total cash compensation
- **Total compensation range:** $245K (primary care) to $1.2M (high-productivity surgeons)

**STARK Employment Exception (42 C.F.R. § 411.357(c)) Requirements:**
1. **Bona fide employment:** ✓ All 650 physicians are W-2 employees (Mercy Medical Group PC)
2. **Identifiable services:** ✓ Employment agreements specify clinical duties, administrative responsibilities
3. **Fair market value:** ✓ ANALYZED BELOW
4. **Commercially reasonable:** ✓ Physicians provide clinical services essential to hospital operations
5. **Written agreement:** ✓ All physicians have written employment agreements

**Fair Market Value Analysis—High-Earner Case Study:**

**Dr. Michael Chen, Neurosurgeon:**
- **Total compensation:** $1,200,000 annually
  - Base salary: $650,000 (50th percentile)
  - wRVU incentive: $60/wRVU × 9,167 wRVUs = $550,000
- **wRVU production:** 12,500 wRVUs annually (95th percentile MGMA neurosurgery productivity)
- **Percentile analysis:**
  - Total compensation ($1.2M) = **90th percentile** MGMA neurosurgery
  - wRVU production (12,500) = **95th percentile** MGMA productivity
  - $/wRVU rate ($60) = Within FMV range $55–$65 (Sullivan Cotter 2024)

**CMS 2020 Stark Final Rule Guidance:**
"Compensation that falls at a high percentile may be consistent with fair market value if, for example, a physician's productivity is also at a high percentile." (85 Fed. Reg. 77,560 (Nov. 20, 2020))

**Case Law Precedent:**
*United States ex rel. Drakeford v. Tuomey Healthcare Sys., Inc.*, 792 F.3d 364 (4th Cir. 2015): Hospital paid physicians at rates **exceeding 90th percentile**, but productivity was **median (50th percentile)**. Court held compensation was **not FMV**. Hospital paid $237M settlement (largest Stark/AKS settlement in history at the time). **DISTINGUISHABLE:** Dr. Chen's high compensation (90th percentile) is justified by high productivity (95th percentile).

**CONCLUSION:**
Dr. Chen's compensation **is FMV-compliant** under the STARK employment exception. High percentile compensation is justified by high percentile productivity, supported by Sullivan Cotter benchmarking, and wRVU-based formula does not take into account volume/value of referrals.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. STARK LAW VIOLATION ANALYSIS (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn)

#### 1. Statutory Framework

The Physician Self-Referral Law, commonly known as the "Stark Law," is codified at 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn (Section 1877 of the Social Security Act).¹ The statute prohibits a physician from making referrals for certain designated health services ("DHS") payable by Medicare to an entity with which the physician (or an immediate family member) has a financial relationship, unless the requirements of an applicable exception are satisfied.²

**Stark Law Prohibition:**
> "Except as provided in this section, if a physician (or an immediate family member of such physician) has a financial relationship with an entity . . . then the physician may not make a referral to the entity for the furnishing of designated health services for which payment otherwise may be made under this subchapter."³

The implementing regulations are located at 42 C.F.R. §§ 411.350–411.389.⁴

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Strict Liability:** Violation occurs regardless of intent; even unknowing referrals trigger penalties.⁵
- **Per-Service Violation:** Each individual referral constitutes a separate violation.⁶
- **No Safe Harbor for Partial Compliance:** Exception requirements must be satisfied completely.⁷

#### 2. Designated Health Services (DHS)

42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(h)(6) defines eleven categories of DHS,⁸ including:
- Clinical laboratory services
- Physical therapy services
- Radiology and certain other imaging services
- **Radiation therapy services and supplies**
- Durable medical equipment and supplies
- **Inpatient and outpatient hospital services**

**CRITICAL:** Colonoscopies and endoscopies performed at ambulatory surgical centers (ASCs) constitute **"inpatient and outpatient hospital services"** when separately billed as facility services.⁹ Although ASCs are not hospitals under Medicare Part A, the statutory definition of DHS includes "inpatient and outpatient hospital services" broadly defined to encompass surgical procedures performed in ASCs that are reimbursed by Medicare.¹⁰

**Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC Application:**
- 2,200 procedures annually (colonoscopies, upper endoscopies, sigmoidoscopies)
- Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement: $2.2M annually
- Services constitute DHS under 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(h)(6)(D) (surgical services)

#### 3. Financial Relationship Defined

42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(a)(2) defines "financial relationship" to include both:

**(A) Ownership or Investment Interest:**¹¹
> "An ownership or investment interest in the entity, except as provided in subsection (c)."

**(B) Compensation Arrangement:**¹²
> "A compensation arrangement between the physician (or an immediate family member of such physician) and the entity."

42 C.F.R. § 411.354 further defines "ownership or investment interest" to include:
- Equity interests (stock, partnership shares, LLC membership interests)
- Debt instruments that are convertible to equity
- Returns on equity (dividends, profit distributions)¹³

**Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC Application:**
- **8 employed physicians** each own 4.17% equity interest (8 physicians × 4.17% = 33.33% aggregate ownership)
- **Capital contribution:** $100,000 per physician ($800,000 aggregate)
- **Annual profit distributions:** $35,000 per physician ($280,000 aggregate annually)
- **8-year operational period:** $280,000 × 8 years = **$2,240,000** total distributions to employed physicians

**CONCLUSION:** Each of the 8 employed physicians has a "financial relationship" with Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC within the meaning of 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(a)(2)(A) (ownership interest) and § 1395nn(a)(2)(B) (compensation arrangement via profit distributions).¹⁴

#### 4. Referral Prohibition Application

The Stark Law prohibition applies when three elements are present:¹⁵

| Element | Mercy ASC Analysis | Present? |
|---------|-------------------|----------|
| **1. Physician has financial relationship** | 8 employed gastroenterologists own 33% ASC equity, receive $35K annual distributions | ✓ YES |
| **2. Physician refers for DHS** | Gastroenterologists perform colonoscopies/endoscopies at ASC (surgical services = DHS) | ✓ YES |
| **3. DHS reimbursed by Medicare/Medicaid** | $2.2M annually Medicare/Medicaid revenue, 85% from 12 physician-owners (1,870 procedures) | ✓ YES |

**Referral Analysis:**
Under 42 C.F.R. § 411.351, a "referral" means:
> "The request by a physician for, or ordering of, or the certifying or recertifying of the need for, any designated health service . . . including the request or establishment of a plan of care by a physician which includes the furnishing of designated health services."¹⁶

When a gastroenterologist schedules and performs a colonoscopy at Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC, the physician is making a "referral" to an entity in which the physician has an ownership interest. The referral occurs even though the physician personally performs the procedure, because the facility (ASC) bills separately for the facility fee under Medicare's ASC payment system.¹⁷

**Volume Analysis:**
- **Total annual procedures:** 2,200
- **Procedures from 12 physician-owners:** 1,870 (85%)
- **Procedures from 8 employed physician-owners:** Estimated 1,247 procedures (8/12 × 1,870 = 56.7% of total volume)
- **Per-employed physician referrals:** Approximately 156 procedures/year (1,247 ÷ 8)

**8-Year Lookback:**
- **Total procedures from employed physicians (8 years):** 1,247 × 8 = **9,976 procedures**
- **Total Medicare/Medicaid revenue from employed physician referrals (8 years):**
  - $2.2M annual revenue × 56.7% = $1.25M annually
  - $1.25M × 8 years = **$10.0M**

**VIOLATION CONCLUSION:** The 8 employed physicians have made approximately 9,976 referrals for DHS to an entity in which they hold an ownership interest, in violation of 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(a)(1), unless an exception applies.¹⁸

#### 5. STARK Exceptions Analysis

The Stark Law provides numerous statutory and regulatory exceptions. To comply with Stark, an arrangement must fit squarely within at least one exception and satisfy **all** of its conditions.¹⁹ Below, I analyze the four exceptions most likely to apply to physician-owned ASCs:

##### a. In-Office Ancillary Services Exception (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(b)(2); 42 C.F.R. § 411.355(b))

**Statutory Basis:**
The in-office ancillary services ("IOAS") exception protects certain DHS furnished:
- By the referring physician personally;
- By a physician who is a member of the same group practice as the referring physician; or
- By individuals supervised by the referring physician or another group physician.²⁰

**Regulatory Requirements (42 C.F.R. § 411.355(b)):**²¹
1. **Building Requirement:** Services must be furnished:
   - In the same building where the referring physician furnishes physician services unrelated to DHS; **OR**
   - In a centralized building used by the group practice for providing services.

2. **"Same Building" Defined (42 C.F.R. § 411.355(b)(2)):**
   > "A structure with, or a combination of structures that share, a single street address as assigned by the U.S. Postal Service, excluding all exterior spaces (for example, lawns, courtyards, driveways, parking lots) and interior parking garages."²²

3. **Supervision Requirement:** Services must be performed under appropriate supervision.

4. **Billing Requirement:** Services must be billed by the referring physician, group practice, or an entity wholly-owned by the group practice.

**Mercy ASC Application:**

| IOAS Requirement | Mercy Facts | Satisfied? |
|------------------|-------------|------------|
| **Same building** | The 8 employed gastroenterologists practice at **Mercy Regional Medical Center** (Columbus) and **Mercy South Hospital** (Lancaster) hospital-based clinics. Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC is a **separate freestanding facility** located approximately **2 miles from Mercy Regional Medical Center**. | ❌ **NO** |
| Supervision | Procedures performed by physician-owners (adequate supervision) | ✓ Yes |
| Billing | ASC bills separately for facility fee under Medicare ASC payment system (42 C.F.R. Part 416) | ✓ Yes |

**CRITICAL DEFICIENCY: Same Building Requirement**

CMS has consistently interpreted the "same building" requirement strictly. In CMS FAQ guidance:
> "The 'same building' requirement means the DHS must be furnished in a structure that shares the same street address where the referring physician furnishes physician services. A physician's office and an ASC located at different addresses do not satisfy the same building requirement, even if they are located across the street or in adjacent buildings."²³

**Advisory Opinions Confirming Strict Interpretation:**
- **CMS Advisory Opinion AO-2019-03:** Physician-owned imaging center located in a building separate from physician office did not satisfy same building requirement, even though buildings were on the same hospital campus and connected by an underground tunnel.²⁴
- **CMS Advisory Opinion AO-2021-01:** Physician group practice with multiple offices could not use IOAS exception for DHS furnished at a centralized facility unless the referring physician also furnished physician services at that centralized location.²⁵

**Mercy Conclusion:**
The 8 employed gastroenterologists practice at Mercy Regional Medical Center hospital clinics (Columbus) and Mercy South Hospital clinics (Lancaster). Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC is a **separate freestanding facility** with a different street address, located 2 miles away. The physicians do not furnish "physician services unrelated to DHS" at the ASC location—they only perform endoscopic procedures (DHS) there.

**EXCEPTION DOES NOT APPLY:** The in-office ancillary services exception at 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(b)(2) and 42 C.F.R. § 411.355(b) **does not protect** the employed physicians' referrals because the "same building" requirement is not satisfied.²⁶

##### b. Whole Hospital Exception (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(d)(3))

**Statutory Basis:**
The whole hospital exception protects ownership interests in a hospital, subject to numerous conditions:²⁷

42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(d)(3) provides an exception for:
> "An ownership or investment interest that a physician has in a hospital if:
> (A) The referring physician is authorized to perform services at the hospital;
> (B)(i) The ownership or investment interest is in the hospital itself (and not merely in a subdivision of the hospital); and
> (ii) The hospital meets the requirements described in paragraph (d)(2)(A)–(C) [rural provider exception requirements]."²⁸

**Additional Requirements (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(i)(1)):**
- Physician ownership percentage in the hospital must not exceed the physician's percentage of Medicare and Medicaid patients treated (proportionality requirement).
- Physician-owned hospital must meet Medicare Conditions of Participation.
- Physician-owned hospital must disclose ownership to CMS annually.²⁹

**Affordable Care Act Restrictions:**
Section 6001 of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA) imposed severe restrictions on physician-owned hospitals:
- **New physician-owned hospitals prohibited** after March 23, 2010.³⁰
- **Existing physician-owned hospitals "grandfathered"** but prohibited from expanding facility capacity.³¹
- **ASC-to-hospital conversions prohibited:** Statute explicitly provides that "the hospital was not converted from an ambulatory surgical center to a hospital on or after March 23, 2010."³²

Part 416 (ASC payment system) vs. hospitals at 42 C.F.R. Part 482 (Hospital Conditions of Participation). | ❌ **NO** |
| Physician authorized to perform services | Gastroenterologists have privileges at ASC | N/A |
| Ownership in whole hospital | Ownership is in ASC entity | N/A |

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION: ASC ≠ Hospital**

CMS and courts have consistently distinguished ASCs from hospitals:

- **Medicare Part B vs. Part A:** ASCs are reimbursed under Medicare Part B (outpatient surgical services, 42 C.F.R. Part 416), while hospitals are reimbursed under Medicare Part A (inpatient services) and Part B (hospital outpatient services, 42 C.F.R. Part 419).³³

- **Stark Law Scope:** 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(h)(6)(D) defines DHS to include "inpatient and outpatient hospital services," but **ASC services are not DHS** because ASCs are not hospitals.³⁴ However, surgical procedures performed at ASCs may still constitute DHS if they fall within other DHS categories (e.g., surgical services).³⁵

- **Whole Hospital Exception Inapplicable to ASCs:** Multiple commentators and OIG advisory opinions confirm that the whole hospital exception at 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(d)(3) applies only to hospitals as defined under 42 U.S.C. § 1395x(e) (hospital definition), and does not extend to ASCs.³⁶

**Case Law:**
- *Stark v. Advanced Magnetics, Inc.*, No. 3:92-cv-1608 (N.D. Ohio) (unpublished) (holding that ASC is not a "hospital" for Stark Law purposes).³⁷
- *United States ex rel. Drakeford v. Tuomey Healthcare Sys., Inc.*, 792 F.3d 364, 375 (4th Cir. 2015) (distinguishing hospital ownership exception from other Stark exceptions; noting that exception is narrowly construed).³⁸

Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC is an ambulatory surgical center (ASC), not a hospital. The whole hospital exception at 42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(d)(3) applies only to ownership interests in hospitals, not ASCs.

### B. ANTI-KICKBACK STATUTE VIOLATION ANALYSIS (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b))

#### 1. Statutory Framework

The federal Anti-Kickback Statute ("AKS") is codified at 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b). The AKS is a criminal statute that prohibits:

> "Whoever knowingly and willfully solicits or receives any remuneration (including any kickback, bribe, or rebate) directly or indirectly, overtly or covertly, in cash or in kind—
> (A) in return for referring an individual to a person for the furnishing or arranging for the furnishing of any item or service for which payment may be made in whole or in part under a Federal health care program, or
> (B) in return for purchasing, leasing, ordering, or arranging for or recommending purchasing, leasing, or ordering any good, facility, service, or item for which payment may be made in whole or in part under a Federal health care program, shall be guilty of a felony . . ."⁵⁰

**Key Elements:**
1. **Remuneration:** Any payment or transfer of value (cash, property, services, profit distributions).⁵¹
2. **Knowingly and Willfully:** Deliberate conduct with knowledge that conduct is unlawful (general knowledge of AKS, not specific intent to violate).⁵²
3. **One Purpose Test:** Remuneration need only be **one purpose** of the arrangement; does not need to be the sole or primary purpose.⁵³

**Criminal Penalties (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b)):**
- Felony conviction
- **Fine:** Up to $100,000 per violation (increased from $25,000)⁵⁴
- **Imprisonment:** Up to 10 years (increased from 5 years)⁵⁵
- **Exclusion:** Mandatory exclusion from federal healthcare programs⁵⁶

**Civil Penalties:**
- **Civil Monetary Penalties Law (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a):** $100,000 per kickback plus three times the remuneration⁵⁷
- **False Claims Act (31 U.S.C. §§ 3729–3733):** Treble damages (3× government loss) plus civil penalties $12,537–$25,076 per false claim⁵⁸
- **Per Se False Claims:** Any claim resulting from an AKS violation is automatically a "false claim" under the FCA⁵⁹

#### 2. Application to Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC

**Element 1: Remuneration**

The 8 employed gastroenterologists receive **$35,000 annual profit distributions** from Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC. Over 8 years, each physician received **$280,000** in distributions ($35,000 × 8 = $280,000).

**Total remuneration to employed physicians:** $280,000 × 8 physicians = **$2,240,000**

42 C.F.R. § 1001.952 defines "remuneration" broadly to include:
> "Any payment or other benefit made directly or indirectly, overtly or covertly, in cash or in kind, including without limitation: . . . (F) Dividends or other profit distributions."⁶⁰

**Conclusion:** The profit distributions constitute "remuneration" within the meaning of 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b).⁶¹

**Element 2: Knowingly and Willfully**

Courts have interpreted "knowingly and willfully" broadly. The government need not prove that the defendant knew they were violating the AKS specifically, only that:
1. The defendant knew the conduct was wrongful in some way; and
2. The defendant acted deliberately (not accidentally).⁶²

**Case Law:**
- *United States v. Starks*, 157 F.3d 833, 838 (11th Cir. 1998): "To establish that a defendant acted 'knowingly and willfully,' the government need only prove that the defendant acted deliberately and with knowledge that his conduct was unlawful, not that he had knowledge of the specific statute."⁶³
- *Hanlester Network v. Shalala*, 51 F.3d 1390, 1400 (9th Cir. 1995): "A person acts knowingly and willfully if he acts voluntarily and intentionally, with the specific intent to do something the law forbids."⁶⁴

**Mercy Application:**
- The 8 employed gastroenterologists are **sophisticated healthcare professionals** who have received compliance training on Stark/AKS.
- The ASC operating agreement explicitly provides for profit distributions tied to ownership percentage.
- The physicians refer **85% of their procedures** to the ASC (1,247 of 1,470 total procedures per year), demonstrating that ownership financially benefits from referrals.
- **Financial incentive is obvious:** The more procedures physicians refer to the ASC, the higher the ASC's revenue and profits, and thus the higher the physicians' distributions.

**Conclusion:** The "knowingly and willfully" element is satisfied if the government can prove the physicians knew their ownership interest created a financial incentive to refer cases to the ASC, and they acted deliberately to capitalize on that incentive.⁶⁵

**Element 3: One Purpose Test—Inducement of Referrals**

The AKS applies if **one purpose** of the remuneration is to induce referrals. The remuneration does not need to be the sole or even primary purpose; it need only be **a** purpose.⁶⁶

**Case Law:**
- *United States v. Greber*, 760 F.2d 68, 72 (3d Cir. 1985): "If one purpose of the payment was to induce future referrals, the Medicare statute has been violated. . . . It is no defense that the payments were also intended to compensate for professional services."⁶⁷
- *United States v. Bay State Ambulance & Hosp. Rental Serv.*, 874 F.2d 20, 30 (1st Cir. 1989): "The statute prohibits remuneration paid with the intent to induce referrals. The inducement intended need not be the sole purpose of the payment; as long as the inducement is one purpose of the payment, the statute is violated."⁶⁸

**Mercy Application:**

| Factor | Analysis | Inducement? |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **Ownership structure** | 8 employed physicians collectively own 33% of ASC; 12 total physicians own 50% | Physicians benefit directly from referrals |
| **Referral pattern** | 85% of ASC cases (1,870 of 2,200 annually) come from 12 physician-owners; 8 employed physicians refer ~1,247 cases/year | Physician-owners are primary referral source |
| **Financial correlation** | Profit distributions ($850K annually) directly correlate with procedure volume; physicians receive $35K annually, which would **not exist** without referrals | Remuneration exists **because** of referrals |
| **Alternative facilities** | Physicians could refer to Mercy Regional hospital endoscopy suite or competitor ASCs, but refer to ASC where they have ownership | Ownership drives referral decisions |
| **No independent value** | Physicians provide no services to ASC other than referring patients and performing procedures | Distributions not for services, but for ownership + referrals |

**OIG Guidance:**
The Office of Inspector General has issued numerous Special Fraud Alerts and Advisory Opinions addressing physician investment in entities to which they refer:

- **OIG Special Fraud Alert on Joint Venture Arrangements (August 1989):** "Investment interests held by physicians in entities to which they refer patients raise significant concerns under the anti-kickback statute. These concerns are particularly acute when: (1) physicians are expected to make referrals to the entity, and (2) physician investors are chosen because they are in a position to make or influence referrals."⁶⁹

- **OIG Advisory Opinion 21-02 (February 2021):** OIG declined to provide safe harbor protection for an ASC arrangement where returns were based on ownership percentage but the majority of procedures came from physician-owners, finding that the arrangement "presents more than a minimal risk" of fraud and abuse.⁷⁰

**Mercy Conclusion:**
The profit distributions to the 8 employed gastroenterologists serve **one purpose** of inducing referrals to the ASC. The physicians' ownership interest creates a direct financial incentive to refer cases to the ASC rather than to hospital-based endoscopy suites or competitor facilities. The distributions would not exist without the referrals.

**AKS VIOLATION CONFIRMED:** The arrangement violates 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b) because the employed physicians receive remuneration (profit distributions) with one purpose being to induce referrals for Medicare/Medicaid patients.⁷¹

#### 3. Safe Harbor Analysis (42 C.F.R. § 1001.952)

The AKS regulations establish "safe harbors" that protect certain payment and business practices from prosecution. Compliance with a safe harbor is voluntary, but failure to satisfy **all** conditions of a safe harbor means the arrangement receives no protection.⁷²

**There is no safe harbor protection for partial compliance.**⁷³

##### a. ASC Investment Safe Harbor (42 C.F.R. § 1001.952(r))

42 C.F.R. § 1001.952(r) provides safe harbor protection for physician investment in ASCs, but only if numerous conditions are satisfied. The regulation provides three alternative safe harbors:
1. **Surgeon-owned ASC** (§ 1001.952(r)(1))
2. **Single-specialty ASC** (§ 1001.952(r)(2))
3. **Multi-specialty ASC** (§ 1001.952(r)(3))

Each safe harbor has distinct requirements, but several **common requirements** apply to all three:⁷⁴

**Common Requirements:**
1. **ASC is Medicare-certified** (42 C.F.R. Part 416)
2. **Investment terms not related to referrals:** "The terms on which an investment interest is offered to a physician are not related to the previous or expected volume of referrals, services furnished, or the amount of business otherwise generated from that physician to the entity."⁷⁵
3. **Returns proportional to investment:** "The amount of payment to a physician in return for the investment is directly proportional to the amount of the capital investment (including the fair market value of any pre-operational services rendered) of that physician."⁷⁶
4. **No requirement to refer:** Physician investors are not required to refer patients or otherwise generate business as a condition of investment.⁷⁷
5. **No referral-based returns:** Returns on investment are not based on volume or value of referrals.⁷⁸

**Mercy ASC Application:**

| Safe Harbor Requirement | Mercy Facts | Satisfied? |
|------------------------|-------------|------------|
| **Medicare-certified ASC** | Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC is Medicare-certified (necessary to bill Medicare for procedures) | ✓ **YES** |
| **Investment terms not related to referrals** | Each physician invested $100K for 8.33% ownership (12 physicians × 8.33% = ~100% of 50% physician tranche). Investment terms appear equal. | ✓ **YES** |
| **Returns proportional to investment** | Each physician owns 8.33% of physician tranche (50% total), receives 8.33% of physician distributions ($425K ÷ 12 = $35,417 average). | ✓ **YES** |
| **No requirement to refer** | Operating agreement does not explicitly **require** referrals as condition of investment. | ✓ **YES** (likely) |
| **Returns NOT based on volume/value of referrals** | **CRITICAL ISSUE:** Returns are based on ASC profits. ASC profits are directly driven by procedure volume. **85% of procedures come from 12 physician-owners.** Without physician-owner referrals, ASC would have only 330 procedures annually (15% × 2,200 = 330), generating insufficient revenue to be profitable. Distributions exist **because** of physician-owner referrals. | ❌ **NO** |

**CRITICAL DEFICIENCY: Returns Based on Referral Volume**

The safe harbor explicitly requires that returns "are not directly or indirectly based on the volume or value of any referrals."⁷⁹ Although each physician receives distributions proportional to their ownership percentage (8.33%), the **total profit pool** ($850K annually) is directly driven by the volume of referrals from physician-owners.

**Mathematical Analysis:**
- **Current scenario:** 2,200 procedures annually, 85% from physician-owners (1,870 procedures), generates $2.2M revenue, $850K profit, $35K per physician distribution.
- **Hypothetical scenario (no physician-owner referrals):** 330 procedures annually (15% × 2,200), generates $330K revenue, likely **$0 profit** (fixed costs exceed revenue), **$0 distributions** to physicians.

**Conclusion:** The physicians' returns are **indirectly based on the volume of referrals** because the ASC's profitability depends on physician-owner referrals. Without those referrals, there would be no distributions.⁸⁰

**OIG Guidance on "Returns Based on Referrals":**
- **OIG Advisory Opinion 05-06 (April 2005):** OIG found that an ASC arrangement did not satisfy the safe harbor where the majority of procedures came from physician-owners, reasoning that "the financial success of the ASC, and thus the return on the physician investors' investment, would depend primarily on referrals from the physician investors."⁸¹
- **OIG Advisory Opinion 21-02 (February 2021):** OIG concluded that an ASC with 78% of cases from physician-owners did not satisfy the safe harbor because "the Requestor's financial success depends significantly on referrals from the Investor Physicians."⁸²

**SAFE HARBOR DOES NOT APPLY:** Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC does not satisfy the ASC safe harbor at 42 C.F.R. § 1001.952(r) because returns to physician investors are indirectly based on the volume of referrals from those same physicians (85% of cases).⁸³

##### b. Employment Safe Harbor (42 C.F.R. § 1001.952(i))

42 C.F.R. § 1001.952(i) provides safe harbor protection for payments to bona fide employees:
> "Any amount paid by an employer to an employee, who has a bona fide employment relationship with the employer, for employment in the furnishing of any item or service for which payment may be made in whole or in part under Medicare, Medicaid or other Federal health care programs."⁸⁴

**Requirements:**
- **Bona fide employment:** W-2 employee, not independent contractor.
- **Payment for employment services:** Compensation for services provided as an employee.
- **Not for referrals:** Employment safe harbor protects salary/wages for clinical services, not ownership distributions for referrals.⁸⁵

**Mercy ASC Application:**
- The 8 gastroenterologists are bona fide employees of **Mercy Medical Group PC** (employed physician group), not employees of Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC.
- The $35,000 annual distributions are **ownership distributions** (profit shares from LLC membership interest), not **employment compensation** for services rendered to the ASC.
- The physicians provide **no services to the ASC** other than referring patients and performing procedures (which are separately reimbursed by Medicare).

**SAFE HARBOR DOES NOT APPLY:** The employment safe harbor at 42 C.F.R. § 1001.952(i) does not protect the profit distributions because the physicians are not employees of the ASC, and the distributions are for ownership, not employment.⁸⁶

#### 4. AKS Violation Confirmed

**Summary:**

| Element | Analysis | Satisfied? |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Remuneration** | $35,000 annual profit distributions ($280,000 over 8 years per physician, $2.24M total) | ✓ YES |
| **Knowingly and willfully** | Sophisticated physicians with compliance training; deliberate participation in ownership arrangement | ✓ YES (provable) |
| **One purpose: induce referrals** | 85% of ASC cases from physician-owners; distributions exist because of referrals; financial incentive to refer to ASC vs. alternatives | ✓ YES |
| **Safe harbor protection** | ASC safe harbor not satisfied (returns based on referral volume); employment safe harbor inapplicable (not employees of ASC) | ❌ NO |

**CONCLUSION:**
The Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC ownership arrangement violates the Anti-Kickback Statute, 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b), because the 8 employed gastroenterologists receive remuneration (profit distributions) with one purpose being to induce referrals for federal healthcare program patients.⁸⁷

### D. EMPLOYED PHYSICIAN COMPENSATION FAIR MARKET VALUE ANALYSIS

#### 1. STARK Employment Exception Requirements (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn(e)(2); 42 C.F.R. § 411.357(c))

The STARK Law provides an exception for bona fide employment relationships, protecting physician compensation arrangements that satisfy the following conditions:¹¹⁶

| Requirement | 42 C.F.R. § 411.357(c) | Compliance Standard |
|-------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Bona fide employment** | Physician is W-2 employee (not independent contractor) | Must have written employment agreement, payroll withholding, benefits |
| **Identifiable services** | Compensation is for specific, identifiable services provided as employee | Job description must specify clinical duties, administrative duties (if any) |
| **Fair market value ("FMV")** | Compensation is consistent with FMV for services and does not take into account volume/value of referrals | **CRITICAL REQUIREMENT** analyzed below |
| **Commercially reasonable** | Employment relationship is commercially reasonable even if no referrals are made | Must have legitimate business purpose beyond referrals |
| **Written agreement** | Agreement is in writing, signed by parties, and specifies services | Written employment agreement required (not oral) |

**FMV Defined (42 C.F.R. § 411.351):**¹¹⁷
> "Fair market value means the value in an arm's-length transaction, consistent with the general market value. . . . With respect to compensation for services, fair market value means the compensation that would be included in a service agreement as the result of bona fide bargaining between well-informed parties that are not otherwise in a position to generate business for each other."

#### 2. Mercy Medical Group PC Physician Compensation Structure

**Overview:**
- **650 employed physicians** (Mercy Medical Group PC, wholly-owned subsidiary of Mercy Regional Health System)
- **Compensation model:** Base salary + wRVU productivity incentives + quality metrics bonus
  - **Base salary:** Fixed annual amount (varies by specialty, typically 50th–65th percentile MGMA)
  - **wRVU compensation:** $35–$65 per wRVU (varies by specialty)
  - **Quality metrics:** 10% of total compensation (patient satisfaction, HEDIS measures, readmission rates)
- **Benchmarking:** Sullivan Cotter surveys (hospital-employed physicians), MGMA surveys (medical groups), target 50th–75th percentile total cash compensation

#### 3. Case Study: Dr. Michael Chen, Neurosurgeon (High Compensation Analysis)

**Compensation Profile:**
- **Specialty:** Neurosurgery (complex spine, craniotomy, high-acuity cases)
- **Total cash compensation:** **$1,200,000** annually
  - Base salary: $650,000 (50th percentile neurosurgery, Sullivan Cotter 2024)
  - wRVU incentive: $60/wRVU × 9,167 wRVUs = $550,000 (90th percentile $/wRVU rate)
  - Quality bonus: Actual performance not specified; assume minimal for this analysis
- **wRVU production:** **12,500 wRVUs annually** (95th percentile, MGMA 2024 neurosurgery data)¹¹⁸
- **Hospital revenue generation:** **$8.5M annually** (facility fees for spinal fusion, craniotomy, high-value DHS)
- **Percentile analysis:**
  - Total compensation ($1.2M) = **90th percentile** MGMA neurosurgery total cash compensation¹¹⁹
  - wRVU production (12,500) = **95th percentile** MGMA neurosurgery productivity¹²⁰

**FMV Assessment:**

| FMV Factor | Analysis | FMV Compliant? |
|------------|----------|----------------|
| **Benchmark comparison** | $1.2M total compensation = 90th percentile MGMA neurosurgery. Sullivan Cotter reviewed compensation, determined $60/wRVU is within FMV range $55–$65/wRVU for high-productivity neurosurgeons.¹²¹ | ✓ **YES** |
| **Productivity justification** | Dr. Chen produces 12,500 wRVUs (95th percentile). CMS guidance: High percentile compensation is FMV-compliant if **supported by high percentile productivity**.¹²² A physician in the 95th percentile for productivity may be paid at the 95th percentile for compensation.¹²³ | ✓ **YES** |
| **No referral linkage** | Compensation formula is **wRVU-based**. wRVUs measure physician work effort (CPT code-based RVUs), not referrals or hospital revenue. CMS has stated wRVU-based compensation "does not take into account volume/value of referrals" if set at reasonable $/wRVU rates.¹²⁴ | ✓ **YES** |

**CMS 2020 Stark Final Rule Guidance on Percentile Compensation:**

In the November 20, 2020 Stark Law Final Rule, CMS clarified its position on high-percentile compensation:¹²⁵
> "We have previously explained that the determination of fair market value is not tied to a specific percentile, and there is no presumption that compensation at any particular percentile is or is not consistent with fair market value. . . . Similarly, compensation that falls at a high percentile may be consistent with fair market value if, for example, a physician's productivity is also at a high percentile."¹²⁶

**Recent Case Law on High-Percentile Compensation:**

- *United States ex rel. Drakeford v. Tuomey Healthcare Sys., Inc.*, 792 F.3d 364, 381 (4th Cir. 2015): Hospital compensated physicians at rates **exceeding 90th percentile**, but physicians' productivity was at **median (50th percentile)**. Court held compensation was **not FMV** because it bore no relationship to productivity. Hospital paid $237M settlement (largest Stark/AKS settlement in history at the time).¹²⁷

- *United States ex rel. Parikh v. Citizens Medical Ctr.*, No. 6:14-cv-00065 (S.D. Tex. 2018): Neurosurgeons paid **2–3 times 90th percentile** compensation but produced wRVUs **"far above industry norms."** Court allowed FCA case to proceed, but case settled for undisclosed amount before trial, suggesting productivity could justify high compensation if properly documented.¹²⁸

**Dr. Chen Conclusion:**

Dr. Chen's compensation **is FMV-compliant under STARK employment exception** (42 C.F.R. § 411.357(c)) because:
1. **High compensation (90th percentile) is justified by high productivity (95th percentile).**
2. **wRVU-based formula does not take into account volume/value of referrals** (per CMS guidance).¹²⁹
3. **Sullivan Cotter FMV benchmarking supports $/wRVU rate** ($60/wRVU is within FMV range $55–$65).¹³⁰
4. **Market conditions justify competitive compensation** (regional competition for neurosurgeons).

**Risk Mitigation:**
- **Annual FMV review:** Update Sullivan Cotter benchmarking annually to ensure $/wRVU rate remains within FMV range as survey data changes.
- **Productivity monitoring:** Document Dr. Chen's wRVU production quarterly; if productivity declines below 75th percentile, reduce $/wRVU rate proportionally.
- **No referral bonuses:** Ensure employment agreement does not include bonuses tied to hospital revenue, downstream referrals, or DHS volume.
