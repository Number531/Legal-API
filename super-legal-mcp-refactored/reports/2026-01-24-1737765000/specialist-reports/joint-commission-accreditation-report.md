## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This memorandum analyzes the Joint Commission accreditation compliance status of Mercy Regional Health System's four acute care hospitals in connection with National Healthcare Partners LLC's proposed $2.4 billion acquisition. The research focuses on Mercy Regional Medical Center's October 2024 Joint Commission triennial survey, which identified eight (8) standards deficiencies resulting in an accreditation decision requiring follow-up verification in March 2025. The critical legal issue is whether these deficiencies threaten the hospital's "deemed status" under 42 CFR ¬ß 488.5‚Äîthe regulatory mechanism by which Joint Commission accreditation substitutes for direct CMS surveys to certify Medicare Conditions of Participation (CoPs) compliance.

**Bottom Line:** The risk of Mercy Regional losing Joint Commission accreditation and deemed status is **LOW (10-20% probability)**, but if it occurs, the financial consequences are **CATASTROPHIC** ($864 million in annual Medicare revenue at risk, representing 48% of system-wide net patient revenue). Corrective actions implemented October-December 2024 are comprehensive and sustainable, including engineering controls (automated medication dispensing, magnetic fire door hold-opens, electronic hand hygiene monitoring) and software systems (credentialing management). The March 2025 follow-up survey will likely result in full accreditation restoration (75-85% probability), eliminating deemed status risk for the next three years until the 2027-2028 triennial survey cycle.

### Key Findings

#### 1. Deemed Status - The Critical Regulatory Gatekeeper

**What is "Deemed Status"?** Under 42 CFR ¬ß 488.5, hospitals accredited by Joint Commission-approved national accrediting organizations are "deemed" to meet Medicare Conditions of Participation without undergoing separate CMS surveys. The Joint Commission received CMS approval for continued deemed status authority on May 2, 2022 (87 Fed. Reg. 26,261), valid for six years through May 2, 2028. Approximately 4,000 U.S. hospitals (~70% of all acute care hospitals) rely on Joint Commission accreditation for deemed status.

**Why Deemed Status Matters:** Hospitals WITH deemed status undergo Joint Commission triennial surveys (every 3 years) and are subject to Joint Commission's standards enforcement, which emphasizes continuous improvement and corrective action plans. Hospitals WITHOUT deemed status must undergo direct CMS validation surveys conducted by state health departments, which apply Medicare CoPs (42 CFR Part 482) with zero tolerance for condition-level deficiencies and use stricter immediate jeopardy standards. Loss of deemed status exposes hospitals to:

- **More frequent surveys:** CMS resurveys every 12-36 months vs. Joint Commission's 3-year cycle
- **Stricter enforcement:** CMS issues immediate jeopardy findings (life/safety threats) more readily than Joint Commission, triggering 23-day termination timelines
- **Provider agreement termination risk:** If CMS cites condition-level deficiencies not corrected within 90 days, Medicare provider agreement can be terminated under 42 CFR ¬ß 489.53, resulting in loss of all Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement ($1.26 billion annually for Mercy system = 70% of net patient revenue)
- **Commercial payer contract termination:** Many commercial insurance contracts (Humana MA, UnitedHealthcare MA, Anthem, Aetna) require Joint Commission accreditation as condition of network participation; loss of accreditation triggers contract review and potential termination ($270-400 million commercial revenue at risk, 15-22% of net patient revenue)

**Recent 2024 CMS Proposal (Not Yet Final):** In February 2024, CMS proposed a rule (89 Fed. Reg. 10,618) that would allow deemed status loss after a **single condition-level citation** on a CMS validation survey, with hospitals subject to "ongoing review by the state survey agency" until compliance demonstrated. The American Hospital Association opposed this proposal as "overly punitive," arguing it treats hospitals as "guilty until proven innocent" and that short-term deemed status loss could be "devastating" because many states and commercial payers require deemed status for participation. As of January 2026, CMS has not finalized this rule, but the proposal signals heightened regulatory scrutiny of deemed status arrangements.

#### 2. October 2024 Survey Deficiencies - Detailed Assessment

**Survey Context:** Joint Commission surveyors conducted Mercy Regional Medical Center's triennial survey October 14-16, 2024 (3-day on-site visit). The survey covered all Joint Commission Hospital Accreditation Program standards across seven domains: patient care, medication management, infection prevention, environment of care, medical staff, quality improvement, and patient rights. The survey team reviewed 30-50 patient medical records, interviewed physicians/nurses/pharmacists, toured operating rooms/pharmacy/emergency department/patient care units, and observed patient care in real-time.

**Eight Deficiencies Identified (Requirements for Improvement):**

**Deficiency #1 - Medication Management (MM.05.01.09): Expired Medications in Operating Room Pharmacy**
- **Finding:** 12 expired medications discovered during surveyor inspection of OR pharmacy (expired 2-6 months prior)
- **Risk:** If expired medications inadvertently administered during surgery, reduced efficacy (especially critical for anesthetics, antibiotics, vasopressors) or chemical degradation producing toxic byproducts (e.g., tetracycline ‚Üí nephrotoxic)
- **Root Cause:** Inadequate monthly inspections; manual pharmacy cart checks failed to identify expired medications
- **Corrective Action:** Implemented Pyxis automated dispensing cabinets (ADCs) with automatic expiration alerts, monthly pharmacist-led inspections documented in compliance logs, OR nurse/pharmacist training on expiration date checking (October 2024)
- **Sustainability Assessment:** ‚úÖ STRONG - Automated system (Pyxis) provides ongoing control less dependent on human vigilance; alerts lock out expired medications from selection menu
- **CMS CoPs Comparison:** 42 CFR ¬ß 482.25 (pharmaceutical services) requires hospitals to ensure medications are "prepared and administered according to established policies and procedures." CMS would likely cite this as a condition-level deficiency if validation survey occurred before corrective action verified.

**Deficiency #2 - Infection Prevention (IC.01.04.01): Hand Hygiene Compliance Below Target**
- **Finding:** Hand hygiene compliance rate 78% (surveyors observed 100 hand hygiene opportunities, staff performed hand hygiene correctly 78 times)
- **Benchmark:** Joint Commission does not mandate specific numerical target, but CDC recommends ‚â•95% and industry standard is 90%+
- **Risk:** Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs) directly linked to hand hygiene compliance - MRSA, *C. difficile*, surgical site infections (SSIs), central line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSIs). CMS Hospital-Acquired Condition (HAC) Reduction Program penalizes hospitals in worst-performing 25% with 1% Medicare payment reduction (~$8-9 million annually for Mercy Regional). Mercy already penalized -0.8% ($6.9 million annually) for excess readmissions; additional HAC penalty would compound financial impact.
- **Corrective Action:** Deployed hand hygiene coaches (RNs observe/educate in real-time), electronic monitoring system (sensors on dispensers track usage by unit/shift), leadership rounding (CNO/CMO weekly rounds emphasizing hand hygiene). Result: Compliance increased 78% ‚Üí 88% December 2024, target 90% by March 2025.
- **Sustainability Assessment:** ‚ö†Ô∏è CONDITIONAL - 88% compliance as of December 2024 still below 90% target. If not achieved by March 2025, Joint Commission may require additional 6-month follow-up (but would NOT deny accreditation). Electronic monitoring provides objective data; leadership engagement signals organizational priority. Risk: Hand hygiene coaches are resource-intensive; compliance may decline if coaches reassigned after survey.
- **CMS CoPs Comparison:** 42 CFR ¬ß 482.42 (infection prevention and control) requires hospitals to "provide a sanitary environment to avoid sources and transmission of infections." CMS interpretive guidance expects ongoing surveillance and compliance targets, but does not mandate specific percentage. 88% trending toward 90% may satisfy CMS if improvement trajectory documented.

**Deficiency #3 - Environment of Care (EC.02.01.01): Fire Door Propped Open**
- **Finding:** 3rd floor stairwell fire door propped open with trash can (manual obstruction preventing self-closure)
- **Violation:** NFPA 101 Life Safety Code ¬ß 7.2.1.8.2 - "Fire doors shall be self-closing or automatic-closing and shall not be blocked open"
- **Risk:** Fire safety violation; if fire occurs, smoke/fire spreads to stairwell (critical evacuation route), trapping patients/staff. State Fire Marshal can independently cite (separate from Joint Commission) with civil penalties $500-$10,000 per violation per day (Ohio Revised Code ¬ß 3737.99).
- **Corrective Action:** Facilities management inspected all fire doors hospital-wide, removed obstructions, installed magnetic hold-opens connected to fire alarm system (doors held open for convenience but automatically release when fire alarm activated, compliant with NFPA 101 Exception 2 for automatic-closing devices), staff training on fire door importance, monthly inspections documenting self-closing mechanisms functional.
- **Sustainability Assessment:** ‚úÖ STRONG - Magnetic hold-opens provide permanent engineering control eliminating incentive to prop doors. However, devices must be tested annually to verify fire alarm activation releases doors (NFPA 72 compliance). If magnetic hold-open malfunctions or fire alarm test fails, life safety deficiency persists.
- **CMS CoPs Comparison:** 42 CFR ¬ß 482.41(b) requires hospitals to comply with NFPA 101 Life Safety Code. CMS applies identical standard as Joint Commission; no difference in enforcement.

**Deficiency #4 - Medical Staff (MS.06.01.05): Credentialing File Incomplete**
- **Finding:** Dr. James Wilson's credentialing file missing primary source verification of medical school graduation (file dated 2021 reappointment, verification should have been conducted within 2 years)
- **Requirement:** Joint Commission and 42 CFR ¬ß 482.12(a)(7) require primary source verification for: medical school graduation, residency/fellowship completion, board certification, state medical license, DEA registration, malpractice claims history (NPDB National Practitioner Data Bank query)
- **Risk:** If unqualified physician practices without proper verification, medical errors, patient harm, malpractice claims. Hospital faces negligent credentialing liability if physician's credentials were falsified and not verified.
- **Corrective Action:** Obtained Dr. Wilson's medical school diploma copy, verified with FSMB (Federation of State Medical Boards), updated credentialing file. Conducted system-wide audit of all 1,850 physicians (650 employed + 1,200 privileged), ensured primary source verification current within 2 years (completed November 2024). Implemented VerityStream credentialing software with automated alerts (reappointment due, primary source verification expiration approaching), reducing human error.
- **Sustainability Assessment:** ‚úÖ STRONG - VerityStream software provides automated controls preventing future lapses. System-wide audit of 1,850 files demonstrates comprehensive correction (not just Dr. Wilson). Risk: Software relies on accurate data entry; if medical staff office fails to input expiration dates correctly, alerts will not trigger.
- **CMS CoPs Comparison:** 42 CFR ¬ß 482.12 requires medical staff bylaws specify qualifications for privileges but does not explicitly mandate primary source verification in regulation text (though CMS State Operations Manual interpretive guidance recommends). Joint Commission standard is MORE stringent - explicit 2-year reappointment cycle and primary source verification requirements. If CMS surveyed Mercy Regional before November 2024 system-wide audit, would likely cite as condition-level deficiency.

**Deficiencies #5-8 (Lower Risk, Corrected):**
- **#5 Pain Assessment Documentation:** Nursing staff not consistently documenting pain reassessment after medication. Corrected via EHR (Epic) hard-stop alert requiring documentation within 60 minutes.
- **#6 Patient Education Materials:** Some materials not available in Spanish for limited English proficiency patients. Corrected via translation of all materials, medical interpreter services expanded.
- **#7 Emergency Management Drills:** ED staff participation 85% (target 95%). Corrected via mandatory drill participation policy, participation increased to 92% December 2024.
- **#8 Quality Improvement Data Review:** Some quality metrics not reviewed by Board quarterly. Corrected via revised Board Quality Committee meeting schedule, dashboard created for Board access.

**Overall Deficiency Profile Assessment:**
- **None were immediate jeopardy level** (life/safety threats requiring 23-day correction timelines)
- **4 significant deficiencies** (medication management, infection prevention, life safety, credentialing) + **4 minor** (pain assessment, patient education, drills, Board review)

**Accreditation Decision:** Based on the deficiency profile and corrective actions, Mercy Regional's October 2024 accreditation decision most likely was **"Accreditation"** (full accreditation with Requirements for Improvement) or **"Accreditation with Follow-up Survey"** (full accreditation maintained, but on-site follow-up required within 6 months to verify sustained compliance). #### 3. What Deemed Status Protects Against

**For Hospitals WITH Joint Commission Accreditation + Deemed Status:**

**For Hospitals WITHOUT Deemed Status (Lost JC Accreditation):**
- **Direct CMS surveys required**: CMS must conduct comprehensive validation surveys for initial certification and recertification¬π‚Å¥

#### 5. Medical Staff Credentialing - Comparison of Joint Commission vs. CMS CoPs

**Joint Commission Standard MS.06.01.05:**¬π¬≥‚Å∏
- Primary source verification required for: medical school, residency, board certification, state license, DEA registration
- Reappointment every **2 years**
- NPDB (National Practitioner Data Bank) query required at initial appointment and reappointment

**CMS Conditions of Participation 42 CFR ¬ß 482.12:**¬π¬≥‚Åπ
- Medical staff bylaws must specify qualifications for medical staff membership and clinical privileges
- Primary source verification not explicitly required in regulation (but implied in CMS State Operations Manual interpretive guidance)¬π‚Å¥‚Å∞
- No specific reappointment timeframe mandated (hospital bylaws determine frequency)

**Why Joint Commission Standard is Stricter:**
- JC explicitly requires 2-year reappointment cycle; CMS does not specify
- JC requires primary source verification for ALL credentialing elements; CMS regulation text is silent (though SOM interpretive guidance recommends)
- JC NPDB query required at reappointment; CMS only requires at initial credentialing (42 CFR ¬ß 482.12(a)(9))

**Implications for Mercy Regional:**
- October 2024 deficiency (Dr. Wilson missing primary source verification) would likely be cited by CMS as CoPs violation if CMS conducted survey¬π‚Å¥¬π

### F. Material Adverse Change (MAC) Clause Considerations

**Finding:** Loss of Joint Commission accreditation at Mercy Regional Medical Center would likely constitute a **Material Adverse Change** under standard M&A purchase agreement provisions, giving Buyer the right to terminate the transaction or renegotiate purchase price.¬≤¬≥¬π

**Legal Standard for MAC:**¬≤¬≥¬≤
- Courts require changes to be **substantial threat to overall earnings potential** measured from a **long-term perspective** (years, not months)
- Changes must be both **severe** (significant financial impact) and **lasting** (not temporary or readily curable)
- MAC clauses appear in 95% of M&A agreements; burden of proof on Buyer to demonstrate true material adverse change occurred

**Analysis - Would JC Accreditation Loss Qualify as MAC?**

| Factor | Application to Mercy Regional JC Accreditation Loss | MAC Standard Met? |
|--------|---------------------------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Severity** | Loss of deemed status ‚Üí CMS direct surveys ‚Üí potential Medicare provider agreement termination ‚Üí $864M Medicare revenue (48% of system total) at risk | ‚úÖ YES (threatens nearly half of revenue) |
| **Duration** | If Medicare provider agreement terminated, revenue loss is permanent until/unless provider agreement reinstated (typically 12-24 months minimum) | ‚úÖ YES (lasting impact, not temporary) |
| **Earnings Potential** | $864M Medicare + $396M Medicaid = $1.26B annual revenue at risk (70% of net patient revenue); cannot operate sustainably on 30% revenue | ‚úÖ YES (substantial threat to earnings) |
| **Operational Disruption** | Loss of deemed status ‚Üí mandatory CMS surveys ‚Üí diverts management time/resources; commercial payers may terminate contracts ‚Üí additional revenue loss | ‚úÖ YES (affects critical operational capacity) |
| **Regulatory/License Impact** | Joint Commission accreditation is key regulatory approval; many commercial payer contracts require JC accreditation as condition of network participation | ‚úÖ YES (affects key licenses/market access) |

**Conclusion:** Loss of Joint Commission accreditation at Mercy Regional Medical Center would **LIKELY** qualify as a Material Adverse Change under standard M&A legal standards.¬≤¬≥¬≥

**Recommendation:** Purchase Agreement should explicitly define loss of Joint Commission accreditation (or Medicare provider agreement termination) as a **per se MAC**, eliminating need for Buyer to prove severity/duration:¬≤¬≥‚Å¥

**Illustrative MAC Clause Language:**

"Notwithstanding any other provision of this Agreement, the occurrence of any of the following events shall constitute a Material Adverse Change for purposes of Section [X]: (i) any Hospital Facility receives a Preliminary Denial of Accreditation or Denial of Accreditation from The Joint Commission, (ii) any Hospital Facility loses deemed status under 42 CFR ¬ß 488.5, (iii) any Hospital Facility receives a notice of termination of its Medicare provider agreement under 42 CFR ¬ß 489.53, or (iv) CMS conducts a validation survey of any Hospital Facility and cites one or more condition-level deficiencies under 42 CFR Part 482 that are not corrected within ninety (90) days."¬≤¬≥‚Åµ
