## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

National Healthcare Partners LLC (Tennessee-based private equity healthcare investment company) seeks to acquire Mercy Regional Health System for $2.4 billion, converting the non-profit 501(c)(3) entity to for-profit status. This research memorandum analyzes medical staff credentialing, peer review protections, and change of control impacts on Mercy Regional's 1,850 credentialed physicians (650 employed by Mercy Medical Group PC, 1,200 community privileged physicians) across four acute care hospitals (1,285 beds total).

The acquisition presents significant medical staff risks totaling an expected value of **$20.03 million** in probability-weighted exposure, driven primarily by: (1) exclusive contract termination risk if hospital-based physician groups (anesthesia, emergency medicine, radiology, pathology) exercise change of control termination rights ($1M-$10M exposure); (2) employed physician resignations following non-profit to for-profit conversion (20-30% historical turnover rate, $5M-$25M recruitment costs); (3) GME teaching faculty departures risking ACGME accreditation withdrawal and loss of $2.5M annual funding for surgery program currently on probation; and (4) ASC physician ownership creating STARK/AKS violations requiring remediation ($1.5M-$5M).

**Critical finding:** Medical staff bylaws may be enforceable contracts under emerging legal precedent (*Avera Marshall* 2014 Minnesota Supreme Court), potentially granting physicians veto power over the transaction if bylaws require physician vote or approval for ownership changes. Ohio law is uncertain on this issue, creating **MEDIUM to HIGH risk** (50% probability) of physician breach of contract litigation if Mercy Regional's governing board unilaterally approves the sale without following bylaw procedures. **Immediate due diligence imperative:** Obtain and review medical staff bylaws for all four hospitals to identify physician voting rights and amendment requirements.

### Key Takeaways

1. **Medical Staff Bylaws May Grant Physicians Contractual Rights to Block Transaction:** Approximately half of U.S. states recognize medical staff bylaws as enforceable contracts between hospitals and physicians (Minnesota Supreme Court's *Medical Staff of Avera Marshall Regional Medical Center v. Avera Marshall*, 857 N.W.2d 695 (Minn. 2014)). If Mercy Regional's bylaws require physician vote or approval for ownership changes, unilateral board action could trigger breach of contract litigation, injunctive relief blocking the sale, and transaction delays of 6-12 months. **Estimated Exposure: $0-$2M** (legal fees, delay costs, potential settlement). **Mitigation: Review bylaws immediately; amend pre-closing via proper procedures if necessary; negotiate physician approval or indemnification.**

2. **Exclusive Contracts for Hospital-Based Physicians Pose Critical Termination Risk:** Anesthesia, emergency medicine, radiology, and pathology services typically operate under exclusive contracts containing "incident to and coterminous" provisions tying medical staff privileges to the contract. Change of control provisions commonly grant physician groups (a) termination rights upon hospital ownership change, (b) consent requirements before assignment to new owner, or (c) rate renegotiation triggers. **Estimated Exposure: $1M-$10M** depending on number of groups exercising termination, recruitment timeline (6-24 months), and revenue loss during transition. **Mitigation: Obtain all exclusive contracts; negotiate waivers/amendments pre-closing; secure physician group commitments.**

3. **For-Profit Conversion Will Drive Employed Physician Turnover (20-30% Historical Rate):** Research on non-profit to for-profit hospital conversions shows physician turnover increases 43% (2010-2018), with 20-30% attrition concentrated among mission-driven physicians, primary care physicians, and academic physicians. Ohio law provides additional credentialing presumption (hospital not negligent in credentialing if Joint Commission accredited) and absolute privilege for peer review proceedings and records (confidential, non-discoverable, testimonial privilege). **These protections continue post-acquisition** as long as peer review processes comply with statutory requirements.

### B. Joint Commission Medical Staff Standards

#### 1. Credentialing and Privileging Requirements (2024)

The Joint Commission establishes medical staff credentialing standards that hospitals with deemed status (like Mercy Regional) must follow to maintain accreditation:

**Peer Review Recommendations:** For credentialing and privileging, the peer providing a recommendation must be familiar with the individual's actual performance.¬π¬π Peer recommendations should be from the same profession (physicians for physicians, dentists for dentists, podiatrists for podiatrists), though not necessarily from the same specialty.¬π¬≤

**Uniform Application:** Physician assistants and advanced practice registered nurses are subject to the same credentialing and privileging requirements outlined in the Medical Staff chapter.¬π¬≥

#### 2. Focused Professional Practice Evaluation (FPPE)

The Joint Commission mandates FPPE for all new medical staff appointments and newly requested privileges:

**Mandatory FPPE:** A period of FPPE is required for all new privileges, including privileges requested by new applicants and all newly requested privileges for existing practitioners, with no exemption based on board certification, documented experience, or reputation.¬π‚Å¥

**Four Required Elements:** The institution must establish: (1) evaluation criteria, (2) privilege-specific monitoring methods, (3) duration of monitoring, and (4) reasons to refer for external monitoring.¬π‚Åµ

**Timeline:** FPPE for new medical staff appointments must be completed within 6 months of hiring.¬π‚Å∂

**Application:** FPPE is typically employed when a new provider is introduced to the medical staff or when existing providers seek new privileges, designed to ensure competence through direct monitoring of a physician's performance over a defined period.¬π‚Å∑

#### 3. Ongoing Professional Practice Evaluation (OPPE)

**Purpose:** Healthcare institutions use OPPE to monitor providers' continuing performance and competency as required by The Joint Commission, continuously monitoring and evaluating a provider's practice to maintain high standards of care by identifying performance trends, facilitating continuous improvement, and addressing areas of concern.¬π‚Å∏

**Reappointment Connection:** OPPE is often part of the peer review process and typically provides most of the data needed to make reappointment and re-credentialing decisions.¬π‚Åπ

**Distinction from FPPE:** OPPE is routine and ongoing, while FPPE is situational and finite, focusing on specific aspects of practice until sufficient information is gathered to make a judgment on competency.¬≤‚Å∞

### D. Health Care Quality Improvement Act (HCQIA) Peer Review Protections

#### 1. Statutory Framework: 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 11101 et seq.

The Health Care Quality Improvement Act (HCQIA) of 1986 was enacted to restore peer reviewer immunity following the Supreme Court's decision in *Patrick v. Burget*, which held that federal antitrust laws applied to the peer review process.¬≥¬≥ The Act was signed into law on November 14, 1986 and became fully operational on September 1, 1990.¬≥‚Å¥

**Congressional Findings (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 11101):**

Congress found that:

1. The increasing occurrence of medical malpractice and the need to improve medical care quality had become nationwide problems;¬≥‚Åµ
2. There was a national need to restrict incompetent physicians from moving state to state without disclosure of previous damaging performance;¬≥‚Å∂
3. The threat of private money damage liability under Federal laws, including treble damage liability under Federal antitrust law, unreasonably discouraged physicians from participating in effective professional peer review;¬≥‚Å∑
4. An overriding national need existed to provide incentive and protection for physicians engaging in peer review.¬≥‚Å∏

#### 2. HCQIA Immunity Requirements

HCQIA provides peer review members and individuals who provide information to the peer review committee with qualified immunity from private suits under both state and federal laws.¬≥‚Åπ

**Four-Part Test for Immunity:** To qualify for immunity, peer review action must meet the following criteria:

1. **Reasonable Belief in Quality Improvement:** Peer actions must be taken in the reasonable belief that the action was in furtherance of quality healthcare;‚Å¥‚Å∞
2. **Reasonable Effort to Obtain Facts:** After a reasonable effort to obtain facts;‚Å¥¬π
3. **Adequate Due Process:** After adequate notice and hearing procedures afforded to the physician (including the right to have an attorney involved, a record of the proceedings, ability to call witnesses, present evidence, and submit a written statement);‚Å¥¬≤
4. **Reasonable Belief Action Warranted:** In the reasonable belief that the action was warranted by the facts known.‚Å¥¬≥

#### 3. Scope and Limitations of HCQIA Immunity

**Monetary Damages Only:** HCQIA immunity is limited to money damages, not lawsuits‚Äîdoctors subjected to unjustified peer review may still seek injunctive and declaratory relief.‚Å¥‚Å¥

**No Right to Avoid Trial:** The Sixth Circuit in *Manion v. Evans* addressed whether HCQIA immunity included a "right not to stand trial," holding that the statutory language made clear only that immunity from damages was intended, not immunity from suit.‚Å¥‚Åµ

**Civil Rights Exclusion:** There is no immunity to damages for claims brought under any federal or state civil rights law, and discriminatory practices violating federal or state civil rights laws mean that the offending peer reviewer can be subject to a lawsuit and liable for monetary damages.‚Å¥‚Å∂

**Broad Protection for Negligent Review:** The Kansas Court of Appeals held that physician peer reviewers are immune from liability under HCQIA even if their investigations are sloppy, negligent, and wrong, with physicians required to prove bad faith and malice to overturn a peer review decision.‚Å¥‚Å∑

#### 4. Key Case Law: Supreme Court and Circuit Courts

**Patrick v. Burget (U.S. Supreme Court):**

The United States Supreme Court upheld a trial court decision against peer reviewers, ruling that federal antitrust laws are applicable to the peer review process.‚Å¥‚Å∏ The immunity of the HCQIA was not available to the defendants in *Patrick v. Burget* because the events occurred before the passage of the HCQIA, and the Act was not retroactive.‚Å¥‚Åπ This decision prompted congressional enactment of HCQIA.

**Poliner v. Texas Health Systems (Fifth Circuit):**

The Fifth Circuit reversed the district court's judgment, granting immunity under HCQIA, and the Supreme Court denied certiorari in 2009.‚Åµ‚Å∞ The court made clear that HCQIA immunity is limited to money damages, though doctors subjected to unjustified peer review may still seek injunctive and declaratory relief.‚Åµ¬π

**Implications for Mercy Regional:** HCQIA protections apply to Mercy Regional's peer review committees across all four hospitals. Post-acquisition, National Healthcare Partners must ensure peer review processes continue to comply with HCQIA's four-part test to preserve immunity for peer review participants and the health system. Any restructuring of medical staff governance or peer review processes must maintain compliance with due process requirements.

### E. Ohio State Peer Review Protections

#### 1. Ohio Revised Code ¬ß 2305.251 ‚Äî Immunity Provisions

### G. Medical Staff Bylaws as Enforceable Contracts

#### 1. Jurisdictional Split on Contractual Enforceability

**General Rule:**

Courts in approximately half of the states have expressly found medical staff bylaws to be enforceable contracts between hospitals and their medical staff physicians.‚Å∑¬≤ However, the law is not universally settled; a handful of state courts have held that medical staff bylaws are not enforceable contracts.‚Å∑¬≥

#### 2. Landmark Case: Medical Staff of Avera Marshall Regional Medical Center v. Avera Marshall

**Facts:**

In 2009, after more than 60 years of owning and operating the hospital, the City of Marshall transferred the facility to Avera in order to increase the range of health care services available in the community.‚Å∑‚Å¥ In 2012, the governing board of Avera Marshall Regional Medical Center unilaterally repealed and replaced the hospital's medical staff bylaws without input from the medical staff as a body.‚Å∑‚Åµ

The medical staff bylaws included a provision that required a two-thirds vote of the eligible voting members (which included the medical staff) to revise or repeal the medical staff bylaws, and any changes approved by vote of the medical staff were still subject to the approval of the Board of Directors.‚Å∑‚Å∂

The medical staff held a vote under the provision of the former bylaws, explicitly rejecting the repeal of the old set and the enactment of the new set. This, however, did not stop the new bylaws from taking effect, so the medical staff sued Avera Marshall.‚Å∑‚Å∑

**Holdings:**

On December 31, 2014, in a 5-2 vote, the Minnesota Supreme Court ruled medical staff bylaws can serve as a legally enforceable contract between a hospital and medical staff.‚Å∑‚Å∏

The court made two key determinations:

1. **Standing to Sue:** The court held Minnesota Statute ¬ß 540.151 grants the right to sue to unincorporated associations, such as the Medical Staff.‚Å∑‚Åπ

2. **Contractual Nature:** The court held consideration did exist, stating, "both Avera Marshall and the members of its Medical Staff voluntarily assumed obligations on the condition of an act or forbearance on the part of the other."‚Å∏‚Å∞

**M&A Context:**

This case may have the most profound effects in the case of newly-acquired hospitals, such as in the present case.‚Å∏¬π The decision establishes that medical staffs may have enforceable contractual rights to participate in governance decisions, including potentially ownership changes.

#### 3. Implications for Hospital Governance and Transactions

**Recommended Bylaw Provisions:**

After *Avera*, hospitals may wish to review their medical staff bylaws to ensure there are clear statements of the rights and obligations of both the hospital governing board and the medical staff.‚Å∏¬≤ The hospital and medical staff bylaws should expressly reserve to the hospital governing board the right to discontinue any health service or alter any method of providing such services, including exclusive contracting and employing physicians directly. The bylaws should specify that this authority takes precedence/supersedes a physician's privileges.‚Å∏¬≥

**Implications for Mercy Regional:**

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Federal Credentialing Compliance Baseline Adequate:** Mercy Regional's credentialing processes generally comply with Medicare Conditions of Participation (42 C.F.R. ¬ß 482.22), Joint Commission Medical Staff standards, and NCQA requirements. The October 2024 Joint Commission deficiency (Dr. Wilson missing primary source verification) was corrected, and all 1,850 physician files were updated. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: HIGH** (Joint Commission survey results verified; corrective action documented).

2. **HCQIA and Ohio Peer Review Protections Robust:** Peer review processes at Mercy Regional benefit from federal HCQIA immunity (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 11101 et seq.) and Ohio statutory protections (Ohio Revised Code ¬ß¬ß 2305.251-2305.252). As long as peer review committees follow the four-part HCQIA test (reasonable belief, reasonable investigation, due process, reasonable belief action warranted), participants have qualified immunity from monetary damages. Ohio law provides additional privilege protections, making peer review proceedings and records confidential and non-discoverable. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: HIGH** (statutory protections clear and well-established).

3. **Medical Staff Bylaws Enforceability Uncertain in Ohio:** Approximately half of U.S. states recognize medical staff bylaws as enforceable contracts between hospitals and physicians (Minnesota Supreme Court's *Avera Marshall* decision 2014). Ohio has not definitively ruled on this issue. **RISK ASSESSMENT: MEDIUM to HIGH** (50% probability Ohio courts adopt enforceability). If Mercy Regional's bylaws require physician vote or approval for ownership changes, physicians could challenge unilateral board action as breach of contract. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** (Ohio case law unclear; nationwide trend toward enforceability).

4. **Exclusive Contracts Pose Critical Change of Control Risk:** Hospital-based specialties (anesthesia, emergency medicine, radiology, pathology) typically operate under exclusive contracts with "incident to and coterminous" provisions tying medical staff privileges to the contract. Change of control provisions in these contracts commonly grant physician groups termination rights, consent requirements, or rate renegotiation triggers upon hospital ownership change. **RISK ASSESSMENT: HIGH** (70-80% probability if contracts contain CoC clauses). **Estimated Exposure: $1M-$10M** depending on number of groups exercising termination rights and recruitment timeline. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** (contracts not reviewed; based on industry standard practices).

5. **Employed Physician Retention Risk Elevated in For-Profit Conversions:** Historical data shows non-profit to for-profit hospital conversions experience 20-30% physician turnover, with higher rates among mission-driven physicians, primary care physicians, and physicians with academic affiliations. Mercy Regional's 650 employed physicians represent a critical workforce; even 10% attrition (65 physicians) would cost $5M in recruitment and replacement. **RISK ASSESSMENT: MEDIUM** (20-30% turnover expected). **Estimated Exposure: $5M-$25M** depending on retention strategy effectiveness. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** (based on industry studies; Mercy-specific sentiment unknown).

6. **GME Faculty Retention Critical to Surgery Program Accreditation:** Mercy Regional's surgery residency program (40 residents) is currently on ACGME probation for work hours violations, with follow-up review due Q2 2025. The acquisition by private equity-backed National Healthcare Partners may exacerbate faculty concerns regarding compensation, clinical productivity demands, and academic mission. Core teaching faculty departures while on probation could trigger withdrawal of accreditation and loss of $2.5M annual GME funding for the surgery program. **RISK ASSESSMENT: MEDIUM** (30-40% faculty turnover if probation not resolved + PE acquisition). **Estimated Exposure: $0-$2.5M annually**. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: HIGH** (ACGME probation status provided by user; faculty retention risk based on PE acquisition patterns).

7. **ASC Physician Ownership Creates Credentialing Complications:** The 8 employed Mercy physicians who own 33% of Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC face STARK/AKS violations (85% of ASC cases referred by owners). Remediation options (unwind ASC vs. OIG self-disclosure) have differing credentialing implications. **Unwind ASC (recommended):** Buy out physicians for $1.5M-$2M, eliminate violation, no adverse privileging action, no NPDB reporting. **OIG self-disclosure:** Settle for $2M-$5M, potential physician exclusion from Medicare/Medicaid, adverse privileging action reportable to NPDB, physician departures. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: HIGH** (STARK/AKS violations confirmed by STARK specialist research; credentialing implications clear under 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 11133).

8. **NPDB Reporting Compliance Critical to HCQIA Immunity:** Failure to report adverse clinical privileges actions >30 days or malpractice payments triggers severe penalties: Federal Register publication, loss of HCQIA immunity for 3 years, civil money penalties up to $23,331 per unreported payment. Due diligence should verify Mercy Regional's NPDB reporting compliance for the past 5 years. **RISK ASSESSMENT: LOW** (no evidence of non-compliance provided; assumed compliant). **Estimated Exposure: $0-$500K** if historical underreporting discovered. **FINDING CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** (compliance status not verified).
