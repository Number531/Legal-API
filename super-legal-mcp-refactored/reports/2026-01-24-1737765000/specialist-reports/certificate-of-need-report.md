### Critical Finding: Ohio Does NOT Require CON for Acute Care Hospital Beds

**This analysis reveals a fundamental discrepancy between the scenario facts and Ohio law.** The scenario describes a Certificate of Need (CON) application filed with the Ohio Department of Health for Mercy Regional Medical Center's 50-bed acute care hospital expansion, with $125 million in project value at risk pending regulatory approval. However, **Ohio does not require Certificate of Need for acute care hospital beds.**¬π

Ohio's CON program applies exclusively to **long-term care facilities** (nursing homes, skilled nursing facilities, and hospital-based skilled nursing beds).¬≤ Ohio regulates only **one service** under CON laws, making it one of the least restrictive CON states in the nation.¬≥ Recent amendments to Ohio's CON statute (House Bill 33, effective September 30, 2024) did not reinstate CON requirements for acute care beds ‚Äî they relate only to the existing long-term care CON program.‚Å¥

**Implication for Transaction:** If Mercy Regional Medical Center is located in Columbus, Ohio (as stated in the scenario), the 50-bed medical/surgical expansion does **NOT require CON approval**, the $125M project is **NOT at risk** from CON denial, and the transaction team should **eliminate CON risk** from the transaction risk registry.

**Alternative Interpretation:** The scenario facts may describe a hypothetical hospital in a different state requiring CON for acute care beds (e.g., North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia, Tennessee, Maryland, Virginia, West Virginia), or may be a hypothetical demonstration scenario. This report addresses **both possibilities:**

- **Cost:** $500K-$1M legal fees
- **Duration:** 12-24 months for court decision
- **Recommendation:** File appeal to preserve rights, but do not delay transaction or rely on appeal success

### Appendix D: Purchase Price Adjustment Formula ‚Äî Worked Example

**Scenario:** CON application denied (final); Buyer elects to proceed to Closing with purchase price adjustment.

**Method 1: Project Value at Risk**
- Total project cost: $125,000,000
- Purchase price reduction (full project cost): **$125,000,000**

**Method 2: Lost EBITDA Capitalization (NPV)**

| Year | Foregone EBITDA | Discount Factor (8.5%) | Present Value |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|---------------|
| 1 | $4,000,000 | 0.9217 | $3,686,800 |
| 2 | $6,000,000 | 0.8495 | $5,097,000 |
| 3 | $8,000,000 | 0.7829 | $6,263,200 |
| 4 | $10,000,000 | 0.7216 | $7,216,000 |
| 5 | $12,000,000 | 0.6650 | $7,980,000 |
| 6 | $12,000,000 | 0.6129 | $7,354,800 |
| 7 | $12,000,000 | 0.5649 | $6,778,800 |
| 8 | $12,000,000 | 0.5207 | $6,248,400 |
| 9 | $12,000,000 | 0.4799 | $5,758,800 |
| 10 | $12,000,000 | 0.4423 | $5,307,600 |
| **TOTAL (10-year NPV)** | ‚Äî | ‚Äî | **$61,691,400** |

**Purchase Price Reduction:** Lesser of $125M (Method 1) or $61.7M (Method 2) = **$61,691,400** (rounded to **$62M**)

**Alternative Calculation (Perpetuity Method):**
- Steady-state EBITDA (Years 5-10): $12M annually
- Capitalization rate (WACC): 8.5%
- Terminal value (Year 5): $12M / 0.085 = $141.2M
- Discounted to present (Year 0): $141.2M √ó 0.6650 = $93.9M
- **Purchase price reduction (perpetuity method): $93.9M**

**Recommendation:** Use **10-year NPV method ($62M)** as conservative estimate. Perpetuity method ($94M) may overstate value (assumes 50-bed expansion operates in perpetuity without need for future capital investment, which is unrealistic). Full project cost method ($125M) does not account for financing already expended (sunk costs for application, architectural plans, etc.).
