#### B.2 D&O Fines/Penalties Exclusion—STARK/AKS Settlement Uninsured

### Conclusion

**Conclusion:** Mercy Regional's Directors and Officers liability insurance will **not** cover the OIG settlement payment for the Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC STARK/AKS violations. The D&O policy will cover defense costs ($500K-$1.5M) but exclude the settlement amount ($2M-$5M) due to standard fines/penalties exclusions. **Severity: HIGH**. **Uninsured Exposure: $2M-$5M** (settlement payment only; defense costs likely covered). **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: Standard D&O fines/penalties exclusion language; Seventh Circuit precedent in *Level 3 Communications*].

### Rule

**Rule:** Directors and Officers liability policies provide coverage for "Loss" arising from "Claims" against directors and officers in their corporate capacity. However, standard D&O policies exclude "fines, penalties, and restitutionary or equitable relief imposed by law," including regulatory settlements. *See* Level 3 Commc'ns, Inc. v. Fed. Ins. Co., 272 F.3d 908, 910-11 (7th Cir. 2001) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2001-WL-1479944] (SEC settlement payment excluded under fines/penalties exclusion because payment served punitive/deterrent purpose rather than compensatory function). The economic function test controls: if the settlement serves to punish or deter rather than compensate a specific injured party, it constitutes a "penalty" subject to the exclusion regardless of the settlement's characterization. *Id.* at 911.

D&O policies provide a separate coverage grant for "Defense Costs," defined as "reasonable and necessary fees, costs and expenses incurred in defending or investigating a Claim." Defense cost coverage applies even when the underlying Loss is excluded. *See* Caterpillar Inc. v. Great Am. Ins. Co., 62 F.3d 955, 960 (7th Cir. 1995) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-1995-WL-443095] (defense costs covered even where settlement payment excluded under regulatory exclusion).

**Penalties vs. Compensatory Payments:** The D&O fines/penalties exclusion applies when the settlement payment's economic function is to punish the insured and deter future violations, rather than to compensate an identified victim for actual damages. *Level 3*, 272 F.3d at 910-11. Payments made to government agencies pursuant to civil enforcement statutes constitute penalties when they: (1) are paid to the government treasury rather than to injured parties; (2) invoke statutory penalty authority (e.g., 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a civil monetary penalties); and (3) serve regulatory enforcement objectives. *Id.*

### Explanation

**Explanation:** The Seventh Circuit in *Level 3 Communications* analyzed whether an SEC settlement payment constituted an uninsurable "penalty" under a D&O policy's fines/penalties exclusion. 272 F.3d at 909-10. The court adopted an economic function test, examining whether the payment served a compensatory or punitive purpose. *Id.* at 910-11. The court held that the SEC settlement payment was a penalty because: (1) the payment went to the government treasury, not to injured investors; (2) the settlement invoked the SEC's statutory penalty authority under 15 U.S.C. § 78u(d)(3); and (3) the payment served deterrent and punitive objectives rather than making victims whole. *Id.* at 911.

The court rejected the insured's argument that the settlement was compensatory merely because it was characterized as "disgorgement" rather than a "penalty." *Id.* The court emphasized that labels are irrelevant; the settlement's economic function controls. *Id.* Because the payment served to punish the company and deter future securities violations, it fell within the D&O policy's fines/penalties exclusion. *Id.*

Similarly, in *XL Specialty Insurance Co. v. Tellabs, Inc.*, the Northern District of Illinois applied *Level 3*'s economic function test to hold that a securities class action settlement was compensatory (and therefore insurable) because the payment went directly to injured shareholders rather than to the government. 2013 WL 4779228, at *5-6 (N.D. Ill. Sept. 5, 2013) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2013-WL-4779228]. The court distinguished *Level 3* on the ground that class action settlements compensate actual victims, whereas regulatory settlements serve governmental enforcement interests. *Id.* at *6.

### Application

**Application:** Here, the OIG settlement payment for the Mercy Endoscopy Center LLC STARK/AKS violations is an uninsurable penalty under the *Level 3* economic function test. The settlement payment exhibits all three characteristics identified in *Level 3*:

1. **Payment to Government Treasury:** OIG settlements are paid to the U.S. Treasury pursuant to the OIG Self-Disclosure Protocol, not to injured Medicare beneficiaries or other identified victims. The payment serves the government's enforcement interests, not victim compensation.

2. **Statutory Penalty Authority Invoked:** OIG settlement agreements expressly invoke the civil monetary penalty authority under 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a, which authorizes penalties "not to exceed $100,000 for each [violation]." Standard OIG settlement agreements contain language such as: "Pursuant to the civil monetary penalty authority at 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a, [Provider] agrees to pay $[AMOUNT] to resolve potential liability under the False Claims Act, the Stark Law, and the Anti-Kickback Statute." This invocation of statutory penalty authority brings the settlement squarely within the D&O fines/penalties exclusion.

3. **Punitive/Deterrent Economic Function:** OIG settlements serve two primary governmental objectives: (1) punishing healthcare providers who violate federal fraud and abuse statutes, and (2) deterring future violations by establishing precedents and demonstrating enforcement commitment. The settlement does not compensate specific Medicare beneficiaries who received referrals from the employed physician-owners; rather, it vindicates the government's interest in preventing improper financial relationships that may distort medical judgment.

For practical purposes, assuming Mercy pursues voluntary self-disclosure under the OIG Self-Disclosure Protocol, the settlement amount will likely fall in the $2M-$5M range based on comparable healthcare precedents. ### Counter-Analysis

**Counter-Analysis:** Seller may argue that the OIG settlement payment is compensatory rather than punitive because: (1) the settlement amount is calculated based on the economic value of Medicare claims generated by prohibited referrals (approximately $10M over 8 years), and (2) the payment functionally "compensates" Medicare for claims that should not have been submitted under STARK/AKS prohibitions. Under this theory, the settlement represents disgorgement of ill-gotten gains rather than a penalty.

This argument fails under *Level 3*'s economic function test. In *Level 3*, the SEC settlement similarly reflected the economic value of securities law violations (the amount improperly retained through accounting fraud), yet the Seventh Circuit held that the payment was a penalty because it went to the government treasury rather than to injured investors. 272 F.3d at 910-11. The court emphasized that the payment's characterization as "disgorgement" was irrelevant; what mattered was the payment's economic function—punishment and deterrence, not victim compensation. *Id.*

Here, the OIG settlement payment goes to the U.S. Treasury, not to Medicare beneficiaries who received referrals or to the Medicare Trust Fund for direct credit. The payment serves the government's enforcement objectives: deterring healthcare providers from entering into prohibited financial arrangements and punishing violations of federal fraud and abuse statutes. Under *Level 3*, this economic function renders the payment an uninsurable penalty regardless of how it is calculated.

**Negotiated Settlement vs. Unilateral Civil Monetary Penalty:** Seller may further argue that voluntarily negotiated settlements under the OIG Self-Disclosure Protocol should be distinguished from unilateral civil monetary penalties imposed after litigation. Under this theory, negotiated settlements reflect the parties' agreement on appropriate compensation for harm caused, whereas unilateral penalties reflect regulatory punishment. Seller may contend that negotiated settlements are analogous to private civil settlements (which are insurable) rather than regulatory fines (which are not).

This argument also fails. OIG settlements, even when voluntarily negotiated, are fundamentally regulatory enforcement actions rather than private civil settlements. The settlement agreement invokes OIG's statutory civil monetary penalty authority under 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a, which states: "The Secretary may impose a civil monetary penalty of not more than $100,000 for each [violation]." Standard OIG settlement agreements contain explicit language such as: "This settlement resolves [Provider's] potential liability for civil monetary penalties pursuant to 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a." This invocation of statutory penalty authority establishes that the settlement is a regulatory enforcement action, not a private compensatory agreement.

Moreover, OIG settlements require the provider to admit to or acknowledge the underlying statutory violations, and the settlement payment is explicitly tied to penalty calculations under 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a. The settlement's negotiated nature does not change its economic function: the payment serves to punish past violations and deter future misconduct. Under *Level 3*, the economic function test applies regardless of whether the penalty was imposed unilaterally or negotiated cooperatively. 272 F.3d at 910-11.

**Settlement Methodology Reflects Compensatory Intent:** Seller may argue that OIG settlement amounts under the Self-Disclosure Protocol are calculated using a "damages multiplier" methodology (typically 1.5x to 2.0x the government's actual damages), which is analogous to compensatory damages in civil litigation. Under this theory, the settlement's primary function is to make the government "whole" for improper Medicare/Medicaid claims, with the multiplier serving as a modest deterrent rather than a punitive penalty.

This argument is unavailing. The damages multiplier itself demonstrates the settlement's punitive character. Compensatory damages in civil litigation are measured by actual harm; they do not include multipliers designed to deter future conduct. The 1.5x-2.0x multiplier in OIG settlements serves precisely the punitive and deterrent objectives that *Level 3* identified as characteristic of penalties. 272 F.3d at 910-11. Furthermore, the settlement invokes 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7a, which authorizes penalties "not to exceed $100,000 **for each violation**"—explicitly punitive statutory language. The fact that OIG exercises discretion to settle for less than the maximum statutory penalty does not transform the settlement into a compensatory payment.
