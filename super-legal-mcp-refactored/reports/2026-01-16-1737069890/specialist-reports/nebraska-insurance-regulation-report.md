## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report analyzes Nebraska insurance regulatory requirements and risks affecting American Financial Holdings LLC's proposed $2.9 billion acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC), a Nebraska-domiciled life insurer operating in 38 states plus the District of Columbia. LLIC's Risk-Based Capital (RBC) ratio of **188%** falls below the NAIC's **200% Company Action Level (CAL) threshold**, triggering mandatory regulatory filing and approval requirements that create material transaction risks. Additionally, LLIC's $850 million Vermont captive reinsurance structure presents a **10-15% probability** of Nebraska Department of Insurance (DOI) disallowance, which would reduce LLIC's RBC ratio to **114%**—triggering potential regulatory seizure.

The acquisition requires three critical Nebraska DOI approvals: (1) **RBC Plan approval** for capital injection remediation, (2) **Surplus note issuance approval** for the preferred capital structure, and (3) **Form A holding company acquisition approval** under Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-2126 to 44-2127. Combined regulatory timeline: **90-120 days** from complete filings, presenting schedule risk to the Q3 2025 target closing date. Probability-weighted expected capital requirement is **$391 million**, significantly exceeding the $150 million base case capital injection, necessitating $500M-$750M committed capital facility to address bear case and worst case scenarios.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. NAIC Risk-Based Capital (RBC) Model Act Framework

#### 1. RBC Action Level Structure

The NAIC Risk-Based Capital (RBC) Model Act establishes a four-tiered regulatory intervention framework based on an insurer's Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) relative to its Authorized Control Level RBC (ACL RBC). The system functions as an early warning mechanism with progressively stringent regulatory actions as capital adequacy declines.¹

**Company Action Level (200% of ACL RBC):**
- **Trigger:** Total Adjusted Capital falls between 150% and 200% of ACL RBC
- **Required Action:** Insurer must submit an RBC Plan within 45 days identifying conditions contributing to capital deficiency and proposing corrective actions²
- **Plan Requirements:** Must include financial projections for current year plus at least 4 years (life insurers), key assumptions, sensitivity analysis, and assessment of business quality and risk exposure³
- **Regulatory Review:** Commissioner reviews plan for adequacy; unsatisfactory plans must be resubmitted within 45 days of notification⁴
- **LLIC Status:** RBC ratio of 188% falls below the 200% CAL threshold, triggering mandatory RBC Plan filing

**Regulatory Action Level (150% of ACL RBC):**
- **Trigger:** TAC falls between 100% and 150% of ACL RBC
- **Required Action:** Insurer must file RBC Plan or revised RBC Plan within 45 days
- **Commissioner Authority:** Must perform examinations or analyses of insurer's business and operations deemed necessary; may issue corrective orders⁵
- **Enhanced Oversight:** Commissioner may prohibit distributions to shareholders, restrict asset transactions, and require prior approval for significant business changes

**Authorized Control Level (100% of ACL RBC):**
- **Trigger:** TAC falls between 70% and 100% of ACL RBC
- **Commissioner Authority:** May place insurer under regulatory control (rehabilitation or liquidation) if commissioner determines such action is in best interests of policyholders and creditors⁶
- **Discretionary Seizure:** Authorization (not mandate) allows regulatory flexibility based on individual circumstances

**Mandatory Control Level (70% of ACL RBC):**
- **Trigger:** TAC falls below 70% of ACL RBC
- **Required Action:** Commissioner **must** place insurer under regulatory control⁷
- **Statutory Mandate:** No discretion; seizure is mandatory to protect policyholders

**Critical Implication for LLIC:** At 188% RBC ratio, LLIC is 38 percentage points above the Regulatory Action Level (150%) where commissioner intervention authority expands significantly, and 88 percentage points above Authorized Control Level (100%) where seizure becomes possible. The proposed $350M capital injection targeting a 204% RBC ratio would restore LLIC to above the 200% CAL threshold.

#### 2. RBC Formula Components for Life Insurance Companies

The NAIC life insurance RBC formula calculates required capital based on four primary risk categories (C-0 through C-4), using a square root of sum of squares methodology to reflect risk diversification benefits.⁸

**C-0: Asset Risk - Affiliated Investments**
- Covers investments in affiliates and subsidiaries
- 100% capital charge to reflect concentration risk and limited liquidity

**C-1: Asset Risk - General Account Assets**
- **C-1cs (Credit Risk):** Bonds, mortgages, and other fixed-income investments; capital charges range from 0.4% (AAA bonds) to 30% (below-investment-grade)
- **C-1o (Other Asset Risk):** Real estate, equity investments, derivatives; capital charges vary by asset class
- For LLIC: $1.02B in below-investment-grade bonds (likely attracting 10-30% RBC factors) materially contributes to C-1 requirements

**C-2: Insurance Risk (Mortality/Morbidity)**
- Reflects risk of adverse mortality experience on life insurance products
- Charges vary by product type: term life (lower), whole life (moderate), universal life (higher due to volatility)
- Group life and annuity mortality risk included

**C-3: Interest Rate Risk and Market Risk**
- **C-3 Phase I:** Asset/liability duration mismatch risk
- **C-3 Phase II (C-3a):** Interest rate risk on products with guarantees (fixed annuities, guaranteed UL)
- **C-3 Phase III (C-3b):** Market risk on variable products with guarantees (VUL, GMWB on variable annuities)
- **C-3c:** Asset-liability modeling requirements
- For LLIC: Duration mismatch of -0.7 years and $800M GMWB separate account exposure contribute materially to C-3 requirements

**C-4: Business Risk**
- **C-4a:** Administrative expenses and general business risk
- **C-4b:** Guaranty fund assessments and other operational risks

**RBC Formula Calculation:**⁹
```
RBC = C-0 + C-4a + √[(C-1cs + C-3c)² + (C-1o + C-3a)² + C-2² + C-3b² + C-4b²]
```

The square root formula (covariance adjustment) recognizes that simultaneous adverse experience across all risk categories is unlikely, allowing partial diversification credit.

**Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) Components:**¹⁰
- Statutory capital and surplus
- Asset Valuation Reserve (AVR)
- 50% of dividend liability
- Less: non-admitted assets, unauthorized reinsurance receivables, certain deferred tax assets

**Company Action Level RBC:** 200% × RBC amount calculated above
**Authorized Control Level RBC:** 100% × RBC amount (= 50% of CAL)

**LLIC Calculation Analysis:**

### B. Nebraska Statutory Framework for RBC Compliance

#### 1. Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-6015 — RBC Reporting Requirements

Nebraska adopted the NAIC Risk-Based Capital Model Act through Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-6001 to 44-6036 (Insurance Risk-Based Capital Act).¹¹ Section 44-6015 establishes annual reporting obligations:

**Filing Deadline:** Every domestic insurer and domestic health organization must "annually, on or prior to March 1" submit an RBC report to the Director of Insurance showing capital levels as of December 31 of the preceding calendar year.¹²

**Calculation Methodologies by Entity Type:**

- **Life and Health Insurers:** Must account for asset risk, adverse insurance experience risk, interest rate risk, and other relevant business risks.¹³

- **Property and Casualty Insurers:** Must factor asset risk, credit risk, underwriting risk, and other business risks.¹⁴

- **Health Organizations:** Follow same formula structure as property and casualty insurers, addressing asset risk, credit risk, underwriting risk, and related factors.¹⁵

**Capital Adequacy Standard:** The statute emphasizes that "an excess of capital over the amount produced by the risk-based capital requirements" is desirable and that entities should maintain capital above mandated RBC levels.¹⁶ This reflects regulatory preference for capital buffers beyond minimum thresholds.

**Director's Adjustment Authority:** Director may adjust inaccurate RBC reports and must notify entities of adjustments with written explanations.¹⁷

**LLIC Reporting Obligation:** As a Nebraska-domiciled life insurer, LLIC must file RBC reports annually by March 1. The 188% RBC ratio disclosed in the research plan would have been reported in LLIC's most recent March 1, 2025 filing (covering calendar year 2024 capital levels).

#### 2. Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-6016 — Company Action Level Event

**Definition:** A company action level event occurs when an insurer files an RBC report showing that "total adjusted capital is greater than or equal to its regulatory action level risk-based capital but less than its company action level risk-based capital."¹⁸ For life insurers, this means TAC between 150% and 200% of ACL RBC.

**RBC Plan Filing Requirements:**¹⁹

Upon triggering a company action level event, the insurer must prepare and submit an RBC Plan to the Director identifying:
1. Conditions contributing to the capital deficiency
2. Proposals for corrective actions the insurer intends to take
3. Financial projections for current year plus at least 4 years (life insurers) or 2 years (health organizations)
4. Key assumptions underlying projections
5. Sensitivity analysis demonstrating impact of assumption changes
6. Assessment of business quality and risk exposure

**Filing Deadlines:**²⁰
- **Initial Plan:** 45 days from the company action level event occurrence
- **Revised Plan (if required):** 45 days from Director's notification of unsatisfactory plan

**Multi-State Filing Requirement:** Domestic insurers must file copies of the RBC Plan with insurance commissioners of other states upon request, if those states maintain similar RBC regulatory requirements.²¹ Given LLIC's 38-state + DC licensing, Nebraska DOI may coordinate review with other states.

**Director's Review Authority:** Director reviews RBC Plan for adequacy and may require revisions if plan does not adequately address capital deficiency or if proposed corrective actions are insufficient.²²

### D. Capital Injection Structures — Surplus Notes vs. Subordinated Debt vs. Equity

#### 1. Surplus Notes — Preferred Capital Instrument for Insurance RBC Compliance

**Regulatory Treatment:**

Surplus notes are unsecured debt obligations issued by insurance companies that are deeply subordinated to policyholder claims and all other creditor claims.³³ Unlike conventional debt, surplus note interest and principal payments require prior approval from the state insurance commissioner, making them unique hybrid instruments.³⁴

Under NAIC Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP), surplus notes are treated as **surplus (equity)** and included in the issuer's Total Adjusted Capital for RBC calculation purposes.³⁵ This contrasts with GAAP treatment, where surplus notes would be classified as debt liabilities.

**Key Features:**³⁶

1. **Commissioner Approval Requirement:** All interest and principal payments require prior written approval from the domiciliary state insurance commissioner. Commissioner may deny approval if payments would impair insurer's financial condition.

2. **Subordination:** Surplus notes are subordinate to:
   - All policyholder claims
   - General creditor claims
   - Other debt obligations
   - Only senior to common equity

3. **Maturity:** Typical maturities range from 10 to 50 years; some issued in perpetuity

4. **Call Provisions:** Often include optional redemption at par after 5-10 years, and mandatory tax event redemption if interest deductibility is threatened by tax law changes

**Tax Treatment:**³⁷

Despite regulatory classification as surplus, surplus notes are **treated as debt for federal income tax purposes**. This allows:
- Interest payments are **tax-deductible** to the insurer (subject to IRC §163 limitations)
- Dual benefit: Regulatory capital credit + tax deduction
- Tax event optional redemption provisions typically included to preserve deductibility

The IRS has consistently ruled that surplus notes constitute bona fide debt instruments based on their fixed maturity, stated interest rate, and arm's-length terms, notwithstanding the commissioner approval requirement for payments.³⁸

**RBC Impact for LLIC:**

If LLIC issues $150M surplus notes (research plan alternative):
- **Pre-Injection TAC:** $1.85B
- **Surplus Note Proceeds:** +$150M (100% counted as TAC under SAP)
- **Post-Injection TAC:** $2.0B
- **RBC Ratio:** 204% ($2.0B ÷ $982M ACL RBC)
- **Result:** Restores compliance above 200% CAL threshold

If LLIC issues $350M surplus notes (task assignment amount):
- **Post-Injection TAC:** $2.20B ($1.85B + $350M)
- **RBC Ratio:** 224% ($2.20B ÷ $982M ACL RBC)
- **Result:** Substantial buffer above 200% CAL threshold

**Nebraska DOI Approval Process:**

Surplus note issuance requires Nebraska Director of Insurance approval before issuance.³⁹ Approval considerations include:
- **Financial Condition:** Does issuance align with RBC Plan remediation strategy?
- **Payment Capacity:** Can insurer service interest payments from operating cash flows without impairing statutory surplus?
- **Use of Proceeds:** Are proceeds designated for RBC compliance, policyholder protection, or prohibited dividend extraction?
- **Terms:** Are interest rate, maturity, and subordination provisions commercially reasonable and protective of policyholders?

**Approval Timeline:** Surplus note approvals typically require 30-60 days for routine issuances. Given LLIC's pending RBC Plan approval and Form A acquisition review, Nebraska DOI may coordinate surplus note approval with overall transaction approval (~90-120 day timeline).

**Precedent:** Surplus note issuances for RBC remediation are common and generally receive regulatory approval when proceeds are clearly designated for capital restoration (not dividend distributions). Recent examples include:
- **Mutual of Omaha Financial Group:** Issued surplus notes in 2024 to enhance RBC ratios⁴⁰
- **Farmers Insurance:** $500M surplus note issuance in October 2024⁴¹

#### 2. Alternative Capital Structures

**Subordinated Debt (Non-Surplus Notes):**

Conventional subordinated debt **does not receive TAC credit** under NAIC RBC formulas.⁴² While subordinated to senior debt and general creditors, subordinated debt without commissioner payment approval is still senior to policyholders and not sufficiently subordinated for SAP surplus treatment.

- **RBC Impact:** $0 TAC credit; increases liabilities, reducing surplus
- **Tax Treatment:** Interest tax-deductible
- **Suitability for LLIC:** Not suitable for RBC remediation; would worsen RBC ratio by increasing liabilities without TAC benefit

**Common Equity Contribution:**

Direct capital contribution from parent or acquirer through equity issuance provides full TAC credit and strongest regulatory capital treatment.⁴³

- **RBC Impact:** $1 equity contribution = $1 TAC increase (100% credit)
- **Tax Treatment:** Not tax-deductible; no interest expense to deduct
- **Regulatory Approval:** Requires Nebraska DOI approval as "material change" under holding company regulations
- **Dividend Restrictions:** Nebraska DOI may impose dividend restrictions to preserve injected capital

### E. Captive Reinsurance Regulatory Scrutiny and Reserve Credit Risk

#### 1. Background on "Shadow Insurance" and AXXX/XXX Reserve Financing

**Regulatory History:**

In the early 2000s, NAIC adopted Regulation XXX (Actuarial Guideline 38) requiring life insurers to hold significantly increased reserves for term life insurance products with level premium periods exceeding 10 years.⁴⁴ Subsequently, AXXX (Actuarial Guideline 38) extended similar requirements to universal life products with secondary guarantees.⁴⁵

Insurers widely criticized these reserve requirements as excessive—often 2-3× the economic reserves needed to support actual policy risks.⁴⁶ To mitigate balance sheet impact, insurers developed "reserve financing" structures:

1. **Captive Formation:** Parent holding company establishes captive reinsurer in jurisdiction with favorable regulatory capital requirements (typically Vermont, South Carolina, Delaware, or offshore)

2. **Reinsurance Ceding:** Life insurer cedes 90-100% of XXX/AXXX reserves to captive via coinsurance treaty

3. **Collateral Structure:** Captive posts minimal "primary security" (high-quality assets) plus "other security" (often parental guarantees, surplus notes from affiliated entities, or letters of credit)

4. **Reserve Credit:** Life insurer takes statutory reserve credit for reinsurance, reducing redundant reserves and improving RBC ratio

**"Shadow Insurance" Regulatory Concerns:**⁴⁷

In 2013, New York Department of Financial Services investigation uncovered $48 billion in captive reinsurance transactions where captives held insufficient assets to honor reinsurance obligations if parent became insolvent.⁴⁸ Regulators labeled these structures "shadow insurance" due to:
- Captive assets backing ceded reserves were often <20% of total reserves
- Remaining 80%+ backed by parental guarantees or affiliated party obligations
- In parent company insolvency scenario, captive would be unable to pay claims, and ceding insurer would need to re-establish reserves, causing capital shortfall

Federal Reserve, Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC), and NAIC expressed concern that shadow insurance transactions artificially inflated insurers' reported RBC ratios without genuine risk transfer or capital adequacy improvement.⁴⁹

#### 2. NAIC Response — Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48) and Model Regulation #787

**Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48):**⁵⁰

Adopted December 2014, effective January 1, 2015, AG48 established uniform national standards governing XXX/AXXX reserve financing arrangements. Key requirements:

1. **Primary Security Calculation:** AG48 defines "Required Level of Primary Security" as economic reserves calculated using company-specific assumptions plus conservative margin. Primary security must consist of high-quality assets held directly at captive (cash, investment-grade bonds, mortgages).

2. **Other Security:** Remaining statutory reserves above Primary Security may be backed by parental guarantees, affiliate surplus notes, letters of credit, or other instruments.

3. **Actuarial Method:** Specifies stochastic modeling methodology for calculating Required Primary Security (similar to Principle-Based Reserves methodology).

4. **Actuarial Opinion Qualification:** Appointed actuary cannot issue unqualified opinion unless captive arrangement complies with AG48 primary/other security requirements.

**Model Regulation #787 (Term and Universal Life Insurance Reserve Financing Model Regulation):**⁵¹

In 2016, NAIC codified AG48 principles into Model Regulation #787, which replaces AG48 in adopting states. As of August 2025, **42 jurisdictions have adopted Model #787**, while **9 jurisdictions continue using AG48**.⁵²

**Nebraska's Status:** Research indicates Nebraska has adopted Model #787 or AG48 (specific statute not located in search results, but Nebraska is NAIC-accredited state and likely compliant with national standards).
