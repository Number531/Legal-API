### Critical Legal Issue: Nebraska Punitive Damages Prohibition

**Nebraska Constitutional Limitation:** Nebraska is one of few states that prohibits punitive damages in all civil suits, including insurance bad faith cases. The Nebraska Constitution considers excessive damages beyond reasonable economic damages as "punitive" and thus unconstitutional.

**Impact on Settlement Leverage:**

The research plan estimated total exposure at $85M-$125M cash value shortfall + **punitive damages 2-3Ã— = $170M-$375M**. However, Nebraska law permits **compensatory damages only** ($85M-$125M), eliminating the punitive multiplier entirely.

**Settlement Strategy Adjustment:** Without punitive damages threat, plaintiffs' settlement leverage is significantly reduced. The settlement should be negotiated at 20-40% of compensatory damages ($85M-$125M = $17M-$50M range), not the higher punitive-inclusive range. This analysis supports the $25M-$45M research plan range as reasonable given compensatory-only damages.

**Available Damages in Nebraska:**
- Consequential damages for economic loss proximately caused by insurer's conduct
- Emotional distress damages (only if severe and substantial, unlikely for policy cash value shortfall)
- Attorney fees
- **NO punitive damages, treble damages, or multipliers**

### Nebraska Legal Framework

**Nebraska Consumer Protection Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. Â§Â§ 59-1601 et seq.):**

**Statutory Basis:** Section 59-1602 prohibits "unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce."

**Elements:**
1. Impact on public interest (850-member class satisfies)
2. Actual harm (cash value shortfall quantified)
3. Injury to business or property (diminished policy values)

**CRITICAL EXEMPTION ISSUE:** Institutions governed by the Nebraska Department of Banking and Finance or Nebraska State Real Estate Commission are generally exempt from the Consumer Protection Act. Nebraska Department of Insurance (DOI) regulates insurance companies, raising potential exemption defense.

**Plaintiff Strategy:** Likely to plead alternative claims to avoid exemption:
- **Breach of contract:** Policy terms allegedly violated by cap rate reductions/COI increases
- **Breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing:** Recognized in Nebraska under Braesch v. Union Ins. Co., 237 Neb. 905, 468 N.W.2d 466 (1991)
- **Common law fraud/misrepresentation:** Misleading illustrations induce policy purchase

**Braesch v. Union Ins. Co. Precedent:**

Nebraska Supreme Court (1991) held:
- Implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing exists in every insurance contract
- Breach provides policyholders with extracontractual remedies (consequential damages, attorney fees)
- Only policyholders (not third-party beneficiaries) have standing
- Bad faith in first-party insurance cases is actionable tort

**Application:** IUL policyholders have contractual privity. Alleged misleading illustrations, cap rate reductions without adequate disclosure, and COI increases may violate implied covenant if done in bad faith. This claim avoids Consumer Protection Act exemption issue.

### NAIC Model Regulation 582: Regulatory Framework and Evolving Standards

**NAIC Regulation 582 (Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation):**

Adopted by majority of U.S. jurisdictions (including Nebraska) to protect consumers and foster education regarding life insurance illustrations.

**Key Requirements:**
1. **Guaranteed vs. Non-Guaranteed Elements:** Illustrations must clearly distinguish guaranteed policy elements (determined at issue) from non-guaranteed elements (not determined at issue, subject to change)

2. **Display Requirements:**
   - Non-guaranteed elements must be shown at same durations as guaranteed elements
   - Both guaranteed and non-guaranteed columns side-by-side
   - If no guaranteed benefit at duration, display zero

3. **Basic Illustration:** Must include policy benefits, premiums, values, credits, charges for both guaranteed and non-guaranteed scenarios

**Application to IUL Claims:**

**Typical Plaintiff Allegations (Based on Comparable Cases):**
- Illustrations showed 8.5% annual index crediting (non-guaranteed) vs. actual 4.2% average performance
- Projections allegedly not reasonable based on realistic assumptions
- Inadequate disclosure of insurer's discretion to change cap rates, participation rates, COI charges
- Disclosures buried in fine print (policy page 18) without sufficient prominence

**Typical Insurer Defenses:**
- Illustrations complied with NAIC Reg 582: guaranteed vs. non-guaranteed columns clearly labeled
- Required disclosure language included
- Projections based on historical S&P 500 returns (10% average 1926-2015) minus reasonable adjustments for caps/fees
- Policy contracts explicitly state cap rates and COI charges subject to change
- Policyholders acknowledged receipt, 30-day free look period

**Regulatory Evolution Strengthens Plaintiff Claims:**

- **Original Reg 582 (1995):** Did not contemplate indexed products; designed for traditional whole/universal life
- **Actuarial Guideline 49 (AG 49) (2015):** Adopted to address IUL illustrations post-dating 1995 regulation
- **AG 49-A (2020):** Insurers circumvented AG 49 with multipliers/bonuses; AG 49-A closed loophole
- **AG 49-B (2023):** Further refinement, described by regulators as "quick fix" for continued illustration gaming

**Strategic Implication:** Evolving regulatory standards demonstrate NAIC/state regulators recognized IUL illustrations were problematic, strengthening plaintiff argument that illustrations were misleading even if technically compliant with regulations at time of sale. Regulatory uncertainty favors settlement over trial risk.

**National Life Case Precedent:** Active litigation (2024-2025) alleges proprietary indices (US Pacesetter Index) did not exist prior to December 2021, but illustrations showed 20-year backtested returns. Court will determine whether backtested performance constitutes misleading illustration under Reg 582.

#### 1. **Statutory Basis: Neb. Rev. Stat. Â§Â§ 59-1601 et seq.**

#### 2. **Elements of a Nebraska Consumer Protection Act Claim**

To sustain an action under the Nebraska Consumer Protection Act, plaintiff must show:

1. **Impact on Public Interest:** The unfair or deceptive act or practice must have an impact upon the public interest
2. **Actual Harm:** Plaintiff must show actual harm due to the defendant's conduct
3. **Injury to Business or Property:** Actual injury to business or property must be demonstrated

**Source:** Fair Contracts.org, NCLC Digital Library

**Application to IUL Illustration Claims:**
- **Public Interest:** Class of 850 policyholders demonstrates impact beyond individual transaction
- **Actual Harm:** Cash value shortfall (illustrated $X vs. actual $Y) = quantifiable harm
- **Injury to Property:** Diminished policy cash values constitute property injury

#### 4. **Nebraska Case Law on Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing**

##### **Braesch v. Union Ins. Co., 237 Neb. 905, 468 N.W.2d 466 (1991)**

**Holding:** Nebraska recognizes the tort of bad faith in first-party insurance cases

**Key Principles:**
1. **Implied Covenant:** There is an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in every insurance contract
2. **Contractual Relationship Required:** The covenant is dependent upon a contractual relationship between plaintiff and insurer
3. **Policyholder Standing:** Beneficiaries who are also policyholders (not just beneficiaries) have standing to bring bad faith claims
4. **Available Remedies:** Breach of implied covenant provides policyholders with extracontractual remedies

**Standard:** The implied covenant requires that none of the parties do anything which will injure the right of another party to receive the benefit of the contract

**Application to IUL Class Action:**
- IUL policyholders have contractual relationship with Liberty Life
- Alleged cap rate reductions and COI increases may violate implied covenant if done in bad faith
- Alleged misleading illustrations may constitute bad faith inducement to enter contract

**Source:** Braesch v. Union Ins. Co., 237 Neb. 905, 468 N.W.2d 466 (1991), law.justia.com; Nebraska Recognizes Insurance First Party Bad Faith Actions, Property Insurance Coverage Law Blog

**Additional Nebraska Bad Faith Precedent:**
- Nebraska recognizes insurance first-party bad faith actions at common law
- Such actions can be brought only by policyholders (not third-party beneficiaries without privity)
- Consequential damages for economic loss proximately caused by insurer's conduct are recoverable
- Emotional distress damages available only when severe and substantial
- Attorney fees may be recoverable

**Source:** ALFA International - Nebraska Insurance Law Compendium

### E. NAIC Model Regulation 582 - Life Insurance Illustration Standards

#### 1. **Regulatory Framework**

**Purpose:** NAIC Model Regulation 582 (Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation) provides rules for life insurance policy illustrations to protect consumers and foster consumer education.

**Applicability:** The regulation applies to all group and individual life insurance policies and certificates with illustrated death benefits exceeding $10,000.

**Adoption:** Adopted by a majority of U.S. jurisdictions (including Nebraska) to ensure life insurance illustrations provide clear, understandable information.

**Source:** NAIC Model Regulation 582, content.naic.org

#### 2. **Guaranteed vs. Non-Guaranteed Elements - Key Definitions**

**"Guaranteed elements":** Premiums, benefits, values, credits, or charges under a policy that are **guaranteed and determined at issue**

**"Non-guaranteed elements":** Premiums, benefits, values, credits, or charges that are **not guaranteed or not determined at issue**

**Examples in IUL Context:**
- **Guaranteed:** Minimum 0% crediting rate floor, maximum COI charges stated in policy
- **Non-guaranteed:** Index crediting rates (e.g., illustrated 8.5% S&P 500 return), current COI charges, participation rates, cap rates

**Source:** NAIC Model Regulation 582, content.naic.org

#### 3. **Disclosure Requirements for Illustrations**

**Display Requirements:**
- If non-guaranteed elements are shown, they must be shown at the **same durations** as corresponding guaranteed elements
- If no guaranteed benefit/value is available at any duration for which non-guaranteed benefit/value is shown, a **zero shall be displayed**
- Illustrations must show both guaranteed and non-guaranteed columns side-by-side

**Basic Illustration Components:**
A "basic illustration" is a ledger or proposal used in the sale of life insurance that shows **both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements**

**Required Elements:**
- Policy benefits (death benefit, cash values)
- Premiums (guaranteed and illustrated)
- Values (cash surrender value, account value)
- Credits (interest crediting, index returns)
- Charges (COI, policy fees, expense charges)

**Source:** NAIC Model Regulation 582, content.naic.org

#### 4. **Application to IUL Illustration Misrepresentation Claims**

**Typical Plaintiff Allegations (Based on Comparable Cases):**

1. **Overly Optimistic Projections:**
   - Illustrations showed 8.5% annual index crediting
   - Actual historical performance: 4.2% average
   - Allegation: Projections not reasonable based on realistic assumptions

2. **Inadequate Disclosure of Insurer's Ability to Change Rates:**
   - Cap rates, participation rates, and COI charges shown as "current" but changeable
   - Allegations: Disclosures buried in fine print on page 18 of policy
   - Insufficient prominence given to insurer's discretion to reduce caps/increase charges

3. **Backtested Performance (National Life Issue):**
   - Proprietary indices (e.g., "US Pacesetter Index") did not exist historically
   - Illustrations used backtested hypothetical returns for 20-year period
   - Allegation: Misleading to show performance history that never actually occurred

**Typical Insurer Defenses:**

1. **Compliance with NAIC Reg 582:**
   - Illustrations clearly labeled guaranteed vs. non-guaranteed columns
   - Required disclosure language included
   - Projections based on historical S&P 500 returns (10% average 1926-2015) minus reasonable adjustments

2. **Contractual Disclosure:**
   - Policy contracts explicitly state cap rates, participation rates, and COI charges are subject to change
   - Contract language controls over illustration
   - Policyholders acknowledged receipt of policy and 30-day free look period

3. **Industry Standards:**
   - Actuarial Guideline 49 (AG 49) adopted 2015 to address IUL illustrations
   - AG 49-A (2020) and AG 49-B (2023) further refined standards
   - Illustrations at time of sale complied with then-current regulatory guidance

**Source:** InsuranceNewsNet (IUL Illustration Litigation Articles); NAIC Model Regulation 582

#### 5. **Regulatory Evolution: AG 49, AG 49-A, AG 49-B**

**Problem with Original Regulation 582:**
The regulation is 30 years old (adopted 1995) and did not contemplate indexed products.

**Actuarial Guideline 49 (2015):**
Adopted to address IUL products created after original 1995 illustration model #582.

**AG 49-A (2020):**
Insurers circumvented AG 49 by offering IUL products with multipliers and bonuses; AG 49-A closed this loophole.

**AG 49-B (2023):**
Referred to by regulators as "a quick fix" to address continued illustration gaming.

**Implication:** Regulatory uncertainty and evolving standards strengthen plaintiff's argument that illustrations were misleading, even if technically compliant with regulations at time of sale.

**Source:** InsuranceNewsNet - Regulators Tackle Troubling Illustration Practices; BonkNote - NAIC Model Regulation 582

### F. Class Certification Analysis Under Fed. R. Civ. P. 23

#### 1. **Rule 23(a) Requirements - Prerequisites for Class Certification**

All four prerequisites must be satisfied:

##### **(1) Numerosity [Rule 23(a)(1)]**
"The class is so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable."

**Application:** 850 IUL policyholders clearly satisfies numerosity requirement. Courts routinely certify classes of 40+ members.

**Precedent:** State Farm settlement involved 450,000 policyholders; Transamerica 70,000 policyholders.

**Conclusion:** Numerosity requirement easily satisfied.

##### **(2) Commonality [Rule 23(a)(2)]**
"There are questions of law or fact common to the class."

**Standard (Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.S. 338 (2011)):**
"Commonality requires the plaintiff to demonstrate that the class members 'have suffered the same injury.' Their claims must depend upon a common contention of such a nature that it is capable of classwide resolutionâ€”which means that determination of its truth or falsity will resolve an issue that is central to the validity of each one of the claims in one stroke."

**Common Questions for IUL Class:**
1. Did Liberty Life's IUL policy illustrations comply with NAIC Regulation 582?
2. Did Liberty Life adequately disclose its ability to change cap rates, participation rates, and COI charges?
3. Were the projected 8.5% index crediting rates reasonable at time of illustration?
4. Did Liberty Life breach the implied covenant of good faith by reducing cap rates and increasing COI charges?

**Conclusion:** Common questions of insurer's conduct and policy terms satisfy commonality.

##### **(3) Typicality [Rule 23(a)(3)]**
"The claims or defenses of the representative parties are typical of the claims or defenses of the class."

**Application:** Named plaintiffs purchased IUL policies during 2015-2020, received illustrations showing 8.5% crediting, experienced same cap rate reductions (12% â†’ 10%) and COI increases as all class members.

**Potential Issue:** Individualized reliance on illustrations (did each policyholder rely on specific illustration projections?).

**Counter-Argument:** Uniform sales process, standard illustration format, common insurer conduct satisfies typicality even with some individual variation.

**Conclusion:** Typicality likely satisfied, though insurer may argue individual reliance issues.

##### **(4) Adequacy of Representation [Rule 23(a)(4)]**
"The representative parties will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class."

**Requirements:**
- Named plaintiffs' interests must align with class
- Class counsel must be qualified, experienced, and able to conduct litigation

**Application:** Experienced class action plaintiffs' attorneys specializing in insurance litigation satisfy adequacy requirement. Named plaintiffs' interests align with class (all seeking damages for cash value shortfall).

**Conclusion:** Adequacy requirement satisfied.

**Source:** Cornell Law School LII - Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23; Class Actions Under Rule 23(a), Paschos Law

#### 2. **Rule 23(b)(3) Requirements - Predominance and Superiority**

##### **(1) Predominance [Rule 23(b)(3)]**
"Questions of law or fact common to class members **predominate over** any questions affecting only individual members."

**Standard:** Much stricter than Rule 23(a) commonality. Requires not only common questions but that such questions **predominate** over individualized issues.

**Supreme Court:** "Predominate" means common issues "are more prevalent or important" than individual ones.

**Common Issues (Favor Class Certification):**
1. Whether Liberty Life's uniform illustration format and projections were misleading
2. Whether cap rate reductions and COI increases violated policy terms
3. Whether Liberty Life adequately disclosed its discretion to change rates
4. Calculation of cash value shortfall using standard formula (illustrated value - actual value)

**Individual Issues (Disfavor Class Certification):**
1. **Reliance:** Did each policyholder rely on specific illustration projections when purchasing?
2. **Causation:** Did misleading illustration cause each policyholder to purchase (or would they have purchased anyway)?
3. **Damages:** Individual variation in premiums paid, cash values, policy durations

**Insurance Class Action Precedent on Predominance:**

**Factors Favoring Certification:**
- Uniform policy language across class
- Common insurer conduct (same cap rate reduction, same COI increase applied to all)
- Formulaic damages calculation possible (illustrated value - actual value)
- Pattern and practice of misleading illustrations (not individualized sales)

**Factors Disfavoring Certification:**
- Individualized reliance (each policyholder received individual illustration, met with agent)
- Variation in financial sophistication and understanding
- Different policy vintages (2015-2020) with different index performance

**Likely Outcome:** Predominance requirement presents closest issue for class certification. Court may:
- Certify class if common issues (uniform illustrations, policy terms) predominate
- Deny certification if individualized reliance issues predominate
- Certify subclasses by policy year or product variation

**Source:** DiCello Levitt - Arguing Class Actions: Predominance; Winston & Strawn - Defeating Motions for Class Certification

##### **(2) Superiority [Rule 23(b)(3)]**
"A class action is superior to other available methods for fairly and efficiently adjudicating the controversy."

**Factors:**
1. **Class members' interests in individually controlling separate actions:** LOW (per-capita recovery likely $29K-$147K average; not economical for individual litigation)
2. **Extent and nature of litigation already begun:** No individual cases identified in search
3. **Desirability of concentrating litigation in one forum:** HIGH (efficiency, consistent rulings, judicial economy)
4. **Likely difficulties in managing class action:** MEDIUM (individual damages calculation required, but formulaic)

**Conclusion:** Superiority requirement satisfied. Class action is superior method given:
- Small per-capita recovery relative to litigation costs
- Common policy terms and insurer conduct
- Efficiency of resolving 850 claims in single proceeding

**Source:** Bona Law - Requirements for Class Certification Under Rule 23; Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23
