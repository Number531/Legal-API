Customer-Specific Suitability**: Broker must have reasonable basis to believe recommendation suitable for particular customer based on investment profile factors: age, other investments, financial situation/needs, tax status, investment objectives, investment experience, investment time horizon, liquidity needs, risk tolerance.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. FINRA Suitability Framework for Variable Annuities

#### 1. FINRA Rule 2111: Three-Component Suitability Standard

FINRA Rule 2111 requires broker-dealers and associated persons to have "a reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy involving a security or securities is suitable for the customer, based on the information obtained through the reasonable diligence of the [firm] or associated person to ascertain the customer's investment profile."

**Three Core Suitability Obligations** (codified in Regulatory Notice 12-25):

1. **Reasonable-Basis Suitability**: A broker must perform reasonable diligence to understand the nature of the recommended security or investment strategy, including potential risks and rewards, and determine whether the recommendation is suitable for at least some investors based on that understanding.

2. **Customer-Specific Suitability**: A broker must have a reasonable basis to believe that the recommendation is suitable for a particular customer based on that customer's investment profile, including: age, other investments, financial situation and needs, tax status, investment objectives, investment experience, investment time horizon, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance.

3. **Quantitative Suitability**: A broker who has actual or de facto control over a customer account must have a reasonable basis for believing that a series of recommended transactions, even if suitable when viewed in isolation, are not excessive and unsuitable for the customer.

**Database Provenance**: FINRA Regulatory Notice 12-25 (May 2012), https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/notices/12-25 [VERIFIED: accessed 2026-01-16]

#### 2. FINRA Rule 2330: Variable Annuity-Specific Requirements

FINRA Rule 2330 establishes sales practice standards specifically for deferred variable annuities. The rule requires:

**Pre-Purchase Suitability Determination**: Members must make reasonable efforts to obtain information concerning the customer's:
- Age
- Annual income
- Financial situation and needs
- Investment experience
- Investment objectives
- Intended use of the deferred variable annuity
- Investment time horizon
- Existing assets
- Liquidity needs
- Liquid net worth
- Risk tolerance
- Tax status

**Exchange/Replacement Analysis**: When recommending a variable annuity exchange, the transaction must be suitable for the particular customer, taking into consideration whether the customer would:
- Incur a surrender charge
- Be subject to commencement of a new surrender period
- Lose existing benefits (such as death, living, or other contractual benefits)
- Be subject to increased fees or charges (such as mortality and expense fees, investment advisory fees, or charges for riders)
- Has had another exchange within the preceding 36 months

**Principal Review Requirement**: A registered principal must review and determine whether to approve a customer's application for a deferred variable annuity before sending the application to the issuing insurance company, no later than seven business days after receiving a complete and correct application.

**Database Provenance**: FINRA Rule 2330, https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/2330 [VERIFIED: accessed 2026-01-16]

#### 3. FINRA Rule 3110: Supervision Requirements

FINRA Rule 3110 requires firms to establish and maintain a system to supervise the activities of associated persons that is reasonably designed to achieve compliance with applicable securities laws, regulations, and FINRA rules.

For variable annuities, Rule 2330 requires specific written supervisory procedures, including:
- Training policies or programs for associated persons who effect and registered principals who review transactions in deferred variable annuities
- Procedures reasonably designed to ensure compliance with suitability requirements
- Systems to detect and prevent unsuitable recommendations

**Database Provenance**: FINRA Rule 3110, https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/rulebooks/finra-rules/3110 [VERIFIED: accessed 2026-01-16]

### E. Suitability Red Flags — Elderly Customers and Variable Annuities

Based on FINRA guidance, enforcement actions, and arbitration precedent, the following fact patterns constitute "red flags" requiring heightened scrutiny:

#### 1. Customer Age and Time Horizon Mismatch

**Red Flag Indicators**:
- Customer age 70+ with variable annuity surrender period extending 7-10 years
- Surrender charge period extends beyond customer's actuarial life expectancy
- Customer age 75+ purchasing new VA with 8-year surrender schedule
- Retired customer with 5-10 year time horizon purchasing long-term product

**Regulatory Basis**: FINRA Rule 2111 (customer-specific suitability), FINRA Rule 2330 (investment time horizon consideration)

**Precedent**: FINRA has stated "based on certain contract features, some customers of advanced age may be unsuitable for a variable annuity investment"

#### 2. Limited Income and Liquidity Needs

**Red Flag Indicators**:
- Customer income limited to Social Security ($35,000-$45,000 annually)
- Customer has near-term liquidity needs (medical expenses, home repairs, family obligations)
- Customer lacks emergency fund or liquid reserves outside VA
- Customer previously expressed need for accessible funds
- Customer age and health suggest foreseeable medical expenses

**Regulatory Basis**: FINRA Rule 2330 requires consideration of "liquidity needs" and "liquid net worth"

**Precedent**: Liberty Life pending case Claimant A (age 82, $250K VA, limited income $35K/year Social Security) represents classic red flag pattern

#### 3. Over-Concentration in Variable Annuities

**Red Flag Indicators**:
- Variable annuity represents >70% of customer's liquid net worth
- Customer places 80% of investable assets in single VA
- Customer lacks diversification outside illiquid annuity products
- Customer's non-VA assets are insufficient to meet living expenses

**Regulatory Basis**: FINRA Rule 2111 (quantitative suitability), general diversification principles

**Precedent**: Liberty Life pending case Claimant B (age 68, $400K VA representing 80% of liquid net worth) exemplifies over-concentration concerns

#### 4. Unsuitable Variable Annuity Exchanges (1035 Exchanges)

**Red Flag Indicators**:
- Customer exchanges existing VA incurring surrender charges
- New VA imposes new surrender period on previously vested funds
- Customer loses existing benefits (guaranteed minimum death benefit, enhanced crediting rates)
- New VA has higher fees than existing contract
- Multiple exchanges within 36 months
- Exchange benefits primarily accrue to broker (commissions) rather than customer

**Regulatory Basis**: FINRA Rule 2330 explicitly requires evaluation of: (1) surrender charges incurred, (2) new surrender period, (3) loss of existing benefits, (4) increased fees, (5) exchanges within preceding 36 months

**Precedent**: Liberty Life pending case Claimant C (age 73, $180K unsuitable 1035 exchange, new 8-year surrender charges) represents classic unsuitable exchange

**Statistical Context**: Wells Fargo enforcement action (2020) involved 101 unsuitable VA switches; Fortune Financial (2023) involved 48 customers with early withdrawals incurring $612K in charges

#### 5. Supervisory Deficiencies

**Red Flag Indicators (Firm-Level)**:
- Branch manager fails to review VA applications before submission to insurer
- Applications submitted without principal approval or e-signature
- Firm lacks automated alerts for VA exchange patterns
- Firm lacks written guidance on VA suitability factors
- High-volume producers bypass supervisory review
- Regional supervisors conduct ad-hoc rather than systematic surveillance
- Firm relies solely on transaction-by-transaction review without pattern analysis
- Training deficiencies: representatives lack understanding of VA features, surrender charges, time horizon requirements

**Regulatory Basis**: FINRA Rule 3110 (supervision), FINRA Rule 2330 (principal review requirements)

**Precedent**:
- Wells Fargo (2020): Lacked alert system for VA switches, failed to send switch letters to 100+ customers
- VALIC (2021): Failed to review approximately 400 solicited transactions
- Transamerica (2021): "Lack of or insufficient training as primary contributor to unsuitable recommendations" resulting in $4.4M customer damages
- Fifth Third (2018): Failed to provide adequate guidance to principals, systemic supervisory failures

### Appendix B: FINRA Suitability Red Flags Checklist for Variable Annuities

**Customer Age and Time Horizon**:
- [ ] Customer age 70+ with VA surrender period >5 years
- [ ] Customer age 75+ purchasing new VA
- [ ] Surrender period extends beyond actuarial life expectancy
- [ ] Customer age + surrender period > 85 years

**Income and Liquidity**:
- [ ] Customer income limited to Social Security (<$45K/year)
- [ ] Customer lacks emergency fund or liquid reserves
- [ ] Customer has expressed near-term liquidity needs
- [ ] Customer's health suggests foreseeable medical expenses

**Concentration and Diversification**:
- [ ] VA represents >60% of liquid net worth
- [ ] VA represents >70% of liquid net worth (HIGH RISK)
- [ ] VA represents >80% of liquid net worth (EXTREME RISK)
- [ ] Customer lacks diversification outside annuity products

**Variable Annuity Exchanges (1035)**:
- [ ] Customer incurs surrender charge from existing contract
- [ ] New VA imposes new surrender period on vested funds
- [ ] Customer loses existing benefits (GMDB, enhanced rates)
- [ ] New VA has higher fees than existing contract
- [ ] Multiple exchanges within 36 months (per Rule 2330)
- [ ] Exchange benefits broker (commissions) vs. customer

**Supervisory Red Flags**:
- [ ] Application submitted without principal approval
- [ ] Principal approved despite obvious red flags
- [ ] No documentation of suitability analysis
- [ ] Agent has multiple red-flag transactions (pattern)
- [ ] Firm lacks automated surveillance for high-risk transactions
