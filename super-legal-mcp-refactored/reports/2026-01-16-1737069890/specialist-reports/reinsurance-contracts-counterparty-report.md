**2. Change of Control Risk: LOW-MEDIUM**

The transaction is structured as a **stock purchase** of Liberty Life Insurance Company, meaning Liberty Life remains the same legal entity before and after closing. This structure is critical because:

- **No Assignment Required**: Reinsurance treaties continue with same counterparty (Liberty Life); no novation or assignment to new entity
- **No Reinsurer Consent (Likely)**: Industry-standard reinsurance treaties typically require **notice only** for change of control at parent/holding company level, not affirmative consent
- **Stock Purchase vs. Asset Purchase**: If transaction were asset purchase, reinsurance treaties would require **novation** (consent of all parties per New York common law) or **assignment** (typically consent with reasonableness standard)

**However**, without access to actual treaty documents, there is residual risk that treaties contain non-standard provisions requiring:
- Reinsurer consent for any change of control (including stock purchase)
- "Sole discretion" consent standard (rather than "reasonableness" standard)
- Reinsurer-initiated recapture rights triggered by change of control

Research indicates these provisions are **relatively uncommon** in modern reinsurance treaties for several reasons:
1. Reinsurers generally prefer to keep profitable treaties in force rather than lose premium income
2. Acquirer often has stronger financial position than target, reducing reinsurer credit risk
3. Stock purchase maintains operational continuity (same management, systems, claim administration)
4. Cedents negotiate aggressively to limit reinsurer recapture rights to preserve treaty value

**American Financial Holdings' financial strength** (Connecticut-based PE-backed financial services investment company) likely viewed favorably by reinsurers compared to existing Liberty Life Holdings LLC parent ($280M net worth). Reinsurers may view acquisition as **credit positive** rather than credit negative.

**Recommended Mitigation**: Proactive outreach to all reinsurers 90 days before closing with consent request package (if required) or courtesy notification (if not required) describing transaction, American Financial Holdings' financial strength, and commitment to operational continuity.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Reinsurance Treaty Legal Framework

#### 1. NAIC Model Law #785: Credit for Reinsurance

**Statutory Framework:**

NAIC Model Law #785 (Credit for Reinsurance Model Law) establishes the framework for when U.S. ceding insurers may take statutory credit for reserves ceded to reinsurers.¹ The 2019 revisions, now adopted in all 56 U.S. jurisdictions by September 2022, implemented the reinsurance collateral provisions of bilateral "Covered Agreements" with the European Union and United Kingdom.²

**Three Categories of Reinsurers:**

1. **Authorized Reinsurers**: Licensed in the ceding insurer's domiciliary state; no collateral required.

2. **Certified Reinsurers**: Domiciled in "Qualified Jurisdictions" and meeting enhanced review standards; reduced collateral based on financial strength rating (ranging from 0% to 100% depending on rating tier).

3. **Unauthorized Reinsurers**: Not licensed in the ceding state; **require 100% collateral** (cash, trust funds, letters of credit, or other approved security) unless qualified under reciprocal jurisdiction provisions.³

**Reciprocal Jurisdiction Reinsurers:**

The 2019 revisions allow reinsurers domiciled in "Reciprocal Jurisdictions" (EU member countries, UK, Switzerland, Japan, and **Bermuda**) to qualify for elimination of collateral requirements if they meet:
- Minimum solvency capital requirement (SCR) of 100% under Solvency II (or equivalent for non-EU jurisdictions)
- Minimum own-funds equivalent to $250 million
- Solvency, governance, and risk management standards deemed equivalent to U.S. requirements⁴

**Bermuda as Reciprocal Jurisdiction:**

The NAIC recognized Bermuda as a reciprocal jurisdiction on October 11, 2019, with minimum Enhanced Capital Requirement (ECR) ratio of 100%.⁵ However, Bermuda reinsurers that do not meet reciprocal jurisdiction qualifications remain **unauthorized** and must post 100% collateral.

**Implications for Liberty Life:**

- **Global Reassurance Ltd. (Bermuda)**: Not qualified as reciprocal jurisdiction reinsurer → Unauthorized → Requires 100% collateral
- **Current Status**: $850M letter of credit from Barclays Bank securing ceded reserves [VERIFIED via transaction disclosure]
- **Regulatory Compliance**: Collateral requirement satisfied; Nebraska allows statutory credit for ceded reserves

#### 2. Nebraska Reinsurance Credit Regulations

**Nebraska Statutory Authority:**

Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-416.06 governs credit for reinsurance, adopting NAIC Model Law principles.⁶ Nebraska Administrative Code Title 210, Chapter 65 implements certified reinsurer provisions.⁷

**Unauthorized Reinsurer Requirements:**

Credit is allowed when reinsurance is ceded to an unauthorized assuming insurer that maintains a **trust fund in a qualified United States financial institution** for the payment of valid claims of its U.S. ceding insurers and their assigns and successors in interest.⁸

**Trust Approval Requirements:**

Credit for reinsurance is not granted unless:
1. The form of the trust and any amendments have been approved by the commissioner of the state where the trust is domiciled or another state commissioner who has accepted principal regulatory oversight.
2. The trust consists of appropriate funds in an amount **at least equal to the trusteed reinsurer's liabilities** attributable to reinsurance ceded by U.S. domiciled insurers.⁹

**Alternative Collateral: Letters of Credit:**

Nebraska regulations permit letters of credit from qualified U.S. financial institutions as acceptable collateral for unauthorized reinsurers.¹⁰ Letters of credit must be:
- Issued by a bank authorized to do business in the United States
- Clean, irrevocable, and unconditional
- For the benefit of the ceding insurer
- In an amount at least equal to statutory reserves ceded plus any amounts due

#### 3. Reinsurance Treaty Structures: Legal and Financial Analysis

##### A. Coinsurance vs. Modified Coinsurance (Mod-Co)

**Coinsurance Structure:**

Under traditional coinsurance, the ceding company transfers both **statutory reserves AND the assets** supporting those reserves to the reinsurer's balance sheet.¹¹ The difference between the value of reserves transferred and assets transferred is the "ceding commission" paid to the cedent. Key characteristics:

- **Reserve Transfer**: Reserves removed from cedent's statutory balance sheet
- **Asset Transfer**: Assets transferred to reinsurer; reinsurer manages investment portfolio
- **Risk Transfer**: Reinsurer assumes mortality, lapse, and investment risk
- **Ceding Commission**: Typically 85-110% of premiums to offset cedent's acquisition costs (DAC)
- **Statutory Impact**: Reduces cedent's assets, liabilities, and surplus (net impact depends on ceding commission)

**Modified Coinsurance Structure:**

Modified coinsurance differs fundamentally: the ceding company **does NOT transfer reserves or assets** to the reinsurer.¹² Instead:

- **Reserve Retention**: Reserves remain on cedent's statutory balance sheet
- **Asset Retention**: Cedent retains and manages investment portfolio
- **Experience Adjustment**: Cedent pays reinsurer quarterly "experience adjustment" settling difference between investment income earned and mortality charges incurred
- **Risk Transfer**: Reinsurer assumes mortality risk only; cedent retains investment and lapse risk
- **Control**: Cedent maintains control over asset allocation and investment strategy
- **Credit Risk Mitigation**: Mod-co structure ensures cedent maintains access to assets if reinsurer fails¹³

**2024 Regulatory Recognition:**

Bermuda Monetary Authority (BMA) guidance issued October 10, 2024, recognizes that funds withheld (FWH) and modified coinsurance structures "have significantly decreased an insurer's counterparty credit risk exposure to Bermudian reinsurers" by keeping assets under cedent control.¹⁴

**Liberty Life's Swiss Re Mod-Co Treaty:**

- **Structure**: 50% quota share modified coinsurance on IUL products issued 2018-2024
- **Face Amount**: $3.2B ceded from $6.4B total IUL block
- **Mod-Co Advantages**:
  - Liberty Life retains control of $1.6B+ in separate account assets backing IUL policies
  - Investment performance risk remains with Liberty Life (appropriate for indexed products)
  - Swiss Re assumes mortality risk only
  - No collateral required (Swiss Re authorized in Nebraska)
- **Counterparty Credit Risk**: LOW (A+ AM Best rating + authorized status + funds withheld structure)

##### B. Yearly Renewable Term (YRT) Reinsurance

**YRT Structure and Mechanics:**

Yearly Renewable Term reinsurance is a form of **excess of retention** reinsurance where:

- **Mortality Risk Only**: YRT transfers only mortality/morbidity risk to reinsurer; no investment or lapse risk transferred¹⁵
- **Net Amount at Risk**: Reinsurer covers portion of death benefit **above cedent's retention limit** (face amount minus reserves)¹⁶
- **Annual Premiums**: YRT premium recalculates each year based on attained age of insured and current net amount at risk
- **Reserve Treatment**: Cedent retains policy reserves; only mortality risk transferred
- **Application**: Commonly used for group life insurance excess of retention coverage

**Liberty Life's Munich Re YRT Treaty:**

- **Structure**: Excess of retention on group term life products
- **Retention**: Liberty Life retains first **$250,000 per life**
- **Coverage**: Munich Re covers excess from $250,000 to **$5,000,000 per life**
- **Purpose**: Volatility protection against occasional large claims on high-earning executives
- **Premium**: Liberty Life pays YRT premium based on age, net amount at risk, and Munich Re's mortality assumptions
- **Counterparty Risk**: VERY LOW (Munich Re A+ rated, authorized reinsurer, no collateral required)
- **Financial Impact**: Minimal exposure; occasional large claims smoothed across reinsurance

**YRT Tax Considerations:**

YRT reinsurance structures must comply with federal tax law requirements to avoid recharacterization. The IRS has ruled that net YRT reinsurance arrangements can reduce net surrender-value-based life insurance reserves for tax purposes when mortality risk is genuinely transferred.¹⁷

### C. Change of Control and Assignment Provisions in M&A Context

#### 1. Legal Framework: Assignment vs. Stock Purchase

**Stock Purchase vs. Asset Purchase Distinction:**

The structure of the M&A transaction determines whether reinsurance treaties require novation, assignment consent, or continue automatically:

- **Stock Purchase** (Liberty Life transaction): Acquirer purchases 100% of stock of Liberty Life Insurance Company; Liberty Life remains the same legal entity; reinsurance treaties remain with same counterparty (Liberty Life).

- **Asset Purchase**: Acquirer purchases specific assets and assumes specific liabilities; policies and reinsurance treaties must be **assigned** or **novated** to new entity; requires consent from reinsurers and potentially policyholders.³³

**Novation Requirements:**

Under New York common law (typical governing law for reinsurance treaties), novation requires four elements:
1. A previously valid obligation
2. Agreement of **all parties** to a new contract
3. Extinguishment of the old contract
4. A valid new contract³⁴

Novation requires **consent of all parties**, including the reinsurer, to extinguish the original treaty and create new obligations with the acquiring entity.³⁵

**Liberty Life Transaction Structure:**

The transaction is structured as a **stock purchase** of Liberty Life Insurance Company by American Financial Holdings LLC. This structure means:

- **No Assignment Required**: Liberty Life remains the same legal entity before and after closing
- **No Novation Required**: Reinsurance treaties continue with same counterparty (Liberty Life)
- **Form A Filing**: Nebraska DOI approval required for change of control (Form A filing under NAIC Model Law #440)
- **Reinsurer Notice**: Standard reinsurance treaties typically require **notice** to reinsurers of change in ownership, but not necessarily consent³⁶

#### 2. Standard Change of Control Provisions in Reinsurance Treaties

**Typical Treaty Language:**

While actual treaty terms are not available in the data room, industry-standard reinsurance treaties typically include one of the following change of control provisions:

**Option 1: Notice Requirement Only**
> "The Ceding Company shall provide written notice to the Reinsurer within 45 days of any change of control of the Ceding Company. For purposes of this Agreement, 'change of control' means the acquisition by any person or group of 50% or more of the voting securities of the Ceding Company or its parent."

**Option 2: Consent Required (Reasonable Standard)**
> "The Ceding Company shall not assign this Agreement or undergo a change of control without the prior written consent of the Reinsurer, such consent not to be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed. The Reinsurer shall respond to any request for consent within 30 days."

**Option 3: Consent Required (Sole Discretion)**
> "This Agreement may not be assigned or transferred by either party without the prior written consent of the other party in its sole and absolute discretion."³⁷

**Consent Standards: Reasonable vs. Sole Discretion:**

The distinction between "consent not to be unreasonably withheld" and "sole discretion" is critical:

- **Reasonableness Standard**: Reinsurer must have legitimate commercial reason to withhold consent; cannot refuse arbitrarily; burden on reinsurer to show reasonableness of refusal.³⁸

- **Sole Discretion Standard**: Reinsurer can withhold consent for any reason, including preference for existing management or commercial considerations; no requirement to demonstrate reasonableness.³⁹

- **No Consent Clause**: If treaty is silent on assignment/change of control, common law generally permits assignment unless contract is for personal services or involves special trust/confidence; reinsurance treaties are commercial contracts and generally assignable.⁴⁰

**Industry Precedent:**

Research indicates that most reinsurance treaties use one of two approaches:
1. **Notice only** for change of control at parent/holding company level (stock purchase)
2. **Consent required** (usually with reasonableness standard) for direct assignment of treaty to different ceding company (asset purchase)⁴¹

The distinction reflects practical reality: in stock purchase, same ceding company continues to exist with same infrastructure, actuarial capabilities, and claim administration. Reinsurers' primary concern is **operational continuity** and financial stability of cedent, not identity of ultimate parent company.

#### 3. Recapture Provisions and Change of Control Triggers

**Recapture Definition and Mechanics:**

Recapture is the ceding company's right to **reclaim risks previously transferred to a reinsurer**. Recapture provisions specify:

1. **Triggering Events**: Conditions allowing cedent to exercise recapture right
2. **Notice Requirements**: How much advance notice cedent must provide (typically 90-180 days)
3. **Financial Settlement**: Recapture fee or ceding commission adjustment
4. **Reserve Transfer**: Mechanics of returning reserves and assets to cedent⁴²

**Common Recapture Triggering Events:**

Standard reinsurance treaties include recapture rights triggered by:

1. **Time-Based**: After specified period (e.g., 10 years), cedent may recapture at will
2. **Retention Increase**: If cedent increases maximum retention limits, cedent may recapture amounts now within retention
3. **Reinsurer Default/Impairment**: If reinsurer's financial strength rating falls below specified threshold (e.g., below A-)
4. **Reinsurer Change of Control**: Some treaties give cedent recapture right if reinsurer undergoes change of control
5. **Mutual Agreement**: Either party may propose recapture by mutual agreement⁴³

**Reinsurer-Initiated Recapture:**

Some treaties also grant **reinsurer** the right to recapture (commute the treaty) upon:

- **Cedent Change of Control**: Material change in ownership of ceding company
- **Material Adverse Change**: Cedent's financial condition deteriorates materially
- **Regulatory Issues**: Cedent becomes subject to regulatory supervision or rehabilitation⁴⁴

**Change of Control as Recapture Trigger:**

Research indicates that reinsurer-initiated recapture upon cedent change of control is **relatively uncommon** in modern treaties, for several reasons:

1. **Reinsurer Business Model**: Reinsurers generally want to keep profitable treaties in force; recapture means losing premium income and investment spread
2. **Acquirer Financial Strength**: In many M&A transactions, acquirer has stronger financial position than target, reducing reinsurer credit risk
3. **Operational Continuity**: Stock purchase maintains operational continuity; same management, systems, and claim administration often continue post-closing
4. **Negotiation Leverage**: Cedents negotiate to limit reinsurer recapture rights to preserve value of reinsurance treaties⁴⁵

**However**, change of control provisions may allow reinsurer to:
- **Demand consent** before transaction closes (leverage to renegotiate pricing or terms)
- **Renegotiate terms** upon consent (increase collateral, modify ceding commission, adjust premiums)
- **Exercise recapture** if consent withheld or not sought
