## IV.A. E&O POLICY COVERAGE FRAMEWORK

### 1. Standard Professional Liability Insurance for Financial Services

**Typical E&O Policy Structure for Insurance Companies/Broker-Dealers:**

Professional liability (Errors & Omissions) insurance for financial services companies, including insurance companies and broker-dealers, provides coverage for claims arising from professional services. The policy responds to actual or alleged mistakes, errors, omissions, negligence, misrepresentation, breach of fiduciary duty, failure to meet client expectations, and violations of regulations.Â¹

**Insuring Agreement â€” "Wrongful Act" Definition:**

E&O policies typically define coverage based on "Wrongful Act," which is the key term defining the scope of insured liability. Every policy differs, and definitions of "Wrongful Act" can vary significantly from one insurer to another. The wrongful act definition determines what professional conduct falls within coverage.Â²

For insurance companies and broker-dealers, the "wrongful act" definition typically encompasses:
- Errors, omissions, or negligent acts in rendering or failing to render professional services
- Misrepresentation or misleading statements made in connection with professional services
- Breach of fiduciary duty to clients
- Violations of regulatory requirements (subject to exclusions)
- Failure to supervise employees or representatives

**Coverage for Regulatory Investigations, Arbitrations, and Class Actions:**

Standard E&O policies for financial professionals cover:
- **Defense costs** for covered professional services, including legal representation
- **Court/arbitration hearing costs** for covered claims
- **Customer settlements, awards, or judgments** for covered claims, up to policy limits
- **FINRA arbitration proceedings** involving suitability claims, misrepresentation allegations, and other professional errorsÂ³

The definition of "claim" in E&O policies should include regulatory and administrative proceedings to ensure coverage for regulatory investigations and enforcement actions.â´

**Coverage Limitations for Securities Law Violations:**

**CRITICAL EXCLUSION:** Standard E&O policies typically **exclude coverage for violation of any securities act**.âµ This creates a significant coverage gap for financial services companies facing claims alleging securities law violations, even when those claims also allege professional negligence or misrepresentation.

This exclusion means that:
- Pure securities law violation claims (e.g., Securities Act of 1933, Investment Advisers Act of 1940 violations) may be entirely excluded
- Mixed claims alleging both securities violations and professional negligence may require allocation between covered and uncovered claims
- Consumer protection claims under state Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices (UDAP) statutes are often excluded or subject to sublimits

### 2. Claims-Made vs. Occurrence Trigger

**Claims-Made Policy Structure:**

E&O policies for financial services professionals are virtually always written on a **claims-made-and-reported basis**, meaning:
- The claim must be first made against the insured **during the policy period**
- The claim must be **reported to the insurer** during the policy period or an extended reporting period
- The wrongful act must occur **after the retroactive date** (if any) specified in the policyâ¶

This differs fundamentally from occurrence-based policies (typical for general liability), where coverage depends on when the injury or damage occurs, not when the claim is made.

**Policy Period and Reporting Requirements:**

Claims-made policies require:
1. **Timely reporting of claims** â€” typically "as soon as practicable" after the insured becomes aware of the claim
2. **Notice of circumstances** provisions â€” allowing insureds to report potential claims (circumstances that may give rise to a claim) before a claim is actually made
3. **Strict compliance** with reporting deadlines â€” failure to report within the policy period or grace period (typically 30-60 days) results in loss of coverageâ·

**Retroactive Date:**

The retroactive date establishes the earliest date for which wrongful acts are covered. Wrongful acts occurring before the retroactive date are excluded from coverage, even if the claim is made during the policy period.

### 3. Self-Insured Retention (SIR) vs. Deductible

**Critical Distinction â€” Financial and Coverage Implications:**

Liberty Life's E&O policy features a **$5 million Self-Insured Retention (SIR)**, which operates fundamentally differently from a deductible:

| Feature | Self-Insured Retention (SIR) | Deductible |
|---------|----------------------------|------------|
| **Payment Obligation** | Insured pays first $5M of loss AND defense costs before insurer obligation triggered | Insurer pays loss, then seeks reimbursement from insured up to deductible amount |
| **Defense Costs** | Typically included within SIR â€” insured pays own defense costs until SIR exhausted | Typically covered by insurer outside deductible |
| **Insurer Duty to Defend** | No duty to defend until SIR exhausted | Duty to defend attaches immediately |
| **Control of Defense** | Insured controls defense for claims within SIR, including choice of counsel | Insurer controls defense and selects counsel |
| **Policy Limits Erosion** | Policy limits typically NOT eroded by SIR (e.g., $5M SIR + $50M limit = $50M coverage excess of SIR) | Policy limits typically eroded by deductible (e.g., $50M limit - $5M deductible = $45M true coverage) |
| **Collateral Requirements** | Typically no collateral required (insurer pays nothing until SIR exhausted) | Often requires letter of credit or other collateral to secure insured's reimbursement obligation |â¸

**Application to Liberty Life Claims:**

With a $5 million SIR:
- **IUL Class Action** ($25M-$45M settlement range): LLIC must pay first $5M, then Chubb covers $20M-$40M (remainder up to $50M policy limit)
- **FINRA Arbitrations** ($830K claims + $200K-$300K defense costs = ~$1.1M total): Entirely within $5M SIR â€” LLIC pays 100%, Chubb pays nothing
- **Defense Cost Allocation**: Critical to determine whether defense costs are "within" or "outside" the SIR â€” if within, LLIC pays all defense costs until total costs + indemnity = $5M

**Per Claim vs. Aggregate SIR:**

Standard practice: SIR applies **per claim**, not aggregate. This means:
- IUL class action: $5M SIR applies
- Each of 12 FINRA arbitrations: Potentially separate $5M SIR **OR** aggregated as "related claims" with single $5M SIR

The policy's claim aggregation provisions determine whether multiple related claims are treated as a single claim for SIR and limits purposes.â¹

## IV.B. NEBRASKA DUTY TO DEFEND STANDARDS

### 1. Seminal Case: Allstate Insurance Co. v. Novak

**Foundational Precedent:**

Any determination of an insurer's duty to defend in Nebraska must begin with the seminal case of **Allstate Insurance Co. v. Novak, 210 Neb. 184, 313 N.W.2d 636 (1981)**, which established that **insurers have a duty to defend whenever facts are ascertained which give rise to the potential for liability under the policy**.Â¹â°

**Holding:**

The Nebraska Supreme Court recognized two separate and distinct contractual obligations:
1. **Duty to Indemnify**: Insurer's agreement to pay on behalf of the insured all sums the insured becomes legally obligated to pay
2. **Duty to Defend**: Separate obligation to defend any suit against the insured, including suits that are "groundless, false or fraudulent"Â¹Â¹

**Key Principle:** The duty to defend is **broader than the duty to indemnify** â€” an insurer may have an obligation to defend even though it might not ultimately have an obligation to pay.

### 2. Investigation Obligation

**Nebraska Standard:**

Under *Allstate v. Novak* and subsequent Nebraska case law, when determining whether to defend, **insurers cannot rely solely on the allegations of the petition** but must conduct an investigation to determine their obligation to defend.Â¹Â²

This differs from strict "four corners" jurisdictions that limit the duty to defend analysis to the face of the complaint and the policy.

**Bad Faith Implications:**

Reckless indifference to the facts or proof submitted by an insured is shown by the insurer's **failure to conduct a proper investigation and subject the results to a reasonable evaluation and review**. This can support a bad faith claim against the insurer.Â¹Â³

### 3. Application to Liberty Life's E&O Policy

**Broader Duty to Defend Than Four-Corners States:**

Nebraska's approach means that Chubb (the E&O carrier) must:
- Review the complaint allegations in the IUL class action and FINRA arbitrations
- **Conduct an independent investigation** of the underlying facts
- Determine whether any potentially covered claims exist
- Defend if there is **any potential for liability** under the E&O policy

Even if the complaint alleges only uncovered securities law violations, if Chubb's investigation reveals potential professional negligence or misrepresentation claims (which may be covered), Chubb likely has a duty to defend the entire action.

**Practical Implication:**

This favorable standard increases the likelihood that Chubb must defend Liberty Life, even if coverage for indemnity is questionable due to securities law exclusions or other policy limitations.

## IV.E. NOTICE REQUIREMENTS AND COMPLIANCE

### 1. Claims-Made Policy Notice Requirements

**General Standard:**

Claims-made-and-reported E&O policies require:
1. Claim must be **first made** during the policy period
2. Claim must be **reported to insurer** during the policy period or extended reporting period
3. Notice must be given within a **"reasonable time"** (typically "as soon as practicable")Â²â·

**Strict Compliance Requirement:**

Timely reporting is a **condition precedent to coverage** under claims-made policies. Failure to provide notice within the policy period or grace period (typically 30-60 days) can result in **loss of coverage regardless of whether the insurer is prejudiced** by the delay.Â²â¸

### 2. Notice-Prejudice Rule â€” Limited Application to Claims-Made Policies

**Occurrence Policies:**

In a majority of jurisdictions, the **"notice-prejudice rule"** provides that an insurer may not deny coverage based on late notice **without demonstrating prejudice**. This rule protects insureds from forfeiting coverage due to technical notice failures when the insurer suffers no harm.Â²â¹

**Claims-Made Policies:**

Most courts hold that the notice-prejudice rule **does not apply to claims-made-and-reported policies**. The date-certain notice requirement **defines the scope of coverage**, and excusing late notice would rewrite a fundamental term of the insurance contract.Â³â°

**Nebraska Position:**

Nebraska appears to follow the majority rule that late notice under a claims-made policy results in loss of coverage **without requiring the insurer to prove prejudice**.Â³Â¹

**Practical Implication:**

Liberty Life's compliance with notice requirements is **critical**. Late notice can forfeit coverage entirely, even if Chubb suffers no prejudice from the delay.

## IV.F. SUCCESSOR LIABILITY AND TAIL COVERAGE FOR M&A TRANSACTION

### 1. Change of Control Impact on Claims-Made Coverage

**Standard Change of Control Provision:**

E&O insurance policies typically contain provisions triggered by a **change in control**, which occurs when there is a change in ownership of more than 50% of voting stock (though some policies may treat changes of more than 25% as a change in control).Â³â´

When a change of control occurs, the E&O policy may:
1. **Terminate automatically** upon consummation of transaction
2. **Require reporting** of the change within specified timeframe (typically 30 days)
3. **Trigger extended reporting period (ERP) election** â€” insured must purchase tail coverage to maintain ability to report future claims arising from pre-closing wrongful actsÂ³âµ

**American Financial Holdings Acquisition of Liberty Life:**

### 2. Extended Reporting Period (Tail Coverage)

**What is Tail Coverage?**

An Extended Reporting Period (ERP), commonly called "tail coverage," extends the reporting period for claims covered by the policy into the future, allowing the insured to report claims arising from prior wrongful acts for a specified period after policy expiration or termination.Â³â¶

**Why Tail Coverage is Critical for M&A:**

Professional liability claims implicating E&O policies don't always arise immediately â€” some can be filed **years after services conclude**. This makes tail coverage essential during transitions such as mergers, acquisitions, or business closures when liabilities from past acts might surface unexpectedly.Â³â·

**Coverage Scope:**

Tail coverage provides:
- **Extended reporting period**: Typically 12-60 months (36 months most common)
- **Coverage for prior wrongful acts**: Claims arising from professional services performed before policy termination
- **Same policy limits**: Tail coverage shares the same limits as the expired policy (not additional limits)
- **Same SIR**: Applies same $5M self-insured retention per claimÂ³â¸

**What Tail Coverage Does NOT Provide:**

- Does not cover wrongful acts occurring **after** policy termination date
- Does not provide additional policy limits (same $50M per claim, $100M aggregate applies to both original policy period and extended reporting period combined)
- Does not cover claims already known or reported before tail purchase

**Alternative to Tail Coverage:**

- Claims arising from Liberty Life's pre-acquisition professional services
- Filed after acquisition closing date
- Within new policy limits and terms

**Comparison:**

| Feature | Tail Coverage (Old Policy) | New Policy with Prior Acts |
|---------|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Cost** | 200%-250% of old premium (one-time) | Higher annual premium ongoing |
| **Limits** | Old limits ($50M per claim, $100M agg) | New limits (potentially higher) |
| **Terms** | Old policy terms (known exclusions, SIR) | New negotiated terms |
| **Duration** | Fixed (e.g., 36 months) | Ongoing (renewable annually) |
| **Suitability** | Clean break; seller pays | Acquirer assumes tail exposure |

**Recommendation:**

**Tail coverage** is typically preferred for pre-closing liabilities:
- Seller retains responsibility
- Acquirer avoids unknown seller liabilities
- Clean separation between pre-closing and post-closing coverage
- Tail costs can be escrowed or deducted from purchase price
