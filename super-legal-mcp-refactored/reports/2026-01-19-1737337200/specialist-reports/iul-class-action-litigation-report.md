### Legal Claims Strength Assessment

**1. Breach of Contract (MEDIUM-HIGH Strength)**
- **Theory:** COI increases exceeded contractual authority; undisclosed profit margins/administrative costs included
- **Defense:** NAIC Model 582 compliance; policy contains maximum COI schedule; cap/floor changes disclosed
- **Precedent:** *Hobish v. AXA Equitable* (N.Y. 2025) dismissed COI claims (favorable to defense); BUT Lincoln National, Transamerica settlements indicate insurers prefer settlement to adverse contract interpretation
- **Exposure:** $25M-$45M compensatory if plaintiff victory

**2. Nebraska Consumer Protection Act (MEDIUM Strength)**
- **Theory:** Illustrations showing 8.5% crediting rates without adequate risk disclosure = deceptive practices (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 59-1601)
- **Defense:** Illustrations showed guaranteed and non-guaranteed columns; 8.5% based on historical S&P 500 1926-2015; policy contracts (not illustrations) govern
- **Industry Standard:** IUL illustrations should use ≤5.5% non-guaranteed rates (8.5% may be viewed as unrealistic)
- **Exposure:** Actual damages + statutory remedies + attorney fees recovery

**3. Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing — Bad Faith (LOW-MEDIUM Strength)**
- **Nebraska Law:** *Braesch v. Union Ins. Co.*, 464 N.W.2d 769 (Neb. 1991) — Tort-based claim; consequential damages; **punitive damages available** for "egregious and outrageous misconduct"
- **Risk:** Discovery of internal emails showing profit-driven COI increases (not actuarial necessity) strengthens bad faith claim
- **Punitive Multiplier:** 2-3× compensatory damages if proven
- **Exposure:** **$50M-$135M** (compensatory + punitives) — **TAIL RISK ELIMINATED BY EARLY SETTLEMENT**

### C. Legal Claims Analysis — *Policyholders v. Liberty Life Insurance Company*

#### 1. Breach of Contract Claims

**Contract Term Interpretation:**
IUL policies contain specific provisions regarding cost of insurance charges. The central dispute typically involves:
- **Policy Language:** Whether the contract explicitly reserves the insurer's right to increase COI charges unilaterally
- **Contractual Limits:** Whether increases must be based solely on mortality experience or may include administrative costs, profit margins, and reserve requirements
- **Guaranteed vs. Non-Guaranteed Elements:** NAIC Model Regulation 582 requires illustrations show both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements, including maximum COI charge schedules

**Policyholder Theory:**
- COI increases exceeded contractual authority
- Insurer improperly included profit margins and expenses unrelated to actual mortality risk
- Policy language ambiguous; construed against drafter (contra proferentem doctrine)

**LLIC Defense:**
- Policy contracts contain express language reserving right to adjust COI charges based on current mortality assumptions and regulatory requirements
- Increases fell within contractual maximum COI charge schedule disclosed in policy
- Economic factors (low interest rates, increased longevity, adverse selection) justified actuarially sound COI adjustments

**Precedent:**
- **Favorable to Plaintiffs:** *Transamerica* settlements ($195M, $88M) involved similar COI increase challenges where insurers settled rather than litigate policy interpretation
- **Favorable to Defense:** *Hobish v. AXA Equitable*, 2025 N.Y. Ct. App. (affirmed dismissal of COI claims, holding AXA did not breach contract terms regarding COI charges) [VERIFIED]
- **Mixed:** Settlement patterns suggest insurers prefer settlement to avoid adverse precedent on contract interpretation

**Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing (Nebraska):**

Under Nebraska law, every insurance contract contains an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. *Braesch v. Union Ins. Co.*, 464 N.W.2d 769 (Neb. 1991). Key elements:
- Insurer must use due care and reasonable diligence to ascertain facts surrounding claims
- Insurer may not unreasonably and in bad faith withhold benefits
- Tort-based claim separate from contract breach, allowing consequential damages and potentially punitive damages

**Application to IUL Class Action:**
- Plaintiffs may allege LLIC exercised COI increase discretion in bad faith by prioritizing profits over policyholder interests
- Evidence of internal documents showing profit motivation rather than actuarial necessity could support bad faith claim
- Nebraska law allows punitive damages for "egregious and outrageous misconduct" in insurance bad faith cases (though availability debated)

Citation: [Nebraska Recognizes Insurance First Party Bad Faith Actions](https://www.propertyinsurancecoveragelaw.com/blog/nebraska-recognizes-insurance-first-party-bad-faith-actions/)

#### 3. ERISA Preemption Analysis (If Group Policies Involved)

**ERISA Coverage Determination:**
If any IUL policies sold as employer-sponsored group life insurance, ERISA § 502(a)(1)(B) may provide exclusive federal jurisdiction.

**Complete Preemption Doctrine:**
When a plaintiff seeks to recover benefits under insurance policy funding employer's plan, this creates "colorable claim for benefits" giving standing to sue under ERISA. State law claims (breach of contract, consumer protection) would be completely preempted.

Citation: [ERISA Preemption 101](https://blog.ifebp.org/erisa-preemption-101/)

**Savings Clause Exception:**
ERISA does not supersede state laws regulating insurance. For fully insured group plans, states may regulate the insurance contract terms indirectly through regulation of insurer.

**Bright-Line Rule:**
- **Fully Insured Plan:** State may regulate indirectly through regulation of insurer and insurance contracts
- **Self-Funded Plan:** State may not regulate (deemer clause exempts from state insurance laws)

Citation: [Primer on ERISA Preemption](https://www.nashp.org/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/ERISA_Primer.pdf)

**Application to LLIC:**
- **Individual IUL Policies:** No ERISA preemption; state law claims proceed in state or federal court
- **Employer-Sponsored Group UL:** ERISA likely preempts state claims; plaintiffs must pursue ERISA § 502(a)(1)(B) denial of benefits claim in federal court

**Strategic Consideration:**
If class includes both individual and group policies, class certification may be complicated by ERISA preemption creating separate subclasses with different governing law.

#### 2. Rule 23(b)(3) Predominance and Superiority

**Predominance (Rule 23(b)(3)):**

**District of Nebraska Standards:**

A plaintiff must affirmatively demonstrate his compliance with the Rule—that is, he must be prepared to prove that there are in fact sufficiently numerous parties, common questions of law or fact, etc."

### G. Early Settlement Negotiation Strategy (RECOMMENDED)

- **Control Narrative:** Early settlement prevents damaging internal documents from entering public record through discovery
