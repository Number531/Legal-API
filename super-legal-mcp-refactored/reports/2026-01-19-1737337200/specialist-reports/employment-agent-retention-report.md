## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

American Financial Holdings LLC's $2.9B acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) presents **substantial employment and agent retention exposure** totaling **$98M-$158M** across seven legal domains: executive severance, WARN Act compliance, ERISA obligations, IRC ¬ß 409A deferred compensation, agent retention bonuses, non-compete unenforceability, and employment litigation. The most critical risk is **agent attrition** (25-35% of 650 captive agents generating 42% of sales), which could reduce annual sales by $220M-$309M ($33M-$46M EBITDA impact, $330M-$463M valuation impact at 10√ó multiple). A comprehensive $41.6M retention bonus program can reduce attrition to 10% and preserve $254M in annual sales, generating positive ROI within 12 months.

### Key Takeaways

- **Agent Retention is Mission-Critical:** Without retention bonuses, 25-35% captive agent attrition (163-228 agents) will result in $220M-$309M annual sales decline, $33M-$46M EBITDA loss, and $330M-$463M valuation destruction (11-16% of purchase price). Comprehensive $41.6M retention program reduces attrition to 10% and generates net benefit of $19.2M in year 1, $60.8M annually thereafter.

- **Executive Severance Exposure: $48M-$52M:** If all 15 top executives terminated within 24 months post-closing (double-trigger change-of-control provisions standard), cash severance + benefits + equity acceleration totals $47.9M-$51.9M. More likely scenario: 50-60% partial turnover = $15M-$22M exposure.

- **WARN Act Compliance Mandatory:** Any workforce reduction ‚â•50 employees at Omaha home office (1,900 employees) triggers 60-day advance notice requirement. Estimated post-acquisition redundancies: 480-710 employees (IT, claims, customer service consolidation). WARN Act violation exposure: $14.4M-$17.9M (back pay + benefits + penalties + attorney fees). Nebraska has NO mini-WARN statute (federal law only applies).

- **ERISA 401(k) Vesting Acceleration: $0-$12.5M:** If LLIC 401(k) plan TERMINATED (rather than merged into acquirer plan), IRC ¬ß 411(d)(3) mandates 100% vesting of all employer match contributions for 833 employees with unvested balances = $12.5M cost. - **IRC ¬ß 409A Deferred Compensation: $24.5M Payout:** Estimated $24.5M in nonqualified deferred comp (SERPs, bonus deferrals, director fees) for 38 executives/directors. - **Non-Compete Agreements Largely Unenforceable:** Nebraska common law PROHIBITS geographic/industry non-competes for employees; only narrow customer non-solicitation allowed (clients with direct agent contact). ### B. WARN ACT COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS

#### 1. Federal WARN Act Requirements (29 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 2101-2109)

**Statutory Framework:**

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act of 1988 (29 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 2101-2109) protects employees, their families, and communities by requiring most employers with 100 or more employees to provide **60 calendar days advance written notice** before plant closings and mass layoffs.‚Å∑

**Employer Coverage:**

The WARN Act applies to employers with at least 100 or more employees (excluding part-time employees who work less than 20 hours per week and employees who have worked less than 6 months in the last 12 months). Federal, state, and local government employers are exempt.‚Å∏

**Triggering Events (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 2102):**

WARN Act notice requirements are triggered by:

1. **Plant Closing:** Permanent or temporary shutdown of a single site of employment, or one or more facilities or operating units within a single site, resulting in employment loss for 50 or more employees (excluding part-time) during any 30-day period.‚Åπ

2. **Mass Layoff:** Employment loss at a single site of employment during any 30-day period for:
   - 500 or more employees (excluding part-time); OR
   - 50-499 employees (excluding part-time) if they constitute at least 33% of the employer's active workforce at the site.¬π‚Å∞

**Notice Recipients (20 C.F.R. ¬ß 639.6):**

Written notice must be provided to:
- Each representative of affected employees (union), or if no union, to each affected employee individually
- State dislocated worker unit (Nebraska Department of Labor)
- Chief elected official of the unit of local government (Omaha Mayor for home office)¬π¬π

**Exceptions (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 2102(b)):**

60-day notice period may be reduced under three exceptions:
1. **Faltering company:** Actively seeking capital/business and reasonable belief that giving notice would preclude obtaining capital
2. **Unforeseeable business circumstances:** Circumstances not reasonably foreseeable when notice would have been required
3. **Natural disaster:** Flood, earthquake, drought, storm, etc.¬π¬≤

Employer must provide as much notice as practicable and state reason for reduced notice period.

**Liability for Violations (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 2104):**

Employers who fail to provide required notice are liable to each affected employee for:
- Back pay (rate of average regular compensation) for each day of violation, up to 60 days
- Value of benefits (health insurance, pension contributions) for each day of violation, up to 60 days
- Civil penalty up to $500 per day for failure to notify local government (paid to local government)¬π¬≥

**Attorney Fees:** Courts may award reasonable attorney fees to prevailing plaintiffs (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 2104(a)(6)).

### C. ERISA PENSION AND RETIREMENT BENEFITS

#### 1. Defined Benefit Pension Plan Requirements (ERISA Title I)

**Statutory Framework:**

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA), Public Law 93-406, codified at 29 U.S.C. ¬ß 1001 et seq., sets standards that private-sector pension plans must follow with regard to plan participation, minimum vesting requirements, fiduciary duties, and plan funding.¬π‚Åπ

ERISA authorized creation of the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC), a federal agency that insures private-sector defined benefit plan benefits up to specified maximums in the case of plan termination. PBGC funding comes from (1) insurance premiums paid by companies whose plans it protects; (2) investments; (3) assets of pension plans PBGC takes over as trustee; and (4) recoveries in bankruptcy.¬≤‚Å∞

**PBGC Reportable Events (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 1343; 29 C.F.R. Part 4043):**

Section 4043 of ERISA requires that plan administrators and sponsors notify PBGC of the occurrence of certain events that may present a risk to a plan sponsor's ability to continue a pension plan. In most cases, reporting is due 30 days after the event occurs (post-event reporting).¬≤¬π

**Change in Contributing Sponsor or Controlled Group:**

For certain events including "Change in Contributing Sponsor or Controlled Group," reporting is waived if the plan qualifies for the **low-default-risk waiver**. For small plans (fewer than 100 participants with accrued benefits), reporting is waived for Change in Contributing Sponsor or Controlled Group events.¬≤¬≤

**Critical Finding for LLIC:**

If LLIC maintains a defined benefit pension plan with ‚â•100 participants:
- Change of control (acquisition by American Financial Holdings) constitutes a "Change in Contributing Sponsor or Controlled Group" reportable event

- Failure to report = regulatory violation (penalties vary)

**ERISA Section 4062(e) ‚Äî Cessation of Operations:**

Section 4062(e) is a provision intended to protect pensions where a company ceases operations and workers lose jobs. Following 2014 amendments, pension plans falling into either category are exempt from 4062(e):
1. Plans with fewer than 100 participants with accrued benefits as of plan valuation date; OR
2. Plans that were at least 90% funded in the plan year before cessation occurred.¬≤¬≥

#### 2. 401(k) Plan Vesting Acceleration upon Plan Termination

**Standard ERISA Vesting Requirements:**

Under ERISA and IRC ¬ß 411(a), employer contributions must vest under one of two schedules:
1. **3-year cliff vesting:** Contributions 100% vested when participant completes 3 years of service; OR
2. **6-year graded vesting:** Gradual vesting over 6 years (20% at year 2, 40% at year 3, 60% at year 4, 80% at year 5, 100% at year 6).¬≤‚Å¥

**Employee elective deferrals (contributions)** are always immediately 100% vested.¬≤‚Åµ

**Automatic Full Vesting Triggers (IRC ¬ß 411(d)(3)):**

When a 401(k) plan terminates, the accrued benefits of all affected employees must become **100% vested** under Internal Revenue Code Section 411(d)(3). This applies to employer nonelective contributions (profit-sharing) and matching contributions.¬≤‚Å∂

Upon plan termination, all affected participants must become 100% vested in their account balances. If the plan is terminated, the account balance of any participant (active or terminated) not fully vested prior to the sale must be made fully vested at plan termination.¬≤‚Å∑

**Partial Plan Termination (20% Turnover Rule):**

If a company has terminated 20% of its workforce, all affected participants will be 100% vested under the IRS partial plan termination rules. The partial termination rule may require that employees who terminate during the transaction period be fully vested in their accounts when the plan's turnover rate for the period is at least 20%.¬≤‚Å∏

### D. IRC ¬ß 409A DEFERRED COMPENSATION COMPLIANCE

#### 1. IRC ¬ß 409A Statutory Framework (26 U.S.C. ¬ß 409A)

**Background:**

Section 409A of the Internal Revenue Code, enacted in 2004, governs nonqualified deferred compensation plans. Under Section 409A, distributions from nonqualified deferred compensation plans can only be payable upon one of six permissible distribution events, including **"a change in ownership or effective control of the corporation, or a change in the ownership of a substantial portion of the assets of the corporation."**¬≤‚Åπ

**Permissible Payment Events (26 C.F.R. ¬ß 1.409A-3):**

Treasury Regulation ¬ß 1.409A-3 specifies permissible payment triggers:
1. Separation from service
2. Death
3. Disability
4. Specified time or fixed schedule
5. **Change in control of corporation**
6. Unforeseeable emergency¬≥‚Å∞

**Change of Control Definition (Treas. Reg. ¬ß 1.409A-3(i)(5)):**

A change in control event occurs when:

**(A) Change in Ownership:** A person (or persons acting as a group) acquires stock that, together with stock already owned, constitutes more than 50% of the total fair market value or total voting power of the stock of the corporation.¬≥¬π

**(B) Change in Effective Control:** Either:
- A person (or persons acting as a group) acquires (or has acquired during 12-month period) 30% or more of total voting power of stock; OR
- Majority of members of corporation's board of directors is replaced during 12-month period by directors whose appointment/election not endorsed by majority of board before date of appointment/election.

**(C) Change in Ownership of Substantial Portion of Assets:** A person (or persons acting as a group) acquires (or has acquired during 12-month period) assets having total gross fair market value equal to ‚â•40% of total gross fair market value of corporation's assets immediately before acquisition(s).¬≥¬≤

**Application to Project Chronos:**

American Financial Holdings LLC's acquisition of 100% equity of Liberty Life Insurance Company (stock purchase) constitutes a **change in ownership under ¬ß 409A** (>50% stock acquisition).

**Implication:** LLIC's nonqualified deferred compensation plans that include "change of control" as a permissible distribution event will TRIGGER upon acquisition closing. Deferred compensation balances become immediately payable.

#### 2. Compliance Requirements and Tax Consequences

**409A Compliance Requirements:**

If a nonqualified deferred compensation plan FAILS to comply with ¬ß 409A (e.g., distribution made upon impermissible event, or election to defer made improperly):

**Tax Penalties (26 U.S.C. ¬ß 409A(a)(1)):**
- **Immediate income inclusion:** Entire deferred amount included in gross income in year of vesting (not year of distribution)
- **20% additional tax:** Imposed on deferred compensation included in gross income
- **Interest charge:** Premium interest tax on underpayments (calculated from year of vesting to year of inclusion)¬≥¬≥

**Example:**
- Executive deferred $500,000 compensation (vested in 2020)
- Plan distributed in 2025 upon change of control (improper trigger not complying with ¬ß 409A)
- Tax consequences to executive:
  - $500,000 ordinary income (2020 rates apply retroactively)
  - $100,000 additional tax (20% penalty)
  - Interest charges (premium rate) from 2020-2025 ‚âà $50,000
  - **Total tax cost:** $150,000 penalty + interest (executive pays, not employer, but reputational/retention impact)

**Employer Reporting Requirements:**

If plan fails ¬ß 409A compliance:
- Employer must report failed 409A amounts on Form W-2, Box 12, Code Z
- Failure to report = penalties under IRC ¬ß 6721 (failure to file correct information returns)

### F. NON-COMPETE AND NON-SOLICIT ENFORCEABILITY

#### 1. Nebraska Non-Compete Law (Common Law Standard ‚Äî No Statute)

**Critical Finding:** Nebraska does NOT have a statute governing noncompetition agreements ancillary to employment relationships generally. Nebraska common law governs these agreements.‚Å¥‚Å∑

**Nebraska Common Law Standard:**

Nebraska courts have adopted a **strict view** of post-employment restrictions on employees and have concluded that a post-employment restriction on an employee must be **no greater than reasonably necessary to protect an employer's legitimate interest** (such as goodwill, confidential information, or trade secrets).‚Å¥‚Å∏

**Geographic Restrictions on Employees ‚Äî PROHIBITED:**

**Traditional non-compete clauses with geographic restrictions are NOT allowed in post-employment restrictions on employees.** Rather, Nebraska courts recognize only a **narrow customer non-solicitation restriction**, which must be properly limited to prevent solicitation of only those customers that the employee specifically did business with and had personal contact.‚Å¥‚Åπ

**Example of Unenforceable Non-Compete (March 2024 Case):**

A non-competition clause was deemed unenforceable under Nebraska law because it was overly broad in that it prevented an employee from accepting employment with any entity that "offers or produces products or services that are substantially similar" to the employer's products. The non-solicitation clause was also found unenforceable because the clause was not limited to restricting the employee from soliciting clients or accounts with whom he actually did business and had personal contact.‚Åµ‚Å∞

**Nebraska's Rejection of Blue Pencil Doctrine:**

**Nebraska precedent has consistently declined to apply the "blue pencil rule,"** which enables courts to reform covenants to make them enforceable. Nebraska is an example of a long-standing adherent to the **red-pencil doctrine**, which many view as a Draconian rule.‚Åµ¬π

**Implication:** If one piece of a restrictive covenant is found unreasonable, the **entire covenant will be unenforceable**. Courts will NOT rewrite overbroad provisions to make them reasonable (unlike majority of states that use "blue pencil" reformation).‚Åµ¬≤

### H. LABOR UNION CONSIDERATIONS

#### 1. NLRA Successorship Doctrine (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 151 et seq.)

**Burns Successorship Standard:**

Under NLRB v. Burns Security Services, Inc., 406 U.S. 272 (1972), the Supreme Court held that successor employers are generally free to set initial terms and conditions of employment. While Burns successors are required to recognize and bargain with an incumbent labor organization, successors are NOT typically obligated to honor the specific collective bargaining agreement negotiated by the predecessor.‚Å∂‚Å¥

**Successorship Criteria:**

The NLRB's successorship doctrine obligates a purchaser/new employer in an asset transaction to recognize and bargain with the union representing a seller's employees if the new employer: (i) continues its predecessor's business in substantially unchanged form, AND (ii) hires predecessor employees as a majority of its post-closing workforce.‚Å∂‚Åµ

**You are a Burns successor if you hire the majority of your employees from the predecessor's workforce, and from their perspective day-to-day life at work remains largely unchanged.**‚Å∂‚Å∂
