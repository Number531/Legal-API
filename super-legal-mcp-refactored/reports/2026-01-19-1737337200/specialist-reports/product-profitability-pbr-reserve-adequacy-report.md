## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. NAIC Valuation Manual VM-20 Principle-Based Reserves Framework

#### 1. Regulatory Background and Implementation Status

**VM-20 Adoption Timeline:**
The NAIC Valuation Manual became operative on January 1, 2017, establishing principle-based reserving (PBR) requirements for life insurance products.¹ All accredited states revised their Standard Valuation Law (Model #820) and Standard Nonforfeiture Law for Life Insurance (Model #808) to implement PBR, which became an NAIC accreditation standard on January 1, 2020.²

**2026 Regulatory Updates:**
Effective January 1, 2026, the NAIC implemented a new Generator of Economic Scenarios (GOES), allowing insurers to use non-prescribed economic scenario generators across PBR requirements for life insurance and annuity products (VM-20, VM-21, VM-22).³ This represents a significant shift from the prior requirement to use only prescribed scenario generators.

**Rationale for PBR:**
Prior to PBR, static formulas and assumptions prescribed by state laws (Commissioners Reserve Valuation Method - CRVM) were used to determine reserves. This rules-based approach sometimes left life insurers with excessive reserves for certain products and inadequate reserves for others.⁴ The principle-based approach allows insurers to "right-size" reserves based on company-specific experience and economic scenarios, subject to regulatory floors and governance requirements.

#### 2. VM-20 Reserve Methodology Components

VM-20 requires calculation of three reserve components:⁵

**a) Net Premium Reserve (NPR):** Mandatory for all policies subject to VM-20. Calculated using prescribed assumptions and methodology, similar to traditional net premium reserves under prior regulation.

**b) Deterministic Reserve (DR):** Calculated in the aggregate using prescribed scenarios. Companies may elect to perform exclusion tests that, if passed, exempt some groups of policies from the Deterministic Reserve calculation.

**c) Stochastic Reserve (SR):** Calculated in the aggregate using stochastic modeling with a minimum of 10,000 economic scenarios. The Stochastic Reserve represents the Conditional Tail Expectation at the 70th percentile (CTE70) of scenario reserves. Companies may elect to perform exclusion tests to exempt certain policies.

**Minimum Reserve Requirement:**
The VM-20 minimum reserve is the greater of:
- NPR + max(DR - NPR, 0) [if stochastic exclusion test passed]
- NPR + max(SR - NPR, 0) [if deterministic exclusion test passed]
- NPR + max(DR - NPR, SR - NPR, 0) [if both exclusion tests failed]

**Product-Specific Requirements:**
For term life insurance and universal life with secondary guarantees (ULSG) subject to Actuarial Guideline 38 (AG38), Deterministic Reserves must be calculated, making the deterministic exclusion test inapplicable.⁶

#### 3. VM-21 Requirements for Variable Annuities with GMWB Riders

**Regulatory Framework:**
VM-21 was adopted by the NAIC on December 2, 2012, effective for all variable annuity contracts issued on or after January 1, 2017.⁷ VM-21 effectively incorporated Actuarial Guideline 43 (AG 43), which previously governed variable annuity reserves.

**CTE70 Reserve Calculation:**
The Conditional Tail Expectation Amount (CTEA) is based on CTE70 and equals the numerical average of the 30 percent largest values of the Scenario Greatest Present Values.⁸ The stochastic reserve is a CTE70 valuation based on underlying scenario reserves calculated with and without hedging effectiveness.

**CTE98 for Risk-Based Capital:**
While VM-21 requires CTE70 for statutory reserves, the C-3 Phase II risk-based capital requirement for variable annuities uses CTE98. The C-3 RBC charge equals 25% of the excess of CTE98 over reserves.⁹ This creates an integrated framework where reserves (CTE70) and capital (CTE98) are calculated using consistent cash flow modeling.

**GMWB-Specific Considerations:**
Guaranteed Minimum Withdrawal Benefits (GMWB) riders provide a guaranteed withdrawal amount for life regardless of the account value (typically 5% annually).¹⁰ Model office analyses for VM-21 include GMWB/GMDB combination riders with varying characteristics:
- Moneyness: In-the-money (ITM) vs. At-the-money (ATM)
- Guarantee strength: Strong vs. weak withdrawal guarantees
- Block maturity: Mature vs. new business
- Hedging approaches: Dynamic hedging vs. static hedging vs. no hedging

#### 2. 2017 CSO Mortality Table Implementation

**Regulatory Timeline:**
In 2015, the NAIC officially adopted the 2017 Commissioners Standard Ordinary (CSO) mortality table to replace the 2001 CSO table.¹⁵ During a three-year phase-in period (January 1, 2017 to December 31, 2019), companies could use either the 2001 CSO or 2017 CSO. Effective January 1, 2020, life insurance companies were required to use only the 2017 CSO table for new business.¹⁶

**Preferred Class Structure:**
The 2017 CSO mortality table includes both smoker and nonsmoker mortality tables and composite mortality tables, supplemented by a preferred class structure:¹⁷
- **Super Preferred Nonsmoker Tables**
- **Preferred Nonsmoker Tables**
- **Residual Standard Nonsmoker Tables**

For each plan of insurance with separate rates for preferred and standard nonsmoker lives, insurers may use these preferred class tables to substitute for the standard Nonsmoker Mortality Table when determining minimum reserves.

**Impact on Reserve Levels:**
The 2017 CSO table reflects improved mortality experience (longer life expectancy) compared to the 2001 CSO table. This generally results in:
- **Lower mortality charges** for term life insurance (fewer expected death claims)
- **Higher reserves** for whole life and universal life products (policyholders expected to live longer)
- **Reduced deficiency reserves** for term products under Regulation XXX

**Tax Code Integration:**
For contracts issued on or after January 1, 2020, use of the 2017 CSO tables became mandatory for testing life insurance contract qualification under IRC Section 7702.¹⁸ This ensures consistency between reserve calculations and tax qualification testing.

**LLIC Application:**
LLIC's product portfolio includes both legacy products (using 2001 CSO or earlier tables) and new business (using 2017 CSO mandatory since January 1, 2020). For VM-20 PBR calculations on 2017+ business, LLIC must use:
- **Anticipated mortality:** Company-specific experience adjusted for trends
- **Prescribed floors:** 2017 CSO table at prescribed percentage (commonly 85% for term, 90% for permanent products)
- **Margins:** Additional margins if company experience insufficient or credibility low

### C. Asset Adequacy Testing and Actuarial Opinion Requirements

#### 1. Regulatory Evolution: AOMR to VM-30

**Historical Framework:**
In December 1990, the NAIC amended the Standard Valuation Law, and in June 1991, the NAIC adopted the Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum Regulation (AOMR).¹⁹ The most significant changes required companies to:
- Name an appointed actuary
- For companies subject to Section 8 of the AOMR, render statements of actuarial opinion on reserve and other liability adequacy based on an asset adequacy analysis (AAA)
- Submit a supporting memorandum describing the AAA methodology

**Transition to VM-30:**
Starting in 2001, the NAIC model AOMR required all actuarial opinions to be based on asset adequacy analysis.²⁰ These requirements have since been superseded by VM-30: Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum Requirements of the Valuation Manual.²¹

**Current VM-30 Requirements (Effective 2017+):**
VM-30 establishes requirements for the annual actuarial opinion and memorandum pursuant to Section 3 of the Standard Valuation Law. The appointed actuary must:
- Render an opinion on the adequacy of reserves and related actuarial items
- Base the opinion on asset adequacy analysis using cash flow testing
- Submit a Regulatory Asset Adequacy Issues Summary (RAAIS) by April 1 following the statement year

#### 2. Cash Flow Testing Under "Moderately Adverse Conditions"

**Methodology:**
Asset adequacy analysis must conform to standards of practice promulgated by the Actuarial Standards Board (ASB).²² Common applications include:
- Principle-based reserves (VM-20, VM-21)
- Asset adequacy analysis for statutory reserves
- Reinsurance risk transfer testing
- Investment strategy analysis

**Moderately Adverse Conditions Standard:**
The appointed actuary must test whether reserves are adequate under moderately adverse conditions, considering:
- **Interest rate scenarios:** Rising and falling interest rate paths
- **Mortality/morbidity scenarios:** Worse-than-expected claims experience
- **Lapse scenarios:** Higher or lower persistency than anticipated
- **Expense scenarios:** Increased unit costs or overhead allocation

If reserves are inadequate under moderately adverse conditions, the actuary must recommend additional reserves (reserve strengthening).

#### 3. Regulation XXX and Asset Adequacy Testing

**Special Requirements for XXX Products:**
Per Regulation XXX (adopted March 1999), if any X factor for any policy in a company is less than 100%, then:²³
- The standard actuarial opinion and memorandum must be based on asset adequacy analysis
- The appointed actuary must annually opine on whether the mortality rates resulting from application of the X factors meet the requirements of the Model

**X Factors Defined:**
X factors are multipliers applied to prescribed deficiency reserve formulas. An X factor less than 100% indicates the company is using less conservative assumptions than the prescribed standard, triggering enhanced scrutiny.

#### 4. Indexed Universal Life (IUL): Cap Rate Dynamics and Profitability Pressures

**Industry Cap Rate Compression (2015-2025):**

**LLIC IUL Cap Rate History:**
Per the research plan, LLIC reduced IUL caps from 12% (2019) to 10% (current), a 200 basis point reduction. This aligns with industry trends but creates significant policyholder dissatisfaction and litigation risk (see Thompson v. Liberty Life class action analysis in T4).

**Hedging Cost Economics:**
IUL profitability depends on the insurer's ability to fund index credits through option strategies:³⁴
- **Bond allocation:** ~95% of assets invested in bonds to ensure 0% floor (principal protection)
- **Option budget:** ~5% of assets available for derivatives to provide upside
- **Hedging strategy:** Sell call option at cap rate, use proceeds to partially offset purchasing call at current market level
- **Market volatility impact:** When VIX exceeds 30, hedging costs can increase from 0.60% to 1.20-1.50% of account value

**Cap Rate Sustainability Under Current Market Conditions:**
2025 market dynamics show:³⁵
- Caps and participation rates increasingly dynamic, adjusting with market conditions rather than remaining static
- Market volatility dramatically increased option costs, with expectations that cap rates may drop further
- Many companies propped up cap rates by increasing costs elsewhere (higher COI charges, higher expenses, higher surrender charges)

**LLIC IUL Profitability Analysis ($1.7B in-force):**
Based on industry profitability targets of 10.0% ROI for AccumIUL and higher costs from Thompson litigation:
- **Target annual profit:** $170M (10% ROI on $1.7B reserves)
- **Actual profitability:** Likely below target due to:
  - Cap rate reductions (12% → 10%) reducing policyholder crediting, increasing lapse risk
  - Thompson class action reserve of $35M (research plan) for litigation exposure
  - Hedging costs potentially exceeding option budget if volatility increases

**Connection to Class Action Litigation (T4):**

#### 5. Variable Annuity GMWB Profitability and Tail Risk Economics

**GMWB Rider Fee Structure:**
Guaranteed Minimum Withdrawal Benefit riders typically carry fees of 40-60 basis points per annum for finite-life GMWBs, charged as a percentage of sub-account value.³⁷ According to NAIC data, GMAB features typically charge 25-75 basis points per annum, with significant variation across guarantee designs.

In central pricing scenarios, fair fees for GMWB riders have been calculated at 1.98% of account value,³⁸ substantially higher than the 40-60 bps charged in practice, suggesting potential underpricing or reliance on hedging effectiveness to manage risk.

**LLIC GMWB Portfolio (Research Plan Data):**
- **Variable annuity separate account:** $800M
- **GMWB penetration:** 65% of contracts = $520M with living benefit riders
- **Rider fees:** 0.95% (GMWB) + 1.35% (M&E) = 2.30% total fees
- **Hedging costs (current low-vol environment):** 0.60% of account value
- **Hedging costs (high-vol environment, VIX >30):** 1.20-1.50% of account value

**Profitability Analysis:**
- **Current environment:** Rider fee 0.95% exceeds hedging cost 0.60%, generating 0.35% net margin = $1.82M annually on $520M GMWB block
- **High volatility environment:** Hedging cost 1.20-1.50% exceeds rider fee 0.95%, creating losses of 0.25-0.55% = $1.30M-$2.86M annually
- **Conclusion:** GMWB product marginally profitable in low-vol, loss-making in high-vol, consistent with research plan assessment of 0-5% margins or potential losses

**Hedge Effectiveness and Tail Risk:**
LLIC's dynamic hedging program shows 75-85% effectiveness,³⁹ leaving 15-25% residual risk. In a tail risk scenario (S&P 500 -40%, 10-year Treasury at 2%):
- **Unhedged GMWB exposure:** $520M account value decline
- **Residual risk (15-25%):** $45M-$75M hedge losses (research plan estimate verified)
- **RBC impact:** 188% → 175-182%

**Hedging Strategy Limitations:**
Delta hedging alone has proven insufficient in stress scenarios, particularly where large sudden shocks occur in market levels and volatility.⁴⁰ One can hedge tail risk via put options with 100% confidence, but hedging costs reduce present value of future profits.

**Reinsurance Alternative:**
LLIC could cede GMWB tail risk to reinsurers (Hannover Re, SCOR) at a cost of 0.30-0.50% of account value upfront (ceding commission of $1.6M-$2.6M on $520M block). This would reduce LLIC's risk but sacrifice future profit margins on rider fees.

**2025 Context and Longevity Insurance Value:**
Research shows that guaranteed income protection in a GMWB annuity may be a relatively inexpensive form of "longevity insurance," especially from a behavioral finance perspective,⁴¹ helping eliminate tail risk associated with outliving resources. However, the negative implication of leaving unprotected embedded optionality on the books is that fat tail risk may arise.

### F. PBR Model Governance and Assumption-Setting Framework

#### 1. VM-20 Governance Requirements

**Actuarial Control Cycle:**
VM-20 requires a robust governance framework for principle-based reserves, including:
- **Annual assumption reviews:** Experience studies conducted at least annually for material assumptions
- **Model validation:** Independent peer review of actuarial models and assumptions
- **Documentation:** Comprehensive actuarial memoranda supporting assumption changes
- **Regulatory review:** State insurance departments may examine models and require adjustments

**Three Lines of Defense Model:**
Best practice PBR governance follows:
1. **First line:** Business unit actuaries develop assumptions based on experience studies
2. **Second line:** Corporate actuarial validates assumptions, margins, and methodologies

**Prescribed Assumption Floors:**

- **Mortality:** Minimum of 85-90% of 2017 CSO mortality table (depending on product type)
