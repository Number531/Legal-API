## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview and Critical Findings

Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) maintains a $2.54 billion reinsurance program representing 21% of total policy reserves ($12B) and 137% of statutory surplus ($1.85B), within industry norms for life insurers (10-25% cession ratios) but exhibiting material concentration and counterparty risks that require immediate attention in the context of American Financial Holdings LLC's $2.9B acquisition. This research identifies **critical tail risk scenarios with 10-12% combined probability** where simultaneous reinsurance disruptions could reduce LLIC's Risk-Based Capital (RBC) ratio from 188% to 120-125%, approaching the 100% Authorized Control Level where Nebraska Department of Insurance may seek regulatory seizure. The probability-weighted expected reinsurance-related capital requirement is **$170M (5.9% of acquisition price)**, driven primarily by Liberty Re VT captive structure risk and Global Re recapture exposure.

### Reinsurance Program Structure and Concentration Risk

LLIC's reinsurance program comprises four major counterparties across three structural categories:

**1. Captive Reinsurance ($850M - 33% of Program):**
Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC, a Vermont special purpose financial captive, holds $850M of AXXX/XXX term life reserves with only $120M in assets (14%) and relies on a $730M parental guarantee (86%) from Liberty Life Holdings, representing 2.6√ó the parent's $280M net worth. This structure is under Nebraska DOI scrutiny for AG48 (Actuarial Guideline 48) compliance, with **10-15% probability** (per T2 specialist findings) that post-acquisition review disallows reserve credit, resulting in catastrophic $730M surplus reduction and RBC decline from 188% to **114% (Regulatory Action Level)**. The acquisition triggers novation requirements transferring the parental guarantee from Liberty Life Holdings to American Financial Holdings, requiring pre-approval from both Vermont Department of Financial Regulation and Nebraska DOI.

**2. Unauthorized Offshore Reinsurance ($850M - 33% of Program):**
Global Reassurance Ltd. (Bermuda, A- rated by A.M. Best) provides 90% quota share reinsurance on $8.5B face amount of term life new issues (2020-2024), ceding $850M reserves with 85% ceding commission and 50% profit sharing. As an unauthorized reinsurer under Nebraska law (Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-416.06), Global Re posts 100% collateral via $850M irrevocable letter of credit from Barclays Bank (A+ rated Fitch). Change of control provisions in the treaty grant Global Re recapture rights if the reinsurer reasonably objects to the acquirer's financial strength or if LLIC's A.M. Best rating falls below A-. Estimated recapture probability: **15-25%**, driven by Global Re's A- rating (one full category below industry leaders), unauthorized status sensitivity to ceding company changes, and Bermuda domicile regulatory arbitrage concerns. If recaptured, LLIC retains $850M term reserves with modest RBC impact (+$20M-$30M C1/C2 risk charges) but loses future ceding commission benefits (+$40M-$60M annual DAC costs on new business).

**3. Authorized Reinsurance ($840M - 34% of Program):**
Swiss Reinsurance Company Ltd. Munich Reinsurance Company (A+ FSR, aa Long-Term ICR) provides yearly renewable term (YRT) excess coverage on group life insurance with $250K retention and $5M maximum per life, ceding approximately $200M reserves. Both reinsurers are authorized (licensed) in the U.S., require no collateral, and exhibit **<5% recapture probability** given superior credit ratings, stable historical relationships, and limited sensitivity to ownership changes in modco/YRT excess structures that minimize reinsurer capital exposure.

**Concentration Risk Violation:**
The top two reinsurers (Liberty Re VT + Global Re) account for $1.7B ceded reserves = **92% of LLIC's $1.85B statutory surplus**, dramatically exceeding NAIC Model 785 concentration thresholds: (1) >20% of gross written premium ceded to single reinsurer requires 30-day regulatory notification, and (2) >50% of surplus recoverable from single reinsurer triggers enhanced regulatory scrutiny. LLIC's 92% concentration requires immediate filing of concentration risk notifications with Nebraska DOI and development of a 3-year diversification plan to reduce top-two concentration to <70% of surplus through addition of 2-3 A+ rated reinsurers (RGA, Hannover Re, SCOR) and gradual shift of new business from Global Re to authorized alternatives.

### Change of Control Risk Assessment and Recapture Scenarios

Standard life reinsurance treaties incorporate change of control provisions granting reinsurers protective rights when ceding companies undergo ownership changes exceeding 50% voting equity. Key provisions include: (1) **advance notice requirements** (60-90 days pre-closing), (2) **novation prerequisites** (tri-party agreement required; no automatic assignment), (3) **recapture rights** (reinsurer may terminate treaty if acquirer's creditworthiness objectionable), and (4) **credit rating triggers** (downgrade below A- may permit additional collateral demands or recapture). Industry precedent from Protective Life's 2015 acquisition by Dai-ichi Life demonstrates that reinsurers rarely exercise recapture rights when acquirers maintain strong credit ratings (A or better) and commit to continuity of underwriting standards, though offshore unauthorized reinsurers exhibit heightened sensitivity.

**Scenario 1: Base Case - No Recapture (70-75% Probability)**
American Financial Holdings maintains A rating; all reinsurers receive satisfactory change of control documentation; Liberty Re VT captive accepted by Nebraska DOI with modest collateral enhancement ($100M-$150M additional LOC); Swiss Re and Munich Re execute routine novation agreements. **Financial Impact:** $750K-$1.5M annual LOC costs; no material RBC impact; RBC ratio remains 188%.

**Scenario 2: Global Re Partial Recapture (15-20% Probability)**
Global Re exercises recapture on 50% of quota share ($425M reserves) due to A- rating sensitivity to ceding company risk or negotiation failure on repricing. Swiss Re, Munich Re, and Liberty Re VT continue unchanged. **Financial Impact:** $425M reserves retained (no net reserve change), +$10M-$15M TAC requirement from increased C1/C2 RBC charges, +$20M-$30M annual DAC financing costs from loss of ceding commission; RBC ratio declines modestly 188% ‚Üí 185-186%.

**Scenario 3: Liberty Re VT + Global Re Recapture (10-12% Probability - CRITICAL)**
Nebraska DOI disallows Liberty Re VT reserve credit (10-15% probability per T2) following AG48 review, and simultaneously Global Re exercises recapture perceiving LLIC financial distress from captive disruption. Swiss Re and Munich Re continue. **Financial Impact:** $730M surplus reduction from Liberty Re VT parental guarantee disallowance + $425M partial Global Re recapture ‚Üí +$12M TAC requirement ‚Üí **Combined RBC impact 188% ‚Üí 120-125%** (approaching 100% Authorized Control Level). **Regulatory Consequences:** Nebraska DOI imposes business plan restrictions, dividend prohibitions, enhanced supervision, and capital raise requirements ($500M-$700M needed to restore 200% Company Action Level). **Deal Impact:** Potentially deal-blocking without pre-arranged capital backstop; requires SPA closing condition addressing reinsurance novation completion or substitute collateral.

**Scenario 4: Catastrophic Multi-Reinsurer Recapture (<3% Probability)**
Liberty Re VT disallowed + Global Re full recapture + Swiss Re modco termination triggered by material adverse change (e.g., LLIC A.M. Best rating downgraded to BBB or below due to cascading effects). **Financial Impact:** $730M + $850M + $50M (Swiss Re RBC) = $830M surplus reduction ‚Üí RBC ratio 188% ‚Üí **95-100% (Authorized Control Level, regulatory seizure risk)**. **Deal Impact:** Deal-blocking; requires $800M-$1B emergency capital commitment from American Financial Holdings as condition precedent to closing.

**Weighted Average Recapture Probability: 8-12%**
Calculated as: (70% √ó 0%) + (18% √ó 25%) + (10% √ó 50%) + (2% √ó 100%) = 11.5% expected recapture rate across program. This indicates modest overall recapture risk in base case but **material tail risk (10-12% probability) requiring $500M-$800M contingent capital** availability.

### Regulatory Approval Requirements and Timeline

**Nebraska Insurance Code (Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-416.06) - Credit for Reinsurance:**
Allows domestic ceding insurers reserve credit only when reinsurers meet authorization/accreditation/certification requirements or post 100% collateral for unauthorized reinsurers. Material reinsurance treaty changes (novations, collateral reductions, recapture provisions) require Nebraska DOI notification within 30 days. Post-acquisition timeline:
- **Day 1-30:** Notify Nebraska DOI of ownership change
- **Day 30-60:** File updated reinsurance concentration notifications under NAIC Model 785
- **Day 60-90:** Submit treaty novation requests for Global Re, Swiss Re, Munich Re
- **Day 90-120:** Nebraska DOI reviews novations, requests collateral adequacy documentation
- **Day 120-180:** DOI approval of novation agreements and reserve credit continuation
**Estimated Timeline to Full Regulatory Approval: 120-180 days post-closing**

**NAIC Form F - Enterprise Risk Report:**
Required under NAIC Insurance Holding Company System Model Act (#440), filed with lead state commissioner (Nebraska for LLIC). Ultimate controlling person (Liberty Life Holdings ‚Üí American Financial Holdings post-acquisition) must file updated Form F within 15 days of ownership change, disclosing: (1) corporate structure chart, (2) parental guarantees (Liberty Re VT $730M), (3) material activities posing enterprise risk, (4) reinsurance pooling agreements, and (5) business plan for next 12 months. Nebraska DOI Financial Analysis Division reviews Form F for enterprise risks; **30-40% probability** DOI requests additional information or risk mitigation plan given 92% surplus concentration. Failure to address DOI concerns may delay RBC Plan approval (critical for $150M capital injection per T1) or impose dividend/new business restrictions.

**Assumption Reinsurance vs. Indemnity Reinsurance:**
All LLIC treaties are indemnity reinsurance (LLIC remains liable to policyholders; reinsurance is contractual between LLIC and reinsurers). **No assumption reinsurance approvals required** (which would require policyholder notices and regulatory approvals in both ceding and assuming states). **No policyholder notifications required** for treaty novations. Regulatory approvals limited to DOI notifications, Form F updates, and concentration risk filings.

**State Guaranty Association Assessments:**
LLIC pays Nebraska Life and Health Insurance Guaranty Association assessments based on direct written premiums ($2.1B), not net premiums (after reinsurance). Assessment cap: 2% of direct premiums annually = $42M maximum. **Only direct insurance covered** (reinsurance not protected by guaranty funds). Industry practice: Reinsurers voluntarily reimburse ceding companies for proportional share of assessments on reinsured policies. LLIC historical assessment exposure: $2.8M (2018), $1.5M (2021), $950K (2023); ongoing exposure $1.5M-$2M annually. Estimated reinsurer reimbursement: $600K-$800K annually (40% of assessments apply to reinsured policies), leaving net LLIC cost $900K-$1.2M annually.

### RBC Impact and Reserve Credit Analysis

**C1 Asset Risk Charge for Reinsurance Recoverables:**
NAIC Life RBC formula imposes 0.5% C1 asset risk charge on net reinsurance recoverables ($1.9B excluding Swiss Re modco funds withheld), reflecting credit risk comparable to NAIC-1 or NAIC-2 rated bonds (investment grade). LLIC's current C1 charge: **$9.5M** (0.51% of $1.85B TAC). This is materially lower than LLIC's below-investment-grade bond exposure ($1.02B with 4-30% C1 charges depending on NAIC designation) due to reinsurance collateralization ($1.58B LOC/guarantees posted).

**Credit Downgrade Sensitivity:**
If Global Re downgraded A- ‚Üí BBB (two notches), C1 charge may increase from 0.5% to 2.0-3.0% (comparable to NAIC-4 bonds), adding $17M incremental RBC charge; RBC ratio 188% ‚Üí 186% (modest decline). Greater concern: Nebraska DOI may require additional collateral (10-20% cushion = $85M-$170M LOC). If Swiss Re or Munich Re lose authorized status (unlikely given A+ ratings), collateral posting requirements: Swiss Re $64M (10% of $640M modco, though funds withheld mitigates), Munich Re $20M-$40M (10-20% of $200M YRT excess if reclassified as Secure-2 certified reinsurer).

**Provision for Reinsurance (PFR) Adequacy:**
Statutory accounting requires provision for reinsurance (liability offsetting recoverable asset) reflecting uncollectible risk, typically 0-5% of recoverables by credit quality. LLIC's recommended PFR: Swiss Re/Munich Re 0% (A+ Superior rated authorized), Global Re 0.5-1.0% ($4.25M-$8.5M, minimal risk given LOC), Liberty Re VT 2-5% ($17M-$42.5M, elevated risk from parental guarantee structure) ‚Üí **Total PFR $21M-$51M (1.1-2.7% of $1.9B recoverables)**. If LLIC carries no PFR, overstates admitted assets by $21M-$51M; if carries $25M-$30M PFR, adequate for current program. Nebraska DOI examiners review PFR adequacy during financial examinations; may require strengthening if insufficient.

**Reserve Credit and Unauthorized Reinsurance Compliance:**
Nebraska law (Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-416.06) requires 100% collateral for unauthorized reinsurers to obtain reserve credit. LLIC compliance status: Global Re 100% compliant ($850M LOC = 100% of $850M ceded reserves); Liberty Re VT under review ($120M assets + $730M parental guarantee = 100% of $850M ceded reserves, but Nebraska DOI may challenge parental guarantee adequacy under AG48 retroactive application). **Collateral gap:** None for Global Re; potential $300M-$500M gap for Liberty Re VT if parental guarantee partially disallowed (requiring supplemental LOC).

### C. Change of Control Provisions and Recapture Risk Analysis

#### 1. Standard Life Reinsurance Treaty Change of Control Language

Based on industry-standard treaty forms (LLIS reinsurance agreements, Society of Actuaries model provisions), typical change of control clauses include:¬≤‚Å¥

**A. Definition of "Change of Control"**
- Direct or indirect acquisition of >50% voting equity interest in ceding company
- Merger, consolidation, or sale of substantially all assets
- Change in controlling shareholder or parent company
- Regulatory seizure or rehabilitation order

**B. Notice Requirements**
- **Advance notice:** 60-90 days prior to anticipated closing (pre-closing notification)
- **Post-closing confirmation:** 30 days after closing with updated corporate structure chart
- **Material information:** Identity of acquirer, financial statements, A.M. Best rating, business plan

**C. Reinsurer Rights Upon Change of Control**

| Right | Standard Provision | Impact on LLIC Acquisition |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------------|
| **Recapture** | May recapture all or part of reinsurance if reinsurer reasonably objects to acquirer's financial strength | Global Re (15-25% risk), Liberty Re VT (10-15% risk per T2) |
| **Novation Requirement** | Treaty does not automatically assign; new tri-party agreement required | LLIC + reinsurer + American Financial Holdings must execute novation |
| **Credit Rating Trigger** | If acquirer rated <A- (A.M. Best), reinsurer may demand additional collateral or recapture | American Financial Holdings credit rating verification required |
| **Consent to Assignment** | Explicit written consent required from reinsurer to assign treaty to acquirer | Must obtain from all reinsurers (Swiss Re, Munich Re, Global Re) |
| **Renegotiation** | Reinsurer may propose modified terms (pricing, collateral, experience sharing) as condition of continuation | Potential cost increase 5-15% on YRT/quota share rates |

**D. Recapture Mechanics and Timeline**

**Standard Recapture Process:**¬≤‚Åµ
1. **Triggering Event:** Change of control notice received by reinsurer
2. **Evaluation Period:** 60-90 days for reinsurer to assess acquirer creditworthiness
3. **Recapture Notice:** Reinsurer provides 180 days advance notice of intent to recapture
4. **Recapture Effective Date:** Typically coincides with policy anniversary dates or treaty anniversary
5. **Reserve Transfer:** Ceded reserves return to LLIC balance sheet; reinsurer refunds reserve credit
6. **Experience Settlement:** Final accounting of premiums, claims, ceding commissions, profit sharing

**Recapture Requirements (Cedent Rights):**¬≤‚Å∂
Recapture in life reinsurance means only the cedent can take back from the reinsurer all like risks covered by the reinsured contract after a specified length of time has elapsed. Recapture must be voluntary on the part of the cedent; otherwise reinsurance treatment will not be allowed in the cedent's financial statements.

**However:** In change of control scenarios, reinsurance treaties typically grant the **reinsurer** (not cedent) a recapture right as a protective measure against deterioration in ceding company creditworthiness or underwriting quality.

#### 4. Reinsurer Insolvency Precedent and Risk

**Historical Reinsurer Insolvencies:**‚Åµ‚Å¥

**A. Mutual Benefit Life (1991)**
- New Jersey-domiciled life insurer (not pure reinsurer but assumed significant reinsurance)

- Largest insurance insolvency at the time

**C. Recent Life Reinsurer Stability (2020-2025)**

**Cut-Through Endorsements:**‚Å∂¬π

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **LLIC's reinsurance program totaling $2.54B ceded reserves (21% of total reserves) is within industry norms but exhibits elevated concentration risk** with 92% of statutory surplus concentrated in two counterparties (Liberty Re VT and Global Re $1.7B combined). This concentration exceeds NAIC Model 785 thresholds and requires Nebraska DOI notifications and ongoing regulatory oversight.

2. **Reinsurance counterparty credit quality is bifurcated**: Swiss Re and Munich Re (both A+ rated, authorized) represent low-risk, stable relationships unlikely to be disrupted by the acquisition (<5% recapture probability), while Global Re (A- rated, unauthorized) and Liberty Re VT (captive) present moderate to high recapture risk (10-25% probability range) requiring proactive mitigation.

3. **Change of control provisions in standard life reinsurance treaties grant reinsurers significant protective rights** including recapture, novation requirements, and credit rating triggers. While Swiss Re and Munich Re unlikely to exercise these rights given American Financial Holdings' strong credit profile, Global Re and Liberty Re VT face heightened scrutiny from both reinsurers and Nebraska DOI.

4. **The Liberty Re VT captive structure ($850M AXXX/XXX reserves, $730M parental guarantee) represents the single largest reinsurance-related risk** with 10-15% probability (per T2) that Nebraska DOI disallows reserve credit post-acquisition, resulting in catastrophic $730M surplus reduction and RBC decline to 114% (Regulatory Action Level). This tail risk scenario is potentially deal-blocking without pre-arranged $600M-$800M capital backstop.

5. **Simultaneous adverse developments across multiple reinsurance relationships (10-12% probability) could reduce LLIC's RBC ratio from 188% to 120-125%**, approaching the 100% Authorized Control Level where Nebraska DOI may seek regulatory seizure. The combined scenario (Liberty Re VT disallowance + Global Re recapture) requires $500M-$700M emergency capital injection, representing material acquisition risk.

6. **Regulatory approval requirements are manageable but time-intensive**: Treaty novations for Global Re, Swiss Re, and Munich Re require reinsurer consent and Nebraska DOI notification (120-180 day timeline). Form F Enterprise Risk Report updates will highlight concentration risk and may trigger additional DOI information requests. NAIC concentration notifications (>20% GWP, >50% surplus) likely already on file but will require updating post-acquisition.

7. **Collateral adequacy is generally sufficient ($1.58B posted for $2.54B ceded reserves) but vulnerable to credit rating downgrades**: If Global Re downgraded from A- to BBB, Nebraska DOI may require additional $85M-$170M collateral. If Swiss Re or Munich Re lose authorized status (unlikely), $64M-$84M collateral required. Barclays LOC for Global Re ($850M) must be renewed annually; 5-10% risk of non-renewal if Global Re creditworthiness deteriorates.

8. **Reinsurance program optimization opportunities exist ($5M-$15M annual savings potential) through repricing, consolidation, and efficiency improvements**, but execution requires 12-18 months and carries implementation risk. Alternative risk transfer (mortality bonds, longevity swaps) remains cost-prohibitive relative to traditional reinsurance given current capital markets pricing.

9. **RBC impact of reinsurance credit risk is modest ($9.5M C1 asset risk charge) under base case** but sensitive to credit rating downgrades. If multiple reinsurers downgraded simultaneously, C1 charges could increase to $45M and collateral requirements to $110M, creating liquidity pressure. Provision for Reinsurance (PFR) of $25M-$30M appears adequate for current reinsurance credit quality but should be reviewed by Nebraska DOI examiners.

10. **The probability-weighted expected reinsurance-related capital requirement is $170M (5.9% of $2.9B acquisition price)**, driven primarily by tail risk scenarios involving Liberty Re VT and Global Re recapture. This quantified exposure should inform purchase price adjustments, escrow structuring, and representations and warranties insurance coverage.
