## IV.L. PRODUCT PROFITABILITY & PRINCIPLE-BASED RESERVES

### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. NAIC Valuation Manual VM-20 Principle-Based Reserving Requirements

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) adopted the Valuation Manual establishing principle-based reserving (PBR) for life insurance products, effective January 1, 2017.¹ VM-20 replaced the formulaic Commissioners Reserve Valuation Method (CRVM) and Actuarial Guidelines AXXX/XXX with a principles-based framework requiring insurers to calculate reserves using company-specific assumptions validated through experience studies.²

**VM-20 Three-Component Reserve Methodology:**

VM-20 requires calculation of three reserve components, with the minimum reserve being the greatest of the three:³

1. **Net Premium Reserve (NPR):** Mandatory for all policies subject to VM-20. Calculated using prescribed mortality tables (2017 Commissioners Standard Ordinary, or 2017 CSO) and conservative interest rate assumptions.⁴

2. **Deterministic Reserve (DR):** Calculated using prescribed adverse scenarios (equity returns, interest rates, lapse rates). For term life insurance and universal life with secondary guarantees (ULSG) subject to Actuarial Guideline 38, Deterministic Reserves must be calculated, making the deterministic exclusion test inapplicable.⁵

3. **Stochastic Reserve (SR):** Calculated using a minimum of 10,000 economic scenarios via approved stochastic modeling software. The Stochastic Reserve represents Conditional Tail Expectation at the 70th percentile (CTE70), meaning the average of the worst 30% of projected scenarios.⁶

**Product Applicability:**

VM-20 applies to all life insurance products issued on or after January 1, 2017, in states that have adopted the Standard Valuation Law Model #820 and Standard Nonforfeiture Law Model #808.⁷ All NAIC-accredited states implemented PBR by January 1, 2020, the effective date of the NAIC accreditation standard.⁸ However, products issued prior to 2017 may continue to be reserved using legacy formula-based methods (CRVM for whole life, Actuarial Guideline 38 for ULSG, Regulation XXX for term life, and Actuarial Guideline AXXX for universal life with secondary guarantees) unless the insurer voluntarily converts the legacy block to VM-20 reserves.⁹

**Model Governance and Regulatory Oversight:**

VM-31 of the NAIC Valuation Manual prescribes requirements for the PBR Actuarial Report, which must be submitted annually to the domiciliary state insurance commissioner.¹⁰ The report must document:
- Modeling methodology and assumptions
- Experience studies supporting company-specific assumptions
- Margins for adverse deviation (prudent estimate vs. best estimate)
- Independent peer review and model validation
- Governance framework (typically "three lines of defense": business actuaries, corporate validation, internal audit)¹¹

Nebraska Department of Insurance, as LLIC's domiciliary regulator, has authority under Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-407.24 to reject VM-20 assumptions deemed insufficiently conservative or inadequately supported by credible experience data.¹²

#### 2. Asset Adequacy Analysis Requirements (VM-30 and AG 55)

**VM-30 Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum (AOAM):**

All life insurers must annually file an Actuarial Opinion certifying that reserves make "good and sufficient provision" for all unmatured obligations under issued policies.¹³ The appointed actuary must perform asset adequacy analysis (AAA) under moderately adverse conditions, testing whether assets supporting reserves are sufficient to meet liabilities under stress scenarios including:
- Interest rate increases/decreases of ±200 basis points
- Equity market declines of 30-50%
- Mortality deterioration of 5-15% above pricing assumptions
- Lapse rate increases or decreases of 20-40% from baseline¹⁴

If the analysis reveals deficiencies, the appointed actuary must issue a qualified or adverse opinion, triggering regulatory examination and potential mandatory reserve strengthening.¹⁵

**AG 55 Reinsurance Asset Adequacy Testing:**

Effective for year-end 2025 annual statements, the NAIC adopted Actuarial Guideline 55 requiring insurers to perform asset adequacy testing on assets securing ceded reinsurance reserves.¹⁶ For treaties utilizing letters of credit (LOC), modified coinsurance trusts, or other non-cash collateral, the insurer must verify that collateral would remain adequate under the same moderately adverse scenarios applied to direct reserves.¹⁷ AG 55 compliance requires expanded actuarial opinion documentation demonstrating reinsurance asset adequacy across all material treaties.¹⁸

#### 3. Product Profitability Standards and Return on Capital Benchmarks

**Society of Actuaries Industry Benchmarks:**

The Society of Actuaries 2021 Universal Life, Indexed Universal Life, and Variable Universal Life Survey provides industry profitability targets:¹⁹
- **Term Life Insurance:** 18-22% return on economic capital (ROE) after breakeven (typically years 7-10)
- **Whole Life Insurance:** 15-20% ROE with stable dividends supporting retention
- **Universal Life / Indexed Universal Life:** 10-14% ROE target, with indexed products requiring additional 100-200 basis points to compensate for hedging cost volatility
- **Variable Annuities with GMWB:** Marginally profitable to breakeven after hedging costs; rider fees 75-125 basis points typically insufficient in high-volatility environments²⁰

**Statutory Profitability Measurement:**

Statutory accounting principles measure profitability through statutory operating gain by line of business, reported in NAIC Annual Statement Exhibit 5 (Analysis of Operations by Lines of Business).²¹ Profitability metrics include:
- Underwriting gain/loss (premiums less benefits, commissions, and expenses)
- Investment income allocated to reserves
- Surplus strain (difference between initial reserves and first-year cash flows)
- Return on statutory capital (operating gain divided by required capital)²²

Unlike GAAP profitability (which capitalizes acquisition costs as DAC and amortizes over policy life), statutory accounting expenses all acquisition costs immediately, creating significant first-year losses followed by profitable renewal years.²³

#### B.2 Reserve Adequacy Testing Reveals Potential Strengthening Requirements

**Conclusion**: LLIC's actuarial assumptions for mortality (85% of 2017 CSO), lapse rates (company-specific), and per-policy expenses ($85 per policy) appear **somewhat aggressive** compared to peer company benchmarks.⁴⁶ Asset adequacy testing under moderately adverse scenarios may reveal deficiencies requiring $150M-$350M reserve strengthening, with aggregate exposure of $695M if mortality, lapse, and expense assumptions are all adjusted upward to peer norms.⁴⁷ **Severity: MEDIUM**. **Probability:** 10-20% (moderately adverse scenario). **Exposure:** $150M-$695M. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: peer company assumption benchmarking from publicly available NAIC Annual Statement data].

**Rule**: Under VM-30 of the NAIC Valuation Manual, appointed actuaries must annually certify that reserves make adequate provision for all policy obligations under moderately adverse conditions.⁴⁸ *See* NAIC Model Regulation § 822 (Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum Regulation) ("[T]he appointed actuary shall... render an opinion relating to... reserves... when considered in light of the assets held by the company... including... the risks inherent in the business and the environment in which the company operates").⁴⁹ Actuarial Standard of Practice No. 22 (Statements of Opinion Based on Asset Adequacy Analysis) requires testing under scenarios that are "moderately adverse" but not "catastrophic," typically defined as the 60th-80th percentile of adverse outcomes.⁵⁰

**Explanation**: Industry practice for setting prudent estimate assumptions balances three factors: (1) company-specific experience from credible data, (2) industry benchmarks for similar products, and (3) margins for adverse deviation to account for parameter uncertainty and emerging risks.⁵¹ Regulators scrutinize assumptions that fall outside the range of peer companies, particularly when assumptions are more optimistic than industry medians.⁵²

In *Milliman Assumption-Setting Best Practices for Principle-Based Reserves* (2023), actuarial consultants analyzed assumption distributions across 62 life insurers' VM-20 filings and found:⁵³

- **Lapse assumptions:** Highly product-specific, but systematic under-estimation of persistency on guaranteed products (ULSG, term with conversion options) has caused industry-wide reserve strengthening of $50B+ since 2015⁵⁴
- **Expense assumptions:** $95-$105 per policy median, with lower assumptions (below $90) requiring detailed expense allocation studies demonstrating economies of scale⁵⁵

The Nebraska Department of Insurance, in its 2024 Market Conduct Examination of LLIC, specifically noted concerns about sales illustration assumptions and policy administration practices, creating elevated scrutiny risk for actuarial assumption validation during the pending RBC Plan review process.⁵⁶

  - IUL shortfall: ($170M target - $110M actual) × perpetuity at 8% = $60M / 0.08 = **$750M NPV value loss**
  - VA GMWB shortfall: ($41.6M target on $520M - $0.3M actual) × perpetuity = **$516M NPV value loss**
  - **Combined:** $1.27B enterprise value at risk if profitability gaps not addressed
- **Result:** **$85M-$150M annual lost profits** requiring strategic remediation
- **Discount Rate Basis:** 8% WACC

**Probability Assessment:**
80% probability that IUL and VA profitability remain below target without active management intervention [METHODOLOGY: Industry data shows cap rate compression and hedging cost volatility are market-driven (not LLIC-specific), requiring product redesign and risk transfer to restore margins; probability based on 90% success rate of product repricing initiatives in industry precedents].⁹⁵

**Counter-Analysis**: LLIC may argue that below-target profitability on IUL and VA products is temporary and market-driven rather than structural:

1. **Market Cycle Argument:** IUL cap rate compression is driven by low implied volatility in equity option markets (VIX averaging 15-18 in 2022-2024), which increases option prices and reduces available cap rates.⁹⁶ If equity volatility normalizes to historical averages (VIX 20-25), option costs decline and cap rates can be restored to 11-12% without product redesign.⁹⁷ However, this assumes mean reversion within 2-3 years, which is uncertain given structural changes in option markets (algorithmic trading, central clearing, Basel III capital requirements for option writers).⁹⁸

2. **VA Fee Increase Opportunity:** Variable annuity rider fees are contractually adjustable on new business, allowing LLIC to immediately increase GMWB fees from 0.95% to 1.10-1.20% to cover high-volatility hedging scenarios.⁹⁹ Industry precedent shows 70-80% of customers accept higher fees when presented as necessary to maintain guarantee features.¹⁰⁰ This would restore VA profitability to 3-5% ROE target without requiring divestiture or tail risk reinsurance.¹⁰¹

3. **Competitive Positioning:** Below-target profitability may be strategic choice to maintain market share and agent productivity during acquisition transition.¹⁰² Post-acquisition, LLIC can reprice products upward (increasing premiums 5-8% on term life, reducing IUL crediting by 50-75 basis points, increasing VA fees) to restore profitability at the cost of 10-15% sales volume reduction.¹⁰³ This trade-off (higher margins, lower volume) may be economically superior to maintaining current pricing.¹⁰⁴

The most likely outcome is **selective repricing and risk transfer**: IUL product redesign with cap floors, VA GMWB tail risk reinsurance, and whole life dividend restoration, achieving 11-13% blended ROE within 18-24 months post-acquisition.¹⁰⁵
