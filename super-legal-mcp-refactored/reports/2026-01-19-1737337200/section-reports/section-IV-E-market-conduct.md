## IV.E. MARKET CONDUCT & REGULATORY COMPLIANCE

### A. Legal Framework

#### A.1 State Market Conduct Examination Authority

State insurance regulators possess comprehensive statutory authority to examine the business and affairs of licensed insurers to ensure compliance with insurance laws and protect policyholders. In Nebraska, the **Insurers Examination Act**, Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-5901 *et seq.*, grants the Director of Insurance broad authority to conduct financial and market conduct examinations of all licensed insurers.1 [VERIFIED: https://nebraskalegislature.gov/laws/statutes.php?statute=44-5901]

The statute authorizes the Director to examine an insurer "as often as the director deems appropriate, but at least once every five years."2 Market conduct examinations differ from financial examinations in scope and focus: while financial examinations assess capital adequacy, reserve sufficiency, and solvency under the Risk-Based Capital (RBC) framework, market conduct examinations evaluate consumer-facing operational practices including claims handling, sales practices, policy servicing, agent licensing compliance, and underwriting fairness.3 [VERIFIED: NAIC Market Regulation Handbook 2024]

Nebraska's examination procedures follow the **NAIC Market Regulation Handbook**, which provides standardized examination protocols, sampling methodologies, and violation classification frameworks.4 The Handbook establishes review categories including:

- **Claims Settlement Practices** (unfair denial, delay, or underpayment)
- **Marketing & Sales** (misrepresentation, twisting, churning, illustration compliance)
- **Underwriting & Rating** (unfair discrimination, proper rate filing usage)
- **Policy Administration** (lapse procedures, reinstatement rights, premium refunds)
- **Producer Licensing** (appointment compliance, continuing education verification)
- **Consumer Complaints** (complaint ratios, response timeliness, resolution adequacy)

The examination process typically follows a multi-phase structure: (1) pre-examination planning and MCAS (Market Conduct Annual Statement) data review, (2) on-site examination with file sampling and interviews, (3) draft report preparation and insurer response opportunity, (4) final report issuance with violation citations and corrective action requirements, and (5) follow-up monitoring of remediation compliance.5 [METHODOLOGY: NAIC examination procedures based on Market Regulation Handbook Chapter 3]

#### A.2 Enforcement Authority and Penalty Framework

Upon finding violations, the Director may impose administrative penalties, require corrective action plans, and condition future licensing or transaction approvals on compliance certification. Nebraska's **Unfair Insurance Practices Act**, Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-1540, prohibits specific unfair claim settlement practices including:

- Failing to acknowledge and act reasonably promptly upon communications with respect to claims
- Failing to adopt and implement reasonable standards for the prompt investigation of claims
- Not attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt, fair, and equitable settlements of claims
- Compelling insureds to institute litigation to recover amounts due by offering substantially less than amounts ultimately recovered

Violations may result in penalties under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-1542, which establishes a tiered penalty structure: (a) up to $1,000 per violation for general violations, (b) up to $10,000 per violation if the Director finds the insurer knowingly committed the violation, and (c) up to $150,000 in aggregate if the Director finds violations were committed "flagrantly and in conscious disregard" of the law or as a "general business practice."6 [VERIFIED: Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-1542(2)]

The statute's "general business practice" language is critical: isolated violations may result in nominal per-violation penalties, while systematic or repeated violations across multiple operational areas trigger aggregate penalty caps and heightened regulatory scrutiny regarding management competence and corporate governance adequacy.

#### A.3 Policy Lapse and Reinstatement Requirements

Nebraska law requires insurers to provide policyholders with adequate notice and opportunity to cure premium defaults before lapsing life insurance policies. Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-502 mandates:

> "Every life insurance policy shall have a grace period of either thirty or thirty-one days within which the payment of any premium after the first may be made, during which period of grace the policy shall continue in force."7 [VERIFIED: Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-502]

While Nebraska statutory law does not expressly mandate pre-lapse notice (distinguishing Nebraska from states like California that require 30-day advance notice), the NAIC Model Regulation on Life Insurance Policy Illustrations (Model #582) and market conduct examination standards impose best-practice expectations for pre-lapse notification.8 Modern industry practice typically includes 15-30 day advance notice before lapse, providing policyholders reasonable opportunity to remit overdue premiums during the grace period.

Reinstatement rights arise from policy contract provisions, typically requiring: (1) written application for reinstatement, (2) evidence of insurability satisfactory to the insurer, (3) payment of all overdue premiums with interest, and (4) repayment or reinstatement of any policy loans.9 Improper denial of reinstatement requests when contractual conditions are met may constitute breach of contract and unfair claim settlement practices.

#### A.4 Agent Licensing and Continuing Education Requirements

Nebraska regulates insurance producers through the **Insurance Producers Licensing Act**, Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-4001 *et seq.* Producers must satisfy initial licensing requirements (pre-licensing education, examination, background check) and maintain licenses through biennial renewal with continuing education (CE) compliance.10 [VERIFIED: Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-4054, 44-4059]

Continuing education requirements mandate **24 credit hours every two-year renewal period**, including **3 hours of ethics** training.11 Additionally, specialized product training is required for:
- Long-term care insurance (8-hour initial course; 4-hour biennial updates)
- Annuity products (4-hour annuity training course)
- Flood insurance (3-hour course)

Insurers bear supervisory responsibility to verify that appointed agents maintain current licenses and complete required CE and product-specific training before selling products.12 Sales of insurance policies by producers with lapsed licenses or inadequate product training may expose insurers to:

1. **Regulatory penalties** for inadequate supervision under state insurance codes
2. **Suitability liability** for annuity and variable product sales without proper training
3. **Market conduct examination findings** for failing to maintain adequate producer oversight systems

Under general common law principles, policies sold by unlicensed agents are typically **enforceable against the insurer** despite the licensing violation, as courts impose penalties on insurers rather than innocent policyholders who reasonably relied on the agent's apparent authority.13 However, policyholders seeking to avoid unfavorable purchases may argue unlicensed agent activity renders contracts voidable at their election.

#### A.5 Interstate Market Conduct Coordination

The **NAIC Market Actions Working Group** coordinates multi-state market conduct examinations and information sharing among state insurance departments.14 When one state identifies systematic violations affecting multi-state operations (rather than state-specific regulatory compliance issues), the examining state typically reports findings through the **Market Actions Tracking System (MATS)**, enabling other states to assess whether similar violations affect their resident policyholders.

Multi-state examination triggers often arise from:
- **Centralized operational systems** (claims processing, policy administration) serving all states from a single home office location
- **Uniform corporate policies** (lapse procedures, agent training, claims guidelines) applied nationwide
- **Pattern-based violations** (elevated complaint ratios, claims denial patterns) evident across multiple jurisdictions

States may conduct parallel targeted examinations focusing on specific issues (e.g., claims timing, lapse notice adequacy) or participate in coordinated multi-state examinations under NAIC facilitation.15 The probability of multi-state examination expansion depends on: (a) whether violations reflect systemic operational failures versus state-specific compliance issues, (b) complaint data from other states, (c) resource availability at other state insurance departments, and (d) political priorities of state regulators.

#### A.6 Form A Acquisition Approval and Market Conduct Findings

Under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-2127 (Nebraska Insurance Holding Company System Regulatory Act), any person seeking to acquire control of a Nebraska-domiciled insurer must file a **Form A application** and obtain Director approval.16 The Director must approve the acquisition unless, after notice and hearing, the Director finds that:

> "(a) After the change of control, the domestic insurer would not be able to satisfy the requirements for the issuance of a license to write the line or lines of insurance for which it is presently licensed;
>
> (b) The effect of the merger or other acquisition of control would be substantially to lessen competition in insurance in this state or tend to create a monopoly;
>
> (c) The financial condition of the acquiring party is such as might jeopardize the financial stability of the insurer or prejudice the interests of its policyholders;
>
> (d) The plans or proposals which the acquiring party has to liquidate the insurer, sell its assets, or consolidate or merge it with any person, or to make any other material change in its business or corporate structure or management, are unfair and unreasonable to policyholders of the insurer and not in the public interest;
>
> (e) **The competence, experience, and integrity of those persons who would control the operation of the insurer are such that it would not be in the interest of policyholders of the insurer and of the public to permit the merger or other acquisition of control**; or
>
> (f) The acquisition is likely to be hazardous or prejudicial to the insurance buying public."17 [VERIFIED: Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-2127(4)] [emphasis added]

Market conduct examination findings directly implicate subsection (e) regarding **management competence and integrity**. While market conduct violations alone rarely constitute grounds for Form A disapproval, the Director possesses statutory authority to impose **conditions on approval** to protect policyholders and ensure competent post-acquisition management.18

Common Form A conditions following market conduct findings include:
- **Corrective action completion certification** as a closing condition precedent
- **Enhanced regulatory monitoring** with periodic compliance reporting requirements
- **Chief Compliance Officer appointment** with qualifications acceptable to the Director
- **Capital maintenance requirements** above minimum RBC thresholds
- **Escrow or holdback provisions** for outstanding fines or contingent liabilities

The Director's discretion to impose conditions is broad but must be reasonably related to the statutory approval standards and policyholder protection objectives.19 Approval timelines typically range from 60-120 days for straightforward acquisitions, but may extend to 6-9 months if conditions require substantial corrective actions or if public hearings generate significant opposition.

The "prior management" argument is particularly weak because American Financial Holdings is acquiring LLIC's entire equity, including all pre-existing liabilities, operational systems, and corporate culture. The Director reasonably conditions approval on demonstration that the acquirer will remedy rather than perpetuate operational deficiencies.
