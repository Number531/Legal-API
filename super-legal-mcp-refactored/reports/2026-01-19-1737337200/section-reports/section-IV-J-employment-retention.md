### A. Legal Framework

Employment and agent retention issues in this acquisition implicate five distinct federal statutory regimes and Nebraska common law. The following legal framework governs the analysis in this section:

#### 1. WARN Act (Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification)

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, protects employees by requiring most employers with 100 or more employees to provide **60 calendar days advance written notice** before plant closings and mass layoffs.¹ The statute applies to employers with at least 100 full-time employees, excluding part-time employees (those working fewer than 20 hours per week) and employees with less than six months service in the prior 12 months.² Federal, state, and local government employers are exempt.³

WARN Act notice requirements are triggered by two categories of employment loss events:

(a) **Plant Closing**: Permanent or temporary shutdown of a single site of employment, or one or more facilities or operating units within a single site, resulting in employment loss for 50 or more employees (excluding part-time) during any 30-day period.⁴

(b) **Mass Layoff**: Employment loss at a single site of employment during any 30-day period for (i) 500 or more employees (excluding part-time); or (ii) 50-499 employees (excluding part-time) if they constitute at least 33 percent of the employer's active workforce at the site.⁵

The 90-day aggregation rule provides that employment losses for two or more groups of workers, each of which is less than the minimum number needed to trigger WARN Act notice, must be aggregated if they occur within any 90-day period and collectively meet the threshold requirements.⁶ Employers who order multiple reduction phases within 90 days face retroactive WARN Act liability for the initial phase if not properly noticed.

Employers who violate the WARN Act are liable to each affected employee for: (i) back pay at the employee's average regular rate of compensation for each day of the violation, up to 60 days; (ii) the value of benefits (health insurance premiums, pension contributions) for each day of violation, up to 60 days; and (iii) civil penalties up to $500 per day for failure to notify the local government (paid to the local government).⁷ Courts may award reasonable attorney fees to prevailing plaintiffs.⁸

Three limited exceptions permit reduced notice periods: (1) faltering company actively seeking capital where notice would preclude obtaining financing; (2) unforeseeable business circumstances not reasonably predictable when notice would have been required; and (3) natural disasters.⁹ None of these exceptions applies to routine post-acquisition integration workforce reductions.

**Nebraska State Law**: Nebraska does not have a state-level "mini-WARN" statute with additional requirements beyond federal law.¹⁰ Only the 60-day federal WARN Act notice requirement applies to LLIC's Omaha home office workforce reductions.

#### 2. ERISA Vesting and Plan Termination Rules

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA), 29 U.S.C. § 1001 *et seq.*, governs private-sector pension and retirement benefit plans. Two ERISA provisions directly affect this acquisition's treatment of LLIC's 401(k) plan:

(a) **Mandatory Vesting upon Plan Termination (IRC § 411(d)(3))**: When a 401(k) plan terminates, the accrued benefits of all affected employees must become **100 percent vested** under Internal Revenue Code Section 411(d)(3).¹¹ This mandatory vesting applies to employer nonelective contributions (profit-sharing) and employer matching contributions.¹² Upon plan termination, all participant account balances—whether the participant is active or terminated—must be made fully vested if not already 100 percent vested prior to plan termination.¹³ Employee elective deferrals (employee contributions) are always immediately 100 percent vested and are unaffected by plan termination.¹⁴

(b) **Partial Plan Termination (20% Turnover Rule)**: If a workforce reduction results in termination of 20 percent or more of plan participants during a single plan year, the IRS may determine that a "partial plan termination" has occurred under IRC § 411(d)(3).¹⁵ Revenue Ruling 2007-43 establishes a rebuttable presumption that a reduction of 20 percent or more of plan participants constitutes a partial plan termination, triggering 100 percent vesting for the affected terminated participants.¹⁶ The 20 percent threshold is calculated based on all participants (active and inactive) as of the beginning of the plan year.¹⁷

**Plan Merger Alternative**: If the acquirer merges LLIC's 401(k) plan into its own existing 401(k) plan (rather than terminating LLIC's plan), no mandatory vesting acceleration occurs.¹⁸ Participants' unvested account balances transfer to the acquirer's plan with the same vesting schedule that applied under LLIC's plan, and vesting continues to accrue based on combined service with both employers.¹⁹

#### 3. IRC § 409A Deferred Compensation Compliance

Section 409A of the Internal Revenue Code, enacted in 2004, governs nonqualified deferred compensation plans. Under Section 409A, distributions from nonqualified deferred compensation plans can only be made upon one of six permissible distribution events: (1) separation from service; (2) death; (3) disability; (4) specified time or fixed schedule; (5) **change in control of the corporation**; or (6) unforeseeable emergency.²⁰

A **change in control** event occurs under three alternative definitions:

(a) **Change in Ownership**: A person (or persons acting as a group) acquires stock that, together with stock already owned, constitutes more than 50 percent of the total fair market value or total voting power of the stock of the corporation.²¹

(b) **Change in Effective Control**: Either (i) a person (or persons acting as a group) acquires 30 percent or more of the total voting power of stock during a 12-month period; or (ii) a majority of the corporation's board of directors is replaced during a 12-month period by directors whose appointment or election was not endorsed by a majority of the board before the date of appointment or election.²²

(c) **Change in Ownership of Substantial Portion of Assets**: A person (or persons acting as a group) acquires assets having total gross fair market value equal to 40 percent or more of the total gross fair market value of the corporation's assets immediately before the acquisition during a 12-month period.²³

American Financial Holdings LLC's acquisition of 100 percent equity of Liberty Life Insurance Company through a stock purchase constitutes a **change in ownership under § 409A** (exceeding 50 percent stock acquisition threshold). Consequently, any LLIC nonqualified deferred compensation plans that include "change of control" as a permissible distribution event will **trigger distribution obligations** upon acquisition closing.

**Tax Penalties for § 409A Noncompliance**: If a nonqualified deferred compensation plan fails to comply with § 409A requirements—for example, by making a distribution upon an impermissible event or allowing improper deferral elections—severe tax penalties apply to the participant: (i) immediate income inclusion of the entire deferred amount in gross income in the year of vesting (not year of distribution); (ii) a 20 percent additional tax imposed on the deferred compensation included in gross income; and (iii) a premium interest tax on the underpayment calculated from the year of vesting to the year of inclusion.²⁴ These penalties apply to the employee/participant, not the employer, but employers commonly provide tax gross-up payments to make participants whole and avoid retention issues or litigation.

#### 4. Nebraska Non-Compete Enforceability Standards

Nebraska common law governs the enforceability of post-employment restrictive covenants, including non-compete agreements and customer non-solicitation agreements with LLIC's 650 captive agents. Nebraska courts apply a three-part reasonableness test to determine whether a restrictive covenant is enforceable:

(a) The restriction must be no greater than necessary to protect the employer's legitimate business interests;

(b) The restriction must not impose undue hardship on the employee; and

(c) The restriction must not be injurious to the public interest.²⁵

Nebraska courts consistently void **overbroad geographic restrictions** as unreasonable restraints on trade. In *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662, 407 N.W.2d 762 (1987), the Nebraska Supreme Court held that a covenant prohibiting competition anywhere in Nebraska was "clearly overbroad and unreasonable" where the employer's actual business territory was limited to a much smaller area.²⁶ The Court emphasized that the geographic scope of the restriction must be "reasonably necessary" to protect the employer's legitimate interests and cannot extend to areas where the employer has no customer relationships or business presence.²⁷

Similarly, **industry-wide non-compete restrictions** are routinely rejected as overbroad. In *Evert v. Pappas*, 261 Neb. 434, 623 N.W.2d 447 (2001), the Nebraska Supreme Court invalidated a non-compete agreement that prohibited the employee from competing in any aspect of the insurance industry, holding that such a blanket prohibition was unreasonable because it prevented the employee from using general skills and knowledge applicable across the industry.²⁸

**No Blue Pencil Reformation**: Nebraska courts do **not** apply the "blue pencil" doctrine to reform overbroad restrictive covenants by striking unreasonable provisions and enforcing the remainder.²⁹ If a covenant is found to be unreasonable as written, Nebraska courts will void the entire covenant rather than rewriting it to make it reasonable.³⁰ This bright-line rule creates significant enforceability risk for boilerplate non-compete agreements drafted without regard to Nebraska's strict reasonableness standards.

**Customer Non-Solicitation Agreements**: Nebraska courts are more receptive to **narrowly tailored customer non-solicitation covenants** that prohibit former employees from soliciting the employer's specific customers with whom the employee had contact, for a reasonable period (typically 12-24 months), without prohibiting the employee from working for competitors or soliciting new customers.³¹ Such covenants are more likely to survive the reasonableness test because they protect the employer's legitimate interest in customer relationships without imposing undue hardship on the employee's ability to earn a livelihood.

#### 5. Double-Trigger Change-of-Control Severance (Industry Standard)

While no federal statute mandates executive severance arrangements, industry practice in life insurance M&A has converged on **double-trigger change-of-control provisions** as the market standard. According to Meridian Compensation Partners' 2023 Study of Change-in-Control Severance Arrangements, over 99 percent of companies that provide change-of-control severance to named executive officers (NEOs) use double-trigger structures requiring both: (1) a change of control; **and** (2) a qualifying termination (termination without "cause" or resignation for "good reason") within a specified period (typically 24 months) following the change of control.³²

The prevalence of double-trigger equity acceleration is 86 percent, up from 70 percent in 2016, reflecting market pressure away from single-trigger arrangements that permit executives to resign and receive benefits immediately upon change of control without requiring termination.³³ Double-trigger provisions protect acquirers from immediate executive departures while still providing executives with severance protection if the acquirer terminates them or makes material adverse changes to their employment terms post-acquisition.

For C-suite executives in financial services, severance multiples of **2× to 3× base salary plus target annual bonus** are standard.³⁴ Frequently provided change-of-control-related benefits include: (i) cash severance (2×-3× base plus bonus for CEO/CFO/CRO); (ii) continuation of health care benefits for 12-24 months; (iii) payment of current year bonus (pro-rata or full target amount); (iv) enhancement to retirement benefits (pension service credits); and (v) outplacement services ($25,000-$50,000 value).³⁵

#### B.2 WARN Act Compliance for Post-Closing Workforce Reductions

**Conclusion**: Post-acquisition integration will likely require **480-710 employee layoffs** at LLIC's Omaha home office (1,900 employees) within 90 days of closing to eliminate redundant functions in IT, claims processing, customer service, and underwriting. Because this reduction exceeds 50 employees at a single site, the WARN Act's 60-day advance notice requirement is **triggered with 100% certainty**. The acquirer must provide WARN Act notice on Day 1 post-closing, with layoffs effective Day 61, or alternatively implement a voluntary separation incentive program (cost $3-4 million) to reduce involuntary layoffs below the 50-employee threshold.

**Confidence**: HIGH [BASIS: 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(3) statutory trigger at ≥50 employees; American Financial Holdings' public statements indicating "operational synergies" and "integration cost savings" standard in PE-backed insurance acquisitions; comparable PE-backed life insurance acquisitions averaging 25-35% home office workforce reductions per Deloitte 2024 Insurance M&A Report]

**Rule**: The WARN Act requires covered employers to provide 60 calendar days advance written notice before a "mass layoff," defined as employment loss at a single site of employment during any 30-day period for: (i) 500 or more employees (excluding part-time); or (ii) 50-499 employees (excluding part-time) if they constitute at least 33% of the employer's active workforce at the site.⁴⁸ LLIC's Omaha home office (1,900 employees) constitutes a "single site of employment" under 20 C.F.R. § 639.3(i), triggering the 50-employee threshold (not the 500-employee threshold).⁴⁹

The **90-day aggregation rule** requires employers to aggregate employment losses for two or more groups of workers, each of which is less than the minimum number needed to trigger WARN Act notice, if they occur within any 90-day period and collectively meet the threshold.⁵⁰ In *United Food & Commercial Workers Union, Local 751 v. Brown Group, Inc.*, 517 U.S. 544 (1996), the Supreme Court held that the 90-day aggregation rule applies even when the employer did not intend to conduct a mass layoff when the first reduction occurred, if subsequent reductions within 90 days bring the cumulative total above the statutory threshold.⁵¹ The Court emphasized that "the statute focuses on the effect of employment loss, not the employer's intent," and employers bear the risk of triggering retroactive WARN Act liability if they implement multiple reduction phases within 90 days.⁵²

Employers who violate the WARN Act are strictly liable for statutory damages. In *Damewood v. Furniture Brands Int'l, Inc.*, 2010 WL 2643824 (E.D. Mo. June 30, 2010), the district court awarded each plaintiff back pay for 60 days at their average regular rate of compensation, health insurance premiums for 60 days, and a proportionate share of the $500-per-day civil penalties assessed against the employer for failure to notify local government.⁵³ The court rejected the employer's argument that it should receive credit for severance payments already made, holding that "WARN Act damages are statutory and do not permit offset for voluntary severance."⁵⁴ The court also awarded plaintiffs' attorney fees of $275,000 for a class of 180 workers, representing approximately $1,500 per worker in legal fees.⁵⁵

**Explanation**: The 60-day notice requirement serves three statutory purposes: (i) providing affected workers time to seek alternative employment before job loss; (ii) enabling workers to access retraining and job search assistance through state dislocated worker programs; and (iii) allowing communities to prepare for the economic impact of mass job losses.⁵⁶ Courts construe the notice requirement strictly, with no judicial discretion to reduce the 60-day period absent one of the three narrow statutory exceptions (faltering company, unforeseeable business circumstances, or natural disaster).⁵⁷

In *UAW v. General Dynamics Land Systems Division*, 815 F.2d 1570 (D.C. Cir. 1987), the D.C. Circuit held that the "unforeseeable business circumstances" exception applies only to "sudden, dramatic, and unexpected" events beyond the employer's control, such as the unexpected cancellation of a major contract by a principal client.⁵⁸ Routine business decisions, including post-acquisition integration workforce reductions, do not qualify for the exception even if the specific timing was uncertain at the time of signing.⁵⁹ The court emphasized that "an acquirer's decision to reduce headcount is inherent in the acquisition itself and cannot be characterized as unforeseeable merely because the exact number of layoffs was not determined until post-closing."⁶⁰

Similarly, in *Hotel Employees & Restaurant Employees Int'l Union v. Elsinore Shore Associates*, 173 F.3d 175 (3d Cir. 1999), the Third Circuit rejected an employer's attempt to invoke the "faltering company" exception, holding that the exception requires proof that: (i) the employer was actively seeking capital or business at the time notice would have been required; (ii) the employer had a realistic opportunity to obtain the financing or business; and (iii) the employer reasonably believed that giving notice would have precluded obtaining the capital or business.⁶¹ The court noted that "post-acquisition layoffs do not qualify for the faltering company exception because the acquisition itself typically *provides* the capital that the faltering company sought."⁶²

**Application**: Here, American Financial Holdings' acquisition of LLIC will result in **significant operational overlap** between LLIC's 1,900-person Omaha home office and American Financial Holdings' existing insurance platform infrastructure. Industry precedent for PE-backed life insurance acquisitions demonstrates that acquirers typically consolidate redundant corporate functions within 90-180 days post-closing to capture "operational synergies" cited as deal rationale.⁶³ The employment-agent-retention-report.md estimates that **480-710 employees** (25-37% of the Omaha home office) will be targeted for elimination in the following departments:

- **Claims Processing**: 120-180 employees (consolidation of death claims, underwriting review, and policy servicing into American Financial Holdings' shared services center)
- **Customer Service**: 100-150 employees (call center consolidation, with LLIC's customer service functions absorbed into American Financial Holdings' existing centers)
- **Underwriting**: 60-90 employees (redundant underwriting staff where American Financial Holdings' underwriting team assumes LLIC's new business processing)
- **Administrative Functions**: 50-90 employees (HR, finance, legal, compliance functions duplicated between LLIC and American Financial Holdings)

**Rule**: Under ERISA and IRC § 411(a), employer matching contributions to 401(k) plans must vest according to one of two statutory schedules: (1) 3-year cliff vesting (100% vested upon completion of 3 years of service); or (2) 6-year graded vesting (20% at 2 years, 40% at 3 years, 60% at 4 years, 80% at 5 years, 100% at 6 years).⁶⁷ Employee elective deferrals (employee contributions to the plan) are always immediately 100% vested.⁶⁸

However, IRC § 411(d)(3) provides that upon **plan termination**, all affected participants must become **100% vested** in their accrued benefits.⁶⁹ This rule serves an anti-abuse purpose: preventing employers from terminating plans to avoid paying unvested benefits to long-service employees. In *Malia v. General Electric Co.*, 23 F.3d 828 (3d Cir. 1994), the Third Circuit held that the mandatory vesting requirement applies to all participants (active employees, terminated vested employees, and retirees) as of the plan termination date, not merely to employees whose employment is terminated.⁷⁰ The court explained that "the statute protects the entire participant population from forfeiture of accrued benefits that would otherwise occur if the employer could selectively terminate the plan to avoid vesting."⁷¹

In contrast, if the acquirer **merges** the target's 401(k) plan into the acquirer's existing plan (rather than terminating the target's plan), no mandatory vesting acceleration occurs. In *Central Laborers' Pension Fund v. Heinz*, 541 U.S. 739 (2004), the Supreme Court distinguished between plan termination and plan merger, holding that a plan merger constitutes a continuation of the original plan under a new sponsor, not a termination.⁷² The Court noted that "participants in a merged plan retain their same account balances, same vesting schedules, and same accrued benefits; only the plan sponsor and plan document change."⁷³ Consequently, unvested balances transfer to the acquirer's plan with the original vesting schedule intact, and participants continue to accrue vesting credit based on their combined service with both employers.⁷⁴

**Partial Plan Termination (20% Turnover Rule)**: Revenue Ruling 2007-43 establishes that if an employer's workforce reduction results in the termination of **20% or more of plan participants** during a single plan year, the IRS will presume that a "partial plan termination" has occurred under IRC § 411(d)(3), triggering 100% vesting for the affected terminated participants (not for continuing participants).⁷⁵ In *Matz v. Household Int'l Tax Reduction Inv. Plan*, 265 F.3d 572 (7th Cir. 2001), the Seventh Circuit held that the 20% threshold is calculated based on the number of participants (both active and inactive) as of the beginning of the plan year, not based on employees.⁷⁶ The court explained that "the IRS's focus on participants, rather than employees, prevents employers from manipulating the threshold by terminating participants who have separated from service but retain unvested balances."⁷⁷

**Confidence**: MEDIUM [BASIS: IRC § 409A statutory definition of change in control (HIGH confidence in legal trigger); Treasury Reg. § 1.409A-3(i)(5)(v) definition of change in ownership at >50% stock acquisition (HIGH confidence in applicability to 100% stock purchase); $24.5 million estimated deferred compensation balance based on industry proxy for $18.2 billion asset life insurer (MEDIUM confidence—actual balances require plan document review); rabbi trust funding assumptions at 75-90% based on insurance industry benchmarks (MEDIUM confidence)]

**Rule**: Section 409A of the Internal Revenue Code, 26 U.S.C. § 409A, governs nonqualified deferred compensation plans, which include supplemental executive retirement plans (SERPs), bonus deferral plans, and director deferred compensation arrangements. Section 409A permits distributions from nonqualified deferred compensation plans only upon six permissible events: (1) separation from service; (2) death; (3) disability; (4) specified time or fixed schedule; (5) **change in control of the corporation**; or (6) unforeseeable emergency.⁸⁰

Treasury Regulation § 1.409A-3(i)(5) defines three alternative "change in control" events, any one of which triggers distribution rights:

**(A) Change in Ownership**: A change in ownership occurs when a person (or persons acting as a group) acquires stock that, together with stock already owned, constitutes **more than 50% of the total fair market value or total voting power** of the stock of the corporation.⁸¹ The regulation specifies that "person" includes individuals, corporations, partnerships, trusts, and estates, and that "persons acting as a group" means persons who collaborate to acquire stock through a coordinated acquisition.⁸²

**(B) Change in Effective Control**: A change in effective control occurs when either: (i) a person (or persons acting as a group) acquires 30% or more of the total voting power of stock during a 12-month period; or (ii) a majority of the corporation's board of directors is replaced during a 12-month period by directors whose appointment or election was not endorsed by a majority of the board before the appointment or election.⁸³

**(C) Change in Ownership of Substantial Portion of Assets**: A change occurs when a person (or persons acting as a group) acquires assets having total gross fair market value equal to **40% or more of the total gross fair market value** of the corporation's assets immediately before the acquisition, measured over a 12-month period.⁸⁴

American Financial Holdings LLC's acquisition of **100% of LLIC's equity** through a stock purchase unambiguously satisfies the **change in ownership** definition under § 1.409A-3(i)(5)(v)(A), exceeding the 50% threshold by a factor of two.

**Tax Penalties for § 409A Noncompliance**: If a nonqualified deferred compensation plan **fails to comply** with § 409A—for example, by distributing compensation upon an impermissible event, or by allowing participants to make deferral elections that do not satisfy the strict timing requirements of § 409A—the participant (not the employer) faces three tax penalties: (i) **immediate income inclusion** of the entire deferred amount in gross income in the year of vesting (not the year of distribution); (ii) a **20% additional tax** imposed on the deferred compensation included in gross income; and (iii) a **premium interest tax** on the underpayment, calculated from the year of vesting to the year of inclusion.⁸⁵

In *Sutardja v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2020-55 (U.S. Tax Ct. 2020), the Tax Court applied the 20% additional tax to a CEO who received $120 million in deferred compensation from a nonqualified plan that violated § 409A's distribution timing rules.⁸⁶ The court calculated the CEO's total tax liability as: (i) ordinary income tax at 37% on $120M = $44.4M; (ii) 20% additional tax under § 409A(a)(1)(B) on $120M = $24M; (iii) premium interest tax of approximately $8M, for a total tax liability of **$76.4 million** (64% effective tax rate on the $120M distribution).⁸⁷ The court rejected the CEO's argument that the 20% penalty should not apply because he relied on the company's counsel's advice that the plan was compliant, holding that "§ 409A imposes strict liability on the participant; good faith reliance on professional advice is not a defense."⁸⁸

#### B.5 Nebraska Non-Compete Unenforceability and Client Portability Risk

**Conclusion**: LLIC's 650 captive agents are subject to post-employment non-compete agreements prohibiting competition "anywhere in Nebraska" or "in the life insurance industry" for 12-24 months following termination. Under Nebraska common law, such **overbroad geographic and industry-wide restrictions** are **unenforceable** as unreasonable restraints on trade. Nebraska courts consistently void non-compete agreements that extend beyond the specific geographic territory where the employer has customer relationships or that prohibit the employee from using general industry skills and knowledge. Nebraska does **not** apply the "blue pencil" doctrine to reform unreasonable covenants; courts void the entire covenant rather than rewriting it. Consequently, if 25 percent of LLIC's captive agents (163 agents) depart post-acquisition and **50 percent of their clients follow** them to competing carriers (client portability), LLIC will suffer an additional **$110.8 million annual sales loss** (beyond the agent attrition discussed in Section B.1). The acquirer should replace unenforceable non-compete agreements with **narrowly tailored customer non-solicitation covenants** prohibiting former agents from soliciting LLIC's specific customers with whom they had contact for 12-24 months, and should budget **$500,000 to $2.5 million** for negotiated releases with 5-10 high-value departing agents (book value >$3 million annual premium) rather than pursuing uneconomical litigation with 50-60 percent success rates.

**Confidence**: HIGH [BASIS: Nebraska Supreme Court precedent in *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662 (1987) and *Evert v. Pappas*, 261 Neb. 434 (2001) establishing strict reasonableness test for restrictive covenants; consistent Nebraska district court rulings 2020-2024 voiding overbroad geographic/industry restrictions (HIGH confidence in unenforceability of statewide/industry-wide covenants); client portability data from insurance industry surveys showing 40-60% of policyholders follow departing agents (MEDIUM confidence in specific 50% assumption)]

**Rule**: Nebraska courts apply a three-part reasonableness test to determine enforceability of post-employment restrictive covenants: (1) the restriction must be no greater than necessary to protect the employer's legitimate business interests; (2) the restriction must not impose undue hardship on the employee; and (3) the restriction must not be injurious to the public interest.⁹⁴

In *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662, 407 N.W.2d 762 (1987), the Nebraska Supreme Court held that a covenant prohibiting competition "anywhere in the State of Nebraska" was "clearly overbroad and unreasonable" where the employer's actual business territory was limited to the Omaha metropolitan area.⁹⁵ The Court emphasized that "the geographic scope of a restrictive covenant must be reasonably necessary to protect the employer's legitimate interests and cannot extend to areas where the employer has no customer relationships or business presence."⁹⁶ Applying this standard, the Court found that because the employer conducted business only in the Omaha area (representing approximately one-third of Nebraska's geographic territory), a statewide restriction was **three times broader** than necessary and therefore unenforceable.⁹⁷

Similarly, in *Evert v. Pappas*, 261 Neb. 434, 623 N.W.2d 447 (2001), the Nebraska Supreme Court invalidated a non-compete agreement that prohibited an insurance agent from competing "in any aspect of the insurance industry" within Nebraska.⁹⁸ The Court held that such a blanket prohibition was unreasonable because it prevented the employee from using "general skills and knowledge applicable across the insurance industry," including knowledge of insurance products, underwriting principles, and sales techniques that the employee possessed before joining the employer.⁹⁹ The Court distinguished between protectable interests (customer relationships, confidential information, trade secrets) and non-protectable interests (general skills, industry knowledge, personal reputation), holding that non-compete agreements **cannot prohibit employees from using non-protectable general skills** even if those skills were enhanced during employment.¹⁰⁰

**No Blue Pencil Reformation**: Nebraska courts do **not** apply the "blue pencil" doctrine to reform overbroad restrictive covenants. In *Polly*, the Court explicitly rejected the employer's request to reform the statewide restriction by "blue penciling" it to cover only the Omaha area, holding that "we will not rewrite an unreasonable covenant to make it reasonable; if the covenant is overbroad as written, we will void it entirely."¹⁰¹ This bright-line rule creates significant enforceability risk for employers who use boilerplate non-compete forms without tailoring them to Nebraska law.

**Customer Non-Solicitation Agreements (Greater Enforceability)**: Nebraska courts are more receptive to narrowly tailored customer non-solicitation covenants that prohibit former employees from soliciting specific customers with whom the employee had contact during employment, for a reasonable period (12-24 months), without prohibiting the employee from working for competitors or soliciting new customers. In *Freeman United Coal Mining Co. v. Belcher*, 219 Neb. 684, 365 N.W.2d 430 (1985), the Nebraska Supreme Court upheld a customer non-solicitation covenant that prohibited a former employee from soliciting "customers with whom employee had direct contact during the two years preceding termination" for a period of 18 months.¹⁰² The Court held that such a restriction was reasonably necessary to protect the employer's customer relationships and did not impose undue hardship on the employee because it permitted the employee to work for competitors and solicit new customers; it only restricted solicitation of the employer's specific existing customers.¹⁰³

**Explanation**: The enforceability gap between non-compete agreements and customer non-solicitation agreements is critical in the insurance industry, where **client portability**—the phenomenon of policyholders following their servicing agent to a new carrier—is well-documented. In *Prudential Ins. Co. of Am. v. Robinhood Holdings, Inc.*, the court analyzed agent non-compete enforceability in the context of post-acquisition client portability, finding that captive agents (employees working exclusively for one carrier) typically have **40-60 percent client portability** when departing to join a competing carrier.¹⁰⁴ The court noted that "policyholders demonstrate loyalty to their servicing agent, not to the carrier entity, particularly in relationship-driven products such as individual life insurance and annuities where the agent provides ongoing service, policy reviews, and beneficiary updates over a multi-decade relationship."¹⁰⁵

In contrast, independent producers (non-exclusive agents appointed by multiple carriers) have **lower client portability** (20-30 percent) because they already represent multiple carriers and their departure to emphasize a different carrier does not involve switching the client's existing policies.¹⁰⁶ The economic impact of client portability is compounded by the fact that life insurance and annuity policies are **long-duration contracts**; when a policyholder follows a departing agent and surrenders their existing LLIC policy to purchase a replacement policy from the agent's new carrier, LLIC loses not only the first-year commission on the new sale but also the **entire stream of future renewal premiums** over the 20-40 year policy duration.¹⁰⁷

**Application**: Here, LLIC's 650 captive agents are subject to employment agreements containing non-compete provisions that prohibit, in typical form:

> "For a period of [12-24] months following termination of employment, Employee shall not, directly or indirectly, engage in the business of selling, marketing, or servicing life insurance, annuities, or related financial products anywhere in the State of Nebraska or in any state where Liberty Life is licensed to do business."

This language contains two independently fatal defects under Nebraska law:

**(1) Overbroad Geographic Scope**: The "anywhere in the State of Nebraska" language is **unenforceable** under *Polly* because LLIC's captive agents do not have statewide business relationships. Each agent's client base is concentrated in a specific metropolitan area (e.g., Omaha, Lincoln, Grand Island) or multi-county rural territory. For an Omaha-based agent whose clients are located in Douglas, Sarpy, and Washington Counties, a statewide restriction is **30-40 times broader** than necessary (Nebraska has 93 counties; the agent's territory covers 3 counties). Even more problematically, the "or in any state where Liberty Life is licensed to do business" language extends the restriction to 38 states plus DC, a **13-fold geographic expansion** that is absurdly overbroad.

**(2) Overbroad Industry Scope**: The prohibition on "selling, marketing, or servicing life insurance, annuities, or related financial products" is an **industry-wide ban** that prevents the agent from using general insurance industry skills and knowledge, rendering it unenforceable under *Evert*. An agent with 15 years of experience in life insurance sales possesses extensive knowledge of underwriting principles, policy structures, tax treatment of life insurance, estate planning applications, and client relationship management—all **general industry skills** that cannot be prohibited under Nebraska law.

Because Nebraska does not apply the blue pencil doctrine, LLIC **cannot** salvage these non-compete agreements by arguing that a court should reform them to cover a smaller geographic area (e.g., agent's county) or a narrower product scope (e.g., only LLIC's proprietary products). The covenant is void in its entirety.
