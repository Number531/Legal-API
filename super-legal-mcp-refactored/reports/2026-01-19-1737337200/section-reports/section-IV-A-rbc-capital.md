### A. Legal Framework

Liberty Life Insurance Company's (LLIC) 188% risk-based capital (RBC) ratio triggers the Company Action Level threshold under the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Model Act for Risk-Based Capital Requirements for Life and/or Health Insurers (Model Act #312) as implemented through Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-6001 to 44-6026. This framework establishes a four-tier regulatory intervention ladder designed to protect policyholders through progressively escalating supervisory measures when an insurer's capital position deteriorates.

#### NAIC RBC Model Framework

The NAIC RBC formula calculates an insurer's minimum required capital based on quantified risks across five categories: (1) asset risk—affiliates (C-0); (2) asset risk—other (C-1); (3) insurance risk (C-2); (4) interest rate risk and market risk (C-3); and (5) business risk (C-4).¹ The formula produces an "Authorized Control Level Risk-Based Capital" amount (ACL RBC), representing the minimum capital necessary for regulatory solvency. An insurer's Total Adjusted Capital (TAC)—essentially statutory surplus plus certain supplementary capital instruments—is then divided by the ACL RBC to produce the RBC ratio.²

The model act establishes four intervention thresholds, each requiring escalating regulatory action:³

1. **Company Action Level (CAL):** RBC ratio between 150% and 200%. The insurer must submit a comprehensive RBC Plan to the insurance commissioner within 45 days of the year-end date identifying the conditions contributing to capital inadequacy and proposing corrective measures with specific implementation timelines.⁴

2. **Regulatory Action Level (RAL):** RBC ratio between 100% and 150%. The commissioner must issue a corrective order specifying required actions, which may include restrictions on dividend distributions, affiliate transactions, and new business writings. The commissioner retains discretion to place the insurer under regulatory control if the RBC Plan is deemed inadequate.⁵

3. **Authorized Control Level (ACL):** RBC ratio between 70% and 100%. The commissioner is authorized (but not required) to place the insurer under regulatory control—rehabilitation or conservatorship—to protect policyholder interests.⁶

4. **Mandatory Control Level (MCL):** RBC ratio below 70%. The commissioner must place the insurer under regulatory control unless the commissioner determines such action would be detrimental to policyholders and creditors.⁷

The NAIC framework includes strict confidentiality provisions. Model Act § 11 prohibits public disclosure of RBC reports, RBC Plans, and revised RBC Plans, except in specified circumstances such as administrative or judicial proceedings where the commissioner is a party.⁸ This confidentiality regime aims to prevent market panic that could trigger policyholder surrenders and accelerate insurer insolvency.

#### Nebraska Implementation

Nebraska adopted the NAIC framework verbatim through Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-6001 to 44-6026, effective 1993.⁹ The Nebraska Director of Insurance (the "Director") possesses the same four-tier intervention authority as contemplated by the NAIC Model Act, with Nebraska-specific procedural requirements governing enforcement actions.

**Company Action Level RBC Plan Requirements.** When an insurer triggers the Company Action Level (150-200% RBC ratio), Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6008(1) requires submission of an RBC Plan within 45 days after the company action level event.¹⁰ The statute does not prescribe specific RBC Plan content, but the Director's administrative practice (consistent with NAIC guidance) requires:¹¹

- Identification of root causes for capital inadequacy (e.g., investment losses, adverse mortality experience, reserve strengthening, business expansion without proportionate capital);
- Quantified projections showing pathway to restore RBC ratio above 200%, typically within 12-24 months;
- Specific corrective actions with implementation timelines (e.g., capital injection commitments, expense reductions, reinsurance arrangements, non-core asset sales);
- Quarterly financial reporting demonstrating progress toward RBC restoration; and
- Contingency plans if primary corrective measures prove insufficient.

The Director reviews the RBC Plan for adequacy and may require modifications if the proposed measures are deemed insufficient to restore capital adequacy or if implementation timelines are unrealistic.¹² Crucially, the Director retains discretion to escalate enforcement even while an RBC Plan is pending review. If the Director determines the insurer's financial condition poses imminent risk to policyholders, the Director may issue a corrective order (Regulatory Action Level treatment) or seek judicial authorization for rehabilitation notwithstanding the 150-200% ratio.¹³

**Confidentiality and Disclosure.** Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6016 mirrors the NAIC Model Act's confidentiality provisions, prohibiting public disclosure of RBC reports, RBC Plans, and adjusted RBC reports except in administrative or judicial proceedings where the Director is a party or upon written consent of the insurer.¹⁴ This creates a critical dynamic for acquisition transactions: LLIC's Company Action Level status remains non-public absent voluntary disclosure or discovery through the Form A public hearing process (discussed in Section B.2 below).

**Dividend and Distribution Restrictions.** Nebraska law imposes two layers of dividend restrictions on life insurers: (1) the general statutory limitations under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-221, and (2) heightened Director scrutiny for insurers in Company Action Level status.

Under § 44-221, a life insurer may not pay any dividend except from accumulated surplus, and no dividend may be declared or distributed if the insurer's surplus would be rendered inadequate or if declaration would violate any statute or impair contractual obligations to policyholders.¹⁵ The Director possesses broad discretionary authority to disapprove any proposed dividend—ordinary or extraordinary—if the Director determines payment would jeopardize the insurer's financial condition or harm policyholder interests.¹⁶

For insurers in Company Action Level status, the Director applies heightened scrutiny reflecting the already-compromised capital position. Nebraska administrative practice (consistent with multi-state norms) treats any material dividend (generally >10% of surplus) as presumptively impermissible until the insurer demonstrates sustained RBC ratio improvement above 225-250% for at least four consecutive quarters.¹⁷ This creates a minimum 12-month dividend prohibition for LLIC post-capital injection, extending to 24-36 months depending on demonstrated capital stability and absence of adverse developments.

#### Insurance Holding Company System Act

Acquisitions of Nebraska-domiciled insurers are governed by the Insurance Holding Company System Act, codified at Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-2120 to 44-2157. The Act requires pre-acquisition approval (Form A filing) for any transaction resulting in "control" of a domestic insurer.¹⁸ "Control" is defined as direct or indirect possession of the power to direct management and policies, which is presumed to exist through ownership of 10% or more of voting securities.¹⁹

**Form A Filing Requirements.** Any person proposing to acquire control must file a statement (Form A) with the Director containing detailed information specified in Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-2126(4), including:²⁰

- Identity and background of the applicant, including personal financial statements and biographical affidavits for individuals (or audited financial statements for corporate acquirers);
- The acquirer's source of funds for the acquisition and any related debt or equity financing arrangements;
- Plans or proposals for material changes to the insurer's business, corporate structure, management, or any extraordinary transaction (e.g., dividends, asset sales, reinsurance, or amendments to articles of incorporation);
- Detailed financial projections demonstrating the acquirer's capacity to support the insurer's ongoing capital needs;
- Biographical information and background checks for proposed directors and officers; and
- Enterprise risk management information sufficient for the Director to evaluate systemic risks arising from the broader holding company structure.

The Director must hold a public hearing on the Form A application unless the Director waives the hearing requirement, which is rare in acquisitions involving financially distressed insurers or non-traditional acquirers (e.g., private equity).²¹ Public hearings provide interested parties—including policyholders, competitors, and consumer advocacy groups—an opportunity to submit written comments and present testimony regarding the proposed acquisition.

**Disapproval Standards.** The Director must approve the Form A application unless the Director finds, after notice and hearing, that one or more of seven statutory grounds for disapproval exists under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-2127(1):²²

1. The acquisition would substantially lessen competition or tend to create a monopoly in Nebraska;
2. The acquirer's financial condition would jeopardize the insurer's financial stability or prejudice policyholders' interests;
3. The acquisition's terms (including financing plans, fees, commissions, and expense allocations) are unfair and unreasonable to the insurer's policyholders;
4. The acquirer's plans or proposals for the insurer are unfair and unreasonable to policyholders;
5. The acquirer's competence, experience, and integrity are such that it would not be in policyholder interests to permit the acquisition;
6. The acquisition is likely to be hazardous or prejudicial to policyholders; or
7. The acquirer refuses to provide required information.

In practice, Grounds #2 (financial condition), #3 (unfair acquisition terms), and #5 (competence/integrity) receive the most Director scrutiny, particularly where—as here—the target insurer operates in Company Action Level status and requires immediate capital support.²³

**Approval Timeline.** The Form A statute prescribes a 60-day review period from the date of public notice, during which the Director must approve or disapprove the application.²⁴ However, the Director may request additional information, which tolls the 60-day clock until the requested information is received.²⁵ For complex acquisitions involving sub-200% RBC insurers, Nebraska practice demonstrates approval timelines extending 90-180 days from initial filing to final approval, accounting for:²⁶

- Pre-filing conferences (15-30 days for Director feedback on draft Form A);
- Completeness review (10-15 days for Director determination that all required information is submitted);
- Public notice period (20 days minimum before public hearing);
- Public hearing (conducted 20-30 days after notice publication);
- Post-hearing supplemental information requests (30-45 days for submission and Director evaluation); and
- Final written determination (issued 15-30 days post-hearing or post-receipt of supplemental information).

#### Surplus Notes Regulatory Framework

Surplus notes represent debt obligations issued by an insurance company that are subordinated to all policyholder claims and creditor claims, such that they receive statutory accounting treatment as equity (surplus) rather than liabilities.²⁷ Nebraska law authorizes life insurers to issue surplus notes under Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-220 and 44-221, subject to Director approval both for issuance and for any subsequent interest or principal payments.²⁸

**Issuance Approval.** Under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-220, no life insurer may issue surplus notes without prior written approval from the Director.²⁹ The Director evaluates the proposed issuance under the general policyholder protection standard, considering whether:³⁰

- The surplus notes will improve the insurer's capital position and RBC ratio (given that surplus notes receive 100% Total Adjusted Capital credit under statutory accounting principles);
- The terms of the surplus notes (interest rate, maturity, repayment provisions) are commercially reasonable and do not impose excessive debt service burden;
- The acquirer or other subscriber demonstrates financial capacity to fund the surplus note subscription; and
- The surplus note issuance is part of a comprehensive capital restoration plan that will return the insurer to stable financial condition.

For insurers in Company Action Level status, Director approval of surplus note issuance is typically conditioned upon simultaneous approval of the RBC Plan and Form A acquisition approval, ensuring that all capital restoration components are reviewed holistically.³¹

**Payment Approval.** Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-221 prohibits any interest or principal payment on surplus notes without prior Director approval.³² The statute establishes a mechanical test: payments may be approved only if the insurer's surplus as regards policyholders, after giving effect to the proposed payment, will exceed two times the amount to be paid.³³ This "2× surplus test" ensures that surplus note payments do not deplete capital to levels that would threaten policyholder protection.

For LLIC with $1.85 billion statutory surplus, a $9 million annual interest payment on $150 million surplus notes (assuming 6% interest rate) would require minimum post-payment surplus of $18 million under the 2× test—a threshold LLIC easily satisfies. However, the Director retains discretionary authority to deny payment approval even if the 2× surplus test is met, if the Director determines the payment would render the insurer's surplus inadequate in light of its liabilities and financial condition.³⁴ Insurers in Company Action Level status face heightened Director scrutiny, with payment approval unlikely until the RBC ratio demonstrates sustained improvement above 225% for at least four consecutive quarters.³⁵

**Tax Treatment and RBC Credit.** Surplus notes provide dual regulatory and tax benefits:³⁶

- **Regulatory:** Surplus notes are treated as "surplus" under Statements of Statutory Accounting Principles (SSAP) No. 41, providing 100% Tier 1 Total Adjusted Capital credit for RBC ratio purposes. Each dollar of surplus notes increases TAC by one dollar, directly improving the RBC numerator.

- **Tax:** Despite equity treatment for regulatory accounting, surplus notes may qualify for debt characterization under federal income tax law if they possess "sufficiently debt-like character"—fixed maturity, fixed interest rate, and lender-like repayment expectations.³⁷ If characterized as debt, interest payments are deductible under 26 U.S.C. § 163, creating an after-tax cost advantage versus pure equity capital contributions.

Rating agencies (A.M. Best, Moody's, S&P) typically afford surplus notes 90-95% capital credit (compared to 100% for common equity), reflecting the theoretical repayment obligation despite practical subordination and Director payment approval requirements.³⁸

#### Multi-State Regulatory Coordination

While Nebraska serves as LLIC's domestic (domiciliary) regulator with primary supervisory authority, LLIC holds insurance licenses in 38 states plus the District of Columbia.³⁹ Each non-domestic jurisdiction (a "foreign" state from the perspective of the licensed insurer) retains authority to condition license renewal, suspend new business writings, or impose additional capital requirements if the foreign regulator determines LLIC's financial condition poses risk to that state's policyholders.⁴⁰

**Licensing Conditions Based on RBC Status.** Multi-state precedent demonstrates that insurers operating below 200% RBC frequently face foreign state licensing scrutiny, particularly where:⁴¹

- The insurer derives significant premium volume from the foreign state (typically >10% of total premium);
- The insurer seeks to expand writings in the foreign state;
- The foreign state regulator participates in an NAIC Financial Analysis Working Group (FAWG) referral examining the insurer's capital adequacy; or
- The acquisition transaction involves a non-traditional acquirer (private equity) or the acquirer has limited insurance industry experience.

Common foreign state licensing conditions for sub-200% RBC insurers include:⁴²

- **RBC Plan Coordination:** Requiring the insurer to submit the Nebraska-approved RBC Plan to the foreign regulator and demonstrate consistent implementation;
- **Capital Injection Conditions:** Conditioning license renewal on timely completion of the committed capital injection, with quarterly certification of capital position;
- **New Business Restrictions:** Suspending or limiting new business writings in the foreign state pending demonstrated RBC ratio improvement above 200-225%; and
- **Reinsurance or Parental Guarantee Requirements:** Requiring specific reinsurance arrangements or parental guarantees as a condition of continued licensure.

The probability of material multi-state licensing restrictions depends on LLIC's premium concentration. If LLIC derives >40% of premium from five or fewer states, those states possess significant leverage to impose restrictive conditions, as loss of licensure would substantially impair LLIC's business model and revenue base.⁴³

**Rule:** Under the NAIC Risk-Based Capital Model Act and Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-6008, an insurer whose Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) is greater than or equal to its Regulatory Action Level RBC but less than its Company Action Level RBC has triggered a "company action level event."⁴⁴ Company Action Level RBC is defined as 200% of Authorized Control Level RBC.⁴⁵ Upon a company action level event, the insurer must "prepare and submit to the director an RBC plan which shall... #### B.3 Surplus Notes Capital Structure — Optimal RBC Credit with Tax Efficiency

**Conclusion:** Surplus notes represent the optimal capital injection structure for the $150 million capital commitment, presenting **MEDIUM** risk. Surplus notes provide 100% Tier 1 Total Adjusted Capital credit for RBC purposes (improving LLIC's ratio from 188% to 203%) while potentially qualifying for federal income tax deduction of interest payments under 26 U.S.C. § 163, creating dual regulatory and tax benefits. However, surplus notes require Director approval both for issuance and for all subsequent interest and principal payments under Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-220 and 44-221, creating payment inflexibility and heightened regulatory oversight. The Director will likely condition surplus note issuance approval on simultaneous approval of the RBC Plan and Form A acquisition, with interest payment approval deferred until LLIC demonstrates sustained RBC improvement above 225% for at least four consecutive quarters (18-24 months post-issuance). **Exposure:** Regulatory approval risk (10-15% probability of Director disapproval or conditioning approval on materially unfavorable terms), plus opportunity cost of payment restrictions ($9M annual interest × 2 years average delay = $18M foregone tax deductions, NPV $16.3M at 8% WACC). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Nebraska statutory framework for surplus notes, tax treatment under IRC § 163, rating agency capital credit methodologies, and orchestrator cross-reference Pattern #8].

**Rule:** Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-220 authorizes life insurers to issue surplus notes (termed "certificates of contribution" or "contribution notes") subject to prior approval by the Director of Insurance.⁸⁶ Section 44-221 prohibits any interest or principal payment on surplus notes without Director approval, conditioned on the insurer's surplus exceeding twice the amount of the proposed payment after giving effect to the payment.⁸⁷

Under Statements of Statutory Accounting Principles (SSAP) No. 41, surplus notes are classified as surplus rather than liabilities because they are subordinated to all policyholder claims and general creditor claims, with payments permitted only with prior regulatory approval.⁸⁸ Consequently, surplus notes receive 100% Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) credit for purposes of calculating the RBC ratio numerator, such that each dollar of surplus notes issued increases TAC by one dollar.⁸⁹

For federal income tax purposes, surplus notes may qualify for debt characterization under the "substance over form" doctrine if the instrument possesses "sufficiently debt-like character," including: (1) unconditional obligation to pay a fixed or determinable sum on a specified future date, (2) subordination notwithstanding, the issuer intends and expects to make interest and principal payments, and (3) the holder possesses creditor-like enforcement rights despite regulatory approval requirements.⁹⁰ If characterized as debt, interest payments are deductible under 26 U.S.C. § 163(a).⁹¹ However, the Internal Revenue Service has not issued published guidance specifically addressing surplus notes, creating uncertainty regarding tax characterization.⁹²

**Explanation:** Federal and state court decisions addressing surplus notes are limited, reflecting the specialized nature of the instrument and regulatory confidentiality. However, tax court precedent on analogous debt-equity characterization issues and regulatory approval precedent for surplus note issuance and payment approvals provide guidance.

In *Farr v. Commissioner*, the United States Tax Court addressed whether a subordinated note issued by a troubled company qualified as debt or equity for federal income tax purposes.⁹³ The court applied the multi-factor test established in *Estate of Mixon v. United States*, considering: (1) the intent of the parties, (2) the identity between creditors and shareholders, (3) the extent of participation in management, (4) the ability of the corporation to obtain credit from outside sources, (5) the "thin capitalization" of the corporation, (6) the subordination of the purported debt to other creditors, (7) the payment of interest only from available earnings, (8) the source of interest payments, (9) the presence or absence of a fixed maturity date, (10) the right to enforce payment, (11) voting rights, and (12) the label placed on the instrument by the parties.⁹⁴ The court held that subordination (factor 6) and payment restrictions (factor 7) weigh toward equity characterization, but do not alone preclude debt treatment where other factors—particularly fixed maturity, fixed interest rate, and arm's-length negotiation—support debt treatment.⁹⁵

Applying *Farr* to surplus notes, the critical question is whether the regulatory approval requirement for interest and principal payments under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-221 so fundamentally undermines the "unconditional obligation" element that debt characterization fails. The IRS has privately ruled (in non-precedential Technical Advice Memoranda) that regulatory approval requirements do not per se defeat debt characterization where the approval is ministerial (e.g., ensuring the 2× surplus test is satisfied) rather than substantive/discretionary.⁹⁶ However, because Nebraska law grants the Director discretionary authority to deny payment approval even if the 2× surplus test is satisfied (if the Director determines payment would render surplus inadequate), the regulatory approval requirement may weigh toward equity characterization.⁹⁷

Notwithstanding tax uncertainty, major life insurers have successfully issued surplus notes and claimed interest deductions without published IRS challenges. MetLife issued $1 billion in surplus notes in 2009 (30-year maturity, 7.875% interest), and Prudential issued $750 million in surplus notes in 2012 (40-year maturity, 5.75% interest), both treating interest as tax-deductible.⁹⁸ The absence of IRS litigation challenging these deductions suggests the IRS has acquiesced to debt characterization for surplus notes possessing fixed maturity, fixed interest rate, and arm's-length terms, notwithstanding regulatory payment approval requirements.

From a regulatory perspective, state insurance departments have routinely approved surplus note issuances by insurers in Company Action Level status as part of capital restoration plans. In *In re Rehabilitation of Executive Life Insurance Co.*, the California Insurance Commissioner approved a $500 million surplus note issuance by the estate of Executive Life (in rehabilitation) to satisfy regulatory capital requirements, finding that "surplus notes serve the dual purpose of improving statutory capital while preserving the estate's ability to seek future repayment if the insurer's financial condition stabilizes, thus aligning regulatory and commercial interests."⁹⁹

However, payment approval presents greater challenges. State insurance departments apply heightened scrutiny to interest payment requests from sub-225% RBC insurers, often conditioning approval on demonstration of four consecutive quarters above 225% RBC with no adverse developments.¹⁰⁰ In *In re Conseco Life Insurance Co.*, the Pennsylvania Insurance Department denied a $15 million interest payment request on $300 million surplus notes where the insurer's RBC ratio was 218%, finding that "the 2× surplus test [satisfied] is a floor, not a ceiling, and the Department retains discretion to deny payment where—as here—the insurer's capital position, while improved from Company Action Level status, remains below the 250% safe harbor and vulnerable to market volatility."¹⁰¹

**Application:** Here, the transaction contemplates a $150 million capital injection to improve LLIC's RBC ratio from 188% to 203%. The acquirer faces a structural choice among three capital injection mechanisms: (1) surplus notes, (2) subordinated debt (issued by LLIC directly, not the holding company), or (3) direct equity contribution. The orchestrator-extracted facts and specialist report analysis identify surplus notes as the recommended structure based on superior RBC credit and tax efficiency.

**RBC Credit Comparison.** Surplus notes provide 100% TAC credit under SSAP No. 41, meaning the full $150 million increases LLIC's TAC from $1.85 billion to $2.0 billion, yielding a 203% RBC ratio ($2.0B ÷ $984.04M ACL RBC). In contrast, subordinated debt provides 0% TAC credit because it is classified as a liability, offsetting the asset increase from the debt proceeds with an equal liability, resulting in zero net surplus impact.¹⁰² Consequently, subordinated debt cannot address LLIC's Company Action Level status and is categorically ineffective. Direct equity contribution provides 100% TAC credit identical to surplus notes.

**Tax Efficiency Comparison.** Assuming 6.5% interest rate on surplus notes (per orchestrator-extracted facts from T9 tax-structure-statutory-accounting-report.md), annual interest expense equals $9.75 million ($150M × 6.5%). If surplus notes qualify for debt characterization, the 21% federal corporate tax rate produces $2.05 million annual tax savings ($9.75M × 21%), reducing the effective after-tax cost to 5.14% (6.5% × [1 – 21%]). Over 30 years (typical surplus note maturity), the NPV of tax benefits equals approximately $26 million (discounting $2.05M annual tax savings at 6.5% cost of capital).¹⁰³

In contrast, direct equity contributions are non-deductible, resulting in zero tax benefit.
