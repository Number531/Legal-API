## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview and Scope

This memorandum analyzes corporate practice of medicine (CPOM) compliance for ComfortCare Partners LLC's proposed $185M acquisition of Gentle Transitions Home Health & Hospice, Inc., focusing on Critical Issue #9: whether Gentle Transitions' medical director arrangements comply with Florida, Georgia, and South Carolina CPOM restrictions. Gentle Transitions operates 8 agencies across three states (Florida: 2 home health + 2 hospice; Georgia: 3 home health + 3 hospice; South Carolina: 1 combined), with 8 medical directors structured as independent contractors (Form 1099) rather than employees (Form W-2). The assignment prompt assumes Florida prohibits corporate practice of medicine and requires analysis of whether independent contractor arrangements satisfy this prohibition. After comprehensive research of statutes, case law, and regulatory guidance, **this assumption is incorrect**: Florida does NOT prohibit corporate practice of medicine for physicians.

### Critical Finding: Florida Permits Corporate Practice of Medicine

**Florida is a permissive jurisdiction** that allows non-physician entities (including hospice and home health agencies) to both employ physicians as W-2 employees OR engage them as independent contractors. [VERIFIED: Permit Health - Florida CPOM Guide; The Health Law Firm - Corporate Practice of Medicine in Florida; Jones Health Law - The Truth About CPOM in Florida]

**Legal Authorities:**

1. **No Statutory Prohibition:** Florida has no statute prohibiting corporate employment of physicians. Fla. Stat. Â§ 458.331 (cited in assignment prompt) establishes grounds for disciplinary action against individual physicians for professional misconduct (fraud, negligence, impairment) but contains NO language prohibiting corporate employment or requiring physician ownership of medical entities. [VERIFIED: Fla. Stat. Â§ 458.331 (2022)]

2. **Case Law Precedent - *Crow v. AHCA* (1996):** The controlling Florida case is *Crow v. Agency for Health Care Administration*, 669 So. 2d 1160 (Fla. 5th DCA 1996), which affirmed a Florida Board of Medicine declaratory statement permitting corporate employment of physicians. In *Crow*, a physician sold his practice to a corporation and became a W-2 employee with flat-fee salary. The Board found this arrangement **permissible** as long as: (a) the physician retained exclusive control over medical diagnosis and treatment, and (b) the corporation had no authority to exercise control over the physician's professional judgment. The Fifth District Court of Appeal upheld this decision, establishing binding precedent that Florida permits corporate practice when physicians retain clinical autonomy. [VERIFIED: *Crow*, 669 So. 2d 1160; Florida Bar Journal article on Board declaratory statements]

3. **Health Care Clinic Act (2003):** While Florida permits corporate practice, the state enacted the Health Care Clinic Act (Fla. Stat. Ch. 400, Part X) to regulate non-physician owned practices. The Act requires practices that are (a) not wholly owned by physicians AND (b) bill insurance to obtain a Health Care Clinic License and employ/contract with a medical director to oversee clinical operations. [VERIFIED: Fla. Stat. Â§ 400.9905 (2020)]

4. **Hospice Exemption:** Licensed hospice agencies are **explicitly EXEMPT** from Health Care Clinic Act requirements. Fla. Stat. Â§ 400.9905(2)(f) exempts "the delivery of hospice services for which the hospice is licensed under part IV of chapter 400." Because Gentle Transitions' Florida agencies (Jacksonville and Tampa hospices) are licensed under Part IV, they are NOT subject to Health Care Clinic Act requirements and can structure medical director relationships as either employment or independent contractor arrangements without statutory constraint. [VERIFIED: Justia - Fla. Stat. Â§ 400.9905]

5. **Legislative Proposals Confirm Current Permissive Status:** The Florida Medical Association proposed Resolution 23-305 (2023) to establish CPOM restrictions by amending Chapter 458 to prohibit non-physician ownership of physician practices. The fact that this resolution was **proposed but not enacted** confirms Florida currently lacks CPOM prohibitions. [VERIFIED: FMA Resolution 23-305]

**Implication:** The foundational premise of the assignment (that Florida prohibits corporate practice and requires analysis of compliance workarounds) is incorrect. Florida permits both W-2 employment and 1099 independent contractor arrangements for medical directors. The relevant legal question is NOT state CPOM compliance but rather IRS worker classification (employee vs. independent contractor) for federal tax purposes.

### Georgia: Permissive (CPOM Statute Repealed 1982)

Georgia repealed its corporate practice of medicine statute (former O.C.G.A. Â§ 43-34-37(9)) in 1982, making it one of the most permissive jurisdictions for physician employment. Corporations can employ physicians as W-2 employees or engage them as independent contractors without restriction. Professional corporations must comply with ownership requirements (only licensed professionals can be shareholders), but general corporations and LLCs face no CPOM limitations. [VERIFIED: Permit Health - Georgia CPOM Guide; Little Health Law - Georgia CPOM]

**Application to Gentle Transitions:** The 6 Georgia agencies (3 home health + 3 hospice in Atlanta, Savannah, Augusta) can use either employment or independent contractor structures without legal risk. Current independent contractor arrangements fully compliant.

### South Carolina: Case Law Restrictions with Modern Uncertainty

South Carolina presents the most complex analysis. The state has no explicit CPOM statute but established restrictions through case law:

**Restrictive Case Law:**
- *Wadsworth v. McRae Drug Company*, 203 S.C. 543, 28 S.E.2d 417 (1943): South Carolina Supreme Court held corporations are prohibited from engaging in the practice of medicine, even through licensed employees.
- *Ezell v. Ritholz*, 188 S.C. 39, 198 S.E. 419 (1938): Unlicensed entities cannot practice licensed professions through employed professionals.

[VERIFIED: Permit Health - South Carolina CPOM Guide]

**Modern Uncertainty:** More recent sources suggest South Carolina may now permit corporate-owned medical practices and direct employment of physicians by non-physician entities, creating tension with 1943 precedent. This likely reflects evolution in regulatory enforcement rather than formal overruling of *Wadsworth*.

**Conservative Recommendation:** Maintain independent contractor structure for Charleston combined agency to minimize risk. Independent contractor arrangements where physicians maintain separate practices and clinical autonomy are less vulnerable to *Wadsworth* prohibition than direct W-2 employment relationships.

Misclassification as independent contractors when relationship is actually employment triggers:

- Back payroll taxes (employer FICA/Medicare 7.65%)
- Penalties for willful misclassification (20-40% of unpaid taxes)
- Interest on unpaid amounts
- Workers' compensation and unemployment insurance liability

**Legal Authority:** Unlike many states that maintain strict CPOM doctrines prohibiting non-physicians from employing physicians or controlling medical practice, Florida has adopted a permissive approach allowing corporate ownership and employment of physicians. ### B. Independent Contractor vs. Employee Analysis Under IRS Common Law Test

Even though Florida permits both employment and independent contractor arrangements for medical directors, the classification has significant federal tax and employment law implications. The IRS uses a three-factor common law test to determine worker classification.

#### 1. IRS Common Law Test Framework

**Legal Authority:** The IRS applies common law principles based on Revenue Ruling 87-41 (1987) to determine whether a worker is an employee or independent contractor for federal employment tax purposes. While Rev. Rul. 87-41 originally set forth 20 specific factors, the modern IRS approach consolidates these into **three primary categories**: (1) behavioral control, (2) financial control, and (3) type of relationship. [VERIFIED: IRS Topic No. 762 - Independent Contractor vs. Employee](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc762); [IRS Technical Guidelines 4.23.5](https://www.irs.gov/irm/part4/irm_04-023-005r)

**Analytical Standard:**
- No single factor is determinative
- All facts and circumstances must be weighed together
- The **degree of control** the business has over the worker is the central inquiry
- Different factors may point in different directions; the totality determines classification

[VERIFIED: Virginia Employment Commission - IRS 20 Factors and Virginia Exemptions](https://www.vec.virginia.gov/irs-20-factors-and-virginia-exemptions-employee-classification); [Physicians Practice - Classifying Independent Contractors in Medical Practice](https://www.physicianspractice.com/view/classifying-independent-contractors-your-medical-practice)]

#### 2. Factor 1: Behavioral Control

**Definition:** Behavioral control examines whether the business has the **right to direct and control** what work is accomplished and how the work is done, through instructions, training, evaluation systems, or other means. [VERIFIED: IRS source cited above]

**Healthcare-Specific Application:**

For physicians serving as medical directors, courts and the IRS examine:

a. **Instructions Given:**
- Detailed instructions on how, when, where to work = Employee
- General parameters with physician autonomy on methods = Independent Contractor

b. **Training Provided:**
- Employer-provided training on procedures/protocols = Employee
- Physician uses own expertise without training = Independent Contractor

c. **Evaluation and Supervision:**
- Direct supervision of daily work = Employee
- Outcome-based evaluation only = Independent Contractor

d. **Medical Autonomy:**
- Critical for physicians: Retention of independent medical judgment is a strong indicator of independent contractor status, even when other factors suggest employment [VERIFIED: Physician's Guide to Independent Contractor Agreement](https://physiciansthrive.com/contract-review/physician-independent-contractor-agreement/)]

#### 3. Factor 2: Financial Control

**Definition:** Financial control examines whether the business directs or controls the financial and business aspects of the worker's job, including payment methods, expense reimbursement, tool/equipment provision, investment in facilities, opportunity for profit/loss, and availability of services to other clients. - Exclusive service to one entity = Employee
- Provides services to multiple clients = Independent Contractor (strong indicator)

This changes the risk analysis from "compliance with prohibition" to "classification accuracy under IRS test."
