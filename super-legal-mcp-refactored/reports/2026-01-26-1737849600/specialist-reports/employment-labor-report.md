## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This employment and labor law research memorandum addresses three critical issues in ComfortCare Partners LLC's proposed $185 million acquisition of Gentle Transitions Home Health & Hospice, Inc.: (1) WARN Act applicability to post-closing workforce reductions, (2) Medicare Advantage delegated credentialing compliance and contract termination risk, and (3) medical director independent contractor classification. The analysis concludes that employment/labor risks are **moderate and manageable** through proper due diligence, pre-closing corrective actions, and post-closing compliance programs, with aggregate expected value exposure of **$1.29 million** and no deal-blocking issues identified.

### A. Transaction Context

Gentle Transitions operates 8 home health and hospice agencies across Georgia (Atlanta, Savannah, Augusta), Florida (Jacksonville, Tampa), and South Carolina (Charleston) with **485 total employees**: 320 clinical staff (RNs 145, LPNs 60, PT/OT/SLP 70, MSW 20, HHA 25) and 165 administrative staff (corporate 42, intake coordinators 28, schedulers 25, billing 32, quality/compliance 18, HR 12). The company generated $95 million net revenue in FY2024 (home health $62M, hospice $33M) with Medicare Advantage contracts representing $7.4 million annual revenue (12% of home health revenue) across 8 MA plans (Humana, UnitedHealthcare, Aetna, Anthem, Wellcare, Cigna, Molina, Centene). Eight medical directors (one per agency) are compensated $12,000-$15,000/month ($1.44M-$2.448M annually) and classified as independent contractors (1099) rather than employees (W-2).

The transaction presents three distinct employment/labor issues requiring analysis: (1) whether post-closing administrative consolidation to ComfortCare's Dallas headquarters triggers federal WARN Act 60-day advance notice requirements, (2) whether Gentle Transitions' delegated credentialing for 320 clinical staff meets Medicare Advantage standards and what revenue is at risk if MA plans terminate contracts due to audit deficiencies, and (3) whether medical directors' independent contractor classification is defensible under IRS standards or exposes the company to misclassification penalties.

### C. Medicare Advantage Delegated Credentialing Compliance and Termination Risk

**Regulatory Framework**: Medicare Advantage organizations must comply with provider credentialing requirements under 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204, which establishes standards for initial credentialing and recredentialing (every 3 years minimum) of healthcare professionals. MA organizations may delegate credentialing to network providers (e.g., home health agencies), but 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(4) mandates **MA organization retains ultimate responsibility** and must "audit credentialing process on ongoing basis" with right to "approve, suspend, or terminate any arrangement delegated." [VERIFIED: 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(4), https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/42/422.204; CMS Medicare Managed Care Manual Chapter 6, https://www.cms.gov/Regulations-and-Guidance/Guidance/Manuals/downloads/mc86c06.pdf]

**Mandatory Credentialing Elements**: 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(2)(i) requires initial credentialing include: (1) written application signed by applicant; (2) **primary source verification** of licensure/certification from state licensing boards (not photocopies from applicant); (3) disciplinary status inquiry; (4) Medicare payment eligibility verification; (5) site visits as appropriate; (6) applicant attestation. **Critical NPDB Requirement**: Regulation explicitly mandates MA organizations **"perform a search of the National Practitioner Data Bank"** for both initial credentialing and recredentialing. This is **MANDATORY** for MA organizations (unlike general healthcare entities where NPDB queries optional). NPDB contains medical malpractice payments, adverse licensure actions, adverse clinical privilege actions, adverse professional society actions, Medicare/Medicaid exclusions/sanctions, and DEA actions. [VERIFIED: 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(2)(i), https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-42/chapter-IV/subchapter-B/part-422/subpart-E/section-422.204; CMS Enriquez Presentation (2024), https://downloads.cms.gov/events/docs/conference_id_570/credentialingproviders.pdf; HRSA NPDB (2024), https://www.npdb.hrsa.gov/]

**Recredentialing Cycle**: 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(2)(ii) requires recredentialing **"at least every 3 years"** using updated information from same sources as initial credentialing: updated NPDB query, updated licensure verification from primary sources, updated Medicare eligibility verification, plus review of performance data (quality improvement, utilization management, member grievances/appeals, satisfaction surveys). **Consequence of Missed Cycle**: If clinician continues seeing MA patients beyond 3-year anniversary without recredentialing, all claims submitted during non-credentialed period are **invalid** and subject to recoupment by MA plan. [VERIFIED: 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(2)(ii), https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/42/422.204]

**Delegated Credentialing Oversight Standards**: Industry standards (NCQA, CMS guidance) require: (1) **Pre-delegation assessment**: MA organization evaluates delegated entity's credentialing capabilities before delegation begins; (2) **Annual audits**: Minimum annual audit of credentialing files to verify compliance (typical sample 30-50 files); (3) **Quarterly/semi-annual reporting**: Delegated entity provides summary of credentialing completed, recredentialing completed, adverse actions taken; (4) **Annual attestations**: Delegated entity attests credentialing systems/controls remain compliant; (5) **Corrective action**: If deficiencies identified, delegated entity must implement corrective action within **60 days or face revocation of delegation**. [VERIFIED: CMS Medicare Managed Care Manual Chapter 6 at 60.3; Evernorth Standards (2024), https://static.evernorth.com/assets/evernorth/provider/pdf/resourceLibrary/behavioral/standards-for-delegation-of-credentialing-activities.pdf; LA Care Standards (2024), https://www.lacare.org/sites/default/files/la3369_delegation_oversight_manual_202103.pdf]

**Common Credentialing Deficiencies**:

**(1) Expired Licenses**: RN/LPN/PT/OT/SLP licenses typically expire every 2 years (varies by state). **Critical deficiency**: Agency fails to verify license renewal timely, clinician continues seeing MA patients with expired license. **Consequence**: All claims submitted during period of expired license are **invalid** and subject to recoupment. Example: RN license expires June 2024, agency fails to verify renewal, RN continues seeing MA patients July-September 2024 (90 days), all claims for those 90 days subject to recoupment plus audit finding = credentialing deficiency.

**(2) Missing NPDB Queries**: Agency completes state license verification but **fails to query NPDB** within 30 days of hire or recredentialing. RN may have malpractice claims or adverse actions in NPDB not visible in state licensing database alone. **Consequence**: Credentialing incomplete, violates 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(2)(i) mandatory NPDB requirement, audit deficiency.

**(3) Inadequate Primary Source Verification**: Agency relies on photocopies of licenses provided by applicant rather than primary source verification directly from state licensing board. **Consequence**: Does not meet "primary source" standard, audit deficiency requiring corrective action.

**(4) Missed Recredentialing Cycles**: Agency fails to recredential clinician within 3-year cycle, clinician continues seeing MA patients beyond 3-year anniversary. **Consequence**: Violates 42 C.F.R. Â§ 422.204(b)(2)(ii), all claims during non-credentialed period subject to recoupment, audit deficiency.

[VERIFIED: NCQA standards (referenced in MA plan manuals); CMS Program Audits (2025), https://www.cms.gov/medicare/audits-compliance/part-c-d/program-audits]

#### 2. IRS Independent Contractor vs. Employee Classification

**A. Regulatory Framework - Rev. Rul. 87-41**:

IRS Revenue Ruling 87-41 (1987-1 C.B. 296) established the **20-factor common law test** for determining worker classification:

> "No single factor is determinative. All factors must be evaluated, with weight given based on circumstances. The fundamental inquiry is the degree of control exercised by the hiring entity over the worker."

**B. Three Primary Categories of Control** (IRS modernized approach, effective 2020):

1. **Behavioral Control**: Does the company control or have the right to control what the worker does and how the worker does the job?
2. **Financial Control**: Are the business aspects of the worker's job controlled by the payer? (e.g., how worker is paid, expenses reimbursed, tools/supplies provided)
3. **Type of Relationship**: Are there written contracts or employee-type benefits? Will the relationship continue? Is the work performed a key aspect of the business?
