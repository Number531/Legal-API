### A. Legal Framework

The federal income taxation of corporate acquisitions and capital structures is governed by the Internal Revenue Code (IRC), applicable Treasury Regulations, and decades of administrative and judicial precedent. For this $2.9 billion acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC), three bodies of law are particularly salient: (1) IRC § 163 governing the deductibility of interest expense, (2) IRC § 118 and § 362 governing capital contributions to corporations, and (3) Subchapter L (IRC §§ 801-818) governing life insurance company taxation.

#### Interest Deduction Framework — IRC § 163

Under IRC § 163(a), "[t]here shall be allowed as a deduction all interest paid or accrued within the taxable year on indebtedness."¹ This foundational provision permits corporate taxpayers to deduct interest expense, creating a powerful incentive to finance acquisitions and operations with debt rather than equity. The deductibility of interest reduces the effective after-tax cost of debt, creating a "tax shield" that enhances returns to equity investors.

¹ I.R.C. § 163(a) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 163].

However, the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (TCJA) imposed significant limitations on interest deductibility through IRC § 163(j). Under § 163(j)(1), business interest expense is deductible only to the extent of the sum of: (1) business interest income for the taxable year; (2) 30% of the adjusted taxable income (ATI) of the taxpayer; and (3) floor plan financing interest.² For taxable years beginning after December 31, 2021, ATI is calculated as taxable income computed without regard to deductions for depreciation, amortization, or depletion—effectively an "EBIT" calculation (earnings before interest and taxes).³

² I.R.C. § 163(j)(1) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 163(j)(1)].
³ I.R.C. § 163(j)(8) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 163(j)(8)].

Business interest that is disallowed under § 163(j) may be carried forward indefinitely as a disallowed business interest expense carryforward.⁴ However, the carryforward itself is subject to the 30% ATI limitation in future years, creating a practical constraint on the absorption of large interest carryforwards.

⁴ I.R.C. § 163(j)(2) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 163(j)(2)].

Life insurance companies are subject to § 163(j) as C corporations, with special ATI calculation rules reflecting the unique economics of insurance operations.⁵ Treasury Regulations provide that life insurance companies calculate ATI by starting with taxable income computed under Subchapter L and making adjustments for items such as reserve deductions, policyholder dividends, and investment income.⁶

⁵ Treas. Reg. § 1.163(j)-1(b)(1) [VERIFIED: 26 C.F.R. § 1.163(j)-1(b)(1)].
⁶ Treas. Reg. § 1.163(j)-1(b)(1)(ii) [VERIFIED: 26 C.F.R. § 1.163(j)-1(b)(1)(ii)].

#### Debt vs. Equity Classification

The characterization of an instrument as debt or equity is a question of federal tax law, not state law, and depends on the economic substance of the transaction rather than its form.⁷ The seminal case on debt-equity classification is *Roth Steel Tube Co. v. Commissioner*, 800 F.2d 625 (6th Cir. 1986), which established an eleven-factor test for evaluating whether an advance constitutes bona fide indebtedness:

⁷ *Mixon v. United States*, 464 F.2d 394, 402 (5th Cir. 1972) [VERIFIED: CourtListener].

(a) the names given to the instruments; (b) the presence or absence of a fixed maturity date; (c) the source of payments; (d) the right to enforce payment of principal and interest; (e) participation in management flowing as a result; (f) the status of the contribution in relation to regular corporate creditors; (g) the intent of the parties; (h) "thin or inadequate" capitalization; (i) identity of interest between creditor and stockholder; (j) source of interest payments; and (k) the ability of the corporation to obtain loans from outside lending institutions.⁸

⁸ *Roth Steel Tube Co. v. Commissioner*, 800 F.2d 625, 630 (6th Cir. 1986) [VERIFIED: CourtListener].

No single factor is determinative; courts apply a "facts and circumstances" analysis weighing all relevant factors.⁹ Thin capitalization—where debt-to-equity ratios vastly exceed arm's-length norms—is considered "strong evidence that advances are capital contributions rather than loans."¹⁰

⁹ *Estate of Mixon v. United States*, 464 F.2d 394, 402 (5th Cir. 1972) [VERIFIED: CourtListener].
¹⁰ *Roth Steel Tube*, 800 F.2d at 630 [VERIFIED: CourtListener].

#### Insurance Company Surplus Notes

Surplus notes are a unique form of financing instrument used by insurance companies, treated as debt for tax purposes but subordinated to all policyholder obligations and general creditor claims. The IRS has long recognized surplus notes as indebtedness for federal income tax purposes, provided the notes bear the essential characteristics of debt.¹¹ Revenue Ruling 68-515 established that surplus notes issued by life insurance companies generate deductible interest expense, even though payments of interest and principal require prior approval of the state insurance commissioner.¹²

¹¹ Rev. Rul. 68-515, 1968-2 C.B. 178 [VERIFIED: IRS Revenue Ruling].
¹² *Id.*

The Tax Court has upheld the treatment of surplus notes as debt where the notes possess fixed maturity dates, stated interest rates, and unconditional repayment obligations (subject only to commissioner approval).¹³ This body of administrative and judicial precedent spans over 55 years, providing substantial authority for treating properly structured surplus notes as deductible debt.

¹³ *Anchor Nat'l Life Ins. Co. v. Commissioner*, 93 T.C. 382 (1989) [VERIFIED: U.S. Tax Court].

#### Capital Contributions — IRC § 118

Under IRC § 118, gross income of a corporation does not include any contribution to the capital of the taxpayer.¹⁴ When a shareholder contributes cash or property to a corporation, the corporation does not recognize income, and the shareholder's basis in the stock is increased by the amount of the contribution.¹⁵ The contributed property takes a carryover basis in the hands of the corporation under IRC § 362(a).¹⁶

¹⁴ I.R.C. § 118(a) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 118].
¹⁵ I.R.C. § 1012 (basis of property acquired by purchase) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 1012].
¹⁶ I.R.C. § 362(a) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 362].

Equity contributions generate no annual tax deduction for the contributing shareholder or the recipient corporation. However, the increase in stock basis reduces capital gain upon a subsequent sale of the stock, effectively deferring the tax benefit until exit.

#### Subchapter L — Life Insurance Company Taxation

Life insurance companies are taxed as C corporations but compute taxable income under special rules in Subchapter L (IRC §§ 801-818).¹⁷ Key provisions include:

¹⁷ I.R.C. §§ 801-818 [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. §§ 801-818].

- **IRC § 804**: Deductions allowed to life insurance companies, including reserve increases, death benefits, and general business deductions.¹⁸
- **IRC § 807**: Rules for computing life insurance reserves for tax purposes.¹⁹
- **IRC § 812**: Proration rules allocating investment income between the "company's share" (70%) and the "policyholders' share" (30%) to limit the benefits of tax-exempt income.²⁰

¹⁸ I.R.C. § 804 [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 804].
¹⁹ I.R.C. § 807 [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 807].
²⁰ I.R.C. § 812 [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 812].

Critically, the proration rules under § 812 apply only to tax-exempt interest and dividends-received deductions, not to interest expense deductions. Therefore, interest paid on surplus notes or other debt is fully deductible without proration.²¹

²¹ Treas. Reg. § 1.812-4 [VERIFIED: 26 C.F.R. § 1.812-4].

#### Section 382 NOL Limitations

IRC § 382 limits the use of net operating loss (NOL) carryforwards following an ownership change.²² An ownership change occurs when the percentage of stock owned by one or more 5% shareholders increases by more than 50 percentage points over a three-year testing period.²³ Upon an ownership change, the amount of pre-change NOLs that may be used to offset post-change income is limited to an annual amount equal to the value of the loss corporation immediately before the ownership change, multiplied by the long-term tax-exempt rate published monthly by the IRS.²⁴

²² I.R.C. § 382(a) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 382].
²³ I.R.C. § 382(g) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 382(g)].
²⁴ I.R.C. § 382(b) [VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 382(b)].

⁵⁶ *See* Staff of the Joint Committee on Taxation, General Explanation of the Tax Reform Act of 1986, at 314-23 (1987) [VERIFIED: JCT Publication].

⁵⁸ Tax Structure Analyst Report, Section IV.E.2 [VERIFIED: specialist-reports/tax-structure-analyst-report.md].
