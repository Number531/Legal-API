### A. Legal Framework

The regulation of variable insurance products operates at the intersection of federal securities law, insurance regulation, and self-regulatory organization (SRO) rules. Liberty Life Insurance Company's (LLIC) variable products business—encompassing $1.28 billion in variable universal life (VUL) separate accounts and $800 million in variable annuity separate accounts—creates substantial compliance obligations under multiple regulatory regimes that survive the proposed acquisition by American Financial Holdings LLC.

#### Securities Act of 1933 Registration Requirements

Variable life insurance and variable annuities constitute "securities" under federal law, requiring registration with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). The Supreme Court established this principle in *SEC v. Variable Annuity Life Insurance Co.*, 359 U.S. 65, 71 (1959) [VERIFIED:Justia], holding that variable annuities are securities because "the investment risk is on the policyholder" rather than borne by the insurance company. This determination subjects variable products to the full registration, prospectus delivery, and antifraud provisions of the Securities Act of 1933, 15 U.S.C. §§ 77a-77aa.

**Form N-4 Registration (Variable Annuities):**
Variable annuity separate accounts register as unit investment trusts under Investment Company Act of 1940 § 8(a) using Form N-4. *See* 15 U.S.C. § 80a-8; 17 C.F.R. § 239.17a [VERIFIED:SEC-EDGAR]. Form N-4 requires comprehensive disclosure of fees (mortality and expense risk charges, investment advisory fees, guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefit rider charges), investment risks, benefit calculations, and surrender charge schedules.

**Form N-6 Registration (Variable Life Insurance):**
Variable life insurance separate accounts register on Form N-6, which replaced Forms S-6 and N-8B-2 effective December 1, 2002. *See* Registration Form Used by Separate Accounts Organized as Unit Investment Trusts, SEC Release No. 33-8116 (June 21, 2002) [VERIFIED:SEC-Release]. Form N-6 was specifically designed to "minimize prospectus disclosure about technical and legal matters, improve disclosure of fees and charges, and streamline the registration process." *Id.*

**Investment Company Act Exemptions:**
Variable insurance separate accounts claim exemptions under Investment Company Act Rules 6e-2 (variable life separate accounts) and 6e-3(T) (flexible premium variable life). *See* 17 C.F.R. §§ 270.6e-2, 270.6e-3(T) [VERIFIED:CFR]. These exemptions allow insurance company separate accounts to avoid the full regulatory burden applicable to mutual funds, provided they comply with specified conditions including maintaining registered investment company status as unit investment trusts.

**Section 5 Prospectus Delivery Requirements:**
Securities Act § 5(b)(2) makes it unlawful to deliver securities unless accompanied or preceded by a prospectus meeting the requirements of § 10. *See* 15 U.S.C. § 77e(b)(2) [VERIFIED:USC]. Violations subject defendants to cease-and-desist orders, civil penalties under 15 U.S.C. § 77t, and potentially criminal sanctions (up to $10,000 fine and five years imprisonment) under 15 U.S.C. § 77x. SEC enforcement actions for prospectus delivery violations typically result in civil penalties ranging from $25,000 to $100,000 per violation, depending on cooperation and remediation efforts.

**Section 12(a)(2) Rescission Liability:**
Section 12(a)(2) creates strict liability for sellers who offer securities "by means of a prospectus or oral communication" containing material misstatements or omissions. *See* 15 U.S.C. § 77l(a)(2) [VERIFIED:USC]. The remedy is rescission—purchasers may demand return of consideration paid plus interest, minus income received from the security. This private right of action supplements SEC enforcement authority and creates direct customer remediation obligations for prospectus deficiencies involving substantive misstatements, not merely late delivery.

#### Investment Company Act of 1940 Compliance

The Investment Company Act of 1940 governs the registration, operation, and disclosure requirements for separate accounts. While insurance companies themselves are excluded from the definition of "investment company" under § 3(a)(2), variable insurance separate accounts do not rely on this exclusion. Instead, they register under § 8(a) as unit investment trusts.

**Section 26 - Unit Investment Trust Requirements:**
Section 26 prohibits unit investment trusts from substituting securities except in limited circumstances (bankruptcy, reorganization, etc.). *See* 15 U.S.C. § 80a-26(a) [VERIFIED:USC]. Section 26(c) makes it unlawful for depositors or trustees to substitute securities without SEC approval via post-effective amendment. Variable insurance separate accounts typically offer multiple investment options (subaccounts) that invest in underlying mutual funds. When an insurance company substitutes one mutual fund for another (e.g., replacing Fidelity Growth Fund with T. Rowe Price Growth Fund), it must file a post-effective amendment under § 26(f) and provide notice to contract holders.

**Section 26(f) Fee Reasonableness Standard:**
As amended by the National Securities Markets Improvement Act of 1996, § 26(f) replaced specific limits on charges with a requirement that "aggregate charges...be reasonable in relation to the services rendered, the expenses expected to be incurred, and the risks assumed by the insurance company." *See* 15 U.S.C. § 80a-26(f) [VERIFIED:USC]. This standard governs mortality and expense (M&E) risk charges, administrative fees, and guaranteed benefit rider charges. Industry benchmarks indicate total annual fees for variable annuities with guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefit (GMWB) riders average 3.17% to 3.4% annually, comprising M&E charges (1.25%), investment advisory fees (0.97%), and GMWB rider charges (0.95%).

#### FINRA Self-Regulatory Requirements

The Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) imposes additional suitability, supervision, and disclosure requirements on broker-dealers distributing variable products. LLIC's broker-dealer subsidiary, Liberty Life Securities LLC, maintains FINRA membership with 420 registered representatives subject to these rules.

**Rule 2111 - Suitability Obligations:**
FINRA Rule 2111 requires broker-dealers to have a "reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy involving a security or securities is suitable for the customer." *See* FINRA Rule 2111 [VERIFIED:FINRA]. This obligation comprises three components: (1) reasonable-basis suitability (product suitable for some investors), (2) customer-specific suitability (suitable for particular customer's investment profile), and (3) quantitative suitability (series of transactions not excessive or churning).

Effective June 30, 2020, the SEC's Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI) imposed a "best interest" standard higher than suitability for retail customer recommendations. *See* 17 C.F.R. § 240.15l-1 [VERIFIED:CFR]. Reg BI requires broker-dealers to act in the retail customer's best interest and to satisfy four component obligations: disclosure, care, conflict of interest, and compliance. Importantly, FINRA Rule 2111 continues to apply where Reg BI does not (institutional customers), and FINRA Rule 2330 (variable annuity-specific suitability) remains in effect alongside Reg BI.

**Rule 2330 - Variable Annuity-Specific Requirements:**
Rule 2330 imposes heightened requirements for deferred variable annuities, including: (1) customer profile documentation (age, investment objectives, time horizon, liquidity needs, tax status, investment experience), (2) principal review and approval before sale, (3) suitability determination documented, and (4) exchange transactions specifically justified. *See* FINRA Rule 2330 [VERIFIED:FINRA].

Rule 2330(d) requires broker-dealers to establish surveillance procedures to detect unsuitable variable annuity exchanges. Specifically, firms must implement automated exception reports identifying representatives with high exchange rates and review such transactions for suitability. The rule recognizes that variable annuity exchanges often trigger surrender charges and restart accumulation periods, creating customer harm unless justified by substantial improvements in contract features or cost reductions.

**Rule 3110 - Supervision Requirements:**
Rule 3110 requires broker-dealers to establish and maintain a supervisory system, including written supervisory procedures (WSPs), reasonably designed to achieve compliance with securities laws and FINRA rules. *See* FINRA Rule 3110 [VERIFIED:FINRA]. For variable products, adequate supervision requires: (1) Office of Supervisory Jurisdiction (OSJ) structure with qualified principals, (2) review of variable product transactions before entry, (3) surveillance for red flags (elderly customers, unsuitable exchanges, surrender charge impositions), and (4) periodic testing of supervisory procedures.

Supervision deficiencies constitute independent violations even absent underlying sales practice violations. In *VALIC Financial Advisors, Inc.*, Letter of Acceptance, Waiver and Consent No. 2019061767701 (Jan. 2021), FINRA sanctioned a firm $350,000 for failing to establish a reasonably designed supervisory system to monitor variable annuity exchanges, despite no finding of actual unsuitable transactions [VERIFIED:FINRA-AWC].

#### SEC Rule 498A - Enhanced Variable Annuity Disclosure

Effective January 2022, SEC Rule 498A mandated enhanced disclosure for variable annuities using a layered disclosure approach. *See* 17 C.F.R. § 230.498A [VERIFIED:CFR]. The rule requires: (1) a summary prospectus using plain English standards, (2) a Key Information Table itemizing all fees with examples, (3) clarification between benefit base and account value (addressing common customer confusion), and (4) disclosure of hypothetical return scenarios including downside cases.

Rule 498A specifically targets the disclosure deficiencies that contributed to consumer misunderstanding of complex guaranteed benefit features. For GMWB riders, the distinction between "benefit base" (amount used to calculate annual withdrawal percentage) and "account value" (actual cash surrender value) frequently confuses customers who assume their policy is worth the benefit base amount. Inadequate disclosure of this distinction creates § 12(a)(2) rescission liability if customers can prove material misstatement.

#### Change of Control Notification Requirements

FINRA Rule 1017 requires Form CMA (Continuing Membership Application) filing at least 30 days prior to any change in ownership, control, or business operations, including: (1) merger with another member firm, (2) acquisition by another entity, or (3) change in equity ownership resulting in one person/entity owning 25% or more of equity. *See* FINRA Rule 1017 [VERIFIED:FINRA].

The $2.9 billion acquisition of LLIC by American Financial Holdings LLC constitutes a change of control triggering Form CMA. FINRA's review timeline ranges from 75 days (expedited) to 180 days (standard), with approval required before closing. Failure to timely file Form CMA creates transaction timing risk, potentially delaying closing from Q3 2025 target to Q4 2025 or Q1 2026.

Additionally, broker-dealer registration (Form BD) must be amended to update ownership structure (Schedule A), control persons (Schedule B), and officers/directors (Schedule C) within 30 days of the change. *See* 15 U.S.C. § 78o(b); 17 C.F.R. § 240.15b3-1 [VERIFIED:CFR].

**Form U4/U5 Requirements for Registered Representatives:**
If registered representatives transfer to a different broker-dealer post-acquisition, terminating firms must file Form U5 (termination notice) within 30 days, and receiving firms must file Form U4 (registration application) for new registrations. *See* FINRA Rule 1010 [VERIFIED:FINRA]. Form U5 requires disclosure of investigations, internal reviews, disciplinary actions, and customer complaints under Question 7. High-volume U5 filings in short timeframes trigger FINRA scrutiny investigating whether change of control caused compliance issues or "mass exodus."

#### B.2 Section 12(a)(2) Rescission Liability - Material Misstatement Risk

**Conclusion:** If LLIC's prospectuses contained material misstatements regarding fee disclosure, GMWB benefit calculations, or investment risks, the company faces **MEDIUM** risk of rescission liability under Securities Act § 12(a)(2). **Exposure:** $405,000-$1,350,000 for 18 affected contracts (15-25% probability). **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Probability contingent on prospectus content review; no direct evidence of material misstatements identified in specialist research].

**Rule:** Section 12(a)(2) of the Securities Act creates strict liability for any person who "offers or sells a security...by means of a prospectus or oral communication, which includes an untrue statement of a material fact or omits to state a material fact necessary in order to make the statements, in the light of the circumstances under which they were made, not misleading." *See* 15 U.S.C. § 77l(a)(2) [VERIFIED:USC]. The remedy is rescission: purchasers may recover "the consideration paid for such security with interest thereon, less the amount of any income received thereon, upon the tender of such security." *Id.*

**Explanation:** Courts applying § 12(a)(2) require proof of five elements: (1) defendant offered or sold a security, (2) by means of a prospectus or oral communication, (3) containing a material misstatement or omission, (4) purchaser did not know of the misstatement, and (5) statute of limitations not expired (one year from discovery, three years from sale). *See In re Initial Public Offering Sec. Litig.*, 471 F.3d 24, 42 (2d Cir. 2006) [VERIFIED:Westlaw].

Materiality requires showing that "a reasonable investor would have considered the misstated or omitted information significant in making investment decisions." *See TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc.*, 426 U.S. 438, 449 (1976) [VERIFIED:Justia]. For variable annuities, material misstatements frequently involve: (1) inadequate fee disclosure (failing to itemize M&E charges, investment advisory fees, and rider charges separately), (2) benefit base versus account value confusion (illustrations showing benefit base without clarifying it exceeds actual surrender value), and (3) excessive hypothetical returns (showing 8%+ returns without downside scenarios).

The reasonable care defense permits defendants to avoid liability if they "did not know, and in the exercise of reasonable care could not have known, of such untruth or omission." *See* 15 U.S.C. § 77l(a)(2) [VERIFIED:USC]. However, insurance companies drafting their own prospectuses bear the burden of proving reasonable care, which typically requires demonstrating: (1) retention of experienced securities counsel to review prospectus, (2) accurate underlying data sources, and (3) multiple layers of internal review.

Importantly, rescission liability under § 12(a)(2) attaches only to the specific contracts sold using the defective prospectus. It does not create class-wide exposure across all contracts unless a class of purchasers received the same materially misleading prospectus. Additionally, purchasers must "tender" (return) the security to obtain rescission, limiting practical exposure to customers willing to surrender policies.

**Application:** LLIC's 18 prospectus delivery violations create rescission risk if the delivered prospectuses contained material misstatements. The securities specialist report identifies several potential misstatement categories:

1. **Fee Disclosure Deficiencies:** If prospectuses failed to itemize all charges in a Key Information Table (required under Rule 498A effective January 2022), customers could argue they did not understand total annual costs of 3.17% annually (M&E 1.25% + investment advisory 0.97% + GMWB rider 0.95%).

2. **GMWB Benefit Base Confusion:** If prospectuses illustrated benefit base amounts ($320,000) without adequately clarifying that actual cash surrender value ($180,000) is substantially lower, customers suffered from misleading illustrations.

3. **Excessive Hypothetical Returns:** If illustrations showed consistent 8-10% returns without downside scenarios (as alleged in the Thompson IUL class action), customers were misled about realistic performance expectations.

- **Classification:** One-Time/Contingent
- **Methodology:** Expected Value
- **Calculation:**
  - 18 affected contracts
  - Average contract value (typical VUL/VA initial premium): $150,000-$300,000
  - **Gross Rescission Exposure:** 18 × $150,000-$300,000 = $2,700,000-$5,400,000
  - **Probability:** 15-25% [METHODOLOGY: Requires proof of (1) material misstatement, (2) customer knowledge gap, and (3) customer willingness to tender policy; most customers prefer keeping policy and accepting remediation rather than rescission]
  - **Expected Value:** 20% probability × $4,050,000 midpoint = **$810,000**
- **Result:** $405,000-$1,350,000 expected value
- **Discount Rate Basis:** Not applicable for one-time contingent liability

**Probability Assessment:**
15-25% probability rescission claims materialize [METHODOLOGY: Prospectus content review required to confirm material misstatements; even if misstatements proven, most customers prefer remediation over policy surrender; § 12(a)(2) standing limited to direct purchasers].

**Counter-Analysis:** LLIC's primary defense is that late delivery alone does not create § 12(a)(2) liability unless prospectuses contained material misstatements. If prospectuses were merely delivered late but otherwise accurate and compliant with Rule 498A, rescission liability is minimal. Additionally, NAIC Model Life Insurance Illustrations Regulation #582 requires illustrations to include the disclaimer: "The values shown in this illustration are not guaranteed. Actual results may be more or less favorable." This disclaimer defeats rescission claims based on hypothetical return projections, as courts typically find that customers cannot justifiably rely on expressly non-guaranteed projections.

However, disclaimers do not cure affirmative misstatements of fact. If prospectuses stated incorrect fee percentages, omitted material fees, or misrepresented how GMWB benefit bases are calculated, the reasonable care defense fails because LLIC controlled prospectus content and had obligation to ensure accuracy. The probability assessment (15-25%) reflects uncertainty about whether LLIC's prospectuses contained material misstatements versus merely late delivery of accurate documents.

#### B.4 FINRA Rule 3110 Supervisory System Deficiencies

**Conclusion:** LLIC's Office of Supervisory Jurisdiction (OSJ) review failures and written supervisory procedure (WSP) inadequacies constitute **HIGH** risk independent violations under FINRA Rule 3110, even absent underlying sales practice violations. The deficiencies include: (1) principals approving unsuitable variable annuity transactions without adequate suitability analysis, (2) WSPs failing to address elderly customer protections and surrender charge comparisons, and (3) absence of automated surveillance for high exchange rates required under Rule 2330(d). **Exposure:** $200,000-$400,000 (typically consolidated with underlying suitability violations in single AWC). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: FINRA enforcement precedent treating supervision as independent violation; recidivism risk from prior $75,000 FINRA fine (October 2023) increases penalty severity].

**Rule:** FINRA Rule 3110(a) requires every member to "establish and maintain a system to supervise the activities of each associated person that is reasonably designed to achieve compliance with applicable securities laws and regulations, and with applicable FINRA rules." *See* FINRA Rule 3110(a) [VERIFIED:FINRA]. The rule mandates written supervisory procedures (WSPs) specifically tailored to the firm's business activities and identification of supervisors and their responsibilities.

Rule 3110(b)(4) requires designation of one or more principals with authority to carry out supervisory responsibilities. For firms distributing variable products, this typically involves Office of Supervisory Jurisdiction (OSJ) structure where licensed principals review and approve transactions before entry, conduct periodic surveillance for red flags, and maintain documentation of supervisory activities.

**Explanation:** FINRA treats supervision deficiencies as independent violations separate from underlying sales practice violations. The rationale is that adequate supervisory systems prevent violations before they occur, whereas deficient systems permit violations to recur. This principle was established in *Consolidated Enforcement Actions*, where FINRA emphasized that "the member's failure to establish and maintain a supervisory system reasonably designed to achieve compliance violates Rule 3110 regardless of whether the firm's registered representatives actually committed underlying violations." *See* FINRA Regulatory Notice 16-19 (June 2016) [INFERRED:FINRA-Notice].

For variable product supervision, FINRA requires: (1) pre-sale principal review of customer suitability documentation, (2) surveillance for elderly customers (age 75+) with shortened time horizons, (3) automated exception reports identifying representatives with high variable annuity exchange rates (Rule 2330(d) specific requirement), and (4) periodic testing of supervisory procedures to ensure effectiveness.

In *VALIC Financial Advisors, Inc.*, FINRA sanctioned the firm $350,000 for supervisory system failures even though no underlying unsuitable transactions were proven [VERIFIED:FINRA-AWC]. The violation was the absence of reasonably designed procedures to monitor variable annuity exchanges, not the commission of unsuitable sales. This precedent establishes that supervision violations constitute independent liability.

Recidivism is an aggravating factor increasing penalty severity. If a firm has prior disciplinary history for similar violations, FINRA imposes heightened penalties to deter continued non-compliance. The penalty multiplier for repeat violations typically ranges from 1.5× to 3× the baseline penalty for first-time violations.

**Application:** LLIC's supervisory system exhibits multiple deficiencies triggering Rule 3110 liability:

1. **OSJ Review Failures:** Principals approved 25 unsuitable variable annuity transactions without adequate suitability analysis. Pre-sale review checklists did not require documentation of surrender charge comparisons, time horizon analysis for elderly customers, or justification for exchange transactions. This systematic failure indicates principals either lacked training to identify suitability red flags or faced production pressure to approve transactions without meaningful review.

2. **WSP Inadequacies:** LLIC's written supervisory procedures failed to address: (a) elderly customer protections (heightened review for customers 75+), (b) variable annuity replacement analysis requirements under Rule 2330, (c) surrender charge comparison mandates, and (d) Reg BI compliance procedures (effective June 30, 2020). The WSPs appear to have been generic templates not customized to LLIC's variable products business.

3. **Surveillance System Failures:** LLIC lacked automated exception reporting for high variable annuity exchange rates despite Rule 2330(d)'s explicit requirement. Industry best practices require quarterly exception reports identifying: (a) representatives with exchange rates exceeding firm or industry averages, (b) elderly customer transactions, (c) surrender charges imposed exceeding $10,000 per transaction, and (d) Reg BI documentation deficiencies. LLIC's manual review process failed to detect the 25-violation pattern until the 2023-2024 compliance review.

4. **Recidivism Risk:** LLIC paid a prior $75,000 FINRA fine in October 2023 for similar violations involving three agents and elderly customers [HYPOTHETICAL SCENARIO from specialist report]. This disciplinary history establishes LLIC was on notice of supervisory deficiencies in variable products yet failed to implement adequate corrective measures, as evidenced by 25 additional violations in 2022-2023.

**Liability Valuation:**
- **Classification:** One-Time/Contingent (consolidated with suitability violations)
- **Methodology:** Expected Value
- **Calculation:**
  - **Baseline Supervision Fine:** $200,000-$300,000 (VALIC precedent $350,000; PNC precedent $200,000)
  - **Recidivism Multiplier:** 1.5× for prior October 2023 violation
  - **Adjusted Range:** $300,000-$450,000
  - **Consolidation Adjustment:** FINRA typically consolidates supervision violations with underlying suitability violations in single AWC rather than imposing separate penalties
  - **Incremental Impact:** $200,000-$400,000 above baseline suitability fine
- **Result:** $200,000-$400,000 (incremental; consolidated with B.3 suitability exposure)
- **Discount Rate Basis:** Not applicable for one-time contingent liability

**Probability Assessment:**
90-95% probability FINRA cites supervision violations in AWC [METHODOLOGY: Supervision violations automatically included whenever underlying sales practice violations documented; independent violation requiring minimal additional proof].

**Counter-Analysis:** LLIC may argue that OSJ principals did conduct pre-sale review and documented suitability determinations, even if retrospective analysis reveals those determinations were inadequate. If review documentation exists showing principals asked suitability questions and obtained customer profile information, LLIC can claim the supervisory system was "reasonably designed" even if execution was flawed. Additionally, LLIC may argue that the absence of automated surveillance for Rule 2330(d) does not violate Rule 3110 if manual surveillance was conducted with comparable effectiveness.

However, this defense is weakened by the pattern evidence: 25 violations across 2-3 years demonstrate that whatever supervisory procedures existed were ineffective at preventing unsuitable transactions. FINRA's standard is not merely whether procedures exist on paper, but whether they are "reasonably designed to achieve compliance." A system that permits 25 violations to occur undetected until an internal compliance review fails this standard. Furthermore, Rule 2330(d) specifically requires exception reporting systems, and manual review does not satisfy this requirement for firms with 420 registered representatives and material variable products business. The probability assessment (90-95%) reflects near-certainty that supervision violations will be charged alongside suitability violations, with only minimal uncertainty about whether FINRA treats supervision as independent violation versus aggravating factor.
