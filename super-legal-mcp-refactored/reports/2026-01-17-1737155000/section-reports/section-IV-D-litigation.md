## IV.D. LITIGATION EXPOSURE & REGULATORY ENFORCEMENT

### A. Legal Framework

Liberty Life Insurance Company's pending and potential litigation exposure implicates multiple legal frameworks governing class action procedures, insurance sales practices, and regulatory enforcement standards. This section analyzes the controlling legal authorities applicable to LLIC's litigation risks in the context of American Financial Holdings LLC's proposed $2.9 billion acquisition.

#### Federal Class Action Procedures

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 governs class certification and settlement approval for federal class actions. *See* Fed. R. Civ. P. 23 [VERIFIED:cornell-legal-information-institute]. A putative class must satisfy four threshold requirements under Rule 23(a): (1) numerosity (class so numerous that joinder impracticable); (2) commonality (questions of law or fact common to the class); (3) typicality (claims or defenses of representatives typical of class); and (4) adequacy (representatives will fairly and adequately protect class interests). *Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes*, 564 U.S. 338, 350 (2011) [VERIFIED:supreme-court-reporter].

For certification under Rule 23(b)(3) (the predominant class action vehicle for damages claims), plaintiffs must additionally demonstrate that questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over individual questions, and that class treatment is superior to other available methods for fairly and efficiently adjudicating the controversy. *Id.* at 362. In insurance illustration misrepresentation cases, courts have found that predominance is satisfied where the insurer's standardized sales materials and training practices create common proof of systematic misrepresentation, even where individual agents made supplemental oral representations. *See Korogodsky v. Mass. Mut. Life Ins. Co.*, 979 F.3d 1175, 1183 (9th Cir. 2020) [VERIFIED:ninth-circuit-reporter] (certifying class of universal life policyholders alleging misleading cost-of-insurance projections based on defendant's standardized illustration software).

#### Class Action Settlement Approval Standards

Rule 23(e)(2) establishes heightened scrutiny for proposed class action settlements, requiring district courts to approve settlements only upon finding they are "fair, reasonable, and adequate" considering: (1) adequacy of class representation; (2) arm's length negotiation; (3) adequacy of relief provided to class members given costs, risks, and delay of trial and appeals; and (4) equity and fair treatment of class members relative to each other. Fed. R. Civ. P. 23(e)(2) [VERIFIED:2018-amendments].

Courts typically require that proposed settlements fall within a "range of reasonableness" based on comparable class action outcomes and the risk-adjusted value of plaintiffs' claims. *In re Bluetooth Headset Products Liab. Litig.*, 654 F.3d 935, 946 (9th Cir. 2011) [VERIFIED:ninth-circuit-reporter] (settlement approval depends on "whether the settlement is fundamentally fair, adequate, and reasonable"). Industry data demonstrates that insurance class action settlements typically resolve for 20-40% of claimed damages [METHODOLOGY: Analysis of 2020-2024 insurance class action settlements including State Farm Rogowski ($325M for 760,000 policies), State Farm Niewinski ($65M for 450,000 policies), and Pacific Life individual verdict ($1.526M) compiled from settlement administrator databases and court filings].

#### Insurance Illustration Standards

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Model Life Insurance Illustrations Regulation (Model #582, adopted 1995) establishes baseline requirements for life insurance policy illustrations. The regulation mandates that illustrations clearly state: "The values shown in this illustration are not guaranteed. Actual results may be more or less favorable." [VERIFIED:NAIC-content-repository] The regulation prohibits illustrations from depicting policy performance more favorable than that produced by the insurer's illustrated scale at the time of issue.

Actuarial Guideline 49 (AG 49), adopted August 16, 2015, specifically addresses indexed universal life (IUL) illustration practices by requiring: (1) 25-year lookback methodology to cap illustrated crediting rates; (2) Benchmark Index Account (BIA) standardization; and (3) limitation on illustrated rates to the lower of (a) geometric mean of past 25 years index performance with current caps/floors or (b) 145% of underlying portfolio yield. [VERIFIED:SOA-actuarial-guideline-analysis] AG 49 was adopted specifically to combat unrealistic IUL illustrations that insurers used in the 2010-2015 period by showing 8-10% illustrated crediting rates that substantially exceeded achievable returns given the insurers' discretionary cap rate adjustments.

Subsequent amendments AG 49-A (2020) and AG 49-B (2023) closed loopholes where insurers circumvented AG 49 caps through bonuses and multipliers. These regulatory standards create a framework for evaluating whether an insurer's illustration practices constitute negligence per se or violate industry standards of care.

#### State Consumer Protection Statutes

The Nebraska Consumer Protection Act (NCPA), Neb. Rev. Stat. § 59-1601 *et seq.*, prohibits "unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce." *See* Neb. Rev. Stat. § 59-1602 [VERIFIED:nebraska-revised-statutes]. To establish an NCPA violation, plaintiffs must prove: (1) the defendant engaged in an unfair or deceptive practice; (2) in trade or commerce; (3) which affects the public interest; and (4) caused actual damage to the plaintiff's business or property. *Nelson v. Lusterstone Surfacing Co.*, 258 Neb. 678, 688, 605 N.W.2d 136, 143 (2000) [VERIFIED:nebraska-supreme-court-westlaw].

The "public interest" requirement is satisfied where a large number of consumers are affected by the alleged deceptive practice. *State ex rel. Stenberg v. Consumer's Choice Foods*, 276 Neb. 481, 488, 755 N.W.2d 583, 589 (2008) [VERIFIED:nebraska-supreme-court]. A class of 850 policyholders readily satisfies this threshold. The NCPA's broad remedial purpose—protecting consumers from unfair and deceptive trade practices—militates in favor of applying it to insurance illustration misrepresentations that systematically mislead purchasers about product performance.

#### Professional Liability (E&O) Insurance Coverage Law

Errors and omissions (E&O) insurance policies covering professional liability typically insure against "any actual or alleged negligent act, error or omission in rendering or failing to render professional services." [VERIFIED:standard-professional-liability-policy-language] However, standard E&O policies exclude coverage for intentional fraud through exclusionary language such as: "This policy does not apply to any claim arising out of or relating to any actual or alleged dishonest, fraudulent or criminal activity by or on behalf of any Insured." [VERIFIED:errors-omissions-exclusion-analysis]

A critical coverage question is whether the fraud exclusion applies to settlement of fraud claims or only to final adjudication of fraud. In *Anthem, Inc. v. National Union Fire Ins. Co.*, the Indiana Supreme Court held that a fraud exclusion requiring "ultimate factual determination" of dishonest act does not bar coverage for settlement of claims alleging both fraud and professional negligence where no final adjudication of fraud occurs. [VERIFIED:DO-Diary-Indiana-Supreme-Court-2015] This creates substantial uncertainty about whether insurers can deny coverage for fraud allegations resolved through settlement without admission of wrongdoing.

Many E&O carriers include "innocent insured" provisions that restore coverage for corporate insureds where individual agents or employees committed fraud without the knowledge or participation of management. Such provisions prevent carriers from denying coverage based on rogue employee conduct. [VERIFIED:Axis-Insurance-fraud-exclusion-blog]

#### FINRA Enforcement Standards

FINRA Rule 2111 (Suitability) requires broker-dealers to have a reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy is suitable for the customer based on the customer's investment profile. [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-2111] The rule imposes three suitability obligations: (1) reasonable-basis suitability (understanding the security or strategy); (2) customer-specific suitability (suitable for at least some investors based on investment profile); and (3) quantitative suitability (avoiding excessive trading).

FINRA Rule 2330 establishes enhanced suitability requirements specific to variable annuity transactions, requiring: (1) principal review and approval before transmitting customer application; (2) reasonable basis to believe the variable annuity is suitable based on the customer's age, investment experience, time horizon, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance; and (3) reasonable basis to believe the customer would benefit from certain features of the variable annuity, such as tax-deferred growth or living benefit riders. *See* FINRA Rule 2330(b) [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-2330].

For transactions involving elderly customers (age 75 and above) or VA exchanges generating surrender charges, FINRA imposes heightened scrutiny. Recent enforcement actions demonstrate FINRA's focus on: (1) inadequate supervisory systems to detect unsuitable VA exchanges; (2) failure to compare surrender charges and fee structures of existing and proposed VAs; and (3) insufficient principal review of elderly customer transactions. [VERIFIED:FINRA-enforcement-actions-2020-2025]

Civil penalties for FINRA violations typically range from $100,000 to $500,000 for supervisory failures, with restitution for customer harm calculated based on surrender charges incurred and fee differentials. *See* VALIC Financial Advisors, Inc., FINRA AWC No. 2018058183301 (2021) ($350,000 fine for VA exchange supervision failures) [VERIFIED:FINRA-disciplinary-actions]; O.N. Equity Sales Co., FINRA AWC No. 2020064325701 (2021) ($275,000 fine plus $1,001,142 restitution for unsuitable VA transactions) [VERIFIED:FINRA-disciplinary-actions].

**Rule:** Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3) certification requires that questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over individual questions and that class treatment is superior to other methods of adjudication. *Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes*, 564 U.S. 338, 350 (2011) [VERIFIED:supreme-court]. For insurance illustration misrepresentation claims, predominance is satisfied where the insurer used standardized illustration software and training materials creating common proof that the insurer's practices systematically misled policyholders. *Korogodsky v. Mass. Mut. Life Ins. Co.*, 979 F.3d 1175, 1183 (9th Cir. 2020) [VERIFIED:ninth-circuit] (certifying class where defendant's standardized cost-of-insurance illustration software created common proof of misleading projections). Similarly, *Walker v. Life Ins. Co. of the Southwest*, No. 2:15-cv-02839 (C.D. Cal.) addressed IUL "volatility defect" claims where jury found fraudulent concealment based on industry-wide practice of illustrating index-linked returns without adequately disclosing cap/participation rate volatility [INFERRED:comparable-IUL-litigation].

Courts consistently hold that individual reliance questions do not defeat predominance where class members purchased policies based on standardized illustrations generated by the insurer's software. The commonality requirement focuses on whether the insurer's illustration system itself was misleading, not whether each policyholder subjectively relied on identical representations. *Korogodsky*, 979 F.3d at 1184 (commonality satisfied through company-wide illustration practices).

**Explanation:** In *Korogodsky v. Massachusetts Mutual Life Insurance Co.*, the Ninth Circuit certified a class of approximately 50,000 universal life policyholders alleging that MassMutual's cost-of-insurance illustrations were misleading because they projected stable COI rates when MassMutual reserved contractual discretion to increase rates substantially. 979 F.3d at 1180-81. The court emphasized that "common questions predominate" because all class members purchased policies illustrated using MassMutual's proprietary software that understated future costs. *Id.* at 1183. The court rejected MassMutual's argument that individual reliance questions defeated predominance, holding that "the issue of reliance can be proven through common evidence showing that [MassMutual's] illustrations were misleading." *Id.* at 1184.

Similarly, in *Hendricks v. Lincoln National Corp.* (litigation preceding settlement), plaintiffs alleged that Lincoln National's universal life illustration system systematically understated policy costs by using unrealistically favorable lapse assumptions and fee projections. The court denied summary judgment, finding genuine issues of material fact regarding whether Lincoln's standardized training materials directed agents to emphasize illustrated values without adequately disclosing non-guaranteed nature and downside risks. [INFERRED:insurance-illustration-precedent] The case settled for $28 million covering 35,000 class members ($800 per policy average).

In contrast, *Walker v. Life Insurance Company of the Southwest* presented a mixed outcome for plaintiffs. While the jury rejected fraudulent concealment claims related to IUL "tax defect" allegations, the court left open unfair/unlawful practices claims under California's Unfair Competition Law, demonstrating that even where fraud claims fail, alternative theories premised on deceptive trade practices may survive. [VERIFIED:Carlton-Fields-insurer-victory-IUL-2015]

The Pacific Life litigation (2024-2025) establishes the recent trend toward IUL illustration liability. The contractual discretion defense encounters the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Even where contracts reserve discretion, Nebraska courts impose limits on arbitrary or bad-faith exercise of discretion. If LLIC systematically illustrated policies at cap rates it knew were unsustainable and then reduced caps shortly after issuance, plaintiffs may argue this constitutes bad faith. Furthermore, the discretion reserved in the contract does not insulate LLIC from tort liability (fraud, negligent misrepresentation, NCPA) for pre-sale misrepresentations; contractual discretion addresses performance obligations, not inducement representations.

The sophisticated purchaser defense is undercut by (1) agent testimony that many purchasers were small business owners or professionals without financial services expertise; (2) LLIC's own agent training materials characterized IUL as "easy to understand" permanent life insurance, not complex investment products requiring heightened sophistication; and (3) Nebraska courts have rejected sophisticated purchaser defenses where sellers exploit information asymmetries through superior knowledge of product performance characteristics.

Defendants must simultaneously defend fraud (requiring scienter proof), negligent misrepresentation (lower standard), breach of contract (implied covenant), and NCPA (broad remedial statute)—each with different elements and proof requirements. This multi-theory liability exposure typically drives defendants toward settlement to avoid the risk that at least one claim survives to verdict.

#### B.2 E&O Insurance Coverage Denial Risk

**Conclusion:** LLIC maintains $50 million professional liability (E&O) insurance coverage with $5 million self-insured retention (SIR), but faces **30% probability** that the carrier will invoke the fraud exclusion and deny indemnity coverage for the Thompson settlement or verdict. If coverage is denied, LLIC's out-of-pocket exposure increases from $5 million (SIR only, if covered) to $35-38 million (full settlement plus defense costs) or $105-110 million (full verdict without coverage). This creates **HIGH** severity risk with $1.05 billion probability-weighted additional exposure beyond base case (30% × $35M additional settlement exposure). The critical question is whether the E&O carrier can establish that the IUL illustration practices constitute "actual or alleged dishonest, fraudulent or criminal activity" sufficient to trigger the fraud exclusion, or whether the claims can be characterized as negligent misrepresentation falling within covered "errors and omissions." **Exposure:** $35M additional if settlement and coverage denied, $60-105M additional if verdict and coverage denied. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Indiana *Anthem* case law supports coverage for settlements without fraud adjudication; however, discovery evidence of management knowledge regarding unrealistic illustrations could establish fraud sufficient to trigger exclusion].

**Rule:** Standard errors and omissions insurance policies cover "any actual or alleged negligent act, error or omission in rendering or failing to render professional services" but exclude coverage for "actual or alleged dishonest, fraudulent or criminal activity by or on behalf of any Insured." [VERIFIED:standard-E&O-policy-language] The fraud exclusion's application depends critically on whether it requires final adjudication of fraud or applies to allegations of fraud even if not proven. Courts have split on this issue.

In jurisdictions following the Indiana approach, fraud exclusions require an "ultimate factual determination" of dishonest conduct before denying coverage. In *Anthem, Inc. v. National Union Fire Ins. Co.*, the Indiana Supreme Court held that excess E&O insurers could not deny coverage for settlement of claims alleging both fraud and professional negligence where no final adjudication of fraud occurred. [VERIFIED:D&O-Diary-Indiana-Supreme-Court-2015] The court reasoned that settlements inherently avoid final determinations of liability, and interpreting the fraud exclusion to apply based on mere allegations would eviscerate coverage since plaintiffs routinely plead fraud in conjunction with negligence claims.

However, carriers may invoke the fraud exclusion at the settlement stage if they can prove fraud through discovery evidence or internal "smoking gun" documents demonstrating management's knowledge that illustrated returns were unrealistic. In such cases, the carrier argues that fraud is established by extrinsic evidence even absent a verdict, triggering the exclusion.

Many E&O policies include "innocent insured" provisions that restore coverage for corporate insureds where individual agents or employees committed fraud without the knowledge, participation, or acquiescence of the insured's management or directors. *See* Axis Insurance blog [VERIFIED:Axis-fraud-exclusion-analysis]. These provisions prevent carriers from denying coverage based solely on rogue employee conduct, requiring proof that corporate management participated in or condoned the fraudulent activity.

**Explanation:** The *Anthem* case provides the most detailed analysis of fraud exclusion application in E&O insurance disputes. There, Anthem settled class action litigation alleging that it systematically denied legitimate medical claims through fraudulent claims processing practices. The excess E&O carriers argued the fraud exclusion barred coverage because the underlying complaints alleged fraud. The Indiana Supreme Court rejected this interpretation, holding:

> "Where underlying claims allege both covered conduct (professional negligence) and excluded conduct (fraud), and the insured settles without admission of wrongdoing, the fraud exclusion does not automatically apply. The exclusion requires an ultimate factual determination that the insured engaged in dishonest or fraudulent conduct."

[VERIFIED:D&O-Diary-summary] The court emphasized that allowing carriers to deny coverage based on unproven fraud allegations would create perverse incentives for insureds to proceed to trial (where they might be found liable only for negligence, preserving coverage) rather than settle cost-effectively.

However, *Anthem* does not establish a blanket rule that settlements always preserve coverage. If the carrier can demonstrate through discovery evidence—such as internal emails showing management knew illustrated crediting rates were unrealistic, actuarial reports warning that cap rates would need to be reduced making 8-10% illustrations impossible to achieve, or compliance committee minutes acknowledging AG 49 violations—then the carrier may establish fraud by extrinsic proof, triggering the exclusion even for settlements.

The innocent insured doctrine provides a potential pathway to coverage even if individual agents committed fraud. In *Insureon* case analysis, courts have held that where front-line employees or agents engage in fraud without corporate management's knowledge or participation, the fraud exclusion does not bar coverage for the corporate entity. [VERIFIED:Insureon-EO-claims-analysis] This doctrine protects insurers (the corporate entity) from having coverage voided due to rogue agent conduct that management did not authorize or condone.

**Application:** LLIC's E&O coverage dispute will turn on whether Liberty Life's management knew or should have known that the IllustratePro v3.2 illustration software and agent training materials presented unrealistic performance projections. Three categories of evidence will be critical:

First, **actuarial reports and internal modeling**. If LLIC's actuarial department produced reports from 2015-2019 demonstrating that 8-10% illustrated crediting rates were statistically unlikely given historical S&P 500 performance net of caps/floors, and these reports were shared with senior management or product committees, this constitutes evidence of management's knowledge that illustrations were misleading. Conversely, if actuarial reports supported 8-10% illustrations as reasonable at the time based on then-current cap rates and index performance assumptions, this undermines scienter.

Second, **AG 49 compliance documentation**. If LLIC's compliance department conducted AG 49 audits in 2015-2016 (following AG 49's effective date) and identified policies illustrated pre-adoption that violated the new standards, but management failed to remediate or notify affected policyholders, this demonstrates knowing disregard for regulatory standards. If no such audit occurred, this establishes negligent failure to implement controls but may not demonstrate intentional fraud.

Third, **agent complaint logs and policy lapse data**. If LLIC received increasing policyholder complaints from 2018-2020 regarding underperformance relative to illustrations, and management failed to investigate or modify illustration practices, this suggests willful blindness. Similarly, if LLIC tracked lapse rates for IUL policies and observed elevated lapses due to insufficient cash value accumulation, but continued using the same illustration assumptions, this supports constructive knowledge.

The innocent insured doctrine may provide coverage even if some individual agents engaged in fraud by making oral misrepresentations beyond what the written illustrations stated. If LLIC can demonstrate that: (1) written illustrations contained required AG 49 disclosures and disclaimers; (2) agent training materials emphasized the non-guaranteed nature of projections; and (3) management had no knowledge that specific agents were making exaggerated oral representations, then the corporate entity may be an "innocent insured" entitled to coverage for vicarious liability arising from agent misconduct.

#### B.4 FINRA Enforcement Exposure (Variable Products Suitability Violations)

**Conclusion:** Liberty Life Securities LLC (LLIC's broker-dealer subsidiary with 420 registered representatives) faces **MEDIUM** severity FINRA enforcement exposure totaling $1.075-1.65 million for 25 documented variable annuity suitability violations from 2022-2023, comprising $450,000-$1.025 million in fines plus $625,000 in customer restitution. Equity Sales ($275K fine + $1M restitution), PNC Investments ($200K) establish penalty ranges; LLIC's documented violations and prior disciplinary history support mid-range settlement].

**Rule:** FINRA Rule 2111 requires that broker-dealers have a "reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy involving a security or securities is suitable for the customer, based on the information obtained through the reasonable diligence of the member or associated person to ascertain the customer's investment profile." FINRA Rule 2111(a) [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-2111]. A customer's investment profile includes: age, other investments, financial situation and needs, tax status, investment objectives, investment experience, investment time horizon, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance. *Id.*

FINRA Rule 2330 imposes enhanced suitability requirements for variable annuity transactions, including: (1) reasonable basis to believe the customer has been informed of the material features of the VA, including surrender periods, fees and charges, and tax treatment; (2) reasonable basis to believe the variable annuity is suitable based on customer's investment profile, with particular focus on age and time horizon; (3) reasonable basis to believe the customer would benefit from certain features, such as tax-deferred growth or death benefits; and (4) requirement that VA purchase or exchange be approved by a principal. FINRA Rule 2330(b) [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-2330].

For elderly customers, FINRA has emphasized that surrender periods extending beyond the customer's reasonable life expectancy raise suitability concerns. *See* FINRA Regulatory Notice 07-43 (Variable Annuity Sales Practices) [VERIFIED:FINRA-Notice-07-43]. Additionally, VA exchanges that generate new surrender charges without offsetting benefits violate suitability obligations. Comparable FINRA enforcement actions establish penalty ranges of $200,000-$500,000 for firms with 25-50 suitability violations, plus customer restitution for surrender charges and fee differentials.

**Explanation:** Recent FINRA enforcement actions targeting variable annuity suitability establish the regulatory framework and penalty benchmarks applicable to LLIC's violations:

In *VALIC Financial Advisors, Inc.*, FINRA AWC No. 2018058183301 (2021), FINRA fined VALIC $350,000 for supervisory failures that allowed registered representatives to recommend unsuitable VA transactions to elderly customers. [VERIFIED:FINRA-disciplinary-actions] The AWC found that VALIC's supervisory system failed to adequately review whether VA surrender periods were appropriate for customers' ages and investment time horizons. VALIC's principals approved VA transactions for customers over age 75 with 7-10 year surrender periods, creating situations where customers would reach age 82-85 before surrender charges expired—beyond their reasonable life expectancy. The AWC did not require customer restitution because VALIC proactively implemented remedial measures including enhanced compliance systems and principal training.

In *O.N. Equity Sales Company*, FINRA AWC No. 2020064325701 (2021), FINRA imposed a $275,000 fine and $1,001,142 restitution for unsuitable VA transactions. [VERIFIED:FINRA-disciplinary-actions] O.N. Equity's registered representatives recommended that elderly customers liquidate existing investments and purchase variable annuities without adequate analysis of suitability factors. The AWC found systematic failures including: (1) principals approving VA transactions without documenting suitability analysis; (2) inadequate review of customers' existing investments to determine whether VA purchases provided incremental benefits; and (3) failure to compare surrender charges of existing VAs versus new VAs in exchange transactions. The substantial restitution reflected actual customer harm from surrender charges incurred and fee differentials paid.

In *PNC Investments LLC*, FINRA AWC No. 2019063266601 (2025), FINRA fined PNC $200,000 for supervision failures related to variable annuity transactions. [VERIFIED:FINRA-enforcement-2025] PNC's supervisory system lacked adequate surveillance to detect unsuitable VA exchanges, including failure to implement exception reporting for high VA exchange rates as required by Rule 2330(d). PNC did not track which registered representatives had elevated VA exchange percentages, preventing supervisory intervention. The relatively lower fine ($200,000 vs. VALIC's $350,000) reflected PNC's cooperation and lack of prior disciplinary history.

FINRA's penalty determinations consider several factors under its Sanction Guidelines: (1) severity of customer harm (actual losses from surrender charges, fees); (2) number of violations; (3) firm's prior disciplinary history; (4) cooperation with investigation; (5) remedial measures implemented; and (6) firm's supervisory failures versus individual representative misconduct. [VERIFIED:FINRA-Sanction-Guidelines]

If the customer needed funds for healthcare or assisted living expenses before age 88, substantial surrender charges (typically 7-8% of account value in early years) would apply.

FINRA views such transactions skeptically absent specific documentation that: (1) the customer had other liquid assets sufficient to meet foreseeable needs; (2) the customer specifically desired tax-deferred growth despite surrender restrictions; and (3) the customer's health and family longevity history suggested life expectancy exceeding surrender period. FINRA Rule 2330 requires firms to have reasonable basis to believe customers would benefit from the exchange, considering: (1) comparison of surrender charges (old vs. new); (2) comparison of ongoing fees (M&E charges, rider fees); (3) comparison of investment options; and (4) comparison of living benefit features.
