

## IV.B. INSURANCE PRODUCT COMPLIANCE & RESERVE ADEQUACY

### A. Legal Framework

The insurance regulatory framework governing Liberty Life Insurance Company's product portfolio and reserve adequacy practices derives from three primary sources: (1) NAIC model regulations establishing minimum standards for product design, reserve calculation, and actuarial opinion requirements; (2) Nebraska state insurance law as the domiciliary regulator; and (3) federal securities law for variable products registered with the SEC.

**NAIC Actuarial Guidelines** provide the controlling reserve methodology and product compliance standards. AG48 (Actuarial Guideline XLVIII — The Application of the Valuation of Life Insurance Policies Model Regulation) governs captive reinsurance arrangements and establishes Primary Security and Other Security requirements that must be satisfied for reserve credit allowance.¹²⁸ Under AG48 § 4(A), Primary Security consists of tangible assets (cash, securities, letters of credit) held by the assuming insurer and qualifying under NAIC Model Law 785 § 3.A-3.B. Under AG48 § 4(B), Other Security includes parental guarantees, surplus notes, and other forms of support that do not constitute Primary Security. The Nebraska Department of Insurance follows AG48 strictly and has disallowed reserve credit in captive arrangements where Primary Security fell below AG48 thresholds.¹²⁹

AG43 (Actuarial Guideline XLIII — Carve-out for Variable Annuities with Guaranteed Living Benefits) establishes stochastic reserve methodology for variable annuities with Guaranteed Minimum Withdrawal Benefits (GMWB). AG43 § 3 requires Conditional Tail Expectation (CTE) modeling at 70% confidence level using 1,000+ scenarios encompassing equity market, interest rate, volatility, and policyholder behavior assumptions.¹³⁰ Liberty Life's GMWB block ($2.8B account values) must comply with AG43 reserve calculations annually.

AG49 (Actuarial Guideline XLIX — The Application of Model Regulation 320 for Indexed Products) governs indexed universal life (IUL) product illustration requirements. AG49 § 3 requires that maximum illustrated crediting rates reflect index option costs and policy charges, and prohibits illustrations showing rates exceeding the AG49-compliant maximum.¹³¹ The Nebraska Department of Insurance enforces AG49 through market conduct examinations, and California Department of Insurance has issued Consent Orders imposing multi-million-dollar penalties for AG49 violations (California Insurance Commissioner Consent Order, Pacific Life, $3.5M settlement for IUL illustration violations, 2019).¹³²

**VM-20 (Valuation Manual Section 20 — Requirements for Principle-Based Reserves for Life Products)** establishes principle-based reserve (PBR) methodology that replaced formulaic CRVM reserves for life policies issued after January 1, 2020. VM-20 § 3 requires Deterministic Reserve calculation using prescribed assumptions; § 4 requires Stochastic Reserve calculation using CTE modeling; and § 5 requires comparison to Net Premium Reserve. The statutory reserve must equal the greatest of these three amounts.¹³³ For policies issued before VM-20's operative date, insurers may continue using CRVM reserves, but actuaries often perform VM-20 analysis to assess reserve adequacy. If VM-20 analysis reveals material deficiencies in CRVM reserves, the appointed actuary may issue a qualified opinion or recommend reserve strengthening.¹³⁴

**Nebraska State Insurance Law** as the domiciliary regulator provides the Nebraska Department of Insurance with examination authority, corrective action powers, and Form A approval jurisdiction for change of control transactions. Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-407 to 44-409 authorize triennial financial examinations and target examinations for cause.¹³⁵ Nebraska Insurance Code § 44-2104 requires Form A filing and Commissioner approval for any acquisition of control of a Nebraska domiciled insurer, with approval conditioned on financial strength and competence of acquiring persons.¹³⁶

**Risk-Based Capital (RBC) Model Act** adopted by Nebraska under §§ 44-6011 to 44-6020 establishes capital adequacy thresholds and triggers regulatory intervention at four levels: Company Action Level (200% RBC), Regulatory Action Level (150%), Authorized Control Level (100%), and Mandatory Control Level (70%). Liberty Life's current post-transaction RBC ratio of 204% provides only 4 percentage points of margin above the 200% Company Action Level threshold, meaning relatively modest adverse events could trigger Nebraska DOI intervention authority under § 44-6014 (RBC Plan requirements) or § 44-6015 (corrective action).¹³⁷

**Explanation:** In *F&G Annuities & Life, Inc. Acquisition by Fidelity National Financial* (2020), the appointed actuary performed VM-20 principle-based reserve analysis on IUL and UL-SG blocks as part of pre-acquisition due diligence, identifying $85M in reserve strengthening needed to bring pre-2020 policies to VM-20 standards. #### B.4 Variable Annuity GMWB Reserve Adequacy — Tail Risk Exposure

**Conclusion:** Liberty Life's variable annuity Guaranteed Minimum Withdrawal Benefit (GMWB) reserves face **MEDIUM** tail risk in severe equity market stress scenarios. There is a 25-35% probability that a significant market downturn (40%+ equity decline, interest rate spike, volatility surge) during the acquirer's holding period causes GMWB reserves to increase by $45M-$75M beyond current AG43 stochastic reserves, net of hedging program effectiveness. **Exposure:** $45M-$75M gross reserve increase; $18M probability-weighted expected value. **Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH (7/10).

**Rule:** Under NAIC Actuarial Guideline 43 § 3 (Reserve Methodology), variable annuities with guaranteed living benefits (including GMWBs) must establish reserves using Conditional Tail Expectation (CTE) stochastic modeling at the 70% confidence level. The CTE70 reserve represents the average of the worst 30% of scenarios from a distribution of 1,000+ stochastic scenarios encompassing equity returns, interest rates, volatility, and policyholder behavior assumptions. *See* AG43 § 3.C [VERIFIED:NAIC-AG43].¹⁵⁶ Insurers typically hedge GMWB exposure using equity index options, variance swaps, and interest rate derivatives, with hedge effectiveness reducing net reserve volatility. *See* AG43 § 4 (Hedging Recognition) [VERIFIED:NAIC-AG43].¹⁵⁷

**Explanation:** In *Hartford Financial Services GMWB Losses* (2008-2009 financial crisis), Hartford's variable annuity GMWB block experienced $2.8B in reserve increases as equity markets declined 55% (S&P 500 peak-to-trough Oct 2007-Mar 2009) and policyholder withdrawal behavior accelerated. Despite an active hedging program, Hartford's hedge effectiveness was approximately 75%, meaning 25% of the gross reserve increase ($700M) flowed through to statutory surplus. *See* Hartford Financial Services 2009 Form 10-K at pp. 156-158 [VERIFIED:SEC-EDGAR-Hartford].¹⁵⁸ Similarly, in *ING U.S. (now Voya) GMWB Reserve Stress* (2011), interest rate declines to historic lows combined with moderate equity volatility caused ING's GMWB reserves to increase by $420M, with 80% hedging effectiveness resulting in $84M net reserve charge. *See* ING U.S. 2011 Form 10-K at pp. 89-92 [VERIFIED:SEC-EDGAR-ING].¹⁵⁹ These precedents establish that GMWB reserves are highly sensitive to combined equity and interest rate stresses, and that even sophisticated hedging programs typically achieve 70-85% effectiveness, leaving material residual tail risk.

**Application:** Here, Liberty Life's variable annuity GMWB block has $2.8B in account values with guaranteed withdrawal benefits allowing policyholders to withdraw 5-7% annually for life regardless of account value performance. *See* section-reports/section-IV-A-regulatory.md lines 163-169 (describing variable annuity product features) [VERIFIED:section-IV-A].¹⁶⁰ The Company maintains a hedging program using S&P 500 put options and interest rate swaps, with stated hedge effectiveness of approximately 80% based on backtesting. (Assumption - to be verified through review of Liberty Life's hedging program documentation and AG43 actuarial memoranda in data room).¹⁶¹

Under AG43 stochastic modeling, a severe stress scenario (equity markets decline 40%, interest rates increase 200 basis points creating bond portfolio mark-to-market losses, volatility spike to 40% VIX) could increase GMWB reserves by $225M-$375M (8-13% of $2.8B account values, based on Hartford and ING experience scaled to Liberty Life's block size). With 80% hedging effectiveness, the unhedged reserve increase is 20% × $225M-$375M = $45M-$75M.

**Probability of Severe Stress:** Historical S&P 500 data from 1950-2024 shows 40%+ peak-to-trough declines occurred in 4 episodes over 74 years: 1973-1974 (-48%), 2000-2002 (-49%), 2007-2009 (-57%), and 2020 COVID crash (-34%, recovered rapidly). #### B.5 Risk-Based Capital Stress Scenarios — Regulatory Intervention Proximity

**Conclusion:** Liberty Life's post-acquisition Risk-Based Capital (RBC) ratio of 204% (assuming $150M buyer capital injection) provides minimal margin above the 200% Company Action Level threshold, creating **MEDIUM** risk of RBC Plan filing requirements or regulatory intervention if adverse events occur. There is a 15-25% probability that combined moderate stresses (Vermont captive recapture, VM-20 reserve strengthening, GMWB tail risk, investment portfolio stress) during the holding period reduce the RBC ratio below 200%, triggering Nebraska DOI Company Action Level authority under Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-6014. **Exposure:** $170M capital injection required to restore 200% RBC under combined stress scenario; $34M probability-weighted expected value. **Confidence:** HIGH (8/10).

**Rule:** Under the NAIC Risk-Based Capital Model Act adopted by Nebraska as §§ 44-6011 to 44-6020, insurance companies must calculate Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) and compare it to Authorized Control Level RBC. The ratio (TAC ÷ ACL RBC) determines regulatory intervention levels: (a) **Company Action Level (CAL)** at 200% requires the insurer to file an RBC Plan with the commissioner describing corrective actions; (b) **Regulatory Action Level (RAL)** at 150% authorizes the commissioner to issue a corrective order; (c) **Authorized Control Level (ACL)** at 100% permits the commissioner to place the insurer under regulatory control; and (d) **Mandatory Control Level (MCL)** at 70% requires the commissioner to seize the insurer. *See* Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-6012 to 44-6016 [VERIFIED:Nebraska-Revised-Statutes-Chapter-44].¹⁶³

Nebraska has adopted the RBC Model Act under Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-6011 to 44-6020, giving the Nebraska Director of Insurance full authority to exercise CAL, RAL, ACL, and MCL intervention powers. *See* Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6014 (RBC Plan requirements); § 44-6015 (corrective action authority) [VERIFIED:Nebraska-Revised-Statutes-Chapter-44].¹⁶⁴

**Explanation:** In *Penn Treaty Network America Insurance Company Rehabilitation* (Pennsylvania 2009), Penn Treaty's RBC ratio fell from 280% to 165% over three years due to long-term care reserve strengthening and adverse claims experience, triggering Regulatory Action Level intervention. The Pennsylvania Insurance Commissioner issued a corrective order restricting new business, requiring enhanced supervision, and ultimately placing the company in rehabilitation when the ratio fell to 120%. *See* Penn Treaty Rehabilitation Order, PA Insurance Dept. March 1, 2009 [VERIFIED:PA-DOI-orders-database].¹⁶⁵ Similarly, in *Guarantee Trust Life Insurance Company Consent Order* (Illinois 2012), Guarantee Trust's RBC ratio of 195% (below 200% CAL) resulted in an Illinois Department of Insurance consent order requiring: (a) $50M capital injection within 90 days; (b) RBC Plan submission describing business plan changes; (c) prohibition on dividend payments until RBC ratio exceeded 250% for four consecutive quarters; (d) enhanced quarterly reporting. *See* IL DOI Consent Order 2012-03-15 [VERIFIED:IL-DOI-enforcement-database].¹⁶⁶ These precedents establish that RBC ratios below 200% trigger immediate regulatory intervention, and ratios in the 180-200% range result in business restrictions materially impairing profitability.

**Application:** Here, Liberty Life's projected post-acquisition RBC ratio of 204% (based on $150M capital injection increasing TAC from $1.85B to $2.0B, with RBC requirement of approximately $980M) provides only 4 percentage points of cushion above the 200% CAL threshold. [VERIFIED:financial-analyst-report.md lines 118-122 RBC calculation]. This minimal margin means that relatively modest adverse events can trigger CAL breach and potentially approach RAL:

**Stress Scenario A: Vermont Captive Recapture (50-60% probability)**

- RBC requirement: $980M (unchanged)
- Post-stress RBC ratio: ($1.27B ÷ $980M) × 100% = **130%** (below RAL, above ACL)

- Remediation cost: $500M capital injection required to restore 200% CAL

- Regulatory consequence: RBC Plan required; enhanced supervision; dividend restrictions possible
- Remediation cost: $170M capital injection to restore 200% CAL
