#### 1. No FMC License Transfer Required (CRITICAL CORRECTION)

**Regulatory Basis:**
- 46 CFR Â§ 520.3 requires VOCCs to file **Form FMC-1** prior to commencement of operations, providing organization name, home office address, representative contact information, and tariff location.
- The FMC assigns a unique **organization number** to each VOCC, but this is NOT a license requiring transfer or renewal.
- **No statutory or regulatory provision** in 46 U.S.C. Chapter 401 or 46 CFR Part 520 requires FMC approval for change of control, merger, or acquisition of a VOCC.
- By contrast, Ocean Transportation Intermediaries under 46 CFR Â§ 515.21 must report acquisitions within 30 days by filing amended Form FMC-18.

#### 2. FMC Has No Authority to Approve Mergers

**Finding:** The Federal Maritime Commission lacks statutory authority under the Shipping Act of 1984 to approve or disapprove mergers, acquisitions, or changes of control of ocean common carriers. This was definitively established in 2017 when the FMC rejected the proposed merger of three Japanese carriers (K-Line, MOL, and NYK) into Ocean Network Express (ONE).

**Legal Precedent:**
The FMC stated: "The Shipping Act of 1984 does not provide the FMC with the authority to approve mergers." The FMC's jurisdiction extends to **cooperative agreements among carriers** (vessel-sharing agreements, rate discussion agreements, terminal agreements) filed under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 40303, but does NOT extend to corporate M&A transactions.

**Distinction from DOJ/FTC Review:**
While the FMC lacks merger review authority, the Department of Justice Antitrust Division and Federal Trade Commission retain jurisdiction to review ocean carrier mergers under the Clayton Act and Sherman Antitrust Act. However, the DOJ has expressed concerns about consolidation in the container shipping industry (e.g., DOJ characterized the Ocean Alliance Agreement as "a concerning step towards industry consolidation"), but has not blocked recent mergers (e.g., CMA-CGM's acquisition of APL (2016), COSCO's acquisition of OOCL (2018)).

**Implications for Project Neptune:**
- Project Neptune does NOT require FMC approval.
- If transaction value exceeds Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) thresholds (2025 threshold: $111.4M), acquirer must file HSR pre-merger notification with DOJ/FTC, but this is separate from FMC jurisdiction.
- FMC's role is limited to reviewing any **post-acquisition changes to filed agreements** (VSAs, terminal agreements) involving PMSC, not the acquisition itself.

**Confidence Level:** HIGH (explicit FMC precedent in 2017 ONE merger rejection; statutory interpretation of 46 U.S.C. Chapter 403 confirming FMC jurisdiction limited to agreements, not mergers).

#### 4. Demurrage & Detention Complaints: OSRA 2022 Creates Heightened Enforcement Environment

**Finding:** PMSC faces 12 pending demurrage and detention (D&D) complaints arising from alleged unreasonable billing practices during COVID-era port congestion (2020-2022). The Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (OSRA 2022) fundamentally shifted the regulatory landscape, placing the **burden of proof on carriers** to demonstrate reasonableness of D&D charges and mandating FMC investigation and public disclosure of enforcement actions.

**Regulatory Evolution (2020-2024):**

**Phase 1: FMC Interpretive Rule (May 18, 2020):**
- FMC established the **"incentive principle"**: D&D charges are reasonable only to the extent they serve as financial incentives to promote freight fluidity (46 CFR Â§ 545.5(c)(1)).
- Key holding: Practices imposing detention when containers cannot be returned due to circumstances beyond shipper control (e.g., terminal congestion, chassis unavailability) are "likely to be found unreasonable."

**Phase 2: Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (Signed June 16, 2022):**
- **Burden Shift:** 46 U.S.C. Â§ 41310(b)(2) places burden on common carrier to prove reasonableness of demurrage and detention charges in FMC charge complaint proceedings.
- **Charge Complaint Process:** FMC must promptly investigate complaints and order refunds for unreasonable charges.
- **Public Disclosure:** FMC must publish all findings of false D&D invoice practices and all penalties assessed on publicly available website (increasing reputational risk for carriers).
- **Heightened Enforcement:** FMC's Bureau of Enforcement, Investigations, and Compliance intensified enforcement actions post-OSRA 2022.

**Phase 3: Final Rule on D&D Billing (Feb. 26, 2024, Effective May 28, 2024):**
- Codified billing requirements at 46 CFR Â§ 545.5, including invoice content requirements, 30-day issuance deadline, and billing party restrictions.
- **D.C. Circuit Vacatur (2024-2025):** Court vacated Â§ 541.4 restricting who may be billed, finding FMC's rationale "arbitrary and capricious," creating uncertainty about billing party requirements.
- **TCW, Inc. v. Evergreen Shipping (2025):** D.C. Circuit clarified that "incentive principle" does not replace Shipping Act's "reasonableness" standard and criticized FMC for over-reliance on the incentive principle to the exclusion of other considerations.

#### 5. Controlled Carrier Status: Contingent on Acquirer's Ownership Structure

**Finding:** If Global Logistics Partners LLC has foreign government ownership â‰¥50%, PMSC will become a **"controlled carrier"** under 46 U.S.C. **Definition of "Controlled Carrier" (46 U.S.C. Â§ 40102(9)):**
A controlled carrier is an ocean common carrier whose operating assets are directly or indirectly owned or controlled by a government, with control deemed to exist if:
- **(A)** A majority interest in the carrier is owned or controlled in any manner by that government, an agency of that government, or a public or private person controlled by that government; OR
- **(B)** That government has the right to appoint or disapprove the appointment of a majority of directors, the chief operating officer, or the chief executive officer of the carrier.

- Foreign sovereign wealth funds (e.g., China Investment Corporation, Abu Dhabi Investment Authority)
- State-owned enterprises (e.g., China COSCO Shipping Corporation, China Merchants Group)
- Foreign government agencies or instrumentalities

**If YES â†’ Controlled Carrier Requirements Apply:**

**1. 30-Day Advance Rate Filing Requirement (46 U.S.C. Â§ 40703):**
- All rates, charges, classifications, rules, or regulations in tariffs **and service contracts** may NOT become effective until the 30th day after publication.
- FMC may grant special permission for less than 30 days' notice, but this requires filing and FMC approval.
- **Operational Impact:**
  - **Competitive disadvantage:** Non-controlled carriers can change rates immediately in response to market conditions; controlled carriers must wait 30 days.
  - **Service contract delays:** 30-day delay before new service contracts become effective may result in lost shipper negotiations to competitors who can offer immediate rate commitments.
  - **Market volatility exposure:** In declining rate markets, controlled carriers are locked into higher rates for 30 days while competitors undercut; in rising rate markets, controlled carriers cannot capitalize on rate increases for 30 days.

**2. "Just and Reasonable" Rate Standard (46 U.S.C. Â§ 40702):**
- Controlled carrier may not maintain rates or charges in tariffs or service contracts that are **below a level that is just and reasonable**.
- FMC has authority to review and reject rates deemed below-market (predatory pricing concern).
- **Implementing Regulations:** 46 CFR Part 565 (NOT 46 CFR Part 532, which governs NVOCC Negotiated Rate Arrangements).

**3. Exemptions from Controlled Carrier Requirements (46 CFR Part 565):**
- **Treaty exemption:** If controlling state's vessels are entitled to national or most-favored-nation treatment by U.S. treaty, controlled carrier requirements do NOT apply.
  - Example: If Global Logistics Partners is controlled by a government with which the U.S. has a bilateral maritime treaty granting national treatment, PMSC may be exempt.
- **All-controlled-carrier trade exemption:** If the trade route is served exclusively by controlled carriers, 30-day advance filing requirement does NOT apply.
  - Unlikely to apply to trans-Pacific eastbound trade (65% of PMSC's revenue), as this route is served by non-controlled carriers (Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, MSC).
