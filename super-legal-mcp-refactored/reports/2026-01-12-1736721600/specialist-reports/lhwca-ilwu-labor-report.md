**2. Section 905(b) Vessel Negligence: Martinez v. PMSC Litigation**

Juan Martinez, longshoreman employed by Pacific Ship Services (stevedoring contractor), filed Section 905(b) vessel negligence action against PMSC seeking $8.5 million damages ($1.2M medical, $2.8M lost wages, $4.5M pain/suffering) after sustaining bilateral leg fractures when container lashing gear (turnbuckle) failed aboard M/V Pacific Horizon (September 2023). Trial scheduled **May 2025**, five months post-closing if transaction closes January 2025.

**Legal Framework (*Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981)):**

Section 905(b) creates limited exception to LHWCA's exclusive remedy provision, allowing injured longshoremen to sue vessel owners for negligence. Vessel owners owe three duties:

1. **Turnover Duty** (most likely applicable to Martinez): Vessel must exercise "ordinary care" to turn over ship and equipment in condition expert stevedore can work with reasonable safety. Must warn of hidden dangers known to vessel or discoverable through reasonable inspection. **Plaintiff's strongest theory**: PMSC failed to inspect turnbuckle before stevedoring operations; defect was latent but discoverable.

2. **Active Control Duty** (likely not applicable): Vessel liable if actively involved in cargo operations and negligently injures longshoreman. Typically not applicable to container lashing (stevedore-exclusive work).

3. **Duty to Intervene** (likely not applicable): Vessel must intervene only if **actual knowledge** (not constructive) that equipment poses danger and stevedore failing to protect workers. High burden for plaintiff.

**PMSC's Defenses:**
- Adequate inspection: Lashing gear inspected August 2023, passed visual inspection, no visible defects
- Expert stevedore doctrine: Container lashing is specialized work; stevedore responsible for selecting and inspecting gear before use
- **Comparative fault**: Martinez failed to inspect turnbuckle before use, failed to stand clear during operations (30-50% fault reduction under maritime comparative fault principles per *Exxon Co. v. Sofec*, 517 U.S. 830 (1996))

### A. LHWCA STATUTORY FRAMEWORK AND EMPLOYER OBLIGATIONS

#### 1. Employer Obligation to Secure Compensation (33 U.S.C. Â§ 904)

Every employer covered by the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act must secure payment of compensation to employees for work-related injuries.Â¹ The statute provides two methods for securing compensation:

1. **Insurance with authorized carrier**: Employer purchases insurance policy from carrier authorized by the U.S. Department of Labor (DOL) to write LHWCA coverage
2. **Self-insurance authorization**: Employer furnishes satisfactory proof of financial ability to pay compensation directly and receives authorization from the Secretary of LaborÂ²

**Key Statutory Provisions:**

33 U.S.C. Â§ 904(a) states: "Every employer shall be liable for and shall secure the payment to his employees of the compensation payable under sections 907, 908, and 909 of this title."Â³

**No-Fault Compensation**: Compensation is payable "irrespective of fault as a cause for the injury."â´ This strict liability standard means employers cannot defend LHWCA claims by arguing employee contributory negligence, assumption of risk, or fellow servant negligence.

**Subcontractor Provisions**: If a subcontractor fails to secure compensation, the general contractor becomes liable for securing payment.âµ However, if the contractor has provided insurance coverage that extends to subcontractor employees, the subcontractor is deemed compliant.

### B. SECTION 905(b) VESSEL NEGLIGENCE LIABILITY

#### 1. Statutory Framework: Third-Party Tort Claims Against Vessel Owners

33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b) creates a limited exception to the LHWCA's exclusive remedy provision, allowing injured longshoremen to sue vessel owners for negligence:

**Section 905(b) Text:**

"In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person, or anyone otherwise entitled to recover damages by reason thereof, may bring an action against such vessel as a third party in accordance with the provisions of section 933 of this title, and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly and any agreements or warranties to the contrary shall be void."Â¹Â³

**Key Legal Consequences:**

1. **Dual Recovery**: Injured longshoreman receives LHWCA benefits from employer (no-fault, statutory schedule) PLUS tort damages from vessel owner (including pain/suffering, lost earning capacity)
2. **No Employer Indemnification**: Employer (stevedore) cannot be held liable to vessel owner for vessel's negligence; traditional "Ryan Stevedoring" implied warranty of workmanlike service indemnification was abolished by 1972 LHWCA amendmentsÂ¹â´
3. **Vessel's Sole Responsibility**: Vessel owner bears full liability for its negligence without contribution from stevedore employer

**Exceptions to Section 905(b) Liability:**

The statute contains two narrow exceptions where vessel owner is NOT liable:

1. **Stevedoring Services Exception**: If the injured person was employed by the vessel to provide stevedoring services, no action is permitted if injury was caused by negligence of persons engaged in providing stevedoring services to the vesselÂ¹âµ
2. **Shipbuilding/Repair Exception**: If injured person was employed to provide shipbuilding, repairing, or breaking services and the employer was the vessel's owner, operator, or charterer, no action permitted against the employer in its capacity as vessel ownerÂ¹â¶

Neither exception applies to Martinez case: Martinez was employed by Pacific Ship Services (independent stevedoring contractor), NOT by PMSC (vessel owner).

#### 2. Scindia Three-Part Test for Vessel Negligence

The Supreme Court's decision in *Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), established the controlling legal framework for vessel owner duties under Section 905(b).Â¹â·

**Background:**

Scindia involved a longshoreman injured when cargo fell from a defective winch during loading operations. The Court held that vessel owners owe three distinct duties to longshoremen working aboard their vessels:Â¹â¸

---

**DUTY 1: TURNOVER DUTY**

**Standard:** The vessel owner "owes a duty to exercise ordinary care under the circumstances to turn over the ship and its equipment in such condition that an expert stevedore can carry on stevedoring operations with reasonable safety."Â¹â¹

**Scope:**
- Applies to ship's gear, equipment, tools, and work spaces used in stevedoring operations
- Vessel must **warn** of hidden or latent dangers known to vessel owner (or that should be known in exercise of reasonable care)
- Duty exists **before** stevedoring operations commenceÂ²â°

**Breach:** If vessel fails to warn of hidden dangers (e.g., defective lashing gear, corroded hatch covers, malfunctioning cranes) and injury results, vessel is liable for negligence.Â²Â¹

**Application to Martinez Case:**
PMSC's defense will likely focus on turnover duty: Did PMSC inspect container lashing gear (turnbuckles) before stevedoring commenced? Did PMSC warn Pacific Ship Services of known defects? User-provided facts state "lashing gear failure" suggests potential turnover duty breach if PMSC knew or should have known of defect.

---

**DUTY 2: ACTIVE CONTROL DUTY**

**Standard:** Vessel may be liable if it "actively involves itself in the cargo operations and negligently injures a longshoreman or if it fails to exercise due care to avoid exposing longshoremen to harm from hazards they may encounter in areas, or from equipment, under the active control of the vessel during the stevedoring operation."Â²Â²

**Scope:**
- Applies when vessel owner's crew **participates** in cargo operations (e.g., operating ship's cranes, directing longshoremen, controlling hatch covers)
- Vessel must exercise due care to avoid creating hazards or exposing longshoremen to danger from vessel-controlled equipmentÂ²Â³

**Breach:** If vessel crew operates equipment negligently during stevedoring (e.g., crane operator swings load over longshoremen without warning, vessel crew member removes safety guards from machinery), vessel is liable.Â²â´

**Application to Martinez Case:**
Active control duty likely NOT applicable unless PMSC crew operated equipment during lashing operations. Typically, container lashing is performed exclusively by stevedore employees without vessel crew involvement.

---

**DUTY 3: DUTY TO INTERVENE**

**Standard:** Vessel owner has duty to intervene to protect longshoremen "only if it has **actual knowledge** that the ship or its gear poses a danger to the longshoremen and that the stevedore is failing, unreasonably, to protect the longshoremen."Â²âµ

**Scope:**
- **Actual knowledge required**: Constructive knowledge (should have known) is insufficientÂ²â¶
- Applies when vessel observes stevedore's unsafe practices but fails to stop work or warn workers
- Highest burden for injured longshoreman to proveÂ²â·

**Breach:** If vessel's master or crew observes longshoreman using defective equipment or working in dangerous manner, and vessel has actual knowledge of danger, vessel must intervene or face liability.Â²â¸

**Application to Martinez Case:**
Duty to intervene likely NOT applicable unless PMSC crew observed Martinez using defective lashing gear before injury and failed to warn or stop work. Plaintiff burden to prove actual knowledge is substantial.

### C. NLRA SUCCESSOR EMPLOYER DOCTRINE

#### 1. Legal Framework: 29 U.S.C. Â§ 158(a)(5) Duty to Bargain

The National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) Section 8(a)(5) requires employers to "bargain collectively" with the representative of their employees.â´â¸ The statute defines "bargaining collectively" as:

"The performance of the mutual obligation of the employer and the representative of the employees to meet at reasonable times and confer in good faith with respect to wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment, or the negotiation of an agreement, or any question arising thereunder, and the execution of a written contract incorporating any agreement reached if requested by either party."â´â¹

**Unfair Labor Practice:**

Failure to bargain in good faith is an unfair labor practice under 29 U.S.C. Â§ 158(a)(5), subjecting employer to:
- NLRB cease-and-desist order
- Affirmative bargaining order
- Back pay remedies (if refusal to bargain resulted in employee terminations or wage reductions)
- Potential strike by union in response to unfair labor practiceâµâ°

#### 2. Burns Doctrine: Successor Employer Not Bound by Predecessor's CBA

In *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services, Inc.*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972), the Supreme Court held that **successor employers must recognize and bargain with incumbent union** if they hire a majority of predecessor's employees and "substantial continuity" of business operations exists, BUT **successor is NOT bound by predecessor's collective bargaining agreement** unless successor expressly assumes the agreement.âµÂ¹

**Burns Facts:**

Burns Security won contract to provide security services at Lockheed factory, replacing Wackenhut Corp. Burns hired 27 of 42 Wackenhut guards (63% of workforce). United Plant Guard Workers (UPG) union demanded Burns honor existing CBA negotiated with Wackenhut. Burns refused, arguing it never agreed to Wackenhut's contract terms.âµÂ²

**Supreme Court Holding (Two-Part Rule):**

1. **Duty to Bargain**: Burns must recognize UPG and bargain in good faith because:
   - Majority of Burns workforce (27 of 42) were former Wackenhut employees
   - Union was recently certified (less than 1 year prior)
   - Business operations remained substantially identicalâµÂ³

2. **No Duty to Honor Predecessor's Contract**: Burns is NOT bound by Wackenhut-UPG collective bargaining agreement provisions (wage rates, benefits, work rules) because Burns did not consent to those terms. Binding successor to predecessor's contract would undermine successor's ability to negotiate terms reflecting new business realities.âµâ´

**Rationale:**

"[A] potential employer may be willing to take over a moribund business only if he can make changes in corporate structure, composition of the labor force, work location, task assignment, and nature of supervision. Saddling such an employer with the terms and conditions of employment contained in the old collective-bargaining contract may make these changes impossible and may discourage and inhibit the transfer of capital."âµâµ

#### 3. Fall River Doctrine: "Substantial Continuity" Test

*Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987), refined the Burns analysis, establishing the **"substantial continuity" test** for determining successor employer status.âµâ¶

**Fall River Facts:**

Sterlingwale textile dyeing plant closed in February 1982, laying off all production employees. Fall River Dyeing (formed by Sterlingwale's former officer and major customer) acquired Sterlingwale's plant, equipment, and began operations September 1982. Fall River hired 36 of 55 former Sterlingwale employees over several months. Union (UNITE) demanded recognition and bargaining; Fall River refused.âµâ·

**Supreme Court Holding:**

Fall River is a **successor employer** obligated to bargain with UNITE because:

1. **Substantial Continuity**: Business operations remained substantially unchanged:
   - Same facility and equipment
   - Same product (textile dyeing services)
   - Same customers
   - Same workforce skills required
   - Majority of employees (eventually 36 of 55) were Sterlingwale employeesâµâ¸

2. **"Substantial and Representative Complement" Rule**: Successor's duty to bargain attaches when successor has hired a "substantial and representative complement" of employees, meaning:
   - Workforce is substantially complete (hiring mostly finished)
   - Job classifications filled
   - Normal operations have commencedâµâ¹

3. **"Continuing Demand" Rule**: Union's premature demand for bargaining (before substantial complement hired) remains in effect. Employer cannot avoid duty by delaying hiring until after union demand expires.â¶â°

**Application to PMSC Acquisition:**

Global Logistics Partners (Acquirer) is acquiring 100% stock of PMSC. This is a **stock purchase**, not asset purchase, which means:
- **Same legal entity**: PMSC continues as same corporation (merely changes ownership)
- **Same employer**: PMSC remains employer of record for all 8,500 employees
- **Same CBAs**: All collective bargaining agreements (including ILWU Pacific Coast Longshore Contract) remain in full force and effectâ¶Â¹

**Key Distinction:**

Stock purchase is NOT a Burns/Fall River successorship scenario because employer (PMSC) does not change. Burns and Fall River apply to asset purchases or service contract takeovers where NEW employer hires predecessor's workforce. In stock purchase, **existing employer continues** with new ownership.â¶Â²

**Legal Consequence:**

- ILWU Pacific Coast Longshore Contract (expires July 1, 2028)
