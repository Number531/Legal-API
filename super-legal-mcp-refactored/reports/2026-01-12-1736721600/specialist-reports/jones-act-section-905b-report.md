### C. SECTION 905(b) VESSEL NEGLIGENCE LIABILITY (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 905(b))

#### 1. Statutory Framework

**33 U.S.C. ¬ß 905(b) ‚Äî Vessel Third-Party Tort Liability:**

Section 905(b) provides: "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person, or anyone otherwise entitled to recover damages by reason thereof, may bring an action against such vessel as a third party in accordance with the provisions of section 933 of this title."¬≤¬π

**Purpose:** Section 905(b) permits longshoremen who receive LHWCA workers' compensation benefits to also pursue traditional negligence claims against vessel owners if the vessel's negligence partly caused their injury.¬≤¬≤ A 905(b) claim allows recovery of **full damages for injury, including pain and suffering, medical expenses, lost wages, lost earning capacity, lost fringe benefits and other losses**.¬≤¬≥

#### 2. The Scindia Test ‚Äî Vessel Owner Duties

***Scindia Steam Navigation Co. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981) [VERIFIED: U.S. Supreme Court]**

The U.S. Supreme Court in *Scindia* established **three primary duties** that vessel owners owe to longshoremen under Section 905(b):¬≤‚Å¥

**1. Turnover Duty:**
The shipowner has a duty with respect to the condition of the ship's gear, equipment, tools, and work space to be used in the stevedoring operations, and if he fails at least to **warn the stevedore of hidden danger** which would have been known to him in the exercise of reasonable care, he has breached his duty and is liable if his negligence causes injury to a longshoreman.¬≤‚Åµ

**2. Active Control Duty:**
The vessel may be liable if it **actively involves itself in the cargo operations** and negligently injures a longshoreman or if it fails to exercise due care to avoid exposing longshoremen to harm from hazards they may encounter in areas, or from equipment, under the active control of the vessel during the stevedoring operation.¬≤‚Å∂

**3. Duty to Intervene:**
What are the shipowner's duties when he learns that an apparently dangerous condition exists or has developed in the cargo operation, which is known to the stevedore and which may cause injury to the longshoreman? Must the owner take some action?¬≤‚Å∑

**Standard:** The controlling standard is that **a vessel is subject to liability if the shipowner knows of or should discover the condition and realizes it involves an unreasonable risk, and fails to exercise reasonable care to protect the longshoremen**.¬≤‚Å∏

**Comparative Negligence Application:**
The doctrine of assumption of risk and contributory negligence do not apply in Section 905(b) claims. Rather, **any negligence on the part of the claimant only serves to reduce the recoverable damages**.¬≤‚Åπ

#### 3. Washington State Comparative Negligence

Washington follows the **"pure comparative negligence"** rule, which means that even if a plaintiff is partially at fault for an accident, the plaintiff can still recover damages. However, compensation will be reduced by the plaintiff's percentage of fault.¬≥‚Å∞

Under Washington State's comparative negligence rule, a person injured in an accident can file a legal claim **even if they bear 99 percent responsibility** for causing the accident. However, any portion of fault for the accident attributable to the injured plaintiff can reduce the plaintiff's financial recovery in proportion to their percentage of responsibility.¬≥¬π

#### 4. Martinez v. PMSC ‚Äî Application of Scindia to Defective Hatch Cover

**Case Posture:**
- **Case:** Martinez v. Pacific Maritime Services Corporation
- **Injury Date:** September 2023
- **Injury:** Longshoreman crushed by container lashing gear failure (alleged defective hatch cover)
- **Injuries Sustained:** Bilateral leg fractures, permanent disability
- **LHWCA Benefits Paid:** $420,000
- **Tort Claim:** $8.5M sought ($1.2M medical + $2.8M lost wages + $4.5M pain/suffering)
- **Trial Date:** Q3 2025 (post-closing)

**Turnover Duty Analysis:**

Under *Scindia*'s turnover duty, the vessel owner has a duty to exercise ordinary care with respect to the condition of the ship's gear, equipment, and work space at the commencement of stevedoring operations.¬≥¬≤ If a **hatch cover was defective** and the vessel owner knew or should have known of the defect in the exercise of reasonable care, the vessel owner had a duty to **warn the stevedore** or correct the condition.¬≥¬≥

**PMSC's Potential Defense:** Gear was inspected in August 2023 (one month before incident) and passed inspection. If PMSC can demonstrate:
1. Regular inspection and maintenance protocols were followed
2. The defect was latent (not discoverable through reasonable inspection)
3. The stevedore was in a better position to discover the defect during operations

Then turnover duty may not have been breached.

**Active Control Duty Analysis:**

If the vessel actively involved itself in the cargo operations where the hatch cover failure occurred, PMSC could be liable under the active control duty for negligently causing injury to Martinez.¬≥‚Å¥ However, if stevedore personnel (not vessel crew) were controlling the hatch cover operations, this duty may not apply.

**Duty to Intervene Analysis:**

If PMSC had actual knowledge of a dangerous condition with the hatch cover and actual knowledge that the stevedore, in the exercise of poor judgment, failed to correct the dangerous condition, PMSC had a duty to intervene.¬≥‚Åµ The facts as presented do not suggest PMSC had actual knowledge of an ongoing dangerous condition during operations.

**Most Likely Liability Theory:** **Turnover duty** ‚Äî defective hatch cover at commencement of operations.

#### 5. Punitive Damages Availability

**CRITICAL ISSUE:** The Martinez claim includes a request for damages that may encompass punitive damages if PMSC's conduct is found to constitute gross negligence (e.g., knowingly operating with defective equipment, failure to maintain inspection records, pattern of safety violations).

**Fifth Circuit Status:** The Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals has not yet addressed this issue at the appellate level.¬≥‚Å∏ **The Martinez case is being litigated in Washington state (likely W.D. Washington ‚Äî Ninth Circuit)**, not the Fifth Circuit, so Ninth Circuit precedent will control.

### D. SUCCESSOR LIABILITY FOR PRE-CLOSING MARITIME TORT CLAIMS

#### 1. Stock Purchase vs. Asset Purchase Distinction

**General Rule:** The risk of unknown liabilities is **lower in asset acquisitions** than in merger or stock transactions.‚Å¥¬π Stock purchases generally carry **higher successor liability risk** because the acquiring company assumes all liabilities of the target company, including contingent and unliquidated liabilities.‚Å¥¬≤

**PMSC Transaction Structure:** **Stock purchase** (acquire 100% equity of PMSC) per research plan.

**Implication:** Global Logistics Partners will assume **all liabilities of PMSC**, including the Martinez ¬ß 905(b) claim and any other pending or unknown maritime tort claims.

#### 2. Maritime Tort Liability in Stock Acquisitions

**Pre-Closing Tort Liability:** Where potentially significant liabilities may be imposed on a buyer long after the transaction closes (such as product liability and environmental clean-up costs), proper pre-closing investigation can identify and quantify the risks. Once identified, the risks can be allocated and mitigated through measures such as **indemnification for liabilities that come to light immediately after closing** and **insurance coverage for other claims**.‚Å¥¬≥

**Maritime-Specific Considerations:**

Maritime law applies to a tort when it satisfies both the "location" and "connection" tests:
- **Location test:** The tort occurs on navigable waters or, for injuries suffered on land, that the injury be caused by a vessel on navigable waters‚Å¥‚Å¥
- **Connection test:** The incident could have a potentially disruptive impact on maritime commerce‚Å¥‚Åµ

**Martinez Case ‚Äî Maritime Tort Status:** **VERIFIED** ‚Äî longshoreman injured on vessel during cargo operations on navigable waters = maritime tort subject to admiralty jurisdiction.

#### 3. Shipowner's Limitation of Liability Act ‚Äî NOT APPLICABLE to Martinez

**Limitation Act Standard:** In order to limit liability under the Shipowner's Limitation of Liability Act (46 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 30501-30512), the owner must prove there was no prior knowledge of any problems that could cause a mishap.‚Å¥‚Å∂ In situations where the shipowner or corporate management was **aware of any negligence** or if the **vessel was unseaworthy**, the limitation of liability does **NOT apply**.‚Å¥‚Å∑

**Application to Martinez:** If PMSC had **actual knowledge** of the defective hatch cover before the September 2023 incident, the Limitation Act would not provide protection. PMSC's defense that the hatch cover passed inspection in August 2023 suggests PMSC did not have actual knowledge of the defect, but this is a factual issue for litigation.

#### 4. Indemnification and Risk Allocation in Purchase Agreement

**Standard Indemnification Provisions for Pre-Closing Torts:**

In M&A transactions involving maritime companies, the purchase agreement should include:

1. **Seller indemnification for pre-closing tort claims:** Seller (PMSC's current owners) indemnifies buyer (Global Logistics Partners) for all liabilities arising from incidents occurring prior to closing date‚Å¥‚Å∏
2. **Escrow or holdback:** Portion of purchase price held in escrow (e.g., $10M-$25M) to secure indemnification obligations for 12-24 months post-closing‚Å¥‚Åπ
3. **Insurance:** Representations & warranties insurance policy covering unknown/undisclosed liabilities, including maritime tort claims‚Åµ‚Å∞

**Martinez Case Timing:** Incident occurred September 2023, trial scheduled Q3 2025 (post-closing). This is a **known liability** that should be:
- **Disclosed in schedules** to purchase agreement
- **Reflected in purchase price adjustment** (reduce price by estimated settlement value $2M-$5M), OR
- **Subject to specific indemnification** by seller with escrow to secure obligation

**Indemnification Challenges:** An indemnification right may be of little value when assets are being purchased from a distressed or insolvent seller.‚Åµ¬π If PMSC's current owners (family-owned, 3rd generation) are rolling equity or taking cash and exiting, the buyer must ensure adequate escrow/holdback to secure indemnification for Martinez settlement/verdict.
