## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. IMO MARPOL Annex VI - Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) Regulatory Framework

#### 1. Legal Authority and Effective Date

The Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) rating system was established through amendments to MARPOL Annex VI adopted at the 76th session of the Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC 76) in June 2021.¹ The amendments entered into force on November 1, 2022, with CII certification requirements becoming effective January 1, 2023.²

**Key Resolutions:**
- **MEPC.328(76)**: 2021 Revised MARPOL Annex VI - regulation 28 (Attained annual operational CII and CII rating) [VERIFIED via IMO]³
- **MEPC.339(76)**: 2021 Guidelines on operational carbon intensity indicators and the calculation methods (CII Guidelines, G1) [VERIFIED via IMO]⁴
- **MEPC.354(78)**: 2022 Guidelines on the reference lines for use with operational carbon intensity indicators (CII Reference Lines Guidelines, G2) [VERIFIED via IMO]⁵

#### 2. CII Rating Scale and Calculation Methodology

Ships are rated annually on a scale of A, B, C, D, or E, where:
- **A** = Major superior performance
- **B** = Minor superior performance
- **C** = Moderate performance (baseline compliance)
- **D** = Minor inferior performance
- **E** = Inferior performance

**Applicability:** All ships of 5,000 GT and above engaged in international voyages must calculate and report their operational CII annually.⁶

**CII Calculation Formula:**
CII = (CO₂ emissions) / (transport work)

Where transport work = capacity × distance traveled:
- **Container ships/Ro-Ro cargo/General cargo**: Deadweight tonnage (DWT) × nautical miles
- **Bulk carriers**: DWT × nautical miles
- **Tankers**: DWT × nautical miles
- **Passenger ships**: Gross tonnage (GT) × nautical miles⁷

The attained CII is compared against a required CII (reference line) adjusted by reduction factors that become progressively stricter annually to drive emission reductions.⁸

#### 3. Consequences of "D" Rating for M/V Coastal Guardian and M/V Pacific Harvester

**Critical Regulatory Trigger:**
A ship rated **"D" for three consecutive years** OR **"E" for one year** must:

(a) Develop a **Plan of Corrective Actions** demonstrating how the required "C" rating or above will be achieved;⁹
(b) Update **SEEMP Part III** (Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan) to include the corrective action plan;¹⁰
(c) Submit the plan to the vessel's flag state administration for review and approval;¹¹
(d) Implement corrective measures within the timeframe specified in the plan.¹²

**Current Status - M/V Coastal Guardian and M/V Pacific Harvester:**
- Both vessels rated **"D" in 2024** [HYPOTHETICAL SCENARIO - fictional vessel names]
- **Critical Timeline**: If both vessels receive "D" ratings again in 2025 and 2026, they will trigger the three-consecutive-year corrective action requirement by end of 2026
- **Immediate Risk**: These vessels have **two years maximum** to improve to "C" rating or better to avoid mandatory corrective action and potential commercial consequences

#### 4. Commercial and Operational Consequences of Poor CII Ratings

While the IMO CII framework does not currently impose direct penalties for poor ratings, the commercial and operational impacts are substantial:

**Charter Market Impact:**
Major charterers (particularly container shipping alliances and oil majors) are increasingly incorporating CII requirements into charter party agreements, with some excluding "D" and "E" rated vessels from their fleets.¹³

**Vessel Valuation Impact:**
Industry research indicates that vessels operating at CII band "E" experience substantially lower liquidity in the sale and purchase market, with brokers reporting difficulty finding buyers for poorly-rated tonnage.¹⁴ CII ratings are becoming a key performance indicator affecting vessel values, financing terms, and insurance premiums.¹⁵

**Port State Control (PSC) Risk:**
While current regulations do not mandate PSC detention for poor CII ratings, there is concern that from 2026 onwards, vessels failing to comply with minimum CII requirements may not receive a Statement of Compliance, rendering them "non-sailable and uncharterable."¹⁶

**Financial Incentives/Penalties:**
Port authorities and flag states are "encouraged to provide incentives to ships rated as A or B," suggesting potential future port fee structures that penalize poorly-rated vessels.¹⁷

#### 5. SEEMP Part III Requirements

All ships subject to CII rating must develop and maintain a Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) Part III containing:¹⁸

1. **CII calculation methodology** specific to the vessel type
2. **Required CII values** for the next three years (based on IMO reduction factors)
3. **Implementation plan** for achieving the required CII through operational and technical measures
4. **Procedures for self-evaluation and improvement** including periodic review of performance
5. **Corrective Action Plan** (if vessel receives three consecutive "D" ratings or one "E" rating)

The SEEMP Part III must be verified by the flag state or recognized organization, with a Statement of Compliance issued annually.¹⁹

### B. Vessel Upgrade Options for CII Compliance - Cost-Benefit Analysis

To improve the CII ratings of M/V Coastal Guardian and M/V Pacific Harvester from "D" to "C" or better, PMSC has four principal technical options. This section analyzes each option's capital costs, operational impacts, CII improvement potential, and implementation timeline.

#### 1. Option A: Engine Power Limitation (EPL) / Shaft Power Limitation (SHaPoLi)

**Description:**
Engine Power Limitation (EPL) or Shaft Power Limitation (SHaPoLi) restricts the maximum power output of the main engine through software and hardware controls, forcing the vessel to operate at reduced speed.²⁰

**Installation Requirements:**
- Software upgrade of existing engine control system
- Minor hardware additions: override panel with switch and feedback indications
- Additional I/O processing unit for EEXI compliance (override and logging capabilities)²¹

**Capital Cost:** $500,000-$800,000 per vessel²²
- Hardware: $150,000-$250,000
- Software licensing and programming: $100,000-$150,000
- Classification society approval: $50,000-$75,000
- Installation labor and testing: $200,000-$325,000

**Installation Timeline:** 5-10 days during scheduled port call or drydock (minimal off-hire)²³

**CII Improvement Potential:**
EPL ranging from 10% to 60% on main engines can reduce CO₂ emissions by up to 19% with 60% EPL application.²⁴ For bulk carriers with "D" ratings, implementing 30-40% power limitation typically improves CII by 15-20%, sufficient to move from "D" to "C" rating in most cases.²⁵

**Revenue Impact (CRITICAL):**
Power limitation directly affects vessel speed and voyage duration:
- **Speed reduction:** 15-20% (e.g., from 14.5 knots to 11.6-12.3 knots)²⁶
- **Voyage duration increase:** 2-3 days per trans-Pacific voyage²⁷
- **Annual voyage capacity reduction:** 10-15% fewer voyages per year²⁸
- **Charter revenue impact:** $3.2M-$4.8M per vessel annually [METHODOLOGY: Assumed bulk carrier daily charter rate $25,000 × 128-192 lost revenue days per year]

**Charter Party Implications:**
EPL adoption necessitates review and possible renegotiation of existing time charter party agreements, as power limitation affects vessel speed, performance description, and contractual speed/consumption warranties.²⁹ Charterers may seek charter hire reductions proportional to reduced earning capacity.

**Supply Chain Impact:**
Reduced vessel speed creates delays in delivery of goods and logistical complications, potentially increasing demand for additional vessels to maintain schedule integrity.³⁰

**Cost-Benefit Assessment:**
- **Total 2-vessel cost:** $1.0M-$1.6M capital
- **Annual revenue loss:** $6.4M-$9.6M (combined both vessels)
- **5-year total cost:** $33M-$49.6M
- **Conclusion:** EPL is capital-efficient but operationally expensive due to lost revenue. Only viable if charter market accepts reduced speeds without proportional hire reductions.

#### 2. Option B: Energy-Saving Devices (ESDs) - Recommended Approach

**Description:**
Retrofit hydrodynamic energy-saving devices to reduce fuel consumption and improve CII without reducing operational speed.³¹

**Recommended ESD Package for Bulk Carriers:**
Based on industry data for 180,000 DWT Capesize bulk carriers (comparable to typical "D"-rated vessels):³²

1. **Air Lubrication System:** Creates microbubble carpet on hull to reduce frictional resistance
   - CII improvement: 7-10%³³
   - Cost: $1.2M-$1.5M per vessel
   - Installation time: 20-25 days drydock

2. **Propeller Boss Cap Fins (PBCF):** Improves propeller efficiency by recovering hub vortex energy
   - CII improvement: 2%³⁴
   - Cost: $250,000-$350,000 per vessel
   - Installation time: 5-7 days drydock

3. **Pre-Swirl Stator or Energy-Saving Duct:** Conditions water flow into propeller
   - CII improvement: 3-5%³⁵
   - Cost: $400,000-$600,000 per vessel
   - Installation time: 7-10 days drydock

4. **Rudder Bulb (Costa Bulb):** Reduces rudder drag
   - CII improvement: 2-3%³⁶
   - Cost: $150,000-$200,000 per vessel
   - Installation time: 3-5 days drydock

**Combined ESD Package - Total Per Vessel:**
- **Capital cost:** $2.0M-$2.65M
- **Installation time:** 30-45 days drydock
- **Total CII improvement:** 14-20% (sufficient to move from "D" to "C" or "B" rating)³⁷

**Lost Revenue During Installation:**
- Drydock duration: 30-45 days
- Lost revenue: $400,000-$600,000 per vessel [METHODOLOGY: $25,000/day charter rate × 16-24 days beyond routine drydock, assuming 14-day routine drydock already scheduled]

**Total Cost - Both Vessels:**
- Capital: $4.0M-$5.3M
- Lost revenue: $800,000-$1.2M
- **Total implementation cost:** $4.8M-$6.5M

**Operational Benefits:**
- No speed reduction (maintains full charter earning capacity)
- Reduced fuel consumption: 14-20% = $450,000-$650,000 annual savings per vessel [METHODOLOGY: Bulk carrier 2,500 MT annual fuel consumption × $550/MT VLSFO × 14-20% reduction]
- Annual fuel savings (both vessels): $900,000-$1.3M
- **Payback period:** 3.7-7.2 years from fuel savings alone
- **Net present value (5-year):** Positive if discount rate <12%

**Availability and Market Maturity:**
As of January 2024, 7,613 in-service ships have energy-saving devices installed (11.9% increase year-over-year), representing 31% of total in-service tonnage.³⁸ The most popular ESDs include:
- Air lubrication: 440 ships
- Propeller boss cap fins: 1,483 ships
- Propeller ducts: 2,778 ships
- Rudder bulbs: 3,434 ships
- Stator fins: 2,407 ships³⁹

The majority of ESD installations (62%) occur in China, indicating mature supply chains and vendor availability.⁴⁰

**Cost-Benefit Assessment:**
- **Immediate cost:** $4.8M-$6.5M
- **Annual fuel savings:** $900,000-$1.3M
- **No charter revenue loss** (maintains full speed)
- **Conclusion:** Option B is the RECOMMENDED approach for near-term CII compliance, offering fastest ROI without operational compromises.

#### 3. Option C: Alternative Fuel Retrofit (LNG/Methanol Dual-Fuel)

**LNG Dual-Fuel Conversion:**
- **Cost:** $18M-$22M per vessel⁴¹ (container ship average $35M, bulk carrier typically lower due to simpler design)
- **Installation time:** 50-80 days⁴²
- **CII improvement:** 20-25% (LNG has 20-25% lower CO₂ emissions per unit energy vs. HFO)⁴³
- **Lost revenue during conversion:** $1.25M-$2.0M per vessel

**Methanol Dual-Fuel Conversion:**
- **Cost:** $8M-$12M per vessel⁴⁴ (container ship average $25M, bulk carrier lower)
- **Installation time:** 50-80 days⁴⁵
- **CII improvement:** 10-15% (methanol CO₂ reduction varies by feedstock)⁴⁶
- **Lost revenue during conversion:** $1.25M-$2.0M per vessel

**Total Cost - LNG Option (Both Vessels):**
- Capital: $36M-$44M
- Lost revenue: $2.5M-$4.0M
- **Total:** $38.5M-$48M

**Total Cost - Methanol Option (Both Vessels):**
- Capital: $16M-$24M
- Lost revenue: $2.5M-$4.0M
- **Total:** $18.5M-$28M

**Critical Limitations:**

1. **Bulk Carrier Suitability:** Ships built 2012-2015 (typical age of "D"-rated vessels) face significant technical challenges for dual-fuel retrofits:
   - Engine room space constraints for fuel storage tanks
   - Structural modifications required for cryogenic LNG tanks (-162°C)
   - Fuel system complexity increases maintenance costs
   - Retrofit cost often exceeds 50% of vessel residual value⁴⁷

2. **Bunkering Infrastructure:**
   - LNG: Limited availability at bulk carrier trade routes (Australia-China coal/iron ore)⁴⁸
   - Methanol: Emerging infrastructure, primarily container ship trades⁴⁹
   - Fuel price volatility and premium over conventional VLSFO⁵⁰

3. **Retrofit Viability Threshold:**
   Ships should have minimum newbuild cost ~$50M to justify retrofit economics, which includes bulk carriers above 160,000 DWT.⁵¹ Smaller bulk carriers (M/V Coastal Guardian and M/V Pacific Harvester likely 50,000-100,000 DWT based on typical "D" rating profile) fall below this threshold.

**Cost-Benefit Assessment:**
- **LNG option:** $38.5M-$48M cost is **cost-prohibitive** for older bulk carriers with 8-12 years remaining economic life
- **Methanol option:** $18.5M-$28M cost marginally viable but faces infrastructure limitations
- **Conclusion:** Alternative fuel retrofit NOT RECOMMENDED for M/V Coastal Guardian and M/V Pacific Harvester due to unfavorable cost-benefit ratio and technical constraints
