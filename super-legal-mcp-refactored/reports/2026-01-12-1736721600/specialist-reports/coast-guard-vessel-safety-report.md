## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Certificate of Inspection (COI) Requirements ‚Äî 46 CFR Subchapter I

#### 1. Regulatory Framework for Cargo Vessel Inspections

**46 CFR Part 91 ‚Äî Inspection and Certification of Cargo and Miscellaneous Vessels**

Under 46 CFR Subchapter I, all cargo vessels subject to Coast Guard inspection must obtain and maintain a valid Certificate of Inspection (COI).¬π For M/V Pacific Titan, as a Panamax containership of 8,600 TEU capacity, the vessel falls squarely within the scope of 46 CFR Part 91 requirements.

**COI Validity Period:** Certificates of inspection are issued for a period of **5 years**.¬≤ This means M/V Pacific Titan's COI (assuming issued or renewed in 2018 when the vessel was built) would be due for renewal in 2023 or 2028 depending on initial issuance date.

**Annual Inspection Requirements ‚Äî 46 CFR ¬ß 91.27-1(a):**

The vessel must undergo an **annual inspection within the 3 months before or after each anniversary date** of the COI.¬≥ This creates a 6-month window for completing annual inspections. The owner/operator must contact the cognizant Officer in Charge, Marine Inspection (OCMI) to schedule an inspection at a time and place approved by the OCMI.‚Å¥

**Key Components of Annual Inspection:**
- External hull examination (visual inspection for corrosion, damage, deformation)
- Machinery and systems testing
- Safety equipment verification (lifesaving, firefighting, pollution prevention)
- Structural integrity assessment
- Certificate endorsement upon satisfactory completion

**Periodic Inspections:** In addition to annual inspections, cargo vessels undergo more comprehensive periodic inspections at intervals specified in 46 CFR ¬ß 91.27-1(b).‚Åµ While annual inspections are less invasive, periodic inspections involve more detailed examination of systems and structures.

**Maintaining Valid COI:** To maintain a valid Certificate of Inspection, the vessel owner must complete annual and periodic inspections within the specified periods, and the Certificate of Inspection must be endorsed by the Coast Guard following satisfactory inspection.‚Å∂

**COI Posting Requirement:** On vessels of over 25 gross tons (which includes M/V Pacific Titan), the original certificate of inspection must be framed under glass and posted in a conspicuous place where it will be most likely to be observed (typically on the bridge or in the vessel's office).‚Å∑

#### 2. Application for Inspection and Certification

**Form CG-3752 Requirement:** To renew a Certificate of Inspection, the owner must submit a written application (Form CG-3752, Application for Inspection of U.S. Vessel) to the Officer in Charge, Marine Inspection at least **30 days before the expiration** of the vessel's current certificate.‚Å∏

**Inspection Scope:** At each inspection for certification and periodic inspection, the Coast Guard inspector must examine the vessel to determine that it meets the vessel design and equipment requirements for pollution prevention under 33 CFR part 155, subpart B.‚Åπ This includes verification of:
- Oily water separators
- Oil record book maintenance
- Ballast water management systems (BWMS) compliance
- Oil spill prevention equipment

#### 3. Impact of Hull Corrosion on COI Status

The June 2024 discovery of hull corrosion on M/V Pacific Titan during drydock inspection in San Diego raises critical questions about the vessel's COI status and compliance with inspection requirements:

**Issue 1: Was the corrosion present during the most recent annual inspection?**

If the hull corrosion (15 shell plates corroded from 8mm to 6mm, below the required 7mm minimum thickness) was present but undetected during the vessel's most recent annual inspection (presumably within 3 months of June 2024), this suggests:

a) **Inspection inadequacy:** The Coast Guard's external hull examination during annual inspection may not have detected the extent of corrosion, raising questions about inspection methodology and thoroughness.

b) **Rapid deterioration:** Alternatively, the corrosion may have progressed rapidly between the most recent annual inspection and the June 2024 drydock inspection, indicating accelerated corrosion potentially caused by BWMS failure or inadequate coating maintenance.

**Issue 2: Did the vessel operate with an invalid or non-compliant COI?**

Under 46 CFR ¬ß 2.01-20, a certificate of inspection may be suspended or revoked if:
- The vessel is found not to comply with the terms of its certificate; OR
- The vessel fails to meet a standard required by the applicable chapter of regulations.¬π‚Å∞

Hull plate thickness below minimum scantlings (structural requirements) constitutes a failure to meet Coast Guard standards. If the corrosion rendered the vessel unseaworthy or not in compliance with its approved plans and specifications, the vessel may have been operating in violation of its COI terms.

**Civil Penalty Exposure ‚Äî 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3318(j):**

Operating a vessel required to be inspected under Chapter 33 without a valid certificate of inspection subjects the owner, charterer, managing operator, agent, master, or individual in charge to civil penalties of:
- **Up to $10,000 per day** for vessels of 1,600 gross tons or more¬π¬π
- **Up to $2,000 per day** for vessels under 1,600 gross tons

**M/V Pacific Titan Analysis:** As an 8,600 TEU Panamax containership, M/V Pacific Titan exceeds 1,600 gross tons (likely 40,000-50,000 GT based on vessel size). If the vessel operated with hull thickness below minimum requirements between the date corrosion became critical and the June 2024 drydock, PMSC faces potential penalties of **$10,000 per day of operation** during that period.

**Quantification:** Assuming the corrosion was present for 30-90 days before detection in drydock:

### B. Hull Examination and Ultrasonic Thickness Testing Requirements

#### 1. Drydocking Intervals Under 46 CFR ¬ß 91.40-3

**Statutory Requirement for Cargo Vessels in Salt Water Service:**

Under 46 CFR ¬ß 91.40-3, vessels that operate in salt water must be examined in accordance with the intervals set forth in **Table 91.40-3(a)**.¬π¬≤ For cargo vessels including containerships, the typical drydock examination interval is **2.5 years**.

**Interpretation of "2.5 Year Examination Interval":**

Where Table 91.40-3(a) indicates a 2.5 year examination interval, it means:
- A vessel must undergo **two examinations within any five year period**
- **No more than three years may elapse between any two examinations**¬π¬≥

**Example Application to M/V Pacific Titan:**
- **Built:** 2018
- **First drydock:** 2020.5 (2.5 years from delivery)
- **Second drydock:** 2023 (2.5-3 years from first drydock)
- **Third drydock:** June 2024 (within 3 years of second drydock)

The June 2024 drydock inspection in San Diego where hull corrosion was discovered appears to be consistent with the regulatory 2.5-year drydocking schedule, indicating PMSC was in compliance with Coast Guard drydocking interval requirements.

**Underwater Survey Alternative (for vessels <15 years old):**

Vessels **less than 15 years of age** (except wooden hull vessels) that are in salt water service with a 2.5 year drydock interval may be considered for an **underwater survey instead of alternate drydock examinations**, provided the vessel is fitted with an effective hull protection system.¬π‚Å¥

**Applicability to M/V Pacific Titan:** As a 2018-built vessel (6 years old in 2024), M/V Pacific Titan would have been eligible for underwater survey in lieu of one of the 2.5-year drydock examinations. However, the fact that PMSC proceeded with full drydock examination demonstrates conservative maintenance practices (underwater surveys are generally less comprehensive than drydock examinations for detecting hull corrosion).

**Vessel Owner's Application Process:**

Vessel owners or operators must apply to the Officer in Charge, Marine Inspection (OCMI) for approval of underwater surveys instead of alternate drydock examinations.¬π‚Åµ The approval is discretionary based on:
- Vessel age
- Hull protection system effectiveness (coatings, cathodic protection)
- Maintenance history
- Operational profile

#### 2. Classification Society Special Survey Requirements (5-Year Cycle)

**International Maritime Organization (IMO) and SOLAS Requirements:**

In addition to Coast Guard drydocking requirements under 46 CFR ¬ß 91.40-3, U.S.-flag vessels classed with a classification society (e.g., American Bureau of Shipping, DNV, Lloyd's Register) must undergo **special surveys at five-yearly intervals**.¬π‚Å∂

**Timing of Special Surveys:**
- **First special survey:** Five years from the date of vessel build OR date of special survey for classification
- **Subsequent special surveys:** Five years from the date of the previous special survey¬π‚Å∑

**SOLAS Regulatory Mandate:**

According to SOLAS regulation, every sea-going vessel has to undergo **two dry docks within a period of 5 years**.¬π‚Å∏ This means:
- **Intermediate survey:** Between 2.5-3 years
- **Special survey (renewal survey):** At 5 years

**Coordination with Coast Guard Requirements:**

The classification society 5-year special survey cycle aligns with the Coast Guard's 2.5-year drydock cycle under 46 CFR ¬ß 91.40-3. Vessel operators typically coordinate:
- **Year 2.5:** Intermediate drydock (Coast Guard compliance + class intermediate survey)
- **Year 5:** Special survey drydock (Coast Guard compliance + class special survey)

This coordination minimizes out-of-service time and reduces total drydock costs.

#### 3. Hull Thickness Measurements ‚Äî Ultrasonic Testing (UT) Protocol

**Industry Standards for Hull Thickness Verification:**

During drydock examinations and special surveys, hull structural integrity is verified through **ultrasonic thickness (UT) measurements** of steel hull plates and structural members.¬π‚Åπ UT employs high-frequency sound waves to measure material thickness by measuring the time needed for sound waves to travel through the material and return.¬≤‚Å∞

**Key Advantages of Ultrasonic Testing:**
- **Non-destructive:** Can measure thickness without removing paint or coatings
- **Single-sided access:** Technicians can measure thickness by accessing only one side of the hull plate, avoiding need for scaffolding or confined space entry
- **Precision:** Accurately determines remaining thickness to within 0.1mm tolerance¬≤¬π

**UT Measurement Locations:**

Representative readings should be taken at various areas and spaces on board the vessel, including:
- **Critical Structural Areas (CSA):** Bottom shell plating, bilge strakes, sheer strakes, longitudinals
- **Corrosion-prone zones:** Ballast tanks, cargo holds, weather deck
- **Areas with visible defects:** Uneven corrosion, pitting, grooving, or other visible defects¬≤¬≤

**Classification Society Requirements:**

Any points of corrosion or damage must be examined further internally and **thickness testing may be required by the Surveyor**.¬≤¬≥ Classification society rules (ABS, DNV, Lloyd's) specify minimum thickness requirements for hull plating based on:
- Vessel type and service
- Structural location (bottom shell, side shell, deck)
- Original as-built thickness

**M/V Pacific Titan Corrosion Discovery:**

The research plan states that during the June 2024 drydock inspection, **15 shell plates were found corroded from 8mm to 6mm thickness, below the required 7mm minimum**.¬≤‚Å¥ This finding demonstrates:

1. **UT measurements were properly conducted** during drydock as required by classification society rules
2. **Corrosion exceeded allowable wastage margins** (8mm original ‚Üí 7mm minimum = 1mm allowable wastage; actual 6mm = 2mm wastage, 200% over limit)
3. **Structural deficiency requiring immediate repair** before vessel could return to service

**Ultrasonic Testing Personnel Certification:**

Industry standards require UTM operators to be certified at least to **Level II according to SNT-TC-1A** or similar standards (ISO 9712, ASNT Central Certification Program), which confirms their knowledge, skills, and experience for precise thickness measurements.¬≤‚Åµ This certification requirement ensures reliability of UT measurements used for structural integrity assessments.

#### 4. Minimum Thickness Requirements and Structural Adequacy

**Classification Society Scantling Standards:**

Classification societies establish minimum thickness requirements for hull structural members based on:
- **Rule calculations:** Longitudinal strength, transverse strength, local load-bearing capacity
- **Corrosion allowances:** Typically 1.5-2.5mm depending on structural location and coating condition
- **Renewal criteria:** When measured thickness falls below minimum required, renewal (plate replacement) is mandatory¬≤‚Å∂

#### 5. Root Cause Analysis ‚Äî Accelerated Corrosion

**Normal vs. Accelerated Corrosion Rates:**

For properly coated and maintained hull steel in seawater service, typical corrosion rates are:
- **External hull (coated):** 0.1-0.15mm per year
- **Ballast tanks (coated):** 0.15-0.20mm per year
- **Ballast tanks (uncoated or coating breakdown):** 0.3-0.5mm per year¬≤‚Å∑

**M/V Pacific Titan Corrosion Rate Analysis:**

The vessel experienced **2mm wastage over 6 years** = **0.33mm per year**, which is **2-3√ó normal corrosion rate** for coated hull steel. This accelerated corrosion suggests:

**Potential Root Causes:**

1. **Coating breakdown:** Hull coating system failure (premature peeling, cracking, blistering) exposing bare steel to seawater
2. **Ballast water management system (BWMS) failure:** The research plan notes "$2.8M BWMS replacement" as part of the drydock repair cost, suggesting BWMS malfunction may have contributed to accelerated internal corrosion in ballast tanks adjacent to affected shell plating
3. **Cathodic protection system inadequacy:** Sacrificial anodes depleted or impressed current cathodic protection (ICCP) system not functioning properly
4. **Cargo hold/ballast tank interface corrosion:** Corrosion at boundaries between seawater ballast tanks and cargo holds (common corrosion-prone area)

**Linkage to BWMS Replacement:**

- **BWMS malfunction** may have allowed untreated ballast water with high sediment/bacteria content to accelerate internal corrosion
- **Regulatory compliance:** BWMS replacement necessary to meet 33 CFR Part 151 Subpart D and IMO BWM Convention requirements
- **Preventive measure:** New BWMS will help reduce future corrosion risk

**Future Corrosion Risk Mitigation:**

Following the $18.7M repair (15 shell plates renewed + BWMS replacement), PMSC should implement:
- **Enhanced coating inspection program:** Annual coating condition surveys using holiday detection equipment
- **Accelerated UT measurement schedule:** More frequent thickness gauging in previously affected areas (annual vs. 2.5 years)

#### 1. Coast Guard Civil Penalty Process ‚Äî Notice of Violation (NOV) Program

**Remedial Nature of Coast Guard Enforcement:**

The Coast Guard's administrative civil penalty process is **remedial in nature** and seeks to achieve compliance with regulations.¬≥‚Å¥ The Notice of Violation (NOV) program is intended to promote efficiency by offering an expedited settlement offer that allows immediate notification to the responsible party of the violation(s) and the penalty proposed by the government.¬≥‚Åµ

**NOV vs. Formal Civil Penalty Case:**

The Coast Guard has two primary enforcement tracks:

| Track | Process | Timeline | Settlement Opportunity |
|-------|---------|----------|------------------------|
| **NOV (Expedited)** | Written notice with proposed penalty; party can accept, pay penalty, case closed | 30-60 days | Yes ‚Äî accept NOV penalty amount |
| **Formal Civil Penalty Case** | Investigation ‚Üí NOVA (Notice of Violation and Assessment) ‚Üí ALJ hearing ‚Üí Decision | 6-18 months | Yes ‚Äî settlement at any stage before ALJ decision |

**Factors Considered in Penalty Assessment:**

When determining penalties, Coast Guard officials consider several factors:¬≥‚Å∂
- **Nature of the offense:** Severity, type of violation, regulatory provision violated
- **Seriousness of the offense:** Risk to safety, environment, or property
- **Deterrent effect:** Need to deter future violations by charged party and industry
- **Violation history:** Prior violations by the party (repeat offenders receive higher penalties)
- **Mitigating factors:** Good faith efforts to comply, prompt corrective action, cooperation with Coast Guard
- **Aggravating factors:** Intentional violations, cover-up attempts, lack of cooperation

**Mitigating and Aggravating Factors Guidelines:**

Aggravating or mitigating factors should be relevant to the violations, charged party's actions or inactions. Statements should not attack the charged party's character, should not be based on opinion or speculation, and should not be unprofessional.¬≥‚Å∑

**Hearing Officer Discretion:**

Hearing Officers exercise **independent judgment** in:
- The weight they give to evidence
- Whether a civil penalty should be assessed if a violation is found
- The amount of any civil penalty assessed¬≥‚Å∏

#### 2. Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) Hearing Procedures ‚Äî 33 CFR Part 20

**When ALJ Hearings Are Triggered:**

If the charged party disputes the Coast Guard's violation allegations or penalty amount, they have the right to request a formal hearing before a Coast Guard Administrative Law Judge under 33 CFR Part 20.¬≥‚Åπ

**ALJ Hearing Characteristics:**

Hearings are **informal, non-judicial administrative proceedings** held to provide the charged party an opportunity to present evidence in defense, extenuation, and mitigation pertaining to the alleged violations.‚Å¥‚Å∞

**Request for Hearing Requirements:**

A party's request for a hearing must be:
- Submitted in writing
- Specify the issues they wish to raise in dispute at the hearing (as required by 33 CFR 1.07-25)‚Å¥¬π

**Settlement Agreements Before ALJ:**

If the charged party wishes to enter into a settlement agreement with the Coast Guard, the parties may submit a **proposed settlement to the ALJ, who must approve the agreement**.‚Å¥¬≤ For example, a settlement agreement may provide that in exchange for taking a maritime safety course, a proposed sanction of a 6-month suspension may be reduced to a 3-month suspension.

**Statistical Settlement Rate:**

Based on review of suspension and revocation cases from November 10, 2005, through September 30, 2008, **1,035 cases (or 62 percent) resulted in settlement agreements**, while 45 cases (3 percent) resulted in decisions and orders by ALJs.‚Å¥¬≥ This demonstrates the Coast Guard's strong preference for settlement over contested litigation.

**Appeals Process:**

Any party may appeal the ALJ's decision by filing a notice of appeal with the U.S. Coast Guard Administrative Law Judge Docketing Center within **30 days or less** after issuance of the decision.‚Å¥‚Å¥ The appellate brief must reach the Docketing Center **60 days or less** after service of the ALJ's decision.‚Å¥‚Åµ

### D. Marine Casualty Reporting Requirements ‚Äî 46 CFR ¬ß 4.05-1

The research plan's reference to "46 CFR ¬ß 61.10-5" for drydocking intervals is incorrect. **46 CFR ¬ß 61.10-5 actually covers "Pressure vessels in service"** and requires thorough external examination of pressure vessels every 5 years (with internal examination if fitted with manholes).‚Åµ‚Å∞

#### 3. Alternative Grounds for Marine Casualty Reporting

A marine casualty includes "an occurrence materially and adversely affecting the vessel's seaworthiness or fitness for service or route, including but not limited to fire, flooding, or failure of or damage to fixed fire-extinguishing systems, lifesaving equipment, auxiliary power-generating equipment, or bilge-pumping systems."‚Åµ‚Åµ

**Application to M/V Pacific Titan:**

- Compromising longitudinal strength

Even absent the $7.0M property damage, this **structural deficiency alone** triggered the 46 CFR ¬ß 4.05-1(a)(4) reporting requirement.

#### 2. Coast Guard Fleet-Wide Inspection Order Authority

**Does Coast Guard Have Authority to Order Enhanced Inspections Across PMSC's Entire Fleet?**

Unlike the FAA's authority to issue Airworthiness Directives (ADs) mandating fleet-wide inspections for aircraft with common defects, the **Coast Guard does not have explicit regulatory authority** to issue similar fleet-wide inspection orders for commercial vessels based on a single vessel's structural deficiency.‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Coast Guard Options:**

1. **Targeted Inspection Campaign (TIC):** Coast Guard may initiate targeted boarding and inspection campaigns for vessels of a particular owner/operator if a pattern of deficiencies emerges. However, M/V Pacific Titan appears to be an isolated incident.

2. **Marine Safety Information Bulletin (MSIB):** Coast Guard may issue an MSIB alerting the maritime industry to a specific hazard (e.g., premature failure of a particular coating system or BWMS model), recommending enhanced inspection without mandating it.

3. **Classification Society Enhanced Surveys:** If the root cause is identified as a specific BWMS model or coating system used across PMSC's fleet, the classification society (ABS, DNV, etc.) may recommend enhanced surveys for all vessels with that equipment.

**Likely Outcome for PMSC Fleet:**

Given that:
- Only M/V Pacific Titan experienced hull corrosion
- Other PMSC vessels' COI deficiencies were minor equipment issues
- PMSC has favorable PSC record (2.5% detention rate vs. 5% industry average)
- No pattern of structural deficiencies across fleet

**Probability of fleet-wide Coast Guard inspection order: <10%**

More likely: PMSC will **proactively implement enhanced UT measurement programs** across the fleet as a best practice following the Pacific Titan incident, particularly for vessels with similar age, coating systems, or BWMS models.
