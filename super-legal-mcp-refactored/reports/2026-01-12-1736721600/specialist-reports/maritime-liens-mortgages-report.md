#### 8. Vessel Arrest and Foreclosure Procedures

If PMSC defaults on ship mortgages or maritime lienholders assert claims, vessels can be arrested through federal admiralty in rem actions (Supplemental Rule C):

**Arrest Procedure:**
1. Claimant files verified complaint in U.S. District Court
2. Court issues Warrant of Arrest in Rem directed to U.S. Marshal
3. Plaintiff deposits $10,000 with Marshal for arrest costs
4. Marshal serves warrant on vessel, taking custody (custodia legis)
5. If not released within 14 days, plaintiff publishes public notice

**Owner's Defenses:**
- Challenge arrest at prompt hearing (lack of maritime lien, improper jurisdiction)
- Post surety bond or P&I club letter of undertaking to release vessel

**Foreclosure Sale (if claimant prevails):**
1. U.S. Marshal appraises vessel and advertises public auction
2. Vessel sold to highest bidder at Marshal's sale
3. **Sale conveys title FREE AND CLEAR of all liens** (46 U.S.C. Â§ 31326(a))
4. Proceeds distributed in statutory priority order:
   - Priority 1: Marshal's costs (custody, sale)
   - Priority 2: Super-priority maritime liens (crew wages, torts, salvage)
   - Priority 3: Preferred ship mortgages
   - Priority 4: Necessaries liens arising before mortgage
   - Priority 5: Unsecured creditors

**Deficiency Judgment:** If sale proceeds insufficient to satisfy mortgage, lender has personal recourse claim against borrower for deficiency.

### A. PREFERRED SHIP MORTGAGE ACT - STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

#### 1. Requirements for Preferred Mortgage Status (46 U.S.C. Â§ 31322)

**Statutory Authority:** 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31322 establishes the conditions that must be met for a mortgage to qualify as a "preferred ship mortgage" with enhanced priority status under federal maritime law.Â¹

**Four Requirements for Preferred Status:**

A mortgage attains preferred status when ALL four conditions are met:

1. **Vessel Coverage:** The mortgage must cover the "whole of the vessel" (cannot be limited to engines, equipment, or portions)Â²
2. **Vessel Documentation:** The vessel must be documented under U.S. flag with the Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center (NVDC), or an application for documentation must be filedÂ³
3. **Eligible Mortgagee:** The lender must be "eligible" under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31322(a)(4), including:
   - Banks and trust companies
   - Qualified maritime lenders
   - Foreign financial institutions (since 1996 amendment allowing foreign lenders to hold preferred mortgages)â´
4. **Recording with Coast Guard:** The mortgage instrument must be recorded with the NVDC in Falling Waters, West Virginiaâµ

**Critical Timing:** A mortgage becomes a preferred mortgage **from the time all four conditions are met**, not from when the vessel is finally documented. Therefore, a mortgage typically attains preferred status when the application for documentation and the instrument have been filed with the NVDC.â¶

**PMSC Fleet Compliance:** All 22 PMSC vessels are documented under U.S. flag, satisfying the vessel documentation requirement. The research plan indicates the 4 lenders (JP Morgan, DNB Bank ASA, Citibank, ING Bank) hold preferred ship mortgages recorded with the NVDC.

#### 2. Recording Procedure with Coast Guard NVDC

**Application Form:** There is NO government-mandated form for a preferred mortgage instrument itself (parties draft mortgage agreements under maritime finance industry standards), but the **Optional Application for Filing (Form CG-5542)** MUST be attached to the mortgage when submitted to the NVDC.â·

**NVDC Role:** The U.S. Coast Guard acts principally as a **recording agency** for mortgages and related instruments. Recording provides public notice of the lien and establishes the mortgage's priority date.â¸

**2024 Electronic Filing Update:** In December 2024, the Coast Guard updated its document submission regulations. Previously, the NVDC accepted paper submissions, fax, or PDF email attachments. #### 3. Benefits of Preferred Mortgage Status

A preferred ship mortgage confers significant advantages to maritime lenders:

1. **Priority Over Most Claims:** Preferred mortgages have priority over nearly all claims against the vessel (subject to exceptions detailed in Section IV.B below)
2. **Federal Court Jurisdiction:** Enforceable through federal admiralty courts with nationwide jurisdiction
3. **In Rem Enforcement:** Lender can arrest the vessel itself (not just sue the borrower personally), providing direct security interest in the collateral
4. **Cross-Border Recognition:** U.S. preferred mortgages are generally recognized by foreign courts under international maritime law principles
5. **Foreclosure Rights:** Lender can force judicial sale of vessel through U.S. Marshal's sale, with proceeds distributed according to statutory priority

### B. MARITIME LIEN PRIORITY - STATUTORY HIERARCHY

#### 1. Priority Scheme Under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31326

**Statutory Authority:** 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31326 governs "Court sales to enforce preferred mortgage liens and maritime liens and priority of claims."Â¹â°

**Key Principle:** When a vessel is sold by court order in an admiralty action in rem to enforce a preferred mortgage or maritime lien, the sale proceeds are distributed according to a statutory priority hierarchy. The vessel is sold **free and clear** of all liens, and claimants are paid from proceeds in order of priority.Â¹Â¹

**Priority Hierarchy (Highest to Lowest):**

2. **Preferred Maritime Liens** (46 U.S.C. Â§ 31301(5)) arising AFTER preferred mortgage filing:Â¹Â³
   - **Crew wages** (seamen, officers)
   - **Stevedore wages** (longshoremen under LHWCA)

#### 3. Necessaries Liens - Priority Over Foreign Mortgages

**46 U.S.C. Â§ 31342 - Establishing Maritime Liens:**

**Definition of "Necessaries" (46 U.S.C. Â§ 31301(4)):**

The term "necessaries" **includes repairs, supplies, towage, and the use of a drydock or marine railway**.Â¹â¹ The statute uses the word "includes," meaning the definition is **non-exclusive**â€”courts have interpreted necessaries broadly to encompass:

- **Bunker fuel** (most common necessaries lien)Â²â°
- **Stevedoring services** (cargo loading/unloading)Â²Â¹
- **Provisions** (food, water, crew supplies)
- **Ship chandlery** (nautical equipment, ropes, paint)
- **Wharfage and dockage** (port fees)
- **Shipyard repairs** (hull repairs, engine overhauls, drydock services)Â²Â²

#### 4. Case Law: *Rainbow Line, Inc. v. M/V Tequila*, 480 F.2d 1024 (2d Cir. 1973)

**Facts:** The vessel LINGLEE was chartered to Rainbow Line. The owner, Simpson, breached the charter by prematurely withdrawing the vessel. Rainbow obtained an arbitration award for $17,849.12. Simpson then sold the vessel to Tequila, Ltd., which renamed it M/V TEQUILA. Empire Commercial Corp. loaned Tequila $180,000 secured by a first preferred mortgage recorded with the Coast Guard. Tequila defaulted within two months. The vessel was arrested in New York, with Rainbow, Empire, and 9 other claimants seeking payment from the vessel. The U.S. Marshal sold the vessel for $162,000â€”insufficient to pay all claims.Â²âµ

**Priority Issue:** If Rainbow's claim for charter breach constituted a "preferred maritime lien," it would have priority over Empire's mortgage (because Rainbow's claim arose before Empire recorded its mortgage). If Rainbow had only a contract claim without preferred lien status, Empire's mortgage would consume the entire sale proceeds, leaving nothing for Rainbow.Â²â¶

**Holding:** The Second Circuit analyzed whether a breach of charter party creates a maritime lien under the Commercial Instruments and Maritime Liens Act (CIMLA). The court ultimately determined that **Rainbow's claim did NOT qualify as a preferred maritime lien**, meaning Empire's preferred mortgage had priority.Â²â·

**Key Principle from *Rainbow Line*:** Not all maritime contract claims create maritime liens with priority over preferred mortgages. Only specific categories of claims (crew wages, tort damages, salvage, general average, necessaries) qualify as "preferred maritime liens" under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31301(5). Charter party breach damages are ordinary contract claims subordinate to preferred mortgages.

#### 5. Bunker Fuel Lien Litigation: O.W. Bunker Bankruptcy Cases

**Context:** The 2015 bankruptcy of O.W. Bunker, a major marine fuel intermediary, spawned extensive litigation over whether physical suppliers (who sold fuel to O.W. Bunker, which resold to vessel owners) had maritime liens against vessels.Â²â¸

**Key Issue:** Under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31342, a supplier has a necessaries lien if it provided fuel "on the order of the owner or a person authorized by the owner" (Â§ 31341). If O.W. Bunker acted as an intermediary/broker, did the ultimate physical supplier have lien rights?Â²â¹

**Fifth Circuit Rule:** Courts denied liens to physical suppliers who contracted with intermediaries unless the vessel owner **specifically directed the selection of the particular supplier or retained control over the supplier's performance**. The Fifth Circuit held that if the intermediary (O.W. Bunker) had discretion to choose the physical supplier, the physical supplier was a subcontractor without lien rights.Â³â°

**Ninth Circuit Rule:** The Ninth Circuit reached a similar conclusion in Yang Ming, finding no agency relationship where the vessel owner had not specifically directed the broker to purchase bunkers from the ultimate supplier.Â³Â¹

**PMSC Due Diligence Implication:** Acquirer should review PMSC's bunker fuel supply chain:

#### 1. Standard Change of Control Provisions in Ship Mortgages

**Industry Practice:** Virtually all ship mortgage loan agreements contain "change of control" provisions that constitute an **Event of Default** if the borrower's ownership changes without lender consent.Â³Â²

**Typical Covenant Language (based on maritime finance market standards):**

> "Borrower shall not, without the prior written consent of Lender, which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld:
> (a) Sell, transfer, assign, or otherwise dispose of any ownership interest in Borrower;
> (b) Merge, consolidate, or effect a change of control of Borrower;
> (c) Sell, mortgage, transfer, or charter the Vessel without Lender's consent."Â³Â³

**Policy Rationale:** Lenders extend ship mortgage financing based on:
- Borrower's maritime industry experience and operational expertise
- Borrower's creditworthiness and balance sheet strength

### D. VESSEL VALUATION AND LOAN-TO-VALUE (LTV) COVENANT ANALYSIS

#### 1. Three Standard Vessel Valuation Methodologies

Professional vessel appraisals employ three recognized approaches under USPAP (Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice):Â³â¹

**Market Approach (Comparable Sales Method):**

The most common method, involving benchmarking a vessel against recent sales of comparable vessels. Appraisers obtain details from similar (though not identical) transactions and adjust selling prices to account for the subject vessel's specific characteristics.â´â°

**Challenge:** Unlike real estate, there is no publicly available database of vessel sales transactions, and buyers/sellers have little incentive to divulge prices, making comparable data difficult to obtain.â´Â¹

**Cost Approach (Depreciated Replacement Cost):**

Values the vessel based on current replacement cost (what it would cost to build a new, identical vessel today) minus depreciation for age, physical deterioration, functional obsolescence, and economic obsolescence.â´Â²

**Challenge:** Quantifying depreciation is complex, as market conditions, technological advancements, and changing regulations (e.g., IMO environmental standards) all impact depreciation rates.â´Â³

**Income Approach (Discounted Cash Flow):**

Calculates the vessel's net income over its remaining operational life and discounts future cash flows to present value. Highly sensitive to freight rate forecasts, which are volatile in maritime markets.â´â´

**Best Practice:** Professional appraisers consider all three methods and reconcile findings to develop a comprehensive opinion of market value, as research shows using only one or two methods does not reach accurate conclusions.â´âµ

#### 3. LTV Covenant Breach Remedies

**Standard Ship Mortgage LTV Covenant:**

Ship mortgage agreements typically require vessels to maintain a maximum LTV ratio (e.g., 85-90% per vessel). The covenant protects lenders by ensuring collateral value exceeds the outstanding loan amount.â´â·

**Typical Covenant Language:**

> "The aggregate outstanding principal amount of all Loans shall not exceed 85% of the appraised market value of the Mortgaged Vessels as determined by independent appraisals conducted annually."â´â¸

**Cure Rights for Breach:**

If vessel values decline and the LTV ratio exceeds the covenant threshold, the borrower typically has **30 days to cure** by either:â´â¹
1. **Posting additional collateral** (mortgage another vessel, pledge cash)
2. **Prepaying loans** in an amount sufficient to restore LTV compliance

**Example:** If a vessel appraised at $150M declines to $120M, and the outstanding loan is $135M:
- Current LTV: 112.5% ($135M Ã· $120M)
- If covenant maximum is 90%, borrower must prepay $27M ($135M - [$120M Ã— 90%])
- OR post $27M additional collateral

**Event of Default:** While some agreements provide cure rights, **failure to cure within 30 days constitutes an Event of Default**, allowing the lender to accelerate the loan and foreclose on the vessel.âµâ°

**Enforceability:** The English court's decision in *The Alkyon* (2021) confirmed that LTV covenant breaches constitute valid Events of Default even where the borrower is current on principal and interest payments.âµÂ¹ However, lenders often prefer to give owners time to refinance or sell the vessel rather than force immediate foreclosure.

### E. VESSEL ARREST AND FORECLOSURE PROCEDURES

#### 1. In Rem Admiralty Jurisdiction - Vessel as "Defendant"

**Legal Doctrine:** Under federal admiralty law (28 U.S.C. Â§ 1333), a vessel is treated as a juridical person subject to *in rem* (against the thing) actions. Claimants with maritime liens can sue the vessel itself, not just the owner personally.â¶â°

**Policy Rationale:** Maritime law historically recognized the vessel as security for maritime debts, allowing creditors to pursue the vessel even if ownership changes. This doctrine protects suppliers, crew, and other claimants who provide services or credit based on the vessel's identity, not the owner's credit.

#### 2. Vessel Arrest Procedure - Supplemental Rule C

**Governing Authority:** Supplemental Rules for Admiralty or Maritime Claims and Asset Forfeiture Actions, Rule C (In Rem Actions: Special Provisions).â¶Â¹

**Step 1: Filing Verified Complaint**

The arresting party files a **verified complaint** in U.S. District Court (admiralty jurisdiction), which includes written verification under penalty of perjury attesting to the truth of the allegations.â¶Â² The complaint must:
- Identify the vessel by name, official number, and location
- State the basis for the maritime lien (e.g., unpaid necessaries, preferred mortgage default)
- Specify the amount claimed

**Step 2: Issuance of Warrant of Arrest**

If the court finds probable cause, the Clerk issues a **Warrant of Arrest in Rem**, directed to the U.S. Marshal.â¶Â³ The arresting party must submit a memorandum of law explaining why the warrant should issue.

**Step 3: Deposit for Marshal's Costs**

The plaintiff must provide a **cash deposit** (typically $10,000) to the U.S. Marshal for:â¶â´
- Cost of arresting the vessel
- Security services while vessel is under arrest
- Insurance during custody
- Wharfage and storage fees

**Step 4: Service by U.S. Marshal**

The U.S. Marshal or Deputy Marshal executes the warrant by physically serving it on the vessel. The Marshal takes **custody of the vessel** (custodia legis), typically by posting notice on the vessel's gangway and assuming control.â¶âµ

**Jurisdictional Limit:** Warrants may be executed only within the issuing district (exception: concurrent jurisdiction between Eastern and Southern Districts of New York).â¶â¶

**Step 5: Notice Requirements**

If the vessel is not released within **14 days** after execution of the warrant, the plaintiff must give **public notice** of the arrest as provided by Rule C(4), typically through publication in maritime trade journals.â¶â·

**Special Notice for Mortgagees:** If the arresting party is a mortgagee, it must provide **written notice to all known lienholders**.â¶â¸

#### 3. Vessel Owner's Right to Contest Arrest

The vessel owner or operator is entitled to a **prompt hearing** in court to contest the appropriateness of the seizure. Grounds for challenging the arrest include:â¶â¹
- Lack of maritime lien (claimant's debt does not qualify)
- Improper jurisdiction (vessel arrested in wrong district)
- Insufficient amount claimed (lien amount exaggerated)

**Release by Posting Security:** The owner can obtain release by posting a **surety bond** or **letter of undertaking** from a P&I club in an amount equal to the claim plus estimated interest and costs. This substitutes the bond for the vessel as security.â·â°

#### 4. Foreclosure Sale by U.S. Marshal

If the claimant prevails on proof of the lien and the owner does not satisfy the judgment, the court orders a **judicial sale** of the vessel by the U.S. Marshal.â·Â¹

**Marshal's Sale Procedure:**

1. **Appraisal:** Court-appointed appraiser values the vessel
2. **Public Advertisement:** Marshal publishes notice of sale (maritime press, local newspapers)
3. **Auction:** Vessel sold to highest bidder at public auction
4. **Marshal's Commission:** U.S. Marshal receives commission (typically 2-3% of sale price)
5. **Title Transfer:** Purchaser receives **Bill of Sale** from U.S. Marshal, conveying title **free and clear** of all liensâ·Â²

**Critical Legal Effect:** Under 46 U.S.C. Â§ 31326(a), when a vessel is sold by court order in an admiralty in rem action, "any claim in the vessel existing on the date of sale is terminated, and the vessel is sold free of all those claims."â·Â³ This provides purchasers with clean title, making U.S. Marshal's sales attractive for investors.

#### 5. Distribution of Sale Proceeds - Priority Waterfall

Sale proceeds are distributed according to the statutory priority hierarchy (46 U.S.C. Â§ 31326):â·â´

**Priority 1: Custodia Legis Expenses**
- U.S. Marshal's costs (arrest, custody, sale)
- Court costs
- Wharfage and insurance during custody

**Priority 2: Preferred Maritime Liens (Super-Priority)**
- Crew wages (seamen, officers)
- Stevedore wages
- Maritime tort damages
- Salvage
- General average

**Priority 3: Preferred Ship Mortgages**
- First preferred mortgage paid first
- Second preferred mortgage paid from surplus (if any)

**Priority 4: Maritime Contract Liens Arising Before Mortgage**
- Necessaries liens (repairs, fuel, supplies)
- Other contract claims

**Priority 5: Unsecured Creditors**
- Any remaining proceeds distributed pro rata

**Deficiency Judgment:** If sale proceeds are insufficient to satisfy the preferred mortgage, the lender has a **personal recourse claim** against the borrower for the deficiency (difference between debt and sale proceeds).â·âµ

#### 6. Example: Ship Mortgage Foreclosure with Multiple Lienholders

**Hypothetical (based on PMSC risk profile):**

M/V Pacific Titan (value $220M, mortgage $200M with JP Morgan) experiences a major casualty. PMSC stops making mortgage payments. JP Morgan forecloses, and the vessel is arrested and sold by U.S. Marshal for $180M (distressed sale discount).

**Claimants:**
1. U.S. Marshal custodia legis costs: $500K
2. Crew wages (unpaid wages during bankruptcy): $2M
3. Shipyard repair lien (emergency repairs before arrest): $8M
4. JP Morgan preferred mortgage: $200M balance

**Distribution:**
- U.S. Marshal: $500K (Priority 1)
- Crew wages: $2M (Priority 2 - super-priority)
- Remaining: $177.5M
- JP Morgan mortgage: $177.5M (Priority 3)
- Shipyard: **$0** (Priority 4 - subordinate to mortgage, no funds remaining)

**Deficiency:** JP Morgan has $22.5M deficiency claim against PMSC ($200M debt - $177.5M recovered).

**Key Insight:** Even though the shipyard's repair lien arose after JP Morgan's mortgage was recorded, the crew wage lien (Priority 2) has super-priority over the mortgage (Priority 3). The shipyard's necessaries lien ranks below the mortgage because the mortgage was recorded before the repairs were performed.

### F. VESSEL TITLE INSURANCE

#### 1. Maritime Title Insurance - Coverage and Purpose

**Definition:** Vessel title insurance protects purchasers and lenders against **undisclosed maritime liens** and defects in vessel ownership or mortgage priority.â·â¶

**Why Needed - Hidden Lien Risk:**

Unlike real estate liens (recorded in county registries), **maritime liens do not appear on public registers**. A necessaries lien, crew wage lien, or tort claim arises **automatically** when the debt is incurred, without any filing requirement.â·â· This creates risk for purchasers and lenders:

- Vessel may have unrecorded liens from prior operations
- Bunker suppliers, repair yards, or crew members may assert liens months or years after services rendered
- Maritime liens **survive changes of ownership**, even by good faith purchasersâ·â¸

**Example:** Acquirer purchases a vessel for $50M, unaware that the prior owner owes $1M in unpaid bunker fuel invoices. The bunker supplier arrests the vessel 6 months after purchase. Even though the acquirer bought the vessel in good faith, the bunker supplier's necessaries lien attaches to the vessel, and the acquirer must pay the $1M lien to release the vessel from arrest.

#### 2. Coverage Provided by Maritime Title Insurance

**Standard Coverage:**

Maritime title insurance policies cover:â·â¹
- **Undisclosed maritime liens** (necessaries, crew wages, tort claims)
- **Ownership disputes** (rival claimants to vessel title)
- **Defective mortgage recording** (errors in NVDC filings affecting preferred mortgage priority)
- **Document forgery** (fraudulent bills of sale or mortgage documents)
- **Errors in vessel documentation** (defective Coast Guard documentation)

**Exclusions:**

Typical policies exclude:
- **Known liens** (disclosed in due diligence)
- **Post-policy liens** (arising after policy effective date)
- **Environmental contamination** (pollution cleanup costs require separate OPA 90 insurance)
- **Governmental fines and penalties**

#### 3. Underwriters and Premium Costs

**Specialized Underwriters:**

Maritime title insurance is a niche product offered by specialized marine insurance underwriters, including:â¸â°
- **NorthStandard** (formerly North of England P&I Club)
- **UK P&I Club**
- **West of England P&I Club**
- **Shipowners' Club**
- **Commercial marine insurance underwriters** (Lloyd's of London syndicates)

**Note:** INTERTANKO (International Association of Independent Tanker Owners) is a trade association, not an insurance provider. However, INTERTANKO members often use the P&I clubs listed above.

**Premium Calculation:**

Maritime title insurance premiums are calculated as a **one-time premium** based on vessel value:â¸Â¹
- **Rate:** 0.5-1.0% of vessel value
- **PMSC Fleet:** 22 vessels, $3.2B appraised value
- **Estimated Premium:** $16M-$32M (one-time cost at closing)

**Premium Factors:**
- **Vessel age and type:** Older vessels with complex trading histories = higher premiums
- **Prior ownership:** Multiple prior owners = higher lien search costs
- **Trading routes:** Vessels trading in high-risk jurisdictions = higher premiums
- **Distressed sales:** Bankruptcy sales or foreclosure purchases = higher premiums due to elevated lien risk

#### 4. Due Diligence Benefit of Title Insurance

**Underwriter's Pre-Binding Lien Search:**

Before issuing a policy, the title insurance underwriter conducts a **comprehensive lien search**:â¸Â²
- USCG NVDC records (preferred mortgages, recorded liens)
- UCC-1 financing statements (state-level security interests)
- Federal court dockets (pending admiralty cases, arrests)
- Crew wage payment verification
- Bunker fuel supplier invoices
- Shipyard and repair yard invoice audits

If the lien search reveals undisclosed liens, the underwriter either:
- **Excludes the lien** from coverage (scheduled exception)
- **Requires payoff** before binding coverage
- **Increases premium** to reflect risk

**PMSC Application:** Given the $2.8B in existing ship mortgages and 87.5% LTV ratio, lenders may **require** the acquirer to obtain title insurance as a closing condition. This provides dual benefits:
1. **Protects acquirer** from undisclosed lien surprises
2. **Protects lenders** by ensuring their preferred mortgages have unimpaired priority
