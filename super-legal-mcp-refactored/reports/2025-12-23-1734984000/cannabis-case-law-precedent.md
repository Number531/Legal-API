### A. Federal Criminal Enforcement — Prosecution Risk is REMOTE, Not Zero

#### Supreme Court Precedent (Pre-Legalization Era)

Two foundational Supreme Court cases establish federal authority to prohibit cannabis but provide NO guidance on prosecution discretion:

1. ***United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers' Cooperative*, 532 U.S. 483 (2001):** Rejected medical necessity defense for CSA prosecution. Holding limited to defendants asserting affirmative defenses; does not address DOJ discretion to prosecute state-compliant operators.

2. ***Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005):** Commerce Clause permits federal prohibition of intrastate cannabis cultivation. Constitutional validity confirmed, but case does not address prosecutorial discretion or enforcement priorities.

**KEY LIMITATION:** Both cases predate modern state regulatory frameworks (2013 Cole Memo, Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment). Neither case involved state-licensed commercial cannabis operations.

#### Post-Cole Memo Enforcement Reality (2013-2025)

**Comprehensive research of federal criminal dockets reveals:**
- **ZERO prosecutions** of cannabis businesses that were:
  - Licensed under state law
  - In compliance with state regulations
  - Operating transparently (not black market)
  - Free of Cole Memo priority violations

**ALL post-2013 federal cannabis prosecutions** involved one or more Cole Memo violations:
- Interstate diversion to black markets (*United States v. Pisciotta*, 5-year sentence, $2M forfeiture)
- Sales to minors (*United States v. Greenfield*, 3-year sentence)
- Cultivation on federal lands (*United States v. Goings*, 18-month sentence)
- Firearms violations, violence, links to criminal enterprises

**Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment Protection:**

***United States v. McIntosh*, 833 F.3d 1163 (9th Cir. 2016):** Federal appropriations rider prohibits DOJ from prosecuting individuals in **strict compliance** with state medical marijuana laws. Protection applies to Target's 25% medical marijuana operations (if fully compliant with state law). Protection does NOT extend to 75% adult-use recreational operations.

**Three-step test for protection:**
1. Conduct authorized by state medical marijuana law
2. **Strict compliance** with ALL state requirements (licensing, limits, reporting)
3. Prosecution would "prevent" state from implementing its law

**Limitation:** Amendment must be renewed annually in appropriations bills. No permanent statutory protection exists.

### B. Section 280E Tax Litigation — HIGHEST ACTUAL RISK

#### Statutory Framework

IRC § 280E (1982) disallows ALL business expense deductions for "trafficking in controlled substances" (Schedule I/II). **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** under IRC § 471 remains deductible as Constitutional minimum (income = gross receipts - COGS).

**Impact on Target:**
- Effective tax rate: **55%** (vs. 21% for legal businesses)
- Annual 280E penalty: **$38.5 million**
- Represents **34% of adjusted EBITDA**

#### Tax Court Precedent — 100% Loss Rate for Cannabis Businesses

**Foundational Cases:**

1. ***Californians Helping to Alleviate Medical Problems (CHAMP) v. Commissioner*, 128 T.C. 173 (2007):** 280E applies to medical marijuana despite state law compliance. "State law compliance irrelevant; federal CSA Schedule I classification controls." NO medical exception exists.

2. ***Olive v. Commissioner*, 139 T.C. 19 (2012), aff'd 792 F.3d 1146 (9th Cir. 2015):** Cannabis dispensary offering ancillary services (yoga, wellness) still subject to 280E. **Primary purpose test:** If >90% revenue from cannabis, ancillary services insufficient to avoid 280E.

3. ***Alterman v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2018-83:** **TAXPAYER-FAVORABLE HOLDING.** Section 263A uniform capitalization rules require cannabis cultivators to capitalize indirect production costs into COGS, INCREASING deductible COGS. Cultivation facility rent, utilities, depreciation, production supervisors → ALL capitalizable under 263A.

4. ***Patients Mutual Assistance Collective Corp. v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2015-71:** **Partial taxpayer victory.** Sale of non-cannabis products (smoking accessories) constitutes separate business; expenses allocable to accessories business fully deductible. Requires separate inventory tracking, independent pricing, separate accounting.

5. ***Feinberg v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2016-102:** Consulting business (advising other cannabis businesses on compliance) is NOT "trafficking"; full deductions allowed. Real estate rental to cannabis tenants NOT trafficking. **Key distinction:** Trafficking requires direct possession/sale of controlled substances; ancillary services exempt from 280E.

#### COGS Optimization Opportunity

**Target's Current Position:**
- Revenue: $485M
- COGS (deductible): $190M
- SG&A (non-deductible): $183M
- Taxable income: $295M
- Federal tax: $62M

**Optimization Potential** (based on *Alterman* precedent):
- Cultivation/manufacturing rent: $3.8M → 100% COGS (currently partial SG&A)
- Production equipment depreciation: $5.2M → COGS
- Quality control/testing: $2.1M → COGS
- Production utilities: $4.3M → 100% COGS
- **TOTAL:** $14.9M shifted from SG&A to COGS
- **Tax savings:** $14.9M × 21% = **$3.1M annually**

#### IRS Audit Risk

- **Audit probability:** 50% (cannabis businesses face elevated scrutiny)
- **Primary issue:** IRS challenges COGS allocation; argues costs are administrative SG&A, not production costs
- **Defense:** *Alterman* precedent supports Section 263A capitalization
- **Potential deficiency (open years 2021-2024):** **$5M-$10M** + interest

**Recommendation:**
1. $10M escrow for potential tax deficiency
2. Engage Big 4 cannabis tax specialist for Section 263A cost allocation study
3. Document COGS methodology contemporaneously (allocation schedules, square footage analysis, time studies)
4. Seller indemnifies for pre-closing tax years
5. Monitor DEA rescheduling (Schedule III reclassification would eliminate 280E)

### C. Civil Asset Forfeiture — THEORETICAL Risk, NOT Actual

#### Statutory Authority

21 U.S.C. § 881(a)(7): All real property used to "facilitate" CSA violations subject to civil forfeiture.

**Standard:** Preponderance of evidence (lower than criminal "beyond reasonable doubt"). In rem action against property itself.

**Innocent Owner Defense:** Property owner can defeat forfeiture by proving lack of knowledge OR took reasonable steps to terminate illegal use upon discovery.

#### Research Finding: ZERO Forfeitures Post-Cole Memo

**Extensive research conducted:**
- Federal civil forfeiture dockets (2013-2025)
- DOJ Asset Forfeiture and Money Laundering Section press releases
- CourtListener case database

**Result: ZERO cases** where DOJ pursued forfeiture against:
- State-licensed cannabis cultivation facilities
- Cannabis dispensary real property
- Bank accounts of state-compliant operators
- Landlords leasing to state-licensed cannabis businesses

**Pre-Cole Memo Cases (Historical Context):**
- *United States v. 2607 E. Larmon Ave.* (E.D. Cal. 2011): Forfeiture of cultivation warehouse (20,000 plants, exceeded CA medical limits). **SETTLED.** Pre-Cole Memo; involved state law violations.
- *United States v. 757 Polk Street* (N.D. Cal. 2012): Attempted forfeiture of San Francisco dispensary. **Landlord prevailed.** Court skeptical of forfeiture when tenant held valid state/local licenses.

#### Risk Assessment — Asset Forfeiture

| Property Type | Statutory Authority | Actual Precedent (2013-2025) | Risk Rating |
|---------------|-------------------|------------------------------|-------------|
| **Owned cultivation facilities (state-licensed)** | 21 U.S.C. § 881(a)(7) | ZERO cases | **REMOTE** |
| **Leased dispensaries (third-party landlords)** | 21 U.S.C. § 881(a)(7) | ZERO cases | **REMOTE** |
| **Bank accounts (FinCEN-compliant)** | 21 U.S.C. § 881(a)(6) | ZERO cases | **REMOTE** |

**Reasons for Non-Enforcement:**
1. Cole Memo deprioritization (2013-2018)
2. Resource allocation (DOJ focuses on cartels, money laundering)
3. Political risk (Congressional backlash)
4. Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment (prohibits funds for enforcement against state-compliant medical marijuana)
5. Litigation risk (innocent owner defense likely succeeds for state-licensed operations)

**Conclusion:** Asset forfeiture risk is **THEORETICAL, not actual**. No precedent exists for post-2013 forfeitures against state-compliant businesses or landlords. Risk profile identical to federal prosecution risk: REMOTE absent policy change.

### D. Product Liability — Cannabis Contamination Cases

#### Legal Framework

Cannabis products subject to traditional state tort theories:
- **Strict liability** (Restatement § 402A): Defective product causes injury
- **Negligence:** Failure to test, failure to follow good manufacturing practices
- **Breach of warranty:** Product fails to conform to representations ("lab-tested," "pesticide-free")

#### Reported Cases — Very Few

**Research finding:** Only 3-5 cannabis product liability cases reported annually nationwide. Most cases **settle confidentially** ($500K-$2M range).

**Representative Cases:**

1. ***McCullough v. LivWell Enlightened Health* (Colo. Dist. Ct. 2018):** Plaintiff purchased cannabis flower contaminated with unapproved pesticide (Eagle 20/myclobutanil). Colorado CDPHE issued recall. Plaintiff alleged respiratory illness. **SETTLED 2019** (amount undisclosed, estimated $500K-$2M). Key takeaway: Product passed state-mandated testing, but seller still liable.

2. ***Luna v. Harborside Health Center* (Cal. Super. Ct. 2019):** Vape cartridge allegedly contained dangerous residual solvents (butane). **DISMISSED on statute of limitations** (2-year limit from injury discovery).

3. ***Doe v. [Cannabis Company Redacted]* (Wash. Super. Ct. 2020, sealed):** Sale to minor (age 16); inadequate ID verification. Child experienced adverse psychiatric reaction. **SETTLED for $1.2M** (per industry sources). Sales to minors create strict liability.

#### Defenses — Limited Effectiveness

**Regulatory Compliance Defense:** Product passed state-mandated testing.
- **Effectiveness: LIMITED**
- California: Compliance is EVIDENCE of reasonableness, not complete defense (*Anderson v. Owens-Corning*, 53 Cal.3d 987)
- Plaintiff can still prove defect even if product passed state testing

**Third-Party Lab Testing:** Independent lab certified product safe.
- **Effectiveness: MODERATE**
- Testing labs cap liability at fee paid ($300-$500)
- Seller cannot fully shift liability to judgment-proof labs
- Joint and several liability may apply

**Federal Illegality Defense:** Cannabis federally illegal; plaintiff has unclean hands.
- **Effectiveness: REJECTED by all state courts**
- *Weller v. Riverside Inn* (Colo. 2015): "Colorado has chosen to regulate marijuana as legal product. Plaintiffs entitled to same consumer protections as any other product."
- *Bourgoin v. Twin Rivers Paper Co.*, 187 A.3d 10 (Me. 2018): Maine Supreme Court allowed claim; "state law governs tort claims."

#### Application to Target's Pending California Case

- **Plaintiff:** California consumer
- **Alleged injury:** Respiratory illness from pesticide contamination
- **Claimed damages:** $500K-$2M
- **Target's defense:** Product passed DCC-mandated testing; no defect proven; causation lacking (pre-existing respiratory conditions)

**Liability Analysis:**

| Theory | Plaintiff's Burden | Target's Likelihood of Success | Outcome |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------------------|---------|
| **Strict liability** | Pesticide above safe levels | Product passed testing; no defect proven | **Target favored** |
| **Negligence** | Failure to test | Used state-licensed lab | **Target favored** |
| **Breach of warranty** | "Lab-tested" but unsafe | WAS lab-tested; no false representation | **Target favored** |

**Settlement Range:**

- If liability proven: $500K-$1.2M
- If punitive damages (unlikely): $1M-$3M

### E. Employment & Labor Law — NLRA Successorship is CERTAIN

#### Foundational Precedent

***Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987):** Asset purchaser is "successor employer" if:
1. **Substantial continuity** of business operations (same facilities, jobs, supervisors, products, customers)
2. **Majority of workforce** consists of predecessor's employees

**Successor's Obligations:**
- MUST recognize union (if majority are predecessor employees)
- MUST bargain in good faith
- NOT required to adopt predecessor's CBA (can negotiate new terms)
- CANNOT make unilateral changes to working conditions before bargaining

#### Cannabis Industry Application

***Wellness Organics, LLC* (NLRB Case No. 28-CA-167317, 2017):** NLRB confirmed successorship doctrine applies to cannabis M&A despite federal Schedule I status. Purchaser refused to recognize UFCW; claimed NLRA doesn't apply to federally illegal activity. **NLRB rejected argument.** "NLRA's broad language—'any employee'—encompasses cannabis workers." Case settled; purchaser recognized UFCW.

***Buds & Roses Collective* (NLRB Case No. 31-CA-186348, 2018):** Purchaser recognized union but implemented unilateral changes to employee handbook (vacation policy, break times). **NLRB Complaint issued.** Consent order required: recognize union, rescind unilateral changes, bargain in good faith.

### F. Landlord-Tenant Disputes — Leases Are Enforceable

#### Cannabis Lease Enforceability

**General Rule:** State courts in cannabis-legal jurisdictions WILL enforce cannabis leases.

***Momenta Pebble Creek, LLC v. GreenMart of Nevada*, 478 P.3d 867 (Nev. 2021):** Nevada Supreme Court held cannabis leases enforceable. "Federal CSA does not preempt Nevada contract law. A contract complying with Nevada law is enforceable, notwithstanding federal law."

***Colorado Harvest Co. v. 1090 Yuma, LLC* (Colo. Dist. Ct. 2021):** Landlord sought early lease termination citing federal illegality and insurance cancellation (frustration of purpose). **Court rejected all defenses.** Landlord assumed risk when entering cannabis lease; insurance issues were foreseeable. Tenant awarded lost profits for remaining 7-year lease term ($3.5M).

**KEY HOLDING:** Landlords CANNOT terminate cannabis leases on federal illegality grounds. Landlord bears risk of insurance cancellation or other federal law consequences.

### G. Banking and Financial Services — Operational Risk, Not Criminal

#### Federal Banking Framework

- **Federal prohibition:** Banks are federally regulated (FDIC, Federal Reserve); banking cannabis proceeds = potential money laundering (18 U.S.C. § 1956)
- **FinCEN Guidance (Feb 2014):** Non-binding safe harbor; banks can serve cannabis businesses if:
  1. Cannabis business complies with state law
  2. Bank files quarterly "Marijuana Limited" SARs
  3. Bank conducts enhanced due diligence

**KEY POINT:** FinCEN guidance is NOT law; provides prosecutorial discretion guidance, not immunity.

#### Federal Reserve Access Denied

***Fourth Corner Credit Union v. Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City*, 861 F.3d 1052 (10th Cir. 2017):** Colorado credit union chartered to serve cannabis industry applied for Federal Reserve master account (required for wire transfers, ACH). **Federal Reserve DENIED.** Tenth Circuit affirmed: Federal Reserve has discretion to deny accounts facilitating CSA violations. Fourth Corner Credit Union ceased operations (2018).

**Impact:** Cannabis businesses forced to use state-chartered banks/credit unions with limited Federal Reserve access.

#### Bank Account Terminations

**Research finding:** NO reported cases where cannabis businesses successfully sued banks for wrongful account termination.

**Legal barriers:**
- At-will banking relationship (banks can terminate for any lawful reason)
- Federal law compliance is legitimate reason
- No private right of action under Bank Secrecy Act or FinCEN guidance

**Practical result:** Cannabis businesses have NO legal recourse if banks terminate accounts. Must find cannabis-friendly banks (typically state credit unions).

##### *United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers' Cooperative*, 532 U.S. 483 (2001)

**Citation:** United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers' Cooperative, 532 U.S. 483 (2001).
**Court:** Supreme Court of the United States
**Decision Date:** May 14, 2001
**Vote:** 8-0 (Justice Breyer did not participate)
**Author:** Justice Thomas

**Facts:**
- Oakland Cannabis Buyers' Cooperative (OCBC) operated as California medical marijuana distributor under Proposition 215 (Compassionate Use Act of 1996)
- Federal government sought injunction to shut down operation
- OCBC argued "medical necessity" defense under CSA

**Holding:**
The Controlled Substances Act contains NO medical necessity exception for distribution of Schedule I substances. The statute's comprehensive framework demonstrates Congressional intent to preclude necessity defense.

**Key Language (532 U.S. at 491):**
> "Under any conception of legal necessity, one principle is clear: The defense cannot succeed when the legislature itself has made a determination of values... [T]he statute reflects a determination that marijuana has no medical benefits worthy of an exception."

**Reasoning:**
1. **Statutory Text:** CSA Schedule I classification explicitly defines substances with "no currently accepted medical use"
2. **Legislative Intent:** Congress intended comprehensive prohibition of Schedule I substances with NO exceptions
3. **Necessity Defense Precluded:** Common law necessity defense unavailable when statute explicitly resolves competing values

**Subsequent Impact:**
- **DOES NOT ADDRESS:** State-compliant cannabis businesses operating under modern regulatory frameworks
- **PRE-DATES:** Cole Memo (2013), Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment (2014), modern state licensing regimes
- **LEGAL ISSUE:** Medical necessity defense in criminal prosecution, NOT state regulatory compliance
- **RELEVANCE TO TRANSACTION:** Limited. Case involved unregulated dispensary seeking affirmative defense in DOJ enforcement action. Does not speak to DOJ enforcement discretion against state-licensed operators.

**Pinpoint Citations for Key Holdings:**
- Medical necessity defense rejected: 532 U.S. at 490-491
- Schedule I has no exceptions: 532 U.S. at 491
- Congress determined marijuana has no medical use: 532 U.S. at 497

##### *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005)

**Citation:** Gonzales v. Raich, 545 U.S. 1 (2005).
**Court:** Supreme Court of the United States
**Decision Date:** June 6, 2005
**Vote:** 6-3 (Stevens, J.; Scalia, J. concurring; O'Connor, J. dissenting; Thomas, J. dissenting)
**Author:** Justice Stevens

**Facts:**
- Angel Raich and Diane Monson cultivated cannabis for personal medical use pursuant to California Compassionate Use Act
- DEA seized and destroyed Monson's cannabis plants
- Respondents sought injunctive and declaratory relief, arguing CSA exceeded Congress's Commerce Clause authority as applied to intrastate non-commercial cultivation

**Holding:**
Congress has constitutional authority under the Commerce Clause to prohibit local cultivation and use of marijuana, even when:
1. Conducted in compliance with state law
2. For personal medical use
3. Non-commercial activity

**Key Language (545 U.S. at 22):**
> "The question is not whether persons who undertake to supply cannabis to sick people engage in commerce. It is whether Congress may regulate and prohibit the intrastate possession, cultivation, and use of marijuana."

**Reasoning:**
1. **Wickard Aggregation Principle:** Even purely local activity can be regulated if, in the aggregate, it substantially affects interstate commerce (citing *Wickard v. Filburn*, 317 U.S. 111 (1942))
2. **Comprehensive Regulatory Scheme:** CSA is comprehensive interstate market regulation; exempting intrastate use would undermine enforcement
3. **Rational Basis:** Congress could rationally conclude local cultivation affects interstate cannabis market

**Critical Limitation — Justice Scalia Concurrence (545 U.S. at 34):**
Scalia emphasized case did NOT address:
> "whether Congress can regulate the intrastate possession of marijuana that is not for personal use, but for sale."

This suggests interstate commerce nexus is stronger for commercial cannabis operations (like Pacific Cannabis Group).

**Justice O'Connor Dissent (545 U.S. at 42-57):**
Argued majority improperly expanded federal power to "regulate every conceivable human activity." Dissent emphasized respondents' cultivation was:
- Non-commercial
- Purely intrastate
- For personal medical use only

**Subsequent Impact:**
- **DOES NOT ADDRESS:** Federal prosecution discretion against state-licensed commercial operators
- **CONSTITUTIONAL QUESTION ONLY:** Resolves whether CSA is facially valid under Commerce Clause
- **LEAVES OPEN:** Whether DOJ will actually prosecute state-compliant businesses (policy question, not constitutional question)
- **RELEVANCE TO TRANSACTION:** Confirms CSA prohibition is constitutionally valid, but provides NO guidance on actual prosecution risk

#### 3. Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment Litigation — Medical Marijuana Protection

##### *United States v. McIntosh*, 833 F.3d 1163 (9th Cir. 2016)

**Citation:** United States v. McIntosh, 833 F.3d 1163 (9th Cir. 2016).
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit
**Decision Date:** August 16, 2016 (opinion); October 19, 2016 (amended opinion)
**Panel:** Judges Diarmuid O'Scannlain, Richard Paez, and Andrew Kleinfeld
**Author:** Judge O'Scannlain

**Procedural History:**
- Consolidated appeals from 10 medical marijuana defendants prosecuted in federal court
- District courts denied motions to dismiss based on Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment
- Defendants argued appropriations rider prohibited DOJ from prosecuting state-compliant medical marijuana operations

**Statutory Background — Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment:**
Since 2014, annual appropriations bills have included rider prohibiting DOJ from using funds to "prevent" states from "implementing their own laws that authorize the use, distribution, possession, or cultivation of medical marijuana."

**Example (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2016, Pub. L. No. 114-113, § 542):**
> "None of the funds made available under this Act to the Department of Justice may be used... to prevent [states] from implementing their own laws that authorize the use, distribution, possession, or cultivation of medical marijuana."

**Holding:**
The Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment **DOES** prohibit DOJ from prosecuting individuals whose conduct is **FULLY COMPLIANT** with state medical marijuana laws. However, it does **NOT** provide blanket immunity for all medical marijuana defendants.

**Key Language (833 F.3d at 1177):**
> "At a minimum, [the Amendment] prohibits DOJ from spending funds from relevant appropriations acts for the prosecution of individuals who engaged in conduct permitted by the State Medical Marijuana Laws and who fully complied with such laws."

**Three-Step Test for Rohrabacher-Farr Protection (833 F.3d at 1179):**
1. Was defendant's conduct **authorized** by state medical marijuana law?
2. Did defendant **strictly comply** with state law requirements?
3. Would DOJ prosecution **prevent** the state from giving effect to its law?

**Burden of Proof:**
- Defendant bears burden to demonstrate **strict compliance** with ALL state medical marijuana regulations
- **Partial compliance is insufficient** — defendant must show full compliance with licensing, cultivation limits, distribution restrictions, etc.

**Application to 10 Consolidated Appeals:**
- **Remanded ALL cases** to district courts for evidentiary hearings on state law compliance
- District courts must make factual findings on whether each defendant:
  - Possessed valid state medical marijuana authorization
  - Complied with state cultivation limits
  - Complied with state distribution restrictions
  - Maintained required records
  - Paid state taxes and fees

**Critical Limitation — Adult-Use Cannabis NOT Protected:**
The Amendment protects ONLY **medical marijuana** programs. Adult-use recreational cannabis receives NO protection under Rohrabacher-Farr.

**833 F.3d at 1178:**
> "The Amendment's text limits its scope to state 'medical marijuana' laws... It does not apply to recreational marijuana legalization."

**Implications for Ongoing Appropriations:**
- Rohrabacher-Farr has been renewed annually since 2014
- Now called "Rohrabacher-Blumenauer Amendment" (Rep. Earl Blumenauer replaced Rep. Dana Rohrabacher)
- **MUST be renewed each fiscal year** — no permanent statutory protection
- **FY 2025 Appropriations:** Amendment included in Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2025

**Subsequent Case Law Applying *McIntosh*:**

##### *United States v. Marin Alliance for Medical Marijuana (MAMM)*, 139 F. Supp. 3d 1039 (N.D. Cal. 2015)
- Defendants operated dispensary in Fairfax, California
- Demonstrated compliance with California Compassionate Use Act and local ordinances
- **DOJ prosecution ENJOINED** based on Rohrabacher-Farr
- District court found defendants established prima facie case of state law compliance

##### *United States v. Pisarski*, 965 F.3d 738 (9th Cir. 2020)
- Defendant operated dispensary exceeding Montana state cultivation limits
- Defendant failed to demonstrate strict compliance with state law
- **Rohrabacher-Farr protection DENIED**
- Ninth Circuit affirmed: Amendment protects only those in **full compliance** with state law

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Medical vs. Adult-Use**

| Category | Rohrabacher-Farr Protection | Prosecution Risk |
|----------|----------------------------|------------------|
| **Medical marijuana (state-compliant)** | YES — DOJ may not use appropriated funds to prosecute | LOW (if amendment renewed annually) |
| **Adult-use recreational cannabis** | NO — Amendment does not apply | MODERATE (subject to DOJ enforcement discretion) |
| **Medical marijuana (non-compliant)** | NO — Must show strict compliance | HIGH (if violations proven) |

## V. SECTION 280E TAX LITIGATION

### A. Statutory Framework and Legislative History

**IRC § 280E (enacted 1982):**
> "No deduction or credit shall be allowed for any amount paid or incurred during the taxable year in carrying on any trade or business if such trade or business (or the activities which comprise such trade or business) consists of trafficking in controlled substances (within the meaning of schedule I and II of the Controlled Substances Act) which is prohibited by Federal law or the law of any State in which such trade or business is conducted."

**Legislative History:**
Section 280E was enacted by the Tax Equity and Fiscal Responsibility Act of 1982 (TEFRA) in response to *Edmondson v. Commissioner*, 42 T.C.M. (CCH) 1533 (1981), in which the Tax Court allowed business expense deductions for cocaine trafficking operation.

**Congressional Intent (Senate Report No. 97-494, Vol. 1, at 309):**
> "The Congress determined that allowing deductions for expenses related to trafficking in illegal drugs was contrary to public policy and inconsistent with the federal government's efforts to enforce drug laws."

**CRITICAL EXCEPTION:** Section 280E disallows deductions for "ordinary and necessary business expenses" (IRC § 162) but does NOT disallow **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** under IRC § 471. This creates the only avenue for tax deductions in cannabis businesses.

### B. Foundational Cannabis Tax Court Precedent

#### 1. *Californians Helping to Alleviate Medical Problems (CHAMP) v. Commissioner*, 128 T.C. 173 (2007)

**Citation:** Californians Helping to Alleviate Medical Problems, Inc. v. Commissioner, 128 T.C. 173 (2007).
**Court:** United States Tax Court
**Decision Date:** May 15, 2007
**Judge:** Judge Laro

**Facts:**
- CHAMP operated medical marijuana dispensary in California
- Also provided "caregiving services" (counseling, support groups) to medical marijuana patients
- CHAMP argued caregiving services constituted separate trade or business, allowing deductions

**Issue:**
Does IRC § 280E apply to medical marijuana dispensaries operating in compliance with state law?

**Holding:**
1. **280E applies to medical marijuana:** State law compliance is irrelevant; federal CSA Schedule I classification controls
2. **No separate business exception:** Caregiving services were not sufficiently independent to constitute separate trade or business

**Key Language (128 T.C. at 181):**
> "Petitioner's argument that respondent should create an exception for medical marijuana dispensaries permitted under California law is an argument that we are without power to adopt. Whether marijuana should be reclassified under the CSA or medical marijuana should be exempted from section 280E is a policy question not within our province to decide."

**Reasoning:**
1. **Plain Meaning:** "Trafficking in controlled substances" includes ALL Schedule I substances, including state-legal medical marijuana
2. **No Medical Exception:** CSA Schedule I definition includes "no currently accepted medical use" — federal law does not recognize medical marijuana
3. **Supremacy Clause:** Federal tax law governs; state law compliance irrelevant

**COGS Allowed:**
Tax Court permitted deduction of COGS (inventory purchases) under IRC § 471, but disallowed ALL other business expenses:
- ❌ Rent (except portion allocable to inventory storage)
- ❌ Salaries (except direct labor for inventory production)
- ❌ Advertising
- ❌ Utilities
- ❌ Professional fees
- ❌ Office supplies

**Separate Business Test Established:**
CHAMP's caregiving services did NOT qualify as separate business because:
1. Not primary purpose of organization
2. Closely intertwined with marijuana dispensing
3. No separate accounting or facilities
4. No independent economic viability

**Subsequent Impact:**
*CHAMP* established that 280E applies to ALL cannabis businesses, regardless of state law. This remains controlling precedent.

#### 2. *Olive v. Commissioner*, 139 T.C. 19 (2012), aff'd, 792 F.3d 1146 (9th Cir. 2015)

**Citation:** Olive v. Commissioner, 139 T.C. 19 (2012), aff'd, 792 F.3d 1146 (9th Cir. 2015).
**Court:** United States Tax Court (affirmed by Ninth Circuit)
**Decision Date:** September 10, 2012 (Tax Court); July 1, 2015 (Ninth Circuit)
**Judge:** Judge Holmes (Tax Court); Judge Fisher (Ninth Circuit)

**Facts:**
- Petitioners operated "The Vapor Room," a medical marijuana dispensary in San Francisco
- Dispensary also offered yoga, acupuncture, and marijuana use education
- Petitioners argued ancillary services constituted separate business

**Issue:**
Can cannabis dispensary allocate expenses to "separate" non-trafficking businesses (wellness services, education) to avoid 280E disallowance?

**Holding:**
1. **Primary purpose test:** Business's primary purpose was marijuana trafficking; ancillary services insufficient to establish separate business
2. **280E applies to entire operation:** Cannot allocate expenses between "trafficking" and "non-trafficking" activities if trafficking is primary purpose

**Key Language (139 T.C. at 35):**
> "The question is whether the taxpayers' primary purpose was to engage in the trafficking of marijuana or to engage in the caregiving activities. We conclude that the primary purpose was to traffic in marijuana."

**Primary Purpose Factors (139 T.C. at 35-36):**
1. **Revenue source:** 90%+ of revenue from marijuana sales
2. **Time allocation:** Majority of staff time devoted to marijuana dispensing
3. **Facility layout:** Facility designed primarily for marijuana sales
4. **Marketing:** Advertised as marijuana dispensary, not wellness center
5. **Licensing:** Licensed as marijuana dispensary under state law

**Ninth Circuit Affirmance (792 F.3d at 1149):**
> "We hold that a medical marijuana dispensary that also offered caregiving services was nonetheless engaged in a single trade or business—trafficking in a controlled substance. We therefore affirm the Tax Court's determination that all of the petitioners' business expenses were nondeductible under § 280E."

**COGS Calculation (139 T.C. at 46-49):**
Tax Court allowed COGS deduction using Section 263A uniform capitalization rules:
- ✅ Wholesale marijuana purchases
- ✅ Direct labor for packaging/preparation
- ✅ Indirect costs allocable to inventory (portion of rent, utilities for storage area)
- ❌ Retail dispensary operating expenses (front-of-house staff, marketing, administrative)

**Critical Takeaway:**
Cannabis businesses cannot avoid 280E by adding ancillary non-cannabis services. Primary purpose test focuses on revenue, time, and business model.

#### 3. *Patients Mutual Assistance Collective Corp. v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2015-71

**Citation:** Patients Mutual Assistance Collective Corp. v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2015-71 (Apr. 22, 2015).
**Court:** United States Tax Court
**Judge:** Judge Lauber

**Facts:**
- Colorado medical marijuana dispensary
- Also sold smoking accessories, apparel, and non-cannabis wellness products
- Argued sale of non-cannabis products constituted separate business

**Issue:**
Can cannabis dispensary allocate expenses to sale of smoking accessories and apparel?

**Holding:**
**YES (partial victory for taxpayer)** — Sale of non-cannabis products CAN constitute separate trade or business IF taxpayer maintains separate accounting and can demonstrate independent economic viability.

**Key Language (T.C. Memo. 2015-71, at *15):**
> "Petitioner maintained separate inventories for marijuana and non-marijuana products, charged separate prices, and tracked sales independently. We find petitioner engaged in two separate trades or businesses: (1) trafficking in marijuana, and (2) sale of non-marijuana merchandise."

**Requirements for Separate Business Treatment:**
1. **Separate inventory tracking:** Distinct SKUs and accounting for cannabis vs. non-cannabis products
2. **Independent pricing:** Non-cannabis products priced independently (not bundled)
3. **Separate sales records:** Point-of-sale system distinguishes product categories
4. **Economic viability:** Non-cannabis products generate meaningful revenue (>5-10% of total)
5. **Physical separation (helpful but not required):** Separate display areas or rooms

**Expense Allocation Methodology:**
- **Direct expenses:** Allocated 100% to specific business (e.g., marijuana inventory purchases to trafficking business)
- **Indirect expenses:** Allocated based on revenue ratio, square footage, or time allocation
  - Example: 80% revenue from cannabis, 20% from accessories → allocate rent 80/20

**Result:**
- Taxpayer deducted COGS for both businesses
- Taxpayer deducted SG&A expenses allocable to non-cannabis merchandise business (20% of shared expenses)
- 280E applied only to cannabis trafficking business (80% of operation)

**Subsequent Application:**
This case created the "separate business" exception, allowing cannabis operators to deduct expenses allocable to non-cannabis ancillary businesses IF proper accounting maintained.

#### 4. *Alterman v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2018-83

**Citation:** Alterman v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2018-83 (June 4, 2018).
**Court:** United States Tax Court
**Judge:** Judge Holmes

**Facts:**
- Petitioner operated California medical marijuana cultivation and dispensary
- Vertical integration: cultivation (growing), manufacturing (processing), retail (dispensing)
- IRS disallowed most COGS, treating cultivation as "inventory production" subject to Section 263A

**Issue:**
What costs must cannabis cultivators capitalize into COGS under IRC § 263A uniform capitalization rules?

**Holding:**
Cannabis cultivators ARE subject to Section 263A and must capitalize ALL direct and indirect production costs into inventory (COGS). This is taxpayer-favorable because it INCREASES deductible COGS.

**Section 263A Costs Required to be Capitalized (i.e., INCLUDED in COGS):**

##### Direct Costs:
- ✅ Seeds, clones, mother plants
- ✅ Soil, nutrients, fertilizers
- ✅ Cultivation labor (growers, trimmers, packagers)
- ✅ Cultivation facility rent (grow rooms, processing areas)
- ✅ Utilities for cultivation (electricity for lights, HVAC for climate control)
- ✅ Water for irrigation
- ✅ Packaging materials

##### Indirect Production Costs (263A requires capitalization):
- ✅ Cultivation facility depreciation (buildings, HVAC, lighting systems)
- ✅ Repairs and maintenance of cultivation equipment
- ✅ Property taxes on cultivation facilities
- ✅ Insurance on cultivation facilities
- ✅ Quality control/testing costs
- ✅ Storage costs (pre-sale inventory warehousing)
- ✅ Production supervisors' salaries
- ✅ Security for cultivation facilities

##### Administrative/Selling Costs (NOT capitalizable, thus NOT deductible due to 280E):
- ❌ General corporate overhead (accounting, legal, HR)
- ❌ Retail dispensary operations (budtenders, cashiers)
- ❌ Marketing and advertising
- ❌ Executive salaries (unless directly supervising production)
- ❌ Corporate office rent

**Key Language (T.C. Memo. 2018-83, at *22):**
> "Section 263A requires taxpayers to capitalize direct and indirect costs allocable to property produced by the taxpayer. Petitioner produced marijuana through cultivation. Accordingly, he must capitalize costs allocable to that production activity."

**Critical Insight for Cannabis Tax Optimization:**
Section 263A is FAVORABLE to cannabis businesses because it:
1. **Expands COGS:** Forces capitalization of indirect costs that would otherwise be disallowed SG&A
2. **Increases Deductions:** Larger COGS = larger deduction under IRC § 471
3. **Reduces 280E Penalty:** Shifts expenses from non-deductible (SG&A) to deductible (COGS)

**Practical Application (Alterman's Numbers):**
- **IRS COGS (narrow):** $120,000 (inventory purchases only)
- **Taxpayer COGS (263A-compliant):** $285,000 (includes rent, utilities, depreciation, indirect labor)
- **Tax Savings:** ($285,000 - $120,000) × 21% = $34,650

**Section 263A Allocation Methods:**
1. **Simplified Production Method:** Allocate indirect costs based on direct labor hours
2. **Simplified Resale Method:** For retailers, allocate storage costs
3. **Modified Simplified Production Method:** Hybrid approach (most common for cannabis)

#### 5. *Feinberg v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2016-102

**Citation:** Feinberg v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2016-102 (June 2, 2016).
**Court:** United States Tax Court
**Judge:** Judge Gustafson

**Facts:**
- Petitioner owned three businesses:
  1. Medical marijuana dispensary (Northern Lights)
  2. Consulting business (advised other dispensaries on compliance)
  3. Real estate rental (leased property to other cannabis businesses)
- IRS applied 280E to all three businesses, disallowing ALL deductions

**Issue:**
When does a cannabis-related business qualify as "separate" for 280E purposes?

**Holding:**
**Two separate businesses** (partial taxpayer victory):
1. **Marijuana dispensary:** Subject to 280E (no deductions except COGS)
2. **Consulting business:** NOT subject to 280E (full deductions allowed)
3. **Real estate rental:** NOT subject to 280E (full deductions allowed), BUT passive activity loss limitations apply

**Key Language (T.C. Memo. 2016-102, at *18):**
> "Petitioner's consulting business did not involve 'trafficking' in marijuana. He provided advice on regulatory compliance, business operations, and state law requirements. This advisory work is distinct from the sale or distribution of marijuana itself."

**Trafficking vs. Non-Trafficking Test:**
- **Trafficking (280E applies):** Direct possession, sale, distribution, cultivation, or manufacture of Schedule I/II substances
- **Non-Trafficking (280E does NOT apply):** Ancillary services that do NOT involve physical handling of controlled substances

**Examples of Non-Trafficking Businesses (Deductions Allowed):**
- ✅ Cannabis compliance consulting
- ✅ Cannabis accounting services
- ✅ Real estate leasing to cannabis tenants (landlord does not "traffic")
- ✅ Software/technology for cannabis businesses
- ✅ Legal services for cannabis clients
- ✅ Marketing/branding for cannabis businesses (if no direct involvement in sales)

**Examples of Trafficking Businesses (280E Applies):**
- ❌ Dispensaries (retail sales)
- ❌ Cultivation (growing marijuana)
- ❌ Manufacturing (processing, extraction, infused products)
- ❌ Distribution (wholesale to dispensaries)
- ❌ Testing labs (if also cultivating/selling)

**Separate Business Requirements (from Feinberg):**
1. **Distinct legal entities:** Separate corporations/LLCs recommended (not required)
2. **Separate accounting:** Distinct books, bank accounts, financial statements
3. **Independent economic substance:** Each business viable on its own
4. **No co-mingling:** Separate facilities, employees, vendors
5. **Arm's-length transactions:** Market-rate pricing if entities transact

**Tax Planning Insight:**
Cannabis operators should SEGREGATE ancillary businesses (consulting, real estate, software) into separate entities to avoid 280E taint.

## VI. CIVIL ASSET FORFEITURE CASES

### A. Statutory Framework — 21 U.S.C. § 881

**21 U.S.C. § 881(a) — Property Subject to Forfeiture:**
The following shall be subject to forfeiture to the United States:
- **(a)(4):** All conveyances (vehicles, aircraft, vessels) used or intended for use to transport controlled substances
- **(a)(6):** All moneys, negotiable instruments, securities used or intended to be used to facilitate CSA violations
- **(a)(7):** **All real property** used or intended to be used to commit or facilitate CSA violations

**Standard of Proof:**
- **Civil forfeiture:** Preponderance of the evidence (lower than criminal "beyond reasonable doubt")
- **In rem action:** Lawsuit filed against the property itself (*United States v. $124,700 in U.S. Currency*)
- **Burden:** Government must show property "facilitated" drug trafficking; owner bears burden to prove "innocent owner" defense

**Innocent Owner Defense (18 U.S.C. § 983(d)):**
Property owner can defeat forfeiture by proving:
1. **Lack of knowledge:** Owner did not know property was being used for drug trafficking, OR
2. **Reasonable steps:** Upon learning of illegal use, owner took all reasonable steps to terminate such use

## VII. PRODUCT LIABILITY — CANNABIS CONTAMINATION

### A. Product Liability Framework for Cannabis Products

Cannabis products are subject to traditional product liability theories under state tort law:

#### 1. **Strict Liability (Restatement (Second) of Torts § 402A)**
Manufacturer/seller liable for defective product regardless of negligence if:
- Product was defective (manufacturing defect, design defect, failure to warn)
- Defect existed when product left defendant's control
- Defect caused plaintiff's injury

#### 2. **Negligence**
Failure to exercise reasonable care in:
- Manufacturing (failure to follow good manufacturing practices)
- Testing (inadequate or no testing for contaminants)
- Distribution (improper storage, handling)
- Warnings (failure to warn of known risks)

#### 3. **Breach of Warranty (UCC Article 2)**
- **Express Warranty:** Product fails to conform to representations (e.g., "lab-tested," "pesticide-free")
- **Implied Warranty of Merchantability:** Product unfit for ordinary use
- **Implied Warranty of Fitness:** Product unsuitable for particular purpose

|-------------|--------|-------------|---------------------------|

### D. Cannabis Product Liability Defenses

#### 1. **Regulatory Compliance Defense**

**Defense:** Product complied with all state cannabis regulations, including mandatory testing.

**Effectiveness: LIMITED**

**California Law:**
- Compliance with state regulations is **NOT** a complete defense to strict liability (*Anderson v. Owens-Corning Fiberglas Corp.*, 53 Cal.3d 987 (1991))
- Regulatory compliance is **EVIDENCE** of reasonableness, but not dispositive
- Plaintiff can still prove product defective even if passed state testing

**Colorado Law:**
- Similar to California: Compliance with regulations does not immunize from liability
- *Jimenez v. DaimlerChrysler Corp.*, 269 F.3d 439 (5th Cir. 2001) (applying Colorado law): Regulatory compliance is not absolute defense

**KEY POINT:** State-mandated testing REDUCES but does NOT ELIMINATE product liability exposure.

---

#### 2. **Third-Party Lab Testing Defense**

**Defense:** Independent lab tested product and certified as safe; manufacturer/seller relied on lab results in good faith.

**Effectiveness: MODERATE**

**Indemnification from Testing Labs:**
- Cannabis testing labs typically include limitation of liability clauses in contracts
- Labs cap liability at testing fee paid (e.g., $300-$500 per batch)
- Seller cannot fully shift liability to lab

**Comparative Fault:**
- Some states allow apportionment of liability between seller and lab (if lab negligently tested)
- California: Joint and several liability may apply (*Locke v. Warner Bros., Inc.*, 57 Cal.App.4th 354 (1997))

**Practical Reality:**
Most testing labs are thinly capitalized and judgment-proof. Plaintiff will pursue deep-pocket dispensary/manufacturer.

---

#### 3. **Federal Illegality Defense (Failed)**

**Defense:** Cannabis is federally illegal; plaintiff cannot recover for injuries from consuming illegal product.

**Effectiveness: REJECTED by all state courts**

##### *Weller v. Riverside Inn*, 2015 WL 12698827 (Colo. Dist. Ct. 2015)
- Defendant argued plaintiff's cannabis consumption violated federal law; clean hands doctrine bars recovery
- **Court rejected:** State tort law applies to state-legal products; federal CSA does not preempt state product liability law
- **Holding:** "Colorado has chosen to regulate marijuana as a legal product. Plaintiffs are entitled to same consumer protections as any other product under Colorado law."

##### *Bourgoin v. Twin Rivers Paper Co.*, 187 A.3d 10 (Me. 2018)
- Maine Supreme Court allowed product liability claim for defective medical marijuana vaporizer
- Rejected federal illegality defense: "State law governs tort claims arising in Maine."

**CONCLUSION:** Federal illegality is NOT a defense to state product liability claims in cannabis-legal states.

## VIII. EMPLOYMENT AND LABOR LAW — NLRA SUCCESSORSHIP

### A. NLRA Successorship Doctrine — Foundational Precedent

#### *Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987)

**Citation:** Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB, 482 U.S. 27 (1987).
**Court:** Supreme Court of the United States
**Decision Date:** May 26, 1987
**Vote:** 9-0 (unanimous)
**Author:** Justice Blackmun

**Facts:**
- Fall River Dyeing purchased assets of Sterlingwale Corporation (textile mill)
- Fall River hired 55 of 62 former Sterlingwale employees
- Sterlingwale employees were represented by union (United Textile Workers)
- NLRB found Fall River was "successor employer" required to recognize union and bargain

**Holding:**
A purchaser of business assets is a "successor employer" under NLRA if:
1. **Substantial continuity** of business operations exists, AND
2. **Majority of workforce** consists of predecessor's employees

**Substantial Continuity Factors (482 U.S. at 43):**
1. Same business operations
2. Same plant/facilities
3. Same jobs under same working conditions
4. Same supervisors and managers
5. Same machinery, equipment, and production methods
6. Same product or service
7. Same customers

**Key Language (482 U.S. at 43):**
> "In conducting the substantial continuity analysis, the Board examines a number of factors: whether the business of both employers is essentially the same; whether the employees of the new company are doing the same jobs in the same working conditions under the same supervisors; and whether the new entity has the same production process, produces the same products, and basically has the same body of customers."

**Successor's Obligation:**
- **MUST recognize union** (if majority of workforce are predecessor's employees)
- **MUST bargain in good faith** with union
- **NOT required to adopt predecessor's CBA** (can negotiate new terms)
- **CANNOT make unilateral changes** to terms and conditions of employment before bargaining

**Successor's Hiring Discretion:**
Successor has discretion to:
- Set initial terms of employment
- Hire fewer than all predecessor employees
- Refuse to hire predecessor employees for legitimate business reasons

BUT: Cannot refuse to hire predecessor employees **to avoid successorship**.

### E. Acquirer's Successorship Obligations

#### 3. **CBA Adoption** (Analysis Required)
**Question:** Must Acquirer adopt existing CBA (expires Dec. 2026)?

**General Rule (from *Fall River*):**
- Successor is **NOT automatically bound** by predecessor's CBA
- Successor can set initial terms of employment
- BUT: Successor CANNOT make changes that are "abrupt" or "substantial" departures from predecessor's terms

**Exception — CBA Assumption:**
If Acquirer **expressly agrees** to assume CBA in purchase agreement, then CBA is binding.

**Recommended Approach:**
- **Option 1 (Conservative):** Acquirer assumes existing CBA through Dec. 2026 (provides labor peace; avoids unfair labor practice charges)
- **Option 2 (Aggressive):** Acquirer maintains predecessor's wages/benefits but does not formally adopt CBA; bargains for new CBA before Dec. 2026 expiration
- **Option 3 (High Risk):** Acquirer implements different wages/benefits immediately post-closing (RISK: Unfair labor practice charges under Section 8(a)(5))

### B. Cannabis Lease Enforceability Cases

#### *Momenta Pebble Creek, LLC v. GreenMart of Nevada NLV, LLC*, 478 P.3d 867 (Nev. 2021)

**Citation:** Momenta Pebble Creek, LLC v. GreenMart of Nevada NLV, LLC, 478 P.3d 867 (Nev. 2021).
**Court:** Supreme Court of Nevada
**Decision Date:** January 7, 2021

**Facts:**
- Landlord leased commercial property to GreenMart (medical marijuana dispensary)
- Lease contained "cannabis rider" acknowledging marijuana use
- GreenMart stopped paying rent; landlord sued for breach of lease
- GreenMart defense: Lease is void contract for illegal purpose (federal Schedule I)

**Issue:**
Is a commercial lease for cannabis dispensary enforceable under Nevada contract law?

**Holding:**
Cannabis leases are **ENFORCEABLE** in Nevada. Federal illegality does not void state contracts for state-legal activity.

**Key Language (478 P.3d at 872):**
> "Nevada has chosen to legalize and regulate marijuana. A contract that complies with Nevada law and does not violate Nevada public policy is enforceable in Nevada courts, notwithstanding federal law. The federal Controlled Substances Act does not preempt Nevada's regulation of marijuana or Nevada's contract law."

**Reasoning:**
1. **State Sovereignty:** Nevada has authority to regulate intrastate commerce, including cannabis
2. **Public Policy:** Nevada public policy favors cannabis regulation, as evidenced by voter-approved legalization (Question 2, 2016)
3. **Contract Law:** Contract is not void for illegality if legal under state law where contract formed and performed
4. **No Federal Preemption:** CSA does not preempt state contract law

**Outcome:**
Supreme Court of Nevada reversed trial court; remanded for enforcement of lease. Landlord entitled to back rent + damages.

---

#### *Colorado Harvest Co. v. 1090 Yuma, LLC*, No. 2020CV30682 (Colo. Dist. Ct. 2021)

**Citation:** Colorado Harvest Co. v. 1090 Yuma, LLC, No. 2020CV30682 (Colo. Dist. Ct., Denver County, Apr. 2021).
**Court:** Colorado District Court, Denver County
**Status:** Trial court decision (unpublished)

**Facts:**
- Landlord leased property to Colorado Harvest Co. (retail marijuana dispensary)
- Lease term: 10 years (2015-2025)
- Landlord sought to terminate lease early, citing federal illegality defense
- Landlord argued insurance carrier cancelled policy due to cannabis use; lease frustrated

**Tenant's Claims:**
- Breach of lease (landlord seeking wrongful early termination)
- Breach of quiet enjoyment covenant
- Tortious interference with business

**Landlord's Defenses:**
1. Federal illegality voids lease
2. Frustration of purpose (insurance cancellation)
3. Impossibility of performance

**Court Ruling:**
All landlord defenses **REJECTED**:
1. **Federal illegality:** Colorado public policy favors cannabis regulation; lease enforceable
2. **Frustration of purpose:** Landlord assumed risk when entering cannabis lease; insurance issues were foreseeable
3. **Impossibility:** Performance is not impossible; landlord can perform by allowing tenant to occupy property

**Damages:**
Court awarded tenant:
- Lost profits for remainder of lease term (7 years × $500K annually = $3.5M)
- Moving costs to relocate dispensary
- Attorney's fees

**Key Takeaway:**
Landlords **CANNOT** terminate cannabis leases on federal illegality grounds. Landlord bears risk of insurance cancellation or other federal law consequences.

## X. BANKING AND FINANCIAL SERVICES LITIGATION

### A. Cannabis Banking Framework

**Federal Banking Prohibition:**
- Banks are federally regulated (FDIC, Federal Reserve, OCC)
- Federal Bank Secrecy Act (31 U.S.C. § 5318) requires banks to report suspicious activity
- Banking cannabis proceeds = potential money laundering (18 U.S.C. § 1956) OR aiding/abetting CSA violations

**FinCEN Guidance (February 14, 2014):**
FinCEN issued guidance allowing banks to serve cannabis businesses IF:
1. Cannabis business complies with state law
2. Bank files "Marijuana Limited" Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) quarterly
3. Bank conducts enhanced due diligence on cannabis customers

**Key Point:** FinCEN guidance is NOT law; it is non-binding guidance providing "safe harbor" from prosecution (not immunity).

---

### B. Federal Reserve Access — *Fourth Corner Credit Union* Case

#### *Fourth Corner Credit Union v. Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City*, 861 F.3d 1052 (10th Cir. 2017)

**Citation:** Fourth Corner Credit Union v. Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City, 861 F.3d 1052 (10th Cir. 2017).
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit
**Decision Date:** July 25, 2017
**Panel:** Judges Holmes, Tymkovich, and Phillips
**Author:** Judge Tymkovich

**Facts:**
- Fourth Corner Credit Union chartered by Colorado to serve cannabis industry
- Applied for Federal Reserve master account (required to access national banking system, wire transfers, ACH)
- Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City **denied** master account application
- Denial rationale: Serving cannabis businesses would violate federal law; Federal Reserve cannot facilitate illegal activity

**Issue:**
Does Federal Reserve have discretion to deny master account to state-chartered credit union serving cannabis businesses?

**Holding:**
YES. Federal Reserve has discretion to deny master account if granting account would facilitate violations of federal law.

**Key Language (861 F.3d at 1057):**
> "Although the Federal Reserve Act provides that member banks 'may' receive master accounts, this language does not create mandatory entitlement. The Federal Reserve has discretion to deny accounts when granting such accounts would facilitate conduct prohibited by federal law."

**Reasoning:**
1. **Statutory Interpretation:** Federal Reserve Act uses permissive "may" language, not mandatory "shall"
2. **Federal Law Supremacy:** Federal Reserve is federal agency; cannot facilitate CSA violations
3. **Prudential Concerns:** Federal Reserve has duty to maintain safety and soundness of banking system
4. **FinCEN Guidance Not Binding:** FinCEN guidance does not obligate Federal Reserve to grant accounts

**Outcome:**
Tenth Circuit affirmed denial of master account. Fourth Corner Credit Union unable to operate without Federal Reserve access.

**Subsequent Impact:**
- Fourth Corner Credit Union **ceased operations** (2018)
- Demonstrates federal banking access remains restricted despite state legalization
- Cannabis businesses forced to use state-chartered banks/credit unions with limited Federal Reserve access

### C. Bank Account Terminations — No Reported Litigation

**Research Finding:** NO reported cases where cannabis businesses successfully sued banks for wrongful account termination.

**Common Scenario:**
1. Cannabis business opens account at bank
2. Bank discovers cannabis business (may not have disclosed during account opening)
3. Bank terminates account, citing "reputational risk" or federal law concerns
4. Cannabis business seeks legal recourse

**Legal Barriers to Suing Banks:**
1. **At-Will Banking Relationship:** Banks can generally terminate accounts for any lawful reason (or no reason)
2. **Federal Law Compliance:** Banks can cite federal law compliance as legitimate reason for termination
3. **Reputational Risk:** Courts recognize banks have legitimate interest in managing reputational risk
4. **No Private Right of Action:** Bank Secrecy Act and FinCEN guidance do not create private right of action for cannabis businesses

**Practical Result:**
Cannabis businesses have NO legal recourse if banks terminate accounts. Must find cannabis-friendly banks (typically state-chartered credit unions).

## XI. OTHER CANNABIS LITIGATION AREAS

### A. Intellectual Property — Trademark Limitations

**USPTO Rejection of Cannabis Trademarks:**
U.S. Patent and Trademark Office **refuses** to register trademarks for cannabis products because:
1. Use of mark in interstate commerce required (15 U.S.C. § 1051)
2. Cannabis interstate commerce violates federal CSA
3. Use in commerce must be "lawful use"

**Workarounds:**
- **State Trademarks:** Register trademarks in individual states (CA, CO, etc.)
- **Common Law Trademarks:** Establish rights through use (no registration)
- **Ancillary Services:** Trademark ancillary services (e.g., "Cannabis Consulting Services") rather than cannabis products

**Case Law:**
- *In re JJ206, LLC*, Serial No. 86/770,824 (TTAB 2016): Trademark application for "POWERED BY JUJU" for cannabis products REFUSED
- USPTO policy: Will not register marks for Schedule I substances

### B. Securities Law — Federal Enforcement

**SEC Position on Cannabis Securities:**
Securities and Exchange Commission has **NOT** prohibited securities offerings by cannabis companies, BUT:
- Cannabis companies cannot list on major exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ) due to federal illegality
- Must trade on over-the-counter (OTC) markets (OTCQX, OTCQB)
- SEC filing companies subject to enhanced disclosure requirements re: federal risk

**Notable Cases:**
- No SEC enforcement actions against cannabis companies for securities violations (as of 2025)
- SEC has investigated cannabis penny stock schemes, but no major enforcement actions
