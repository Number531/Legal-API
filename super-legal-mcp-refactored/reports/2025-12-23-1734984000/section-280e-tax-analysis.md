## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. IRC Â§ 280E Statutory Framework

#### 1. Statutory Text and Codification

**26 U.S.C. Â§ 280E - Expenditures in Connection with the Illegal Sale of Drugs**

> "No deduction or credit shall be allowed for any amount paid or incurred during the taxable year in carrying on any trade or business if such trade or business (or the activities which comprise such trade or business) consists of trafficking in controlled substances (within the meaning of schedule I and II of the Controlled Substances Act) which is prohibited by Federal law or the law of any State in which such trade or business is conducted."

**Enacted:** Tax Equity and Fiscal Responsibility Act of 1982 (TEFRA), Pub. L. No. 97-248, Â§ 351, 96 Stat. 324, 640 (September 3, 1982).

**Codification:** Title 26 (Internal Revenue Code), Subtitle A (Income Taxes), Chapter 1 (Normal Taxes and Surtaxes), Subchapter B (Computation of Taxable Income), Part IX (Items Not Deductible).

**Effective Date:** Applicable to amounts paid or incurred after the date of enactment (September 3, 1982) in taxable years ending after such date.

#### 2. Legislative History and Congressional Intent

**Background â€” Jeffrey Edmondson Case:**

Section 280E was enacted in direct response to *Edmondson v. Commissioner*, 42 T.C.M. (CCH) 1533 (1981), in which the Tax Court allowed a convicted cocaine, marijuana, and amphetamine trafficker to deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses (rent, packaging, telephone, vehicle expenses) under IRC Â§ 162(a). The court held that illegal businesses were entitled to the same deductions as legal businesses.

**Congressional Response:**

The Joint Committee on Taxation explained the rationale for Â§ 280E:

> "The [Senate Finance] committee has been informed that some taxpayers engaged in trafficking in drugs have attempted to reduce the Federal income tax they owe on profits from such activity by taking deductions for all of the ordinary and necessary expenses paid or incurred in carrying on such activity. The committee believes that to allow such deductions is inappropriate as a matter of public policy."

*Staff of the Joint Comm. on Taxation, 97th Cong., General Explanation of the Revenue Provisions of the Tax Equity and Fiscal Responsibility Act of 1982, at 309-10 (Comm. Print 1982)* ("Blue Book").

**Congressional Intent â€” Public Policy Doctrine:**

Congress invoked the public policy doctrine established in *Commissioner v. Tellier*, 383 U.S. 687 (1966), and *Tank Truck Rentals, Inc. v. Commissioner*, 356 U.S. 30 (1958), which held that certain deductions may be disallowed when allowing them would "frustrate sharply defined national or state policies proscribing particular types of conduct."

The legislative history states: "If such deductions were allowed, the Federal income tax laws would be seen as providing an indirect government subsidy of the trafficking."

#### 3. Elements of Section 280E Disallowance

For Section 280E to apply, three elements must be met:

**Element 1: Trade or Business**

The taxpayer must be engaged in a "trade or business" within the meaning of IRC Â§ 162. This requires:
- Regular and continuous activity
- Profit motive
- Substantial business operations

*Application to Cannabis:* Courts have consistently held that state-licensed cannabis operations constitute a "trade or business." *See Olive v. Commissioner*, 139 T.C. 19, 23 (2012) ("Petitioners clearly were engaged in a trade or business").

**Element 2: Trafficking in Controlled Substances**

The trade or business must consist of "trafficking in controlled substances" within the meaning of Schedule I or Schedule II of the Controlled Substances Act.

**Definition of "Trafficking":**
Section 280E does not define "trafficking," but the legislative history and case law interpret it broadly:

- **Not limited to illegal sales:** The term encompasses any commercial dealing in controlled substances, including state-authorized activities. *Olive*, 139 T.C. at 27-28.

- **CSA definition (21 U.S.C. Â§ 802(44)):** "Traffic" means "to manufacture, distribute, dispense, or possess with intent to distribute." This definition applies for Â§ 280E purposes. *Id.*

- **State legalization irrelevant:** State authorization does not affect federal tax treatment. Cannabis remains Schedule I under federal law (21 U.S.C. Â§ 812(c), Schedule I(c)(10)).

**Element 3: Prohibited by Federal or State Law**

The activity must be prohibited by federal law or the law of any state in which the business is conducted.

- **Federal prohibition:** Cannabis is Schedule I under the Controlled Substances Act, prohibited for all purposes except FDA-approved research.

- **State authorization irrelevant:** Even if state law authorizes cannabis, federal prohibition triggers Â§ 280E. *Olive*, 139 T.C. at 28 ("the fact that a business's activity is sanctioned by state law does not prevent application of Â§ 280E").

#### 4. Scope of Disallowance â€” Business Deductions Under IRC Â§ 162

Section 280E disallows "any deduction or credit" for amounts paid or incurred in carrying on the trafficking business. This includes:

**Ordinary and Necessary Business Expenses (IRC Â§ 162(a)):**
- Rent and occupancy costs (retail, office, corporate)
- Utilities (non-production)
- Advertising and marketing
- Professional fees (legal, accounting, consulting)
- Insurance (non-inventory)
- Salaries and wages (non-production employees)
- Office supplies and administrative expenses
- Travel and entertainment
- Repairs and maintenance (non-production facilities)
- Depreciation (non-production assets)

**Credits:**
