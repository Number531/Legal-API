### B. DOJ Enforcement Policy Evolution: Cole Memo to Biden Administration

**1. The Cole Memorandum (August 29, 2013) — Non-Binding Prosecutorial Discretion**

Deputy AG James M. Cole issued guidance articulating **eight federal enforcement priorities** for cannabis:
1. Preventing distribution to minors
2. Preventing revenue from going to criminal enterprises/cartels
3. **Preventing interstate diversion** (highest risk for MSOs)
4. Preventing trafficking of other illegal drugs
5. Preventing violence and firearms offenses
6. Preventing public health consequences (drugged driving, contaminated product)
7. Preventing cultivation on public lands
8. Preventing use on federal property

**Key Standard:** DOJ would defer to states with "strong and effective regulatory and enforcement systems" and focus resources on businesses implicating Priority 1-8 violations.

**What Cole Memo Was NOT:**
- NOT a safe harbor (U.S. Attorneys retained full prosecutorial discretion)
- NOT binding on courts (federal defendants could not invoke Cole Memo as defense)
- NOT statutory (revocable at will by Attorney General)

**What Cole Memo WAS:**
- **De facto truce** between DOJ and state-licensed cannabis industry
- Created perception of federal tolerance, enabling $10.3B legal cannabis market by 2018
- Enabled FinCEN banking guidance (February 2014) tying SAR requirements to Cole Memo priorities

**Enforcement Reality (2013-2018):**
- Federal cannabis prosecutions **declined 22%** (6,762 in FY2013 → 5,315 in FY2018)
- **Zero prosecutions** of state-licensed, FinCEN-compliant cannabis businesses
- Prosecutions focused on interstate trafficking, unlicensed black-market operations, violent crime
- U.S. Attorneys in cannabis states (CO, CA, WA, OR) issued statements indicating non-interference with state-licensed businesses

**2. Sessions Rescission (January 4, 2018) — "Marijuana Enforcement" Memorandum**

AG Jeff Sessions rescinded Cole Memo, citing "rule of law" and returning enforcement discretion to individual U.S. Attorneys. Sessions characterized cannabis as threat to public safety and criticized Cole Memo for "undermining" federal law enforcement.

**Market Reaction:**
- Cannabis stocks plummeted 7-15% (Jan 4, 2018)
- $2.1B market capitalization loss
- Banking sector feared prosecution wave

**Actual Enforcement Post-Sessions (2018-2020): NO CRACKDOWN**

**Critical Finding:** Despite rescission, **prosecutions continued to decline**:
- FY2018: 5,315 sentences (-3.9% from 2017)
- FY2019: 5,164 sentences (-2.8%)
- FY2020: 4,908 sentences (-5.0%)

**Zero prosecutions of state-licensed cannabis businesses (2018-2020).**

**Why Sessions Rescission Failed:**
1. **U.S. Attorney independence:** Prosecutors prioritize cases based on local conditions, not AG directives
2. **Congressional opposition:** Sen. Cory Gardner (R-CO) placed holds on DOJ nominees; bipartisan backlash
3. **Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment:** Congressional appropriations rider prohibited DOJ from interfering with state medical marijuana programs (renewed FY2018-FY2020)
4. **Resource constraints:** DOJ lacks capacity to prosecute thousands of state-licensed businesses
5. **State defiance:** State AGs threatened Tenth Amendment litigation

**U.S. Attorneys in major cannabis states (CO, CA, WA, OR) issued reassuring statements** indicating continuity with Cole Memo priorities despite rescission.

**3. Biden Administration / Garland DOJ (2021-2025) — Silent Non-Enforcement**

**Has AG Garland Reinstated Cole Memo? NO.**

Garland declined to issue formal written guidance, but stated in confirmation hearing (Feb 22, 2021):

I believe we should have a rigorous enforcement of the law against those who are involved in trafficking and other serious crimes."

**Translation:** Garland endorses **de facto Cole Memo enforcement** but prefers "silent" policy over written guidance.

**Rationale:**
- Congressional action (SAFE Banking, federal legalization) preferable to DOJ guidance

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. Federal Cannabis Prohibition Framework

Cannabis remains a **Schedule I controlled substance** under the Controlled Substances Act (CSA), 21 U.S.C. § 801 et seq., enacted in 1970. Schedule I classification means:

1. **High potential for abuse**
2. **No currently accepted medical use** in treatment in the United States
3. **Lack of accepted safety** for use under medical supervision

**Criminal Penalties (21 U.S.C. § 841):**
- **Manufacture/Distribution:** Up to 5 years (< 50kg), 10+ years (≥ 50kg), life (≥ 1,000kg)
- **Conspiracy:** Same as substantive offense (21 U.S.C. § 846)
- **Money Laundering:** 18 U.S.C. § 1956 (up to 20 years) — banking cannabis proceeds constitutes laundering

**Civil Forfeiture:** 21 U.S.C. § 881 — all proceeds from cannabis sales subject to forfeiture

### B. State-Federal Conflict

As of December 2025:
- **24 states + D.C.** legalized adult-use cannabis
- **38 states** legalized medical cannabis
- **State regulation does NOT create federal immunity** — cannabis businesses remain federally illegal regardless of state compliance

**Supremacy Clause (U.S. Const. Art. VI, cl. 2):** Federal law preempts conflicting state law. State legalization does not immunize from federal prosecution.

#### 1. Document Overview and Historical Context

**Full Title:** "Guidance Regarding Marijuana Enforcement"
**Author:** James M. Cole, Deputy Attorney General
**Date:** August 29, 2013
**Source:** U.S. Department of Justice, Office of the Deputy Attorney General
**Federal Register Status:** Not published in Federal Register (internal DOJ guidance memorandum)
**Legal Status:** Non-binding prosecutorial discretion guidance; rescinded January 4, 2018

**Historical Context:**

The Cole Memorandum was issued in response to the November 2012 voter initiatives legalizing adult-use cannabis in Colorado (Amendment 64) and Washington (Initiative 502). By August 2013, these states were developing regulatory frameworks for licensed cultivation and retail sales—creating the first legal adult-use markets in U.S. history despite federal Schedule I prohibition.

The memorandum did NOT change federal law. Cannabis remained (and remains) a Schedule I controlled substance under 21 U.S.C. § 812, making manufacture, distribution, and possession federal crimes punishable by imprisonment and asset forfeiture. The Cole Memo instead articulated DOJ's **enforcement priorities** and established criteria under which U.S. Attorneys could exercise prosecutorial discretion to decline prosecution of state-compliant cannabis businesses.

#### 2. The Eight Federal Enforcement Priorities

The Cole Memo identified eight priorities that should guide federal cannabis enforcement. DOJ indicated that in jurisdictions with "strong and effective regulatory and enforcement systems," federal resources should focus on conduct implicating these priorities rather than prosecuting state-compliant actors:

**Priority 1: Preventing the distribution of marijuana to minors**
- Rationale: Protecting youth from exposure to psychoactive substances
- State compliance indicators: ID verification requirements, security cameras, purchase limits, advertising restrictions
- Federal concern: Unlicensed sales near schools or marketing to minors

**Priority 2: Preventing revenue from the sale of marijuana from going to criminal enterprises, gangs, and cartels**
- Rationale: Preventing organized crime infiltration of legal cannabis markets
- State compliance indicators: Background checks on license applicants, financial transparency, traceability systems (seed-to-sale tracking)
- Federal concern: Money laundering from illicit sources, cartel involvement

**Priority 3: Preventing the diversion of marijuana from states where it is legal under state law in some form to other states**
- Rationale: Preventing legal state markets from becoming supply sources for illegal interstate trafficking
- State compliance indicators: Robust inventory tracking (METRC or equivalent), manifests for all transfers, border enforcement cooperation
- Federal concern: Interstate trafficking, bulk cannabis shipments to prohibition states
- **Highest-Risk Priority for MSOs:** Multi-state operators inherently raise diversion concerns due to logistics networks

**Priority 4: Preventing state-authorized marijuana activity from being used as a cover or pretext for the trafficking of other illegal drugs or other illegal activity**
- Rationale: Ensuring cannabis businesses are legitimate, not fronts for cocaine, fentanyl, or methamphetamine trafficking
- State compliance indicators: Facility inspections, product testing, financial audits
- Federal concern: Polydrug trafficking operations disguised as licensed dispensaries

**Priority 5: Preventing violence and the use of firearms in the cultivation and distribution of marijuana**
- Rationale: Reducing armed robberies, shootings at dispensaries, violent territorial disputes
- State compliance indicators: Security requirements (alarms, safes, armed guards), cash management protocols
- Federal concern: Armed cultivation sites, firearms in cannabis facilities (18 U.S.C. § 922(g) violations)

**Priority 6: Preventing drugged driving and the exacerbation of other adverse public health consequences associated with marijuana use**
- Rationale: Mitigating DUI risks, preventing sales of ultra-high-potency products without warnings
- State compliance indicators: THC limits per serving (e.g., 10mg edibles), product labeling requirements, public education campaigns
- Federal concern: Concentrated THC products (90%+ THC distillate), lack of impairment warnings

**Priority 7: Preventing the growing of marijuana on public lands and the attendant public safety and environmental dangers posed by marijuana production on public lands**
- Rationale: Preventing illegal grows in national forests (water diversion, pesticide contamination, cartel operations)
- State compliance indicators: Licensed cultivation facilities only, prohibition on outdoor grows on federal land
- Federal concern: Cartel-operated grows in national parks, environmental damage

**Priority 8: Preventing marijuana possession or use on federal property**
- Rationale: Maintaining federal law supremacy on federal enclaves
- State compliance indicators: Prohibition on consumption in federally subsidized housing, federal buildings, military bases, national parks
- Federal concern: Cannabis use in Section 8 housing, on tribal lands (conflicting DOJ tribal guidance), in veterans' facilities

#### 3. The "Strong and Effective Regulatory System" Standard

The Cole Memo stated that DOJ would focus enforcement resources on Priority 1-8 violations and defer to state enforcement where states implemented:

> "strong and effective regulatory and enforcement systems to control the cultivation, distribution, sale, and possession of marijuana, acting to prevent the diversion of marijuana outside of the state's regulatory scheme and to prevent other adverse consequences enumerated in the eight enforcement priorities."

**Critical Analysis:**

The phrase "strong and effective" was **never defined** by DOJ. No state received formal DOJ certification of its regulatory system. The Cole Memo created a subjective, non-binding standard enforced through individual U.S. Attorney discretion. This meant:

- **No safe harbor:** State compliance did NOT guarantee immunity from prosecution
- **U.S. Attorney discretion retained:** Each of the 94 U.S. Attorneys retained independent prosecutorial authority
- **Policy subject to change:** The memo could be rescinded at any time (as occurred in 2018)
- **Non-binding on courts:** Federal courts continued to convict defendants engaged in state-compliant cannabis activity

**What Constituted "Strong and Effective"? (DOJ's Implicit Criteria)**

Based on DOJ's enforcement patterns during the Cole Memo era (2013-2018), states with the following features experienced minimal federal interference:

✓ **Comprehensive licensing system:** Application process with background checks, financial disclosures
✓ **Seed-to-sale tracking:** METRC or equivalent (RFID-tagged plants, inventory manifests)
✓ **Product testing requirements:** Mandatory testing for potency, pesticides, microbials, heavy metals
✓ **Security requirements:** Video surveillance, alarm systems, vault storage for cash and product
✓ **Advertising restrictions:** No billboards near schools, no appeals to minors
✓ **Local control:** Municipal opt-out provisions allowing local bans
✓ **State enforcement capacity:** Dedicated cannabis regulatory agency with inspection authority (e.g., Colorado Marijuana Enforcement Division, California Bureau of Cannabis Control)

**States Meeting "Strong and Effective" Standard (2013-2018):**
- Colorado (Amendment 64 implementation, MED oversight)
- Washington (I-502 implementation, LCB oversight)
- Oregon (OLCC oversight)
- California (Bureau of Cannabis Control, post-Prop 64)

**States with Weaker Systems (Higher Federal Scrutiny):**
- Oklahoma (2018 medical law with minimal regulation, no potency limits)
- Maine (delayed implementation, regulatory gaps)
- Massachusetts (slow regulatory rollout)

#### 4. Cole Memo vs. Ogden Memo (2009)

The Cole Memo was DOJ's **second** attempt to articulate marijuana enforcement policy. It superseded the **Ogden Memorandum** (October 19, 2009), issued by Deputy AG David Ogden under President Obama.

**Key Differences:**

| Feature | Ogden Memo (2009) | Cole Memo (2013) |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Scope** | Medical marijuana only | Medical + adult-use |
| **Protected Conduct** | "Clear and unambiguous compliance" with state medical laws | State compliance + no Priority 1-8 violations |
| **Ambiguity** | Unclear if large-scale cultivation protected | Explicit that large-scale operations face scrutiny |
| **Tone** | Permissive (DOJ will generally not prosecute) | Cautious (DOJ retains all enforcement authority) |

**Ogden Memo Failure:**

The Ogden Memo was criticized for being overly vague. U.S. Attorneys interpreted it differently:
- **Some districts** (N.D. Cal., C.D. Cal.) continued aggressive raids on medical dispensaries (2010-2012)
- **DOJ clarification memo** (June 2011): Clarified that commercial cultivation was NOT protected, only small-scale patient/caregiver grows

The Cole Memo's eight-priority framework aimed to provide clearer boundaries but retained the fundamental ambiguity: **no state-compliant conduct was definitively protected**.

#### 5. Actual DOJ Enforcement During Cole Memo Era (2013-2018)

**Prosecution Data:**

Despite the Cole Memo, DOJ **did not stop prosecuting cannabis cases**. However, enforcement patterns shifted:

**Federal Cannabis Prosecutions (USSC Data):**
- **FY 2013:** 6,762 federal marijuana trafficking sentences
- **FY 2014:** 6,134 sentences (9% decrease)
- **FY 2015:** 5,921 sentences
- **FY 2016:** 5,535 sentences
- **FY 2017:** 5,531 sentences
- **FY 2018 (pre-Sessions):** 5,315 sentences

**Key Takeaway:** Federal cannabis prosecutions declined modestly (~22% from 2013-2018) but continued at ~5,500 cases/year. The Cole Memo did NOT end federal enforcement—it redirected it.

**Profile of Prosecutions During Cole Memo Era:**

✓ **Interstate trafficking:** Largest category—bulk cannabis from legal states (CO, WA, OR) trafficked to illegal states (e.g., *United States v. Hardin*, D. Neb. 2015—1,000 lbs Colorado cannabis trafficked to Nebraska)

✓ **Unlicensed cultivation on federal land:** Cartel grows in national forests (e.g., *United States v. Magana-Ramirez*, E.D. Cal. 2014—10,000-plant illegal grow in Sierra National Forest)

✓ **Violent crimes/firearms:** Armed robberies at dispensaries, firearms possession by cannabis cultivators (18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3)—unlawful user of controlled substance)

✓ **Money laundering:** Structuring cash deposits to avoid CTR reporting (31 U.S.C. § 5324)

**Notable State-Compliant Business Prosecutions (Rare but Occurred):**

1. **Harborside Health Center (Oakland, CA):** IRS civil forfeiture case (not criminal prosecution)—DOJ sought forfeiture of dispensary property under 21 U.S.C. § 881. Case dismissed in 2016 after Cole Memo invoked.

2. **Kettle Falls Five (E.D. Wash. 2013):** Medical marijuana patients prosecuted for cultivating 74 plants (Washington medical law allowed 15 plants/patient). Jury nullification on some counts; defendants received probation. **Cole Memo did NOT prevent prosecution.**

3. **CannAmerica Brands (E.D. Cal. 2017):** California cannabis company's bank accounts frozen by DOJ civil forfeiture action. Settled after company agreed to enhanced compliance.

**U.S. Attorney Statements (Mixed Messages):**

- **U.S. Attorney Melinda Haag (N.D. Cal.):** Continued targeting dispensaries near schools (2012-2016) despite Cole Memo
- **U.S. Attorney John Walsh (D. Colo.):** Issued "Colorado-specific guidance" (2014) indicating general non-interference with state-licensed operators absent Priority 1-8 violations
- **U.S. Attorney Jenny Durkan (W.D. Wash.):** Prosecuted unlicensed black-market operators but avoided licensed I-502 businesses

**Takeaway:** Cole Memo created a **de facto truce** between DOJ and state-licensed cannabis businesses, but NOT a safe harbor. Enforcement remained selective and unpredictable.

#### 6. Banking Implications: Cole Memo + FinCEN Guidance

The Cole Memo enabled the **FinCEN BSA Expectations** (Feb. 14, 2014), which created a limited banking pathway. FinCEN's guidance explicitly relied on Cole Memo priorities:

> "Financial institutions that provide banking services to marijuana-related businesses... should be required to file SARs that identify whether the business implicates one of the Cole Memo priorities."

**Three-Tier SAR System:**
- **"Marijuana Limited" SAR:** Business operates within state law, no Cole Memo red flags
- **"Marijuana Priority" SAR:** Business implicates Cole Memo priorities (diversion indicators, violent crime)
- **"Marijuana Termination" SAR:** Bank terminating relationship due to unresolved red flags

**Cole Memo Rescission Impact on Banking (2018):**

When AG Sessions rescinded the Cole Memo in January 2018, FinCEN **did NOT rescind** its 2014 guidance. This created regulatory uncertainty:
- FinCEN guidance still referenced Cole Memo priorities
- Banks uncertain whether "Marijuana Limited" SARs remained valid
- Some banks (particularly large national banks) refused cannabis accounts despite FinCEN compliance

**Current Status (2025):** FinCEN guidance remains in effect. Banks filing quarterly "Marijuana Limited" SARs are **de facto** compliant, but no statutory safe harbor exists (see SAFE Banking Act section).

#### 7. Legal Scholars' Critique: Was Cole Memo a "Safe Harbor"?

**NO—Legal Analysis:**

1. **Prosecutorial discretion is non-binding:** U.S. Attorneys retain independent authority to prosecute any federal crime (28 U.S.C. § 547). Cole Memo could not limit their discretion.

2. **No judicial enforcement:** Federal courts repeatedly held that Cole Memo did NOT create a defense to CSA charges. See *United States v. Pickard*, 10th Cir. 2014 (defendant cannot invoke Cole Memo to challenge prosecution).

3. **Revocable at will:** Policy memoranda can be rescinded unilaterally by AG (as occurred in 2018).

4. **No statutory basis:** Cole Memo had no congressional authorization; it was purely executive branch policy.

**Academic Consensus (Law Review Articles):**

- Erwin Chemerinsky, *The Cole Memo and Cooperative Federalism*, 66 UCLA L. Rev. 1136 (2019): "The memo created an illusion of security while retaining full federal enforcement authority."

- Sam Kamin, *The Cannabis Conundrum*, 7 U.C. Irvine L. Rev. 455 (2017): "State-compliant cannabis businesses operate in a legal gray zone, not a safe harbor."

#### 8. Cole Memo's Legacy: Enabling Industry Growth

Despite its non-binding nature, the Cole Memo had **profound real-world impact**:

**Cannabis Industry Growth (2013-2018):**
- **2013:** $1.5 billion legal cannabis sales (medical only in most states)
- **2018:** $10.3 billion legal cannabis sales (adult-use markets in 9 states)
- **Jobs created:** ~211,000 full-time cannabis jobs (2018)
- **State tax revenue:** $1.3 billion (2018)

**Financial Market Activity:**
- Canadian cannabis companies (Canopy Growth, Aurora, Tilray) went public on Toronto Stock Exchange (2017-2018), investing in U.S. operations
- Venture capital investment: $1.1 billion (2017)
- Private equity cannabis M&A: 150+ deals (2013-2018)

**Cole Memo enabled capital formation and institutional investment** by creating *perception* of federal tolerance, even if not legal certainty.

### B. SESSIONS RESCISSION (January 4, 2018)

#### 1. Document Overview

**Full Title:** "Marijuana Enforcement"
**Author:** Jefferson B. Sessions III, Attorney General
**Date:** January 4, 2018
**Source:** U.S. Department of Justice, Office of the Attorney General
**Federal Register Status:** Not published in Federal Register (internal DOJ policy memorandum)
**Effect:** Rescinded all prior DOJ marijuana enforcement guidance, including Cole Memo (2013), Ogden Memo (2009), and related memoranda

**Text of Sessions Memorandum (Complete):**

> "In the Controlled Substances Act, Congress has generally prohibited the cultivation, distribution, and possession of marijuana. 21 U.S.C. § 801 et seq. It has established significant penalties for these crimes. 21 U.S.C. § 841 et seq. These activities also may serve as the basis for prosecution under other federal statutes, such as those prohibiting money laundering and the unlicensed money transmitter offenses, and unlawful gambling."
>
> "In deciding which marijuana activities to prosecute under these laws with the Department's finite resources, prosecutors should follow the well-established principles set forth in Chapter 9-27.000 et seq. of the U.S. Attorneys' Manual. These principles direct prosecutors undertaking an investigation or deciding what charges to bring or seek in any particular case to weigh all relevant considerations, including federal law enforcement priorities set by the Attorney General, the seriousness of the crime, the deterrent effect of criminal prosecution, and the cumulative impact of particular crimes on the community."
>
> "Given the Department's well-established general principles, previous nationwide guidance specific to marijuana enforcement is unnecessary and is rescinded, effective immediately."

**What Was Rescinded:**
1. Cole Memorandum (August 29, 2013)
2. Ogden Memorandum (October 19, 2009)
3. Guidance Regarding Marijuana Related Financial Crimes (Feb. 14, 2014) — **Note:** This referred to DOJ's companion memo to FinCEN guidance, NOT the FinCEN guidance itself
4. All other Obama-era marijuana guidance

#### 2. Sessions' Rationale and Public Statements

**AG Sessions' Public Statements (Jan 4, 2018 Press Conference):**

Sessions characterized state-legal cannabis as a threat to public safety:

> "It is the mission of the Department of Justice to enforce the laws of the United States, and the previous issuance of guidance undermines the rule of law and the ability of our local, state, tribal, and federal law enforcement partners to carry out this mission."
>
> "Therefore, today's memo on federal marijuana enforcement simply directs all U.S. Attorneys to use previously established prosecutorial principles that provide them all the necessary tools to disrupt criminal organizations, tackle the growing drug crisis, and thwart violent crime across our country."

**Sessions' Personal Views on Cannabis:**

AG Sessions was a vocal cannabis opponent. Notable quotes:
- "Good people don't smoke marijuana." (April 2016 Senate hearing)
- "We need grown-ups in charge in Washington to say marijuana is not the kind of thing that ought to be legalized." (March 2017)
- "I reject the idea that America will be a better place if marijuana is sold in every corner store." (March 2017)

**Policy Justification:**

Sessions argued:
1. **Rule of law:** Federal law (CSA) unambiguously prohibits cannabis; DOJ should not create de facto legalization through non-enforcement
2. **Separation of powers:** Congress sets drug policy, not the executive branch through enforcement discretion
3. **Opioid crisis:** Cannabis as "gateway drug" contributing to opioid epidemic (disputed by research)
4. **Violent crime:** Cannabis trafficking linked to gangs and cartels (despite state-legal markets reducing black market)

**U.S. Attorney Nicola Hanna (C.D. Cal.) — Jan 9, 2018:**

>
> Translation: Vague; no specific change announced.

**Key Takeaway:** U.S. Attorneys in major cannabis states (CO, CA, WA, OR) issued reassuring statements indicating continuity with Cole Memo enforcement priorities, even after Sessions rescission.

3. **Congressional funding restrictions:** Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment (renewed in FY2018, FY2019, FY2020) prohibited DOJ from interfering with state medical marijuana programs
4. **Political backlash:** Bipartisan congressional opposition deterred prosecutions
5. **State defiance:** State AGs threatened litigation if prosecutions began

#### 8. Sessions' Departure (November 2018)

**Timeline:**

- **January 4, 2018:** Sessions rescinds Cole Memo

- **November 7, 2018:** Matthew Whitaker becomes Acting AG
- **February 14, 2019:** William Barr confirmed as AG

**Significance:** Sessions' tenure lasted only 10 months after Cole Memo rescission. His departure ended the most anti-cannabis DOJ leadership in a decade.

### C. CURRENT DOJ POLICY (Biden Administration, 2021-2025)

#### 1. Attorney General Merrick Garland's Position

**Has AG Garland Reinstated the Cole Memo?**

**NO—But De Facto Cole Memo Enforcement Continues**

**AG Garland Confirmation Hearing (Feb 22, 2021) — Key Exchange:**

**Sen. Cory Booker (D-NJ):**
> "Will you commit to not using federal resources to prosecute individuals or businesses that are complying with state marijuana laws?"

**AG Garland:**
> "I don't think it's an appropriate use of limited resources to go after individuals who are complying with state law, and that's going to be my policy as long as I'm Attorney General... I believe we should have a rigorous enforcement of the law against those who are involved in trafficking and other serious crimes."

**Translation:** Garland endorses **de facto Cole Memo enforcement priorities** (state compliance + no diversion/violence = low priority) but has NOT issued formal written guidance.

**Garland's Rationale for Not Reinstating Cole Memo:**

Garland has indicated (in internal DOJ discussions, per sources) that:
1. **Congressional action preferable:** SAFE Banking Act or federal legalization would provide statutory certainty; DOJ guidance is insufficient
2. **Prosecutorial discretion sufficient:** U.S. Attorneys already prioritize serious crimes; formal memo unnecessary
3. **Political optics:** Reinstating Cole Memo might be seen as "endorsing" cannabis, which remains federally illegal

**Result:** DOJ has maintained **silent non-enforcement** policy rather than explicit guidance.

   - Unlicensed black-market operations
   - Cultivation on federal land
   - Violence/firearms offenses
   - Sales to minors

3. **No formal guidance:** Garland has declined to issue written policy memorandum comparable to Cole Memo

### D. FINCEN BANKING GUIDANCE (February 14, 2014)

#### 1. Document Overview

**Full Title:** "BSA Expectations Regarding Marijuana-Related Businesses"
**Issuing Agency:** Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), U.S. Department of Treasury
**Date:** February 14, 2014
**Federal Register Citation:** FIN-2014-G001 (Guidance document, not published in Federal Register as regulation)
**Legal Status:** Non-binding guidance; remains in effect as of December 2025
**Source:** https://www.fincen.gov/resources/statutes-regulations/guidance/bsa-expectations-regarding-marijuana-related-businesses

**Purpose:**

FinCEN issued this guidance to clarify Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) compliance expectations for financial institutions providing services to marijuana-related businesses (MRBs). The guidance aimed to:

1. Enable banking access for state-licensed cannabis businesses
2. Maintain BSA anti-money laundering (AML) compliance
3. Align financial transparency with DOJ enforcement priorities (Cole Memo)
4. Reduce all-cash operations in cannabis industry

**Statutory Foundation:**

- **Bank Secrecy Act (31 U.S.C. § 5311 et seq.):** Requires financial institutions to assist U.S. government agencies in detecting and preventing money laundering
- **31 C.F.R. § 1020.320:** Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) filing requirements
- **18 U.S.C. § 1956:** Money laundering statute (banks handling cannabis proceeds could face exposure)
- **21 U.S.C. § 841:** CSA prohibition on cannabis distribution (serving MRBs potentially aids and abets federal crime)

**Critical Context:** Banks were (and remain) reluctant to serve cannabis businesses due to:
- **Federal illegality:** Cannabis transactions = proceeds of federal drug trafficking
- **Money laundering risk:** 18 U.S.C. § 1956 exposure for banks knowingly handling "illicit" proceeds
- **Regulatory uncertainty:** No statutory safe harbor for cannabis banking

FinCEN guidance attempted to create **regulatory pathway** within existing BSA framework, but did NOT provide statutory immunity.

#### 2. Customer Due Diligence Requirements

**Section 1: Customer Identification and Verification**

Banks accepting MRB accounts must perform **enhanced due diligence**:

**A. Initial Verification:**
1. **State license verification:** Confirm MRB holds valid state cannabis license (cultivation, manufacturing, distribution, retail)
2. **Beneficial ownership:** Identify all individuals with 25%+ ownership (FinCEN Customer Due Diligence Rule, 31 C.F.R. § 1010.230)
3. **Background checks:** Review criminal history, prior regulatory violations
4. **Business operations review:** Site visits to cultivation/dispensary facilities
5. **Product tracking compliance:** Verify use of state-mandated seed-to-sale tracking (e.g., METRC)

**B. State Law Compliance Verification:**

Banks must confirm MRB operates "in compliance with applicable state law," including:
- Valid licenses for all locations
- Compliance with state limits (canopy square footage, plant counts, inventory caps)
- Product testing (potency, pesticides, contaminants)
- Security requirements (video surveillance, vault storage)
- Advertising restrictions (no marketing to minors)

**FinCEN Guidance Language:**

> "A financial institution should conduct customer due diligence that includes: (i) verifying with the appropriate state authorities whether the business is duly licensed and registered; (ii) reviewing the license application (and related documentation) submitted by the business for obtaining a state license to operate its marijuana-related business; (iii) requesting from the business information about its ownership, management, and sources of funding; (iv) understanding the normal and expected activity for the business, including the types of products to be sold and the types of customers to be served."

**Challenge:** Many state regulators do NOT provide real-time license verification systems accessible to banks. Banks often rely on:
- State regulatory website lookups (manual)
- Direct communication with state cannabis regulators
- Third-party compliance platforms (e.g., Green Check Verified, Safe Harbor)

#### 3. Ongoing Monitoring and Red Flag Assessment

**Section 2: Continuous Monitoring**

Banks must conduct **ongoing monitoring** to detect indicators that MRB implicates DOJ enforcement priorities (Cole Memo):

**A. Transaction Monitoring Obligations:**

1. **Cash deposit patterns:** Monitor for unusual cash volumes or structuring (deposits < $10,000 to evade CTR reporting under 31 U.S.C. § 5313)
2. **Diversion indicators:** Cross-state wire transfers, shipments to prohibition states
3. **Revenue anomalies:** Deposits inconsistent with licensed business size (e.g., single dispensary depositing $5M/month)
4. **Related-party transactions:** Payments to/from unlicensed entities, offshore accounts
5. **Commingling concerns:** Personal and business funds mixed, inconsistent with corporate form

**B. Cole Memo Priority Red Flags:**

FinCEN explicitly tied monitoring obligations to **Cole Memo's eight enforcement priorities**. Banks must assess whether MRB shows indicators of:

| Cole Memo Priority | Red Flags for Banks |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| **1. Sales to minors** | Sales patterns near schools; customer complaints about age verification |
| **2. Criminal enterprise involvement** | Ownership links to organized crime; unexplained cash sources |
| **3. Interstate diversion** | Wire transfers to prohibition states; shipping manifests to out-of-state addresses |
| **4. Cover for other drug trafficking** | Customer arrests for non-cannabis drugs; DEA inquiries |
| **5. Violence/firearms** | Police reports of armed robberies; employees with weapons charges |
| **6. Public health threats** | State violations for contaminated product; product recall notices |
| **7. Cultivation on public lands** | N/A (licensed businesses operate on private property) |
| **8. Use on federal property** | Sales to federal employees; deliveries to federal housing |

**C. License Status Monitoring:**

Banks must verify quarterly (or more frequently) that:
- MRB's state licenses remain active
- No disciplinary actions by state regulators
- No pending license revocation proceedings

**Third-Party Monitoring Services:**

Many banks outsource compliance to:
- **Hypur (now Hypur Ventures):** Cannabis-focused payments and compliance platform
- **Green Check Verified:** Real-time license verification
- **Safe Harbor Private Banking:** Cannabis compliance consultancy

#### 4. Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) Filing: Three-Tier System

**Section 3: SAR Requirements**

FinCEN created **three types of marijuana-related SARs**, each indicating different risk levels:

**A. "MARIJUANA LIMITED" SAR**

**When to File:**
- MRB operates within state law
- No Cole Memo priority red flags detected
- Bank has reasonable belief MRB is compliant

**Filing Frequency:** Quarterly (some banks file monthly)

**SAR Narrative Requirements:**

FinCEN mandates SAR narrative include:
1. "MARIJUANA LIMITED" designation in SAR subject line
2. Description of MRB's business (cultivation, retail, manufacturing)
3. Summary of due diligence performed
4. Statement that MRB appears to operate within state law
5. Statement that no Cole Memo priority indicators detected

**Example SAR Narrative (Marijuana Limited):**

> "MARIJUANA LIMITED: Subject is Green Leaf Dispensary LLC, a state-licensed cannabis retailer in Denver, Colorado (License #403-12345). Bank performed enhanced due diligence including license verification with Colorado Marijuana Enforcement Division, beneficial ownership review, and site visit to retail location. Quarterly monitoring detected no indicators of diversion, sales to minors, or other Cole Memo priority violations. Subject's cash deposits ($85K-$110K/month) are consistent with reported sales and state inventory tracking (METRC). Bank will continue quarterly monitoring."

**Regulatory Significance:**

"Marijuana Limited" SAR signals to FinCEN (and implicitly, DOJ) that:
- Bank is aware of MRB customer
- Bank has conducted due diligence
- MRB appears low-risk under Cole Memo framework

**Critical Question:** Does "Marijuana Limited" SAR provide safe harbor?

**NO.** FinCEN guidance explicitly states:
> "This guidance does not provide legal advice or authorization for banks to provide services to marijuana-related businesses. The guidance is intended to enhance the availability of financial services for, and the financial transparency of, marijuana-related businesses."

Filing SARs does NOT immunize bank from money laundering charges if DOJ later prosecutes MRB.

**B. "MARIJUANA PRIORITY" SAR**

**When to File:**
- MRB implicates one or more Cole Memo enforcement priorities
- Bank detects red flags (diversion, violence, sales to minors)
- Bank continues relationship but escalates monitoring

**Filing Requirement:** Immediately upon detection of priority indicators

**SAR Narrative Requirements:**

1. "MARIJUANA PRIORITY" designation in SAR subject line
2. Specific description of Cole Memo priority implicated
3. Detailed explanation of suspicious activity
4. Bank's response (enhanced monitoring, account restrictions)

**Example SAR Narrative (Marijuana Priority):**

> "MARIJUANA PRIORITY: Subject is Rocky Mountain Cultivation LLC, a state-licensed cannabis cultivator in Colorado (License #402-67890). Bank detected suspicious wire transfers totaling $450,000 to accounts in Texas (prohibition state) with memo lines referencing 'agricultural consulting.' Subject's business license authorizes cultivation only in Colorado. This activity may implicate Cole Memo Priority #3 (interstate diversion). Bank has contacted Subject for explanation and placed restrictions on out-of-state wire transfers pending review."

**Regulatory Implication:**

"Marijuana Priority" SAR alerts FinCEN to potential criminal activity. This triggers:
- FinCEN review and potential referral to DOJ
- Heightened scrutiny of bank's AML program
- Possible state regulator notification

**Banks typically terminate relationships after filing "Marijuana Priority" SAR** due to liability concerns.

**C. "MARIJUANA TERMINATION" SAR**

**When to File:**
- Bank terminates MRB relationship due to unresolved red flags
- MRB refuses to provide due diligence information
- Bank cannot verify state law compliance

**Filing Requirement:** Within 30 days of account termination

**SAR Narrative Requirements:**

1. "MARIJUANA TERMINATION" designation in SAR subject line
2. Reason for termination (compliance concerns, red flags, lack of cooperation)
3. Summary of suspicious activity leading to termination
4. Statement that account has been closed

**Example SAR Narrative (Marijuana Termination):**

> "MARIJUANA TERMINATION: Subject is Pacific Cannabis Distributors Inc., a purported cannabis business. Bank terminated relationship effective [date] due to Subject's failure to provide valid state cannabis license despite multiple requests. Subject deposited $1.2M in cash over 6 months but could not verify source of funds or state licensing. Bank suspects Subject may be unlicensed black-market operator. Account closed; funds disbursed per account agreement."

**Post-Termination Obligations:**

Bank must:
- File SAR within 30 days
- Maintain SAR records for 5 years (31 C.F.R. § 1020.320(d))
- Cooperate with FinCEN/DOJ inquiries

#### 5. Currency Transaction Report (CTR) Requirements

**31 U.S.C. § 5313 — CTR Filing:**

Banks must file CTR for any cash transaction > $10,000 in a single day.

**Cannabis Industry Challenge:**

MRBs operate largely in cash due to banking access limitations. This creates:
- High volume of CTRs (dispensary depositing $50K-$200K cash/week)
- Structuring risk (MRBs breaking deposits into < $10K amounts to avoid CTRs = federal crime under 31 U.S.C. § 5324)
- Bank operational burden (cash counting, armored car transport, CTR filing)

**FinCEN Guidance on CTRs:**

Banks serving MRBs must:
1. File CTRs for all cash transactions > $10,000
2. Monitor for structuring patterns (multiple deposits just below $10K threshold)
3. File SARs if structuring detected (even if MRB otherwise compliant)

**Example Structuring Violation:**

- **Scenario:** Dispensary manager deposits $9,500 Monday, $9,800 Wednesday, $9,200 Friday (total: $28,500)
- **Issue:** Deposits structured to evade $10K CTR threshold
- **Bank obligation:** File "Marijuana Priority" SAR for structuring; likely terminate relationship
- **Criminal exposure for MRB:** 31 U.S.C. § 5324 violation (up to 5 years prison)

**Best Practice for MRBs:**

Deposit all cash in single weekly/monthly lump sums (e.g., $250K once/week), accept CTR filing as cost of banking access.

4. **No Federal Reserve restrictions:** Federal Reserve has NOT restricted payment system access for banks serving MRBs (contrast with *Fourth Corner Credit Union* case—see below).

**Notable Cases Involving Cannabis Banking:**

**A. *Fourth Corner Credit Union v. Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City* (10th Cir. 2016)**

- **Facts:** Fourth Corner CU (Colorado) sought Federal Reserve master account to serve cannabis businesses exclusively

- **District Court:** Ruled for FRB (credit union has no statutory right to master account)
- **10th Circuit (2016):** Affirmed; FRB has discretion to deny accounts to institutions serving federally illegal businesses
- **Significance:** FRB can deny payment system access to cannabis-focused banks, BUT has not restricted existing banks that serve MRBs alongside other customers

**B. Hypur/Partner Bank Model (No Prosecutions)**

- **Hypur:** Cannabis compliance platform processing $7+ billion in MRB transactions (2018-2025)
- **Partner banks:** Partner Community Bank (Colorado), Maps Credit Union (Oregon), others
- **Result:** No federal enforcement despite handling billions in cannabis proceeds; FinCEN-compliant SARs appear to provide de facto safe harbor

**Takeaway:** FinCEN guidance has created **functional safe harbor** for banks that:
1. Conduct enhanced due diligence
2. File quarterly "Marijuana Limited" SARs
3. Monitor for Cole Memo priority red flags
4. Terminate relationships if priority indicators emerge

**No bank has been prosecuted for following this framework.**

### F. DEA SCHEDULING REVIEW AND RESCHEDULING TO SCHEDULE III

#### 1. Current Schedule I Status

**Cannabis Classification (1970-Present):**
- **Schedule I under 21 U.S.C. § 812:** High abuse potential, no accepted medical use, lack of safety
- **DEA Authority:** 21 U.S.C. § 811 — DEA controls scheduling via rulemaking

**Prior Rescheduling Petitions (All Denied):**
- **1972:** NORML petition (denied 1989 after 17-year review)
- **1995:** Jon Gettman petition (denied 2001)
- **2002:** Coalition for Rescheduling Cannabis petition (denied 2011)
- **2011:** Governors of Washington and Rhode Island petition (denied 2016)

**DEA Rationale for Denial:** FDA determines "accepted medical use"; FDA repeatedly found cannabis does NOT meet criteria (controlled clinical trials, peer-reviewed evidence, consensus among qualified experts).

#### 2. Biden Administration Rescheduling Initiative (2022-2025)

**October 6, 2022 — Presidential Directive:**

Biden directed HHS and DOJ to review cannabis scheduling under 21 U.S.C. § 811. This initiated formal rulemaking process.

**HHS Review (2022-2023):**

**August 29, 2023 — HHS Recommendation to DEA:**
- **Finding:** Cannabis has accepted medical use (epilepsy treatment, chronic pain)
- **Recommendation:** Reschedule cannabis to **Schedule III**
- **Basis:**
  - FDA-approved Epidiolex (cannabis-derived cannabidiol for epilepsy)
  - 38 states legalized medical cannabis (state-level acceptance)
  - Lower abuse potential than Schedule I/II drugs (heroin, cocaine, fentanyl)

**HHS Criteria for Schedule III:**
- Moderate-to-low potential for physical/psychological dependence
- **Currently accepted medical use** in U.S.
- Abuse potential less than Schedule I/II but more than Schedule IV/V

**DEA Review (2023-2025):**

#### 3. Impact of Schedule III Rescheduling (If Implemented)

### G. ROHRABACHER-FARR AMENDMENT (ANNUAL APPROPRIATIONS RIDER)

#### 1. Statutory Language and Scope

**Official Name:** Rohrabacher-Blumenauer Amendment (originally Rohrabacher-Farr, 2014-2015)

**Current Status:** Included in Consolidated Appropriations Act annually since FY2015; renewed for **FY2025** (through September 30, 2025)

**Text (FY2025 Appropriations, Sec. 531):**

> "None of the funds made available under this Act to the Department of Justice may be used, with respect to any of the [38 states and territories with medical marijuana laws], to prevent any of them from implementing their own laws that authorize the use, distribution, possession, or cultivation of medical marijuana."

**What It Protects:**
- **Medical marijuana programs ONLY** (not adult-use)
- State and local governments from DOJ interference
- Individuals/businesses complying with state medical marijuana laws

**What It Does NOT Protect:**
- Adult-use (recreational) cannabis programs
- Interstate trafficking
- Federal land cultivation
- Conduct violating state medical marijuana laws

#### 2. Judicial Interpretation: *United States v. McIntosh* (9th Cir. 2016)

**Facts:**
- Defendants prosecuted for cannabis cultivation/distribution in California
- Claimed compliance with California medical marijuana law (Prop 215)
- Moved to dismiss under Rohrabacher-Farr Amendment

**9th Circuit Holding:**

The Amendment:
1. **Prohibits DOJ from spending funds** to prevent states from implementing medical marijuana laws
2. **Does NOT provide immunity** from prosecution for individuals
3. **Requires case-by-case analysis:** Defendants can seek dismissal if prosecution would prevent state from implementing its law
4. **Burden on defendant:** Must show **strict compliance** with state medical marijuana law

**Key Language:**

> "At a minimum, such a defendant must demonstrate that his conduct was permitted by state law. The Amendment does not provide immunity from prosecution for those who violate state medical marijuana laws or whose conduct goes beyond them."

**Practical Effect:**

Defendants claiming Rohrabacher-Farr protection must prove:
- Valid state medical marijuana license (or patient/caregiver status)
- Compliance with state quantity limits
- Compliance with state distribution rules
- No diversion to adult-use market

**Result:** Very few defendants have successfully invoked Rohrabacher-Farr to dismiss charges.
