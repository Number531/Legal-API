## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This insurance coverage analysis evaluates the adequacy of Pinnacle Investment Management's Directors & Officers (D&O), Errors & Omissions (E&O), and fiduciary liability insurance programs in the context of Global Asset Partners' $1.8B acquisition. The assessment identifies **three critical coverage gaps** requiring immediate remediation, quantifies aggregate uninsured exposure at **$23.875M-$94.5M** (base to adverse case), and recommends targeted enhancements reducing uninsured risk by **80-90%** at an incremental annual premium cost of **$275K-$500K** (24-45% increase over current estimated program).

### Key Findings

#### 7. Tail Coverage Post-Acquisition ‚Äî Cost Avoidance Strategy

**Claims-Made Policy Risk:**
Pinnacle's D&O, E&O, and fiduciary liability policies are **claims-made** (cover claims first made during policy period, not occurrence-based). If policies cancelled post-acquisition without tail coverage or prior acts coverage from new insurer, claims reported after cancellation date **NOT covered** even if underlying act occurred during policy period.

**Tail Coverage (Extended Reporting Period) Cost:**
- Industry standard: **150-350%** of annual premium (medical malpractice 250-300%, RIA sector typically lower 150-200%)
- D&O premium $250K-$350K ‚Üí Tail $375K-$1.225M
- E&O premium $400K-$500K ‚Üí Tail $600K-$1.75M
- Fiduciary Liability premium $200K-$250K ‚Üí Tail $300K-$875K
- **TOTAL TAIL COST: $1.275M-$3.85M** (worst case 350% multiple)
- **Conservative estimate (150-200% multiple): $1.65M-$2.2M**

**Prior Acts Coverage Alternative:**
- New insurer (potentially Global Asset Partners' existing insurers) agrees to cover "prior acts" (acts occurring before new policy inception date)
- Retroactive date on new policy set to match Pinnacle's old policy retroactive date (continuous coverage)
- **Avoids tail purchase** from old insurer
- **Cost:** 0-25% surcharge on new policy premium = $0-$300K vs. tail $1.65M-$3.3M
- **Savings: $1.35M-$3.3M** (80-90% cost reduction)

**Limitation:**
- Prior acts coverage **defers** tail requirement (does not eliminate)
- If Pinnacle entity later dissolved/merged into Global Asset Partners: Must purchase tail at that time
- Works only if Pinnacle continues operations under new insurer indefinitely

**Recommended Strategy:**

**Phase 1 (60-90 Days Pre-Closing):**
1. Engage Global Asset Partners' insurance broker
2. Submit underwriting applications for Pinnacle D&O/E&O/fiduciary liability with **prior acts coverage** to 3-5 insurers
3. Highlight favorable underwriting factors: Clean loss history, enhanced compliance post-SEC exam (T1 remediation $1.2M-$1.8M), strong valuation procedures (T3 independent pricing services)

**Phase 2 (Closing):**
- **If prior acts quotes favorable (0-25% surcharge):** Bind prior acts coverage, avoid tail purchase, **save $1.65M-$3.3M**
- **If prior acts unavailable/expensive (>50% surcharge):** Purchase tail coverage, negotiate seller pays 50-100% (legacy pre-acquisition liability), fund from purchase price escrow

**Phase 3 (Post-Closing):**
- If Pinnacle entity continues indefinitely under new insurer: Prior acts coverage defers tail indefinitely (optimal outcome)
- If Pinnacle merged into Global Asset Partners 12-36 months post-closing: Purchase tail at merger date (deferred cost, buyer responsibility at that time based on then-current premium)

**Expected Outcome:** Prior acts coverage likely obtainable given Pinnacle's profile (strong compliance program post-remediation, no Section 36(b) litigation history, ERISA fiduciary insurance permissible under Section 410). **Probability 60-70%** of avoiding $1.65M-$3.3M tail cost.

### A. Directors & Officers (D&O) Liability Insurance

#### 1. Coverage Purpose and Structure

**D&O Policy Coverage Sides:**

D&O liability insurance typically provides three distinct coverage components:

- **Side A Coverage:** Personal liability of directors and officers when entity cannot or will not indemnify
  - Protects individual directors/officers from personal asset exposure
  - "Difference in conditions" coverage (fills gaps in corporate indemnification)
  - Typically non-indemnifiable claims: bankruptcy of entity, regulatory fines/penalties, employment claims

- **Side B Coverage:** Corporate reimbursement for indemnification obligations
  - Reimburses company when it indemnifies directors/officers for covered claims
  - Most common coverage trigger in practice (company indemnifies, then seeks insurance reimbursement)

- **Side C Coverage (Entity Coverage):** Direct liability of the company
  - Securities claims against corporate entity itself
  - Typically triggered in public company or registered investment company context

**Mutual Fund Context:**

For Pinnacle's 4 registered mutual funds, D&O insurance covers:
- 8 directors (6 independent + 2 interested) from personal liability for board decisions
- Fund entity itself for securities claims (prospectus liability, Investment Company Act violations)
- Advisory fees paid by funds to Pinnacle implicate Section 15(c) renewal/Section 36(b) excessive fee exposure

**Claims Covered:**
- Shareholder derivative suits alleging breach of fiduciary duty
- Section 36(b) excessive fee litigation (Investment Company Act)
- SEC enforcement actions against directors/officers
- Prospectus misstatements/omissions (Securities Act Section 11/12)
- State law breach of duty claims

**Defense Costs:**
- D&O policies typically cover defense costs (legal fees, expert witnesses, discovery costs)
- Defense costs coverage standard: "Duty to defend" (broader than duty to indemnify, based on complaint allegations)
- Defense costs may be included within policy limits ("wasting" policy) or outside limits ("non-wasting") ‚Äî most modern D&O policies are wasting (defense costs erode available limits)

**Exclusions (Standard):**
- Fraud/dishonesty (if adjudicated in final non-appealable judgment)
- Personal profit/improper advantage (director profited from breach)
- Intentional violation of law (willful/knowing)
- Prior acts (claims arising from facts known before policy inception, for claims-made policies)
- Bodily injury/property damage (covered by general liability, not D&O)
- Employment practices (covered by EPLI if rider purchased)
- Pollution (environmental liability)
- ERISA (covered by separate fiduciary liability policy)

#### 4. Section 36(b) Excessive Fee Litigation Risk and D&O Coverage

**Section 36(b) Overview:**

Investment Company Act Section 36(b) creates private right of action for fund shareholders to sue for breach of fiduciary duty with respect to management fees:

> "The investment adviser of a registered investment company shall be deemed to have a fiduciary duty with respect to the receipt of compensation for services, or of payments of a material nature, paid by such registered investment company... to such investment adviser or any affiliated person of such investment adviser."‚Å¥

**Gartenberg Standard (Pre-2010):**
Courts applied *Gartenberg v. Merrill Lynch Asset Management* factors:
- Nature and quality of services
- Profitability of fund to adviser
- Fall-out benefits (ancillary revenues from fund operations)
- Economies of scale
- Comparative fee structures

**Jones v. Harris (2010):**
Supreme Court clarified Section 36(b) standard:‚Åµ
- Adviser must charge fee "so disproportionately large that it bears no reasonable relationship to the services rendered and could not have been the product of arm's length bargaining"
- Plaintiff must show adviser "charged a fee that is so disproportionately large"
- High burden for plaintiffs (explains why no Section 36(b) verdict for plaintiffs ever)

**Litigation Outcomes:**

Per Cornerstone Research analysis of Section 36(b) cases 2010-2020:‚Å∂
- 85% of cases dismissed at motion to dismiss or summary judgment stage
- 15% settled (settlement amounts typically not disclosed publicly)
- $0 plaintiff verdicts at trial

However, defense costs substantial even when defendants prevail:

> "Some defense counsel have informally estimated that litigation costs in section 36(b) lawsuits may range from three to four times higher for advisers than for plaintiffs... Section 36(b) lawsuits that survive early legal challenges and proceed to the discovery stage of litigation can generate substantial defense costs, in the form of legal and expert expenses, for defendant advisers."‚Å∑

**Estimated Defense Costs:**
- Motion to dismiss stage: $500K-$1M (legal fees, expert declarations)
- Summary judgment stage: $2M-$4M (extensive discovery, expert reports, depositions)
- Trial (rare): $5M-$10M+

**Settlement Amounts (Estimated):**

While specific Section 36(b) settlements not publicly disclosed, comparable excessive fee settlements in ERISA 401(k) context provide proxy:
- Small fund complex (<$5B AUM): $2M-$5M settlements typical
- Mid-size fund complex ($10B-$20B AUM): $5M-$15M settlements
- Large fund complex (>$50B AUM): $15M-$50M+ settlements

**Pinnacle Exposure:**

### C. Fiduciary Liability Insurance (ERISA)

#### 1. Coverage Purpose and Structure

**Fiduciary Liability Policy Coverage:**

ERISA fiduciary liability insurance protects investment advisers serving as ERISA fiduciaries from claims related to retirement plan assets:

- **Breach of fiduciary duty:** ERISA Section 404(a) prudent expert standard violations
- **Prohibited transactions:** ERISA Section 406 self-dealing, conflicts of interest
- **DOL investigations:** Department of Labor audits, examinations, enforcement actions
- **Plan participant lawsuits:** Class actions by plan participants alleging fiduciary breaches
- **Plan sponsor lawsuits:** Claims by plan sponsors (employer) for breach of investment management agreement
- **Co-fiduciary liability:** Liability for breaches by other plan fiduciaries (ERISA Section 405)

**Claims Covered:**¬≤¬≥
- Imprudent investment selection (underperforming funds, excessive risk)
- Excessive fees (revenue sharing, 12b-1 fees, undisclosed compensation)
- Failure to monitor investments (failure to replace underperforming funds)
- Failure to follow plan documents (investment policy statement deviations)
- Prohibited transactions (cross-trading, self-dealing, party-in-interest transactions)

**Defense Costs:**
- Fiduciary liability policies cover defense costs for DOL investigations and litigation
- Duty to defend applies (even if claim meritless, insurer pays counsel)

**Settlements and Judgments:**
- Policy covers settlements negotiated with plan participants/sponsors
- Policy covers judgments awarded by courts

**Exclusions (Standard):**
- **ERISA excise taxes:** 15% excise tax on prohibited transactions (ERISA Section 4975) typically **NOT insurable** (penalty, public policy exclusion)
- **DOL civil penalties:** ERISA Section 502(l) civil penalties typically excluded
- **Fraud/intentional misconduct:** If fiduciary intentionally engaged in self-dealing, no coverage
- **Fines/penalties:** Regulatory fines generally excluded (but defense costs covered)
- **Bodily injury/property damage:** Not applicable to fiduciary context
- **Prior acts:** Claims-made policy excludes claims arising from facts known before policy inception

#### 2. ERISA Section 410 ‚Äî Statutory Permissibility of Fiduciary Insurance

**ERISA Section 410(a) ‚Äî Prohibition on Exculpatory Provisions:**

ERISA Section 410(a), 29 U.S.C. ¬ß 1110(a), provides:¬≤‚Å¥

> "Except as provided in sections 1108(b)(3) and 1108(c)(3) of this title, any provision in an agreement or instrument which **purports to relieve a fiduciary from responsibility or liability** for any responsibility, obligation, or duty under this part **shall be void as against public policy**."

**ERISA Section 410(b) ‚Äî Insurance Permitted:**

However, Section 410(b) creates exception:¬≤‚Åµ

> "Nothing in this subchapter shall preclude‚Äî
> (1) an employer or an employee organization from **purchasing insurance** for its directors, officers, or employees to cover liability or losses occurring by reason of the act or omission of a fiduciary, if such insurance **permits recourse by the insurer against the fiduciary** in the case of a breach of a fiduciary obligation by such fiduciary;
> (2) a fiduciary from **purchasing insurance to cover liability** under this part from and in his own behalf; or
> (3) an employer or an employee organization from **purchasing insurance to cover potential liability** of one or more persons who serve in a fiduciary capacity with regard to an employee benefit plan."

**DOL Interpretation ‚Äî Indemnification vs. Insurance:**

Department of Labor Interpretive Bulletin 75-4, 29 C.F.R. ¬ß 2509.75-4, clarifies:¬≤‚Å∂

> "The Department of Labor interprets section 410(a) as **rendering void any arrangement for indemnification of a fiduciary of an employee benefit plan by the plan**, as such an arrangement would, in effect, relieve the fiduciary of responsibility and liability to the plan."

However:
> "Section 410 of ERISA allows the purchase of [fiduciary liability insurance], though Section 410 is also the provision that **prohibits a plan from using its assets to indemnify a fiduciary** for a breach of fiduciary duty."¬≤‚Å∑

**Key Requirement:**

Fiduciary liability insurance premiums **cannot be paid from plan assets**:¬≤‚Å∏
> "To meet ERISA 410(b)(1) requirements, the extra charge cannot be paid out of plan assets. Either each fiduciary must pay for it individually or (as is common) **the employer pays for the endorsement from its corporate assets**."

**Pinnacle Application:**

- ‚úÖ Pinnacle can purchase fiduciary liability insurance covering $17.4B ERISA assets
- ‚úÖ Insurance premiums paid from Pinnacle corporate funds (not from ERISA plan assets)
- ‚úÖ Policy must permit insurer recourse against Pinnacle if breach of fiduciary duty proven (standard policy term)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Insurance cannot cover ERISA excise taxes or DOL civil penalties (public policy exclusion)

#### 5. ERISA Prohibited Transaction Risk and Excise Tax Insurability

**T2 Employment-Labor-Analyst ERISA Analysis:**

**ERISA Section 4975 Excise Tax:**

Internal Revenue Code Section 4975 imposes excise tax on prohibited transactions:¬≥¬π

> "Section 4975 imposes a **15 percent excise tax** against disqualified persons, which are substantially similar to ERISA parties in interest, who engage in prohibited transactions with qualified pension plans."

**Correction Within 90 Days:**
- If prohibited transaction corrected within 90-day period: 15% tax imposed
- If not corrected within 90 days: Additional 100% tax imposed (total 115% of amount involved)

**Insurability of Excise Tax:**

ERISA excise taxes are **penalties**, generally **uninsurable** under public policy doctrine:¬≥¬≤

> "Penalties and fines are typically NOT insurable as penalty, but defense costs covered by fiduciary liability policy."

**Rationale:**
- Allowing insurance for penalties would undermine deterrent effect of ERISA enforcement
- Public policy: Fiduciaries should bear personal responsibility for intentional prohibited transactions
- Distinguishes between:
  - ‚úÖ **Defense costs** investigating/defending DOL prohibited transaction allegations: **Covered**
  - ‚ùå **Excise tax** if prohibited transaction confirmed: **NOT covered**
  - ‚úÖ **Restitution to plan participants** (make-whole damages): **Covered** (some policies)

**Pinnacle Exposure:**

- Excise tax: $1.5M (15% √ó $10M)
- **Fiduciary liability insurance:** Covers **$0** of excise tax (penalty exclusion)
- **Pinnacle liability:** $1.5M out-of-pocket

**Defense Costs Covered:**
