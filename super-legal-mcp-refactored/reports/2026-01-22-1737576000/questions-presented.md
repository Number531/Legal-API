## OVERVIEW

This memorandum addresses ten critical legal questions arising from Global Asset Partners LLC's proposed $1.8 billion acquisition of Pinnacle Investment Management, Inc., a Delaware corporation operating as a registered investment adviser (SEC File No. 801-45678) with $42.5 billion in assets under management. The Questions Presented follow the Under/Does/When format required for senior partner and board-level decision-making, ensuring each question is answerable as Yes/No/Probably Yes/Probably No with determinative facts embedded in the question structure.

The questions are ordered by deal-blocking potential, with the highest-risk regulatory and fiduciary issues presented first, followed by commercial, valuation, and operational matters that affect purchase price and transaction structure but may not independently block the transaction.

- **Brief Answer Target:** Section IV.D (Private Fund Regulation), Section IV.I (Employment and Retention)

### Question 7: Investment Company Act 1940 Advisory Contract Assignment and Client Consent (Deal-Blocking Risk: MEDIUM)

**Under** Investment Company Act Section 15(a)(4) (requiring shareholder approval of new investment advisory contract upon "assignment" as defined by Section 2(a)(4) as transfer resulting in change of control), **does** Global Asset Partners LLC's acquisition of 100% of Pinnacle's stock constitute an "assignment" of the investment advisory contracts between Pinnacle and its four registered mutual funds (Large Cap Growth Fund, International Fund, Small Cap Value Fund, and Balanced Fund, with combined AUM of $12.8 billion and generating $76 million in annual advisory fees representing 20% of Pinnacle's total revenue), thereby requiring Pinnacle to: (1) call special shareholder meetings for all four funds within 60 days of definitive merger agreement, (2) deliver proxy materials explaining the change of control and requesting approval of new investment advisory contracts with identical terms, (3) obtain majority shareholder approval (>50% of shares voted, not >50% of all outstanding shares under Investment Company Act Section 15(a)(4) and SEC interpretive guidance), and **when** does failure to obtain shareholder approval for one or more funds require Pinnacle to operate under interim advisory contracts for up to 150 days under Investment Company Act Rule 15a-4(b)(2), after which the advisory relationship must terminate, resulting in loss of $19 million in annual advisory fees per fund if approval fails?

- **Jurisdiction:** Federal (SEC enforcement authority under Investment Company Act 1940)
- **Assigned Specialists:** securities-researcher (T1), commercial-contracts-analyst (T4)
- **Priority:** HIGH
- **Brief Answer Target:** Section IV.B (Investment Company Act 1940)
- **Deal-Blocking Rationale:** The SEC Staff's position in interpretive releases (most recently in 2018) treats private equity acquisitions of 100% of RIA stock as "assignments" requiring shareholder approval under Section 15(a)(4), even if the advisory contract itself does not change. Failure to obtain shareholder approval for one or more mutual funds within the 150-day interim contract period requires Pinnacle to terminate advisory services to that fund and allows the fund board to hire a replacement adviser, resulting in immediate and permanent loss of advisory fee revenue. If the $3.2 billion International Fund (generating $19 million in annual fees) shareholders vote down the new advisory contract due to concerns about private equity ownership and the fund's -120 basis points underperformance versus benchmark over the trailing three years, Pinnacle loses 5% of total revenue permanently.

### Question 9: Massachusetts Non-Compete Enforceability and Portfolio Manager Retention Risk (Deal-Blocking Risk: MEDIUM)

**Under** the Massachusetts Non-Compete Agreement Act (M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L, effective October 1, 2018), **are** Pinnacle's non-compete agreements with eight senior portfolio managers (managing $35 billion AUM, representing 83% of total AUM, with individual portfolios ranging from $3.2 billion to $6.8 billion) enforceable when the agreements: (1) restrict portfolio managers from joining competing investment advisers for 12 months following termination, (2) provide for 6-month garden leave at 100% of base salary (exceeding the statutory minimum of 50% base salary during the restricted period), (3) apply only to "exempt" employees under the Fair Labor Standards Act (all eight portfolio managers earn $400,000 to $1.2 million in base salary, well above the $684/week exempt threshold), and (4) are supported by Pinnacle's trade secret protection rationale (proprietary investment strategies, client lists, portfolio holdings, and performance attribution methodologies qualify as trade secrets under the Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, M.G.L. c. 93, §§ 42-42G), and **when** does the practical reality that institutional clients maintain personal relationships with portfolio managers and may terminate Pinnacle's advisory agreement to follow a departing portfolio manager to a new firm render non-compete agreements ineffective at preventing client attrition (estimated at 30-40% client retention loss if a senior portfolio manager managing $5 billion departs), thereby requiring Global Asset Partners to implement alternative retention strategies including $45 million in retention bonuses over three years for the top 15 investment professionals and $150 million in earnout payments to founders contingent on maintaining $40 billion AUM threshold in years 1-3 post-closing?

- **Jurisdiction:** Massachusetts state law (non-compete enforceability)
- **Assigned Specialists:** case-law-analyst (T8), employment-labor-analyst (T2), commercial-contracts-analyst (T4)
- **Priority:** HIGH
- **Brief Answer Target:** Section IV.I (Employment and Retention)
- **Deal-Blocking Rationale:** The concentration of 83% of AUM in portfolios managed by eight senior portfolio managers creates existential retention risk. While Pinnacle's non-compete agreements comply with Massachusetts Reform Act statutory requirements (12-month duration, garden leave exceeding 50% salary, exempt employees, trade secret protection rationale), the enforceability of non-competes in the investment management industry faces practical limitations: (1) institutional clients retain the contractual right to terminate advisory agreements on 30-90 days' notice and may follow departing portfolio managers regardless of non-compete validity, (2) litigation to enforce non-competes requires 6-18 months to obtain preliminary injunction and final judgment, during which clients may have already transitioned to the departing manager's new firm, and (3) courts balance employer's trade secret protection interest against employee's right to earn a livelihood, often narrowing non-compete scope or reducing duration. If two of the eight senior portfolio managers depart within 12 months post-acquisition and their $10 billion combined AUM follows them (at 30-40% attrition rate), Pinnacle loses $60-80 million in annual revenue (16-21% of FY2024 revenue), materially impairing the investment thesis.

### Question 10: Tax Treatment of Carried Interest and Deal Structure Optimization (Deal-Blocking Risk: LOW)

**Under** Section 1061 of the Internal Revenue Code (enacted by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, extending the long-term capital gain holding period from one year to three years for "applicable partnership interests" receiving carried interest / performance fees), **does** Pinnacle's $23 million in performance fees earned in FY2024 from its two hedge funds (calculated as 20% of profits above a hurdle rate, typically 8% annually) qualify for preferential long-term capital gain treatment at 20% federal tax rate (plus 3.8% net investment income tax under Section 1411, totaling 23.8%) versus ordinary income treatment at 37% federal rate (plus 3.8% NIIT, totaling 40.8%), when the average holding period for securities in the Credit Opportunities Fund and Opportunity Fund is 18-24 months (with some positions held less than three years and therefore failing Section 1061's three-year holding requirement), resulting in mixed tax treatment where an estimated $8-12 million of the $23 million in carried interest is taxed as ordinary income and $11-15 million qualifies for capital gain treatment, and **when** should Global Asset Partners structure the acquisition as a stock purchase (preserving Pinnacle's corporate charter and tax attributes including $18 million in net operating loss carryforwards, but limiting acquirer's basis step-up) versus an asset purchase with Section 338(h)(10) election (allowing full fair market value basis step-up on $1.8 billion purchase price, generating $40-60 million in incremental annual tax depreciation/amortization over 15 years, but potentially forfeiting Pinnacle's NOL carryforwards under Section 382 ownership change limitations)?

- **Jurisdiction:** Federal tax law (IRS enforcement authority)
- **Assigned Specialists:** tax-structure-analyst (T5), financial-analyst (T3)
- **Priority:** MEDIUM
- **Brief Answer Target:** Section IV.J (Tax Considerations)
- **Deal-Blocking Rationale:** While tax structure optimization is not deal-blocking, it materially affects after-tax purchase price. Section 1061's three-year holding requirement converted an estimated $8-12 million of Pinnacle's FY2024 carried interest from long-term capital gain (23.8% effective rate) to ordinary income (40.8% effective rate), increasing tax liability by $1.36-2.04 million annually (17% rate differential × $8-12 million). Post-acquisition, if Global Asset Partners transitions Pinnacle's hedge fund investment strategy to longer holding periods (averaging 36+ months) to preserve carried interest capital gain treatment, the strategy shift may reduce portfolio turnover and force retention of underperforming positions, potentially degrading investment performance and triggering client redemptions. Alternatively, if Global Asset Partners elects Section 338(h)(10) asset purchase treatment to obtain basis step-up, the resulting Section 382 ownership change limits annual NOL utilization to approximately $1.8 million per year (simplified calculation: $1.8B purchase price × long-term tax-exempt rate ~1% = $18M NOL value / 10-year utilization = $1.8M/year), forfeiting immediate use of Pinnacle's $18 million NOL carryforward and deferring tax benefits over 10 years.

## QUESTION PRIORITY RATIONALE

### CRITICAL / HIGH Priority (Questions 1-2)

**Question 1 (SEC Examination Deficiencies)** and **Question 2 (ERISA Prohibited Transactions)** represent the highest deal-blocking risk because unresolved regulatory violations may result in:
1. **SEC/DOL denial** of change-of-control application or Form ADV/Form 5500 amendments
2. **Enforcement actions** post-closing (cease-and-desist orders, fines, undertakings requiring consultant oversight)
3. **Client terminations** upon discovery of regulatory violations (ERISA plan sponsors have fiduciary duty to prudently select and monitor investment advisers, and continued engagement of an adviser with unresolved prohibited transaction violations may itself constitute a breach of fiduciary duty)
4. **D&O/E&O insurance coverage disputes** (insurers may deny coverage for "known violations" existing at policy inception)

### HIGH Priority (Questions 3-7)

**Question 3 (Side Letter MFN)**, **Question 4 (Key Person Risk)**, **Question 5 (Client Concentration)**, **Question 6 (Valuation Markdown)**, and **Question 7 (Investment Company Act Assignment)** represent material revenue and valuation risks that affect purchase price but are not independently deal-blocking if disclosed and quantified. These issues are addressable through:
1. **Purchase price adjustment** (escrow, holdback, earnout tied to AUM retention)
2. **Contractual remediation** (limited partner consent to amend key person provisions, client retention agreements)
3. **Regulatory approval process** (shareholder meetings for mutual fund advisory contract assignment)
4. **Independent valuation** (third-party pricing to confirm NAV accuracy)

### MEDIUM Priority (Questions 8-9)

**Question 8 (Marketing Rule)** and **Question 9 (Non-Compete Enforceability)** represent compliance remediation and operational risks that are manageable with appropriate policies and retention strategies. Marketing Rule violations are typically resolved through consent orders and amended disclosures without material fines. Non-compete agreements, while compliant with Massachusetts law, face practical enforcement challenges that require supplementation with retention bonuses and equity incentives.

### LOW Priority (Question 10)

**Question 10 (Tax Structure)** represents optimization analysis that does not affect transaction viability but informs deal structure and post-acquisition tax planning. The choice between stock purchase and asset purchase (with Section 338(h)(10) election) involves trade-offs between NOL preservation and basis step-up that can be modeled during negotiation.
