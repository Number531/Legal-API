### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Colorado Water Supply Contract Law

Colorado water supply agreements are governed by a multi-layered legal framework comprising statutory authority, common law contract principles, and federal reclamation law where Bureau of Reclamation projects are involved.

**a. Authority for Water Contracting**

Colorado water districts and municipalities possess statutory authority to enter water supply contracts under C.R.S. § 37-45-131 ("Sale of water by contract").¹ This provision authorizes water conservancy districts to execute long-term wholesale agreements for municipal and industrial use.² Colorado water rights, characterized as usufructuary property rights under the prior appropriation doctrine (C.R.S. § 37-92-103),³ may be leased, exchanged, or contracted without permanent transfer of the underlying water right.⁴ The Colorado General Assembly has affirmed that water law "does not impair, limit, or otherwise affect the rights of persons or entities to enter into agreements, contracts, or memoranda of understanding with other persons or entities relating to the appropriation, movement, or use of water."⁵

**b. Assignment and Change of Control Provisions**

Colorado commercial contracts, including water supply agreements, typically incorporate strict anti-assignment provisions requiring mutual consent for transfer. The Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 2, adopted in Colorado as C.R.S. § 4-2-210, governs assignment and delegation where water is sold as "goods."⁶ Under UCC principles:

- A prohibition on assignment of "the contract" bars only **delegation of performance**, not assignment of rights to receive payment.⁷
- An assignment of "the contract" includes both assignment of rights and delegation of duties unless circumstances indicate otherwise.⁸
- Anti-assignment clauses are generally enforceable to prevent assignment without counterparty consent.⁹

**Critical distinction for acquisitions**: Stock purchases (acquiring shares of the contracting entity) typically **do not trigger assignment provisions** because the legal entity remains unchanged. However, **change-of-control clauses** may require notification or consent if the contracting party's ownership changes.¹⁰ Asset purchases, by contrast, constitute assignments requiring counterparty consent under typical anti-assignment language.¹¹

**c. Wholesale Water Service Obligations**

Colorado public utilities are subject to certificated service territory requirements and obligations to provide adequate service under CPUC oversight. Wholesale water contracts between utilities and smaller municipalities or special districts typically extend 10-40 years to justify infrastructure investment, with automatic renewal provisions subject to termination notice periods of 1-5 years.¹² Wholesale rates generally track the supplier's retail rates through automatic adjustment clauses, ensuring cost recovery while maintaining wholesale discounts to retail pricing.¹³

#### 2. Federal Reclamation Law for USBR Projects

**a. Bureau of Reclamation Project Structure**

The Grand Valley Diversion Project (Grand Valley Project) was constructed by the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation (USBR) beginning in 1913-1916 under the Reclamation Act of 1902 and subsequent project-specific legislation.¹⁴ In 1949, USBR transferred dam and canal operations to the Grand Valley Water Users Association (GVWUA), a Colorado nonprofit corporation organized in 1905.¹⁵ GVWUA operates 55 miles of Government Highline Canal and 150 miles of project laterals serving over 23,000 irrigable acres.¹⁶

Participation in USBR reclamation projects is governed by use authorizations, contracts, or easements granting rights to divert project water. These instruments remain subject to federal regulatory oversight even after operational transfer to local water user associations.

**b. Assignment and Transfer Requirements — 43 CFR Part 429**

Federal regulations governing use of Bureau of Reclamation land, facilities, and waterbodies impose mandatory approval requirements for any assignment or transfer:

> **43 C.F.R. § 429.2(b)**: "Use authorizations may not be transferred or assigned to others **without prior written approval of Reclamation**, unless specifically provided for in the use authorization..."¹⁷

> **43 C.F.R. § 429.28(d)**: Use authorizations must include "the conditions under which the use authorization may be renewed, terminated, amended, **assigned or transferred**..."¹⁸

For long-term project participation agreements:

> **43 C.F.R. § 429.17**: Use authorizations for easements or rights-of-way exceeding 25 years are subject to approval from **water user organizations** under contract obligation for repayment.¹⁹

This provision means that if MSWC's Grand Valley Project participation is structured as a long-term easement (>25 years), GVWUA possesses veto rights over assignment to AWI. The practical effect is that **AWI must obtain both USBR approval and GVWUA approval** for transfer of MSWC's 45,000 AF/year Colorado River allocation via the Grand Valley Project.

**c. USBR Approval Criteria**

USBR evaluates assignment requests under criteria established at 43 C.F.R. §§ 429.14-15, examining:²⁰

- Whether the assignment interferes with project operations
- Financial capacity of the assignee to meet payment obligations
- Demonstration of continued beneficial use of water
- Environmental compliance (NEPA review where appropriate)
- Public interest considerations, including foreign ownership concerns

The approval process typically requires 60-120 days for administrative review absent objections. However, if GVWUA objects or environmental groups challenge foreign ownership (45% CPPIB/Macquarie), the timeline may extend to 6-12 months and require formal proceedings.

#### 3. Wastewater Reuse Regulation — Colorado Regulation 84

Colorado Regulation 84 (5 CCR 1002-84) establishes standards for non-potable water reuse, defining reclaimed water as "domestic wastewater that has received secondary treatment by a domestic treatment works and such additional treatment as to enable the wastewater to meet the standards for approved uses."²¹

**Approved non-potable uses** include landscape irrigation (residential, commercial, golf courses, parks), agricultural irrigation, industrial processes (cooling towers, manufacturing), toilet/urinal flushing in commercial buildings, fire protection systems, and construction dust control.²² Treatment standards require E. coli levels of 0 detected in ≥75% of samples and not exceeding 126/100 mL monthly, with turbidity ≤5 NTU in no more than 5% of monthly samples.²³

Wastewater reuse represents a **climate-resilient supply** because effluent flows remain consistent year-round regardless of precipitation, in contrast to surface water allocations subject to curtailment during drought.²⁴ Colorado has utilized deliberate non-potable reuse since the 1960s, with 27+ jurisdictions employing reuse water for irrigation and industrial applications.²⁵

#### B.4 South Metro Reuse Water Expansion Opportunity and Change-of-Control Consent

**Conclusion:** MSWC's South Metro Wastewater Authority reuse water purchase agreement (5,000 AF/year, $4M-$7.5M annually) presents **MEDIUM** risk of change-of-control consent requirement but also represents **critical strategic opportunity** for drought-proof supply expansion. However, South Metro benefits from selling effluent (generates revenue for WWTP operations, environmental benefit of reuse vs. discharge), making termination unlikely (10-20% probability). The **strategic priority** is negotiating **expansion rights** for additional 5,000-10,000 AF/year to offset Colorado River curtailment with climate-resilient supply. If Colorado River curtailments persist (70-80% probability), expanding reuse water from 5,000 AF/year to 10,000-15,000 AF/year replaces volatile agricultural leases with stable, drought-proof supply at comparable cost ($1,000-$1,500/AF for expansion vs. $850-$1,200/AF agricultural leases). Pre-closing negotiation for **right of first refusal (ROFR)** on South Metro expansion capacity is **critical**—without ROFR, competing utilities (Aurora, Denver Water) could secure expansion capacity, foreclosing MSWC's supply diversification strategy. **Exposure:** $6.6M-$14.7M NPV if South Metro renegotiates pricing upward (30-40% probability); **Opportunity:** $50M-$100M NPV value of securing drought-proof expansion rights. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Colorado Regulation 84 standards, South Metro WWTP capacity analysis, reuse water market pricing 2024].

**Rule:** Colorado Regulation 84 (5 CCR 1002-84) establishes standards for non-potable water reuse, defining reclaimed water as "domestic wastewater that has received secondary treatment by a domestic treatment works and such additional treatment as to enable the wastewater to meet the standards for approved uses."²¹ Approved non-potable uses include landscape irrigation, agricultural irrigation, industrial processes, toilet/urinal flushing, fire protection, and construction dust control.²² Treatment standards require E. coli 0 detected in ≥75% of samples and ≤126/100 mL monthly, with turbidity ≤5 NTU in ≤5% of monthly samples.²³

Wastewater effluent constitutes a **drought-proof supply** because flows remain consistent year-round regardless of precipitation, contrasting with surface water allocations subject to curtailment.²⁴ Colorado has utilized deliberate non-potable reuse since the 1960s, with 27+ jurisdictions employing reuse water.²⁵ Reuse water pricing ranges from $500/AF (basic secondary effluent for agricultural irrigation) to $12,000-$13,000/AF (advanced treatment for indirect potable reuse).⁴⁹ Non-potable reuse for parks, golf courses, and industrial cooling typically costs $800-$1,500/AF (mid-range: secondary + UV disinfection).

**Explanation:** Wastewater reuse represents the water sector's paradigm shift toward circular economy principles. Municipalities increasingly value reuse water as climate-resilient supply to offset surface water curtailment risks. Unlike Colorado River allocations subject to interstate compact administration and climate variability, wastewater flows correlate with population (sewer discharges), remaining stable during drought. This reliability premium justifies reuse water pricing above agricultural leases ($850-$1,200/AF) despite comparable treatment requirements.

Reuse water purchase agreements between municipal water utilities and wastewater authorities typically extend 10-20 years to justify distribution infrastructure investment. Key contractual provisions include:

- **Volume commitments**: Fixed annual volume (AF/year) with optional additional purchases
- **Pricing mechanisms**: CPI-indexed escalation (predictable) vs. periodic renegotiation (volatile)
- **Water quality guarantees**: Wastewater authority warrants Regulation 84 compliance; water utility responsible for additional treatment if needed
- **Reliability**: Wastewater authority obligated to deliver year-round, subject to force majeure (WWTP outage, pipeline failure)
- **Expansion rights**: Right of first refusal (ROFR) for additional capacity if wastewater authority expands WWTP
- **Assignment**: Requires wastewater authority consent for change of control or assignment

The **expansion rights** provision is strategically critical. If the reuse water purchaser lacks ROFR, competing utilities can secure expansion capacity through direct negotiation with the wastewater authority, foreclosing the original purchaser's supply augmentation strategy. In Colorado's competitive municipal water market, Aurora, Denver Water, and Colorado Springs aggressively pursue reuse water contracts to diversify drought-vulnerable surface supply portfolios.

**Application:** MSWC purchases 5,000 AF/year treated effluent from South Metro Wastewater Authority for non-potable reuse (parks, golf courses, industrial cooling, landscape irrigation).⁵⁰ Estimated contract cost is $4M-$7.5M annually ($800-$1,500/AF midpoint pricing), recoverable in MSWC's CPUC rates as purchased water supply.⁵¹ Treatment standard is secondary + UV disinfection by South Metro, with MSWC providing distribution infrastructure to deliver reuse water to end users (parks, golf courses, commercial/industrial customers).
