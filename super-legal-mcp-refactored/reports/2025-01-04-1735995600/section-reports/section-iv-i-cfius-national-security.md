## IV.I. CFIUS AND NATIONAL SECURITY REVIEW

### A. Legal Framework

The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) operates under Section 721 of the Defense Production Act of 1950, as amended and codified at 50 U.S.C. § 4565.¹ CFIUS is an interagency committee chaired by the Secretary of the Treasury with authority to review certain foreign investments in U.S. businesses and real estate transactions for national security implications.² The Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act of 2018 (FIRRMA), enacted August 13, 2018, significantly expanded CFIUS jurisdiction to include non-controlling investments in critical infrastructure, critical technology, and sensitive personal data (collectively, "TID U.S. businesses"), and established mandatory filing requirements for certain high-risk transactions.³ FIRRMA's implementing regulations at 31 C.F.R. Parts 800-802 became effective February 13, 2020.⁴

#### 1. CFIUS Jurisdiction: Covered Control Transactions

Under 31 C.F.R. § 800.211, CFIUS has jurisdiction over "covered transactions," which include:

**Covered Control Transactions:** Any transaction by or with a foreign person that could result in foreign control of a U.S. business.⁵ "Control" means the power, direct or indirect, to determine important matters affecting an entity, including decisions regarding management, significant policies, and the sale or pledge of principal assets.⁶ A foreign person holding 25% or more voting interest in an entity is presumed to have control absent evidence to the contrary.⁷

**Covered Investments:** FIRRMA expanded CFIUS jurisdiction to include non-controlling investments by foreign persons in TID U.S. businesses where the investment affords the foreign person access to material nonpublic technical information, board membership or observer rights, or involvement in substantive decisionmaking.⁸

The proposed American Water Infrastructure LLC (AWI) acquisition of Mountain States Water Company (MSWC) constitutes a **covered control transaction** because AWI—a foreign person by virtue of 45% aggregate foreign ownership—is acquiring 100% of MSWC's equity through stock purchase, resulting in foreign control of a U.S. business.⁹

**[ANALYSIS CONFIDENCE: HIGH—Based on statutory definitions at 31 C.F.R. § 800.211 and transaction structure (100% stock acquisition)]**

#### 2. MSWC as TID U.S. Business: Critical Infrastructure

Under 31 C.F.R. § 800.248, a "TID U.S. business" includes any U.S. business that performs specified functions with respect to "covered investment critical infrastructure."¹⁰ Water supply systems are explicitly designated as covered investment critical infrastructure in Appendix A to 31 C.F.R. Part 800, specifically including:

> "Public water systems, as defined in section 1401(4)(A) of the Safe Drinking Water Act (42 U.S.C. 300f(4)(A)), as amended, or treatment works, as defined in section 212(2)(A) of the Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. 1292(2)), as amended, in the United States that: (i) Serve more than 100,000 persons in a Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA); or (ii) Serve any U.S. military installation."¹¹

MSWC operates 28 public water systems serving 1.35 million people across 45 municipalities in four Metropolitan Statistical Areas: Denver-Aurora-Lakewood MSA (population 2.96 million), Colorado Springs MSA (755,000), Fort Collins MSA (360,000), and Boulder MSA (330,000).¹² With 485,000 water service connections spanning 850 square miles, MSWC unambiguously satisfies the ">100,000 persons in an MSA" threshold.¹³

**CONCLUSION:** MSWC is a TID U.S. business under 31 C.F.R. § 800.248(b) by virtue of operating public water systems serving substantially more than 100,000 persons in multiple Metropolitan Statistical Areas, triggering CFIUS's expanded jurisdiction over this covered control transaction.¹⁴

**[ANALYSIS CONFIDENCE: HIGH—MSWC's 1.35 million population served across four MSAs unambiguously meets statutory threshold]**

#### 3. Foreign Person Classification: AWI Ownership Structure

Under 31 C.F.R. § 800.224, a "foreign person" includes any foreign national, foreign government, foreign entity, or any entity over which control is exercised or exercisable by a foreign national, foreign government, or foreign entity.¹⁵ The regulations employ a 25% voting interest threshold: an entity with 25% or more voting interest held by foreign persons is presumed to be under foreign control absent evidence to the contrary.¹⁶

AWI's ownership structure comprises:

**Total Foreign Ownership: 45% (CPPIB 30% + Macquarie 15%)**

Despite Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia being designated "excepted foreign states" under 31 C.F.R. § 800.219,¹⁷ AWI's 45% aggregate foreign ownership exceeds the 25% control threshold, classifying AWI as a **foreign person** for CFIUS purposes.¹⁸ The excepted foreign state status provides certain procedural exemptions (e.g., from specific mandatory filing triggers), but does not eliminate CFIUS jurisdiction over covered control transactions involving TID U.S. businesses.¹⁹

CPPIB, as a Canadian Crown corporation managing C$777 billion in pension assets under statutory authority, is functionally a foreign government entity notwithstanding Canada's excepted foreign state status.²⁰ While CPPIB operates at arm's length from the Government of Canada and emphasizes its institutional investor status, its statutory creation, governance structure, and mandate to manage government-mandated pension contributions render it a government-controlled entity for CFIUS analytical purposes.²¹

**Rule:** Under 50 U.S.C. § 4565(l), CFIUS may negotiate, enter into, or impose agreements or conditions to address national security risks identified during review.⁷⁹ The November 18, 2024 final rule enhanced CFIUS's mitigation authority, allowing CFIUS to require parties to provide substantive response to proposed mitigation terms within three business days and imposing $5 million per violation penalties for material mitigation breaches.⁸⁰ Treasury guidance identifies typical mitigation categories for critical infrastructure: (a) governance and management (Board composition, veto rights restrictions, management citizenship), (b) security measures (security officers, security committees, access restrictions), (c) information security (cyber security protocols, data protections, incident reporting), and (d) monitoring and compliance (CFIUS audit rights, annual certifications, third-party monitors).⁸¹ CFIUS 2024 Annual Report shows 25 of 209 notices (12%) resulted in mitigation measures, down from 18% in 2023.⁸²

**Explanation:** Mitigation agreements function as contracts between CFIUS (representing U.S. government national security interests) and transaction parties, imposing enforceable obligations that survive closing and continue throughout foreign ownership period.⁸³ For water utilities, CFIUS's principal concerns involve: (1) **cyber security vulnerabilities** in SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems controlling water treatment plants, distribution network valves, and pump stations—foreign control could enable cyber-attacks disrupting water supply or introducing contaminants; (2) **proximity to military installations**—foreign ownership of utilities serving military bases creates espionage opportunities through infrastructure access and consumption pattern analysis; (3) **operational resilience**—foreign ownership could enable supply disruption during international conflicts or diplomatic disputes; and (4) **technology and data protection**—operational data regarding population densities, consumption patterns, and infrastructure vulnerabilities could inform foreign intelligence services' understanding of U.S. regional vulnerabilities.⁸⁴ Board composition requirements serve as CFIUS's primary governance tool: majority U.S. directors ensure operational decisions prioritize U.S. interests; CFIUS-approved directors (typically former government officials or cleared personnel) provide CFIUS with visibility into operations; and prohibitions on foreign veto rights prevent foreign investors from blocking security enhancements or cooperation with U.S. authorities.⁸⁵ Security officers function as CFIUS's operational interface: appointed by company but approved by CFIUS, security officers monitor compliance with mitigation terms, report violations to CFIUS, and coordinate with relevant U.S. government agencies (e.g., FBI, NSA for cyber threats; DoD for military installation coordination; DHS for critical infrastructure protection).⁸⁶
