### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Commercial General Liability Insurance Structure

Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance provides coverage for third-party claims of bodily injury and property damage arising from the insured's business operations.¹ The standard Insurance Services Office (ISO) CGL policy form CG 00 01 establishes the foundational structure for liability insurance in the United States, including coverage grants, exclusions, conditions, and definitions.² MSWC's $50 million general liability policy with Zurich Insurance Company is presumed to follow this standard ISO framework, subject to verification from policy documents.³

The CGL policy's insuring agreement provides that the insurer "will pay those sums that the insured becomes legally obligated to pay as damages because of 'bodily injury' or 'property damage' to which this insurance applies."⁴ Coverage is subject to policy limits, deductibles, and exclusions that may eliminate or restrict the insurer's obligations under specific circumstances.⁵

**Coverage Triggers:**

CGL policies operate on either an "occurrence" or "claims-made" basis.⁶ Occurrence-based policies cover bodily injury or property damage that occurs during the policy period, regardless of when the claim is filed.⁷ Claims-made policies cover claims first made during the policy period, regardless of when the injury or damage occurred.⁸ For long-tail exposures such as PFAS contamination and lead service line exposure, the distinction between occurrence and claims-made triggers significantly affects coverage availability.⁹

Water utility liability policies typically follow occurrence-based triggers, providing broader temporal coverage for environmental contamination claims where injury manifestation may occur years or decades after the policy period.¹⁰ This analysis assumes MSWC's Zurich policy operates on an occurrence basis, consistent with industry standards for water utility CGL coverage.¹¹

#### 2. Pollution Exclusion Framework

The pollution exclusion represents the primary coverage defense for insurers facing environmental contamination claims.¹² The standard ISO pollution exclusion clause f(1) states:

> "This insurance does not apply to: 'Bodily injury' or 'property damage' arising out of the actual, alleged or threatened discharge, dispersal, seepage, migration, release or escape of 'pollutants'..."¹³

The term "pollutants" is expansively defined as "any solid, liquid, gaseous or thermal irritant or contaminant, including smoke, vapor, soot, fumes, acids, alkalis, chemicals and waste."¹⁴ Courts have interpreted this definition broadly to encompass virtually any substance that causes environmental harm or contamination.¹⁵

**Evolution of Pollution Exclusions:**

Pre-1986 CGL policies included pollution exclusions with a "sudden and accidental" exception, providing coverage for unexpected and unintended discharges of pollutants.¹⁶ Following the 1986 ISO form revision, the absolute pollution exclusion eliminated this exception, categorically barring coverage for all pollution-related claims regardless of intent or foreseeability.¹⁷ Post-1986 policies represent the dominant form in current use, including policies issued to water utilities in the 2020s.¹⁸

In June 2023, ISO published PFAS-specific exclusion endorsements that explicitly exclude coverage for bodily injury and property damage arising from per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances.¹⁹ Given MSWC's policy likely renewed in 2023-2024, the presence of PFAS-specific exclusions represents a material coverage concern requiring immediate verification from policy documents.²⁰

#### 3. Duty to Defend versus Duty to Indemnify

The insurer's duty to defend is broader than its duty to indemnify and arises independently from coverage obligations.²¹ Under *Gray v. Zurich Insurance Co.*, 65 Cal. 2d 263, 275 (1966), an insurer "must defend a suit which potentially seeks damages within the coverage of the policy."²² The California Supreme Court established that an insurer is relieved of its duty to defend only if the complaint "can by no conceivable theory raise a single issue which would bring it within the policy coverage."²³

Colorado applies the "eight corners rule" (also called the four corners rule), comparing only the allegations in the plaintiff's complaint with the language in the insurance policy without resort to extrinsic evidence.²⁴ The Colorado Supreme Court has held that extrinsic evidence may be used only to interpret ambiguous policy language, not to determine whether coverage exists under unambiguous policy terms.²⁵

**Duty to Indemnify Standard:**

The duty to indemnify is narrower and arises only after a determination that the insured's actual liability falls within policy coverage.²⁶ In *Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Admiral Insurance Co.*, 10 Cal. 4th 645 (1995), the California Supreme Court held that while an insurer must defend if any potential for coverage exists, the insurer must indemnify only if a covered loss is proven.²⁷ This temporal distinction creates significant strategic implications for coverage disputes: insurers may defend under a reservation of rights while continuing to investigate coverage issues and preserving exclusion defenses.²⁸

#### 4. Reservation of Rights and Independent Counsel

When an insurer has coverage concerns but chooses to provide a defense, it typically issues a "reservation of rights" letter notifying the insured that the insurer reserves all policy defenses while providing defense counsel.²⁹ Colorado law requires an insurer to raise or reserve all defenses within a reasonable time after learning of such defenses, or those defenses may be waived.³⁰

**Cumis Counsel Rights:**

When an insurer defends under a reservation of rights that creates a conflict of interest between insurer and insured, the insured may be entitled to independent counsel (Cumis counsel) paid by the insurer.³¹ Colorado is classified as a "one client" state, meaning defense counsel represents only the insured, not the insurer.³² However, no Colorado court has explicitly held that a reservation of rights automatically triggers entitlement to independent counsel.³³ Most states require a case-by-case showing of an actual, present conflict of interest before mandating independent counsel.³⁴

#### 5. Colorado Bad Faith Insurance Standards

Colorado Revised Statutes § 10-3-1115(1)(a) prohibits insurance companies from "unreasonably deny[ing] or delay[ing] payment of a claim of benefits" to first-party claimants.³⁵ An insurer's denial is unreasonable "if the insurer delayed or denied authorizing payment of a covered benefit without a reasonable basis for that action."³⁶

**"Fairly Debatable" Doctrine:**

Under Colorado law, it is reasonable for an insurer to challenge claims that are "fairly debatable."³⁷ If "reasonable minds" could disagree as to coverage-determining facts or law, such circumstance weighs against a finding that the insurer acted unreasonably.³⁸ However, the "fairly debatable" standard is not outcome-determinative and does not necessarily defeat a bad faith claim as a matter of law.³⁹

**Remedies under CRS § 10-3-1116:**

Colorado's bad faith statute allows first-party claimants to recover reasonable attorneys' fees, court costs, and "two times the covered benefit" if the insurer delayed or denied payment without a reasonable basis.⁴⁰ The quantum of damages depends on the existence and amount of "covered benefits," creating uncertainty when exclusions may bar coverage entirely.⁴¹

**Explanation:** In *American Bar Association, Applying the Known Loss Doctrine to Liability Insurance* (Spring 2017), courts have applied the known loss doctrine to bar coverage where the insured knew before policy inception that: (1) an event had occurred (e.g., environmental contamination detected), (2) the event was likely to give rise to liability claims, and (3) the magnitude of potential liability was substantial.¹⁰² The doctrine does not require certainty of liability, only knowledge that a claim is substantially probable.¹⁰³

Some courts apply an "insurer knowledge exception," holding that the insurer may not invoke the known loss doctrine if the insurer also knew of the loss prior to policy inception.¹⁰⁴ This exception prevents insurers from accepting premiums while intending to deny coverage based on pre-existing conditions known to both parties.¹⁰⁵

#### B.6 Insurance Subrogation Against Manufacturer Settlements

**Conclusion:** If Zurich provides any coverage for PFAS defense or indemnity, Zurich may assert subrogation rights against MSWC's manufacturer settlement recoveries from 3M, DuPont, Chemours, and Corteva. However, if the pollution exclusion bars coverage entirely, Zurich has no subrogation rights and MSWC retains full benefit of manufacturer recoveries. **Exposure:** $0-$80M reduction in MSWC's net manufacturer recovery if Zurich subrogate. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Subrogation rights exist only if insurer pays covered benefits; pollution exclusion likely bars coverage, eliminating subrogation].

**Rule:** Insurance subrogation is an equitable doctrine permitting an insurer that has paid a claim to step into the insured's shoes and pursue recovery from third parties legally responsible for the loss.¹³⁴ Subrogation rights arise only to the extent the insurer has paid covered benefits on behalf of the insured.¹³⁵ If policy exclusions bar coverage and the insurer pays nothing, no subrogation rights exist.¹³⁶

**Explanation:** In manufacturer product liability settlements such as the 3M and DuPont PFAS settlements, water utilities recovering settlement funds may face subrogation claims from their insurers if those insurers paid PFAS-related defense or indemnity costs.¹³⁷ The insurer's subrogation rights are limited to the amount the insurer paid; the insured retains recovery amounts exceeding the insurer's payments.¹³⁸

Subrogation becomes materially important where manufacturer settlements substantially exceed insurance payments.¹³⁹ For example, if an insurer paid $10M in defense and indemnity but the manufacturer settlement provides $80M, the insurer may claim $10M subrogation while the insured retains $70M.¹⁴⁰

**Application:** Here, MSWC expects to recover $50M-$115M (median $80M) from manufacturer settlements (3M $12.5B, DuPont $1.18B, Chemours, Corteva).¹⁴¹ If Zurich's pollution exclusion bars coverage for both defense and indemnity, Zurich will have paid $0 for PFAS-related claims, eliminating any subrogation rights.¹⁴² In this scenario (70-85% probability), MSWC retains 100% of manufacturer recoveries (subject to CPUC customer credit requirements).¹⁴³

If Zurich defends under a reservation of rights (20-30% probability), paying $5M-$15M in defense costs, Zurich may claim subrogation of $5M-$15M from MSWC's manufacturer recoveries.¹⁴⁴ If Zurich defends and indemnifies (5-10% probability), paying up to $50M policy limits, Zurich may claim subrogation of $50M from manufacturer recoveries.¹⁴⁵

However, MSWC may face a larger subrogation issue with manufacturer litigation settlements directly: if MSWC settles the customer class action for $100M and separately recovers $80M from manufacturers, the customer class may argue that MSWC should credit the $80M manufacturer recovery against the $100M settlement, reducing MSWC's net payment obligation to $20M.¹⁴⁶ This settlement credit issue is distinct from insurance subrogation but materially affects MSWC's net PFAS exposure.¹⁴⁷

**Liability Valuation:**
- **Classification:** Contingent Reduction in Recovery (insurance subrogation against manufacturer settlement)
- **Methodology:** Expected Value (probability × magnitude)
- **Calculation:**
  - Manufacturer recovery expected: $80M (median)
  - If Zurich pays $0 (70-85% probability): Zero subrogation, MSWC retains $80M
  - If Zurich pays defense only $10M (20-30% probability): $10M subrogation, MSWC retains $70M
  - If Zurich pays $50M limits (5-10% probability): $50M subrogation, MSWC retains $30M
  - **Expected subrogation: (75% × $0) + (25% × $10M) + (7.5% × $50M) = $6.25M**
  - **Expected net manufacturer recovery to MSWC: $80M - $6.25M = $73.75M**
- **Result:** $73.75M expected net recovery, subject to 75-100% CPUC customer credit
- **Note:** Insurance subrogation risk is low given high probability (70-85%) pollution exclusion bars coverage entirely

**Probability Assessment:**
70-85% probability Zurich has no subrogation rights (pollution exclusion bars coverage) [METHODOLOGY: Subrogation rights exist only if insurer pays covered benefits; pollution exclusion analysis (Section B.1) shows 70-85% probability zero coverage; no payment = no subrogation rights].

**Counter-Analysis:** If Zurich successfully argues a duty to defend exists despite the pollution exclusion, Zurich will claim subrogation rights equal to defense costs paid.¹⁴⁸ Defense costs for 18-24 months of PFAS class action litigation could reach $5M-$15M, materially reducing MSWC's net manufacturer recovery.¹⁴⁹ Additionally, Zurich may negotiate a "cut-through" agreement where Zurich agrees to provide defense/indemnity in exchange for a percentage of manufacturer recoveries, effectively converting potential coverage into a settlement credit.¹⁵⁰ There is 15-25% probability Zurich and MSWC negotiate a coverage settlement involving shared manufacturer recovery proceeds.¹⁵¹
