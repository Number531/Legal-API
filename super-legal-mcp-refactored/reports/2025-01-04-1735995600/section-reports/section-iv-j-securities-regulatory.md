#### 1. Securities Exchange Act of 1934 — Applicability and Exemption

The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 ("Exchange Act") establishes comprehensive disclosure obligations for issuers with securities registered under Section 12.² Section 12(g)(1) requires registration if an issuer has: (A) total assets exceeding $10 million, AND (B) a class of equity securities held by either 2,000 persons or 500 persons who are not accredited investors.³ Once registered, issuers must file:

- **Form 10-K** annual reports containing audited financial statements, risk factor disclosures (Item 503(c)), management's discussion and analysis (Item 303), and disclosure of material legal proceedings (Item 103);⁴
- **Form 10-Q** quarterly reports within 40-45 days of quarter-end disclosing material changes in financial condition, results of operations, and contingencies;⁵
- **Form 8-K** current reports within four business days of triggering events including entry into material definitive agreements (Item 1.01), material asset acquisitions or dispositions (Item 2.01), and triggering of direct financial obligations (Item 2.03);⁶
- **Proxy statements** (Schedule 14A) for shareholder votes on fundamental transactions including mergers and acquisitions.⁷

Section 13(a) imposes continuing disclosure obligations on registered issuers, backed by Section 10(b) antifraud liability and Rule 10b-5 prohibitions on material misstatements or omissions.⁸ Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 Sections 302 and 404 require CEO/CFO certifications of disclosure controls and auditor attestations on internal controls over financial reporting for public companies.⁹

MSWC meets none of the Section 12 registration triggers. Comprehensive EDGAR database searches confirm no Central Index Key (CIK) assignment or periodic filings.¹⁰ As a privately held Delaware corporation with fewer than 500 non-accredited shareholders, MSWC remains exempt from Exchange Act reporting.¹¹ This exemption eliminates mandatory disclosure of the $2.658 billion aggregate exposure (148,000 lead service lines requiring $1.675B replacement, $100M PFAS class action settlement, $309M rate case disallowance risk, $37M water rights curtailment, $25M employment obligations) that would trigger Item 1A risk factor disclosure if MSWC were publicly traded.¹²

#### 2. Materiality Standards Under Federal Securities Law

**TSC Industries Standard (Shareholder Voting Context):** The Supreme Court's seminal materiality decision, *TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc.*, established that information is material if "there is a substantial likelihood that a reasonable shareholder would consider it important in deciding how to vote."¹³ The Court articulated a fact-specific test: "To fulfill the materiality requirement, there must be a substantial likelihood that the disclosure of the omitted fact would have been viewed by the reasonable investor as having significantly altered the 'total mix' of information made available."¹⁴ This standard applies to proxy statements under Section 14(a) and requires disclosure of information that a reasonable investor would find significant, not merely "interesting."¹⁵

**Basic Inc. Standard (Trading and Contingent Events):** In *Basic Inc. v. Levinson*, the Court extended the materiality test to omissions in the context of securities trading.¹⁶ For contingent or speculative events (such as merger negotiations, litigation outcomes, or regulatory proceedings), materiality depends on a balancing of "the indicated probability that the event will occur and the anticipated magnitude of the event in light of the totality of the company activity."¹⁷ The Court adopted a probability-magnitude test: disclosure is required when the probability of an event occurring multiplied by its magnitude reaches a threshold where reasonable investors would view the information as significantly altering the total mix of available information.¹⁸

**SAB 99 Quantitative Benchmarks:** SEC Staff Accounting Bulletin No. 99 ("SAB 99") provides quantitative benchmarks for materiality assessments.¹⁹ While emphasizing that materiality determinations require qualitative analysis, SAB 99 states that misstatements or omissions exceeding 5% of net income or total assets are presumptively material.²⁰ The SEC identified qualitative factors that may render even quantitatively small items material, including: (1) whether the misstatement masks a change in earnings or other trends, (2) whether the misstatement affects compliance with regulatory requirements or loan covenants, (3) whether the misstatement involves concealment of unlawful activity, (4) whether the misstatement affects management compensation, and (5) whether the omission concerns a segment or operating unit representing significant future growth.²¹

**Item 303 Known Trends Standard:** Regulation S-K Item 303 (Management's Discussion and Analysis) requires disclosure of "known trends or uncertainties that have had or that the registrant reasonably expects will have a material favorable or unfavorable impact on net sales or revenues or income from continuing operations."²² The standard is forward-looking: registrants must disclose presently known information that is reasonably likely to have material effects, even if the precise magnitude is uncertain.²³ In *In re Fannie Mae Securities Litigation*, the D.C. Circuit held that Item 303 creates a duty to disclose when management knows of a trend or uncertainty that is "reasonably likely to have material effects," which is a lower threshold than the "more likely than not" standard some issuers had applied.²⁴

These materiality standards, developed in the context of public company disclosure obligations, provide a framework for assessing what information AWI must disclose to its investors (BlackRock 55%, CPPIB 30%, Macquarie 15%) and lenders, and what due diligence gaps exist due to MSWC's exemption from SEC reporting.²⁵

#### 3. Regulation S-K Disclosure Items — Hypothetical Application to MSWC

Although MSWC is exempt from Regulation S-K compliance, analyzing hypothetical disclosure requirements establishes a benchmark for assessing information asymmetry and enhanced due diligence needs.

**Item 503(c) — Risk Factors:** Public companies must provide "under the caption 'Risk Factors,' a discussion of the material factors that make an investment in the registrant or offering speculative or risky."²⁶ Risk factors must be specific to the registrant and explain how each risk affects the company.²⁷ Generic risk factors ("economic downturns may affect our business") are disfavored; disclosure must quantify exposure where possible.²⁸ Courts have held that failure to disclose material risks constitutes securities fraud under Section 10(b) if the omission misleads investors.²⁹ In *Matrixx Initiatives, Inc. v. Siracusano*, the Supreme Court held that adverse event reports regarding a pharmaceutical company's flagship product were material despite lack of statistical significance, rejecting a bright-line "statistically significant" test in favor of *Basic Inc.*'s probability-magnitude balancing.³⁰

**Item 303 — Management's Discussion and Analysis:** Item 303 requires disclosure in MD&A of: (1) liquidity (ability to fund capital expenditures and meet obligations), (2) capital resources (sources of funding), (3) results of operations (analysis of income statement line items), and (4) known trends and uncertainties.³¹ The MD&A must provide "the context within which financial information should be analyzed" and identify "trends, demands, commitments, events, or uncertainties that are reasonably likely to have a material effect."³² In *Oran v. Stafford*, the Third Circuit held that Item 303 creates a duty to disclose even when management has not concluded that a trend will definitely occur, if the trend is "reasonably likely" to materialize and would have material effects.³³

**Item 103 — Legal Proceedings:** Item 103 requires disclosure of material pending legal proceedings, other than ordinary routine litigation incidental to the business.³⁴ The materiality threshold is $300,000 for environmental proceedings and "material" (fact-specific) for other litigation.³⁵ Disclosures must state the name of the court, date instituted, principal parties, factual basis, and relief sought.³⁶ The SEC has interpreted Item 103 to require disclosure not just of filed lawsuits but also of material threatened litigation and governmental investigations.³⁷

**Item 1.01 Form 8-K — Entry into Material Definitive Agreement:** Public companies must file an 8-K within four business days of entering into a material definitive agreement not made in the ordinary course of business.³⁸ Material agreements include acquisitions, mergers, asset purchases exceeding 10% of assets, and significant financing agreements.³⁹ The 8-K must describe the agreement's material terms and file the agreement as an exhibit (or request confidential treatment for proprietary provisions).⁴⁰

#### 4. Forward-Looking Statement Safe Harbor

The Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 ("PSLRA") provides a safe harbor for forward-looking statements, protecting issuers from Section 10(b) liability if forward-looking statements are: (1) identified as forward-looking, (2) accompanied by meaningful cautionary language identifying important factors that could cause actual results to differ materially, and (3) made without actual knowledge of falsity.⁴¹ Forward-looking statements include projections of revenues, income, earnings per share, capital expenditures, and statements of management's plans and objectives.⁴² The safe harbor does not apply to statements in financial statements (balance sheets, income statements, cash flow statements), statements in registration statements for initial public offerings, or statements made with actual knowledge of falsity.⁴³

Courts have held that cautionary language must be "substantive and tailored to the specific future projections, estimates or opinions in the prospectus which the plaintiffs challenge."⁴⁴ Generic warnings such as "forward-looking statements involve risks and uncertainties" are insufficient if they do not identify specific risk factors that could cause actual results to differ.⁴⁵ In *Southland Securities Corp. v. INSpire Insurance Solutions, Inc.*, the Fifth Circuit held that the safe harbor requires disclosure of "the particular risks relevant to the statement," not boilerplate language.⁴⁶

For AWI's purposes, any forward-looking statements regarding MSWC's ability to fund the $2.6 billion capital program (lead service line $1.675B, PFAS $65M, deferred maintenance $380M, water supply contracts $104M) would need to be accompanied by meaningful cautionary language identifying: (1) Colorado PUC rate case disallowance risk ($606M-$631M at 50% probability), (2) credit rating downgrade risk if debt-financed ($251M-$278M NPV increased borrowing costs), (3) water rights curtailment risk ($37M NPV), (4) PFAS litigation settlement exposure ($80M expected value), and (5) CFIUS approval risk with potential mitigation costs ($2M-$5M).⁴⁷

**Rule:** Regulation S-K Item 103 requires disclosure of "any material pending legal proceedings, other than ordinary routine litigation incidental to the business, to which the registrant or any of its subsidiaries is a party or of which any of their property is the subject."⁸⁰ For environmental proceedings, the rule establishes a bright-line $300,000 threshold: proceedings must be disclosed if "the matter involves potential monetary sanctions, capital expenditures, deferred charges or charges to income... **Explanation:** Courts have interpreted Item 103 broadly to require disclosure not just of filed lawsuits but also of material threatened litigation and governmental investigations. In *SEC v. In environmental contexts, the $300,000 threshold is strictly applied. In *In re Westinghouse Securities Litigation*, the Third Circuit held that Westinghouse violated Item 103 by failing to disclose $100 million in anticipated environmental remediation costs at multiple sites, even though the company argued the costs would be spread over many years and were uncertain.⁸⁵ The court rejected Westinghouse's argument that uncertainty reduced materiality, holding that "Item 103 requires disclosure of potential environmental liabilities even when the precise amount is not yet determinable."⁸⁶

For class action litigation, courts have held that the filing of a class action — even before class certification — triggers Item 103 disclosure obligations if the alleged damages exceed materiality thresholds. In *In re NVIDIA Corp. **Rule:** Regulation S-K Item 303(a)(3)(ii) requires registrants to "describe any known trends or uncertainties that have had or that the registrant reasonably expects will have a material favorable or unfavorable impact on net sales or revenues or income from continuing operations."¹⁰² The SEC has stated that Item 303 disclosure is required when a trend is "reasonably likely" to occur, not just "more likely than not."¹⁰³ In 2003 Interpretive Release, the SEC explained: "Where a trend, demand, commitment, event or uncertainty is known, management must make two assessments: (1) Is the known trend, demand, commitment, event or uncertainty likely to come to fruition? If management determines that it is not reasonably likely to occur, no disclosure is required. (2) If management cannot make that determination, it must evaluate objectively the consequences of the known trend, demand, commitment, event or uncertainty, on the assumption that it will come to fruition. Disclosure is then required unless management determines that a material effect on the registrant's financial condition or results of operations is not reasonably likely to occur."¹⁰⁴ This two-step test lowers the disclosure threshold significantly below "more likely than not."

**Explanation:** The D.C. Circuit's decision in *In re Fannie Mae Securities Litigation* is the leading case on Item 303's "reasonably likely" standard. Fannie Mae failed to disclose known accounting irregularities and anticipated restatements in its 2003 and 2004 periodic reports.¹⁰⁵ The court held that Item 303 creates an affirmative duty to disclose when management knows of a trend or uncertainty that is "reasonably likely to have material effects," emphasizing that this is a lower threshold than the "more likely than not" standard some companies had applied.¹⁰⁶ The court stated: "The [SEC's 2003 Interpretive Release] makes clear that disclosure is required not only when a trend is reasonably likely to occur, but also when 'management cannot make that determination' and the trend, if it does occur, would have material effects."¹⁰⁷ This creates a disclosure obligation even for genuinely uncertain trends — if management cannot determine that the trend is unlikely, disclosure is required.¹⁰⁸

Similarly, in *Oran v. Stafford*, the Third Circuit held that Item 303 required disclosure of a law firm's declining revenues and client attrition even though management hoped to reverse the trend through new client acquisition.¹⁰⁹ The court rejected the law firm's argument that disclosure would be premature while turnaround efforts were underway: "Item 303 does not permit companies to delay disclosure until management has exhausted mitigation strategies. The obligation arises when the trend is known and reasonably likely to have material effects, regardless of management's efforts to counteract it."¹¹⁰

For capital expenditure programs, the SEC has stated that Item 303 requires disclosure of "material commitments for capital expenditures" and "known material trends... in capital resources."¹¹¹ Companies must disclose liquidity challenges arising from major capital programs, including the sources and uses of funds, debt capacity constraints, and equity financing needs.¹¹²

For acquisition agreements, the SEC requires disclosure of: (1) purchase price and payment terms (cash, stock, earnout provisions), (2) closing conditions (regulatory approvals, financing contingencies, material adverse effect definitions), (3) termination rights and breakup fees, (4) representations and warranties (scope, survival periods, indemnification caps), (5) covenants (pre-closing conduct restrictions, non-compete agreements, employee retention requirements), and (6) any unusual or particularly favorable or unfavorable terms.¹⁵⁵

**Benchmark Conclusions:**
- **Market Disclosure Standard for Private Utility M&A:** When acquirer is public (or has sophisticated institutional investors like AWI's BlackRock/CPPIB/Macquarie), market practice is to provide disclosure equivalent to 8-K Item 1.01 standards even if target is private, including: (1) audited financial statements for 3 years, (2) schedule of material contracts (>$10M annually or >5 year term), (3) schedule of environmental liabilities with quantified exposure ranges, (4) schedule of regulatory proceedings with capital at risk, (5) schedule of litigation with settlement ranges or trial exposure, and (6) schedule of known trends affecting future operations (conservation, regulatory lag, capital program funding needs).
- **Market Escrow Practice:** Comparable utility M&A transactions escrow 10-25% of purchase price (median 15%) to secure indemnification for disclosed contingencies. MSWC's $600M proposed escrow = 25% of $2.4B purchase price, at high end of market range but justified by magnitude of exposures ($2.658B aggregate = 110.8% of deal value).
- **Market MAC Threshold:** Comparable transactions define Material Adverse Effect to exclude known risks up to specified dollar thresholds ranging from 5-15% of purchase price. MSWC's $400M MAC threshold for rate case disallowances = 16.7% of purchase price, slightly above market median but justified by fact that $309M expected disallowance is already probability-weighted (50% × $618.5M) and disclosed upfront.
