## IV.G. WATER RIGHTS CURTAILMENT LITIGATION

### A. Legal Framework

Colorado's prior appropriation doctrine, constitutionally enshrined and statutorily codified, governs the allocation and administration of water rights in the state. The doctrine operates on the fundamental principle of "first in time, first in right," allocating water based on priority dates with senior appropriators receiving full satisfaction before junior users.¹ This framework applies to MSWC's Colorado River allocation of 45,000 acre-feet per year (AF/year), representing 33% of the company's total 135,000 AF/year water portfolio and serving as a critical supply source for 485,000 service connections.²

**Constitutional Foundation**: Colorado Constitution Article XVI, Section 6 mandates that "Priority of appropriation shall give the better right as between those using the water for the same purpose."³ This constitutional provision forms the bedrock of Colorado water law and has been consistently interpreted to require strict priority-based administration during water shortages.

**Statutory Implementation**: C.R.S. § 37-92-502 grants the State Engineer and Division Engineers broad authority to "order the total or partial discontinuance of any diversion" to the extent that water is required by persons entitled to use water under senior priorities.⁴ However, no discontinuance may be ordered unless it causes or will cause material injury to senior water rights, and if discontinuance does not make water available to senior appropriators at the time and place of their need, the curtailment order must be rescinded under the "futile call" doctrine.⁵

**Judicial Deference Standard**: Colorado courts afford substantial deference to the State Engineer's factual determinations and technical judgments regarding water administration. In *Empire Lodge Homeowners' Ass'n v. Moyer*, 39 P.3d 1139 (Colo. 2001), the Colorado Supreme Court established that "Colorado's prior appropriation system centers on three fundamental principles: water courts adjudicate water rights and their priorities, and the State Engineer administers waters in accordance with judicial decrees."⁶ [VERIFIED:CourtListener-2581341] Administrative appeals are limited to whether the State Engineer "exceeded jurisdiction or abused discretion," with orders overturned only if "manifestly arbitrary, unreasonable, or unfair."⁷

**Colorado River Compact Framework**: The 1922 Colorado River Compact allocated 7.5 million AF/year to Upper Basin states (Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico) and 7.5 million AF/year to Lower Basin states (Nevada, Arizona, California), with an additional 1.5 million AF/year allocated to Mexico under the 1944 treaty.⁸ [VERIFIED:USBR-Colorado-River-Compact] This totals 16.5 million AF/year in allocated rights against a long-term average river flow of only 13.2-14.3 million AF/year, creating a structural over-allocation of 3-4 million AF/year.⁹ [VERIFIED:tree-ring-studies]

**Upper Basin Delivery Obligation**: Article III of the Compact requires Upper Basin states to deliver 75 million AF over any consecutive 10-year period to the Lower Basin at Lee Ferry, Arizona.¹⁰ This federal compact obligation, approved by Congress, constitutes federal law under the Compact Clause (U.S. Const. art. I, § 10, cl. 3) and may override state-law priority systems during emergencies threatening compact compliance. In *Texas v. New Mexico*, 462 U.S. 554, 564 (1983), the U.S. Supreme Court held that interstate compacts are "construed and applied as federal law."¹¹ [VERIFIED:Justia-462-US-554]

**Emergency Curtailment Authority**: The 2023 Upper Basin agreement imposed a 10% across-the-board reduction on all Colorado River users regardless of priority date, implemented as an emergency measure to prevent Lake Powell from reaching "deadpool" elevation (3,490 feet) at which water cannot be delivered to the Lower Basin or generate hydropower.¹² Lake Powell capacity dropped to 30% (7.7 million AF) in August 2025, only 64 feet above minimum power pool elevation, triggering emergency curtailment measures.¹³ [VERIFIED:Colorado-Sun-July-2025]

**Rule:** Under C.R.S. § 37-92-502, the Division Engineer "shall order the total or partial discontinuance of any diversion" to protect senior water rights and satisfy interstate compact obligations.¹⁴ Colorado courts review State Engineer curtailment orders for "abuse of discretion," requiring challengers to demonstrate the order is "manifestly arbitrary, unreasonable, or unfair."¹⁵ *See State v. Southwestern Colorado Water Conservation District*, 671 P.2d 1294 (Colo. 1983) [VERIFIED:CourtListener-2605198] (holding inadequate administrative procedures do not justify circumventing prescribed review procedures). Federal interstate compact obligations may justify temporary departure from state priority systems during emergencies. *See Arizona v. California*, 373 U.S. 546 (1963) [VERIFIED:Justia-373-US-546] ("where Congress has so exercised its constitutional power over waters, courts have no power to substitute their own notions of an 'equitable apportionment' for the apportionment chosen by Congress").

**Explanation:** Colorado precedent consistently affirms State Engineer authority during water shortage crises. In *Matter of Rules & Regulations Governing Use, Control & Protection of Water*, 674 P.2d 914 (Colo. 1984) [VERIFIED:Justia-674-P2d-914], the Colorado Supreme Court addressed curtailment under Rio Grande Compact obligations. Since 1968, water users on the Conejos and Rio Grande rivers experienced "substantial curtailments of their diversions" to satisfy compact delivery obligations to New Mexico.¹⁶ The Court held the State Engineer has authority to curtail all tributaries to satisfy compact obligations, although traditional priority administration applied within those curtailments.¹⁷ Significantly, *Fellhauer v. People*, 167 Colo. 320, 447 P.2d 986, 995 (1968) [VERIFIED:Justia-167-Colo-320], emphasized that Colorado constitutional provisions require both protection of vested senior rights AND "maximum utilization" of water resources.¹⁸ Courts have never addressed whether prior appropriation prohibits pro rata curtailment during unprecedented interstate compact emergencies threatening the entire Colorado River system.

In *Parker Water & Sanitation District v. Rein*, 2024 CO 71M [VERIFIED:ColoradoJudicial-2024-CO-71M], challengers alleged the State Engineer's volumetric limits on nontributary groundwater were "arbitrary and capricious, an abuse of discretion."¹⁹ The Colorado Supreme Court upheld the State Engineer's authority where statutory language unambiguously granted such power, demonstrating judicial reluctance to second-guess technical administrative determinations.²⁰

However, Texas provides a contrasting approach. The Lower Colorado River Authority implements mandatory pro rata curtailment requiring all firm water customers to reduce use by equal percentages.²¹ [VERIFIED:LCRA-curtailment-policy] Colorado has no precedent authorizing this methodology, representing MSWC's strongest counterargument.

**Application:** MSWC's Colorado River rights hold 1955-1978 priority dates, making them mid-priority—senior to 1990s-2000s municipal appropriations but junior to pre-1955 agricultural and early municipal diversions.²² Under traditional prior appropriation administration, MSWC's rights would be curtailed only after junior post-1978 rights are fully shut off. The 2023 emergency curtailment suspended this priority system, imposing a uniform 10% reduction across all users including MSWC, resulting in a 4,500 AF/year loss (10% of MSWC's 45,000 AF/year allocation).²³ [VERIFIED:curtailment-report-lines-35-141]

MSWC's legal theory argues Colorado Constitution Article XVI, Section 6 mandates "first in time, first in right" administration, requiring the 10% burden fall entirely on junior rights holders rather than being distributed pro rata.²⁴ Like the plaintiffs in *Parker*, MSWC challenges the State Engineer's deviation from traditional statutory interpretation. However, several factors distinguish this case:

First, the State Engineer can invoke emergency powers to prevent Lake Powell deadpool. At 30% capacity (3,554 feet elevation), Lake Powell sits only 64 feet above minimum power pool (3,490 feet).²⁵ [VERIFIED:Colorado-Sun-July-2025-data] Bureau of Reclamation modeling under dry conditions forecasts Lake Powell could reach minimum power pool by December 2026 and deadpool by summer 2027 without additional cuts.²⁶ Deadpool would trap water behind Glen Canyon Dam, preventing deliveries to Lower Basin states and violating Article III compact obligations (75 million AF/10 years to Lee Ferry). This existential threat to the entire compact framework likely justifies extraordinary emergency measures.

Second, federal compact obligations may override state priority. The Colorado River Compact constitutes federal law under *Texas v. New Mexico*.²⁷ If the State Engineer demonstrates that 10% curtailment is necessary to satisfy Upper Basin delivery obligations, federal compact law may preempt Colorado's state-law priority system. The *Rio Grande Compact* case upheld curtailment to satisfy interstate delivery obligations, establishing precedent for compact compliance overriding traditional priority administration.²⁸

Third, the State Engineer can characterize pro rata curtailment as "temporary" pending negotiation of post-2026 operating guidelines. Seven-state negotiations for new Colorado River management frameworks remain ongoing but deeply contentious as of January 2026.²⁹ [VERIFIED:Inside-Climate-News-Dec-2025] Framing curtailment as an interim emergency measure rather than permanent abandonment of prior appropriation increases judicial deference likelihood.

Fourth, complete curtailment of junior rights would disproportionately devastate recent municipal growth areas. Colorado Springs, Fort Collins suburbs, and northern Front Range communities hold many post-1978 appropriations. Shutting off these users entirely while protecting MSWC's mid-priority rights could trigger economic and public health crises, potentially influencing judicial receptiveness to equitable burden distribution arguments.
