## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Purchased Water Agreements (Agricultural Leases)

#### 1. Legal Framework for Agricultural Water Leases in Colorado

**a. Colorado Water Rights as Property**

Colorado water rights are usufructuary property rights governed by prior appropriation doctrine.‚Å¥ Water rights holders may lease, loan, or exchange legally stored water without losing the underlying water right or permanently selling it.‚Åµ The Colorado General Assembly affirmed that water law "does not impair, limit, or otherwise affect the rights of persons or entities to enter into agreements, contracts, or memoranda of understanding with other persons or entities relating to the appropriation, movement, or use of water."‚Å∂

**b. Temporary Transfers and Change of Use**

Temporary water transfers from agricultural to municipal use require water court approval to ensure no injury to other appropriators.‚Å∑ However, **short-term leases** (typically 1-3 years) for irrigation season water are commonly structured as **annual contracts** that do not change the decreed use, avoiding water court proceedings. These "dry-year option" agreements allow municipalities to purchase agricultural water during drought years when farmers voluntarily fallow land.‚Å∏

**c. Assignment Provisions Under UCC Article 2**

Colorado water supply contracts for the sale of water may be governed by the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) Article 2 if water is sold as "goods."‚Åπ Under C.R.S. ¬ß 4-2-210:

- A prohibition on assignment of "the contract" bars only **delegation of performance**, not assignment of rights to receive payment.¬π‚Å∞
- An assignment of "the contract" includes both assignment of rights and delegation of duties unless circumstances indicate otherwise.¬π¬π
- **Anti-assignment clauses** are common in water supply contracts and are generally enforceable to prevent assignment without counterparty consent.¬π¬≤

### B. Wholesale Water Contracts

#### 1. Legal Framework for Wholesale Water Supply Agreements

**a. Authority for Wholesale Water Contracts**

Colorado water districts and municipalities are authorized to enter into contracts for wholesale water supply under C.R.S. ¬ß 37-45-131 ("Sale of water by contract").¬π‚Å∂ Water conservancy districts' contracts may be for municipal and industrial use by the recipient.¬π‚Å∑ These wholesale arrangements allow smaller municipalities and special districts to purchase treated water from larger utilities (like MSWC) rather than construct duplicate treatment infrastructure.

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Purchased Water Agreements (Agricultural Leases)**: MSWC's agricultural water lease portfolio ($3M-$12M annually) faces **HIGH RISK** of price volatility and non-renewal post-acquisition. Short-term (1-year) leases provide no supply security. AWI should proactively negotiate multi-year leases pre-closing and diversify supply via reuse water expansion.

2. **Wholesale Water Contracts**: MSWC's wholesale customer contracts ($7M annually, 2,800 connections) present **MEDIUM RISK**. Stock purchase structure avoids formal assignment, but change-of-control notification likely required. Proactive customer outreach and CPUC approval process will address concerns.

3. **USBR Grand Valley Diversion Project**: MSWC's 45,000 AF/year Colorado River allocation (33% of total supply) via USBR participation presents **HIGH RISK** due to federal assignment approval requirements (43 CFR ¬ß 429.2(b)). USBR denial would be **deal-blocking**. AWI must submit assignment request 6 months pre-closing and coordinate with GVWUA.

4. **South Metro Reuse Water Agreement**: MSWC's 5,000 AF/year reuse water purchase ($4M-$7.5M annually) presents **MEDIUM RISK**. Contract likely requires change-of-control notification/consent. **Critical strategic opportunity**: Negotiate expansion rights for additional 5,000-10,000 AF/year to offset Colorado River curtailment with drought-proof supply.

5. **Total Annual Exposure**: Commercial contract costs range from **$15.24M (best case)** to **$31.29M (worst case)**, with base case **$22.44M annually**. Most costs are passed through to customers via Purchased Water Adjustment clause, mitigating financial risk to MSWC but increasing regulatory risk (CPUC rate case scrutiny).

6. **20-Year NPV**: Supplemental water purchases (agricultural leases + reuse expansion) total **$115M-$230M NPV** over 20 years if Colorado River curtailment continues. This cost is material relative to $2.4B purchase price (5-10% of transaction value).
