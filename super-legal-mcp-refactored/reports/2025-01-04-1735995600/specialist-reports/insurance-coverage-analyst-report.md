## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

Mountain States Water Company (MSWC) faces a PFAS class action with estimated settlement exposure of **$50M-$150M**, but its $50 million Commercial General Liability (CGL) policy with Zurich Insurance Company will likely provide **zero coverage** due to the standard pollution exclusion. This insurance coverage analysis concludes that MSWC's net uninsured exposure is **$30M-$100M** (midpoint: **$65M**) after accounting for manufacturer litigation recovery of $20M-$50M from the 3M and DuPont PFAS settlements.

### Key Findings

**1. Pollution Exclusion Likely Bars Coverage (70-85% Probability)**

The standard CGL pollution exclusion will likely bar both the duty to defend and duty to indemnify for the PFAS class action. In *National Foam, Inc. v. Zurich American Insurance Co.*, 2025 WL 699361 (N.D. Cal. Feb. 26, 2025), a federal court held that PFAS contamination of water supplies through "indirect exposure" (drinking contaminated water) constitutes "general environmental pollution" within the scope of pollution exclusions. The court distinguished this from "direct exposure" cases where plaintiffs were exposed to PFAS through ordinary product use, finding no duty to defend indirect exposure claims.

MSWC's case falls squarely within the "indirect exposure" category: 45,000 customers allege bodily injury and property damage from drinking PFAS-contaminated water. Although MSWC argues it is a "passive receiver" who distributes pre-contaminated water rather than an active "discharger" of pollutants, courts have applied pollution exclusions broadly to water contamination scenarios. The *National Foam* precedent suggests Zurich will successfully deny coverage based on the pollution exclusion.

**2. MSWC's "Passive Receiver" Argument Has Limited Success Probability (15-30%)**

MSWC may argue that as a water distributor—not a manufacturer or discharger—it did not "release" or "discharge" pollutants within the meaning of the pollution exclusion. This argument finds support in:

- **Legislative Recognition**: Congress has considered multiple bills (including H.R. 1267, 119th Cong.) to protect water utilities from CERCLA liability as "passive receivers" who do not produce PFAS but merely deliver water from contaminated sources.
- **Regulatory Distinction**: EPA's 2024 PFAS rule distinguishes between entities that released PFAS into the environment (manufacturers, military bases using AFFF) and water utilities that passively received contaminated source water.
- **Lack of Colorado Precedent**: No Colorado Supreme Court or Court of Appeals cases specifically address pollution exclusions in water utility PFAS contamination contexts.

However, *National Foam*'s broad application of pollution exclusions to water contamination cases significantly undermines this argument. The 15-30% success probability reflects the difficulty of distinguishing MSWC's conduct from traditional pollution scenarios under standard CGL policy language.

**3. Duty to Defend May Exist Despite Exclusion (20-30% Probability)**

Under *Gray v. Zurich Insurance Co.*, 65 Cal. 2d 263 (1966), an insurer must defend if the underlying complaint "potentially seeks damages within the coverage of the policy"—even if the insurer ultimately has no duty to indemnify. Colorado applies the "eight corners rule," comparing only the complaint's allegations to the policy language without considering extrinsic evidence.

If the PFAS class action complaint alleges broad theories of liability (breach of implied warranty, negligence, medical monitoring, property value diminution) without explicitly alleging "pollution," "environmental contamination," or "discharge of pollutants," Zurich may have a duty to defend even if the pollution exclusion ultimately bars indemnification. However, the complaint's specific allegations are critical and have not yet been reviewed for this analysis.

**Most Likely Scenario**: Zurich will defend MSWC under a reservation of rights, preserving its pollution exclusion defense while providing defense counsel. This allows Zurich to investigate coverage issues (policy inception date, known loss doctrine, exclusion applicability) while avoiding immediate bad faith exposure.

**4. Net Uninsured Exposure: $30M-$100M (Midpoint: $65M)**

Even if Zurich provides full coverage, the $50M policy limits are insufficient for the estimated $50M-$150M class action settlement range. After accounting for manufacturer litigation recovery of $20M-$50M from the 3M ($12.5B) and DuPont ($1.18B) PFAS settlements, MSWC's net uninsured exposure is:

**Coverage Scenarios:**
- **Scenario 1 (70-85% probability)**: Pollution exclusion applies → Zero coverage → Net exposure: **$30M-$100M** ($50M-$150M settlement - $20M-$50M manufacturer recovery)
- **Scenario 2 (10-20% probability)**: Zurich defends only, denies indemnity → Defense costs covered ($5M-$15M), settlement self-funded → Net exposure: **$35M-$115M**
- **Scenario 3 (5-10% probability)**: Full coverage → $50M coverage cap → Net exposure: **$0-$50M** (excess of $50M limits after manufacturer recovery)

**Expected Value Calculation (Probability-Weighted):**
- (0.775 × $65M) + (0.15 × $75M) + (0.075 × $25M) = **$62.5M expected uninsured exposure**

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Pollution Exclusion Framework and PFAS as "Pollutant"

#### 1. Standard ISO Pollution Exclusion Language

The standard Commercial General Liability (CGL) policy includes a pollution exclusion that typically states: "We will not pay for... 'bodily injury' or 'property damage' arising out of the actual, alleged or threatened discharge, dispersal, seepage, migration, release or escape of 'pollutants'..."¹

The term "pollutants" is broadly defined as "any solid, liquid, gaseous or thermal irritant or contaminant, including smoke, vapor, soot, fumes, acids, alkalis, chemicals and waste."²

**Evolution of Pollution Exclusions:**
- **Pre-1986**: Limited pollution exclusions with "sudden and accidental" exception
- **Post-1986**: Absolute pollution exclusions eliminating the "sudden and accidental" exception³
- **2023-Present**: PFAS-specific exclusions published by Insurance Services Office (ISO) in June 2023, expressly excluding PFAS-related claims for bodily injury and property damage⁴

#### 2. PFAS as "Pollutants" Under Policy Definitions

**Insurer's Position:**
PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) qualify as "pollutants" under standard CGL definitions because they are:
- **Chemicals**: PFAS are synthetic chemical compounds containing carbon-fluorine bonds
- **Contaminants**: PFAS contaminate water sources and cause adverse health effects
- **Environmental irritants**: PFAS cause thyroid disease, developmental effects, cancer, and other health harms⁵

**Courts' Application:**
Early decisions have held that pollution exclusions bar coverage for PFAS claims, with courts applying the broad definition of "pollutants" to include PFAS compounds.⁶ For example, in *Grange Insurance Co. v. Cycle-Tex, Inc.*, a Georgia federal court held that a Total Pollution Exclusion in a liability policy precluded coverage for a company sued for discharging harmful PFAS into waterways.⁷

#### 3. "Release" or "Discharge" Element

**Insurer's Argument:**
PFAS contamination constitutes a "release of pollutants" under the exclusion because:
- PFAS entered the environment through firefighting foam applications at airports/military bases
- Manufacturing facilities (3M, DuPont, Chemours) discharged PFAS into waterways
- Water utilities distribute this contaminated water to customers = continued "release"

**Water Utility Counter-Argument:**
Water utilities argue they are **"passive receivers"** who:
- Do not produce or manufacture PFAS
- Receive PFAS through contaminated source waters (surface water, aquifers)
- Treat water to drinking water standards but PFAS removal not required until EPA's 2024 rule
- Do not "discharge" PFAS into the environment; they distribute pre-contaminated water⁸

**Legislative Recognition of Passive Receiver Status:**
Congress has considered multiple bills to protect drinking water and wastewater utilities from CERCLA liability as "passive receivers" under the 2024 EPA designation of PFAS as hazardous substances.⁹ This legislative recognition supports the argument that water utilities are distinct from manufacturers/dischargers who actively released PFAS into the environment.

### B. Duty to Defend vs. Duty to Indemnify — PFAS Class Action Context

#### 1. Broad Duty to Defend Standard

**The "Potential for Coverage" Test:**
Under *Gray v. Zurich Insurance Co.*, 65 Cal. 2d 263, 275 (1966), an insurer "must defend a suit which potentially seeks damages within the coverage of the policy."¹⁰ This standard has been widely adopted across jurisdictions, including by federal courts applying Colorado law.¹¹

The California Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed that an insurer will only be relieved of a duty to defend if the third-party complaint can "by no conceivable theory" raise a "single issue" which would bring it within the policy coverage.¹²

**Colorado's Eight Corners Rule:**
Colorado applies the "eight corners rule" (also called the four corners rule), which strictly compares the allegations in the plaintiff's complaint with the language in the insurance policy.¹³ The rule gets its name from examining the four corners of the insurance policy and the four corners of the underlying complaint.¹⁴

**Limitation on Extrinsic Evidence:**
The Colorado Supreme Court has held that extrinsic evidence can only be used to interpret ambiguous policy language, not unambiguous policy language.¹⁵ Colorado generally refuses to allow extrinsic evidence to determine coverage.¹⁶ However, the Tenth Circuit has predicted narrow exceptions where an insurer must consider allegations in parallel complaints if such allegations tend to show a duty to defend.¹⁷

#### 2. Narrower Duty to Indemnify

**The Montrose Standard:**
In *Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Admiral Insurance Co.*, 10 Cal. 4th 645 (1995), the California Supreme Court held that the duty to indemnify is narrower than the duty to defend.¹⁸ While an insurer must defend if there is any potential for coverage based on the complaint's allegations, the insurer must indemnify only if an actual covered loss is proven.¹⁹

**Timing Distinction:**
The duty to defend arises at the outset of litigation based on the allegations, while the duty to indemnify does not arise until a judgment or settlement determines actual liability.²⁰ This creates a significant temporal gap where insurers may defend under reservation of rights while continuing to investigate coverage issues.

#### 3. Application to MSWC's PFAS Class Action

**Arguments for Duty to Defend (Despite Pollution Exclusion):**
- **Broad Complaint Allegations**: The PFAS class action complaint alleges breach of implied warranty, negligence, medical monitoring, and property value diminution²¹
- **No Express "Pollution" Language**: If the complaint does not use terms like "pollution," "discharge," or "environmental contamination," the eight corners rule may require defense²²
- **"Potential" Coverage Sufficient**: Even if the pollution exclusion ultimately applies to indemnity, the insurer must defend if any theory in the complaint could potentially lead to coverage²³

**Arguments Against Duty to Defend:**
- **Unambiguous Exclusion Application**: Courts have held that pollution exclusions clearly apply to PFAS contamination of water supplies as "general environmental pollution"²⁴
- **In *National Foam v. Zurich* (N.D. Cal. Feb. 26, 2025)**: Court distinguished between direct exposure (duty to defend) and indirect exposure through contaminated drinking water (no duty to defend under pollution exclusion)²⁵

**Likely Outcome for MSWC:**
The pollution exclusion will likely bar the **duty to indemnify** based on *National Foam*'s holding that water contamination cases fall within the scope of pollution exclusions as "general environmental exposure."²⁶ However, MSWC may have a stronger argument for a **duty to defend** if the complaint's allegations are broad enough to potentially encompass non-pollution theories of liability (breach of warranty, negligence unrelated to environmental pollution).

### C. Reservation of Rights and Independent Counsel

#### 1. Reservation of Rights Letters

**Standard Practice:**
When an insurer has coverage concerns but chooses to defend, it typically issues a "reservation of rights" letter to preserve coverage defenses while providing a defense.²⁷ This letter notifies the insured that:
- The insurer is defending under a reservation of its right to later deny coverage
- The insurer preserves all policy defenses, including the pollution exclusion
- The defense does not constitute an admission of coverage²⁸

**No Waiver of Exclusions:**
Issuing a reservation of rights does not waive exclusions or estop the insurer from later denying coverage, provided the reservation is timely.²⁹ Colorado law requires an insurer to raise or reserve all defenses within a reasonable time after learning of such defenses, or those defenses may be waived.³⁰

#### 2. Independent Counsel / Cumis Counsel

**Conflict of Interest Standard:**
When an insurer defends under a reservation of rights that creates a conflict of interest between insurer and insured, the insured may be entitled to independent counsel paid by the insurer.³¹ This is known as "Cumis counsel" after the California case *San Diego Federal Credit Union v. Cumis Insurance Society*.³²

**Colorado's Position:**
Colorado is classified as a "one client" state, meaning defense counsel represents only the insured, not the insurer.³³ However, no Colorado court has explicitly held that reservation of rights automatically triggers a right to independent counsel.³⁴ Most states require a case-by-case examination showing an actual, present conflict of interest.³⁵

**MSWC's Scenario:**
If Zurich defends MSWC under a reservation of rights based on the pollution exclusion, MSWC may argue that a conflict exists because:
- Zurich's coverage position (pollution exclusion applies) conflicts with MSWC's defense strategy (PFAS contamination is third-party, not MSWC's "discharge")
- Facts establishing coverage (MSWC as passive distributor vs. discharger) are the same facts MSWC needs to prove in the underlying litigation³⁶

### E. Known Loss Doctrine and Pre-Existing Contamination

#### 1. The Known Loss Doctrine

**Principle:**
The known loss doctrine "provides that there is no coverage for a loss that has already occurred at the time the insured applied for the policy."⁵⁰ Insurance coverage is premised on the concept of fortuity—a loss that occurs by chance or accident.⁵¹ Insurance companies insure against a risk of possible loss, not a certainty.⁵²

**Standard of Knowledge:**
Courts disagree on the required level of knowledge:
- Some courts require **actual knowledge** of the loss⁵³
- Other courts require only **"reason to know"** or **"evidence of probable loss"**⁵⁴
- For liability insurance, some courts hold that the rule applies if the insured knows it is **"substantially probable"** that the insured will be liable⁵⁵

**Insurer's Knowledge Exception:**
Some courts hold that the insurer may not use the known loss doctrine to defeat coverage if it also knew of the loss prior to the policy's inception date.⁵⁶

#### 2. Application to MSWC's PFAS Contamination

**Timeline of MSWC's Knowledge:**
- **2020-2023**: EPA's UCMR5 (Unregulated Contaminant Monitoring Rule) required testing for 29 PFAS compounds⁵⁷
- **2023-2024**: MSWC completed UCMR5 testing, detecting PFAS in 8 systems at 5-22 ppt⁵⁸
- **March 2023**: EPA proposed PFAS rule with 4 ppt MCLs for PFOA/PFOS⁵⁹
- **June 2024**: PFAS class action filed⁶⁰
- **Policy Inception**: If MSWC's current Zurich policy incepted after MSWC learned of PFAS detections (likely 2024), known loss doctrine may bar coverage⁶¹

**Insurer's Argument:**
Zurich may argue that MSWC knew of:
- PFAS contamination in 8 systems (detected 2023-2024)
- Likelihood of class action litigation (EPA rule proposed March 2023, class actions filed nationwide 2023-2024)
- Substantial probability of liability (water utilities settling PFAS cases for $50M-$150M)⁶²

**MSWC's Counter-Argument:**
- PFAS detections occurred, but liability was not certain at policy inception
- No EPA regulation in effect until April 2024 (final rule), no violation of law when policy incepted⁶³
- Class action not filed until June 2024, after policy inception (if policy incepted Jan 2024)⁶⁴
- Known loss doctrine should not apply where insured's liability is uncertain and "fairly debatable"⁶⁵

**Likely Outcome:**
If MSWC's policy incepted **after** UCMR5 testing revealed PFAS (2023-2024) and **after** EPA proposed MCLs (March 2023), Zurich has a strong known loss argument. However, if MSWC can show that liability was not substantially certain at policy inception, MSWC may defeat the known loss defense.⁶⁶

### G. Colorado Bad Faith and Declaratory Judgment Actions

#### 1. Colorado Bad Faith Insurance Standards

**Statutory Standard: CRS § 10-3-1115:**
Colorado law prohibits insurance companies from "unreasonably deny[ing] or delay[ing] payment of a claim of benefits" to first-party claimants.⁸⁹ An insurer's delay is unreasonable "if the insurer delayed or denied authorizing payment of a covered benefit without a reasonable basis for that action."⁹⁰

**Burden of Proof:**
- **Statutory claims (CRS §§ 10-3-1115, 10-3-1116)**: Lesser burden—insured need only prove the denial was "without a reasonable basis"⁹¹
- **Common law bad faith claims**: Insured must prove insurer acted with knowledge of or reckless disregard of the fact that no reasonable basis existed for denying the claim⁹²

**"Fairly Debatable" Doctrine:**
Under Colorado law, it is reasonable for an insurer to challenge claims that are "fairly debatable."⁹³ If "reasonable minds" could disagree as to the coverage-determining facts or law, such circumstance weighs against a finding that the insurer acted unreasonably.⁹⁴

**Critical Limitation:**
That a claim is "fairly debatable" weighs against a finding of bad faith; however, this factor is not outcome-determinative and is not "necessarily sufficient to defeat a bad faith claim as a matter of law."⁹⁵ The fairly debatable standard applies to common law bad faith claims and **not** to statutory delay claims under CRS § 10-3-1115.⁹⁶

**Remedies: CRS § 10-3-1116:**
Colorado's bad faith statute allows first-party claimants to recover reasonable attorneys' fees, court costs, and **"two times the covered benefit"** if the insurer delayed or denied without a reasonable basis.⁹⁷

#### 2. Declaratory Judgment Actions for Coverage Disputes

**Colorado Declaratory Judgment Act: CRS § 13-51-101:**
Colorado's Uniform Declaratory Judgments Law affords parties relief from uncertainty with respect to their rights and status under law.⁹⁸ The statute has been specifically used for insurance coverage questions.⁹⁹

**Actual Controversy Requirement:**
A proceeding for declaratory judgment must be based upon an actual controversy. Regarding insurance coverage, a controversy exists where an insured has submitted a claim and the insurer determined that it did not intend unilaterally to admit liability, even though the insurer had not in fact denied such claim.¹⁰⁰

**Typical Timeline:**
Coverage declaratory judgment actions typically take **12-18 months** from filing to decision, involving:
- Pleadings and motions to dismiss (3-4 months)
- Discovery on coverage issues (6-8 months)
- Summary judgment briefing and decision (3-6 months)¹⁰¹

**Parallel to Underlying Litigation:**
MSWC's PFAS class action is expected to settle in Q2-Q3 2026.¹⁰² If Zurich denies coverage or reserves rights, and MSWC files a declaratory judgment action in Q1 2026, the coverage litigation will likely **not** be resolved before the class action settlement.¹⁰³ This creates significant uncertainty for MSWC's settlement strategy and funding.

#### 3. MSWC's Bad Faith Exposure Analysis

**Scenario 1: Zurich Denies Coverage Outright**
If Zurich denies coverage based on the pollution exclusion without defending, MSWC may argue:
- The pollution exclusion is ambiguous as applied to passive distributors vs. active dischargers¹⁰⁴
- The duty to defend is triggered by the complaint's broad allegations¹⁰⁵

**Bad Faith Risk for Zurich:** MEDIUM. The pollution exclusion's application to PFAS is "fairly debatable" given recent case law distinctions between direct and indirect exposure.¹⁰⁷
