## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This report analyzes the Colorado Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) regulatory approval requirements for American Water Infrastructure LLC's ("AWI") proposed $2.4 billion acquisition of Mountain States Water Company ("MSWC"), Colorado's second-largest investor-owned water utility serving 485,000 connections across 28 systems. The transaction requires CPUC change of control approval under C.R.S. ¬ß 40-5-101, with an anticipated 6-12 month regulatory review timeline. Additionally, this report evaluates rate case disallowance risks associated with MSWC's $2.6 billion capital liability backlog (lead service line replacement $1.95B, PFAS treatment $288M, deferred maintenance $380M) expected to be litigated in MSWC's Q2 2026 general rate case.

**Key Finding**: Colorado PUC approval is **highly likely (85-90% probability)** but will be **conditioned on** (1) 3-year rate freeze deferring $45M-$75M in rate increases, (2) $18M customer bill credits, (3) $750M capital investment commitments, (4) domestic control protections mitigating 45% foreign ownership concerns (CPPIB 30%, Macquarie 15%), and (5) local Colorado management retention. Rate case disallowance risk is **substantial**: $271M-$296M expected disallowances across customer-side lead service lines ($223M), PFAS treatment ($10M-$16M), and deferred maintenance ($38M-$57M), translating to $25.8M-$28.1M in annual lost revenue requirement.

### I.A. Colorado PUC Jurisdiction and Change of Control Requirements

**Statutory Authority:**
Colorado's Public Utilities Law (C.R.S. Title 40, Articles 1-7) grants the Colorado PUC comprehensive regulatory authority over investor-owned water companies, including the power to approve or deny changes of control. Under C.R.S. ¬ß 40-5-101 and related provisions, **no utility may transfer a certificate of public convenience and necessity, transfer or obtain a controlling interest in any utility (whether by asset transfer, stock transfer, merger, or other business combination), or transfer jurisdictional assets outside the normal course of business without prior PUC authorization.**

The AWI acquisition of MSWC unambiguously falls within this statutory mandate. AWI's purchase of 100% of MSWC's equity interests constitutes a "transfer of controlling interest" requiring a formal PUC application, public notice, opportunity for intervention, discovery, potential evidentiary hearing, and Commission decision. The Commission is statutorily required to issue a decision within 250 days, although in practice proceedings range from 6 to 12 months depending on intervenor participation and procedural complexity.

**Public Interest Standard:**
The Colorado Supreme Court has interpreted the PUC's "public interest" mandate as **"a balancing of the interests of the shareholders in a reasonable rate of return and the rights of the ratepayers to receive adequate service at a price which reflects the cost of service."** (*Colorado Municipal League v. Public Utilities Commission*, 687 P.2d 350, 356 (Colo. 1984)). This balancing framework requires the PUC to evaluate:

1. **Rate Impact**: Will the transaction result in rate increases for customers? Can the acquirer commit to a rate freeze during the transition period?
2. **Service Quality**: Will water quality, reliability, and customer service standards be maintained or improved?
3. **Financial Capability**: Is the acquiring utility financially sound and capable of funding mandated capital investments ($2.6B in MSWC's case)?
4. **Local Presence**: Will management and operations remain Colorado-based?
5. **Capital Commitments**: Will the acquirer make enforceable commitments to infrastructure improvements?
6. **Foreign Ownership**: If significant foreign ownership exists, what domestic control protections are in place?

**Standard of Review:**
Courts reviewing PUC decisions apply a deferential "arbitrary and capricious" standard, requiring that Commission orders be "based upon evidence introduced before it," "supported by findings of fact," and reflect application of "relevant legislative standards." (*Mountain States Telephone & Telegraph Co. v. Public Utilities Commission*, 754 P.2d 928, 931 (Colo. 1988)). This standard provides substantial deference to PUC expertise while ensuring decisions are evidence-based rather than administrative convenience.

### I.B. Foreign Ownership Analysis ‚Äî 45% CPPIB/Macquarie Stake

**AWI Ownership Structure:**
- **BlackRock (55%)**: U.S. private equity firm (Blackstone-managed fund) = **U.S. person**
- **Canada Pension Plan Investment Board ("CPPIB") (30%)**: Canadian federal government sovereign wealth fund managing CAD $632 billion in assets = **foreign government entity**
- **Macquarie Infrastructure Partners (15%)**: UK-based infrastructure fund (Sydney-domiciled) = **foreign person**

**Total foreign ownership: 45%** (CPPIB 30% + Macquarie 15%), representing a **minority but substantial** foreign stake in a U.S. critical infrastructure asset.

**Regulatory Precedent for Foreign-Owned Utilities:**
Canadian and Australian pension funds have become dominant players in U.S. infrastructure investments, including water utilities, toll roads, airports, and energy transmission. Notable precedents include the 2016 sale of the Chicago Skyway's 99-year lease to a consortium of CPPIB, OMERS, and OTPP for $2.8 billion, demonstrating that **Canadian pension fund ownership of U.S. critical infrastructure is not prohibited** but is subject to regulatory oversight.

The Securities and Exchange Commission's historical guidance on foreign ownership of U.S. utilities established that "most commenters appeared to agree that the Holding Company Act did not, or should not, prohibit foreign ownership of U.S. utilities, and commenters agreed that foreign holding companies would and should be subject to the same regulatory requirements as U.S. companies." This precedent confirms that **foreign ownership per se is not disqualifying**, but foreign-owned utilities must meet identical regulatory standards as domestic utilities.

**CFIUS Considerations (Parallel Federal Review):**
The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) has jurisdiction over foreign investments in critical infrastructure, particularly water utilities serving military installations or other national security-sensitive facilities. While CFIUS review is beyond the scope of this Colorado PUC analysis, CFIUS approval (or mitigation agreement) will likely be required parallel to PUC review. Standard CFIUS mitigation measures for foreign-owned utilities include:
- Board composition requirements (majority U.S. directors)
- Security officers for handling sensitive information
- Cybersecurity protections and incident reporting
- Notification requirements for ownership changes
- Federal audit rights

**Colorado PUC Domestic Control Requirements (Expected):**
To obtain PUC approval, AWI will need to demonstrate domestic control protections ensuring that operational decisions remain under U.S. control notwithstanding 45% foreign passive investment. Expected PUC conditions include:

1. **Board Composition**: Majority (4 of 7) directors must be U.S. citizens, independent of CPPIB/Macquarie
2. **No Foreign Veto Rights**: Reserved matters list ensuring operational decisions (rate case filings, capital expenditure budgets, water rights transactions, service territory expansions/contractions) are controlled by U.S. directors without CPPIB/Macquarie veto rights
3. **Local Colorado Management**: CEO, COO, CFO, and Chief Engineer must be based in Denver and U.S. citizens for minimum 5 years post-closing
4. **Dividend Repatriation Limits**: Maximum 50% of annual net income distributable to investors; remaining 50% retained for system reinvestment (preventing excessive capital extraction to Toronto/Sydney)
5. **Annual Reporting to PUC**: Disclosure of any changes in foreign ownership percentages, control structures, or investor agreements that could affect operational autonomy

**Probability Assessment:**
Foreign ownership at 45% is **substantial but not unprecedented** in U.S. utility infrastructure. Provided AWI implements robust domestic control protections demonstrating that (1) operational control rests with U.S. management, (2) capital allocation decisions are made in Colorado (not Toronto or Sydney), and (3) CPPIB/Macquarie are passive economic investors without operational control, the PUC is **likely to approve the transaction with enhanced domestic control conditions and reporting requirements** (85-90% approval probability with conditions).

**Deal-Blocking Risk from Foreign Ownership: 10-15%** (low, but not de minimis ‚Äî PUC could deny if AWI fails to demonstrate adequate domestic control or if intervenors raise persuasive national security concerns regarding Canadian government ownership of Colorado's second-largest water utility).

### I.C. Expected Approval Conditions and Financial Impact

Based on national utility merger precedent (Evergy merger 2018, Kansas/Missouri PUC approvals) and regulatory settlement practice, the Colorado PUC will likely impose the following conditions:

#### 1. Rate Freeze (High Probability: 80-90%)
**Duration**: 3 years (January 1, 2026 ‚Äì December 31, 2028, assuming Q1 2026 approval)
**Scope**: Base rates frozen at current levels; purchased water adjustment clauses and other cost trackers remain operative
**Financial Impact**: Foregone rate increases estimated $15M-$25M annually (assuming MSWC would otherwise file for 3-5% annual base rate increases to fund capital programs)
**Total 3-year impact**: $45M-$75M in deferred rate recovery, reducing AWI's cash flow during peak capital expenditure period

**Rationale**: Rate freezes are standard conditions for utility acquisitions, providing customer protection during ownership transition and ensuring that transaction costs (legal fees, financing costs, integration expenses) are not passed through to ratepayers. The 3-year duration aligns with typical utility merger conditions in Kansas, Missouri, Illinois, and other Midwestern states with comparable regulatory frameworks.

#### 2. Customer Bill Credits (Moderate Probability: 60-70%)
**Amount**: $18M (midpoint of $12M-$24M typical range observed in comparable utility merger settlements)
**Distribution Method**: One-time bill credit of approximately $37 per connection (485,000 MSWC connections)
**Purpose**: Offset transaction costs, provide immediate customer benefit demonstrating AWI commitment to customer value, reduce intervenor opposition

**Precedent**: The 2018 Evergy merger (Great Plains Energy + Westar Energy) included customer bill credits as a core settlement term, along with rate moratoriums. Kansas and Missouri commissions approved the merger contingent on these customer protection measures.

#### 3. Capital Investment Commitments (High Probability: 85-95%)
**Amount**: $750M over 5 years (2026-2030)
**Components**:
- Lead service line replacement: $700M (initial phase of 10-year $1.95B program, 2026-2034 compliance timeline under EPA Lead and Copper Rule Revisions)
- PFAS treatment installation: $50M (accelerated installation 2026-2027, ahead of anticipated 2028-2029 EPA compliance deadline)
- Deferred maintenance: $200M prioritized cast iron main replacements (out of $380M total backlog)

**Purpose**: Demonstrate AWI's financial capability to fund MSWC's mandated infrastructure improvements. This commitment addresses PUC concerns regarding whether a foreign-controlled acquirer will prioritize U.S. infrastructure investments or extract capital for international deployments.

**Enforceability**: Capital commitments typically include annual reporting requirements, with PUC enforcement authority to impose penalties or revoke transaction approval if commitments are not met.

#### 4. Management Retention and Local Presence (Certain: 95%+)
**Requirements**:
- CEO, COO, CFO, Chief Engineer remain Denver-based for minimum 5 years post-closing
- No relocation of operational headquarters outside Colorado
- Customer service centers remain in-state
- Local hiring preferences for new positions

**Purpose**: Ensure operational continuity, maintain local responsiveness to customer and municipal concerns, prevent "absentee landlord" management structure where key decisions are made remotely from Toronto, Sydney, or New York.

#### 5. Domestic Control Protections (Certain: 95%+, given 45% foreign ownership)
**Requirements**: As detailed in Section I.B above ‚Äî Board composition, no foreign veto rights, dividend caps, annual PUC reporting.

### I.D. Rate Case Disallowance Risk Analysis ‚Äî $271M-$658M at Risk

MSWC's anticipated Q2 2026 general rate case will seek recovery of $2.6 billion in capital liabilities accrued under prior ownership:
- **Lead service line replacement**: $1.95B ($1.395B utility-side + $558M customer-side)
- **PFAS treatment**: $50M-$80M capital + $8M-$12M annual O&M (NPV $208M-$288M)
- **Deferred maintenance**: $380M (1,200 miles cast iron mains 80-100 years old, 65-85 main breaks annually)

Colorado PUC applies the **prudent investment test**, disallowing capital costs that were not "reasonable at the time they were incurred, given the circumstances and what was known or knowable at the time." Three categories of capital face substantial disallowance risk:

#### 1. Customer-Side Lead Service Line Replacement ($558M at Risk)

**Issue**: Under Colorado water law (as practiced by Denver Water and Englewood Utilities), **service lines from the curb stop to the residence are customer-owned and customer-maintained**. Property owners are traditionally responsible for replacing customer-side lines at their own expense (estimated $3,000-$5,000 per line).

**MSWC's Position**: Seeking full rate recovery for both utility-side ($1.395B) and customer-side ($558M) replacement costs, arguing:
1. **Public health necessity**: Lead exposure poses severe health risks, especially for children; partial replacements (utility-side only) create lead-copper galvanic corrosion risks worsening water quality
2. **Affordability**: Low-income customers (40% of MSWC customer base) cannot afford $3K-$5K out-of-pocket costs for customer-side replacement
3. **Precedent**: Denver Water (municipal utility) replaced customer-side lead lines at no charge, funded through system-wide rate increases over 30 years

**PUC Likely Position**: Partial recovery based on low-income assistance justification.

**Estimated Disallowance Scenarios**:
- **Best case (Denver Water model)**: 0% disallowance, full $558M recovery ‚Äî **20% probability** ‚Äî PUC accepts public health argument for system-wide cost socialization
- **Base case (low-income assistance)**: 40% recovery ($223M), 60% disallowance ($335M) ‚Äî **60% probability** ‚Äî PUC allows recovery for low-income customers unable to afford replacement, disallows recovery for higher-income customers who should bear traditional customer-side responsibility
- **Worst case (strict customer responsibility)**: 100% disallowance ($558M) ‚Äî **20% probability** ‚Äî PUC applies traditional ownership principles, rejecting public health argument as insufficient to override property law

**Expected value disallowance: $223M** (60% probability base case: $558M √ó 60% disallowance = $335M, but base case assumes 40% recovery, 60% disallowance = $335M disallowed)
**Annual revenue impact: $21.2M** (@ 9.5% authorized ROE √ó $223M rate base disallowance)

#### 2. PFAS Treatment "Late Investment" Disallowance ($10M-$16M at Risk)

**Issue**: MSWC detected PFAS contamination (5-22 ppt PFOA/PFOS) in 8 of 28 water systems during EPA's UCMR5 monitoring program (2023-2024). EPA's final PFAS rule (expected Q2-Q3 2025) will establish 4 ppt MCL for PFOA/PFOS, with compliance deadline 3 years post-final rule (2028-2029). MSWC plans to install GAC (granular activated carbon) treatment at 8 systems in 2028-2029 to meet the compliance deadline.

**Prudence Issue**: **Reactive vs. proactive timing**. Utilities that detected PFAS contamination in 2023-2024 but delay treatment installation until the 2028-2029 compliance deadline face disallowance risk for "late" investment. Regulatory precedent from other state PUCs shows that utilities installing treatment **proactively** (ahead of compliance deadlines) receive full cost recovery, while utilities installing treatment **reactively** (at compliance deadline) face 20-30% disallowances for failure to anticipate known regulatory requirements.

**Estimated Disallowance Scenarios**:
- **Best case (proactive treatment)**: 0% disallowance ‚Äî **30% probability** ‚Äî PUC accepts that treatment installation 2026-2027 (ahead of deadline) demonstrates proactive public health protection
- **Base case (compliance-driven)**: 20-25% disallowance ($10M-$20M) ‚Äî **50% probability** ‚Äî PUC accepts that MSWC complied with EPA timeline but disallows portion of costs for delay between PFAS detection (2023-2024) and treatment installation (2028-2029)
- **Worst case (reactive/late)**: 30% disallowance ($15M-$24M) ‚Äî **20% probability** ‚Äî PUC finds MSWC should have installed treatment immediately upon PFAS detection rather than waiting for EPA final rule

**Expected value disallowance: $10M-$16M** (base case 20-25%)
**Annual revenue impact: $1.0M-$1.5M** (@ 9.5% ROE)

**Mitigation Strategy**: Accelerate PFAS treatment installation to 2026-2027 (1-2 years ahead of deadline) to reduce disallowance risk to 0-10%. Cost of acceleration: $2.5M-$4M in additional financing costs. Benefit: Avoid $15M-$24M worst-case disallowance. **Net benefit: $11M-$20M.**

#### 3. Deferred Maintenance Imprudence Disallowance ($38M-$57M at Risk)

**Issue**: MSWC has deferred $380M in main replacements over the past 5-10 years, resulting in 1,200 miles of cast iron mains (28% of system) aging 80-100 years old with 65-85 main breaks annually. The PUC may disallow a portion of deferred maintenance capital, finding that MSWC imprudently deferred preventive maintenance, allowed infrastructure to deteriorate to crisis levels, and is now seeking ratepayer funding for emergency repairs that could have been avoided through timely maintenance.

**EPA Context**: EPA's December 2024 Water Affordability Needs Assessment concluded: **"Not implementing necessary rate increases can also cause deferred maintenance, resulting in higher costs."** This analysis suggests that utilities deferring maintenance to avoid rate case filings may face prudence challenges when seeking belated cost recovery.

**MSWC's Defense**:
- 2020-2024 drought and conservation mandates reduced MSWC revenues 8% ($54M cumulatively), constraining capital budgets
- MSWC prioritized EPA compliance (SDWA MCL violations, lead service line replacement planning) over discretionary main replacements
- Asset management plan demonstrates forward-looking risk-based prioritization (replacing highest-risk mains first)

**Estimated Disallowance Scenarios**:
- **Best case (justified deferrals)**: 0% disallowance ‚Äî **20% probability** ‚Äî PUC accepts that revenue constraints and competing EPA mandates justified deferrals
- **Base case (partial imprudence)**: 10-15% disallowance ($38M-$57M) ‚Äî **60% probability** ‚Äî PUC finds some deferrals justified but disallows portion attributable to management decisions to avoid rate cases
- **Worst case (significant imprudence)**: 20% disallowance ($76M) ‚Äî **20% probability** ‚Äî PUC finds MSWC systematically underfunded maintenance to preserve earnings

**Expected value disallowance: $38M-$57M** (base case 10-15%)
**Annual revenue impact: $3.6M-$5.4M** (@ 9.5% ROE)

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Colorado PUC Statutory Jurisdiction Over Water Utilities

#### 1. Legislative Framework ‚Äî Public Utilities Law

Colorado's Public Utilities Law is codified in C.R.S. Title 40, Articles 1-7, collectively known as the "Public Utilities Law."¬π The statute establishes comprehensive regulatory authority over public utilities operating within Colorado, including investor-owned water companies.¬≤

**Statutory Basis for PUC Authority:**
- **C.R.S. ¬ß 40-1-101**: Establishes Title 40, Articles 1-7 as the "Public Utilities Law" governing public utilities and the regulatory commission.¬≥
- **C.R.S. ¬ß 40-5-101**: Requires public utilities to obtain a Certificate of Public Convenience and Necessity (CPCN) before constructing new facilities or extending existing systems.‚Å¥
- **C.R.S. ¬ß 40-5-102**: Governs the issuance of CPCNs and the public interest standard for new construction approvals.‚Åµ

The Colorado General Assembly has directed that the PUC "must first give paramount consideration to the public interest," requiring "constant attention to achieve the appropriate balance between the needs of Colorado customers for safe and reliable utility services at reasonable rates and the needs of utility service providers to earn a reasonable profit and to sustain a reliable utility infrastructure throughout Colorado."‚Å∂

#### 2. Change of Control and Transfer Approval Requirements

**Transfer of Control Statute:**
Under Colorado law, a utility cannot effectuate a change of control without PUC approval. Specifically, a utility seeking to:
- Transfer a certificate of public convenience and necessity;
- Transfer or obtain a controlling interest in any utility (whether effected by transfer of assets, transfer of stock, merger, or other business combination); or
- Transfer assets subject to PUC jurisdiction outside the normal course of business

**must file an application with the Commission and obtain authority before completing the transaction.**‚Å∑

**Regulatory Framework:**
The PUC's Rules of Practice and Procedure, codified at 4 CCR 723-1 (Rules 1000-1999), govern all Commission proceedings.‚Å∏ Specific rules apply to different utility types:
- **Electric utilities**: 4 CCR 723-3, Rule 3104 (transfer applications)‚Åπ
- **Gas utilities**: 4 CCR 723-4, Rule 4104¬π‚Å∞
- **Water utilities**: 4 CCR 723-3 (general provisions applicable to investor-owned water companies)¬π¬π

**Application Requirements:**
An application for transfer of control must demonstrate that the proposed transaction serves the public interest. The Commission reviews applications through a formal proceeding that includes:
1. Public notice of the application to all interested persons
2. Establishment of a timeline for requests to participate as a party
3. Discovery (if parties intervene)
4. Public hearing (for contested matters)
5. Commission decision within statutory timeframe¬π¬≤

**Statutory Timeline:**
The Commission is required by statute to issue a final decision within **250 days**, unless extended by extraordinary circumstances.¬π¬≥ However, in practice, transfer proceedings can range from one month to several years depending on the number of intervening parties, complexity of issues, and the Commission's calendar.¬π‚Å¥

### B. Public Interest Standard for Change of Control Approvals

#### 1. Judicial Interpretation of "Public Interest"

The Colorado Supreme Court has established clear precedent on the PUC's application of the "public interest" standard in utility regulatory matters:

**Balancing Test:**
The term "public interest" involves **a balancing of the interests of the shareholders in a reasonable rate of return and the rights of the ratepayers to receive adequate service at a price which reflects the cost of service.**¬π‚Åµ This balancing framework requires the PUC to consider both utility investor interests (fair return on investment, financial viability) and customer interests (affordable rates, reliable service, service quality).¬π‚Å∂

**Legislative Standards:**
Title 40, Articles 3 and 4 of the Colorado Public Utilities Law provide "sufficient general standards for guidance relative to utility regulation," and the PUC has "long emphasized and utilized the criterion of 'public interest.'"¬π‚Å∑ The Colorado legislature has directed that the PUC must give "paramount consideration to the public interest" in all regulatory decisions.¬π‚Å∏

**Standard of Review:**
Courts reviewing PUC decisions apply a deferential standard, examining whether:
1. The PUC has regularly pursued its authority;
2. The PUC adhered to federal and state constitutions;
3. The decision is just and reasonable; and
4. The conclusions accord with the evidence.¬π‚Åπ

In determining whether the PUC has regularly pursued its authority, courts consider whether:
- The order is based upon evidence introduced before the Commission;
- The order is supported by findings of fact;
- The PUC applied the relevant legislative standards; and
- The PUC acted within its statutory authority.¬≤‚Å∞

**Arbitrary and Capricious Standard:**
Orders that are "arbitrary and capricious or a clear abuse of discretion" must be set aside.¬≤¬π An order is arbitrary and capricious when "issued solely as a matter of administrative convenience without sufficient factual basis in the record."¬≤¬≤ This standard provides substantial deference to PUC expertise while ensuring decisions are grounded in evidence.

#### 2. Public Interest Factors in Change of Control Proceedings

Although the Colorado statutes do not enumerate specific factors for change of control approvals, standard regulatory practice and precedent from other jurisdictions suggest the PUC typically evaluates:

**Rate Impact:**
- Will the transaction result in rate increases for customers?
- Can the acquiring utility commit to a rate freeze or rate cap for a transition period (2-3 years typical)?
- Are customer credits or bill reductions proposed to offset any transaction costs?

**Service Quality and Reliability:**
- Will the change of ownership maintain or improve service quality?
- Does the acquiring utility have technical capability and operational experience?
- Will water quality standards continue to be met or improved?

**Financial Capability:**
- Is the acquiring utility financially sound and capable of making necessary capital investments?
- Will the transaction result in excessive debt or leverage that could impair future operations?
- Can the new owner fund mandated infrastructure improvements (lead service line replacement, PFAS treatment, system maintenance)?

**Local Presence and Management:**
- Will corporate headquarters or regional offices remain in Colorado?
- Will key management personnel (CEO, COO, CFO, Chief Engineer) be retained or replaced with Colorado-based executives?
- Will operational and customer service functions remain in-state?

**Capital Investment Commitments:**
- Will the acquiring utility commit to specific capital expenditures for system improvements?
- Are there enforceable commitments for infrastructure upgrades, leak reduction, or water loss mitigation?

**Foreign Ownership Considerations:**
- If the acquiring utility has significant foreign ownership, what domestic control protections are in place?
- Is the Board of Directors majority U.S. citizens?
- Are operational decisions free from foreign government influence or veto rights?
- Are dividend repatriation policies reasonable and protective of utility reinvestment needs?

### D. Rate Case Procedures and Revenue Requirement Determinations

#### 1. Revenue Requirement Formula

Colorado PUC rate cases employ the standard regulatory formula for calculating a utility's revenue requirement:

**Revenue Requirement = (Rate Base √ó Allowed Rate of Return) + Operating Expenses + Depreciation + Taxes**¬≥¬π

More specifically:
- **Rate Base (RB)**: Gross investment in utility plant minus accumulated depreciation ‚Äî the value of property dedicated to providing service to customers¬≥¬≤
- **Rate of Return (R)**: The weighted average cost of capital (WACC), composed of the utility's cost of debt and authorized return on equity (ROE)¬≥¬≥
- **Operating Costs (OC)**: Prudently incurred costs for operations, maintenance, and customer service¬≥‚Å¥
- **Depreciation (D)**: Annual depreciation expense for recovery of capital investments over asset useful lives¬≥‚Åµ
- **Taxes (T)**: Income taxes, property taxes, and other regulatory assessments¬≥‚Å∂

#### 2. Authorized Return on Equity (ROE) for Water Utilities

**National Water Utility ROE Benchmarks:**
Water utility ROEs nationally have ranged from **9.2% to 9.8%** in recent years.¬≥‚Å∑ Specific state commission decisions include:
- **Kentucky**: 9.7% (litigated case, high end of range)¬≥‚Å∏
- **North Carolina**: 9.75% (litigated case)¬≥‚Åπ
- **National Average (2020)**: 8.48% (Q1 2020 average for completed water utility rate cases, down from 9.63% in 2019)‚Å¥‚Å∞

**Colorado PUC Practice:**
The Colorado PUC "approves rates for services provided by investor-owned water companies" and "ensures that Colorado water providers earn a return sufficient for their long-term economic viability and their ability to update their physical plant or equipment to provide reliable, safe services."‚Å¥¬π

While specific Colorado water utility ROE authorizations were not located in public search results, the PUC's mandate to ensure "long-term economic viability" suggests authorized ROEs consistent with national water utility averages: **approximately 9.0%-9.5%** for well-capitalized investor-owned water companies.‚Å¥¬≤

The Commission "exercises the greatest degree of judgment" in determining the fair return on equity, and Colorado courts will not disturb the PUC's ROE decision "as long as it falls within a 'zone of reasonableness.'"‚Å¥¬≥

#### 3. Prudence Standard and Cost Disallowance Risk

**Prudent Investment Test:**
To determine whether capital costs should be included in the rate base, Colorado PUC applies the **prudent investment test or standard**, determining **"if the costs were reasonable at the time they were incurred, and given the circumstances and what was known or knowable at the time."**‚Å¥‚Å¥

**Grounds for Disallowance:**
When a utility fails to demonstrate prudent decision-making, the PUC may disallow capital costs from the rate base. Common grounds for disallowance include:‚Å¥‚Åµ

1. **Imprudence**: Failure to demonstrate prudent decision-making at the time costs were incurred
2. **Excess Capacity**: Plant or infrastructure in excess of what is needed by ratepayers (regardless of prudence)
3. **Cost Cap Violations**: Completion of facilities at costs exceeding PUC-approved construction budgets
4. **Deferred Maintenance**: Failure to perform timely maintenance resulting in excessive future costs‚Å¥‚Å∂

**Ratepayer Protection Principle:**
**"Ratepayers have no obligation to compensate utility management and shareholders for imprudent decisions and behavior."**‚Å¥‚Å∑ This principle protects customers from bearing the costs of poor utility management decisions and places financial risk on shareholders for management failures.

#### 4. Lead Service Line Replacement Cost Recovery in Colorado

**Ownership and Responsibility:**
In Colorado water systems (as exemplified by Denver Water and Englewood Utilities):
- **Utility-owned**: Service line from water main to curb stop (utility's responsibility)‚Å¥‚Å∏
- **Customer-owned**: Service line from curb stop to residence (property owner's responsibility)‚Å¥‚Åπ

**Denver Water Precedent (Municipal Utility, Not PUC-Regulated):**
Denver Water's Lead Reduction Program provides a useful benchmark, although Denver Water is a municipal utility not subject to Colorado PUC jurisdiction:
- **15-year program**, estimated cost $670 million‚Åµ‚Å∞
- **Replaces customer-owned lead service lines with copper at no direct charge to customers**‚Åµ¬π
- **Financing**: Debt and cash funding spread over 30 years across all customers‚Åµ¬≤
- **Federal support**: $6 million EPA grant, $40 million principal forgiveness, $76 million from Bipartisan Infrastructure Law via Colorado State Revolving Fund‚Åµ¬≥
- **Rate recovery**: Costs spread across all customers through water rates (not direct charges to affected properties)‚Åµ‚Å¥

**Reimbursement Option:**
Denver Water offers $3,800 reimbursement (approximately 50% of utility replacement cost) for customers who complete replacements outside the program schedule.‚Åµ‚Åµ

**Englewood Utilities Precedent (Municipal Utility):**
- Secured WIFIA (Water Infrastructure Financing and Innovation Act) low-interest loans and grant funds‚Åµ‚Å∂
- Property owners who opt out may pay up to $12,000 for customer-side replacement‚Åµ‚Å∑
- Costs recovered through rate base with federal funding reducing customer impact‚Åµ‚Å∏

- **Customer bill credits**

This precedent demonstrates that **rate freezes, customer credits, and management retention** are standard conditions for utility acquisition approvals.

#### 2. Settlement Practice and Precedent Value

Many utility rate cases and mergers are resolved by **"black-box" settlements**, where parties settle differences but avoid disclosing specific terms to prevent establishing precedent.‚Å∑¬π Settlements typically:
- Are **not used to establish binding precedent** for future proceedings‚Å∑¬≤
- Reflect **negotiated compromises** rather than Commission findings of fact‚Å∑¬≥
- Provide **administrative efficiency** but limited evidentiary value for future cases‚Å∑‚Å¥
