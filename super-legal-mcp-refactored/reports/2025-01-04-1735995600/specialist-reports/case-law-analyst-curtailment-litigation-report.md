### Section II: Prior Appropriation Legal Framework

**Colorado's Constitutional Doctrine**: Colorado's prior appropriation system ("first in time, first in right") is constitutionally mandated (Colo. Const. art. XVI, ¬ß 6) and codified at C.R.S. ¬ß 37-92-103. Senior appropriators (earlier priority dates) receive full allocation before junior appropriators receive any water during shortages.

**State Engineer Curtailment Authority**: C.R.S. ¬ß 37-92-502 grants Division Engineers authority to "order the total or partial discontinuance of any diversion" to protect senior rights. In *Fellhauer v. People*, 167 Colo. 320, 447 P.2d 986 (1968), the Colorado Supreme Court upheld State Engineer authority to curtail junior appropriators but required "investigation disclose that doing so would make additional water available to senior appropriators."

**Judicial Review Standard**: Courts review State Engineer orders for "abuse of discretion" ‚Äî orders are overturned only if "manifestly arbitrary, unreasonable, or unfair" (*In re Storey*, 2022 CO 48). Recent precedent affirms deference: *Parker Water & Sanitation District v. State Engineer* (2024) upheld State Engineer authority to impose volumetric limits on well permits where statutory language was unambiguous.

**Critical Gap**: Colorado has **no precedent** for pro rata curtailment (equal percentage reductions across all users regardless of priority). Texas uses pro rata curtailment (LCRA), but Colorado rigidly adheres to priority-based administration. This is MSWC's strongest argument ‚Äî but untested in Colorado courts during a Compact emergency.

### Section III: Colorado River Compact and Emergency Curtailment

**Compact Over-Allocation**: The 1922 Colorado River Compact allocated 7.5 million AF/year to Upper Basin states and 7.5 million AF/year to Lower Basin states, plus 1.5 million AF/year to Mexico (total 16.5 million AF/year). However, the river's long-term average flow is only 13.2-14.3 million AF/year (per tree ring studies), creating a structural over-allocation of 3-4 million AF/year.

**Upper Basin Delivery Obligation**: Article III of the Compact requires Upper Basin states to deliver 75 million AF over any 10-year period to the Lower Basin (measured at Lee Ferry, Arizona). Failure to meet this obligation could trigger Lower Basin litigation against Upper Basin states.

**2023 Lower Basin Agreement (Not Upper Basin)**: In May 2023, Lower Basin states (California, Arizona, Nevada) agreed to 3 million AF conservation over 3 years (14% cuts), compensated with $1.2 billion federal funding. Upper Basin states **declined to participate**, arguing they already use less than the Lower Basin. This means Upper Basin curtailment (if imposed) would be unilateral State Engineer action or federal directive, not negotiated agreement.

**Compact Curtailment Risk**: "Compact curtailment" means post-1922 water rights are vulnerable to having no water. Front Range municipalities (including MSWC per scenario) and many agricultural diversions are post-1922 rights. In determining curtailment under a Compact call, "uses of water under rights perfected prior to November 24, 1922, are excluded." The legal framework for how post-1922 rights would be curtailed (strict priority vs. pro rata) remains uncertain.

**Federal Law Supremacy**: The Colorado River Compact is an interstate compact approved by Congress, giving it federal law status under the Compact Clause (U.S. Const. art. I, ¬ß 10, cl. 3). In *Texas v. New Mexico*, 462 U.S. 554, 564 (1983), the U.S. Supreme Court held interstate compacts are "construed and applied as federal law." This means federal Compact obligations may override Colorado's state-law prior appropriation system.

**Lake Powell Deadpool Emergency**: Lake Powell deadpool occurs at 3,490 feet ‚Äî below this level, water cannot be delivered to the Lower Basin or generate hydroelectric power at Glen Canyon Dam. This constitutes an emergency threatening interstate Compact compliance and federal project operations, potentially justifying temporary departure from strict prior appropriation.

1. **Judicial Deference to State Engineer Emergency Powers**: *Parker v. State Engineer* (2024) and prior cases affirm courts overturn State Engineer actions only if "manifestly arbitrary, unreasonable, or unfair." Emergency curtailment to prevent Compact violations and deadpool likely survives this deferential standard.

2. **Federal Compact Supremacy**: If the State Engineer demonstrates 10% curtailment is necessary to satisfy Upper Basin delivery obligations (75 million AF/10 years at Lee Ferry), federal Compact law may override Colorado's state prior appropriation system. Precedent: Rio Grande Compact case (1984) upheld curtailment to satisfy interstate obligations, departing from traditional priority administration.

3. **Emergency "Maximum Utilization" Rationale**: *Fellhauer v. People* emphasized Colorado law requires both protection of senior rights AND "maximum utilization" of water resources. A court could interpret emergency curtailment as consistent with "maximum utilization" ‚Äî preventing deadpool ensures the river system remains functional for all users long-term, even if it temporarily departs from strict priority.

4. **No Colorado Pro Rata Curtailment Precedent**: MSWC's strongest argument is that pro rata curtailment has no Colorado precedent. However: (a) no Colorado case has addressed pro rata curtailment during a Compact emergency; (b) courts may distinguish routine shortage (where priority applies) from Compact crisis (where emergency allocation is permissible); (c) State Engineer could argue this is temporary pending post-2026 negotiations, not permanent pro rata departure from priority.

**Partial Relief Possibility (10-15%)**: Court could modify the curtailment formula to give greater weight to priority dates (e.g., 5% cut for pre-1970 rights, 15% cut for post-1990 rights) while upholding State Engineer's emergency allocation authority. This would provide MSWC some relief while preserving emergency powers.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Colorado Prior Appropriation Doctrine ‚Äî Legal Framework

#### 1. Constitutional Foundation

Colorado's water law is founded on the doctrine of prior appropriation, commonly summarized as "first in time, first in right."¬π This principle was enshrined in the Colorado Constitution following the 1859 gold rush, when miners who were first to divert water gained rights superior to later arrivals.¬≤ The Colorado Constitution of 1876 codified this doctrine in Article XVI, Section 6, providing that "Priority of appropriation shall give the better right as between those using the water for the same purpose."¬≥

#### 2. Statutory Framework ‚Äî C.R.S. ¬ß 37-92-103

The Colorado Revised Statutes define the core elements of prior appropriation:

- **Priority**: "The seniority by date as of which a water right is entitled to use."‚Å¥
- **Water Right**: "A right to use in accordance with its priority a certain portion of the waters of the state by reason of the appropriation of the same."‚Åµ

The prior appropriation system allocates the physical supply of water in a stream among water rights owners based on the priority date of their water right, with older "senior" water rights entitled to receive water prior to later "junior" water rights.‚Å∂

#### 3. State Engineer Administration ‚Äî C.R.S. ¬ß 37-92-502

The Colorado State Engineer and Division Engineers possess broad authority to administer water rights and enforce the priority system:

**Curtailment Authority**: C.R.S. ¬ß 37-92-502 provides that "[e]ach division engineer shall order the total or partial discontinuance of any diversion in his division to the extent that the water being diverted is required by persons entitled to use water under water rights having senior priorities."‚Å∑

**Measurement and Monitoring**: The State Engineer may "order any owner or user of a water right to install and maintain at such owner's or user's expense necessary meters, gauges, or other measuring devices."‚Å∏

**Enforcement**: If a State Engineer order is not complied with, the State Engineer may seek an injunction through the water judge under C.R.S. ¬ß 37-92-503.‚Åπ

#### 4. Fellhauer v. People ‚Äî Curtailment Precedent

In the landmark 1968 case *Fellhauer v. People*, 167 Colo. 320, 447 P.2d 986 (1968), the Colorado Supreme Court addressed the State Engineer's authority to curtail water appropriations:

**Holding**: The State Engineer may shut down junior appropriators' wells following procedures used in the distribution of waters for over one hundred years, but "only curtail water appropriation after investigation disclose[s] that doing so would make additional water available to senior appropriators."¬π‚Å∞

**Constitutional Mandate**: The Court emphasized that Colorado's constitutional provisions require both protection of vested senior rights AND "maximum utilization" of the state's water resources.¬π¬π

**Regulatory Requirements**: Wells can be regulated only in compliance with "reasonable rules, regulations, standards and a plan established by the state engineer prior to the issuance of the regulative orders."¬π¬≤

### B. Colorado River Compact (1922) and Curtailment Framework

#### 1. Compact Allocations and Over-Appropriation

The Colorado River Compact of 1922 divided the Colorado River Basin into Upper Basin states (Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico) and Lower Basin states (Nevada, Arizona, California), apportioning to each basin "the beneficial use of 7.5 million acre-feet of Colorado River water per year."¬π¬≥

**Delivery Obligation**: The Compact stipulates that the Upper Basin states must not deplete the Colorado River flow below an aggregate of 75 million acre-feet for any 10 consecutive years, measured at Lee Ferry in northern Arizona.¬π‚Å¥

**Mexico Treaty**: In addition to the 15 million AF allocated to the two basins, the 1944 treaty with Mexico allocated an additional 1.5 million AF per year to Mexico, bringing total allocations to 16.5 million AF/year.¬π‚Åµ

**Over-Allocation Crisis**: The Compact allocations were based on an expectation that the river's average flow was 16.4 million AF/year. However, tree ring studies have concluded that the long-term average water flow of the Colorado River is significantly less ‚Äî estimates include 13.2 million AF/year, 13.5 million AF/year, and 14.3 million AF/year.¬π‚Å∂ This means the river is structurally over-allocated by approximately 3-4 million AF/year.

#### 2. 2023 Lower Basin Conservation Agreement

In May 2023, the Department of the Interior and the three Lower Basin states announced a consensus-based proposal to conserve a total of 3 million acre-feet (MAF) of water prior to 2026, with approximately 14% water usage cuts across the Southwest over three years.¬π‚Å∑ The federal government agreed to compensate 2.3 MAF of these reductions using $1.2 billion from the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act.¬π‚Å∏

California's allocation would be cut by approximately 10% in most years under the Lower Basin proposal ‚Äî significant because California, with senior rights to Colorado River water, has historically been sheltered from mandatory conservation during droughts.¬π‚Åπ

**Upper Basin Non-Participation**: The 2023 agreement was primarily a Lower Basin agreement. The Upper Basin states declined to contribute, arguing they should not take cuts because they already use less than the Lower Basin.¬≤‚Å∞

#### 3. "Compact Curtailment" Risk for Post-1922 Water Rights

**Definition**: "Compact curtailment," sometimes described as a "compact call," means that water rights junior to or filed since the Colorado River Compact of 1922 would be vulnerable to having no water.¬≤¬π

**Colorado's Vulnerability**: Front Range municipal water providers and many of Colorado's agricultural diversions are post-1922 compact rights.¬≤¬≤ This potentially includes most of Colorado's Front Range cities, which derive approximately half of their water from the Colorado River and its tributaries.¬≤¬≥ Some agricultural rights on the Western Slope are also post-1922.¬≤‚Å¥

**Legal Complexity**: In determining curtailment relationships under a compact call, "uses of water under rights perfected prior to November 24, 1922, are excluded" from curtailment.¬≤‚Åµ However, experts note: "It's not as simple as pre-1922 rights are protected and post-1922 rights are going to be subject to curtailment based on the existing prior appropriation system."¬≤‚Å∂

**Litigation Risk**: Multiple pathways to litigation exist, including Lower Basin states suing Upper Basin states for violating Article III of the 1922 Compact, or states suing the Secretary of the Interior.¬≤‚Å∑ Litigation "would be lengthy, expensive, and fraught with uncertainties," with a reasonable chance it would ultimately reach the U.S. Supreme Court.¬≤‚Å∏

### C. Judicial Review of State Engineer Orders ‚Äî Standard of Deference

#### 1. Water Court Jurisdiction Over State Engineer Actions

Colorado water courts possess jurisdiction to review certain State Engineer and Division Engineer actions, including:

- Enforcement orders and curtailment directives;
- Approvals of temporary changes of water rights and substitute water supply plans;
- Refusals to enforce reductions or implement shutdowns of undecreed water uses or decreed junior water rights after a "call" was placed by a senior water right.¬≤‚Åπ

#### 2. Administrative Deference Standards

**Federal Administrative Law Parallel**: Administrative agencies such as the State Engineer are granted varying levels of discretion to interpret laws and court rulings, and courts grant various levels of deference to agency interpretations of statutes.¬≥‚Å∞ Under the Federal Administrative Procedures Act, judicial review of administrative agencies is intended to override actions found to be "arbitrary and capricious, in excess of statutory jurisdiction, without observance of procedure required by law, or unsupported by substantial evidence."¬≥¬π

**Chevron Deference (Historical)**: The 1984 U.S. Supreme Court case *Chevron v. Natural Resources Defense Council* granted deference to administrative agencies' reasonable policy-making decisions.¬≥¬≤ This principle has relevance to Colorado because the State Engineer administers water court decrees.¬≥¬≥

**NOTE**: The U.S. Supreme Court overruled *Chevron* deference in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), eliminating mandatory deference to federal agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes. However, this ruling applies to federal agencies; Colorado courts may continue to apply state-law administrative deference principles to the State Engineer.

#### 3. *Simpson v. Bijou Irrigation Co.* ‚Äî Limitation on State Engineer Authority

In *Simpson v. Bijou Irrigation Co.*, 69 P.3d 50 (Colo. 2003), the Colorado Supreme Court significantly limited the State Engineer's administrative authority in favor of judicial oversight:

**Holding**: The General Assembly intended to assign the matter of approving ongoing out-of-priority groundwater diversions using replacement water exclusively to the water courts, rather than to the State Engineer.¬≥‚Å¥

**Rationale**: In 1969 and again in 1977, when the General Assembly repealed the State Engineer's short-lived temporary augmentation plan approval authority, it did so "due to concern over overlapping administrative and judicial authority and the inordinate amount of power this would have vested in the State Engineer."¬≥‚Åµ

**Safeguards Against Administrative Discretion**: The Court interpreted the procedures set forth in C.R.S. ¬ß 37-92-304 as "the means chosen by the legislature to provide safeguards against the unreasonable exercise of administrative discretion by the State Engineer."¬≥‚Å∂

**Standard of Review**: The Court reviews *de novo* the water court's interpretation and declaration of Colorado law.¬≥‚Å∑

#### 4. Recent Cases (2020-2024): Abuse of Discretion Standard

**Parker Water & Sanitation District v. State Engineer (2024)**: The Colorado Supreme Court addressed challenges to the State Engineer's authority to impose total volumetric limits on nontributary groundwater withdrawals. Arguments alleged the State Engineer's actions were "arbitrary and capricious, an abuse of discretion."¬≥‚Å∏ The Court held that C.R.S. ¬ß 37-90-137 unambiguously imposes a total volumetric limit on well permits, affirming the State Engineer's authority.¬≥‚Åπ

**Abuse of Discretion Standard**: A court abuses its discretion only when its ruling is "manifestly arbitrary, unreasonable, or unfair." *In re Storey*, 2022 CO 48.‚Å¥‚Å∞

**Administrative Appeals Standard**: For appeals involving state officers, the district court's review is limited to "whether the body or officer has exceeded its jurisdiction or abused its discretion, based on the evidence in the record."‚Å¥¬π
