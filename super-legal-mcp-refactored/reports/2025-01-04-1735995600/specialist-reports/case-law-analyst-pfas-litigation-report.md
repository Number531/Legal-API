## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. MSWC's PFAS Contamination Context

Mountain States Water Company (MSWC) operates 28 public water systems serving 1.35 million people across 45 Colorado municipalities. PFAS testing conducted pursuant to EPA's Unregulated Contaminant Monitoring Rule 5 (UCMR5) in 2023-2024 revealed that **8 of MSWC's systems detected PFOA/PFOS at concentrations ranging from 5-22 parts per trillion (ppt)**, affecting approximately 180,000 customer connections (37% of MSWC's customer base).

These concentrations exceed the EPA's proposed Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) of 4 ppt for PFOA and PFOS, finalized in 2024 with a compliance deadline of 2028-2029. PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are synthetic chemicals known as "forever chemicals" due to their persistence in the environment and human body, associated with health risks including cancer, thyroid disease, and developmental effects.

### B. The Customers v. MSWC Class Action

According to the task parameters, a class action lawsuit titled *Customers v. MSWC* was filed in June 2024 in Denver County District Court, Colorado, on behalf of 45,000 customers served by the 8 systems with PFAS detections. The lawsuit asserts three primary claims:

1. **Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability**: Plaintiffs allege that water was sold with an implied warranty that it is fit for its ordinary purpose (drinking water), and PFAS contamination breached this warranty. Damages sought: refund of water bills pro rata, estimated $15M-$25M.

2. **Negligence**: Plaintiffs allege MSWC owed a duty to provide safe drinking water, knew or should have known of PFAS contamination (testing data available since early 2020s), and failed to install treatment. Damages sought: medical monitoring costs of $1,500-$3,000 per person √ó 135,000 people (45,000 households √ó 3 average) = **$202M-$405M**.

3. **Diminished Property Value**: Plaintiffs allege homes served by PFAS-contaminated systems are worth less due to disclosure requirements and buyer concerns. Damages sought: $10,000-$25,000 per property √ó 45,000 properties = **$450M-$1.125 billion**, supported by real estate appraisals and sales comparisons.

**Total potential verdict exposure: $650M-$1.5 billion** if the case proceeds to trial.

### C. National PFAS Litigation Landscape

PFAS litigation has exploded nationwide, with thousands of cases consolidated in MDL 2873 (*In re: Aqueous Film-Forming Foams Products Liability Litigation*) in the U.S. District Court for the District of South Carolina. Major manufacturer settlements include:

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Colorado Legal Framework for Plaintiffs' Claims

#### 1. Breach of Implied Warranty (C.R.S. ¬ß 4-2-314)

**Statutory Standard**: Colorado's Uniform Commercial Code provides that "a warranty that goods shall be merchantable is implied in a contract for their sale if the seller is a merchant with respect to goods of that kind." C.R.S. ¬ß 4-2-314(1) (2024).¬π To be merchantable, goods must "pass without objection in the trade under the contract description" and "are fit for the ordinary purposes for which such goods are used." C.R.S. ¬ß 4-2-314(2)(a), (c).¬≤

**Application to Water Sales**: Water sold by a public utility for household consumption must be fit for drinking‚Äîits ordinary purpose. Contaminated water that exceeds EPA health-based standards arguably fails this merchantability requirement.

**Damages Measure**: "The measure of damages for breach of warranty is the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted." C.R.S. ¬ß 4-2-714(2).¬≥ Incidental and consequential damages may also be recovered. C.R.S. ¬ß 4-2-714(3).‚Å¥

**MSWC's Defenses**:
- **No Regulatory Violation at Time of Delivery**: EPA's final PFAS MCL rule was not effective until 2024-2025. Water delivered prior to this date met all regulatory standards in effect. Courts may be reluctant to impose warranty liability for compliance with then-applicable law.
- **UCC Applicability**: Water service may be characterized as a "service" rather than a "good," potentially taking the transaction outside Article 2's warranty provisions. However, Colorado courts have applied UCC warranty principles to defective products causing personal injury.‚Åµ

**Legal Precedent on Contaminated Products**: In warranty breach claims involving contaminated products, "a plaintiff can establish a prima facie case of breach of warranty and injury resulting from that breach, where it is shown that the plaintiff used the product according to the manufacturer's directions for its intended use, and the product was shown to be contaminated."‚Å∂

#### 2. Negligence

**Colorado Negligence Standard**: To establish negligence under Colorado law, plaintiffs must prove: (1) duty of care owed by defendant to plaintiff, (2) breach of that duty, (3) injury suffered by plaintiff, and (4) proximate causation between breach and injury.‚Å∑

**Duty of Water Utility**: Water utilities have a duty to provide safe drinking water that complies with applicable regulatory standards. However, the scope of this duty is typically defined by statutory and regulatory requirements (Safe Drinking Water Act, Colorado Primary Drinking Water Regulations 5 CCR 1002-11).‚Å∏

**Breach Allegation**: Plaintiffs argue MSWC knew or should have known of PFAS contamination through UCMR testing but failed to install treatment until legally required to do so by EPA's 2024 MCL rule.

**Causation Challenge**: Plaintiffs must establish that PFAS exposure from MSWC's water supply actually caused or will cause injury. Medical monitoring damages require proof of exposure sufficient to increase health risks and justify monitoring costs.

**MSWC's Defenses**:
- **No Violation of Standard of Care**: MSWC was not legally required to treat for PFAS until EPA finalized the MCL in 2024. Acting in accordance with regulatory requirements typically satisfies the duty of care.
- **Third-Party Contamination**: PFAS contamination originated from firefighting foam used at airports, military bases, and by manufacturers (3M, DuPont, Chemours). MSWC is a distributor, not the source. Negligence liability typically requires causation‚Äîthat defendant's conduct caused the harm.‚Åπ

#### 3. Medical Monitoring Damages in Colorado

**Critical Development**: Colorado courts have recently **rejected** medical monitoring claims by asymptomatic plaintiffs in toxic exposure cases. In *Smith v. Terumo BCT, Inc.*, 2025 WL 3029699 (Colo. Ct. App. Oct. 30, 2025), the Colorado Court of Appeals held that "one of the basic principles of law is that a party may not recover damages if he has not suffered an injury," and that "a person cannot pursue a tort claim for future death, future physical injury, or future property damage."¬π‚Å∞

**Impact on MSWC Case**: The *Smith* decision creates a significant obstacle to plaintiffs' $202M-$405M medical monitoring claim. If plaintiffs cannot demonstrate **present physical injury** from PFAS exposure, Colorado courts may dismiss this component of damages on a motion to dismiss or summary judgment.

**Jurisdictional Split**: Other jurisdictions permit medical monitoring for asymptomatic plaintiffs where exposure creates a reasonable need for monitoring.¬π¬π However, Colorado now joins the majority of states rejecting such claims.¬π¬≤

#### 4. Property Value Diminution

**Colorado Disclosure Requirements**: Under C.R.S. ¬ß 38-35.7-102, sellers of residential property must disclose "material defects" and conditions affecting property value, desirability, or safety, including environmental hazards.¬π¬≥ Homes served by PFAS-contaminated water systems may trigger disclosure obligations, potentially reducing property values.

**Materiality Standard**: The U.S. Supreme Court's decision in *TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc.*, 426 U.S. 438 (1976), established that an omitted fact is material "if there is a substantial likelihood that a reasonable shareholder would consider it important."¬π‚Å¥ This standard has been adopted in Colorado real estate law: an "adverse material fact" is one "that a reasonable person would ascribe actual significance to."¬π‚Åµ

**Proving Diminution**: Colorado courts require plaintiffs to prove actual diminution in property value, typically through:
- Appraisal testimony comparing PFAS-affected properties to comparable unaffected properties
- Actual sales data showing reduced prices
- Expert testimony on buyer behavior and disclosure impacts¬π‚Å∂

**MSWC's Defenses**:
- **Speculative Damages**: Without actual sales demonstrating reduced values, diminution claims are speculative and may not satisfy Colorado's requirement for proved damages.¬π‚Å∑
- **EPA Compliance by 2028**: MSWC is installing GAC treatment to comply with EPA's MCL by the 2028-2029 deadline. Once treatment is operational, the basis for disclosure and diminution may be eliminated.

**Precedent on Environmental Contamination**: In *In re Estate of Gattis*, Colorado courts held that sellers have an affirmative duty to disclose latent defects affecting property value, and buyers may recover damages including "repair costs and depreciation" for undisclosed environmental hazards.¬π‚Å∏

### B. Motion to Dismiss Analysis (C.R.C.P. 12(b)(5))

#### 1. Colorado Pleading Standard Post-*Warne v. Hall*

Colorado adopted the federal *Twombly*/*Iqbal* plausibility standard in **2016 CO 50**, requiring plaintiffs to plead facts that "allow the court to draw the reasonable inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct alleged."¬π‚Åπ This replaced the prior "no set of facts" standard, significantly raising the pleading bar.

**Requirements for Survival**: Complaints must contain:
- Well-pleaded factual allegations (not legal conclusions or "bare assertions")
- Sufficient detail to nudge claims from "conceivable to plausible"
- Specific facts supporting each element of the claim¬≤‚Å∞

**Standard of Review**: Courts must "accept all matters of material fact in the complaint as true and view the allegations in the light most favorable to the plaintiff," but may dismiss if "the plaintiff's factual allegations cannot support a claim as a matter of law."¬≤¬π

#### 2. Likely Outcomes on Motion to Dismiss

**Claim 1 (Breach of Warranty): 50-60% Survival Probability**

**Strengths**:
- Plaintiffs plausibly allege that water contaminated with PFAS above EPA's health-based MCL is not "fit for ordinary purpose" (drinking)
- EPA's 4 ppt MCL provides objective standard for unfitness
- MSWC sold water as a "good" under the UCC

**Weaknesses**:
- MSWC did not violate any regulation in effect at time of delivery (regulatory compliance defense)
- Water service may be characterized as "service" not "goods," removing UCC applicability
- Damages measure unclear‚Äîrefund of water bills pro rata may be speculative

**Prediction**: Moderate likelihood of survival. Colorado courts have applied warranty principles to contaminated products,¬≤¬≤ but the absence of a regulatory violation at time of delivery creates a strong defense. Expect partial dismissal on damages issues.

**Claim 2 (Negligence): 40-50% Survival Probability**

**Strengths**:
- Water utilities owe duty to provide safe drinking water
- MSWC knew of PFAS contamination through UCMR testing
- EPA's MCL establishes health-based benchmark for safety

**Weaknesses**:
- **No Standard of Care Violation**: Acting in compliance with regulatory requirements (no EPA MCL until 2024) typically satisfies duty of care
- **Third-Party Causation**: PFAS originated from manufacturers (3M, DuPont) and firefighting foam users, not MSWC. MSWC is distributor, not source.
- **Medical Monitoring Foreclosed**: *Smith v. Terumo BCT* (Oct. 2025) rejected medical monitoring for asymptomatic plaintiffs in Colorado¬≤¬≥

**Prediction**: Likely dismissal or significant narrowing. The *Smith* decision eliminates $202M-$405M in medical monitoring damages, leaving only general negligence claims. Without present physical injury, plaintiffs face high bar.

**Claim 3 (Property Value Diminution): 60-70% Survival Probability**

**Strengths**:
- Colorado law requires disclosure of environmental hazards affecting property value (C.R.S. ¬ß 38-35.7-102)
- Plaintiffs plausibly allege PFAS contamination is "adverse material fact" under *TSC Industries* standard
- Real estate appraisals and sales comparisons (alleged) provide factual basis

**Weaknesses**:
- **Speculative Damages**: Colorado requires proof of actual diminution, typically through actual sales data. Appraisals alone may be insufficient.¬≤‚Å¥
- **Future Treatment**: MSWC is installing GAC treatment by 2028-2029. Once operational, disclosure concerns may be eliminated.
- **Causation**: Plaintiffs must show PFAS contamination (not general market conditions) caused diminution.

**Prediction**: Likely survives motion to dismiss on pleadings, but faces challenges at summary judgment or trial on proof of actual damages.

**Consolidated Assessment**: The motion to dismiss (Q1 2026 hearing) will likely result in:
- **Partial dismissal** of medical monitoring damages ($202M-$405M) based on *Smith v. Terumo BCT*
