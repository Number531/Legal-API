## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Key Findings

Mountain States Water Company (MSWC) faces a **mandatory $1.4 billion to $1.95 billion lead service line replacement obligation** under EPA's Lead and Copper Rule Improvements (LCRI), finalized October 8, 2024 and effective December 30, 2024. This represents one of the largest lead service line replacement programs among U.S. investor-owned water utilities and creates critical transaction risks for the Project Aquarius $2.4B acquisition.

**MSWC's service line inventory (completed September 2024) identified:**
- 148,000 lead service lines (31% of total 475,000 service lines)
- 38,000 galvanized requiring replacement (GRR) lines (8%)
- 62,000 lead status unknown (13%, presumed lead until verified)
- **Total confirmed requiring replacement: 186,000 lines (lead + GRR)**

The LCRI mandates complete replacement of all lead and GRR service lines within 10 years (2027-2037), regardless of current lead levels. This eliminates the conditional nature of prior regulations and imposes a **mandatory 18,600 service lines per year replacement pace**, translating to **annual capital requirements of $140 million to $195 million**.

### Critical Regulatory Exposure: Customer-Side Rate Recovery Risk

The central uncertainty is whether the Colorado Public Utilities Commission (PUC) will allow MSWC to recover **$558 million in customer-side replacement costs** through rates. This represents 29% of the total $1.953 billion program cost.

**Legal Background:**
Under Colorado law, customers own the service line from the water main connection to their property. Traditionally, customer-owned infrastructure is the customer's financial responsibility, not the utility's. However, EPA's LCRI "whole line" replacement policy prohibits partial replacement (utility-side only) because disturbing lead pipes without full replacement can temporarily increase lead levels and leave customers exposed.

**The Precedent Gap:**
My research identified **no specific Colorado PUC rate case decisions** regarding customer-side lead service line replacement cost recovery for investor-owned water utilities. The Colorado PUC does not regulate municipal systems like Denver Water, which has replaced customer-side lines at utility expense ($670M program over 15 years). This means MSWC's program will be among the first tests of the Colorado PUC's approach for investor-owned utilities.

**Other State Precedents (Not Binding on Colorado):**
- **Pennsylvania:** Act 120 (2018) expressly authorizes customer-side cost recovery; Pennsylvania PUC approved Pennsylvania American Water's plan
- **Indiana:** HEA 1519 allows utilities to include customer-side costs in rate base
- **New Jersey:** P.L. 2021, c. 183 requires customer-side costs be included in rate base
- **Wisconsin:** State law authorizes customer-side financial assistance programs

**Rate Recovery Risk Assessment:**
- **Utility-side costs ($1.395B):** LOW risk (5-10% disallowance) - prudent investment to comply with federal mandates
- **Customer-side costs ($558M):** HIGH risk (50-70% disallowance) - unprecedented request for investor-owned utility to replace customer-owned infrastructure

**Quantified Scenarios:**
1. **Best Case:** Full recovery of $1.953B ‚Üí $0 shareholder impact
2. **Base Case:** 40% customer-side recovery ‚Üí $335M disallowed + $120M low-income assistance = **$350M shareholder impact**
3. **Worst Case:** 0% customer-side recovery ‚Üí $558M disallowed + $178M low-income assistance + $75M enforcement = **$800M shareholder impact**

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. EPA Lead and Copper Rule Regulatory Framework

The Safe Drinking Water Act, 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 300f et seq., authorizes EPA to establish National Primary Drinking Water Regulations (NPDWRs) to protect public health. The Lead and Copper Rule, codified at 40 CFR Part 141 Subpart I, regulates lead and copper in public water systems.

**Regulatory Timeline:**
- **Original Lead and Copper Rule (1991)**: Established action level of 15 ppb for lead
- **Lead and Copper Rule Revisions (LCRR, 2021)**: Required service line inventories by October 16, 2024
- **Lead and Copper Rule Improvements (LCRI, October 8, 2024)**: Final rule effective December 30, 2024, mandates 10-year replacement timeline for all lead and GRR lines

### B. MSWC's Water System Profile

Mountain States Water Company operates 28 separate public water systems (PWS IDs) in Colorado, serving 485,000 water service connections (1.35 million people) across 45 municipalities in the Colorado Front Range.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. EPA Lead and Copper Rule Improvements (LCRI) - Mandatory Requirements

**1. Service Line Inventory Requirements (40 CFR ¬ß 141.84(a))**

All community water systems, regardless of size, must develop and maintain a publicly accessible inventory of service lines under 40 CFR ¬ß 141.84(a).¬π The initial inventory was due to the state by October 16, 2024. MSWC completed this requirement in September 2024. [VERIFIED: MSWC inventory completion September 2024]

**Material Classification Requirements:**
Water systems must identify each service line as: (1) lead, (2) non-lead, (3) galvanized requiring replacement (GRR) if it has ever been downstream of a lead or unknown service line, or (4) lead status unknown.¬≤

**Scope:**
The inventory must describe both water system-owned and customer-owned segments of the service line.¬≥

**Public Accessibility:**
Systems serving more than 50,000 people must make the inventory available online.‚Å¥ MSWC serves 485,000 connections (1.35 million people), triggering this online publication requirement.

**2. Galvanized Requiring Replacement (GRR) Service Lines**

GRR indicates a galvanized steel service line that is or was ever "downstream" of a lead service line, or is currently downstream of a pipe for which the lead status is unknown.‚Åµ Given their downstream locations, these galvanized pipes could have absorbed lead that leached from upstream lead components.‚Å∂ Even after removing an upstream lead service line, the galvanized pipe that was once downstream may still pose a contamination risk due to absorbed lead.‚Å∑

MSWC identified 38,000 GRR service lines (8% of total system). Under the LCRI, these must be replaced along with lead service lines within the mandatory 10-year timeline.‚Å∏

**3. Mandatory 10-Year Replacement Timeline**

EPA's final Lead and Copper Rule Improvements regulation, issued October 8, 2024 and effective December 30, 2024, requires the complete replacement of all lead and GRR service lines within 10 years, regardless of the system's current lead levels.‚Åπ

**Prior LCRR Requirement (2021):**
Under the 2021 LCRR, if a water system exceeded the 15 ppb action level after installing corrosion control treatment, it was required to replace a minimum of 3% of known or potential lead service lines annually.¬π‚Å∞

**LCRI Mandatory Timeline (2024):**
The LCRI eliminates the conditional nature of replacement and mandates 10-year full replacement for ALL systems with lead or GRR lines, regardless of action level exceedances.¬π¬π The compliance date is November 1, 2027, with the 10-year replacement period running from 2027-2037.¬π¬≤

**4. Action Level Exceedance Triggers**

The EPA's action level for lead in public water systems is 15 ppb (0.015 mg/L).¬π¬≥ If lead concentrations exceed 15 ppb in more than 10% of customer taps sampled (the 90th percentile concentration), the system must undertake additional corrosion control actions.¬π‚Å¥

**Public Notification:**
Beginning October 16, 2024, an exceedance of the action level for lead requires Tier 1 public notification within 24 hours for any community water system or non-transient non-community water system.¬π‚Åµ

**MSWC's Status:**
Five of MSWC's 28 public water systems exceeded the 15 ppb action level (PWS IDs: CO0145023, CO0145029, CO0145031, CO0145038, CO0145042). While the new LCRI mandates replacement regardless of action level, these exceedances demonstrate actual lead contamination in MSWC's distribution system and elevate regulatory scrutiny.

**5. Future Action Level Reduction to 10 ppb**

The LCRI reduces the lead action level to 10 ppb from the current 15 ppb, effective with the November 1, 2027 compliance date.¬π‚Å∂ This lower action level will likely result in additional MSWC systems exceeding the threshold, further emphasizing the urgency of the lead service line replacement program.

### E. Rate Recovery Risk Analysis

**1. Colorado PUC Rate-Making Framework**

The Colorado Public Utilities Commission regulates rates for investor-owned water utilities under C.R.S. ¬ß 40-1-101 et seq. (Colorado PUC Act). Rate cases determine the utility's revenue requirement based on:
- Rate base (utility's investment in assets used to serve customers)
- Operating expenses (including depreciation, taxes, O&M)
- Return on equity (ROE) - MSWC's authorized ROE is 9.5%‚Å¥¬≥

**Rate Recovery Formula:**
Revenue Requirement = Rate Base √ó (ROE √ó % Equity + Cost of Debt √ó % Debt) + Operating Expenses

**2. Prudent Investment Standard**

For capital investments to be included in rate base, the Colorado PUC applies a "prudent investment" standard: the utility must demonstrate the investment was reasonable and necessary to serve customers.

**Utility-Side LSL Replacement:**
- **Likely Recoverable:** Replacing utility-owned infrastructure to comply with federal EPA mandates is a prudent investment serving public health and safety
- **Precedent:** Utilities routinely recover main replacement, treatment plant upgrades, and other capital projects in rate base
- **Risk Level:** LOW (5-10% disallowance risk for utility-owned portion)

**Customer-Side LSL Replacement:**
- **At Risk:** Customer-owned infrastructure is traditionally the customer's responsibility, not the utility's
- **Counterargument:** EPA's "whole line" replacement policy (partial replacement leaves lead exposure) creates public health necessity
- **Precedent Gap:** No Colorado PUC decisions specifically addressing customer-side LSL cost recovery for investor-owned utilities
- **Risk Level:** HIGH (50-70% disallowance risk for customer-owned portion absent specific PUC authorization)
