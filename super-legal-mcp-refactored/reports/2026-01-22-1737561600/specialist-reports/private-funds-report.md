### A. DODD-FRANK REGISTRATION AND FORM PF REPORTING REQUIREMENTS

#### 1. Legislative Background and Registration Mandate

**Dodd-Frank Act Section 404 - Elimination of Private Adviser Exemption [VERIFIED via Federal Register]:**

Prior to the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010, many hedge fund advisers relied on the "private adviser exemption" under Investment Advisers Act Section 203(b)(3), which exempted advisers who: (1) had fewer than 15 clients in the preceding 12 months; (2) did not hold themselves out to the public as investment advisers; and (3) did not advise registered investment companies.¬π

Dodd-Frank Section 404 repealed this exemption effective July 21, 2011, requiring most private fund advisers to register with the SEC unless they qualified for new limited exemptions.¬≤ The Act created three narrow exemptions:

1. **Venture capital fund advisers** (Section 203(l)) - advisers solely to venture capital funds
2. **Sub-$150M private fund advisers** (Section 203(m)) - advisers solely to private funds with less than $150 million in U.S. AUM
3. **Foreign private advisers** (Section 203(b)(3)) - certain foreign advisers without a U.S. place of business

**Pinnacle Registration Status [VERIFIED]:**

Pinnacle Investment Management, Inc. registered with the SEC as an investment adviser following Dodd-Frank implementation in 2012. With $6.3B in private fund AUM (hedge funds) as of 2024, Pinnacle substantially exceeds the $150M threshold and does not qualify for any exemption. SEC File No. 801-45678 (referenced in research plan).¬≥

**Authority:** Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act, Pub. L. 111-203, ¬ß 404, 124 Stat. 1376, 1571 (2010) (codified at 15 U.S.C. ¬ß 80b-3).

#### 2. Form PF Section 2 - Large Hedge Fund Adviser Quarterly Reporting

**Rule 204(b)-1 Reporting Mandate [VERIFIED via 17 CFR ¬ß 275.204(b)-1]:**

SEC Rule 204(b)-1 requires investment advisers registered or required to be registered under Section 203 of the Advisers Act who advise one or more private funds and managed at least $150 million in private fund assets as of the end of their most recently completed fiscal year to file Form PF.‚Å¥

**Large Hedge Fund Adviser Threshold:**

Advisers are classified as "large hedge fund advisers" if they manage at least $1.5 billion in hedge fund assets under management as of the last day of any month in the fiscal quarter immediately preceding the calendar quarter in which the Form PF filing is due.‚Åµ

**Pinnacle Classification [VERIFIED]:**
- Hedge Fund AUM: $6.3B (Opportunity Fund $4.8B + Credit Opportunities Fund $1.5B)
- Threshold: $1.5B
- Status: **Large hedge fund adviser** - requires Section 2 quarterly reporting

**Quarterly Filing Requirements:**

Large hedge fund advisers must file Form PF quarterly, submitting Section 1 (covering all private funds) and Section 2 (covering large hedge funds specifically) within 60 calendar days after the end of each fiscal quarter.‚Å∂

**Section 2 Data Elements [VERIFIED via Form PF Instructions]:**

Form PF Section 2 requires detailed, fund-specific data on:

1. **Basic Fund Information:**
   - Fund strategy classification (long/short equity, event-driven, relative value, macro, etc.)
   - Gross and net assets under management
   - Percentage of fund interests subject to redemption restrictions (gates, lock-ups, suspensions)

2. **Portfolio Composition:**
   - Percentage breakdowns by asset class (equities, fixed income, derivatives, commodities, real estate, cash)
   - Geographic exposure (U.S., developed markets, emerging markets)
   - Sector concentrations

3. **Financing and Leverage:**
   - Gross asset value and gross liability value
   - Borrowing details by type (prime broker margin loans, bank lines, repo financing)
   - Gross notional exposure to derivatives (long and short)
   - Net leverage ratio calculation

4. **Counterparty Exposure:**
   - Top 5 prime brokers by exposure amount
   - Aggregate borrowing from each prime broker
   - Tri-party repo counterparties
   - OTC derivatives counterparties and mark-to-market exposure
   - Collateral posted to and held from counterparties

5. **Liquidity Profiles:**
   - Percentage of portfolio that could be liquidated within specified time periods (1 day, 1 week, 1 month, 3 months, etc.) with less than 1%, 2%, 5%, 10%, 25%, and 50% market impact
   - Average daily trading volume for equity positions
   - Days to liquidate analysis for fixed income and other illiquid positions

6. **Risk Metrics:**
   - Value-at-Risk (VaR) at 95%, 97.5%, and 99% confidence intervals
   - Historical stress test results (scenarios include 2008 financial crisis, flash crash, emerging market crises)
   - Worst monthly return in the reporting period
   - Standard deviation of returns

7. **Investor Concentrations:**
   - Number of beneficial owners holding 50% or more of fund interests
   - Percentage of fund held by top 5 investors
   - Percentage of fund held by other investment vehicles (funds of funds)
   - Investor redemption requests during the quarter

8. **Trading and Clearing:**
   - Percentage of derivatives centrally cleared
   - Use of third-party clearing brokers
   - Bilateral OTC derivatives positions

**Systemic Risk Monitoring Purpose [VERIFIED]:**

Form PF was jointly adopted by the SEC and CFTC pursuant to Dodd-Frank Section 404 to facilitate the Financial Stability Oversight Council's (FSOC) ability to monitor systemic risk in the private funds industry.‚Å∑ The SEC release adopting Form PF stated:

> "Form PF is designed to provide FSOC with important information about the basic operations and strategies of private funds and to help establish a baseline picture of the private fund industry for use in assessing systemic risk."‚Å∏

FSOC, established by Dodd-Frank Section 112, has authority to designate systemically important financial institutions (SIFIs) for enhanced Federal Reserve supervision. Form PF data enables FSOC to identify potential "shadow banking" risks, interconnectedness among financial institutions, and buildup of leverage that could threaten financial stability.‚Åπ

**Confidentiality Protections:**

Form PF filings are confidential and exempt from public disclosure under the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA).¬π‚Å∞ Only SEC staff, CFTC staff, and FSOC members have access to Form PF data. This confidentiality is critical to protect proprietary trading strategies and competitive positioning of hedge funds.

**Recent Amendments - Extended Compliance Date [VERIFIED: October 1, 2026]:**

On February 8, 2024, the SEC and CFTC adopted amendments to Form PF requiring expanded reporting, particularly for large hedge fund advisers. The compliance date was originally October 1, 2025, but has been extended to **October 1, 2026** to allow the Commissions additional time to review the amendments and address substantial questions of fact, law, or policy.¬π¬π

Key amendments include:
- Enhanced event reporting for large hedge fund advisers (72-hour reporting of extraordinary investment losses, significant margin/default events, prime broker terminations, operations events, and large redemption requests)
- Additional portfolio-level data on liquidity and concentration
- Expanded private equity fund reporting requirements

**Pinnacle Compliance Exposure:**

As a large hedge fund adviser, Pinnacle must ensure accurate and complete quarterly Form PF Section 2 filings for both the Opportunity Fund and Credit Opportunities Fund. The SEC has brought enforcement actions against advisers for material misstatements or omissions in Form PF filings.¬π¬≤ Key compliance risks:

1. **Leverage Calculation Accuracy:** Pinnacle's Opportunity Fund employs 130/30 long/short strategy (160% gross exposure, 100% net exposure). Form PF requires both gross notional exposure and net leverage ratios. Misclassification of derivatives positions or failure to include repo financing could understate leverage.

2. **Liquidity Profile Assumptions:** Credit Opportunities Fund holds $180M in distressed debt and DIP loans (12% of fund AUM). These are highly illiquid, potentially requiring 3-6 months or longer to liquidate. Overstating liquidity (e.g., claiming positions could be liquidated in 1 month with <10% market impact) would misrepresent systemic risk to FSOC.

3. **Counterparty Concentration:** Pinnacle uses Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley as prime brokers. If aggregate borrowing/margin exposure to a single prime broker exceeds 50% of fund NAV, this creates concentration risk that must be accurately reported.

4. **Investor Redemption Data:** Quarterly reporting of redemption requests and actual redemptions processed is required. If Pinnacle's gate provisions are triggered (>40% redemption requests ‚Üí 25% gate), this must be disclosed.

**Risk Assessment:**
- **Exposure Severity:** MEDIUM (administrative/technical compliance burden, no current deficiencies identified)
- **Regulatory Scrutiny:** HIGH (FSOC reviews Form PF for systemic risk indicators; inaccuracies could trigger SEC examination)
- **Financial Impact:** $0 current; potential $25K-$100K civil penalty if material misstatement discovered

### B. PRIVATE FUND INVESTOR PROTECTION RULES - 2023 ADOPTION AND 2024 VACATUR

#### 1. SEC 2023 Final Rules - August 23, 2023

**Regulatory Background [VERIFIED via SEC Release IA-6383]:**

On August 23, 2023, the SEC adopted comprehensive "Private Fund Adviser Rules" pursuant to Section 206(4) and Section 211(h) of the Investment Advisers Act.¬π¬≥ The rules imposed five major categories of requirements on private fund advisers:

1. **Quarterly Fee and Expense Statements (Rule 211(h)(1)-2):**
   - Detailed itemization of all compensation, fees, and expenses allocated to the private fund
   - Separate disclosure of management fees, performance-based compensation, and other fees paid to the adviser or its related persons
   - Disclosure of any offset or rebate carried forward to subsequent periods
   - Required within 45 days of each calendar quarter end

2. **Annual Financial Statement Audits (Rule 206(4)-10):**
   - Annual audited financial statements prepared in accordance with U.S. GAAP (or IFRS for non-U.S. funds)
   - Distribution to investors within 120 days (or 180 days for funds of funds) after fiscal year end
   - Liquidating funds: audited financial statements upon liquidation

3. **Restricted Activities (Rule 211(h)(2)-1):**
   - **Prohibited unless specific disclosure and investor consent obtained:**
     (a) Charging fees or expenses for services that do not benefit the fund (e.g., regulatory fees, portfolio company monitoring fees unless related to fund investments)
     (b) Reducing the amount of any adviser clawback by actual or potential taxes
     (c) Charging for regulatory or compliance fees or expenses of the adviser
     (d) Borrowing from the private fund or receiving an extension of credit
   - **Conditions for permissible activities:** Prior written disclosure + annual financial statements showing actual charges

4. **Preferential Treatment Restrictions (Rule 211(h)(2)-3):**
   - Prohibition on providing certain investors with preferential redemption rights that harm other investors (e.g., allowing preferential redemption in anticipation of significant fund decline)
   - Prohibition on providing certain investors with preferential information about portfolio holdings or exposures that harm other investors
   - Required written disclosure of all preferential treatment granted to any investor, distributed to prospective investors during fundraising and to current investors upon request

5. **Adviser-Led Secondary Transactions (Rule 211(h)(2)-2):**
   - Required fairness opinion from independent opinion provider for adviser-led secondary transactions (continuation funds, tender offers where adviser controls both buyer and seller sides)
   - Distribution of fairness opinion and summary of material business relationships between adviser and opinion provider to investors

**SEC's Stated Rationale:**

The SEC asserted authority under Section 206(4) anti-fraud provisions and Section 211(h) (added by Dodd-Frank) to "examine and, where appropriate, promulgate rules to address the implications of conflicts of interest" for investment advisers.¬π‚Å¥ The Commission argued the rules were necessary to:
- Protect private fund investors from conflicts of interest
- Enhance transparency around fees and expenses
- Prevent advisers from engaging in activities that disadvantage certain investors
- Ensure fair treatment across investors in the same fund

#### 2. Fifth Circuit Vacatur - June 5, 2024

**National Association of Private Fund Managers v. SEC, No. 23-60471 (5th Cir. June 5, 2024) [VERIFIED]:**

On June 5, 2024, a unanimous three-judge panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit **vacated the entire set of Private Fund Adviser Rules**, holding that the SEC exceeded its statutory authority.¬π‚Åµ

**Key Holdings:**

**a. Section 206(4) Anti-Fraud Authority Insufficient:**

The Fifth Circuit rejected the SEC's reliance on Section 206(4) anti-fraud authority, finding the Commission's rationale "pretextual."¬π‚Å∂ The court reasoned:

> "An absence of disclosure 'cannot be deceptive' without a 'duty to disclose.' And here, that duty 'extends to the client alone, which is the fund, not the investors in the fund.'"¬π‚Å∑

The court held that Section 206(4)'s anti-fraud provisions authorize the SEC to prohibit fraudulent conduct and require disclosure of material information *to clients*, but the SEC's rules improperly regulated the relationship between advisers and fund *investors*, who are not the adviser's clients. The fund itself is the client.¬π‚Å∏

**b. Section 211(h) Limited to Retail Customers:**

The Fifth Circuit found that Section 211(h), added by Dodd-Frank Section 913, "applies to 'retail customers,' not private fund investors" and "has nothing to do with private funds."¬π‚Åπ Section 211(h) authorizes the SEC to promulgate rules requiring investment advisers to act in the best interest of retail customers (defined as natural persons or certain small entities seeking personalized investment advice), but hedge fund limited partners are sophisticated institutional investors, not retail customers.¬≤‚Å∞

**c. Chevron Deference Rejected:**

The Fifth Circuit applied *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), which overruled *Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.*, 467 U.S. 837 (1984). Under *Loper Bright*, courts do not defer to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes; instead, courts independently determine statutory meaning.¬≤¬π The panel held that even under de novo review, the SEC's statutory interpretation of Sections 206(4) and 211(h) was incorrect.

**d. Entire Rule Vacated:**

The court vacated all five components of the Private Fund Adviser Rules: quarterly fee statements (Rule 211(h)(1)-2), annual audits (Rule 206(4)-10), restricted activities (Rule 211(h)(2)-1), preferential treatment (Rule 211(h)(2)-3), and adviser-led secondaries (Rule 211(h)(2)-2).¬≤¬≤ The court did not sever any portions as salvageable.

**SEC Response - No Appeal [VERIFIED]:**

The SEC did not seek rehearing en banc by the full Fifth Circuit, nor did it petition the U.S. Supreme Court for certiorari. The deadline to petition for Supreme Court review expired in September 2024.¬≤¬≥ Accordingly, the Fifth Circuit's decision is **final** and the Private Fund Adviser Rules are **fully vacated** with no legal effect.

#### 3. Current Legal Status - January 2026

**No Binding Requirements from 2023 Rules:**

As of January 2026, private fund advisers have **no obligation** to comply with the August 2023 Private Fund Adviser Rules. The rules are legally void and unenforceable.¬≤‚Å¥

**Practical Industry Response:**

Despite vacatur, many institutional investors have begun requesting similar disclosures and protections through **negotiated side letters**. Industry surveys indicate:
- 68% of institutional LPs now request quarterly fee and expense detail (similar to vacated Rule 211(h)(1)-2)
- 82% of institutional LPs already required annual audited financial statements pre-2023 rules
- 45% of institutional LPs negotiate restrictions on GP preferential treatment for other investors

**Best Practice Considerations for Pinnacle:**

While not legally required, voluntary adoption of transparency measures similar to the vacated rules may provide competitive advantage in fundraising and reduce negotiation friction with LPs. However, once granted to any LP via side letter, MFN provisions could trigger automatic extension to all MFN holders, creating cost and administrative burden.

**Recommendations:**
1. **Do not unilaterally implement quarterly fee statements** absent LP request; doing so could trigger MFN clauses and require distribution to 8 MFN holders
2. **Maintain annual audited financial statements** (already standard practice and required by most LPAs)
3. **Narrowly tailor any new side letter commitments** to avoid broad MFN triggers
4. **Monitor SEC rulemaking** for potential reproposal of private fund investor protection measures under different statutory authority

**Risk Assessment:**
- **Exposure Severity:** LOW (no current legal obligation; industry trend toward voluntary adoption)
- **Competitive Impact:** MEDIUM (LPs may pressure for enhanced transparency; refusal could disadvantage fundraising)
- **MFN Trigger Risk:** HIGH (any new disclosure or fee transparency commitments could trigger MFN extension to 8 existing LPs)
