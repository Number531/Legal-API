#### 2. DOL Regulatory Interpretation â€“ 29 C.F.R. Â§ 2510.3-21

The Department of Labor's **Retirement Security Rule** (effective September 23, 2024) significantly **broadened** the definition of who qualifies as an investment advice fiduciary.

**Pre-2024 Framework (1975 Regulation):**
The 1975 regulation established a narrow **five-part test** requiring:
1. Advice provided on a **regular basis**
2. Pursuant to a **mutual agreement, arrangement, or understanding**
3. That advice would serve as a **primary basis** for investment decisions
4. Advice **individualized** to the plan's needs
5. For **direct or indirect compensation**

This test significantly narrowed ERISA Section 3(21)(A)(ii)'s "plain and expansive language." *See* [Federal Register, Definition of Investment Advice Fiduciary](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/04/25/2024-08065/retirement-security-rule-definition-of-an-investment-advice-fiduciary).

**2024 Updated Framework:**
The April 25, 2024 final rule **replaced** the five-part test with a **context-based standard**:

A person provides investment advice and becomes a fiduciary when:
- The person **makes professional investment recommendations** to retirement investors on a regular basis as part of their business, AND
- The recommendation is made under circumstances indicating to a **reasonable investor** that:
  - The recommendation is based on review of the investor's **particular needs**
  - It reflects **professional or expert judgment**
  - It may be **relied upon** as intended to advance the investor's **best interest**

OR

- The person **represents or acknowledges** acting as a fiduciary under ERISA Title I or Title II

*See* 29 C.F.R. Â§ 2510.3-21 (2024) [VERIFIED via [eCFR](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-29/subtitle-B/chapter-XXV/subchapter-B/part-2510/section-2510.3-21)].

#### 4. Continuing Duty to Monitor â€“ *Tibble v. Edison*

**Landmark Case:** *Tibble v. Edison Int'l*, 575 U.S. 523 (2015)

**Facts:** Plan beneficiaries sued fiduciaries for offering higher-priced **retail-class mutual funds** when materially identical lower-priced **institutional-class funds** were available.

**Issue:** Did ERISA's six-year statute of limitations bar claims when initial investment selection occurred >6 years before filing?

**Supreme Court Holding (Unanimous):**
ERISA fiduciaries have a **continuing duty to monitor** plan investments after initial selection. The duty to monitor is **separate and apart** from the initial duty to select prudent investments.

> "ERISA's fiduciary duty is 'derived from the common law of trusts,' which provides that a trustee has a **continuing duty**â€”separate and apart from the duty to exercise prudence in selecting investments at the outsetâ€”to **monitor, and remove imprudent, trust investments**."

575 U.S. at 528-29 [VERIFIED via [Justia - Tibble v. Edison](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/575/523/)].

**Implication:** A plaintiff's claim alleging breach of the continuing duty of prudence is timely if it occurred within six years of suit, **even if the initial investment decision was made earlier**.

**Application to Pinnacle:**
- Pinnacle must **continuously monitor** all investments in ERISA plan portfolios
- Must review whether lower-cost alternatives become available (e.g., retail vs. institutional share classes)
- Must document ongoing monitoring process with periodic reviews
- Failure to monitor creates ERISA fiduciary breach liability regardless of how long investment has been held

### C. Prohibited Transactions â€“ ERISA Section 406

#### 1. Statutory Prohibitions

**29 U.S.C. Â§ 1106(a) â€“ Transactions with Parties-in-Interest**

Section 406(a) prohibits a fiduciary from causing a plan to engage in a transaction if the fiduciary knows or should know the transaction constitutes:

**(a)(1) Direct or indirect:**
- **(A) Sale or exchange, or leasing**, of any property between the plan and a party in interest
- **(B) Lending of money or other extension of credit** between the plan and a party in interest
- **(C) Furnishing of goods, services, or facilities** between the plan and a party in interest
- **(D) Transfer to, or use by or for the benefit of** a party in interest, of any assets of the plan

29 U.S.C. Â§ 1106(a) [VERIFIED via [Cornell LII - 29 USC 1106](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/1106)].

**29 U.S.C. Â§ 1106(b) â€“ Self-Dealing and Conflicts**

Section 406(b) prohibits a fiduciary from:

- **(b)(1) Dealing with plan assets in his own interest** or for his own account
- **(b)(2) Acting in any transaction involving the plan on behalf of a party** (or representing a party) **whose interests are adverse** to the interests of the plan or its participants
- **(b)(3) Receiving any consideration for his own personal account** from any party dealing with the plan in connection with a transaction involving plan assets

#### 2. Cross-Trading as Prohibited Transaction

**General Rule:** Cross-trading between ERISA plans and other accounts managed by the same adviser is **prohibited** under Section 406(b)(2).

> "Generally, under Â§ 406(b)(2), 'cross-trades' where at least one of the accounts is an ERISA-covered account, will be prohibited."

*See* [Bloomberg Law, If You Cross-Trade Securities, Make Sure Not to Cross ERISA](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/us-law-week/if-you-cross-trade-securities-make-sure-not-to-cross-erisa).

**Rationale:** Section 406(b)(2) prohibits a fiduciary from "acting in any transaction involving the plan on behalf of a party (or representing a party) whose interests are adverse to the interests of the plan."

When an investment adviser executes a cross-trade:
- **ERISA Plan A** (seller) is a client
- **Client B** (buyer) is another client (hedge fund, separate account, or another plan)
- The **adviser represents both parties** with potentially adverse interests (one wants highest price, other wants lowest price)

This creates an **inherent conflict of interest** violating Section 406(b)(2).
