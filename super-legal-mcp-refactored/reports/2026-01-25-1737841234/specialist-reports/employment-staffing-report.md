## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This report analyzes employment and labor law compliance for Sunset Senior Living Group's 1,850-employee workforce (980 nursing staff) in connection with Silver Oak Healthcare's proposed $425M acquisition. The analysis addresses federal minimum staffing requirements, CMS regulatory developments, staff turnover crisis (85% CNA, 67% overall nursing), recruitment costs ($3.5M annually), retention strategies ($11.3M investment recommended), and Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act exposure for potential Orange County facility closure ($453K probability-weighted liability).

**CRITICAL REGULATORY DEVELOPMENT:** On December 3, 2025, CMS repealed the minimum staffing standards finalized in May 2024 (3.48 PPD total nursing, 0.55 RN PPD, 2.45 CNA PPD). Section 71111 of Public Law 119-21 prohibits CMS from implementing, administering, or enforcing these standards until after September 30, 2034. This eliminates the previously anticipated mandatory $4.3M annual staffing cost increase. **However**, this report concludes that quality-driven staffing increases remain a business necessity to address survey deficiencies (F880 citations at 25% of facilities), low star ratings (33% portfolio at 1-2 stars), and competitive disadvantage.

### Key Findings

**1. Federal Minimum Staffing Requirements ‚Äî Repeal Creates Strategic Choice**

Sunset currently staffs at 3.45 hours per patient day (PPD) total nursing (RN 0.45, LPN 0.80, CNA 2.20), based on 542,025 annual patient days across 12 facilities. Under legacy 42 CFR ¬ß 483.35 qualitative standards ("sufficient staff"), Sunset meets minimum RN 8-hours/day requirement at all facilities but faces F880 (insufficient staffing) citations at 25% of facilities where actual staffing falls below CMS case-mix expected levels (Henderson: 2.8 PPD actual vs. 3.6 PPD expected = 22% understaffed).

The May 2024 CMS final rule would have required 3.48 PPD total (0.55 RN minimum, 2.45 CNA minimum), necessitating 26 additional RN FTE and 65 additional CNA FTE at annual cost of $4.3M. The December 2025 repeal removes this mandate through 2034. **Sunset is no longer legally required to hire these 91 additional FTE absent federal regulation.**

**Strategic Implication:** Despite repeal, Sunset should voluntarily increase staffing to industry benchmark 3.48 PPD to:
- **Address survey compliance risks:** F880 citations create DPNA exposure ($500K-$1M per facility based on FY2024 history)
- **Improve star ratings:** Staffing component (20% of overall rating) currently drags down portfolio (average 2.6 staffing stars)
- **Compete for referrals:** Discharge planners/hospitals prefer well-staffed facilities (3.5+ PPD)
- **Reduce turnover:** Adequate staffing reduces workload stress, improves retention
- **Comply with state mandates:** California AB 1502 requires 3.5 PPD for 3 California facilities

**Recommended Quality-Driven Staffing Investment:** $4.3M-$4.7M annually (26 RN FTE @ $35/hour + 65 CNA FTE @ $18/hour + $332K one-time recruitment), generating $2M-$4M quality benefits (DPNA avoidance, star rating/occupancy gains, CMP reduction). **Net cost: $300K-$2.7M annually** (versus $52M current EBITDA = 0.6-5.2% impact).

#### 1. Final Rule CMS-3442-F (May 10, 2024)

**Regulatory Citation:** 89 Fed. Reg. 40876 (May 10, 2024)¬≥

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services published a final rule establishing the first-ever federal minimum quantitative staffing standards for long-term care facilities participating in Medicare and Medicaid programs.

**Minimum Staffing Standards Finalized:**

| Component | Minimum Requirement | Implementation Date (Non-Rural) | Implementation Date (Rural) |
|-----------|--------------------|---------------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Total Nursing Hours** | 3.48 PPD | May 11, 2026 | May 10, 2027 |
| **RN Direct Care** | 0.55 PPD minimum | May 11, 2026 | May 10, 2027 |
| **CNA Direct Care** | 2.45 PPD minimum | May 11, 2026 | May 10, 2027 |
| **24/7 RN Onsite** | RN onsite 24 hours/7 days (available for direct care) | May 11, 2026 | May 10, 2027 |

**Rationale (from CMS Final Rule):** CMS determined that minimum staffing standards were necessary to ensure safe, quality care based on evidence that staffing levels correlate with resident outcomes (pressure ulcers, falls, hospitalizations, mortality). CMS cited studies demonstrating that facilities with higher RN and CNA staffing have better clinical outcomes.‚Å¥

**Phase-In Schedule:** The final rule did not include a phased implementation approach; facilities were required to comply fully by the implementation dates (May 11, 2026 for non-rural, May 10, 2027 for rural).‚Åµ

**Exemptions and Hardship Waivers:** CMS finalized exemptions for facilities in areas with healthcare staff shortages (provider-to-population ratio ‚â•20% below national average), with facilities required to submit evidence to state survey agencies for exemption consideration.‚Å∂

#### 2. Repeal of Minimum Staffing Standards (December 3, 2025)

**CRITICAL DEVELOPMENT:** On December 3, 2025, CMS published a final rule **repealing** the minimum staffing standards established in the May 10, 2024 final rule.‚Å∑

**Statutory Basis for Repeal:** Section 71111 of Public Law 119-21 (signed July 4, 2025) prohibits CMS from implementing, administering, or enforcing the minimum staffing standards (42 CFR ¬ß 483.35(b)(1) [24/7 RN requirement] and ¬ß 483.35(c)(1) [0.55 RN PPD and 2.45 CNA PPD minimums]) **until after September 30, 2034.**‚Å∏

**Effective Date of Repeal:** The repeal rule was effective immediately upon publication (December 3, 2025), removing the minimum staffing requirements from 42 CFR ¬ß 483.35.‚Åπ

**What Remains in Effect:**
- ‚úÖ Enhanced Facility Assessment requirements (42 CFR ¬ß 483.70(e)) requiring facilities to assess resources and competencies needed to meet resident needs
- ‚úÖ Medicaid Institutional Payment Transparency Reporting requirements (42 CFR ¬ß 447.275) requiring states to report Medicaid payment data
- ‚ùå Quantitative staffing minimums (3.48 PPD total, 0.55 RN, 2.45 CNA) ‚Äî **REPEALED**
- ‚ùå 24/7 RN onsite requirement ‚Äî **REPEALED**

### G. WARN Act Analysis (Orange County Closure Scenario)

#### 1. Federal WARN Act Requirements (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 2101 et seq.)

**Statutory Framework:** The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act requires covered employers to provide 60 days' advance written notice before:
1. **Plant closing:** Permanent or temporary shutdown of single site, results in employment loss for ‚â•50 employees during any 30-day period¬≥‚Å¥
2. **Mass layoff:** Employment loss at single site for (a) ‚â•50 employees (if ‚â•33% of workforce), or (b) ‚â•500 employees (regardless of percentage)¬≥‚Åµ

**Covered Employers:** Private businesses and nonprofits with ‚â•100 full-time employees (excluding employees who worked <6 months in last 12 months and employees working <20 hours/week average).¬≥‚Å∂

**"Employment Loss" Definition:**
- Termination (other than for cause, voluntary departure, or retirement)
- Layoff exceeding 6 months
- Reduction in hours of work >50% during each month of any 6-month period¬≥‚Å∑

**Notice Recipients:**
1. Each affected employee (written individual notice)
2. State dislocated worker unit (state workforce agency)
3. Chief elected official of local government (mayor, county executive)
4. Union representatives (if applicable)¬≥‚Å∏

**Exceptions to 60-Day Notice:**
- **Faltering company exception:** Company actively seeking capital/business to avoid closure, and advance notice would preclude obtaining capital (applies ONLY to plant closings, not mass layoffs)¬≥‚Åπ
- **Unforeseeable business circumstances:** Sudden, dramatic, unexpected events beyond employer's control‚Å¥‚Å∞
- **Natural disaster:** Flood, earthquake, drought, storm, similar natural disaster‚Å¥¬π

**Penalties for Noncompliance:**
- **Back pay:** Up to 60 days' wages for each affected employee (covering period of violation, max 60 days)
- **Benefits:** Value of benefits employee would have received during violation period (health insurance, pension contributions, etc.)
- **Civil penalty:** Up to $500/day (payable to local government) if employer failed to notify local government‚Å¥¬≤

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

**1. CMS Minimum Staffing Rule Repeal Eliminates Mandatory $4.3M Annual Cost**

The December 2025 repeal of CMS minimum staffing standards (blocked until September 2034 by Public Law 119-21 ¬ß 71111) removes the previously anticipated regulatory mandate to hire 26 RN FTE + 65 CNA FTE. **However**, this does NOT eliminate the quality-driven business necessity for staffing increases. Sunset's F880 citations (insufficient staffing at 25% of facilities), low star ratings (33% at 1-2 stars), and high turnover (67% vs. 52% national) demonstrate that staffing deficiencies create survey compliance risks, occupancy loss, and competitive disadvantage **independent of federal mandate**.

**IMPLICATION:** Staffing increases should be treated as **quality improvement investment** (not regulatory compliance cost), with ROI measured via DPNA avoidance, star rating improvement, and occupancy gains. Estimated net cost: $4.3M-$6.4M annually after benefits.

**2. Turnover Crisis Demands Immediate Retention Intervention**

Sunset's 85% CNA turnover (vs. 42%-65% national) and 67% overall nursing turnover (vs. 52%-54% national) cost $3.5M annually in recruitment + agency staffing, plus unmeasured quality degradation. Root causes:
- **Wage gaps:** CNAs earn $16-$18/hour vs. $18-$22 market (10-22% below market)
- **Workload stress:** F880-cited facilities understaffed (2.8 PPD vs. 3.6 expected at Henderson)
- **Lack of career development:** No tuition reimbursement, limited CNA‚ÜíLPN‚ÜíRN pathways
- **Inadequate training:** 2-week orientation insufficient for high-acuity residents

**Retention investment of $11.3M annually** (wages $7.2M + benefits $3.7M + career development $402K) would reduce turnover to industry norms (60% CNA, 40% LPN, 30% RN), saving $817K recruitment costs + $2M-$4M quality benefits. **Net cost: $6.4M-$10.5M annually** (12-20% EBITDA impact).

**RECOMMENDATION:** Phase retention investment over 2 years (50% Year 1, 50% Year 2) to moderate EBITDA impact while demonstrating measurable turnover reduction to lenders/investors.

**3. Orange County WARN Act Exposure Manageable with Proper Planning**

If Orange County terminates (28% probability by March 2028 per SFF trajectory), Silver Oak faces WARN Act notice requirements for 155 employees. **Key risk:** CMS may issue 23-day immediate jeopardy termination notice, leaving insufficient time for 60-day WARN notice (shortfall liability $1.45M). **Probability-weighted WARN exposure: $453K.**
