## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview and Critical Finding

Silver Oak Healthcare's proposed $425 million acquisition of Sunset Senior Living Group faces substantial False Claims Act qui tam exposure stemming from the pending litigation *United States ex rel. Sarah Martinez v. Sunset Senior Living Group, LLC* (D. Ariz., filed May 2023, unsealed December 2024). This litigation represents the **second-largest transaction risk** after the Orange County Special Focus Facility termination threat, with theoretical treble damages plus civil monetary penalties ranging **$58.7 million to $77.2 million**. However, based on comprehensive precedent analysis, clinical judgment defense viability, and settlement multiplier trends, realistic resolution exposure is **$8 million to $15 million in settlement damages plus $4.5 million to $7 million in Corporate Integrity Agreement costs over five years**, yielding total all-in cost of **$11.5 million to $21 million**.

The Department of Justice U.S. Attorney's Office for the District of Arizona has been investigating since June 2023 through Civil Investigative Demands but has not yet decided whether to intervene. Based on damages magnitude, statistical population size (2,400 Medicare residents 2019-2022), and recent enforcement patterns, DOJ intervention is assessed at **60-65% probability** with decision expected Q1-Q2 2025. If DOJ declines intervention, relator-only settlement reduces exposure to $5-$8 million with no Corporate Integrity Agreement requirement, leveraging the relator's diminished prosecutorial resources and subpoena power.

### Three Categories of Alleged Violations

**1. Therapy Upcoding Under RUG-IV Payment System (Pre-October 2019)**

Relator Martinez alleges Sunset systematically manipulated therapy billing under the Resource Utilization Group version IV (RUG-IV) payment methodology that governed skilled nursing facility Medicare reimbursement until September 30, 2019. RUG-IV created powerful financial incentives to provide exactly 720 minutes of therapy per week to achieve "ultra-high" rehabilitation tier (RUX) classification, which increased daily reimbursement by $300-400 per resident compared to clinically appropriate lower tiers.

The complaint alleges facility administrators pressured therapy staff to provide medically unnecessary therapy to reach minute thresholds regardless of resident tolerance or clinical needs. Specific allegations include: (a) documentation inflation where 60-90 minute therapy sessions were recorded as 120-150 minutes; (b) residents received therapy far exceeding clinical assessments solely to maximize revenue; and (c) corporate policies directed rehabilitation directors to maintain high RUG scores through threshold targeting.

**Industry Context:** Sunset's alleged conduct mirrors an industry-wide RUG-IV gaming epidemic documented by the Office of Inspector General, which found 20% of all Medicare Part A skilled nursing claims were upcoded in 2009, causing $1.2 billion in improper payments. Recent settlements include Grand Healthcare System ($21.3 million, September 2024), Interface Rehab ($2 million for "unreasonable and unnecessary Ultra High" therapy at 11 facilities, April 2024), and Quality Therapy & Consultation ($9.7 million, June 2019). These precedents establish that targeting ultra-high therapy levels for revenue rather than medical necessity constitutes False Claims Act violations.

**2. Medically Unnecessary Services Under PDPM (October 2019-2022)**

On October 1, 2019, CMS replaced RUG-IV with the Patient-Driven Payment Model (PDPM) specifically to eliminate minute-based payment incentives. However, Martinez alleges Sunset adapted fraudulent practices to the new system by manipulating MDS assessments to inflate case-mix classifications. The complaint describes systematic classification of residents as requiring "extensive services" when "limited assistance" was clinically appropriate, particularly in the nursing component where higher acuity generates significantly higher daily rates.

Notably, a 2024 academic study found that skilled nursing facilities which "gamed" RUG-IV by targeting threshold minutes "quickly began upcoding patients under PDPM"—demonstrating that unscrupulous operators adapt billing fraud to new payment models rather than reforming compliance culture. This research finding directly supports the government's theory that Sunset's misconduct continued through 2022, three years after the RUG-IV system ended.

**3. Anti-Kickback Statute Violations — Medical Director Compensation**

The complaint identifies Dr. Robert Johnson, who received $15,000 monthly ($180,000 annually) as medical director at a Sunset facility from 2019-2023. Martinez alleges this compensation was excessive for minimal duties—"one meeting per month, approximately one hour, no other responsibilities"—and constituted remuneration to induce referrals in violation of 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b). Dr. Johnson reportedly referred 150+ Medicare patients annually to Sunset facilities, generating approximately $2.34 million in annual Medicare Part A revenue.

If Martinez's characterization is accurate (12 hours annually of work for $180,000), the effective hourly rate of $15,000/hour is indefensible under any fair market value analysis. However, if Sunset can demonstrate Dr. Johnson's actual duties included legitimate 8-10 hours weekly commitment (416-520 hours annually) for care planning, quality oversight, infection control, and policy review, the effective rate of $346-$433/hour falls within reasonable benchmarks for physician administrative work. This evidentiary dispute will be central to the Anti-Kickback defense.

### Clinical Judgment Defense — AseraCare Precedent

The Eleventh Circuit's decision in *United States ex rel. Paradies v. AseraCare, Inc.*, 912 F.3d 1205 (11th Cir. 2019), provides Sunset's strongest legal defense. In AseraCare, a hospice provider faced $200+ million exposure based on government expert physicians opining that patients were not terminally ill. After a jury verdict finding 104 of 123 claims false, the district judge granted judgment as a matter of law, holding that "a contradiction based on clinical judgment or opinion alone cannot constitute falsity under the FCA as a matter of law."

The Eleventh Circuit affirmed, establishing that a claim "cannot be 'false'—and thus cannot trigger FCA liability—if the underlying clinical judgment does not reflect an objective falsehood." The court held the government "must show something more than the mere difference of reasonable opinion" and that clinical judgment represents a reasonable interpretation as long as it falls within the range of professionally acceptable opinions. After eight years of litigation and favorable appellate ruling, AseraCare settled for $1 million (0.5% of claimed damages) with **no Corporate Integrity Agreement required**—an unprecedented outcome recognizing medical judgment cases differ from intentional fraud schemes.

**Application to Sunset:**

Therapy medical necessity determinations are inherently subjective. Whether a post-hip replacement resident needs 45 minutes or 120 minutes of physical therapy daily involves professional clinical assessment, not objective facts. Similarly, MDS classifications of "extensive services" versus "limited assistance" require professional evaluation of functional abilities, cognitive status, and rehabilitation potential. If Sunset's therapists exercised professional discretion in good faith, disagreement with government experts does not establish fraud under AseraCare.

**Critical Distinctions Weakening Defense:**

However, Sunset faces obstacles that distinguish this case from AseraCare's favorable outcome:

1. **Documentation Inflation:** If Martinez proves therapy minutes were fabricated (documenting 120 minutes when only 60 minutes provided), this constitutes objective falsity, not clinical disagreement
2. **Corporate Pressure:** Emails, policies, or testimony showing administrators pressured therapists to reach specific minute thresholds undermines good faith clinical judgment
3. **Statistical Clustering:** Analysis showing residents disproportionately concentrated at exactly 720 minutes (RUX threshold) suggests systematic gaming rather than clinical variation
4. **Post-PDPM Continuation:** Adapting fraudulent practices from RUG-IV to PDPM demonstrates intent to maximize revenue rather than genuine assessment differences

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. FALSE CLAIMS ACT ALLEGATIONS — THERAPY UPCODING (PRE-PDPM)

#### 1. RUG-IV Ultra-High Reimbursement Tier Manipulation

The Martinez qui tam complaint alleges that Sunset systematically manipulated therapy billing under the Resource Utilization Group version IV (RUG-IV) payment system, which governed skilled nursing facility Medicare reimbursement from October 2010 until September 30, 2019.¹ RUG-IV employed a minute-based payment methodology where reimbursement rates increased substantially when residents received therapy at specific threshold levels.

**Ultra-High RUG Category Requirements:**

The "ultra-high" rehabilitation tier (RUX) — one of the highest reimbursement levels — could be achieved when patients received 720 minutes or more of therapy per week.² Under RUG-IV, facilities had strong financial incentives to provide exactly 720 minutes of therapy regardless of medical necessity, as crossing the threshold could increase daily reimbursement by $300-400 per resident.³

**Alleged Fraudulent Practices at Sunset (2019-October 2019):**

According to relator Martinez, Sunset instructed therapy staff to provide unnecessary therapy to reach the 720-minute weekly quota for ultra-high tier classification. The complaint alleges:

- **Documentation Inflation:** Therapy sessions lasting 60-90 minutes were documented as 120-150 minutes to reach RUX tier thresholds
- **Medically Unnecessary Services:** Residents received therapy far exceeding clinical needs solely to maximize reimbursement
- **Corporate Pressure:** Facility administrators pressured rehabilitation directors to maintain high RUG scores regardless of resident tolerance or medical appropriateness

**Illustrative Example - "John Doe" Resident:**

The complaint describes a resident who underwent hip replacement surgery and was admitted to a Sunset facility for post-acute rehabilitation. Clinical assessment indicated the resident needed 45 minutes of physical therapy three times per week (135 minutes/week total). However, facility staff allegedly scheduled 120 minutes per day, six days per week (720 minutes/week) to reach the RUX tier. This resulted in:

- Billed rate: $850/day (RUX tier)
- Appropriate rate: $520/day (lower tier matching clinical needs)
- Overcharge: $330/day × 20-day Medicare stay = $6,600 per resident

#### 2. Industry-Wide RUG-IV Upcoding Problem

Sunset's alleged conduct reflects an industry-wide pattern documented by federal regulators and multiple False Claims Act prosecutions:

**OIG Findings:**

A 2012 Office of Inspector General report found approximately 20% of all Medicare Part A skilled nursing facility claims were upcoded to higher RUG groups in 2009, resulting in $1.2 billion in improper payments.⁴

**Recent Settlements and Judgments:**

- **Grand Healthcare System (September 2024):** Paid $21.3 million to settle allegations of billing for services that were "unreasonable, unnecessary, unskilled or that did not occur as billed."⁵

- **Interface Rehab (April 2024):** California-based therapy company agreed to pay $2 million to resolve allegations that it overbilled for services at 11 skilled nursing facilities, submitting Medicare Part A claims for medically "unreasonable and unnecessary Ultra High" therapy levels.⁶

- **Quality Therapy & Consultation Inc. (June 2019):** The company and four skilled nursing facilities agreed to pay $9.7 million to resolve allegations of "upcoding" patients' Resource Utilization Group scores.⁷

**Common Fraudulent Patterns Identified:**

Recent FCA complaints have established several recurring fraudulent practices:

- Systematic upcoding to higher RUG levels due to corporate "pressure" on front-line care providers
- Targeting therapy minutes at or around RUG threshold levels (e.g., exactly 720 minutes for ultra-high)
- Increasing therapy during "look back" periods when MDS assessments determine reimbursement rates
- Providing skilled therapy that was not required for functional improvement⁸

#### 3. Post-PDPM Continuation of Upcoding Practices

A 2024 academic study found that skilled nursing facilities that "gamed" RUG-IV by targeting threshold minutes quickly adapted to upcode under the Patient-Driven Payment Model (PDPM) after its October 1, 2019 implementation.⁹ This finding is particularly relevant to Sunset, as Martinez alleges fraudulent billing continued through 2022 — well into the PDPM era.

### B. MEDICALLY UNNECESSARY SERVICES UNDER PDPM (OCTOBER 2019-2022)

#### 1. PDPM Payment Model Transition

On October 1, 2019, CMS replaced RUG-IV with the Patient-Driven Payment Model (PDPM), fundamentally changing skilled nursing facility reimbursement.¹⁰ Unlike RUG-IV's minute-based system, PDPM classifies residents into payment categories based on clinical characteristics and care needs across five case-mix adjusted components:

- Physical therapy and occupational therapy (PT/OT)
- Speech-language pathology (SLP)
- Nursing
- Non-therapy ancillary services (NTA)
- Non-case-mix component

PDPM was designed to eliminate the perverse incentive to provide therapy solely to increase reimbursement. However, the Martinez complaint alleges Sunset continued fraudulent practices by manipulating MDS assessments to inflate case-mix classifications.

#### 2. Alleged Classification Manipulation

**"Extensive Services" vs. "Limited Assistance" Fraud:**

The complaint alleges that Sunset systematically classified residents as requiring "extensive services" when "limited assistance" was clinically appropriate. Under PDPM's variable per diem structure, higher acuity classifications generate significantly higher daily reimbursement rates, particularly in the nursing component.

**Illustrative Example - "Jane Smith" Resident:**

A post-stroke resident was admitted requiring intensive rehabilitation. According to the complaint:

- **MDS Documentation:** Facility documented "extensive" physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech-language pathology services for the entire 28-day Medicare Part A stay
- **Clinical Reality:** Progress notes indicate resident progressed rapidly and required only "limited" assistance after the first week

### C. ANTI-KICKBACK STATUTE VIOLATIONS — MEDICAL DIRECTOR COMPENSATION

#### 1. Dr. Robert Johnson Arrangement

The Martinez complaint identifies a specific physician compensation arrangement that allegedly violates the Anti-Kickback Statute, 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b):

**Compensation Structure:**
- Monthly payment: $15,000
- Annual compensation: $180,000
- Contract term: 2019-2023 (alleged ongoing)

**Alleged Minimal Duties:**
- One monthly meeting (approximately 1 hour)
- No other documented responsibilities
- Estimated time commitment: 12 hours annually

**Referral Volume:**
- Dr. Johnson referred 150+ Medicare patients per year to Sunset facilities
- Patients generated substantial Medicare Part A revenue at $625/day average × 25-day average stay = $15,625 per patient × 150 patients = $2.34 million annual revenue from Johnson referrals

**Relator's Kickback Theory:**

Martinez alleges the compensation was excessive for the services performed and constituted remuneration to induce referrals. At $15,000/month for 12 hours of work annually, the effective hourly rate would be $15,000/hour — far above any reasonable fair market value benchmark for medical director services.

#### 2. Anti-Kickback Statute Legal Framework

The Anti-Kickback Statute makes it a criminal offense to "knowingly and willfully" offer or pay "remuneration" to induce referrals of patients covered by federal healthcare programs.¹² Violations are felonies punishable by up to five years imprisonment, fines up to $25,000 per violation, and exclusion from federal healthcare programs.

**Safe Harbor Requirements:**

To qualify for the employment safe harbor under 42 CFR § 1001.952(i), compensation arrangements must satisfy:

1. Bona fide employment relationship
2. Compensation consistent with fair market value for services actually performed
3. Compensation not determined in a manner that accounts for the volume or value of referrals¹³

**Fair Market Value Standard:**

OIG guidance emphasizes that compensation "should be at fair market value for the work or service performed—not inflated to compensate for the physician's ability to generate other revenues."¹⁴ Compensation above the 75th percentile of market benchmarks raises red flags; compensation above the 90th percentile is presumptively excessive absent extraordinary justification.

#### 3. Comparable Anti-Kickback Precedents

**United States ex rel. Drakeford v. Tuomey Healthcare System:**

This landmark Fourth Circuit case involved part-time physician employment contracts with compensation above fair market value.¹⁵ Key holdings:

- **Jury Verdict (2013):** Found Tuomey submitted 21,000+ false claims stemming from Stark Law and Anti-Kickback Statute violations
- **Damages:** $237 million treble damages judgment (later settled for $72.4 million due to inability to pay)
- **Legal Principle:** Compensation above fair market value creates rebuttable presumption of intent to induce referrals; burden shifts to defendant to prove legitimate business purpose
- **Relator Award:** Whistleblower received $18.1 million (25% of settlement)

The Tuomey court emphasized that paying physicians "compensation that far exceeded fair market value and included part of the money [the hospital] received from Medicare for the referred procedures" violated both Stark Law and the Anti-Kickback Statute.¹⁶

**United States v. Halifax Hospital Medical Center:**

In 2014, Halifax Hospital in Florida settled FCA allegations stemming from neurosurgeon compensation arrangements for $85 million.¹⁷ The case involved:

- Physicians paid $500,000-$1,000,000 annually above fair market value
- Compensation structure designed to induce referrals
- Every claim submitted for services referred by overpaid physicians constituted a false claim
- Settlement represented approximately 2× multiplier on single damages

**Key Distinction - Halifax Anti-Kickback Outcome:**

Notably, the district court in Halifax ultimately dismissed the anti-kickback claims, finding the physicians qualified for the bona fide employment exception because they were controlled by the hospital rather than operating as independent contractors.¹⁸ However, the Stark Law violations remained, and the case settled for $85 million — demonstrating that technical safe harbor compliance does not eliminate settlement exposure when compensation arrangements are commercially unreasonable.

#### 4. Fair Market Value Benchmarking Analysis

**Sullivan Cotter Survey Data:**

Sullivan Cotter's Physician Compensation and Productivity Survey is the healthcare industry's leading fair market value benchmark, used in over 800 organizations covering 285,500+ physicians across 237 specialties.¹⁹ While specific medical director compensation data for skilled nursing facilities is proprietary, general industry benchmarks indicate:

- **100-150 bed SNF:** $100,000-$150,000 annually for 8-12 hours/week commitment
- **150+ bed SNF:** $150,000-$200,000 annually for comparable duties
- **Typical responsibilities:** Care planning meetings, quality committee, infection control oversight, staff education, policy review, on-call availability

**Dr. Johnson Compensation Analysis:**

At $180,000 annually, Dr. Johnson's compensation falls at the high end of the typical range. However, the critical weakness in Sunset's defense is the alleged minimal time commitment:

- **If duties are truly 1 hour/month (12 hours/year):** Effective rate of $15,000/hour is indefensible
- **If duties include legitimate 8-12 hours/week (416-624 hours/year):** $288-$433/hour is within reasonable range for physician administrative work

**Defense Strategy:**

Sunset's viability depends on demonstrating that Dr. Johnson's actual duties substantially exceeded the "one meeting per month" allegation. If contemporaneous time logs, meeting minutes, policy review documentation, and quality committee participation records show 8+ hours weekly, the arrangement becomes defensible.

### D. CLINICAL JUDGMENT DEFENSE — AseraCare Precedent

#### 1. United States ex rel. Paradies v. AseraCare — Landmark Medical Necessity Decision

The Eleventh Circuit's decision in *AseraCare* represents the most significant FCA defense victory in medical necessity cases and provides Sunset's strongest legal argument.²⁰

**Factual Background:**

Three former AseraCare employees filed a qui tam complaint alleging the hospice provider knowingly billed Medicare for patients who were not terminally ill (life expectancy exceeding six months). The relators presented expert physicians who reviewed medical records and opined that patients did not meet hospice eligibility criteria.

**Procedural History:**

- **Trial (2014):** Jury found 104 of 123 claims false, resulting in $200+ million potential exposure
- **Post-Trial:** District judge granted judgment as matter of law, holding clinical disagreement alone cannot establish falsity
- **Appeal (Eleventh Circuit 2019):** Affirmed, establishing strong medical judgment defense

**Key Holdings:**

The Eleventh Circuit held that a hospice claim "cannot be 'false'—and thus cannot trigger FCA liability—if the underlying clinical judgment does not reflect an objective falsehood."²¹ The court established:

1. **Mere Disagreement Insufficient:** "The Government must show something more than the mere difference of reasonable opinion concerning the prognosis of a patient's likely longevity"²²

2. **Objective Falsity Required:** Clinical judgment represents a reasonable interpretation of medical records as long as it falls within the range of professionally acceptable opinions

3. **Expert Battle Limitations:** Government cannot establish falsity solely by presenting competing expert opinions that disagree with treating physicians' assessments

**Settlement:**

After eight years of litigation and favorable appellate ruling, AseraCare settled for $1 million on a $200+ million claimed damages case — representing approximately 0.5% of potential exposure.²³ Critically, the settlement required no Corporate Integrity Agreement, preserving AseraCare's compliance reputation.

#### 2. Application to Sunset's Therapy Necessity Allegations

**Parallel Arguments:**

Sunset can assert identical legal theories to AseraCare:

- **Therapy Medical Necessity is Subjective:** Determining whether a post-acute resident needs 45 minutes or 120 minutes of physical therapy per day involves clinical judgment, not objective facts
- **MDS Classification Subjectivity:** Classifying a resident as "extensive services" vs. "limited assistance" requires professional assessment of functional abilities, cognitive status, and rehabilitation potential
- **Reasonable Disagreement:** If Sunset's therapists exercised professional discretion in good faith, disagreement with government experts does not establish fraud

**Critical Distinctions from AseraCare:**

However, Sunset faces several obstacles that weaken the clinical judgment defense:

1. **Documentation Inflation Allegations:** If Martinez can prove therapy minutes were fabricated (documenting 120 minutes when only 60 minutes provided), this constitutes objective falsity, not clinical disagreement

2. **Corporate Pressure Evidence:** Emails, policies, or testimony showing administrators pressured therapists to reach specific minute thresholds regardless of clinical assessment undermine good faith

3. **Pattern Evidence:** Statistical analysis showing Sunset residents disproportionately clustered at exactly 720 minutes (RUX threshold) suggests systematic gaming rather than clinical variation

4. **Post-PDPM Continuation:** Adapting fraudulent practices from RUG-IV to PDPM demonstrates intent to maximize revenue rather than genuine clinical assessment differences

#### 3. Burden of Proof Analysis

**Government's Initial Burden:**

Under AseraCare, the government must establish:
- Sunset's clinical assessments reflected "objective falsity"
- Services were not medically reasonable and necessary
- Sunset knew or should have known the services exceeded medical necessity

**Sunset's Defensive Burden:**

Once government presents prima facie case, Sunset must demonstrate:
- Therapists exercised independent professional judgment
- MDS assessments reflected genuine clinical evaluation
- Documentation accurately reflected services provided
- No corporate policies incentivized upcoding

**Evidentiary Battle:**

The case will likely turn on:
- Therapy staff testimony regarding corporate pressure and documentation practices
- Expert witness analysis of medical records and therapy appropriateness
- Statistical evidence of threshold clustering
- Internal emails and policy documents regarding revenue targets

### G. CORPORATE INTEGRITY AGREEMENT REQUIREMENTS AND COSTS

#### 1. CIA Overview and Prevalence

A Corporate Integrity Agreement (CIA) is a negotiated agreement between a healthcare provider and the Office of Inspector General (OIG) that establishes specific compliance obligations as part of an FCA settlement.²⁶ CIAs are extremely common in FCA settlements involving healthcare providers:

- **2005-2017 Analysis:** Approximately 50% of all OIG agreements involved three provider types: individual/small group practices, hospitals, and **skilled nursing facilities**²⁷
- **Standard Duration:** 5 years (some 3-year agreements for less serious violations)
- **Purpose:** Prevent future fraud through enhanced oversight, compliance infrastructure, and independent monitoring

#### 2. Standard CIA Components for Skilled Nursing Facilities

Based on OIG's published CIAs for comparable SNF therapy upcoding and medical necessity cases, Sunset would face:²⁸

**A. Independent Review Organization (IRO) Engagement**

Sunset must retain an IRO—typically a Big Four accounting firm (Deloitte, EY, PwC, KPMG) or specialized healthcare compliance consultancy—to conduct quarterly or semi-annual audits.

**IRO Audit Scope:**
- Claims review: 50-100 claims per facility per quarter (12 facilities × 75 claims average = 900 claims/quarter)
- Medical record review: IRO nurses and coders review therapy documentation, MDS assessments, physician certifications
- Billing compliance: Compare billed amounts to medical necessity supported by clinical documentation
- Findings reporting: IRO submits detailed reports to OIG identifying overpayments, compliance deficiencies, and systemic issues

**IRO Costs:**
- **Annual fees:** $500,000-$800,000 (depends on portfolio size, claim volume, complexity)
- **Five-year total:** $2.5M-$4M
- **Upfront implementation:** $200K-$400K (IRO selection, engagement terms, initial audit protocol development)

**B. Compliance Program Requirements**

CIAs mandate comprehensive compliance infrastructure:

1. **Compliance Officer:** Designated senior executive with direct board reporting, salary $150K-$250K annually
2. **Compliance Committee:** Board-level oversight committee, quarterly meetings
3. **Written Policies:** Detailed compliance policies covering billing, coding, medical necessity, physician arrangements
4. **Hotline:** Confidential reporting mechanism for employees to report suspected fraud
5. **Exclusion Screening:** Monthly checks of all employees/contractors against OIG exclusion list, SAM.gov

**Compliance Program Costs:**
- Compliance officer salary: $200K annually × 5 years = $1M
- Policy development: $100K-$150K (external consultants)
- Hotline service: $20K-$30K annually = $100K-$150K over 5 years
- **Subtotal:** $1.2M-$1.3M

**C. Training Requirements**

All employees must receive annual compliance training addressing the specific conduct that led to the FCA settlement:

- **Mandatory topics:** Therapy medical necessity, accurate MDS coding, physician compensation compliance, False Claims Act basics
- **Annual training:** All 1,850 employees (clinical staff, billing staff, administrators)
- **Tracking:** Attendance records, test scores, certification of completion
- **OIG-approved curriculum:** Must submit training materials to OIG for pre-approval

**Training Costs:**
- Curriculum development: $150K (initial year)
- Annual delivery: $100K-$150K (instructor time, materials, employee hours)
- **Five-year total:** $650K-$900K

**D. Reporting Obligations**

CIAs require extensive ongoing reporting to OIG:

1. **Reportable Events (within 30 days):**
   - Discovery of material overpayments (aggregate >$25K-$50K threshold)
   - Immediate jeopardy survey citations
   - Additional government investigations or legal proceedings
   - Changes in ownership or corporate structure

2. **Annual Implementation Reports:**
   - Detailed description of compliance program activities
   - Training completion statistics
   - Hotline utilization metrics
   - Corrective action implementation

3. **IRO Reports (quarterly or semi-annual):**
   - Claims audit findings
   - Overpayment calculations
   - Root cause analysis of deficiencies

**Reporting Costs:**
- Staff time: 0.5 FTE compliance analyst ($50K annually)
- Legal review: $50K-$100K annually (outside counsel review before submission)
- **Five-year total:** $500K-$750K

**E. Overpayment Repayment**

If IRO audits identify overpayments during the CIA term:
- Sunset must repay within 60 days of IRO report
- Interest accrues on delayed repayments
- Failure to repay triggers breach consequences

**F. Breach Consequences**

CIA violations result in:
- **Stipulated penalties:** $50,000-$100,000 per breach (e.g., failure to conduct training, late IRO report submission)
- **Material breach:** OIG may impose exclusion from Medicare/Medicaid participation, effectively shutting down the SNF business

#### 2. Federal Discharge Protection Framework — 42 CFR § 483.15(c)

**Permitted Discharge Reasons (Exclusive List):**

A facility may only transfer or discharge a resident when:³⁰

1. Transfer/discharge necessary for resident's welfare, and facility cannot meet resident's needs
2. Resident's health has improved sufficiently, and resident no longer needs facility services
3. Safety or health of other individuals would be endangered
4. Resident failed to pay after reasonable notice
5. Facility ceases to operate

**Required Demonstration:**

For behavioral discharges under reason #3, facility must document:³¹
- Specific incidents demonstrating danger to others
- Clinical assessment of behavioral triggers
- Interventions attempted (medication adjustment, behavioral plan, environmental modifications)
- Why facility cannot manage behaviors with available resources

**Economic Motivation Prohibition:**

Facilities cannot discharge residents because:
- Medicaid reimbursement is lower than Medicare or private pay
- Resident requires "too much" nursing or aide time relative to reimbursement
- Facility prefers to admit higher-paying residents

#### 3. Comparable Case Law

While the search did not locate "Bethel Nursing Facility v. Arkansas Dep't of Human Services," the principle is well-established across jurisdictions:

**Legal Standard:**

Courts uniformly hold that economic considerations are impermissible grounds for nursing home discharge. The burden of proof rests on the facility to demonstrate medical necessity or safety justification.³²

**California-Specific Precedent:**

California has particularly strong resident protections. Health & Safety Code § 1599.1 prohibits discrimination against Medicaid beneficiaries in admission, transfer, or discharge decisions.³³ Facilities that systematically discharge Medicaid residents face:
- License suspension or revocation (California Department of Public Health enforcement)
- State False Claims Act liability (California Government Code § 12650 et seq.)
- Civil penalties up to $100,000 per violation

### I. DOJ INTERVENTION DECISION AND TIMING

#### 1. Intervention Decision Framework

Under the False Claims Act, 31 U.S.C. § 3730(b)(2), qui tam complaints are filed under seal for a minimum of 60 days to allow the government to investigate and decide whether to intervene.³⁴ In complex healthcare cases, this seal period typically extends 6-12 months or longer.

**Martinez Timeline:**
- Complaint filed: May 2023 (under seal)
- Investigation commenced: June 2023 (CIDs issued)
- Unsealed: December 2024 (19 months after filing)
- Intervention decision: Expected Q1-Q2 2025

**Significance of Unsealing Without Intervention Decision:**

The December 2024 unsealing before DOJ announced intervention decision suggests:
- Investigation remains active (DOJ has not declined)
- Government needs additional time for analysis
- Potential settlement discussions may be occurring

#### 2. Intervention Statistics and Implications

**National DOJ Intervention Rates (2020-2024):**³⁵

- Healthcare qui tam cases: 22-25% intervention rate
- SNF/therapy cases specifically: 30-35% intervention rate (higher due to statistical analysis viability)
- Overall qui tam filings: 20-23% intervention rate

**Intervention Factors Favoring Government:**

Martinez case presents strong intervention indicators:
1. **Statistical Evidence:** 2,400 Medicare admissions over 4 years provides robust sample for statistical sampling and extrapolation
2. **Document Production:** Sunset has produced extensive documentation in response to CIDs (billing records, policies, MDS assessments)
3. **Relator Credibility:** Martinez was Director of Rehabilitation with direct oversight of therapy billing, providing insider knowledge
4. **Precedent Strength:** Multiple recent settlements in similar SNF therapy upcoding cases demonstrate DOJ success pattern
5. **Damages Magnitude:** $13.4M-$40.2M potential recovery justifies DOJ resource allocation

**Intervention Factors Favoring Declination:**

1. **Clinical Judgment Defense:** AseraCare precedent creates litigation risk if case relies solely on expert disagreement
2. **PDPM Transition Complexity:** Shift from RUG-IV to PDPM mid-investigation period complicates damages calculation
3. **No Criminal Referral:** No indication of parallel criminal investigation suggests conduct may be viewed as aggressive billing rather than criminal fraud

**Intervention Probability Assessment:** **60-65%** (above national average due to damages magnitude and precedent strength)

#### 3. Declination Scenarios

**Scenario A: Full Declination**

If DOJ declines intervention, Martinez proceeds alone as relator. Implications:

- **Settlement leverage reduced:** Relator lacks government's resources and subpoena power
- **Settlement range:** $3M-$8M (defendants discount heavily without government backing)
- **No CIA required:** Relators cannot impose Corporate Integrity Agreements (OIG prerogative)
- **Litigation risk lower:** Relators rarely take medical necessity cases to trial due to AseraCare litigation expense

**Scenario B: Partial Intervention**

DOJ may intervene on certain claims (e.g., Anti-Kickback violations) while declining therapy medical necessity claims. This hybrid approach allows:

- Government prosecutes strongest claims (Dr. Johnson kickback allegations)
- Relator proceeds alone on weaker clinical judgment claims
- Settlement bifurcated by claim type

**Scenario C: Deferred Intervention**

DOJ may decline initially but retain right to intervene later under 31 U.S.C. § 3730(c)(3) if relator's litigation develops favorable evidence. This preserves government's option while avoiding immediate resource commitment.

### J. DEFENSE STRATEGIES AND MITIGATION OPTIONS

#### 1. Clinical Judgment Defense (Primary Strategy)

**Implementation:**

- Retain defense expert physicians/therapists to review medical records
- Demonstrate therapy assessments reflected reasonable professional judgment
- Emphasize subjective nature of MDS classifications (extensive vs. limited)
- Distinguish from objective falsity (fabricated therapy minutes)

**Evidentiary Support:**

- Therapist testimony: Staff exercised independent clinical discretion
- Facility policies: No quotas or minute targets imposed
- Resident outcomes: Therapy resulted in functional improvements (supports medical necessity)
- Peer comparison: Sunset's therapy utilization aligns with regional norms

**Limitations:**

Defense fails if government proves:
- Documentation fabrication (therapy minutes recorded but not provided)
- Corporate policies directing minimum minute targets
- Statistical clustering at exactly 720 minutes (RUX threshold)

#### 2. Fair Market Value Defense (Medical Director Arrangement)

**Implementation:**

- Retain healthcare compensation expert (Sullivan Cotter, Mercer, BDO)
- Conduct retrospective FMV analysis of Dr. Johnson arrangement
- Document actual duties performed (time logs, meeting minutes, policy reviews)
- Compare to benchmark data for medical directors at 150+ bed SNFs

**Target Outcome:**

Demonstrate Dr. Johnson compensation of $180,000 annually falls within 75th percentile of market benchmarks for documented duties. If actual time commitment was 8-10 hours/week (416-520 hours/year), effective rate of $346-$433/hour is reasonable for physician administrative work.

**Remediation Steps:**

- Prospectively reduce compensation to 50th percentile benchmark ($140K-$160K)
- Enhance time-tracking requirements (monthly hour logs)
- Update contract language to explicitly disclaim referral-based compensation
- Apply to all 12 facilities' medical director contracts

#### 3. Voluntary Disclosure and Cooperation

**Self-Disclosure to OIG:**

Even though qui tam already filed, Sunset can engage OIG Self-Disclosure Protocol (SDP) for conduct discovered beyond Martinez allegations.³⁶ Benefits:

- Demonstrates good faith commitment to compliance
- May reduce settlement multiplier (1.5× vs. 1.75-2×)
- Potentially avoid CIA if disclosure shows robust remediation

**Cooperation Elements:**

- Provide fulsome document production beyond CID requirements
- Conduct internal investigation and share findings (within privilege limits)
- Implement prospective compliance enhancements (training, policies, audit protocols)
- Discipline or terminate employees involved in misconduct

#### 4. Relator Credibility Challenges

**Investigation of Relator:**

- Review Martinez personnel file: disciplinary history, performance reviews, termination justification
- Interview former colleagues regarding Martinez's credibility and job performance
- Examine Martinez's therapy billing decisions during tenure (did she participate in alleged conduct?)

**Retaliation Defense:**

If Sunset terminated Martinez for legitimate performance reasons unrelated to whistleblowing, this undermines her credibility and weakens retaliation claim under 31 U.S.C. § 3730(h).

#### 5. Statistical Sampling Challenges

**Methodology Attacks:**

If government employs statistical extrapolation (common in large-population cases):

- Challenge sample representativeness
- Dispute universe definition (which residents properly included in population?)
- Contest overpayment calculation methodology (appropriate comparison tier)
- Demand claim-by-claim review rather than extrapolation (Eleventh Circuit permits extrapolation, but methodology must be statistically sound)³⁷
