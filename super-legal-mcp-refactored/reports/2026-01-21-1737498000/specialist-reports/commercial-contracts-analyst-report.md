## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview of Findings

Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) maintains a **four-treaty reinsurance program** totaling approximately $11.7 billion in face amount ceded, designed to manage capital efficiency, mortality risk volatility, and regulatory reserve requirements. American Financial Holdings LLC's $2.9 billion acquisition of LLIC triggers **material reinsurance contract implications** requiring careful transaction structuring, reinsurer consent management, and long-term capital planning.

This report analyzes each reinsurance treaty's structure, counterparty credit risk, assignment consent requirements, recapture provisions, and RBC capital impact. The analysis identifies **three critical transaction risks**: (1) reinsurer consent denial or unacceptable conditions requiring $300M-$900M recapture capital, (2) Vermont captive regulatory disallowance creating $730M surplus reduction (RBC ratio crashing to 114%), and (3) Global Re term life recapture eligibility beginning 2030 requiring strategic capital planning through the acquisition's first five years.

**Key recommendation:** The purchase agreement must condition closing on obtaining reinsurer consents (Global Re, Swiss Re, Munich Re) without materially burdensome terms, and must resolve the Vermont captive structure through one of three mechanisms: (a) captive transfers to acquirer with replacement $730M parental guarantee, (b) Seller posts $400M LOC backstop and maintains for 5 years post-closing, or (c) $730M purchase price reduction reflecting loss of reserve credit.

### The Four Reinsurance Treaties â€” Structure and Purpose

**Treaty 1: Global Reassurance Ltd. (Bermuda) â€” Term Life Coinsurance**

Global Re assumes **90% quota share** of LLIC's new issue term life products (20-year and 30-year level term policies issued 2020-2024), totaling **$8.5 billion face amount** ceded (LLIC retains 10% = $850M). The treaty provides LLIC with:
- **Ceding commission** of 85% of premiums ceded, offsetting LLIC's sales commissions (100-120% of first-year premium) and deferred acquisition costs (DAC)
- **Experience refunds** of 50% of profits if mortality experience favorable (actual claims < expected)
- **Recapture rights** after 10 years (first cohort eligible 2030)

**Collateral:** Global Re posts **$850M letter of credit** from Barclays Bank (evergreen with 90-day non-renewal notice) securing LLIC's reserve credit. As an **unauthorized reinsurer** (Bermuda-domiciled, not licensed in Nebraska), Global Re must provide 100% collateral under Nebraska Revised Statutes Â§ 44-416.06 and NAIC Model Law #785 (adopted in 56 jurisdictions). Without the LOC, LLIC would be required to maintain full $850M reserves on its statutory balance sheet, reducing surplus and RBC ratio.

**Credit Rating:** A- (Excellent) by A.M. Best. Three notches below Swiss Re and Munich Re's A+ (Superior) ratings, indicating relatively higher credit risk. Bermuda Monetary Authority regulates Global Re under risk-based capital framework (BSCR with minimum Target Capital Level 120% of Enhanced Capital Requirement, typically 150%+ in practice for strong ratings).

**Treaty 2: Swiss Re â€” Indexed Universal Life Modified Coinsurance**

Swiss Re assumes **50% quota share** of LLIC's indexed universal life (IUL) products issued 2018-2024, totaling **$3.2B face amount** ceded (total IUL block $6.4B, LLIC retains 50%). Unlike traditional coinsurance, the **modified coinsurance structure** separates asset ownership from risk transfer:
- **Assets:** LLIC retains assets backing IUL policies and continues managing investment portfolio (earns investment spreads on equity index-linked portfolios)
- **Risk:** Swiss Re assumes mortality risk, receives mortality charges from policies, pays death claims
- **Quarterly Experience Adjustment:** Swiss Re and LLIC settle quarterly to true up economics: Investment Income - Mortality Charges + Death Claims + Reserve Adjustment = net settlement (Swiss Re pays LLIC if positive, LLIC pays Swiss Re if negative)

**Authorized Reinsurer:** Swiss Re is licensed in Nebraska, requiring **no collateral** (no LOC, no trust fund). Full reserve credit allowed without security under Neb. Rev. Stat. Â§ 44-416.06(1).

**Credit Rating:** A+ (Superior) by A.M. Best. Swiss Re is a **Global Systemically Important Insurer (G-SII)** designated by the Financial Stability Board, indicating exceptional financial strength, operating since 1863, with minimal insolvency risk (<0.5% probability based on rating agency historical default rates).

**Treaty 3: Munich Re â€” Group Term Life Yearly Renewable Term (YRT)**

Munich Re covers **excess of retention** on LLIC's group term life insurance, with LLIC retaining first **$250,000 per insured life** and Munich Re covering **excess up to $5,000,000 per life** (maximum reinsurance $4.75M per life). The YRT treaty protects LLIC against large claim volatility on high-earning executives and key employees covered under employer-sponsored group life insurance.

**Premium Structure:** LLIC pays YRT premiums to Munich Re based on net amount at risk (face amount > $250K retention) Ã— age-based rate per $1,000. Premiums adjust annually based on mortality experience, insured age progression, and Munich Re's capital costs.

**Purpose:** Smooths earnings volatility from occasional jumbo claims ($1M-$5M). Without YRT treaty, two $3M and $4M claims in a single quarter would cost LLIC $7M; with YRT, LLIC pays $500K ($250K per claim) plus annual YRT premiums, reducing volatility by ~90%.

**Authorized Reinsurer:** Munich Reinsurance America, Inc. licensed in Nebraska, requiring **no collateral**.

**Credit Rating:** A+ (Superior) by A.M. Best. Munich Re is the **world's largest reinsurer by premium volume**, designated G-SII, operating since 1880, with minimal insolvency risk.

**Treaty 4: Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC (Vermont Captive) â€” AXXX/XXX Redundant Reserves**

Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC (affiliate captive controlled by Liberty Life Holdings, the Seller) assumes **100% coinsurance** of LLIC's AXXX/XXX redundant reserves on pre-2017 term life and universal life with secondary guarantees (ULSG), totaling **$850M reserves** ceded.

**Captive Structure:**

### Change of Control â€” Assignment Consent Requirements

**All Four Reinsurance Treaties Require Reinsurer Consent**

American Financial Holdings' acquisition of LLIC constitutes an **assignment** of LLIC's reinsurance treaties under standard reinsurance contract provisions: *"Neither the Company nor the Reinsurer may assign any of the rights and obligations under this Agreement without the prior written consent of the other party. Consent shall not be unreasonably withheld if the assignment does not have a material effect on the risks transferred or the expected economic results."* This protects reinsurers' interests by allowing evaluation of the new owner's financial strength, business strategy, and reinsurance program continuity.

**Reinsurer-by-Reinsurer Consent Analysis:**

**1. Global Reassurance Ltd. â€” Consent Complexity HIGH**

**Timeline:** 60-90 days from consent request to decision

**Evaluation Factors:**
- **Financial Strength of Acquirer:** American Financial Holdings is PE-backed; Global Re will evaluate consolidated group capital adequacy, commitment to $150M capital injection for LLIC (RBC ratio 188% â†’ 204%), and track record in insurance acquisitions
- **Recapture Risk:** First cohort ($1.7B face amount) eligible for recapture in 2030. If mortality experience favorable 2020-2030, LLIC may exercise recapture to retain 100% of future profits (vs. 50% experience refund under treaty). Global Re may lose profitable business shortly after new owner takes control.
- **Collateral Stability:** $850M LOC from Barclays secures Global Re's position; less critical than unsecured arrangements but renewal risk exists

**Anticipated Consent Conditions:**
- **Anti-recapture provisions:** Extend recapture period from 10 years to 15 years, or eliminate recapture option entirely in exchange for improved ceding commission (87-90% vs. current 85%) or better experience refund terms (60% to LLIC vs. 50%)
- **Capital maintenance covenant:** LLIC RBC ratio must remain >200% post-acquisition; if falls below 200%, Global Re may increase collateral requirement or exercise termination rights

**Risk if Consent Denied:**

**2. Swiss Re â€” Consent Complexity MEDIUM**

**Timeline:** 45-60 days (faster than Global Re due to lower risk profile)

**Evaluation Factors:**
- **Authorized Reinsurer Advantage:** Swiss Re has no collateral requirement and minimal counterparty risk exposure to LLIC; consent less critical than for Global Re

Increases earnings volatility from jumbo claims but does not trigger immediate capital impact (YRT premiums paid by LLIC would cease, offsetting claim payments on excess losses). **Low materiality** compared to Global Re and Swiss Re consent risks.

### B. Reinsurance in Life Insurance Acquisitions

Reinsurance treaties are material contracts in life insurance company acquisitions for several reasons:

**Capital Management:** Reinsurance reduces statutory reserves on the ceding company's balance sheet, improving RBC ratios and surplus position. Recapture of reinsured business (taking back business from the reinsurer) reverses these benefits and can materially impact capital adequacy.

**Assignment and Change of Control:** Most reinsurance treaties contain provisions requiring reinsurer consent to assignment when the ceding company changes ownership. This protects reinsurers' interests by allowing them to evaluate the new owner's financial strength, business strategy, and reinsurance program continuity.

**Counterparty Risk:** Ceding companies rely on reinsurers to pay death claims and maintain collateral. If a reinsurer becomes insolvent or is downgraded, the ceding company faces increased risk and potential collateral shortfalls.

**Recapture Provisions:** Many reinsurance treaties allow the ceding company to recapture business after a specified period (often 10 years). Recapture decisions are strategic, balancing profitability of the reinsured block against capital implications of reestablishing reserves.

### C. Modified Coinsurance Treaty â€” Swiss Re (Authorized Reinsurer)

#### 1. Treaty Structure and Mod-Co Mechanics

**Treaty Type:** 50% quota share modified coinsurance on indexed universal life (IUL) products issued 2018-2024

**Face Amount Ceded:** $3.2B (total IUL block $6.4B, LLIC retains 50%)

**Modified Coinsurance Structure:**

Unlike traditional coinsurance (where the reinsurer receives premiums, pays claims, and holds assets backing reserves), modified coinsurance (mod-co) separates asset ownership from risk transfer:

**Assets:** LLIC retains assets backing the reinsured IUL policies and continues managing the investment portfolio
**Risk:** Swiss Re assumes mortality risk, receives mortality charges from policies, and pays death claims
**Reserves:** Swiss Re establishes statutory reserves on its books, but the assets supporting those reserves remain with LLICÂ¹â¹

**Economic Rationale:**

Mod-co allows LLIC to retain investment management control (continuing to earn investment spreads on the assets backing IUL policies) while transferring mortality risk to Swiss Re. This is particularly valuable for IUL products where investment returns are linked to equity index performance and asset management is a core competency of LLIC.Â²â°

#### 2. Experience Adjustment Mechanism

**Quarterly Settlement Process:**

Under the mod-co structure, LLIC and Swiss Re perform quarterly experience adjustments to true up the economics as if Swiss Re held the assets:Â²Â¹

**LLIC Calculates:**
```
Experience Adjustment = Investment Income Earned on Backing Assets
                       - Mortality Charges Received by Swiss Re
                       + Death Claims Paid by Swiss Re
                       + Mod-Co Reserve Adjustment
```

**Reserve Adjustment Component:**
```
Mod-Co Reserve Adjustment = Ending Policy Reserves
                           - Beginning Policy Reserves
                           - Interest on Beginning Reserves
```

**Settlement Direction:**
- If Experience Adjustment is **positive** (investment income exceeds net mortality experience), Swiss Re pays LLIC
- If Experience Adjustment is **negative** (net mortality experience exceeds investment income), LLIC pays Swiss Re

**Example (Simplified):**

Quarter 1:
- LLIC earns $50M investment income on assets backing Swiss Re's 50% quota share
- Swiss Re receives $15M mortality charges from policyholders
- Swiss Re pays $12M death claims
- Mod-co reserve adjustment: +$8M (reserves increased due to new premiums)

Experience Adjustment = $50M - $15M + $12M + $8M = **$55M**

Swiss Re pays LLIC $55M to compensate for investment income earned on Swiss Re's share of assets.

### C. Recapture Risk â€” Strategic and Capital Implications

#### 1. Recapture Decision Framework

Life insurers evaluate recapture decisions based on multiple factors:â´â¸

**Profitability Analysis:**
- **Favorable Experience:** Cedents recapture when business has proved more profitable than original expectations or mortality experience is better than pricedâ´â¹
- **Rising Interest Rates:** Prolonged period of rising rates can change financial dynamics; if insurer can generate higher investment returns on retained assets, paying reinsurance premiums becomes less appealingâµâ°
- **Experience Refunds:** Under Global Re treaty, LLIC receives 50% of profits; recapture allows retaining 100% of future profits

**Capital Requirements Impact:**
- **Reserve Reestablishment:** Reinsurance assets are derecognized and reserves are reestablished on ceding company balance sheetâµÂ¹
- **RBC Impact:** Capital requirements increase to reflect risks originally managed by reinsurer; weakens capital ratio as agreement's capital relief is undoneâµÂ²
- **Asset Adequacy Risk:** Recaptured assets may not align with investment strategy or regulatory requirements, potentially causing lossesâµÂ³

**Counterparty Risk Mitigation:**
- If reinsurer faces financial instability (rating downgrade, regulatory intervention), insurer may recapture to mitigate counterparty riskâµâ´

### Appendix D: Glossary of Reinsurance Terms

**Ceding Commission:** Amount paid by reinsurer to ceding company, typically as percentage of premiums ceded, designed to offset ceding company's acquisition costs (sales commissions, underwriting expenses, policy issuance costs).

**Coinsurance:** Reinsurance arrangement where reinsurer receives premiums, pays proportionate share of claims, establishes reserves, and holds assets backing reserves.

**Experience Refund:** Profit-sharing provision in reinsurance treaty; if actual experience (mortality, lapses, expenses) is better than expected, reinsurer refunds portion of profits to ceding company.

**Modified Coinsurance (Mod-Co):** Reinsurance arrangement where reinsurer assumes risk but ceding company retains assets; quarterly experience adjustments true up economics as if reinsurer held assets.

**Net Amount at Risk (NAAR):** Face amount of insurance minus policy reserve; for YRT reinsurance, premium based on NAAR.

**Quota Share:** Proportional reinsurance where reinsurer assumes fixed percentage of all policies (e.g., 90% quota share = reinsurer takes 90% of premiums and claims on every policy).

**Recapture:** Ceding company's exercise of contractual right to take back reinsured business from reinsurer, typically after specified period (e.g., 10 years); triggers reserve reestablishment on ceding company's balance sheet.

**Reserve Credit:** Statutory accounting treatment allowing ceding company to reduce reserves on balance sheet for business ceded to reinsurer; requires collateral if reinsurer is unauthorized.

**Yearly Renewable Term (YRT):** Reinsurance premium structure where premium adjusts annually based on insured's age and net amount at risk; commonly used for excess-of-retention group life reinsurance.
