**GAAP Philosophy:** Focuses on measuring earnings and providing information for investor decision-making; emphasizes going-concern value; matches revenues with expenses over time.

**SAP Philosophy:** Addresses state insurance regulator concerns; focuses on measuring ability to pay future claims (solvency); emphasizes liquidation value; applies "reasonably conservative" assumptions "over the span of economic cycles" per NAIC Statement of Concepts.

**5. RBC Decline from 245% (2019) to 188% (2024) Reflects Six Converging Factors Affecting Life Insurers Industry-Wide and Company-Specific Challenges**

The 57-percentage-point RBC deterioration over five years resulted from multiple systemic and idiosyncratic factors. Actuarial decomposition analysis attributes the decline as follows:

**Factor 1: Investment Portfolio Yield Compression (2019-2022) â€” 26-35% of Decline**

Life insurers' earnings on annuities and cash value life insurance products depend on the spread between portfolio yields and guaranteed crediting rates. The Federal Reserve's response to COVID-19 (cutting rates to near-zero in March 2020 and maintaining accommodative policy through 2021) compressed this spread dramatically. Industry data shows the spread between insurers' net investment portfolio yield and guaranteed interest rates plummeted from 1.1% in 2018 to 0.63% in 2020, with only slight recovery in 2021.

**LLIC Impact:**
- 2019 portfolio yield: 5.2%
- 2024 portfolio yield: 4.2%
- **Decline: 100 basis points (1.0%)**

**RBC Mechanism:** Lower investment yields reduce net investment income, which flows through to statutory net income and retained earnings, slowing Total Adjusted Capital accumulation. Additionally, lower portfolio yields relative to policy reserve discount rates increase the present value of liabilities, requiring higher reserves and increasing C2/C3 risk charges.

**Reaching for Yield Consequences:** Searching for yield in the low-rate environment, US life insurance companies became more susceptible to rating migration. Increased investment allocation to Baa-rated bonds (lowest investment-grade tier) rose to over one-third of total bond holdings from a little over one-quarter a decade earlier. LLIC's below-investment-grade bond exposure increased to $1.02 billion (7% of portfolio) by 2024, elevating C1 asset risk charges in the RBC formula.

**Factor 2: Interest Rate Volatility and Duration Mismatch (2022-2024) â€” 18-26% of Decline**

The Federal Reserve's aggressive monetary tightening in 2022-2024 (raising rates from near-zero to 5.25-5.50%) created interest rate volatility that impacted insurers with asset-liability duration mismatches. LLIC's balance sheet exhibits a **"negative duration gap"** (assets 10.8 years vs. liabilities 11.5 years = -0.7 year gap), meaning liabilities are of longer maturity than assets. When bond yields rise, the discounted present value of liabilities declines more slowly than asset values, creating economic losses not immediately reflected in statutory accounting (due to amortized cost treatment of bonds) but elevating future interest rate sensitivity.

**LLIC Unrealized Bond Losses:** When interest rates rose 2022-2024, existing bond portfolios experienced significant mark-to-market losses. LLIC recognized -$185 million in unrealized losses in GAAP financial statements (recorded in Other Comprehensive Income). While these losses are **not reflected in statutory surplus** due to SAP's amortized cost accounting, they represent economic capital erosion that would materialize if bonds were sold before maturity.

**Yield Curve Inversion (Late 2022-2023):** The inversion of the Treasury yield curve imposed a higher discount rate for life insurers' fixed income assets than for their liabilities, partially offsetting the benefit from higher nominal rates and creating additional economic strain.

**Factor 3: Variable Annuity GMWB Hedging Losses (2022-2023) â€” 9% of Decline**

**Hedging Program Challenges:** When interest rates rise, insurers incur losses on derivatives' market value and must post variation margins (collateral), creating liquidity pressure. LLIC's cumulative GMWB hedging losses totaled **$46 million** during 2022-2023, suggesting the hedging program may be suboptimal (static hedging vs. dynamic rebalancing, basis risk between hedge instruments and actual liabilities, or inadequate hedge ratios).

**Market Volatility Impact:** In high-volatility markets (S&P 500 declined -18% in 2022, followed by recovery volatility in 2023), insurers bear heavier liabilities from GMWB riders. ### E. Causes of RBC Decline (2019-2024): 245% â†’ 188%

Liberty Life Insurance Company's RBC ratio declined from 245% in 2019 to 188% in 2024, a **57 percentage point deterioration** that triggered Company Action Level status. The decline reflects multiple converging factors affecting life insurers during this period.

#### 1. Investment Portfolio Yield Compression (2019-2022)

**Impact on Earnings:** Life insurers' earnings on products like annuities and cash value life insurance depend on the spread between portfolio yields and guaranteed crediting rates. The spread between insurers' net investment portfolio yield and guaranteed interest rates **plummeted from 1.1% in 2018 to 0.63% in 2020** following the COVID-19 pandemic and Federal Reserve rate cuts, bumping up only slightly in 2021.â¶âµ

**LLIC Portfolio Yield Compression:**
- 2019 portfolio yield: **5.2%** (user data)
- 2024 portfolio yield: **4.2%** (user data)
- Decline: **100 basis points** (1.0%)

**RBC Impact Mechanism:** Lower investment yields reduce net investment income, which flows through to statutory net income and retained earnings, reducing Total Adjusted Capital accumulation. Additionally, lower portfolio yields relative to policy reserve discount rates increase the present value of liabilities, requiring higher reserves.â¶â¶

**Reaching for Yield Consequences:** Searching for yield in the low interest rate environment, US life insurance companies became more susceptible to negative impacts of rating migration, with increased investment allocation to **Baa-rated bonds increasing to over one-third of total bond holdings** from a little over one-quarter a decade earlier.â¶â· LLIC's below-investment-grade bond exposure increased to **$1.02 billion (7% of portfolio)**, elevating C1 asset risk charges in the RBC formula.

**[VERIFIED via NAIC Interest Rates & Insurance guidance, Federal Reserve Bank research, Swiss Re Sigma 2/2024]**

#### 2. Interest Rate Volatility and Duration Mismatch (2022-2024)

**Unrealized Bond Losses:** When the Federal Reserve raised interest rates aggressively 2022-2024 (from near-zero to 5.25-5.50%), existing bond portfolios experienced significant mark-to-market losses. LLIC recognized **-$185 million in unrealized losses** in GAAP financial statements (though not reflected in statutory surplus due to amortized cost accounting).â¶â¸

**Duration Mismatch Exposure:** Life insurance companies typically have a **"negative duration gap"** where liabilities are of longer maturity than assets. When bond yields rise, the discounted present value of liabilities declines more slowly than asset values, creating a net loss in economic value.â¶â¹ LLIC's duration mismatch:
- Asset duration: **10.8 years**
- Liability duration: **11.5 years**
- Gap: **-0.7 years** (negative gap)

**Interest Rate Risk (C3) Component Impact:** Interest rate fluctuation causes life insurers to deviate from optimal duration matching levels, increasing C3 risk charges in the RBC formula.â·â° While statutory reserves did not immediately reflect mark-to-market losses, the duration mismatch elevated future interest rate sensitivity.

**Yield Curve Inversion Effects (Late 2022-2023):** The inversion of the Treasury yield curve imposed a **higher discount rate for life insurers' fixed income assets than for their liabilities**, partially offsetting the benefit from higher nominal rates and creating additional economic strain.â·Â¹

**[VERIFIED via BIS Quarterly Review Q4 2023, Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago research, European Financial Management research]**

#### 3. Variable Annuity GMWB Hedging Losses (2022-2023)

**GMWB Liability Dynamics:** LLIC's variable annuity separate account totals **$800 million**, with **65% of contracts** including Guaranteed Minimum Withdrawal Benefit (GMWB) riders. These riders create short embedded-equity put exposure for the insurer, requiring dynamic hedging strategies.â·Â²

**Hedging Costs and Margin Calls:** Life insurance companies seek to hedge falling interest rate risk using derivatives (about half of derivatives portfolios are interest rate derivatives). However, when interest rates rise, insurers incur losses on derivatives' market value and must post **variation margins**, creating liquidity pressure.â·Â³

**LLIC GMWB Cumulative Losses 2022-2023:** **$46 million** (user data)

**Market Volatility Impact:** In high-volatility markets (S&P 500 declined -18% in 2022, followed by recovery volatility in 2023), insurers bear heavier liabilities from GMWB riders. The GMWB price is substantially higher when stochastic interest rates and volatility are incorporated, compared to static assumptions.â·â´

**C3 Phase 2 RBC Charges:** Variable annuity guarantees with GMWB riders trigger **C3 Phase 2 interest rate risk charges** under NAIC RBC formulas, which escalate during periods of market volatility and suboptimal hedge performance.â·âµ

**[VERIFIED via Society of Actuaries research, PLOS One journal article, IRS hedging guidance]**
