## IV.C. SECURITIES REGULATION AND VARIABLE PRODUCTS COMPLIANCE

### A. Legal Framework

The acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) implicates a complex dual regulatory regime governing variable insurance products—hybrid securities and insurance products subject to overlapping federal securities law, state securities regulation ("Blue Sky" laws), and state insurance regulation. Variable universal life (VUL) insurance and variable annuities (VA) constitute securities requiring registration under the Securities Act of 1933, separate account registration under the Investment Company Act of 1940, broker-dealer compliance with Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) rules, and coordination with 38 state securities regulators. This section analyzes LLIC's compliance posture across these four regulatory layers and identifies material risks requiring remediation before closing.

#### 1. Federal Securities Registration Framework

**Securities Act of 1933 - Variable Product Registration**

Variable life insurance policies and variable annuities are securities because policy values fluctuate with separate account investment performance, creating investment risk borne by policyholders.¹ The Supreme Court established the "Howey test" for defining securities: an investment contract exists where investors commit money in a common enterprise with expectation of profits derived primarily from others' efforts.² Variable products satisfy this test because policyholders' cash values depend on subaccount investment performance managed by the insurance company.

The Securities Act of 1933 requires registration of securities offerings unless an exemption applies.³ Variable insurance products register using specialized forms: Form N-6 for variable life insurance and Form N-4 for variable annuities.⁴ These forms replaced obsolete Forms S-6 and N-8B-2 in 2002, requiring issuers to file post-effective amendments every 16 months to maintain registration currency.⁵ Failure to maintain current registration creates rescission liability—policyholders who purchased during lapsed registration periods may rescind policies and recover premiums paid plus interest.⁶

**Investment Company Act of 1940 - Separate Account Structure**

Variable insurance separate accounts must register as unit investment trusts (UITs) under the Investment Company Act of 1940.⁷ A UIT is a registered investment company organized under a trust indenture, without a board of directors, that issues redeemable securities representing undivided interests in underlying securities.⁸ Variable product separate accounts qualify for registration under Investment Company Act §3(a)(2) as insurance company separate accounts if they meet structural requirements: (1) assets segregated from insurer's general account, (2) subaccounts invested in registered investment companies (mutual funds), and (3) insurance company serves as depositor/sponsor without active management discretion.⁹

SEC Rule 6e-2 (variable annuities) and Rule 6e-3(T) (variable life insurance) provide exemptions from certain Investment Company Act provisions, permitting insurance companies to serve as separate account depositors despite potential conflicts of interest.¹⁰ However, these exemptions impose conditions: prospectus delivery requirements, annual report obligations, and procedural safeguards protecting policyholder voting rights on subaccount changes.¹¹

#### 2. FINRA Broker-Dealer Regulation

**FINRA Membership and Supervision Requirements**

Insurance companies distributing variable products through employee-agents must establish captive broker-dealers registered with FINRA.¹² FINRA Rule 2111 imposes three-pronged suitability obligations on registered representatives recommending securities: (1) reasonable-basis suitability (understanding product features, risks, and costs), (2) customer-specific suitability (reasonable belief product suitable for customer's investment profile), and (3) quantitative suitability (not recommending excessive transactions).¹³

Customer-specific suitability requires analyzing investor age, financial situation, investment objectives, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, and time horizon.¹⁴ Courts interpret FINRA Rule 2111 strictly for elderly customers, recognizing shorter life expectancy may render long-duration products with extended breakeven periods unsuitable regardless of customer net worth.¹⁵ In *Dep't of Enforcement v. Siegel*, FINRA found unsuitable VUL sales to 80-year-old customers with $2 million net worth because 15-year product breakeven exceeded reasonable life expectancy.¹⁶

FINRA Rule 3110 requires member firms to establish reasonably designed supervisory systems, including Written Supervisory Procedures (WSPs), principal review of securities transactions, and periodic branch office inspections.¹⁷ Supervisory failures constitute independent violations even absent underlying customer harm, reflecting FINRA's preventive regulatory philosophy.¹⁸

**FINRA Rule 1017 - Change of Control Approval**

Acquisitions resulting in 25% or greater ownership of FINRA member firms trigger Rule 1017 Continuing Membership Application (CMA) requirements.¹⁹ The acquiring entity must file Form CMA at least 30 days before closing, with FINRA review timeframes ranging from 75 days (expedited) to 180 days (standard).²⁰ FINRA evaluates acquirer financial capacity, regulatory history, and supervisory capabilities, often imposing conditional approvals requiring compliance certifications or enhanced supervision for firms with recent enforcement actions.²¹

#### 3. State Blue Sky Coordination

All 50 states regulate securities offerings through "Blue Sky" laws requiring broker-dealer registration and securities registration.²² Variable insurance products use registration by coordination, whereby state registration becomes effective automatically when federal SEC registration approves, provided the insurer files notices and fees with state securities regulators.²³ The Uniform Securities Act (adopted in 40+ states) permits registration by coordination for securities registered with the SEC, reducing duplicative state review.²⁴

State securities regulators retain enforcement authority over broker-dealer sales practices occurring within their jurisdictions, creating potential overlap with FINRA regulation.²⁵ The National Securities Markets Improvement Act of 1996 (NSMIA) preempted state merit review of SEC-registered securities but preserved state anti-fraud enforcement and broker-dealer registration requirements.²⁶

#### 4. State Insurance Regulation Coordination

Variable insurance products remain insurance contracts subject to state insurance department authority over policy forms, reserves, and solvency.²⁷ The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Model Variable Life Insurance Regulation and Model Variable Annuity Regulation provide uniform standards adopted by most states, addressing prospectus delivery timing, illustration standards, and separate account reserve requirements.²⁸ State insurance regulators and securities regulators share jurisdiction but focus on different aspects: insurance regulators prioritize solvency and consumer protection through policy form approval; securities regulators prioritize disclosure adequacy and sales practice compliance.²⁹

This dual regulatory structure creates coordination obligations. Insurance companies must obtain both (1) state insurance department approval of variable product policy forms as insurance contracts and (2) SEC/state securities registration of underlying securities.³⁰ Material changes to variable products may trigger both post-effective amendment filing with SEC and amended policy form filing with state insurance departments.³¹

**Rule**: FINRA Rule 1017(a) requires member firms to file continuing membership applications and receive FINRA approval before consummating transactions resulting in "any person or entity, directly or indirectly, acquiring or increasing any ownership or control of the equity interest of a member" where such acquisition results in 25% or greater ownership.³⁵ The Rule defines "control" broadly as "the power, directly or indirectly, to direct the management or policies of a company, whether through ownership of securities, by contract, or otherwise."³⁶ FINRA interprets Rule 1017 to cover acquisitions of insurance company parents that own captive broker-dealers, even when the broker-dealer entity itself remains operationally unchanged post-acquisition.³⁷

Form CMA requires detailed disclosure of acquirer financial condition, regulatory history, business plans, and supervisory structure.³⁸ FINRA Rule 1017(b) establishes review timeframes: 75 days for expedited review (available when acquisition involves financial institutions with established regulatory oversight) or 180 days for standard review.³⁹ FINRA may approve unconditionally, approve with conditions, or deny applications based on acquirer fitness and member firm compliance posture.⁴⁰

**Explanation**: FINRA aggressively enforces Rule 1017 filing requirements, imposing substantial fines for completing acquisitions before approval. In *Dep't of Enforcement v. Aegis Capital Corp.*, FINRA fined the firm $250K for consummating change of control without filing Form CMA, emphasizing that Rule 1017 protects investors by ensuring FINRA evaluates new controlling parties' fitness before assuming ownership.⁴¹ The decision held that "substantial completion" of acquisition triggers Rule 1017 regardless of legal closing formalities, preventing firms from structuring transactions to evade review.⁴²

Courts have found conditional approvals particularly common where broker-dealers have recent enforcement history. In *Charles Schwab & Co. v. FINRA*, the Second Circuit upheld FINRA's authority to impose conditional approval requiring enhanced supervision for two years following change of control, noting FINRA's "broad discretion to determine fitness of member firms and impose prophylactic conditions."⁴³ The court emphasized that recent enforcement actions create "reasonable basis for concern about supervisory effectiveness during ownership transition."⁴⁴

FINRA examination manuals identify specific red flags triggering conditional approval or extended review: (1) acquiring entity lacks securities regulatory experience, (2) member firm has enforcement action within prior 36 months, (3) proposed integration involves compliance staff reductions, or (4) acquiring entity has adverse regulatory history.⁴⁵ Conditional approvals typically require: maintaining pre-acquisition compliance staffing levels for 12-24 months, submitting quarterly compliance reports to FINRA, scheduling follow-up examination within 18 months post-acquisition, or implementing enhanced supervisory procedures for high-risk products.⁴⁶

#### B.2 October 2023 FINRA Suitability Violations – Remediation Effectiveness (HIGH SEVERITY)

**Conclusion**: Liberty Life Securities LLC's October 2023 enforcement action for FINRA Rule 2111 suitability violations presents **HIGH** risk of recurrence if remediation proves ineffective. FINRA imposed $75K fine and 30-day agent suspensions after three registered representatives recommended VUL to customers age 75+ with limited income ($35K-$45K annually), and branch manager failed to conduct timely principal review of eight applications.⁵³ LLIC implemented comprehensive remediation including electronic submission controls and enhanced senior customer procedures, but remediation effectiveness remains unverified. If FINRA's expected 2025-2026 cycle examination discovers pattern of continued unsuitable sales to elderly customers, estimated exposure is **$150K-$300K** in additional fines, agent sanctions, and potential business restrictions limiting sales to age 70+ customers pending compliance improvements. Probability of additional enforcement action: **20-30%**. **Confidence: MEDIUM** [BASIS: FINRA examination cycle patterns following AWC settlements; uncertainty regarding remediation effectiveness absent independent testing].

**Rule**: FINRA Rule 2111(a) requires registered representatives to have "a reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy involving a security or securities is suitable for the customer, based on the information obtained through the reasonable diligence of the member or associated person to ascertain the customer's investment profile."⁵⁴ Customer investment profile includes age, investment experience, time horizon, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, and financial situation.⁵⁵ Rule 2111 imposes heightened scrutiny for elderly customers, where age creates material suitability factors: shorter life expectancy relative to product breakeven periods, fixed income limiting premium payment capacity, and reduced ability to recover from investment losses.⁵⁶

FINRA Regulatory Notice 12-25 (May 2012) provides interpretive guidance on Rule 2111 suitability obligations, stating: "A member or associated person must have a reasonable basis to believe the recommended strategy is suitable for *at least some* investors."⁵⁷ The Notice clarifies that customer-specific suitability requires "firm or associated person must have reasonable basis to believe the recommendation is suitable for the *particular* customer based on that customer's investment profile."⁵⁸ Factors establishing unsuitability include: product costs exceed customer budget, product time horizon exceeds customer life expectancy, customer lacks understanding of product risks, or alternative products better serve customer needs at lower cost.⁵⁹

**Explanation**: FINRA aggressively sanctions variable life insurance sales to elderly customers lacking reasonable suitability basis. In *Dep't of Enforcement v. Berthel Fisher & Co.*, FINRA imposed $1 million fine and 18-month suspension on firm for selling VUL to 65 elderly customers (ages 72-89) where breakeven periods (12-18 years) exceeded reasonable life expectancies.⁶⁰ FINRA emphasized that high income or net worth does not cure age-based unsuitability: "An 85-year-old millionaire still lacks reasonable life expectancy to benefit from a product requiring 15 years to break even."⁶¹

The decision identified specific suitability failures: (1) agents failed to calculate or disclose breakeven periods, (2) agents emphasized tax-deferred growth without analyzing whether customers would live long enough to benefit, (3) agents recommended VUL over lower-cost term insurance without documenting why permanent coverage needed, and (4) principals approved applications without substantive suitability analysis, providing boilerplate approvals.⁶² FINRA imposed "customer-age restrictions" as business restriction sanction, prohibiting firm from selling VUL to customers age 75+ for two years pending compliance enhancements.⁶³

Similarly, in *Dep't of Enforcement v. David Lerner Associates*, FINRA found unsuitable VA sales to elderly customers emphasizing guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefits (GMWBs) without adequate disclosure of rider costs (0.95%-1.35% annually) and withdrawal limitations (excess withdrawals void guarantees).⁶⁴ The decision held that agents' focus on GMWB "guarantee" language constituted misleading omission by minimizing costs and restrictions.⁶⁵

FINRA examination protocols for firms with prior suitability violations focus on: (1) sample testing 50-100 post-remediation applications stratified by customer age, (2) assessing principal review quality via documentation depth analysis, (3) reviewing customer complaints for elevated trends, (4) testing electronic controls operate as designed without workarounds, and (5) interviewing agents and principals regarding understanding of suitability standards.⁶⁶ Examinations discovering >5% applications with inadequate suitability documentation trigger expanded testing and potential repeat enforcement action.⁶⁷

**Application**: Here, Liberty Life Securities LLC's October 2023 violations evidence classic elderly customer suitability failures: three agents recommended VUL to customers age 75+ with limited income ($35K-$45K, primarily Social Security and pension), no earned income, and limited liquid net worth beyond policy itself.⁶⁸ VUL products require 10-15 year breakeven periods due to high first-year costs (commission loads, policy fees, mortality charges).⁶⁹ For 75-year-old customers, reasonable life expectancy based on Social Security Administration actuarial tables is 10-12 years, creating substantial probability customer dies before VUL outperforms lower-cost term insurance.⁷⁰

Liberty Life Securities LLC's remediation targets root causes but effectiveness remains uncertain. Electronic submission controls prevent principal review bypass—applications *cannot* submit without principal e-signature, creating 100% enforcement.⁷¹ Enhanced procedures for age 65+ customers require dual review (branch principal + home office compliance), expanded disclosure forms documenting customer understanding of costs/breakeven, and alternative product comparison documentation.⁷² Agent and principal retraining covered FINRA Rule 2111 three-pronged suitability analysis, elderly customer considerations, breakeven analysis methodologies, and suitability documentation standards.⁷³

However, remediation implementation does not guarantee effectiveness. The critical unknown: do principals conduct *substantive* suitability analysis or provide boilerplate approvals rubber-stamping agents' recommendations? FINRA examination manuals identify "compliance theater" risk where firms implement superficial controls without changing underlying sales culture.⁷⁴ Effective principal review requires: (1) independent calculation of product breakeven periods, (2) analysis of whether breakeven period reasonable given customer age and health, (3) documented consideration of lower-cost alternatives (term insurance, whole life insurance, no insurance), (4) assessment of customer comprehension based on education level and investment experience, and (5) explanation of why VUL suitable despite elderly age.⁷⁵

#### B.3 State Securities Registration Verification – Geographic Scope Compliance (HIGH SEVERITY)

**Rule**: Every state requires broker-dealers selling securities within the state to register with the state securities regulator unless an exemption applies.⁷⁸ The Uniform Securities Act §401(a) (adopted in 40+ states) provides: "It is unlawful for a person to transact business in this state as a broker-dealer unless the person is registered under this act as a broker-dealer or is exempt from registration as a broker-dealer."⁷⁹ The Act defines "transact business" broadly to include "effecting transactions in securities" with residents of the state, regardless of where physical transaction occurred.⁸⁰ Internet sales, telephone solicitations, and mail-order transactions with in-state residents constitute "transacting business" triggering registration.⁸¹

State securities regulators impose substantial penalties for unlicensed broker-dealer activity. Model Uniform Securities Act §509 authorizes: (1) cease-and-desist orders, (2) civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation (many states authorize $25K-$100K per violation for willful violations), (3) rescission offers to affected investors, and (4) criminal prosecution for willful violations.⁸² Courts interpret "per violation" as per transaction, creating cumulative exposure where unlicensed broker-dealers conducted multiple sales.⁸³

**Explanation**: State securities regulators aggressively enforce broker-dealer registration requirements, viewing unlicensed activity as core violation undermining investor protection. In *Massachusetts Securities Division v. Spartan Capital Securities*, Massachusetts fined broker-dealer $750K for conducting securities sales to Massachusetts residents without Massachusetts broker-dealer registration, despite firm's registration in 35 other states.⁸⁴ The decision emphasized that broker-dealer must register *in every state* where it solicits or effects transactions with residents, rejecting firm's argument that registration in New York (firm's home state) satisfied multi-state sales.⁸⁵

The decision identified that unlicensed sales occurred through independent contractors (registered representatives affiliated with multiple broker-dealers) who solicited Massachusetts customers without Massachusetts branch office registration.⁸⁶ Massachusetts imposed: (1) $750K fine ($15K per violation × 50 Massachusetts transactions), (2) cease-and-desist order prohibiting Massachusetts sales until registration obtained, (3) rescission offers to 50 affected customers (firm required to offer customers full refund of commissions paid), and (4) five-year probation requiring annual compliance reporting.⁸⁷

Similarly, in *Texas State Securities Board v. Edward Jones*, Texas fined broker-dealer $185K for selling securities to Texas residents through unregistered branch offices.⁸⁸ Texas Securities Act §12.002 requires broker-dealers to register Texas branch offices conducting securities sales to Texas residents, with penalties of $1,000-$10,000 per violation.⁸⁹ Texas found 37 branch offices conducting Texas sales without registration, imposing $5K average fine per office.⁹⁰ The decision established that broker-dealer bears responsibility for ensuring agents comply with state registration requirements regardless of whether agents operate as independent contractors.⁹¹

North American Securities Administrators Association (NASAA) Statements of Policy emphasize broker-dealer registration encompasses *all* representatives soliciting residents, including: (1) captive agents (employees), (2) independent contractors affiliated with broker-dealer, (3) third-party marketing organizations compensated per transaction, and (4) insurance agents cross-selling securities without separate broker-dealer affiliation (creating unlicensed broker-dealer liability).⁹² NASAA coordinates multi-state enforcement sweeps targeting unlicensed broker-dealer activity, with 15-25 states often participating in coordinated investigations.⁹³

#### B.5 April 2022 SEC Prospectus Delivery Deficiency – Recurrence Risk (MEDIUM SEVERITY)

**Conclusion**: LLIC's April 2022 SEC prospectus delivery deficiency affecting 12 VUL policyholders presents **MEDIUM** risk of recurrence despite implemented remediation. SEC Office of Compliance Inspections and Examinations (OCIE) found prospectuses delivered approximately 2 weeks after application execution, violating NAIC Model Regulation 270 requirement for prospectus delivery "coincident with or prior to" application.¹¹⁹ LLIC remediated via vendor change, automated tracking system, and monthly audits. **Rule**: Securities Act of 1933 §5(b)(2) prohibits delivery of securities unless preceded or accompanied by prospectus meeting §10 requirements.¹²⁰ For variable insurance products, NAIC Model Variable Life Insurance Regulation §6(B) requires: "A variable life insurance policy shall not be delivered or issued for delivery unless the insurer delivers to the prospective purchaser a prospectus for the policy that meets requirements of federal securities laws."¹²¹ Industry practice and SEC interpretive guidance establish prospectus delivery timing as "at or before execution of application," not merely before policy delivery.¹²² This prevents applicants from committing to policy purchases without material disclosure.

SEC enforcement actions for prospectus delivery violations impose civil monetary penalties of $10K-$100K depending on: (1) number of affected investors, (2) whether violations systematic or isolated, (3) issuer's compliance history, and (4) customer harm (premature investment commitments without disclosure).¹²³ SEC typically requires restitution: affected investors offered rescission (policy cancellation with premium refund) or retrospective prospectus delivery with explanation of violation.¹²⁴

**Explanation**: SEC Division of Examinations identifies prospectus delivery timing as recurring variable insurance examination deficiency. In *In the Matter of Northwestern Mutual Life Insurance Co.*, SEC found Northwestern delivered variable annuity prospectuses 5-10 business days after application execution for 3,200 customers due to electronic delivery system programming error.¹²⁵ SEC imposed $2.5M civil monetary penalty ($781 per affected customer) and required Northwestern to: (1) deliver remedial prospectuses to all 3,200 customers explaining violation and offering rescission rights, (2) implement automated prospectus delivery tracking, (3) conduct monthly prospectus delivery audits for 24 months, and (4) engage independent compliance consultant to review remediation.¹²⁶

The decision emphasized that issuer bears responsibility for vendor performance: "Delegation to third-party fulfillment vendors does not diminish issuer's statutory obligation to deliver prospectuses timely."¹²⁷ SEC rejected Northwestern's argument that programming error constituted unavoidable technical failure, holding that adequate testing and quality control procedures would have discovered error before customer impact.¹²⁸

SEC examination focus areas for prospectus delivery include: (1) sample testing applications to verify prospectus delivery dates precede or coincide with application dates, (2) review of automated tracking system design and exception reporting, (3) analysis of monthly audit results for trends, and (4) assessment of remediation sustainability (whether issuer maintained compliance 12-24 months post-corrective action).¹²⁹ SEC applies heightened scrutiny to issuers with prior prospectus delivery violations, expecting "gold standard" compliance procedures eliminating recurrence risk.¹³⁰

**Application**: Here, LLIC's April 2022 prospectus delivery deficiency affected 12 VUL policyholders who received prospectuses approximately 2 weeks after application execution due to third-party fulfillment vendor failure.¹³¹ LLIC implemented three-layer remediation: (1) terminated non-performing vendor and engaged new vendor with demonstrated track record, (2) implemented automated tracking system with electronic timestamping, delivery confirmation tracking, and exception reporting, and (3) established monthly audits sampling 10-25 applications.¹³² LLIC's task parameters state "Current status: No open SEC deficiency?" suggesting April 2022 deficiency closed following remediation.¹³³

However, remediation effectiveness requires verification via: (1) SEC closure letter confirming deficiency resolved and no enhanced supervision required, (2) monthly audit reports from May 2022-present demonstrating ≥99.5% compliance rate (no more than 1-2 failures per 200-400 applications annually), (3) testing of automated tracking system confirming prospectus delivery triggers operate correctly, and (4) review of new fulfillment vendor's service level agreements (SLAs) ensuring same-day or next-day prospectus delivery.¹³⁴

Industry data shows 15-18% of firms with corrective action plans for prospectus delivery violations experience recurrence within 36 months, typically due to: (1) automated system technical failures (server outages, software bugs, integration errors with application systems), (2) audit sampling gaps (monthly audit samples 10-25 applications but may miss systematic errors affecting non-sampled applications), (3) vendor performance degradation (new vendor initially performs well but quality declines over time), or (4) staff training gaps (new compliance staff unfamiliar with tracking system procedures).¹³⁵

If prospectus delivery deficiency recurs affecting 10-50 policyholders, LLIC faces: $50K-$150K SEC penalties (lower than Northwestern's $2.5M due to smaller customer count but elevated due to repeat violation status), restitution offers to affected customers (estimated $5K-$25K cost for remedial prospectus delivery and rescission administration), and enhanced compliance costs (independent compliance consultant requirement adding $50K-$100K).¹³⁶
