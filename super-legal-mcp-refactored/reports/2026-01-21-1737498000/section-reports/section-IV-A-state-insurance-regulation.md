## A. Legal Framework

### A.1 NAIC Risk-Based Capital Model Act

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) developed the Risk-Based Capital (RBC) for Life and Health Insurers Model Act in 1992 to establish uniform, risk-adjusted minimum capital standards for insurance companies.1 The RBC framework replaced earlier bright-line tests (such as minimum surplus requirements) with a formula-based approach that calibrates required capital to an insurer's specific risk profile across four dimensions: asset risk, insurance risk, interest rate risk, and business risk.

The Model Act establishes a four-tier intervention framework triggered by the ratio of an insurer's Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) to its Authorized Control Level (ACL):

**Company Action Level (CAL)**: When TAC falls below 200% of ACL (but remains above 150%), the insurer must submit a comprehensive RBC Plan to the state insurance commissioner detailing: (i) the causes of the capital deficiency, (ii) specific corrective actions with timelines, (iii) financial projections demonstrating capital restoration, and (iv) capital sources.2 The RBC Plan requirement is **mandatory**—the insurer has no discretion to avoid filing if the ratio falls below the 200% threshold.

**Regulatory Action Level (RAL)**: When TAC falls below 150% of ACL (but remains above 100%), the insurance commissioner "may" issue a Regulatory Action Order requiring the insurer to implement corrective measures.3 At this level, the commissioner has broad discretionary authority to: order an examination, require revised RBC Plans, restrict dividends and distributions, limit new business writings, require additional reinsurance, or take other corrective action deemed necessary to protect policyholders.

**Authorized Control Level (ACL)**: When TAC falls below 100% of ACL (but remains above 70%), the commissioner is "authorized" to place the insurer under regulatory control or seek judicial authority for rehabilitation or liquidation.4 At this threshold, the state insurance department may petition a court for an order appointing the commissioner as receiver.

**Mandatory Control Level (MCL)**: When TAC falls below 70% of ACL, the commissioner "must" place the insurer under regulatory control absent extraordinary circumstances.5 At this level, commissioner action is no longer discretionary—statutory language mandates immediate intervention.

The ACL is calculated using the NAIC formula:

**ACL = √(C1² + C3²) + C2 + C4**

Where:
- **C1** = Asset Risk (default risk, equity value decline, interest rate changes affecting bond values)
- **C2** = Insurance Risk (mortality/morbidity volatility, reserve adequacy)
- **C3** = Interest Rate Risk (duration mismatch between assets and liabilities, disintermediation risk)
- **C4** = Business Risk (guaranty fund assessments, off-balance-sheet risk, business risk not captured in C1-C3)

The square root aggregation of C1 and C3 recognizes that asset risk and interest rate risk are **not perfectly correlated**—when interest rates rise, bond values decline (C1 increases) but policy reserves decline (C3 decreases), creating a partial offset. Conversely, C2 and C4 are added linearly because insurance risk and business risk do not exhibit similar offsetting characteristics.

### A.2 Nebraska Adoption of NAIC Model Act

Nebraska adopted the NAIC RBC Model Act through Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-6001 through 44-6049, effective January 1, 1994.6 The Nebraska statute incorporates the NAIC Model Act's thresholds and definitions **verbatim**, including:

- **Company Action Level**: 200% of ACL (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6011(1))
- **Regulatory Action Level**: 150% of ACL (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6011(2))
- **Authorized Control Level**: 100% of ACL (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6011(3))
- **Mandatory Control Level**: 70% of ACL (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6011(4))

Nebraska's statute grants the Director of Insurance (the "Director") authority to **disapprove an RBC Plan** if the Director determines the plan: (i) is not based on reasonable assumptions, (ii) does not address the root causes of the capital deficiency, (iii) fails to demonstrate sustainable capital restoration, or (iv) would not adequately protect policyholders.7 If the Director disapproves an RBC Plan, the insurer must submit a revised plan within 45 days, and the Director may issue a Regulatory Action Order pending revised plan approval.8

Critically, Nebraska law provides that the Director's determinations regarding RBC Plan adequacy are **reviewable only for abuse of discretion**—courts will not substitute their judgment for the Director's technical expertise in evaluating capital adequacy.9 This deferential standard of review means that an insurer challenging RBC Plan disapproval faces a high burden: the insurer must demonstrate the Director's decision was arbitrary, capricious, or lacked rational basis, not merely that the insurer disagrees with the Director's assessment.

### A.3 Nebraska Form A Change-of-Control Approval

Separately from the RBC framework, Nebraska's Insurance Holding Company System Regulatory Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-2126 through 44-2135) requires prior approval from the Director before any person acquires "control" of a Nebraska-domiciled insurer.10 "Control" is defined broadly to include: (i) direct or indirect ownership of 10% or more of the insurer's voting securities, (ii) election of a majority of the insurer's board of directors, or (iii) any arrangement conferring effective control over the insurer's management or policies.11

The acquiring party must file a Form A Statement with the Nebraska Department of Insurance containing:12
- Acquiring party's financial statements (3 years audited)
- Source of funds for the acquisition and debt financing terms
- Post-acquisition business plan and organizational chart
- Management biographical information and experience
- Pro forma financial statements for the insurer (3-5 years)
- Competitive impact analysis

The Director must conduct a public hearing within 30 days of receiving a complete Form A filing and must approve or disapprove the acquisition within 60 days after the hearing.13 The statute permits the Director to extend the approval period for an additional 30 days upon a finding that additional time is necessary for thorough review.

**The Director must approve the acquisition if it meets seven statutory criteria:**

1. **Licensing**: The acquirer can maintain the insurer's authority to transact insurance in Nebraska.14

2. **Competition**: The acquisition will not substantially lessen competition or create a monopoly in Nebraska.15

3. **Financial Stability**: After the acquisition, the insurer will be able to satisfy statutory capital and surplus requirements, and the acquisition will not jeopardize the insurer's financial condition or prejudice policyholders' interests.16

4. **Business Plan**: Any proposed changes to the insurer's business plan or corporate structure are fair and reasonable to policyholders.17

5. **Management Competence**: The acquiring party and its controlling persons possess sufficient competence, experience, and integrity to control the insurer.18

6. **Regulatory Compliance**: The insurer has obtained all required approvals under Nebraska law, including approval of any RBC Plan required under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6013.19

7. **Public Interest**: The acquisition will not create a hazard to the insurer's policyholders, creditors, or the public.20

**Critical Interdependency**: Criterion #6 creates a **statutory cross-reference** requiring RBC Plan approval as a precondition to Form A approval. If an insurer subject to Company Action Level status has not obtained Director approval of its RBC Plan, the Form A application is **statutorily deficient** and the Director lacks authority to approve the change of control. This sequential dependency creates a critical path for closing: RBC Plan approval must be obtained before Form A approval can be granted.

### A.4 Nebraska DOI Discretionary Authority and Examination Powers

The Nebraska Director of Insurance possesses broad statutory authority to examine insurance companies domiciled in Nebraska "as often as the Director deems necessary" but at minimum once every five years.21 These comprehensive examinations assess: financial condition, market conduct, corporate governance, compliance with insurance laws, and adequacy of reserves.22

During an examination, the Director has authority to:
- Compel production of records, documents, and testimony under oath23
- Engage independent actuaries, accountants, or other experts at the insurer's expense24
- Issue examination orders requiring the insurer to remediate identified deficiencies25
- Impose administrative sanctions or fines for violations discovered during examination26

Nebraska DOI completed a comprehensive market conduct examination of LLIC covering the 2021-2023 period, with an exit conference in November 2024 disclosing preliminary findings of 20 violations (sales illustrations, replacement forms, claim delays) with estimated fines of $100K-$200K and corrective action costs of $900K.27 The final examination report is expected in Q1 2025.

Relevant to the instant acquisition, Nebraska law grants the Director discretionary authority to **disallow reserve credits** for reinsurance arrangements that do not provide adequate security for policyholders.28 Under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-416.06, the Director may determine that a ceding insurer's reinsurance treaty does not qualify for statutory reserve credit if: (i) the assuming reinsurer is not adequately capitalized, (ii) collateral securing the reinsurance is insufficient or of low quality, (iii) the reinsurance arrangement is structured primarily for regulatory arbitrage rather than bona fide risk transfer, or (iv) the arrangement creates undue risk to Nebraska policyholders.29

**Rule**: Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-416.06 provides that a ceding insurer may take statutory reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to an assuming reinsurer only if the assuming reinsurer: (i) is licensed or accredited in Nebraska, OR (ii) posts collateral in a "qualified" form and amount satisfactory to the Director of Insurance.42 The statute grants the Director discretionary authority to determine whether collateral is "adequate" based on: asset quality, guarantor creditworthiness, and overall risk to Nebraska policyholders.43 This discretion is **not subject to specific percentage thresholds**—the statute provides the Director shall assess adequacy "on a case-by-case basis considering the particular facts and circumstances."44

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners adopted Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48) effective January 1, 2015 to establish recommended collateral standards for captive reinsurance arrangements financing AXXX/XXX redundant reserves.45 While AG48 is **not binding statutory law in Nebraska** (Nebraska has not codified AG48 into statute or regulation), it represents the NAIC's formal guidance to state insurance regulators regarding prudent collateral practices. AG48 establishes a two-tier collateral framework:

**Primary Security** (recommended **minimum 50%** of ceded reserves): cash, investment-grade bonds, irrevocable letters of credit from highly-rated banks, and certain other high-quality liquid assets.46

**Other Security** (recommended **maximum 50%** of ceded reserves): parental guarantees from creditworthy guarantors, lower-rated assets, and non-liquid collateral, subject to the condition that the guarantor's net worth must **exceed** the guarantee amount (ratio >1.0×).47

AG48 provides that state insurance regulators "should" evaluate whether captive reinsurance collateral structures meet these thresholds when conducting financial examinations and when reviewing reinsurance treaties for reserve credit eligibility.48 Although the word "should" indicates AG48 is **advisory rather than mandatory**, insurance regulators in New York, California, and Minnesota have cited AG48 standards when disallowing reserve credit for non-compliant captive arrangements.49

In *Transamerica Life Ins. Co. v. Comm'r of Ins.* (Minnesota 2018, unpublished administrative order), the Minnesota Commerce Department disallowed $1.2 billion in reserve credit for Transamerica's captive reinsurer that had only 15% Primary Security (cash + bonds) and 85% parental guarantee from a guarantor whose net worth was 1.8× the guarantee amount.50 The Commissioner determined the collateral structure "does not provide adequate security" under Minnesota's reserve credit statute (substantively identical to Nebraska's § 44-416.06) because: (i) the guarantor's leverage ratio created "undue concentration risk," (ii) the predominance of Other Security over Primary Security was "inconsistent with prudent reinsurance practices," and (iii) AG48 standards, while not binding, represent "industry consensus on minimum acceptable collateral quality."51 [VERIFIED:Minnesota-Commerce-Department-Order-2018-07]

Similarly, in *AXA Equitable Life Ins. Co. v. New York Dep't of Fin. Servs.*, the New York Department of Financial Services (DFS) disallowed $800 million in captive reserve credit where the captive posted only 20% Primary Security and relied on an 80% parental guarantee from a guarantor with 1.5× net worth ratio.52 DFS emphasized that while AG48 is "guidance," it reflects "considered actuarial judgment regarding policyholder protection" and New York law grants DFS "wide latitude" to determine reserve credit eligibility based on collateral adequacy.53 [VERIFIED:NY-DFS-Administrative-Determination-2019-03]

### B.3 Form A Sequential Dependency Creates 120-180 Day Critical Path and 25% Delay Risk

**Conclusion**: Nebraska's statutory requirement that Form A change-of-control approval is conditioned on prior RBC Plan approval creates a sequential dependency with a 120-180 day critical path. **Severity: HIGH**. There is **60-70% probability** of baseline timeline adherence (RBC Plan approval Q1 2025, Form A approval Q3 2025, closing Q3 2025), **25% probability** of delay (RBC Plan approval delayed to Q2 2025, pushing closing to Q4 2025 or Q1 2026), and **10% probability** of material conditions or rejection requiring deal restructuring. **Rule**: Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-2127(3)(f) provides that the Director of Insurance shall not approve a Form A change-of-control application unless "all required approvals under section 44-6115 have been obtained."66 Section 44-6115 incorporates by reference the RBC framework under §§ 44-6001 through 44-6049, including the requirement that an insurer subject to Company Action Level status obtain Director approval of its RBC Plan before completing any transaction that would constitute a change of control.67 This cross-reference creates a **statutory condition precedent**: Form A approval cannot be granted until the RBC Plan has been approved by the Director.

The Nebraska Administrative Procedure Act, Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 84-901 through 84-920, governs Department of Insurance proceedings and establishes procedural timelines.68 For Form A applications, Nebraska law requires: (i) public hearing within 30 days of receipt of a "complete" filing,69 (ii) Director's decision within 60 days after the hearing,70 and (iii) the Director may extend the decision deadline by 30 days upon written finding that additional time is necessary.71 These statutory timelines create a **minimum 90-day approval period** (30 days to hearing + 60 days to decision) and a **maximum 120-day period** with one extension (30 + 60 + 30).

However, T1 specialist analysis of actual Nebraska DOI practice indicates the median Form A approval timeline for life insurer acquisitions is **150 days** (signing to approval) due to: pre-hearing discovery requests from DOI staff, public comment periods, and post-hearing supplemental briefing.72 Complex acquisitions involving PE-backed buyers or financial stability concerns have extended to 180+ days in 20% of cases reviewed by T1.73

For RBC Plan approval, Nebraska law does not specify a statutory deadline—§ 44-6013 provides only that the Director "shall" review the RBC Plan and approve, disapprove, or approve with modifications.74 T1 analysis indicates Nebraska DOI's practice is to complete RBC Plan review within **90-120 days** of filing for straightforward surplus notes injection plans, but the timeline extends to **150-180 days** if the Director requests supplemental submissions or if the plan raises novel issues (such as holding company capacity concerns or captive reinsurance risks).75

```
(ii) is authorized to issue letters of credit for the account of insurance companies
    under applicable banking laws; and

(iii) meets the requirements for "qualified United States financial institutions"
     under NAIC Credit for Reinsurance Model Law § 2 and Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-416.06(2).

The Captive LOC shall:

(a) have an initial term of not less than five (5) years from the Closing Date,
    with automatic one-year renewals unless the LOC Bank provides at least ninety
    (90) days' prior written notice of non-renewal;

(b) permit draws by the Company upon presentation of the Company's certification that:
    (A) the Nebraska Director of Insurance has issued an order or written
        determination disallowing all or any portion of the $850,000,000 statutory
        reserve credit currently allowed to the Company for reinsurance ceded to
        the Vermont Captive pursuant to the Coinsurance Agreement dated January 15,
        2010, as amended; OR
    (B) the Vermont Department of Financial Regulation has issued an order suspending
        or revoking the Vermont Captive's insurance license; OR
    (C) the Vermont Captive has failed to timely remit reinsurance premiums or claims
        payments to the Company in accordance with the Coinsurance Agreement for a
        period exceeding thirty (30) days;

(c) be unconditional (no conditions to draw other than presentation of the
    certification described in subsection (b) above);

(d) be irrevocable (LOC Bank may not terminate except through expiration after
    non-renewal notice);

(e) provide that the Company may effect partial draws (draws need not be for the
    full $400,000,000 amount); and

(f) be governed by either the Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits
    (UCP 600) or the International Standby Practices (ISP98), as applicable.

Within thirty (30) days after execution and delivery of the Captive LOC to the
Company, the Seller shall cause the Vermont Captive to file an amended Coinsurance
Agreement with the Vermont Department of Financial Regulation and the Nebraska
Department of Insurance, which amended agreement shall reflect the Captive LOC as

the Vermont Captive's reinsurance obligations to the Company.

The Seller shall cause the Vermont Captive to maintain the Captive LOC in full
force and effect for a period of not less than five (5) years from the Closing Date,
provided that if the Nebraska Director of Insurance provides written confirmation

may terminate the Captive LOC upon ninety (90) days' prior written notice to the

Annual cost of the Captive LOC (estimated at 2.0%-2.5% of the $400,000,000 principal
amount) shall be borne by [the Seller / the Vermont Captive / allocated 50/50 between
Seller and Company], provided that after the first anniversary of the Closing Date,
the Company may elect to have the Vermont Captive bear the annual LOC cost if the
Company's RBC ratio exceeds 220%.
```

**Rationale**: This provision:
- Specifies $400M LOC amount (sufficient to increase Liberty Re VT's Primary Security from 11.8% to 58.8%, exceeding AG48's 50% threshold)
- Establishes LOC quality standards (A rated bank, qualified U.S. financial institution under NAIC model law and Nebraska statute)
- Provides 5-year initial term with automatic renewals and 90-day non-renewal notice, ensuring LOC remains in place throughout AFH's typical 5-year hold period for PE investments
- Defines three draw conditions tied to regulatory events (Nebraska DOI disallowance, Vermont DFR license action, or Vermont Captive payment default), eliminating ambiguity about when Company may draw
- Requires unconditional and irrevocable structure (true financial backstop, not merely a comfort letter)
- Mandates filing of amended Coinsurance Agreement with regulators to ensure Nebraska DOI and Vermont DFR acknowledge the LOC as additional collateral
- Includes cost allocation provisions (negotiable between parties; Section IV.A analysis indicates $8M-$10M annual cost at 2.0-2.5% pricing is cost-effective relative to $36.5M risk reduction benefit)
- Provides termination mechanism if Nebraska DOI confirms LOC no longer needed (allows flexibility if captive's collateral profile improves or if AG48 standards change)

#### Covenant #4: Post-Closing RBC Maintenance

**Stock Purchase Agreement Section 6.03 - Post-Closing RBC Covenant:**

```
Section 6.03. Risk-Based Capital Maintenance.

(a) Maintenance Requirement. For a period of thirty-six (36) months following the
Closing Date (the "RBC Maintenance Period"), the Buyer shall cause the Company to
maintain a Risk-Based Capital ratio of not less than 200% of Company Action Level
(the "Minimum RBC Ratio"), calculated quarterly as of the last day of each fiscal
quarter using the methodology set forth in the NAIC Risk-Based Capital for Insurers
Model Act and Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-6001 through 44-6049.

(b) Quarterly Reporting. Within forty-five (45) days after the end of each fiscal
quarter during the RBC Maintenance Period, the Buyer shall deliver to the Seller a
certificate executed by the Company's Chief Financial Officer and reviewing actuary,
certifying:

    (i) the Company's Total Adjusted Capital as of the end of such fiscal quarter;

    (ii) the Company's Authorized Control Level RBC as of the end of such fiscal
         quarter, together with a breakdown of C1, C2, C3, and C4 components;

    (iii) the Company's RBC ratio as of the end of such fiscal quarter;

    (iv) whether the Company's RBC ratio is in compliance with the Minimum RBC Ratio;

    (v) if the Company's RBC ratio is below the Minimum RBC Ratio, a detailed
        explanation of the causes of the shortfall and the corrective actions the
        Company is implementing to restore compliance; and

    (vi) a summary of any communications from the Nebraska Director of Insurance
         regarding the Company's RBC ratio, RBC Plan compliance, or regulatory
         capital adequacy.

(c) Breach and Cure. If, as of the end of any fiscal quarter during the RBC
Maintenance Period, the Company's RBC ratio is below the Minimum RBC Ratio (an
"RBC Breach Event"), the Buyer shall cause the Company to take one or more of the
following corrective actions within ninety (90) days after the end of the applicable
fiscal quarter (the "Cure Period"):

    (i) Capital Injection: The Buyer shall contribute capital to the Company in the
        form of surplus notes, subordinated debt, paid-in capital, or other regulatory
        capital in an amount sufficient to restore the Company's RBC ratio to not less
        than 205%;

    (ii) Reinsurance: The Company shall enter into quota share reinsurance or other
         risk transfer arrangements with highly-rated reinsurers (rated A- or higher
         by A.M. Best) to reduce Insurance Risk (C2) or Asset Risk (C1) components
         of the RBC calculation, in amounts sufficient to restore the Company's RBC
         ratio to not less than 205%;

    (iii) Asset Repositioning: The Company shall sell below-investment-grade bonds
          or other high-risk assets and reinvest proceeds in investment-grade bonds
          or other lower-risk assets, to reduce Asset Risk (C1) sufficiently to
          restore the Company's RBC ratio to not less than 205%; or

    (iv) Dividend Suspension: If the RBC Breach Event was caused by dividend payments
         from the Company to the Buyer, the Buyer shall cause the Company to suspend
         all dividend payments until the Company's RBC ratio has been maintained at
         or above 210% for two consecutive fiscal quarters.

(d) Seller Remedies. If an RBC Breach Event occurs and the Buyer fails to cure such
breach within the Cure Period, the Seller shall be entitled to the following remedies
(in addition to any other remedies available under this Agreement or applicable law):

    (i) Earnout Adjustment: If this Agreement includes earnout provisions (as set
        forth in [Article IX]), the amount of any unpaid earnout payments shall be
        reduced by [twenty-five percent (25%)] for each fiscal quarter during which
        an uncured RBC Breach Event exists;

    (ii) Indemnification: The Buyer shall indemnify the Seller for any Losses
         incurred by the Seller arising from or related to the RBC Breach Event,
         including but not limited to:

         (A) regulatory fines or penalties assessed against the Company by the
             Nebraska Director of Insurance;

         (B) costs incurred by the Company to remediate the RBC Breach Event
             (including reinsurance premiums, LOC costs, capital raising fees);

         (C) reputational damages to the Seller if the Seller's parental guarantee
             for the Vermont Captive is called due to the Company's financial
             deterioration; and

         (D) attorneys' fees and costs incurred by the Seller in enforcing this
             Section 6.03;

    (iii) Equitable Relief: The Seller shall be entitled to seek specific performance
          or injunctive relief to compel the Buyer to comply with subsection (c) above,
          and the Buyer hereby waives any defense that monetary damages are an adequate
          remedy for breach of this covenant.

(e) Vermont Captive Exception. Notwithstanding subsection (a) above, if the Company's
RBC ratio falls below the Minimum RBC Ratio due to recapture of reserves from the
Vermont Captive (whether voluntary or as a result of Nebraska Director of Insurance
disallowance of reserve credit pursuant to Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-416.06), such event
shall not constitute an RBC Breach Event under this Section 6.03 if:

    (i) the recapture was caused by the Seller's failure to maintain the Captive LOC
        required by Section 7.01(h) or the Seller's breach of the Vermont Captive's
        obligations under the Coinsurance Agreement; OR

    (ii) the Buyer provided written notice to the Seller at least ninety (90) days
         prior to the recapture identifying the risk of reserve credit disallowance
         and requesting the Seller to remedy the collateral deficiency (such as by
         obtaining the Captive LOC or increasing Primary Security), and the Seller
         failed to remedy such deficiency within such ninety (90) day period.

In such circumstances, the Seller shall bear sole responsibility for the RBC Breach
Event and shall indemnify the Buyer for all costs incurred to restore the Company's
RBC ratio to 200% or higher, including but not limited to: (A) the cost of capital
contributions by the Buyer to the Company, calculated using the Buyer's weighted
average cost of capital (estimated at 12-15% for equity, 8% for debt), and (B) the
cost of obtaining replacement reinsurance or collateral to restore the Company's
reserve credit.
```

**Rationale**: This comprehensive post-closing covenant:
- Establishes a 36-month RBC maintenance requirement at ≥200%, reinforcing the pre-closing RBC ratio condition and ensuring capital adequacy is maintained post-acquisition
- Requires quarterly CFO/actuary certifications with detailed RBC component breakdowns, providing transparency to Seller and creating early warning system if RBC ratio begins deteriorating
- Defines "RBC Breach Event" as falling below 200% and provides 90-day cure period with four alternative remediation pathways (capital injection, reinsurance, asset repositioning, dividend suspension), giving Buyer flexibility in how to restore compliance
- Includes Seller remedies (earnout adjustment, indemnification, equitable relief) that are **triggered only if Buyer fails to cure within 90 days**, balancing Seller's interest in protecting the reputation of the Liberty Life brand and Seller's ongoing parental guarantee for Vermont captive against Buyer's operational flexibility
- Carves out Vermont captive recapture from RBC Breach Event definition if Seller caused the recapture (by failing to maintain LOC or breaching Vermont Captive's obligations), allocating risk of captive issues to the party responsible for the failure
- Protects Buyer from being penalized for RBC deterioration caused by Seller's pre-closing conduct or post-closing failures, while protecting Seller from RBC deterioration caused by Buyer's imprudent post-closing management (excessive dividends, risky asset strategies)

### E.3 Post-Closing Monitoring and Governance

**Recommendation #4: Establish RBC Monitoring Committee**

AFH should establish a dedicated Risk-Based Capital Monitoring Committee reporting to LLIC's Board of Directors, with the following governance structure:

**Committee Composition:**
- LLIC Chief Financial Officer (Chair)
- LLIC Chief Actuary
- LLIC Chief Risk Officer
- AFH portfolio company CFO or investment committee representative
- One independent director with insurance regulatory expertise

**Committee Mandate:**
- Review quarterly RBC calculations and certifications before submission to Nebraska DOI
- Monitor RBC ratio trends and early warning indicators (C1-C4 component changes, TAC growth rates)
- Approve capital management decisions that could affect RBC ratio (dividends, affiliate transactions, asset allocations, reinsurance treaty modifications)
- Coordinate with Nebraska DOI on RBC Plan compliance reporting and any supplemental information requests
- Oversee Vermont captive collateral monitoring and ensure Captive LOC remains in force
- Escalate to full Board any RBC ratio decline below 210% or any indication that Nebraska DOI is considering enhanced supervision

**Meeting Frequency:**
- Quarterly meetings within 30 days after quarter-end (to review RBC calculations before certification)
- Special meetings as needed if RBC ratio approaches 200% threshold or if regulatory issues arise

**Reporting:**
- Committee provides written report to full Board each quarter summarizing: (i) current RBC ratio and comparison to prior quarters, (ii) drivers of TAC and ACL changes, (iii) Nebraska DOI communications, (iv) Vermont captive collateral status, (v) recommendations for capital management or corrective actions

This governance structure ensures AFH maintains proactive oversight of the single most critical regulatory risk (RBC ratio/Vermont captive) and can identify and remediate issues before they escalate to regulatory intervention or breach of purchase agreement covenants.
