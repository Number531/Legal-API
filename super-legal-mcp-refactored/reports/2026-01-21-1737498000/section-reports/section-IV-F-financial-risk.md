### A. Legal Framework

The financial risk analysis for this life insurance acquisition operates at the intersection of federal tax law, state insurance regulation, NAIC risk-based capital standards, and M&A valuation principles. This framework governs how financial exposures are quantified, how capital structures achieve regulatory compliance while optimizing tax efficiency, and how purchase price adjustments reflect probability-weighted risks in insurance company transactions.

#### 1. NAIC Risk-Based Capital Framework and State Adoption

**Primary Authority:** The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Risk-Based Capital (RBC) for Insurers Model Act #312 (2012 revision) establishes the regulatory capital adequacy framework for U.S. life insurance companies. Nebraska has adopted this framework through Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6011 et seq., which governs Liberty Life Insurance Company as a Nebraska-domiciled insurer.¹

**RBC Formula and Action Levels:** The RBC system calculates an insurer's minimum required capital (Authorized Control Level, or "ACL") using a formula that incorporates four risk components:
- **C1 (Asset Risk):** Default and market value risk on bonds, stocks, mortgages, and real estate
- **C2 (Insurance Risk):** Mortality, morbidity, and lapse risk on insurance policies
- **C3 (Interest Rate Risk):** Asset-liability duration mismatch and disintermediation risk
- **C4 (Business Risk):** General business risks including guaranty fund assessments

The formula combines these components using a covariance adjustment: ACL = √(C1² + C3²) + C2 + C4, recognizing that asset and interest rate risks are partially correlated while insurance and business risks are independent.²

**Regulatory Action Levels:** The ratio of Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) to ACL determines regulatory intervention:
- **Company Action Level (200-150% of ACL):** Insurer must file RBC Plan with state insurance department
- **Regulatory Action Level (150-100% of ACL):** Commissioner may issue corrective orders
- **Authorized Control Level (100-70% of ACL):** Commissioner authorized to seize insurer
- **Mandatory Control Level (<70% of ACL):** Commissioner must seize insurer³

**Current Application to LLIC:** Liberty Life Insurance Company reports TAC of $1.85 billion and ACL of $982 million, yielding an RBC ratio of 188.4%—within Company Action Level range.⁴ The company filed an RBC Plan in November 2024 proposing a $150 million capital injection to restore the ratio to 204%, crossing the 200% threshold.⁵

#### 2. Federal Tax Treatment of Capital Structures

**Surplus Notes as Hybrid Instruments:** Surplus notes occupy a unique position in insurance capital structures, treated as debt for federal income tax purposes but as equity for statutory accounting. This dual characterization creates significant tax arbitrage opportunities confirmed in Rev. Rul. 68-515 (1968) and *Anchor National Life Insurance Company v. Commissioner*, 93 T.C. 382 (1989).⁶

**IRC § 163(a) Interest Deduction:** Interest payments on surplus notes are deductible to the issuing insurance company under IRC § 163(a) despite state insurance commissioner approval requirements for payment. The Tax Court in *Anchor National* rejected the IRS's argument that commissioner approval created a contingency defeating debt classification, holding that subordination and regulatory oversight do not convert debt to equity for tax purposes.⁷

**Consolidated Return Elimination:** Where a parent company and life insurance subsidiary file consolidated returns under IRC § 1501-1505, intercompany interest income and expense are eliminated under Treas. Reg. § 1.1502-13, creating zero net tax impact at the consolidated group level.⁸ However, IRC § 1504(c)(2) imposes a five-year affiliation requirement before life insurance companies can join consolidated returns, potentially delaying elimination benefits.⁹

**After-Tax Cost Comparison:** For a $150 million capital injection at 6.5% interest:
- **Surplus Notes:** $9.75M annual interest × (1 - 21% tax rate) = $7.70M after-tax cost (5.13% effective rate)
- **Subordinated Debt:** $10.5M at 7.0% × 79% = $8.30M after-tax cost (5.53% effective rate)
- **Common Equity:** No deduction; cost equals required return (10-15%) with no tax benefit

The tax efficiency of surplus notes, combined with 100% regulatory capital credit, makes them the optimal structure for insurance company recapitalizations.¹⁰

#### 3. Statutory Accounting Principles vs. GAAP

**Fundamental Philosophical Divergence:** Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) emphasize earnings measurement and going-concern valuation for investor decision-making. Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP), mandated by state insurance regulators under NAIC guidance, prioritize solvency measurement and liquidation value to protect policyholders.¹¹

**Key SAP Conservatism Principles:**
- **Immediate Expensing:** Deferred acquisition costs (DAC) capitalized under GAAP are expensed immediately under SAP
- **Conservative Reserves:** Statutory reserves use prescribed mortality tables and conservative interest rate assumptions, typically exceeding GAAP reserves by 10-15%
- **Non-Admitted Assets:** Assets without near-term liquidity (goodwill, deferred tax assets, furniture/equipment) are excluded from statutory surplus
- **Amortized Cost Accounting:** Bonds held to maturity are carried at amortized cost rather than fair value, avoiding mark-to-market volatility¹²

**LLIC GAAP-to-SAP Reconciliation:** Liberty Life's GAAP equity of $2.25 billion exceeds statutory surplus (TAC) of $1.85 billion by $400 million. This $400 million differential reflects:
- **-$450M:** Deferred acquisition costs elimination
- **-$1,200M:** Increased policy reserves under statutory mortality/interest assumptions
- **+$185M:** Reversal of unrealized bond losses (amortized cost treatment)
- **-$400M:** Non-admitted assets (goodwill, DTAs, furniture)
- **+$465M:** Asset Valuation Reserve and other SAP adjustments

This reconciliation explains why LLIC's financial statements appear adequate from a GAAP perspective (12.4% equity-to-assets ratio) while triggering regulatory action from an RBC perspective (188% ratio below 200% threshold).¹³

#### 4. M&A Purchase Price Adjustment Principles

**Probability-Weighted Exposure Methodology:** Insurance M&A purchase price adjustments reflect the expected value of identified risks, calculated as Σ(Probability × Magnitude) across all material exposures. This methodology differs from simple aggregation of gross exposures, recognizing that not all risks will materialize simultaneously.¹⁴

**Adjustment Categories:**
- **High-Probability Exposures (>50%):** Reflected dollar-for-dollar in price reduction (e.g., known litigation settlements, regulatory fines)
- **Medium-Probability Exposures (10-50%):** Reflected with asymmetric risk premium (typically 1.5× multiplier to account for downside bias)
- **Low-Probability Tail Risks (<10%):** Addressed through escrow holdbacks rather than price adjustments¹⁵

**Escrow vs. Price Adjustment Allocation:** Industry practice distinguishes between risks suitable for price reduction (highly probable, quantifiable, short-tail) and those better addressed through escrow (contingent, long-tail, requiring post-closing monitoring). Escrow structures typically range from 5-10% of purchase price for complex insurance transactions with material contingencies.¹⁶

**IRR Considerations:** Private equity acquirers typically require 12-15% IRR for life insurance acquisitions. Below-target returns may be justified by strategic rationale (platform value, synergies, tax efficiency) but require explicit board acknowledgment of the return shortfall and compensating factors.¹⁷

#### B.3 Finding HIGH-001: RBC Capital Injection Structure—Surplus Notes Optimal, Equity Economically Inferior

**Conclusion:** The proposed $150 million capital injection via surplus notes is the optimal structure for achieving both regulatory capital compliance (100% Total Adjusted Capital credit) and tax efficiency (5.13% after-tax cost). Alternative structures—subordinated debt (5.53% after-tax cost, limited TAC credit) and common equity (100% TAC credit, but no financial return until RBC >300%)—are economically inferior. **Severity:** HIGH (affects post-closing capital structure and holding company returns). **Exposure:** Selecting suboptimal structure would increase after-tax cost by $0.60M-$4.05M annually ($0.60M for subordinated debt vs. surplus notes; $4.05M+ for equity vs. surplus notes assuming 10% required return). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: T7 tax analyst report; IRC § 163(a); Rev. Rul. 68-515; *Anchor National*, 93 T.C. 382].

**Rule:** Federal income tax law treats surplus notes issued by insurance companies as debt instruments eligible for interest deduction under IRC § 163(a), notwithstanding their treatment as equity under state statutory accounting principles. This dual characterization was established by Rev. Rul. 68-515 (1968-2 C.B. 297) and confirmed by the Tax Court in *Anchor National Life Insurance Company v. Commissioner*, 93 T.C. 382 (1989).³³ The Tax Court rejected the IRS argument that state insurance commissioner approval requirements for interest and principal payments created a contingency that defeats debt classification, holding that subordination and regulatory oversight are permissible debt characteristics that do not convert instruments to equity.³⁴

For regulatory capital purposes, NAIC Risk-Based Capital Model Act #312 provides that surplus notes receive 100% Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) credit because they are subordinated to all policyholder claims and recorded as equity under statutory accounting principles.³⁵ Nebraska has adopted this treatment in Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6014, defining "total adjusted capital" to include surplus notes without limitation up to one-third of TAC from all sources.³⁶

**Explanation:** The surplus note structure creates regulatory capital arbitrage: instruments that function economically as deeply subordinated debt (paying fixed interest, maturing at a specified date, not participating in equity upside) receive equity treatment for regulatory capital while retaining debt treatment for tax purposes. This arbitrage is not considered abusive because it serves regulatory policy goals—increasing insurer capital while providing a financial return to capital providers that makes the infusion economically viable.³⁷

The Tax Court's reasoning in *Anchor National* is instructive. The IRS challenged surplus notes' debt classification on two grounds: (1) interest and principal payments required commissioner approval, creating contingency; and (2) subordination to policyholders made the notes equity-like. The Tax Court rejected both arguments. On contingency, the court held that commissioner approval is "a routine administrative matter" that does not defeat debt classification, analogizing to corporate debt with trustee approval requirements for payments.³⁸ On subordination, the court held that deeply subordinated debt remains debt, citing debt hierarchy principles in bankruptcy law.³⁹

The alternative capital structures present material disadvantages:

**Subordinated Debt:** While interest is clearly tax-deductible (identical to surplus notes), subordinated debt receives limited regulatory capital credit. The NAIC's 2017 memorandum on senior debt and surplus notes in group capital calculations acknowledges that "structurally subordinated debt should receive equity credit to some degree" but provides no uniform standard.⁴⁰ State insurance departments have discretion to assign TAC credit percentages, typically ranging from 25-50% based on degree of subordination and collateral. Nebraska insurance code does not explicitly address subordinated debt TAC treatment, requiring case-by-case Nebraska DOI approval. If Nebraska DOI grants only 25-50% TAC credit, the $150M subordinated debt would increase TAC by only $37.5M-$75M (vs. $150M for surplus notes), producing post-injection RBC ratio of 193-197% (potentially still below the 200% Company Action Level threshold).⁴¹

**Common Equity:** Equity contributions receive full 100% TAC credit (identical to surplus notes), but IRC § 118 prohibits tax deductions for capital contributions—equity provides no deduction to LLIC.⁴² More critically, Nebraska insurance statutes restrict dividend payments from insurers with RBC ratios below 200%, and industry practice requires RBC >300% before insurance commissioners approve dividends.⁴³ LLIC's post-injection RBC ratio of 204% would remain below the 300% informal threshold, preventing dividend payments to American Financial Holdings for an estimated 3-5 years while retained earnings accumulate. This makes equity economically equivalent to a zero-coupon instrument with uncertain maturity—the parent receives no return until LLIC's financial condition improves sufficiently to support dividends.⁴⁴

**Rule:** M&A purchase price adjustments for target company liabilities and contingencies follow probability-weighted expected value principles rather than simple aggregation of gross exposures. The American Bar Association's *Model Stock Purchase Agreement with Commentary* (2d ed. 2010) provides that adjustments should reflect "losses that are reasonably likely to occur" based on probability-weighted analysis.⁴⁶ For life insurance acquisitions specifically, the Society of Actuaries' guidance on embedded value appraisals requires that "the time value of options and guarantees" and "the value of financial options inherent in discretionary benefits" be quantified using stochastic modeling.⁴⁷

Purchase price adjustments typically employ a three-tier structure:
1. **Near-Certain Exposures (>70% probability):** Reflected dollar-for-dollar as price reduction
2. **Material Contingencies (25-70% probability):** Reflected as price reduction using probability weighting or escrow holdback
3. **Tail Risks (<25% probability):** Addressed solely through escrow or indemnification, not price adjustment⁴⁸

**Explanation:** The distinction between price adjustments and escrow mechanisms reflects timing and information asymmetry. Price adjustments address exposures where both parties have symmetric information at closing and can agree on expected value. Escrow addresses exposures where information will emerge post-closing (e.g., regulatory approval outcomes, litigation settlements, reinsurer consents) making pre-closing valuation difficult.⁴⁹

Insurance company acquisitions present unique valuation challenges because regulatory capital requirements create non-linear value destruction. An exposure that reduces statutory surplus by $100M does not simply reduce enterprise value by $100M—it may trigger regulatory intervention (RBC action levels), restrict dividends, increase reinsurance costs, cause rating downgrades, and create competitive disadvantages that multiply the economic impact.⁵⁰ This non-linearity justifies applying a "regulatory risk premium" (typically 1.25-1.5× multiplier) to exposures that threaten capital adequacy thresholds.⁵¹

#### B.5 Finding HIGH-003: IRR Analysis Shows 9.8% Return Below 15% Hurdle, Requiring Strategic Justification

**Conclusion:** Discounted cash flow analysis of the LLIC acquisition at the $2.76B adjusted purchase price (after $140M reduction) yields an internal rate of return of **9.8%** over a 10-year hold period, materially below American Financial Holdings' stated 15% hurdle rate for insurance acquisitions. Adjusted IRR including synergies: 12.8% (closer to hurdle but still below). **Severity:** HIGH (below-target returns require Board explicit acknowledgment). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: T6 DCF modeling; AFH standard underwriting criteria].

**Rule:** Private equity investment committees typically establish minimum IRR hurdle rates for each asset class reflecting required returns given risk profile. For life insurance acquisitions, industry surveys indicate PE firms target 12-18% IRR depending on insurer financial strength, product mix, and growth prospects.⁵⁴ Proceeding with acquisitions below hurdle rate requires documented strategic rationale showing that (1) non-financial benefits (platform value, synergies, tax attributes) justify the return shortfall, or (2) conservatism in base case assumptions means upside scenarios have reasonable probability of achieving hurdle returns.⁵⁵

The IRR calculation for insurance company acquisitions must account for several insurance-specific factors:
- **Dividend Restrictions:** Regulatory limits on dividend payments (typically RBC >300%) restrict cash distributions to parent company, affecting cash-on-cash returns
- **Statutory Earnings Volatility:** Statutory accounting produces more volatile reported earnings than GAAP, affecting exit valuation multiples
- **Capital Requirements:** Ongoing capital needs for product growth or RBC maintenance reduce distributable cash flow
- **Hold Period Assumptions:** Insurance companies typically require 7-10 year hold periods for PE investors due to market cyclicality and regulatory approval timelines for sales⁵⁶

**Explanation:** The difference between IRR hurdle rates and expected returns reflects the asymmetry of private equity economics. PE funds promise limited partners (LPs) target returns (typically 20-25% gross IRR for traditional PE, 12-18% for insurance PE) to attract capital commitments. After deducting management fees (2% annually) and carried interest (20-30% of profits above hurdle), LP net returns typically run 300-500 basis points below gross returns.⁵⁷ This means a PE fund targeting 15% LP net returns must generate 18-20% gross portfolio returns, making 12-15% gross returns on individual deals problematic unless offset by higher returns in other portfolio companies.

Insurance PE investors also face unique exit challenges. Strategic acquirers pay premiums for scale and market position, but the universe of strategic buyers for life insurers has contracted since 2008 (major banks exited life insurance; concentration among top 20 life insurers increased from 65% to 78% market share 2000-2024).⁵⁸ This buyer scarcity compresses exit multiples, requiring PE investors to create value through operational improvements and capital efficiency rather than multiple expansion.

**Application:** The T6 financial analyst's DCF model for LLIC projects cash flows under three scenarios (Base Case, Upside, Downside) and calculates IRR using terminal value based on market EBITDA multiples for comparable life insurers:

**Counter-Analysis:** American Financial Holdings' investment committee may challenge the acquisition on return grounds: (1) PE funds cannot justify below-hurdle acquisitions solely on strategic synergies because LPs evaluate fund performance on realized cash returns, not pro forma synergy claims; (2) the synergy value estimates ($12M admin savings, $25M P&C cross-sales) lack detailed implementation plans and may prove optimistic; (3) market timing arguments (multiple expansion from 8.5× to 12×) are speculative—multiples could remain depressed or compress further if recession occurs; (4) alternative uses of $2.76B capital might generate returns exceeding 9.8% with lower risk (e.g., portfolio of smaller insurance agencies at 15-18% IRR).

These challenges are substantial and highlight the tension between financial return discipline and strategic/platform building. The counterarguments must be addressed transparently:

On PE LP expectations (argument 1): While LP returns drive fund economics, sophisticated LPs distinguish between financial/growth equity strategies (targeting pure financial returns) and platform/roll-up strategies (building ecosystems with sub-hurdle core platforms but higher-return bolt-on acquisitions). If AFH is positioned as an insurance platform investment, LPs may accept 12-13% IRR on the anchor asset (LLIC) with expectations of 18-20% returns on subsequent bolt-ons that leverage LLIC's infrastructure.⁶⁵

On synergy execution risk (argument 2): The $12M admin savings are relatively low-risk because third-party admin contracts are cancellable and LLIC's existing infrastructure has proven capacity to support 2 million policies. On alternative capital deployment (argument 4): This is the strongest counterargument. If AFH has access to acquisitions generating 15%+ IRRs with similar risk, LLIC should be declined. The Board must confirm that (a) LLIC represents rare platform opportunity not easily replicated, and (b) pipeline of alternative opportunities is insufficient to deploy AFH's committed capital base.
