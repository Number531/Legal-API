## IV.I. EMPLOYMENT, LABOR, AND AGENT RETENTION

### A. Legal Framework

The legal framework governing employment and labor issues in the acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC) encompasses multiple federal and state statutes regulating workforce transitions, restrictive covenants, and employee benefits. This section addresses four primary areas of legal concern: (1) agent retention and attrition risk, (2) compliance with the Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act, (3) non-compete agreement enforceability, and (4) continuation of employee benefit plans under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) and the Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (COBRA).

#### 1. Agent Classification and Employment Law Applicability

LLIC employs 650 captive insurance agents as W-2 employees (not independent contractors) [FACT-REG: #044]. These agents are subject to the full panoply of employment law protections, including wage and hour laws, anti-discrimination statutes, and state-specific employment regulations. The IRS independent contractor classification analysis under Revenue Ruling 87-41 examines behavioral control, financial control, and relationship factors.¹ LLIC's captive agents satisfy the employee classification test because: (1) LLIC exercises behavioral control through training, supervision, and performance management; (2) LLIC provides employee benefits including health insurance and 401(k) retirement plans; and (3) agents execute employment agreements containing exclusivity provisions.²

This employee classification has material implications for retention strategies. Unlike independent contractors, captive agents are subject to non-compete agreements that may restrict post-departure competition, though enforceability varies dramatically by state jurisdiction. Additionally, employee status triggers WARN Act obligations if workforce reductions occur, creates ERISA fiduciary duties for benefit plan administration, and subjects the employer to state-specific employment regulations including non-compete restrictions in jurisdictions like California, Oklahoma, and North Dakota where such covenants are void per se.³

#### 2. Federal WARN Act Requirements

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act, codified at 29 U.S.C. § 2101 *et seq.*, imposes mandatory 60-day advance written notice requirements for plant closings and mass layoffs.⁴ The Act applies to employers with 100 or more full-time employees (or combined part-time/full-time totaling 4,000+ hours per week). LLIC, with 2,800 total employees including 1,900 at its Omaha headquarters, exceeds this threshold [FACT-REG: #043].

A "mass layoff" triggering WARN Act notice requirements occurs when: (1) 50-499 employees are terminated at a single employment site, AND (2) the terminations constitute at least 33% of the site's workforce, OR (3) 500 or more employees are terminated at a single site regardless of percentage.⁵ The Act's 90-day aggregation rule requires that employment losses occurring in two or more groups over a 90-day period be aggregated to determine whether triggering thresholds are met, unless the employer demonstrates that the separate actions are distinct and independent.⁶

Penalties for WARN Act violations include: (1) back pay for up to 60 days for each affected employee; (2) benefits including health insurance premiums for the 60-day period; and (3) civil penalties up to $500 per day to local government units for failure to provide required notice.⁷ For LLIC's Omaha headquarters (1,900 employees constituting a single employment site), termination of 650 or more employees (34% of workforce) without 60-day advance notice would trigger liability of approximately $6.41 million calculated as 650 employees × $60,000 average annual salary × (60/365 days) [FACT-REG: #049].⁸

Nebraska does not have a state-level "mini-WARN" statute imposing requirements stricter than federal law.⁹ However, LLIC operates in 38 states plus the District of Columbia, several of which have enacted mini-WARN statutes with lower thresholds or longer notice periods. For example, New York and New Jersey require 90-day advance notice (30 days more than federal WARN), and California applies to employers with 75 or more employees (versus 100 federally).¹⁰ Multi-state operations require state-by-state WARN Act compliance analysis if workforce reductions are contemplated.

#### 3. Non-Compete Agreement Enforceability

Non-compete agreements (restrictive covenants not to compete) are governed by state common law and statute, with enforceability varying dramatically across LLIC's 38-state operational footprint. The enforceability spectrum ranges from void per se (California, Oklahoma, North Dakota) to enforceable if reasonable (majority of jurisdictions including Nebraska) to highly restricted for employees below income thresholds (Colorado, Minnesota).

**California (Business and Professions Code § 16600):** California Business and Professions Code § 16600 provides that "every contract by which anyone is restrained from engaging in a lawful profession, trade, or business of any kind is to that extent void."¹¹ California courts have interpreted this statute to render employee non-compete agreements categorically unenforceable except in narrow circumstances (sale of business, dissolution of partnership, dissociation of partner).¹² This prohibition applies regardless of the reasonableness of the covenant's scope, duration, or geographic limitation. An estimated 15-20% of LLIC's 650 captive agents (approximately 98-131 agents) operate in California, Oklahoma, North Dakota, or Colorado where non-competes are void or highly restricted [FACT-REG: #050].¹³

**Nebraska (Home State Jurisdiction):** Nebraska follows the common law reasonableness standard for non-compete agreements, requiring that restrictive covenants be: (1) reasonable in duration (typically no more than 3 years); (2) reasonable in geographic scope; and (3) reasonably necessary to protect the employer's legitimate business interests.¹⁴ The Nebraska Supreme Court's decision in *Mertz v. Pharmacists Mutual Insurance Co.*, 625 N.W.2d 197 (Neb. 2001), is controlling authority for insurance agent non-compete agreements.¹⁵

In *Mertz*, the court held unenforceable a non-compete agreement that restricted an insurance agent from soliciting **all potential customers** in a territory, including customers with whom the agent had never worked or met.¹⁶ The court emphasized that non-compete agreements for insurance agents are enforceable only if limited to customers with whom the agent "actually did business and has personal contact."¹⁷ Critically, Nebraska courts apply a "no blue penciling" rule: if any provision of the covenant is unreasonable, the **entire covenant is void** and courts will not modify overbroad provisions to salvage enforceability.¹⁸ This all-or-nothing approach creates substantial risk if LLIC's standard agent employment agreement contains overbroad restrictions prohibiting all insurance sales (versus sales only to LLIC's existing customers) or covering excessive geographic territories.

**Other Jurisdictions:** In the remaining 33 states where LLIC operates, non-compete agreements are generally enforceable if reasonable in scope, duration, and geographic reach. Most jurisdictions require that the covenant: (1) protect a legitimate business interest (customer relationships, confidential information, specialized training); (2) impose no greater restraint than necessary; and (3) not be contrary to public policy.¹⁹ Success rates for enforcement vary from 50-75% in the best-case scenarios, with outcomes highly fact-dependent on the specific covenant language and circumstances of departure.²⁰

#### 4. ERISA and COBRA Requirements

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA), codified at 29 U.S.C. § 1001 *et seq.*, governs private employer-sponsored employee benefit plans including retirement plans, health insurance, life insurance, and disability coverage.²¹ ERISA imposes fiduciary duties on plan sponsors, requires participant disclosures (Summary Plan Descriptions, annual Form 5500 reporting), and prohibits discriminatory plan designs favoring highly compensated employees.²²

LLIC maintains ERISA-governed employee benefit plans for its 2,800 employees, including: (1) a 401(k) defined contribution retirement plan with employer matching contributions (estimated $6.72 million annual cost); (2) group health insurance (estimated $23.8 million annual employer premium); and (3) group life and disability insurance (estimated $2.24 million annual cost).²³ Total estimated annual employee benefits expense is approximately $32 million [INFERRED: T9 industry benchmark calculations].²⁴

The Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1985 (COBRA), codified at 29 U.S.C. § 1161 *et seq.*, requires employers with 20 or more employees to offer continuation of group health coverage to qualified beneficiaries upon qualifying events including termination of employment.²⁵ COBRA imposes administrative obligations but not premium subsidy requirements; qualified beneficiaries pay 102% of the applicable premium (100% of cost plus 2% administrative fee) for continuation coverage lasting 18 to 36 months depending on the qualifying event.²⁶

ERISA § 208 and Internal Revenue Code § 414(l) impose anti-cutback protections for plan mergers, prohibiting any reduction in accrued benefits when employers consolidate retirement plans.²⁷ These provisions are particularly relevant if American Financial Holdings (AFH) elects to merge LLIC's 401(k) plan into AFH's existing retirement plan post-acquisition. Participants' vested account balances are fully protected, and any change in investment lineup must offer comparable risk-return options to avoid breaching ERISA fiduciary duties.²⁸

**Explanation:** The insurance industry experiences predictable post-M&A disruption to distribution channels. AM Best's 2024 analysis of 47 life insurance acquisitions over the preceding decade found that acquirers experienced average captive agent attrition of 23.5% within 24 months when no proactive retention program was implemented.³³ Attrition drivers include: (1) ownership uncertainty regarding compensation stability, product portfolio changes, and management philosophy; (2) aggressive recruiting by competitors offering signing bonuses of $10,000-$25,000 to top producers; (3) cultural integration friction when acquirers impose new processes, technology systems, or production quotas; (4) non-compete unenforceability in key states (15-20% of agents in California, Oklahoma, North Dakota, Colorado face no legal barrier to immediate competitive activity); and (5) agent concerns about policyholder confidence following ownership change.³⁴

Industry precedent demonstrates the efficacy of retention bonuses. A 2023 study by Vantage Circle analyzing retention programs across financial services M&A found that employers implementing two-stage vesting bonuses (50% at 12 months, 50% at 24 months post-closing) with production requirements (maintain 80% of prior-year sales) achieved attrition rates of 10-15% versus 25-35% baseline.³⁵ The key design elements correlated with success included: (1) tiered incentives based on agent production value (higher bonuses for top producers); (2) staggered vesting to maintain retention pressure across 24-month critical period; (3) clawback provisions requiring repayment if agent departs within 6 months of payment; and (4) communication of program details within 30 days of acquisition announcement to forestall competitor recruiting.³⁶

#### B.3 WARN Act Compliance

**Conclusion:** American Financial Holdings' acquisition of LLIC presents **LOW** risk of WARN Act liability because AFH does not plan workforce reductions at LLIC's Omaha headquarters or other employment sites. If AFH later determines that workforce reductions are necessary, compliance with WARN Act 60-day advance notice requirements eliminates statutory liability. **Exposure:** $0 (no planned reductions). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: AFH's stated retention strategy; no workforce reduction plans disclosed in transaction planning].⁶⁶

**Rule:** The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act, 29 U.S.C. § 2101 *et seq.*, requires covered employers to provide 60 calendar days' advance written notice before mass layoffs or plant closings.⁶⁷ A "mass layoff" triggering notice requirements occurs when: (1) an employment loss affects 50-499 employees during any 30-day period at a single employment site, AND (2) the employment loss represents at least one-third of the active workforce at that site; OR (3) 500 or more employees experience employment loss at a single site regardless of percentage.⁶⁸ "Employment loss" includes termination (other than for cause, voluntary departure, or retirement), layoff exceeding 6 months, or reduction in work hours by more than 50% for 6 months or more.⁶⁹

Employers who violate WARN Act notice requirements are liable to affected employees for: (1) back pay at the employee's final rate of compensation for up to 60 days; (2) the value of benefits (including health insurance premiums) that would have been provided during the 60-day period; and (3) civil penalties of up to $500 per day payable to local government units for failure to provide notice.⁷⁰ Penalties are calculated on a per-employee basis, making large-scale workforce reductions without notice extraordinarily expensive.

**Explanation:** The WARN Act's legislative purpose is to provide workers and communities advance notice of job loss, enabling workers to seek new employment or training opportunities before termination becomes effective.⁷¹ Courts interpret WARN Act thresholds strictly; employers bear the burden of demonstrating that notice exceptions apply (unforeseen business circumstances, faltering company exceptions).⁷² The 90-day aggregation rule prevents employers from circumventing notice requirements through staged reductions, requiring that separate employment actions within 90 days be aggregated unless the employer proves that distinct business decisions motivated each action.⁷³

LLIC's Omaha headquarters (1,900 employees) constitutes a "single site of employment" for WARN Act purposes [FACT-REG: #043].⁷⁴ Termination of 650 or more employees (34% of the Omaha workforce) would trigger mass layoff provisions requiring 60-day advance notice.⁷⁵ If AFH provided no notice, liability would equal approximately $6.41 million, calculated as:

- 650 employees × $60,000 average annual compensation = $39 million total annual wages
- $39 million ÷ 365 days × 60 days = $6.41 million back pay
- Plus health insurance premiums: 650 employees × $12,000 annual premium ÷ 365 × 60 days = $1.28 million
- Total: $7.69 million (T9 uses $6.41 million figure excluding benefits for conservative estimate) [FACT-REG: #049]⁷⁶

State mini-WARN statutes may impose stricter requirements. New York and New Jersey require 90-day notice (versus 60 days federally), and California applies to employers with 75 or more employees (versus 100 federally).⁷⁷ LLIC's multi-state operations require jurisdiction-specific analysis if workforce reductions are contemplated in states with mini-WARN statutes.

**Application:** American Financial Holdings has not disclosed plans for workforce reductions at LLIC. To the contrary, AFH's acquisition strategy emphasizes retention of LLIC's workforce to ensure operational continuity and maintain customer relationships.⁷⁸ The $46 million combined agent and producer retention investment [$22 million captive agents + $24 million independent producers] reflects AFH's commitment to preserving the distribution channel rather than downsizing operations [FACT-REG: #048].⁷⁹

If AFH later determines that workforce reductions are necessary for operational efficiency or cost synergies, WARN Act compliance requires:

1. **Threshold Analysis:** Determine whether planned reductions meet mass layoff thresholds:
   - Omaha headquarters: Reduction of 634 or more employees triggers 33% threshold (34% of 1,900)
   - Regional offices: Assess each site separately for single-site employment loss thresholds

2. **Notice Preparation:** Provide 60-day advance written notice to:
   - Each affected employee (or authorized representative if union represented)
   - Nebraska State Dislocated Worker Unit
   - Chief elected official of local government unit where employment site is located⁸⁰

3. **Notice Content:** Include employer name and address, employment site address, contact information, anticipated date of layoff, whether layoff is permanent or temporary, job titles affected, and whether bumping rights exist.⁸¹

4. **State Compliance:** Analyze mini-WARN requirements for any states where reductions occur (90-day notice for New York/New Jersey operations).

  - Probability of workforce reductions without notice: 0% (no reductions planned; if planned, notice will be provided)
  - **Expected value: $0** [FACT-REG: #049]

- **Discount Rate Basis:** Not applicable (zero exposure)

**Probability Assessment:**
0% probability of WARN Act liability [METHODOLOGY: (1) AFH has not disclosed workforce reduction plans; (2) retention strategy emphasizes workforce preservation; (3) if reductions later become necessary, 60-day notice eliminates statutory liability].⁸²

**Counter-Analysis:** LLIC's Seller may argue that AFH's retention assurances are not binding post-closing and that market conditions or integration challenges could necessitate workforce reductions within 12 months of closing. This concern has merit from a risk allocation perspective. However, three factors mitigate this risk: (1) WARN Act compliance is ministerial—providing 60-day notice eliminates statutory liability even if reductions occur; (2) AFH's financial model assumes workforce retention, and deviations from plan would require board-level approval creating internal controls against precipitous layoffs; and (3) insurance industry M&A precedent shows that acquirers rarely reduce home office headcount by more than 10-15% (190-285 employees at Omaha), well below the 634-employee threshold triggering mass layoff provisions.⁸³ For contractual protection, the Purchase Agreement should include a representation that AFH has no current plan to implement workforce reductions triggering WARN Act requirements, and indemnification provisions should allocate WARN Act liability to AFH for post-closing actions.

#### B.4 ERISA and COBRA Compliance

**Conclusion:** LLIC's employee benefit plans (401(k) retirement, group health, life, and disability insurance) present **LOW** risk and routine compliance obligations under ERISA and COBRA. Annual benefits expense of approximately $32 million continues post-acquisition, and compliance costs for plan administration, COBRA notice procedures, and potential plan merger are estimated at $50,000-$150,000. **Exposure:** $50K-$150K compliance costs. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: T9 analysis of standard ERISA plan compliance; industry benchmark benefits administration costs].⁸⁷

**Rule:** The Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA), 29 U.S.C. § 1001 *et seq.*, imposes fiduciary duties on employers sponsoring employee benefit plans, requiring that plan fiduciaries act "solely in the interest of participants and beneficiaries" with "the care, skill, prudence, and diligence under the circumstances then prevailing that a prudent man acting in a like capacity and familiar with such matters would use."⁸⁸ ERISA mandates participant disclosures including Summary Plan Descriptions (SPDs), Summary Annual Reports, and annual Form 5500 reporting to the Department of Labor.⁸⁹

ERISA § 208 and Internal Revenue Code § 414(l) impose anti-cutback protections for plan mergers: any merger or consolidation of qualified retirement plans cannot reduce the accrued benefit of any participant.⁹⁰ For defined contribution plans like LLIC's 401(k), this requires that participants' vested account balances be fully protected in any plan transfer or merger.⁹¹ If American Financial Holdings merges LLIC's 401(k) plan into AFH's existing retirement plan, participants must receive advance notice of investment option changes, and the merged plan must offer investment alternatives with comparable risk-return characteristics to satisfy ERISA fiduciary duties.⁹²

COBRA (29 U.S.C. § 1161 *et seq.*) requires employers with 20 or more employees to offer continuation coverage to qualified beneficiaries following qualifying events including termination of employment.⁹³ Critically, COBRA imposes **administrative obligations** but not premium subsidy requirements: qualified beneficiaries pay 102% of the applicable premium (100% of cost plus 2% administrative charge) for coverage lasting 18 months (termination), 36 months (divorce, death), or 29 months (disability within first 60 days of COBRA).⁹⁴ Employers face penalties for COBRA notice failures: Internal Revenue Code § 4980B imposes excise taxes of $100 per day per affected individual (up to maximum of $200 per family per day), and ERISA § 502(c) authorizes Department of Labor civil penalties of up to $110 per day per participant.⁹⁵

**Explanation:** ERISA compliance is a routine operational matter for established employers like LLIC. The statute's requirements—participant communications, Form 5500 filing, fiduciary oversight of plan administration and investment options—are well-established and supported by robust third-party administrator (TPA) infrastructure.⁹⁶ Post-acquisition compliance issues typically arise in three scenarios: (1) failure to provide COBRA notices timely following terminations; (2) imprudent investment option changes in 401(k) plan mergers violating ERISA fiduciary duties; or (3) inadequate participant communication regarding benefit plan changes causing confusion or missed election deadlines.⁹⁷

The most complex ERISA issue in M&A contexts is 401(k) plan integration. Employers must choose among three options: (1) maintain LLIC's 401(k) plan separately indefinitely; (2) merge LLIC's plan into AFH's existing 401(k) plan (requiring 12-24 months for participant communication, system integration, and regulatory filings); or (3) terminate LLIC's plan and distribute participant account balances (requiring 6-12 months and triggering participant rollover decisions).⁹⁸ Industry practice strongly favors Option 1 or 2; Option 3 (plan termination) is disfavored because it forces participants to make immediate rollover decisions and may result in taxable distributions for participants who fail to roll over to IRAs or new employer plans within 60 days.⁹⁹
