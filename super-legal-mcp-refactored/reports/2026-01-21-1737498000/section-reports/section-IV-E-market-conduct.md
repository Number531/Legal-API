### A. Legal Framework

The Nebraska Department of Insurance (Nebraska DOI) conducts market conduct examinations of Nebraska-domiciled insurers pursuant to comprehensive statutory authority designed to protect policyholders and ensure regulatory compliance. Liberty Life Insurance Company (LLIC), as a Nebraska-domiciled life insurer, is subject to periodic market conduct examinations covering sales practices, underwriting, claims handling, advertising, agent licensing, complaint handling, policy form compliance, and premium billing.

#### 1. Nebraska Examination Authority

**Primary Statutory Authority:** Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-5901 *et seq.* (Insurers Examination Act) grants the Director of Insurance broad authority to conduct examinations of licensed insurers to verify compliance with Nebraska insurance laws and regulations.¹ [VERIFIED: Nebraska Legislature website] The Act provides:

> "The director may make an examination of the affairs of any company as often as he or she deems it prudent, but shall, at a minimum, make an examination of every company not less frequently than once every five years."²

**Additional Authority:** Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-105 grants the Department authority to examine insurance companies before issuing certificates of authority and during operations to ensure they meet state requirements and protect shareholders, policyholders, creditors, and the public.³ [VERIFIED: Nebraska Legislature website]

#### 2. NAIC Market Conduct Examination Standards

Nebraska DOI conducts market conduct examinations following the **NAIC Market Regulation Handbook**, which establishes uniform examination procedures adopted by state insurance regulators nationwide.⁴ [VERIFIED: NAIC website] The Handbook is updated annually to reflect newly adopted NAIC model laws, regulations, and bulletins, providing standardized methodologies for:

- Examination planning and scoping
- Fieldwork procedures and sampling methodologies
- Report preparation and exit conference protocols
- Follow-up and corrective action monitoring

Comprehensive market conduct examinations typically cover a three-year examination period and assess eight core functional areas: (1) sales practices and illustrations, (2) underwriting practices, (3) claims handling, (4) advertising and marketing, (5) agent licensing and appointments, (6) consumer complaint handling, (7) policy form compliance, and (8) premium billing and accounting.⁵ [VERIFIED: NAIC Market Regulation Handbook 2024]

#### 3. Sales Illustrations Regulatory Framework

**NAIC Model Regulation 582:** The NAIC Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation (Model #582) establishes minimum standards for life insurance policy illustrations to protect consumers and foster consumer education.⁶ [VERIFIED: NAIC website] The regulation distinguishes between:

- **Guaranteed elements:** Premiums, benefits, values determined at policy issue and guaranteed in the policy
- **Non-guaranteed elements:** Premiums, benefits, values not guaranteed or not determined at issue (e.g., dividends on participating whole life, credited interest rates on universal life/indexed universal life)

**Nebraska Adoption:** Nebraska has adopted NAIC Model 582 in **Title 210, Chapter 72** of the Nebraska Administrative Code.⁷ [VERIFIED: Justia Regulations] The regulation requires that "basic illustrations" must be ledgers or proposals showing **both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements in side-by-side columns** with clear disclosure language stating "Non-guaranteed elements subject to change."⁸

**Actuarial Guideline 49 (AG49):** AG49, adopted by the NAIC in 2015 and effective January 1, 2016, establishes additional illustration requirements specifically for indexed universal life (IUL) products to prevent misleading illustrations showing unrealistically high credited interest rates.⁹ [VERIFIED: NAIC website] AG49 requires:

- Maximum illustrated rate capped at the **greater of:** (a) the average of credited rates over the preceding 12 months, or (b) the current credited rate
- Illustrations must show both the maximum illustrated rate and a lower "disciplined current scale" rate
- Annual certification by appointed actuary that illustrated rates comply with AG49 methodology

**Delivery and Retention Requirements:** If a basic illustration is used in the sale of a life insurance policy, a signed copy must be submitted to the insurer at time of policy application and provided to the applicant. The insurer must retain copies for three years after the policy is no longer in force.¹⁰ [VERIFIED: Nebraska Title 210, Chapter 72, Section 210-72-009]

#### 4. Replacement Forms Regulatory Framework

**Nebraska Replacement Regulation:** Nebraska **Title 210, Chapter 19** regulates the replacement of existing life insurance and annuities to protect purchasers by establishing minimum standards of conduct.¹¹ [VERIFIED: Nebraska DOI website] The regulation is promulgated pursuant to Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-1533 and 44-101.01 and aims to:

- Assure purchasers receive information to make decisions in their best interest
- Reduce opportunities for misrepresentation and incomplete disclosures
- Establish penalties for non-compliance

**Replacement Definition:** "Replacement" occurs when a policyholder surrenders an existing policy (from another insurer or an older policy from the same insurer) and purchases a new policy, potentially losing accumulated benefits, incurring surrender charges, or facing higher premiums due to increased age.¹²

**Required Elements:** When replacement occurs, insurers and producers must provide:

- **Notice Regarding Replacement form** delivered to applicant at time of application
- **Comparison disclosure** showing existing policy versus proposed policy (premiums, death benefits, cash values, surrender charges, policy loans)
- **Loss of benefits disclosure** explaining surrender charges and forfeiture of accumulated benefits (e.g., dividends, lower premium rates based on younger issue age)
- **Agent and applicant signatures** on Notice form
- **Copy of Notice provided to existing insurer** (conservation opportunity for existing insurer to contact policyholder)

#### 5. Claims Handling Regulatory Framework

**Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act:** Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-1536 to 44-1544 establish the Unfair Insurance Claims Settlement Practices Act.¹³ [VERIFIED: Nebraska Legislature website] Section 44-1540 prohibits unfair claims settlement practices including:

- Knowingly misrepresenting relevant facts or policy provisions
- Failing to acknowledge with reasonable promptness pertinent communications regarding claims
- **Failing to adopt and implement reasonable standards for prompt investigation and settlement of claims**
- **Not attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt, fair, and equitable settlement of claims when liability has become reasonably clear**¹⁴

**30-Day Death Claims Payment Requirement:** Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-3,143 requires life insurance companies to pay interest on death benefit proceeds if four conditions are met: (1) the insured was a Nebraska resident on date of death, (2) death occurred on or after June 6, 1991, (3) the beneficiary elects in writing to receive proceeds in lump-sum payment, and (4) payment is not made within **30 days** of the insurer receiving proof of death.¹⁵ [VERIFIED: Nebraska Legislature website]

Interest accrues from day 31 onward at the statutory rate specified in Neb. Rev. Stat. § 45-103 until payment is made.¹⁶ Violations of § 44-3,143 automatically constitute unfair claims settlement practices under §§ 44-1536 *et seq.*

#### 6. Administrative Penalty Authority

**Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-1542** authorizes the Director of Insurance to impose monetary penalties for violations of the Unfair Insurance Claims Settlement Practices Act:¹⁷ [VERIFIED: Nebraska Legislature website]

- **Standard penalty:** Not more than **$1,000 per violation**, aggregate cap **$30,000**
- **Enhanced penalty:** If violation committed "flagrantly and in conscious disregard" of the Act, not more than **$15,000 per violation**, aggregate cap **$150,000**

The Director may also issue cease and desist orders, suspend or revoke licenses, and require corrective action plans to remedy identified deficiencies.¹⁸
