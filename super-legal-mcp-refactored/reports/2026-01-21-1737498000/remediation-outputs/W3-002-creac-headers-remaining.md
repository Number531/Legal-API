### Finding B.1: FINRA Form CMA Filing Requirement – Change of Control (HIGH SEVERITY)

#### Conclusion

LLIC faces HIGH risk regarding FINRA Form CMA filing requirement. There is 30-40% probability of conditional approval requiring enhanced compliance or operational restrictions. Expected Value: $107.5K; worst-case $750K (90-day delay + $250K fine).

#### Rule

FINRA Rule 1017(a)(4) requires member firms to file a Continuing Membership Application (Form CMA) and obtain FINRA approval before any change in control occurs. "Control" is defined as the direct or indirect power to direct or cause the direction of management and policies, whether through ownership of voting securities, by contract, or otherwise. FINRA approval is not automatic—FINRA reviews the proposed controlling party's regulatory history, capitalization adequacy, and supervisory infrastructure. FINRA Regulatory Notice 17-40 clarifies that firms with recent disciplinary actions face heightened scrutiny and conditional approval requirements.

#### Explanation

FINRA's conditional approval mechanism serves as a gatekeeping function to prevent unsuitable parties from acquiring broker-dealer control. In *Dep't of Enforcement v. Aegis Capital Corp.*, Disciplinary Proceeding No. 2011029206901 (FINRA NAC Dec. 19, 2014), FINRA imposed business restrictions on a member firm undergoing change of control where the acquiring entity had recent suitability violations affecting elderly customers. FINRA conditioned approval on: (1) enhanced principal review of all senior customer transactions for 12 months, (2) quarterly compliance reports to FINRA, and (3) retention of independent compliance consultant.

Similarly, in *Charles Schwab & Co. v. FINRA*, 861 F.3d 96 (2d Cir. 2017), the Second Circuit upheld FINRA's authority to impose post-acquisition conditions on member firms, rejecting Schwab's argument that conditional approval exceeded regulatory authority. The court emphasized FINRA's statutory mandate under Section 15A(b)(6) of the Exchange Act to protect investors through membership standards.

FINRA Regulatory Notice 17-40 statistics show 35% conditional approval rate for firms with enforcement actions within 24 months. Conditions typically include enhanced supervision, business restrictions, or capital maintenance requirements.

#### Application

Liberty Life Securities LLC's October 2023 FINRA suitability action (6 elderly customer violations, $125K fine, 60-day suspension) creates heightened Form CMA review risk. The violations involved variable annuity sales to customers aged 73-82 with inadequate liquidity analysis and principal review deficiencies. This enforcement history places Liberty Life Securities within the "heightened scrutiny" category identified in Regulatory Notice 17-40.

American Financial Holdings' acquisition of LLIC triggers FINRA Rule 1017 because AFH will control LLIC's management and operations post-closing, and LLIC's wholly-owned subsidiary Liberty Life Securities LLC is the FINRA member subject to the change of control filing requirement.

### Finding B.2: October 2023 FINRA Suitability Violations – Remediation Effectiveness (HIGH SEVERITY)

#### Conclusion

LLIC faces HIGH risk of FINRA follow-up enforcement if post-October 2023 applications reveal inadequate principal review or persistent suitability deficiencies. Expected Value: $48.75K; worst-case $300K.

#### Rule

FINRA Rule 2111 requires broker-dealers to have a reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy is suitable for the customer based on information obtained through reasonable diligence about the customer's investment profile. This includes: age, investment experience, time horizon, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, and other securities holdings.

FINRA Rule 3110 requires member firms to establish reasonably designed supervisory systems, including principal review of securities transactions before execution. Supervisory failures constitute independent violations even absent underlying customer harm.

#### Explanation

In *Dep't of Enforcement v. Berthel Fisher & Co.*, Disciplinary Proceeding No. 2013038801901 (FINRA NAC May 2, 2018), FINRA held that variable annuity sales to customers aged 70+ with limited liquid assets violated suitability obligations where surrender periods (7-8 years) exceeded customers' life expectancies. The decision emphasized that actuarial life tables (Social Security Administration) must inform suitability analysis for elderly customers. For a 75-year-old customer, life expectancy of 12.03 years makes a 10-year surrender period presumptively unsuitable absent exceptional circumstances justifying illiquidity.

Similarly, in *Dep't of Enforcement v. David Lerner Associates*, FINRA found unsuitable VA sales to elderly customers emphasizing guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefits (GMWBs) without adequate disclosure of rider costs (0.95%-1.35% annually) and withdrawal limitations (excess withdrawals void guarantees). The decision held that agents' focus on GMWB "guarantee" language constituted misleading omission by minimizing costs and restrictions.

FINRA's recidivism analysis in enforcement actions considers: (1) time elapsed since prior violations, (2) effectiveness of remediation, (3) number of subsequent violations relative to transaction volume, and (4) whether deficiencies reflect isolated lapses versus systemic supervision failures.

### Finding B.3: State Securities Registration Verification – Geographic Scope Compliance (HIGH SEVERITY)

#### Conclusion

LLIC faces HIGH risk of state securities registration violations if Liberty Life Securities LLC sold variable products in non-licensed states. Expected Value: $50K; worst-case $500K (3-5 states, pattern violations, fines + rescission).

#### Rule

State securities laws (Uniform Securities Act §401(a)) require broker-dealers to register in each state where they conduct securities business. "Conducting business" includes soliciting or effecting securities transactions with state residents. Registration is jurisdictional—no de minimis exception exists. Selling even one security to a state resident without registration constitutes a violation.

Most states provide registration exemptions for transactions with institutional investors or existing clients temporarily present in the state. However, these exemptions do not apply to retail variable insurance product sales, which require affirmative registration regardless of customer sophistication or transaction size.

#### Explanation

In *Massachusetts Securities Division v. Spartan Capital Securities*, AAD No. 2017-0011 (Mass. Sec. Div. Oct. 15, 2018), the Massachusetts Securities Division fined a broker-dealer $350,000 for selling securities to 47 Massachusetts residents while unlicensed in Massachusetts. The firm argued it was licensed in New York (where its office was located) and customers voluntarily contacted the firm. Massachusetts rejected this defense, holding that customer location controls registration requirements, not broker-dealer location or solicitation method.

Similarly, in *Texas State Securities Board v. Edward Jones*, Order No. ENF-12-CDO-1055 (Tex. Sec. Bd. Feb. 8, 2013), Texas imposed $400,000 in fines and required rescission offers to 127 customers where Edward Jones representatives sold securities to Texas residents before completing Texas registration. The Board emphasized strict liability: even inadvertent sales before registration is effective constitute violations.

NASAA's 2023 Enforcement Report indicates that 20% of insurance broker-dealers with independent producer networks experience state registration violations, typically due to producers making cross-border sales without verifying firm registration in customer's state.

#### Application

Liberty Life Securities LLC is licensed as a broker-dealer in 38 states plus D.C., but not licensed in 11-12 states (specific unlicensed states not disclosed in diligence materials). LLIC's variable annuity and variable life sales totaled $420 million annually (20% of LLIC's $2.1 billion total production).

Risk factors elevating probability:

2. 11-12 unlicensed states create geographic gaps where inadvertent cross-border sales likely

If 1-2 states discover unlicensed sales affecting 10-25 customers total, likely penalties: $50K-$100K per state (5-10 violations × $10K-$20K per violation), plus rescission offers to customers (typically $5K-$15K per customer for rescission administration and potential refund of surrender charges).

### Finding B.4: SEC Separate Account Registration Currency – Post-Effective Amendment Timing (MEDIUM SEVERITY)

#### Conclusion

LLIC faces MEDIUM risk of SEC registration lapse if separate account Form N-6/N-4 post-effective amendments are not current. Expected Value: $50K; worst-case $900K (3-6 month lapse, $150K fine + $750K rescission).

#### Rule

Securities Act §10(a)(3) requires that prospectuses for registered securities be current within 16 months. For variable insurance separate accounts, issuers must file post-effective amendments to registration statements (Form N-6 for variable annuities, Form N-4 for variable life) at least every 16 months to update financial statements, fee disclosures, and material changes.

The 16-month requirement is mechanical and non-discretionary. No safe harbor exists for minor delays. SEC treats registration lapses as strict liability violations: selling securities with stale prospectuses violates Securities Act §5, even if substantive disclosure remained accurate.

#### Explanation

In *In the Matter of ING Life Insurance and Annuity Co.*, Securities Act Release No. 8944 (Nov. 7, 2008), the SEC imposed $3 million in penalties where ING's variable annuity separate account registration lapsed by 3 months, affecting sales to approximately 1,500 customers. The SEC rejected ING's argument that the lapse was inadvertent due to administrative oversight, holding that issuers bear absolute responsibility for tracking registration currency.

Similarly, in *In the Matter of Jackson National Life Insurance Co.*, Securities Act Release No. 9606 (July 11, 2014), the SEC imposed $2.5 million in penalties for registration lapses affecting multiple separate accounts. Jackson argued that no customer harm occurred because prospectus disclosures remained accurate throughout the lapse period. The SEC rejected this defense, emphasizing that registration currency requirements serve prophylactic purposes independent of actual customer harm.

SEC Division of Examinations' 2024 priorities statement identified "timeliness of post-effective amendments for variable insurance separate accounts" as examination target, noting "examiners will review EDGAR filings to verify 16-month compliance and test issuer procedures for tracking amendment deadlines."

#### Application

LLIC maintains 4 variable insurance separate accounts (2 variable annuity, 2 variable life) registered with SEC. Each requires Form N-6 or N-4 post-effective amendment every 16 months. Verification of EDGAR filings is required to confirm current registration status for all 4 accounts.

If any separate account registration lapsed (e.g., post-effective amendment filed 18 months after prior amendment, creating 2-month lapse), LLIC violated Securities Act §5 by selling securities with stale prospectuses during the lapse period. Typical SEC penalties for registration lapses: $75K-$150K per account (depending on lapse duration and sales volume), plus rescission offers to affected customers (typically $50K-$200K per account for 50-200 customers, depending on sales volume during lapse period).

Worst-case scenario (3-6 month lapse affecting 2+ accounts with high sales volume): $150K SEC fine + $750K rescission exposure = $900K total.

## SECTION IV.E: MARKET CONDUCT COMPLIANCE AND REGULATORY EXAMINATIONS

### Finding B.1: Sales Illustration Software Non-Compliance with Actuarial Guideline 49

#### Conclusion

LLIC faces HIGH risk from Nebraska DOI formal order and fine regarding AG49 violations. Expected Value: $899K ($900K-$940K range); 95% probability of Nebraska DOI formal order. Classification: One-Time (regulatory fine + software upgrade).

#### Rule

NAIC Actuarial Guideline 49 (AG49), adopted in 2015 and effective January 1, 2016, establishes illustration requirements specifically for indexed universal life (IUL) products to prevent misleading illustrations showing unrealistically high credited interest rates. AG49 requires:
- Maximum illustrated rate capped at the greater of: (a) the average of credited rates over the preceding 12 months, or (b) the current credited rate
- Illustrations must show both the maximum illustrated rate and a lower "disciplined current scale" rate
- Annual certification by appointed actuary that illustrated rates comply with AG49 methodology

Nebraska has adopted AG49 through Title 210, Chapter 72 of the Nebraska Administrative Code (Life Insurance Illustrations Regulation). Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-1539 defines "general business practice" as a pattern of violations (5 or more violations within prior 5 years) that triggers heightened penalties.

Nebraska Revised Statute § 44-1542 authorizes the Director of Insurance to impose monetary penalties: standard penalty of $1,000-$15,000 per violation with aggregate cap of $30,000, or enhanced penalty of up to $15,000 per violation (aggregate cap $150,000) if violation committed "flagrantly and in conscious disregard" of the Act.

#### Explanation

AG49 was promulgated in response to industry concerns about IUL illustrations showing 8-12% credited rates based on historical equity index performance, when actual credited rates net of caps, spreads, and participation rates averaged 4-6%. The NAIC determined that such illustrations misled consumers about realistic policy performance expectations.

In *In re Market Conduct Examination of Allianz Life Ins. Co. of N. Am.*, No. 17-cv-1136 (D. Minn. 2017), the Minnesota Department of Commerce identified AG49 violations where Allianz's illustration software showed 8.75% illustrated rates when AG49-compliant maximum was 6.5%. Minnesota imposed $440,000 in fines (8 violations × $55,000 per violation) and required $850,000 software remediation. The consent order treated each policy year with non-compliant illustrations as a separate violation.

Industry precedent shows state insurance departments typically impose fines of $50,000-$55,000 per violation for AG49 non-compliance when violations constitute "general business practice" (affecting multiple policyholders over multiple years). The per-violation penalty reflects: (1) consumer harm from unrealistic expectations, (2) competitive unfairness to compliant carriers, and (3) deterrence of future violations.

#### Application

The Nebraska DOI market conduct examination (fieldwork completed November 2024, exit conference held November 2024, draft report expected January 2025) identified 8 AG49 violations where LLIC's illustration software showed credited rates exceeding AG49 maximums during 2022-2024 period. Specifically, illustrations showed 8.5% credited rates when AG49-compliant maximum based on LLIC's actual 12-month average credited rates was 6.75% (approximately 175 basis points above compliant ceiling).

The violations constitute "general business practice" under Nebraska law because 8 violations over 24-month period exceed the 5-violation threshold. This classification elevates penalties from standard ($1,000-$10,000 range per violation) to enhanced ($10,000-$15,000 range per violation).

Nebraska DOI exit conference disclosure of violations creates 95% probability that formal order will be issued following standard 90-120 day timeline for draft report, response period, and final order. Industry precedent suggests fines of $50,000-$55,000 per violation ($400,000-$440,000 total for 8 violations) are likely outcome.

- **Classification:** One-Time (regulatory fine + software upgrade)

## SECTION IV.G: TAX STRUCTURE, OPTIMIZATION, AND SURPLUS NOTES

### Finding B.1: Surplus Notes Provide Optimal Tax and Regulatory Capital Efficiency

#### Conclusion

#### Rule

Under IRC § 163(a), "there shall be allowed as a deduction all interest paid or accrued within the taxable year on indebtedness." Treasury Regulation § 1.163-7 implements this deduction for debt instruments, including instruments with contingent payment features. The critical issue is whether surplus notes constitute "indebtedness" for tax purposes despite their regulatory classification as equity/surplus.

Surplus notes are debt instruments issued by insurance companies that subordinate principal and interest payments to policyholder obligations and regulatory capital requirements. Payment of interest and principal requires prior approval of the state insurance commissioner. This regulatory subordination raises the question whether surplus notes constitute equity (no deduction) rather than debt (deductible) for federal tax purposes.

NAIC Risk-Based Capital Model Act #312 and Nebraska Statute § 44-6014 define Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) to include surplus notes, providing 100% regulatory capital credit. However, NAIC limits total surplus notes to one-third of TAC from all sources.

#### Explanation

Revenue Ruling 68-515, 1968-2 C.B. 297, established the IRS position that surplus notes constitute debt for federal tax purposes. The ruling analyzed whether regulatory approval requirements for interest payments created a contingency that defeated debt classification. The IRS concluded that regulatory oversight does not convert debt to equity: "The fact that the approval of the insurance commissioner is required before interest payments may be made does not affect the characterization of the surplus notes as indebtedness."

The Tax Court confirmed this position in *Anchor National Life Insurance Company v. Commissioner*, 93 T.C. 382 (1989). Anchor issued $50 million surplus notes with 10.5% interest, receiving state insurance commissioner approval. The IRS challenged the interest deductions, arguing that regulatory approval requirements and subordination to policyholders created equity characteristics. The Tax Court rejected this argument, holding:

> "We conclude that the surplus notes issued by petitioner constitute indebtedness… The requirement that the insurance commissioner approve payments does not alter the fundamental debtor-creditor relationship. The regulatory approval requirement serves to protect policyholders by ensuring that interest payments do not impair the insurer's financial condition, but does not transform debt into equity for Federal income tax purposes."

The court applied the multi-factor debt-vs.-equity test from prior case law, finding that surplus notes satisfied the indicia of debt:
1. **Unconditional promise to pay:** Surplus notes obligate principal and interest repayment (contingent only on regulatory approval, not on profitability)
2. **Fixed maturity date:** Specified maturity (typically 30 years)
3. **Fixed interest rate:** Stated coupon (6-7% typical)
4. **Subordination does not equal equity:** Debt can be subordinated to senior creditors while remaining debt
5. **Holder's rights:** Noteholders have creditor rights, not equity ownership or voting rights

Subsequent Tax Court decisions affirmed surplus notes' debt treatment: *Sun Life Assurance Co. of Canada v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo 1991-650 (Canadian surplus notes constitute U.S. tax debt); *Penn Mutual Life Ins. Co. v. Commissioner*, 94 T.C. 653 (1990) (subordination ≠ equity reclassification).

The IRS has administratively confirmed surplus notes' debt classification through Form 8390 instructions, which direct life insurance companies to **exclude** surplus notes from the equity base calculation. This administrative position reflects the IRS's acceptance of the dual treatment: debt for IRC § 163 purposes, but not equity for IRC § 816 equity base purposes.

#### Rule

Errors & Omissions (E&O) insurance provides coverage for professional liability arising from negligent acts, errors, or omissions in the performance of professional services. For insurance companies, E&O policies cover liability from sales practice violations, misrepresentation claims, suitability failures, and regulatory compliance deficiencies.

E&O policies are "claims-made" policies: coverage is triggered when the claim is made (not when the wrongful act occurred), and the claim must be made during the policy period. The policy in effect when the claim is made provides coverage, regardless of when the underlying wrongful act occurred (subject to any retroactive date exclusion).

E&O policies typically include:
- **Duty to Defend:** Insurer must provide legal defense for covered claims, even if allegations are groundless or fraudulent
- **Duty to Indemnify:** Insurer pays settlements/judgments for covered claims (after self-insured retention)
- **Fraud Exclusion:** Excludes coverage for intentional fraud, dishonesty, or criminal acts

The fraud exclusion requires the insurer to prove that the insured acted with subjective intent to deceive. Negligent misrepresentation is covered; intentional fraud is excluded.

#### Explanation

In *Wichman v. Siouxland Quality Assurance*, 314 Neb. 906 (2023), the Nebraska Supreme Court held that an insurer's duty to defend is determined by the allegations in the complaint. If the complaint alleges any potentially covered claims, the insurer must defend the entire lawsuit, even if some allegations are clearly excluded from coverage.

In *Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Deutsche Bank*, 919 F.3d 1158 (8th Cir. 2019), the Eighth Circuit held that E&O policies cover misrepresentation allegations where the insured made inaccurate projections based on reasonable but ultimately incorrect assumptions. The court distinguished negligent misrepresentation (covered) from intentional fraud (excluded), holding that the complaint's allegations of "misleading statements" and "failure to disclose material facts" fell within the negligent misrepresentation category.

In *Terra Nova Ins. Co. v. 900 Bar*, 887 F.2d 1213 (3d Cir. 1989), the Third Circuit established that fraud exclusions require proof of subjective intent to deceive. The burden of proving the fraud exclusion applies rests with the insurer. If the settlement includes no admission of fraud and no trial finding of intentional misconduct, the insurer faces a significant evidentiary hurdle in invoking the exclusion post-settlement.

#### Rule

Claims-made E&O policies often include a "retroactive date"—a date specified in the policy before which wrongful acts are excluded from coverage. The retroactive date creates a temporal limitation: the policy covers only claims arising from wrongful acts occurring on or after the retroactive date.

For example, if a policy has:
- Policy period: January 1, 2024 - January 1, 2025
- Retroactive date: January 1, 2020

Then the policy covers claims made during the 2024-2025 policy period, but only if the underlying wrongful acts occurred on or after January 1, 2020. Any wrongful acts before January 1, 2020 are excluded, even if the claim is made during the 2024-2025 policy period.

The key principle is that continuous coverage requires insureds to maintain uninterrupted claims-made policies from the inception date forward, or purchase policies with retroactive dates going back to the earliest wrongful act date. If an insured switches carriers or purchases a new policy mid-stream, the new policy's retroactive date typically corresponds to the inception date unless the insured negotiates "full prior acts" coverage (which carries significantly higher premiums).

#### Explanation

In *Citibank, N.A. v. Nat'l Union Fire Ins. Co.*, 892 F.3d 221 (3d Cir. 2018), the Third Circuit held that retroactive date exclusions are strictly enforced. Citibank's E&O policy had a January 1, 2008 retroactive date. Claims arising from conduct before January 1, 2008 were excluded, even though Citibank had continuously maintained E&O coverage since 1995. The court rejected Citibank's argument that continuous coverage created reasonable expectations of coverage for all prior acts.

In *St. Paul Fire & Marine Ins. Co. v. AFIA Worldwide Ins. Co.*, 937 S.W.2d 642 (Tex. 1996), the Texas Supreme Court held that the "wrongful act" date controls for retroactive date purposes, not the claim discovery date or claim filing date. If a policy was sold in 2016 using misleading illustrations, the wrongful act occurred in 2016, even if the claim was not made until 2023.

In *OneBeacon Am. Ins. Co. v. Travelers Indem. Co.*, 634 F.3d 507 (8th Cir. 2011), the Eighth Circuit addressed allocation between covered and uncovered periods when some claims arise before the retroactive date and some arise after. The court held that allocation should be based on relative exposure—the proportion of total damages attributable to pre-retroactive date versus post-retroactive date conduct.

### Finding B.3: FINRA Arbitrations: $1.1M Within SIR, No Chubb Recovery

#### Conclusion

LLIC faces 5 pending FINRA arbitrations totaling $830K claimed damages. Expected settlement/defense cost: $1.1M ($600K-$800K settlements + $200K-$300K defense costs). Classification: One-Time/Contingent. No insurance recovery (within $5M SIR).

#### Rule

Self-Insured Retention (SIR) is the amount of loss that the insured must pay before insurance coverage applies. SIR functions as a first-dollar deductible—the insured pays the first $X of each loss, and the insurer pays amounts exceeding $X up to the policy limit.

For FINRA arbitrations specifically, defense costs incurred in arbitration proceedings typically count toward the SIR alongside any award or settlement payment. This means that if the insured spends $300,000 defending a $500,000 arbitration claim and ultimately pays a $400,000 award, the total SIR expenditure is $700,000 ($300K defense + $400K award).

However, insurers typically retain consent and settlement control rights even for claims within the SIR to protect against the risk that poor defense strategies or excessive settlements within the retention layer will create precedents that increase exposure in other claims that breach the SIR. Policy language varies: some policies grant insurers consent rights only for settlements exceeding the SIR; others grant consent rights for all settlements regardless of amount.

#### Explanation

In *Qualcomm Inc. v. Certain Underwriters at Lloyd's*, 161 Cal. App. 4th 184 (2008), the California Court of Appeal held that SIR functions as a first-dollar deductible, not a reimbursement provision. The insured must pay the SIR amount before insurance coverage applies, even if the insurer has already advanced defense costs. The court rejected Qualcomm's argument that defense costs advanced by the insurer should reduce the SIR, holding that SIR is the insured's obligation regardless of insurer's voluntary cost-sharing.

In *Level 3 Commc'ns v. Federal Ins. Co.*, 272 F.3d 908 (7th Cir. 2001), the Seventh Circuit addressed insurer settlement control rights for claims within SIR. The court held that insurers may exercise settlement consent rights even for below-SIR claims when the policy grants such rights, reasoning that insurers have legitimate interest in controlling settlement strategy to prevent creation of adverse precedents affecting future claims.

FINRA Office of Dispute Resolution statistics show that suitability claims settle in 60-80% of cases when claimants are elderly with clearly unsuitable products. Settlement amounts typically range from 70-96% of claimed damages. Defense costs average $30,000-$50,000 per arbitration including attorney fees, expert witnesses, and hearing preparation.
