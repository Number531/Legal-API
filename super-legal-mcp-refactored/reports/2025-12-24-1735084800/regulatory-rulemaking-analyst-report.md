### B. Regulatory Framework Overview

#### 1. Change of Ownership - Title IV Eligibility Cessation

Under 34 CFR Â§ 600.31, a change of ownership resulting in a change of control causes an institution to **immediately cease qualifying** as an eligible Title IV institution. The acquisition of Midwest University (nonprofit) by American Education Holdings (for-profit) constitutes a change of control under four independent grounds: (a) acquisition of nonprofit institution by for-profit entity; (b) change in governance structure and composition of governing board; (c) transfer of substantial control to third party; and (d) change from nonprofit to proprietary tax status. Midwest University cannot assume continued Title IV eligibility post-closing. The institution must **reestablish eligibility through recertification** as if it were a new applicant for Title IV participation.

The recertification process requires: (i) **90-day advance notice** to Department prior to anticipated closing date (34 CFR Â§ 600.20(g)(1)(i)); (ii) **10-day post-closing application** through E-App system within 10 business days of change of ownership (Â§ 600.20(g)(1)(ii)); (iii) **same-day audited balance sheet** (for proprietary institutions) or audited statement of financial position (for nonprofits converting to proprietary) as of closing date; (iv) **proof of HLC accreditation approval** including clear statement that HLC has approved the change of ownership; and (v) **financial responsibility demonstration** including composite score calculation and, if applicable, letter of credit equal to 10%-25% of prior year Title IV funds.

#### 2. Financial Responsibility Standards - Composite Score Methodology

The Department determines an institution's financial responsibility using a **three-ratio composite score** methodology (34 CFR Â§ 668.171-668.174): **(a) Primary Reserve Ratio** (measures viability and liquidity), **(b) Equity Ratio** (measures capital resources and borrowing capacity), and **(c) Net Income Ratio** (measures profitability and sustainability). Each ratio is calculated from audited financial statements, converted to a strength factor score using algorithmic formulas in Appendices A and B to Subpart L, weighted according to institution type, and summed to produce a composite score ranging from -1.0 to +3.0.

**Critical Weighting Difference for Nonprofit vs. For-Profit**:
- **Nonprofit institutions** (Appendix B): Primary Reserve 40%, Equity 40%, Net Income 20%
- **Proprietary institutions** (Appendix A): Primary Reserve 30%, Equity 40%, Net Income 30%

Midwest University currently scores **2.3** as a nonprofit, placing it squarely in the "financially responsible" zone (â‰¥1.5). However, upon conversion to for-profit status post-acquisition, MU will be reclassified as proprietary and subject to the for-profit weighting formula, which places **greater emphasis on profitability** (30% weight on net income vs. 20% for nonprofits).

**Composite Score Thresholds and Consequences**:

| Score Range | Status | Requirements | Title IV Participation |
|-------------|--------|--------------|------------------------|
| **1.5 to 3.0** | Financially Responsible | None | Full participation; advance payment method |
| **1.0 to 1.4** | "Zone" - Not Responsible | 10% LOC ($18.2M); HCM1 | Conditional; provisional certification |
| **< 1.0** | Not Financially Responsible | 25% LOC ($45.5M); HCM2 | Conditional; reimbursement payment method |

The **2023 Final Rule** (88 Fed. Reg. 74,568, effective July 1, 2024) significantly enhanced financial oversight post-change of ownership, imposing: (i) mandatory financial protection (LOC or cash) for institutions not financially responsible following ownership change; (ii) extended provisional certification up to 3 complete award years (vs. prior 2-year maximum); (iii) new financial tests on owners and entities exercising substantial control; (iv) expanded triggering events for LOC requirements (composite score <1.5, withdrawal of owner certification, judgments >$1M); and (v) enhanced reporting on related-party transactions.

#### 4. 90/10 Rule - For-Profit Institution Revenue Requirement

The 90/10 rule (20 U.S.C. Â§ 1094(d)) applies **exclusively to proprietary (for-profit) institutions**, requiring that they derive **at least 10% of revenue from non-federal sources** to remain Title IV eligible. The rule ensures market viability by preventing institutions from depending entirely on federal student aid. **Currently, the rule does not apply** to Midwest University as a nonprofit 501(c)(3). **Upon conversion to for-profit status**, MU immediately becomes subject to the 90/10 rule.

**American Rescue Plan Act 2021 Expansion**: Effective for fiscal years beginning January 1, 2023, the definition of "federal revenue" was expanded to include **GI Bill benefits and Department of Defense Tuition Assistance**, which were previously excluded (creating the "90/10 loophole" that incentivized aggressive veteran recruitment). Post-ARPA, the rule effectively becomes **"85/15"** for institutions with significant military/veteran populations.

**MU's Current Revenue Profile**:

- Title IV Revenue: $182M (26.8% of total)
  - Pell Grants: $38M
  - Direct Loans: $144M
- Non-Federal Revenue: $498M (73.2% of total)

**90/10 Compliance Assessment**: **LOW RISK**. MU's current 26.8% federal revenue percentage provides a **63.2 percentage point cushion** below the 90% threshold. To violate the 90/10 rule, MU would need to increase Title IV revenue from $182M to $612M (assuming total revenue remains constant at $680M), a **236% increase** in federal aid. This would require dramatic enrollment mix shift toward Pell-eligible students, massive tuition increases without corresponding increases in institutional aid, or collapse of non-federal revenue sources.

**Sanctions for Non-Compliance**: (a) **Single year violation**: Institution enters provisional eligibility for 2 years; must report 90/10 quarterly; subject to program reviews. (b) **Two consecutive years violation**: Institution loses Title IV eligibility for **at least 2 fiscal years**; must demonstrate compliance for 2 additional years without Title IV participation to regain eligibility. This is effectively a **4-year Title IV ban**, which would be catastrophic for MU given its 26.8% dependency on federal funds ($182M).

**Nonprofit-to-For-Profit Conversion Enhanced Scrutiny**: The 2023 Final Rule imposes additional requirements on converting institutions: (i) must demonstrate 90/10 compliance for **2 complete consecutive fiscal years** before Department approves conversion for Title IV purposes; (ii) prohibited from advertising as nonprofit until Department approves conversion; (iii) must continue demonstrating gainful employment compliance (if applicable); and (iv) enhanced reporting on revenue sources, ownership, and related-party transactions for 2-year period post-conversion.

#### 5. HLC Change of Control - Condition Precedent to Closing

Higher Learning Commission (HLC) accreditation is a **mandatory prerequisite** for Title IV eligibility (20 U.S.C. Â§ 1099b(a); 34 CFR Â§ 600.4(a)(5)(i)). Under federal regulations governing recognized accrediting agencies (34 CFR Â§ 602.22(a)(1)(ii)(B)), HLC **must** have policies requiring approval of substantive changes including **"any change in the legal status, form of control, or ownership of the institution."** HLC policy (INST.G.20.010) mandates that institutions **must receive HLC approval prior to undergoing** a Change of Control. Consummating the transaction without prior HLC approval results in **loss of accreditation** and immediate loss of Title IV eligibility.

**HLC Application and Review Process**:
1. **Pre-application consultation** (1-3 months, strongly recommended): Informal discussions with HLC staff to preview transaction structure, address concerns, and determine documentation requirements.

2. **Application preparation and submission** (2-3 months): Institution prepares comprehensive Application for Change of Control including: (a) detailed transaction description and structure; (b) information on new owners and controlling entities; (c) financial projections and viability analysis; (d) evidence of continued mission compatibility; (e) governance structure documentation; (f) student outcome impact assessment; and (g) stakeholder engagement summary.

3. **HLC staff and peer review** (2-4 months): HLC conducts Change of Control Evaluation through peer reviewers, potentially including: (a) consultation with transactional attorneys and corporate finance experts; (b) review of financial pro formas and debt service coverage; (c) assessment of mission compatibility; (d) on-site Change of Control Visit (at HLC discretion); and (e) preparation of Change of Control Report for Board.

4. **HLC Board decision** (occurs at next scheduled Board meeting after review completion): HLC Board of Trustees meets three times per year (typically February, June, November). Board has **sole decision-making authority** to approve or deny application. Approval may be conditional (e.g., enhanced reporting, specific commitments).

5. **Post-approval monitoring**: Upon approval, institution is placed on **"Accredited Change of Control"** status for typically **no more than 2 years**. During this period, HLC monitors: (a) continued compliance with accreditation criteria; (b) financial stability and sustainability; (c) educational quality maintenance; and (d) mission integrity preservation.

**Total HLC Timeline**: **6-9 months** from initial consultation to Board decision, depending on application complexity, transaction structure complexity, whether on-site visit required, and alignment with Board meeting schedule.

**HLC Denial Grounds**: Board may deny based on: (i) **financial instability** (insufficient resources post-transaction to support educational mission); (ii) **mission incompatibility** (proposed changes inconsistent with accredited mission and Criteria for Accreditation); (iii) **governance concerns** (inadequate structure, conflicts of interest); (iv) **educational quality risks** (concerns about maintaining academic standards, student support); or (v) **prior performance issues** (new owner's track record with other institutions shows quality decline, compliance issues, or closures).

#### 6. DOE Recertification Timeline and Provisional Certification

**Post-Closing Recertification Process**:

**Day -90 (Pre-Closing)**: Institution provides 90-day advance notice to Department including proposed transaction description, state authorization, HLC correspondence, and financial statements.

**Conditions During Provisional Period**: (i) subject to heightened cash monitoring if composite score <1.5 (HCM1 for score 1.0-1.4; HCM2 for score <1.0); (ii) reimbursement payment method if HCM2 (institution disburses from own funds, then requests reimbursement); (iii) enhanced reporting requirements; (iv) subject to program reviews and site visits; (v) cannot expand eligibility (add locations, programs) without prior Department approval.

### C. Critical Transaction Structuring Decisions

#### Decision 1: Debt Placement (Holdco vs. Institutional)

**Option A: Holdco Debt (Recommended)**
- Structure: American Education Holdings LLC borrows $700M at Holdco level; MU receives equity investment
- MU Balance Sheet Impact: None (debt not on MU books)
- Composite Score Impact: Preserved at ~2.3 (financially responsible)
- LOC Requirement: None

- Regulatory Risk: Medium (Department may scrutinize related-party transactions, challenge substance over form)

**Option B: Institutional Debt (Not Recommended)**
- Structure: MU borrows acquisition debt or guarantees Holdco debt
- MU Balance Sheet Impact: $500M-$700M debt liability recognized
- Composite Score Impact: Declines to 0.8-1.6 range
- LOC Requirement: $18.2M (if score 1.0-1.4) to $45.5M (if score <1.0)
- Cash Flow: MU directly services acquisition debt from operations
- Regulatory Risk: Low (straightforward debt recognition) but high financial responsibility risk

**Recommended Structure**: Holdco debt with **equity infusion** to MU. AEH raises $700M debt at Holdco level, contributes $200M-$300M equity to MU, uses MU equity stake as collateral. MU's balance sheet shows equity investment but no debt. MU pays dividends to AEH (within reasonable limits) for debt service. Department likely to accept if: (i) MU maintains financial autonomy; (ii) dividend payments do not impair operations; (iii) MU retains reserves for educational mission; and (iv) no guarantee or related-party arrangements that effectively transfer risk to MU.

- Disadvantages: (i) Closing delayed 6-9 months for HLC review; (ii) financing commitments may expire; (iii) less opportunity to address concerns; (iv) Board meeting misalignment risk

**Recommendation**: Begin **informal pre-application consultation** with HLC 6 months before signing, submit **formal application** within 30 days of signing, target **HLC Board approval** at meeting 6-8 months post-signing. This balances early engagement benefits with transaction momentum.

**Change of Ownership - Institutional Eligibility Cessation**: Under 34 CFR Â§ 600.31, when a private nonprofit, private for-profit, or public institution undergoes a change in ownership that results in a change in control, it **ceases to qualify as an eligible institution** upon that change.Â¹

#### 2. Triggering Events for Private Nonprofit Institutions

### B. Financial Responsibility Standards and Composite Score (34 CFR Â§ 668.171-668.174)

#### 2. Composite Score Calculation Methodology

**Three-Ratio System**: The Secretary determines an institution's composite score by calculating three financial ratios and applying a strength factor algorithm to each:â¹

1. **Primary Reserve Ratio**: Measures the institution's viability and liquidity
2. **Equity Ratio**: Measures the institution's capital resources and ability to borrow
3. **Net Income Ratio**: Measures the institution's profitability

**Calculation Process** (34 CFR Â§ 668.172):Â¹â°

Step 1: Calculate the result of the Primary Reserve, Equity, and Net Income ratios using financial statement data

Step 2: Calculate the strength factor score for each ratio by using the corresponding algorithm (contained in Appendices A and B to Subpart L)

Step 3: Calculate the weighted score for each ratio by multiplying the strength factor score by its corresponding weighting percentage

Step 4: Sum the resulting weighted scores to arrive at the composite score

Step 5: Round the composite score to one digit after the decimal point

#### 1. Cohort Default Rate Definition and Calculation

**Three-Year Cohort Default Rate (CDR)**: The Department calculates an institution's CDR by identifying students who received Direct Loans and/or FFEL loans for attendance at the institution and who **entered repayment** during a particular federal fiscal year (October 1 - September 30).â¸â°

**Default Definition**: A borrower is in default if they are **270 days or more delinquent** on a loan payment.â¸Â¹

**Calculation Formula**:â¸Â²
```
Number of borrowers in cohort who default within 3 years

```

**Cohort Composition**: A cohort for a fiscal year consists of all current and former students who, during that fiscal year, entered repayment on any Federal Stafford loan, Federal SLS loan, Direct Subsidized loan, or Direct Unsubsidized loan received to attend the institution.â¸Â³

**Small Cohort Adjustment**: For institutions with **fewer than 30 borrowers** in a cohort, the CDR is calculated by dividing the total number of borrowers in that cohort **and the two most recent prior cohorts** who are in default by the total number of borrowers in all three cohorts.â¸â´
