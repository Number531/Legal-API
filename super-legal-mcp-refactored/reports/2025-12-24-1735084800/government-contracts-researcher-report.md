## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This memorandum analyzes federal research grant compliance and export control regulations affecting the proposed $780 million acquisition of Midwest University (MU) by a for-profit entity. MU currently operates as a private nonprofit research university with $68 million in annual research expenditures, of which $52 million (76%) derives from federal grants. The acquisition proposes to convert MU from nonprofit to for-profit status, triggering substantial regulatory complications across two domains: (1) federal grant transferability and compliance, and (2) export control regulations governing defense-related research and foreign national researchers.

**Bottom Line Up Front:** The nonprofit-to-for-profit conversion creates **material transaction risks** totaling $25M-$50M in likely impact over five years, including potential loss of $10M-$20M in non-transferable grants, $8.3M annual indirect cost rate reduction, $1M-$3M export control penalty exposure, and $700K-$1.3M annual compliance cost increases. The transaction remains viable only with: (1) structural solutions such as a nonprofit research subsidiary to preserve restricted grants; (2) federal agency consultations confirming grant transfer eligibility and fundamental research exemption maintenance; and (3) comprehensive export control audits verifying compliance for 28 foreign nationals from high-risk countries. **Minimum 6-9 month timeline** required for regulatory approvals before closing.

**Non-Transferable Grant Mechanisms:** Critical research reveals that certain NIH grant types are **explicitly restricted to nonprofit/domestic institutions** and **cannot be transferred** to for-profit entities under any circumstances:

- **T32 (Institutional Training Grants):** Domestic nonprofit only
- **K01 (Mentored Research Scientist Development Awards):** Domestic institutions only

**Enhanced Scrutiny Expected:** NSF will scrutinize whether for-profit acquisition compromises:
- Fundamental research nature of projects
- Broader impacts mission (education, diversity, outreach)

**Timeline:** Grant transfer process requires **90-120 days** for agency notification plus **60-90 days** for approval processing. Total minimum lead time: **6 months** before closing.

#### 5. Fundamental Research Exemption - Transaction Outcome-Determinative

**Exemption Framework:** Both ITAR and EAR provide critical exemptions for university fundamental research:

**ITAR Exemption (22 CFR ¬ß 120.11(8)):** "Fundamental research" means basic and applied research in science and engineering at accredited U.S. institutions of higher learning where results ordinarily will be published and shared broadly within scientific community.

**EAR Exemption (15 CFR ¬ß 734.8):** "Technology" or "software" that arises during or results from fundamental research and is intended to be published is not subject to EAR.

**Exemption Requirements:**
- Research conducted at **accredited university**
- Results **ordinarily published** and shared broadly
- **No restrictions** on publication or dissemination
- **No prepublication review** by sponsor
- Researchers have **not accepted restrictions** for proprietary or national security reasons

**Why Exemption Matters:** The fundamental research exemption is the primary mechanism allowing universities to employ foreign nationals on research projects without obtaining individual deemed export licenses for each person. It dramatically reduces compliance burden and enables open scientific collaboration.

**Impact of For-Profit Conversion:** For-profit status creates multiple challenges to maintaining fundamental research exemption:

| Factor | Nonprofit University | For-Profit MU | Exemption Impact |
|--------|---------------------|---------------|------------------|
| **Educational mission** | Primary institutional purpose | Secondary to profit motive | ‚ö†Ô∏è Weakens presumption |
| **Publication commitment** | Strong presumption of open publication | Commercial interests may restrict | ‚ö†Ô∏è Creates doubt |
| **Accreditation** | Standard for universities | Must maintain accreditation | ‚ö†Ô∏è Uncertain if for-profit affects accreditation |
| **Proprietary restrictions** | Typically minimal | Profit motive suggests IP protection | ‚ö†Ô∏è Contradicts "no restrictions" requirement |
| **Regulatory interpretation** | Well-established | Ambiguous for for-profit | ‚ö†Ô∏è Risk of adverse determination |

**Regulatory Ambiguity:** Neither ITAR nor EAR explicitly addresses whether for-profit universities qualify for fundamental research exemption. DDTC and BIS have not issued definitive guidance on this question. The exemption language focuses on "accredited institutions of higher learning" and "ordinarily published" results, not tax status per se. However, for-profit status creates a **rebuttable presumption** that research serves commercial purposes with proprietary restrictions.

**Consequence if Exemption Lost:**

If DDTC/BIS determine that for-profit MU does not qualify for fundamental research exemption, operational impacts include:

1. **All research involving defense articles** (ITAR) or controlled dual-use items (EAR) requires authorization
2. **All 28 foreign nationals** require individual deemed export licenses (current: only 8)
3. **Export classification** required for all research projects before foreign national access
4. **Technology Control Plans** required for all controlled research (current: only 2)
5. **Severe restrictions** on international collaboration
6. **Publication delays** for export control pre-publication reviews
7. **ITAR registration** may become mandatory ($3,000 annual fee + substantial reporting burden)

**Compliance Cost Impact:** Analysis in Section E.6 estimates **$700K-$1.3M annual cost increase** if fundamental research exemption is lost:
- Deemed export licenses: $50K-$100K (legal fees for 20+ additional licenses)

#### 1. Financial Conflict of Interest (42 CFR Part 50, Subpart F)

**Regulatory Authority:** 42 CFR Part 50, Subpart F establishes standards to ensure that the design, conduct, and reporting of NIH-funded research is free from bias resulting from investigator financial conflicts of interest.¬π

#### 2. Institutional Review Board Requirements (45 CFR Part 46 - Common Rule)

d) **Monitoring and Audits:**

**Criminal Penalties:**
- Willful unlawful export/disclosure: Up to **10 years imprisonment** and/or **$1,000,000 fine** per violation
- Conspiracy to violate ITAR: Up to 5 years imprisonment‚Å∑¬≤

**Additional Consequences:**
- **Debarment**: Prohibition from participating in export of defense articles
- **Loss of export privileges**: Inability to engage in ITAR-controlled activities
- **Reputational damage**: Public disclosure of violations‚Å∑¬≥

#### 5. Fundamental Research Exemption Under ITAR

**Exemption Definition** (22 CFR ¬ß 120.11(8)): "Fundamental research" means basic and applied research in science and engineering at accredited U.S. institutions of higher learning where results ordinarily will be published and shared broadly within scientific community.‚Å∑‚Å¥

**Exemption Requirements:**
- Research conducted at **accredited university**
- Results **ordinarily published** and shared broadly
- **Not restricted** from publication or dissemination
- **No prepublication review** by sponsor‚Å∑‚Åµ

**Exemption Does NOT Apply When:**
- Research subject to publication restrictions
- Foreign nationals excluded from participation
- Sponsor retains prepublication approval rights
- Results classified or subject to national security restrictions‚Å∑‚Å∂

**Critical Issue for MU Acquisition:** For-profit acquisition raises question: Does MU remain "accredited institution of higher learning" for ITAR fundamental research exemption?

**DDTC Interpretation:** Fundamental research exemption focused on **educational mission** and **publication commitment**, not tax status per se. However, for-profit status creates presumption of commercial application and proprietary restrictions.‚Å∑‚Å∑

### E. EAR EXPORT CONTROL COMPLIANCE

#### 1. Export Administration Regulations Overview

**Regulatory Authority:** 15 CFR Parts 730-774, administered by the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS), U.S. Department of Commerce.‚Å∏¬≤

**Current Status (2025):** Title 15 CFR is up to date as of July 24, 2025, last amended May 2, 2025.‚Å∏¬≥

**Scope:** EAR controls dual-use items (commercial and military applications) and certain purely commercial items:
- **Commerce Control List (CCL)**: Export Control Classification Numbers (ECCNs) for controlled items
- **EAR99**: Items not on CCL, generally no license required (but restrictions for embargoed countries)‚Å∏‚Å¥

**Key Difference from ITAR:**
- ITAR: Military/defense-specific items (presumptive license requirement for all countries)
- EAR: Dual-use and commercial items (license depends on item, destination, end-use, end-user)‚Å∏‚Åµ

#### 2. Fundamental Research Exemption Under EAR

**Exemption Definition** (15 CFR ¬ß 734.8): "Technology" or "software" that arises during or results from fundamental research and is intended to be published is **not subject to the EAR**.‚Å∏‚Å∂

**Fundamental Research Definition** (15 CFR ¬ß 734.8(c)): Research in science, engineering, or mathematics where:
- Results **ordinarily published** and shared broadly within research community
- Researchers have **not accepted restrictions** for proprietary or national security reasons‚Å∏‚Å∑

**Application to Universities:** Research conducted by scientists, engineers, or students at a university normally will be considered fundamental research.‚Å∏‚Å∏

**Exemption Does NOT Apply When:**
- **Publication restrictions** on scientific and technical information
- Institution **accepted restrictions** on foreign national participation due to proprietary/national security reasons
- Sponsor retains **prepublication review** rights
- Research funded by intelligence community (automatic restriction)‚Å∏‚Åπ

**Educational Information Exclusion** (15 CFR ¬ß 734.9): Information taught in courses listed in university catalogues and associated teaching laboratories is excluded from EAR, even if concerning controlled items.‚Åπ‚Å∞

**Publicly Available Information** (15 CFR ¬ß 734.7): Technology or software already published or will be published is exempt. Includes:
- Information released at open conferences (all technically qualified persons eligible to attend, can take notes)
- Patents and published patent applications
- Information available in public libraries‚Åπ¬π

#### 3. Deemed Export Under EAR

**Deemed Export Definition** (15 CFR ¬ß 734.13): Release of technology or source code subject to EAR to a foreign national in the United States is **deemed to be an export** to the foreign national's most recent country of citizenship or permanent residency.‚Åπ¬≤

**Foreign National Definition:** Person who is not:
- U.S. citizen
- U.S. lawful permanent resident (green card holder)
- Protected individual (refugee, asylee)‚Åπ¬≥

**License Requirements:** Deemed exports require licenses on **item-by-item, country-by-country basis**. Not all items require licenses; depends on:
- ECCN classification of technology
- Country(ies) of foreign national's citizenship/residence
- End-use of technology
- Denied party screening results‚Åπ‚Å¥
