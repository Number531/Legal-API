#### c. Adjustment Mechanics and Closing Consideration Structure

The purchase price adjustment should be implemented through a multi-component structure that allocates risk based on timing and controllability:

**Initial Closing Payment**: $450,000,000 (cash at closing)
- Represents uncontested value of tangible assets (real property, equipment, intellectual property)
- Subject to working capital adjustment (+/- $10M) based on 60-day post-closing reconciliation¹⁵

**Regulatory Approval Escrow**: $75,000,000 (held 12-18 months)
- Released upon: (1) HLC substantive change approval final and non-appealable; (2) Title IV provisional certification obtained; (3) ABA change of control review completed without adverse action
- 50% release at 12 months if HLC and Title IV approvals obtained; remaining 50% at 18 months if ABA approval obtained
- If any approval denied or provisional status imposed with material restrictions, escrow funds offset buyer losses¹⁶

**Indemnification Holdback**: $50,000,000 (held 24-36 months)
- Secures seller indemnification obligations for: (1) Title IX litigation; (2) EEOC charges; (3) donor litigation related to endowment; (4) pre-closing breach of representations and warranties
- Released at 24 months if no pending claims; extended to 36 months if claims filed but unresolved
- Cap on seller indemnification: $50M (amount of holdback)¹⁷

**Earnout (Performance-Based Consideration)**: $50,000,000-$100,000,000
- Tied to achievement of operational milestones over 36 months post-closing:
  * $20M upon achieving 90/10 rule compliance (≤89% Title IV revenue) for two consecutive fiscal years
  * $15M upon ABA full accreditation retention (no provisional or probationary status) for 24 months
  * $15M upon law school bar passage rate maintenance ≥75% for two consecutive bar administration cycles
  * $25M upon composite score improvement to ≥1.5 without LOC requirement
  * $25M upon enrollment growth to 25,000 students (from baseline 22,500) within 36 months
- Earnout milestones incentivize seller cooperation during transition while protecting buyer from overpayment if performance targets unmet¹⁸

**Total Consideration Structure**:
- Initial closing payment: $450M
- Regulatory approval escrow: $75M
- Indemnification holdback: $50M
- Earnout (maximum): $100M
- **Maximum Total Consideration**: $675M (if all earnout milestones achieved)
- **Minimum Total Consideration**: $575M (if earnout milestones fail)

This structure reflects the **$156M-$267M risk adjustment** calculated above while providing seller with upside participation if buyer successfully navigates regulatory approvals and achieves performance targets.

### 2. Escrow and Indemnification Structure

#### a. General Escrow: Regulatory Approval Contingencies

**Escrow Amount**: $75,000,000

**Purpose**: The regulatory approval escrow protects buyer from catastrophic losses if critical regulatory approvals (HLC accreditation, Title IV eligibility, ABA law school approval) are denied, delayed, or granted with material restrictions that impair the university's operations or financial performance.

**Rationale for $75M Amount**: The escrow amount corresponds to the Title IV letter of credit requirement ($75M-$125M per Section II analysis).¹⁹ If the Department of Education requires a $100M LOC and buyer must post such security, the $75M escrow partially offsets this unexpected capital requirement. If HLC denies accreditation approval (15-20% probability), the $75M escrow provides partial compensation for buyer's inability to operate the university as contemplated.²⁰

**Release Schedule**:

| Milestone | Release Amount | Timeline | Verification Mechanism |
|-----------|----------------|----------|------------------------|
| HLC substantive change approval (final, non-appealable) | $25M | 6-9 months post-closing | Copy of HLC Commission action letter confirming approval without conditions materially adverse to buyer²¹ |
| Title IV provisional certification obtained (Department of Education) | $25M | 4-6 months post-closing | Copy of Department approval letter granting provisional Program Participation Agreement²² |
| ABA change of control review completed (no provisional/probationary status) | $25M | 9-12 months post-closing | Copy of ABA Council letter confirming full accreditation maintained without restrictions²³ |

**Forfeiture Scenarios**:

If any critical approval is denied or granted with material adverse restrictions, buyer may offset losses against the escrow:
- HLC denial: Buyer retains entire $75M escrow plus right to terminate transaction and unwind consideration (refund $450M closing payment, minus costs to date)²⁴
- Title IV eligibility suspension or LOC requirement >$100M: Buyer retains escrow pro rata based on incremental capital requirement²⁵
- ABA provisional accreditation: Buyer retains $15M-$25M (law school portion) and may seek additional indemnification if enrollment declines >15%²⁶

#### c. Indemnification Caps, Baskets, and Survival Periods

**Seller Indemnification Obligations** (Article 12 of Purchase Agreement):

**Indemnification Basket**: $2,500,000
- Buyer must absorb first $2.5M in losses before seller indemnification obligations arise
- Basket is "tipping" (once exceeded, seller liable for dollar one, not just excess)³⁴
- Rationale: Prevents nickel-and-dime claims; buyer assumes ordinary business risks; threshold appropriate for transaction of this magnitude

**Indemnification Cap**: $50,000,000
- Maximum aggregate seller indemnification liability across all claims: $50M
- Equals the indemnification holdback amount—seller's maximum exposure is escrow forfeiture
- **Exceptions to Cap** (uncapped liabilities):
  * Fraud or intentional misrepresentation by seller
  * Breaches of fundamental representations (organization, authority, title to assets, accreditation status, financial statements)
  * Title IX litigation and EEOC charge (covered by purpose-specific escrows, not general cap)
  * Donor litigation related to endowment misappropriation (if bifurcated structure not approved by Indiana AG)³⁵

**Survival Periods**:

| Representation/Warranty Category | Survival Period | Rationale |

### 3. Alternative Transaction Structures

The baseline transaction structure contemplates an asset purchase by American Education Holdings, with Midwest University converting from 501(c)(3) nonprofit to for-profit C corporation status. However, four alternative structures merit evaluation for risk mitigation and regulatory approval optimization:

#### a. Current Structure Assessment: Asset Purchase with For-Profit Conversion

**Structure**: American Education Holdings acquires substantially all assets and assumes specified liabilities of Midwest University. MU converts to for-profit C corporation (or dissolves with assets transferred pre-dissolution).

**Advantages**:
- Buyer controls asset selection (excludes unwanted liabilities)⁴⁵
- Depreciable basis step-up for tangible assets ($450M buildings, equipment, furniture)⁴⁶
- Cleaner successor liability analysis (only assumed liabilities transfer)⁴⁷

**Disadvantages**:
- Triggers change of control review for all seven programmatic accreditors plus HLC⁴⁸
- Requires Indiana Attorney General approval for nonprofit asset transfer (12-18 month process)⁴⁹
- Loss of 501(c)(3) tax exemption creates $180M capital gains tax on endowment (mitigated only by bifurcated Foundation structure)⁵⁰
- Students perceive discontinuity (old institution closed, new institution opened), increasing transfer risk⁵¹
- Alumni relations disruption (institutional history severed)⁵²
- Accreditation agencies skeptical of for-profit ownership (InfiLaw precedent: 0% survival rate for law schools)⁵³

**Recommendation**: Current structure creates maximum regulatory friction. Consider alternatives that preserve nonprofit status for core educational functions while enabling private investment and operational control.

#### b. Nonprofit Subsidiary Model

**Structure**: American Education Holdings establishes a nonprofit subsidiary ("NewCo") organized as 501(c)(3) tax-exempt corporation. NewCo acquires Midwest University through merger of equals (both nonprofits). American Education Holdings controls NewCo through board appointment rights despite lacking equity ownership.

**Legal Framework**: IRS permits "controlled nonprofit" structures where for-profit entities exercise operational control over nonprofits without destroying exempt status, provided: (1) nonprofit's articles and bylaws ensure exclusive dedication to exempt purposes; (2) no profits inure to private benefit; (3) for-profit parent does not extract excessive management fees; (4) nonprofit maintains independent board majority.⁵⁴

**Governance Structure**:
- NewCo has 9-member board: 5 independent directors (educators, community leaders), 4 American Education Holdings nominees
- American Education Holdings retains operational control through: (1) appointment of CEO/President; (2) approval rights over annual budget; (3) management services agreement (fee = 10-15% of revenue)
- NewCo articles contain perpetual commitment to educational mission; any dissolution requires assets distributed to other 501(c)(3) educational institutions⁵⁵

**Advantages**:
- **Preserves 501(c)(3) status**: No capital gains tax on $650M endowment; ongoing tax exemption saves $25M-$35M annually in property tax, sales tax, UBIT⁵⁶
- **Maintains Title IV eligibility without for-profit restrictions**: No 90/10 rule, no gainful employment rule, lower default rate scrutiny, no composite score requirements⁵⁷
- **Higher accreditation approval probability**: HLC and ABA more receptive to nonprofit-to-nonprofit transition; InfiLaw stigma avoided⁵⁸
- **Student/alumni continuity**: Midwest University continues as same legal entity; no perceived closure⁵⁹
- **Research grant preservation**: Federal agencies prefer nonprofit recipients; NIH and NSF grants continue without change of control complications⁶⁰

**Disadvantages**:
- **Limited profit extraction**: Management fees must be reasonable (10-15% of revenue = $68M-$102M annually); no equity dividends or distributions to AEH⁶¹
- **Independent board**: 5 of 9 directors independent of AEH; operational control constrained by fiduciary duties to nonprofit⁶²
- **Exit complexity**: AEH cannot sell equity interest (no equity ownership); can only sell management contract or appoint new management company⁶³
- **IRS private benefit scrutiny**: Excessive management fees or improper profit extraction triggers exempt status revocation⁶⁴

**Recommendation**: Nonprofit subsidiary structure optimizes regulatory approval probability and tax efficiency but reduces AEH's return on investment due to profit extraction limitations. Suitable if AEH's business model focuses on management fee revenue rather than equity appreciation.

#### c. Bifurcated Endowment Foundation Structure (MANDATORY per Section V)

*See* Section V.C at pp. 175-185 analyzing Indiana Attorney General cy pres enforcement authority under charitable trust doctrine; comprehensive case law analysis (*Queen of Angels Hospital v. Younger*, 66 Cal. App. 3d 359; *Grace Community Church*, 815 F.3d 1097) establishes 80-90% probability Indiana AG denies or challenges direct transfer of $468M donor-restricted endowment to for-profit entity, making bifurcated Foundation structure MANDATORY rather than optional for transaction viability.⁶⁵

**Structure**: Donor-restricted endowment funds ($468M) transfer to newly formed Midwest University Educational Foundation, Inc. (501(c)(3) private foundation) with independent board. Board-designated quasi-endowment ($182M) transfers to for-profit Midwest University as buyer asset. Foundation makes annual grants ($23.4M minimum per IRC § 4942) to support MU's educational programs.⁶⁶

**Implementation Timeline**:
- **Months 0-3**: Form Foundation, file IRS Form 1023, recruit independent board
- **Months 3-9**: Obtain IRS 501(c)(3) determination letter
- **Months 6-14**: File Indiana Attorney General petition, respond to AG investigation, negotiate approval conditions
- **Month 14-18**: Receive AG approval order, satisfy any conditions
- **Closing**: Transfer $468M to Foundation simultaneously with asset purchase closing⁶⁷

**Critical Success Factors**:
- Independent Foundation board (majority unaffiliated with AEH)⁶⁸
- Perpetual commitment that grants support educational purposes consistent with donor intent⁶⁹
- Competitive grant process (Foundation considers applications from other Indiana educational institutions, even if MU receives priority)⁷⁰
- Grant restrictions prohibiting use for profit distributions, debt service, or non-educational purposes⁷¹

**Financial Impact**:
