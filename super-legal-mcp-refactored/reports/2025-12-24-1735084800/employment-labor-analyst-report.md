### A. Critical Employment Law Issues

#### 1. Tenure Rights and Successor Liability ($200M-$680M Exposure)

**The Issue:** Faculty handbooks are **enforceable contracts** in most U.S. jurisdictions, and courts have consistently held that **tenure rights survive change of ownership** under successor liability doctrine. AEH will inherit MU's contractual obligations to 680 tenured faculty members, including continuous employment, academic freedom protections, and dismissal procedures requiring either documented cause or formal financial exigency.

**The Law:**
- **Faculty handbooks as contracts:** AAUP has documented that faculty handbooks with clear language on tenure, dismissal procedures, and shared governance are binding contracts enforceable in court.
- **Successor liability:** When a transaction functions as a "de facto merger" (substantial continuity of operations, employees, location, and assets), the successor employer inherits predecessor's employment contracts.
- ***Jacobs v. Mundelein College***, 628 N.E.2d 201 (Ill. App. 1993): Illinois court held that tenure rights survived institutional affiliation because no financial exigency had been formally declared per faculty handbook requirements.

**Application to Project Athena:**
- **De facto merger factors all present:** University continues operations, 4,090 employees transfer, same Indiana campus, all assets and accreditation transfer, MU dissolves post-sale.
- **Financial exigency unavailable:** $780M acquisition price demonstrates MU is NOT in financial crisis. AAUP defines financial exigency as "imminent financial crisis threatening survival of institution as a whole." Post-acquisition cost-cutting is business strategy, not exigency.
- **Dismissal for cause impractical:** Requires documented incompetence, neglect of duty, or misconduct; hearing before faculty committee; burden of proof on administration. Process takes 12-24 months per faculty member.

**Litigation Risk if Tenure Violated:**
- **Per-plaintiff exposure:** $300K-$1M (back pay, front pay, reinstatement, attorneys' fees)
- **680 tenured faculty potential plaintiffs:** $204M-$680M aggregate exposure
- **Class action likely:** Faculty would band together for collective litigation
- **Accreditation jeopardy:** Higher Learning Commission could withdraw accreditation if tenure protections eliminated (catastrophic loss of federal student aid eligibility)

**Strategic Imperative:**
AEH **MUST** publicly commit to honoring tenure for all 680 current tenured faculty. This is non-negotiable for transaction success. Any attempt to circumvent tenure obligations will result in:
- Multi-hundred-million-dollar litigation
- Loss of institutional accreditation

**The Law:**
- **Title VII prohibits discrimination:** Based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin (42 U.S.C. Â§ 2000e et seq.)
- **No academic exemption:** Congress removed academia's Title VII exemption in 1972. *University of Pennsylvania v. EEOC*, 493 U.S. 182 (1990) held that universities cannot assert academic freedom to withhold peer review materials from EEOC.
- **Prima facie case (McDonnell Douglas):** Protected class membership + qualification + adverse action + disparate treatment
- **2025 evolution:** Recent Supreme Court case (*Ames v. Ohio*) clarified that plaintiffs can prove discrimination without perfect comparators if evidence raises reasonable inference of unlawful discrimination.

**Dr. Chen's Claims:**

**A. Race/Sex Discrimination:**
- **Prima facie elements likely met:** Asian female (protected classes), tenure-qualified candidate, tenure denied, potential white male comparators granted tenure with similar/weaker credentials
- **Evidence to develop:** Tenure rates by race/sex (2014-2024), comparator files (publications, teaching evaluations), department meeting minutes, peer review letters
- **Pretext indicators:** Subjective criteria ("collegiality"), shifting justifications for denial, statistical disparities

**B. Retaliation:**
- **Elements:** Protected activity (prior complaint about discrimination/harassment) + adverse action (tenure denial) + causal connection
- **Temporal proximity standard:** Close timing (days/weeks) sufficient alone; 3-4 months insufficient alone; requires additional evidence
- **Chen analysis:** Need to determine when prior complaint filed relative to May 2024 tenure denial
- **Strong if:** Same decision-makers, inconsistent explanations, favorable reviews pre-complaint/negative post-complaint

#### 4. Shared Governance and Accreditation Risk ($1M-$5M + Loss of Accreditation)

**The Issue:** Faculty handbooks typically incorporate AAUP principles on "shared governance," giving Faculty Senate authority over curriculum, academic policies, and tenure decisions. These provisions are contractually binding. Eliminating Faculty Senate or marginalizing faculty input could breach contracts AND jeopardize accreditation.

**The Law:**
- **AAUP 1966 Statement on Government of Colleges and Universities:** Establishes shared responsibility among governing board (fiduciary oversight), administration (operations), and faculty (curriculum, instruction, faculty appointments).
- **Contractual incorporation:** When faculty handbook references AAUP principles, they become part of employment contract.
- **Accreditation requirement:** Higher Learning Commission (HLC) Criterion 5.A requires institutions to have governance structures that "promote effective leadership and support collaborative processes."

**MU Faculty Senate Authority (Typical):**
- Curriculum design and degree requirements
- Grading standards and academic policies

**Post-Acquisition Risk:**
AEH's for-profit business model creates tension with shared governance:
- **AEH wants:** Operational efficiency, standardized curriculum, cost reduction, top-down decision-making
- **Faculty expect:** Academic freedom, collegial governance, input on all academic policies, protection from corporate control

### B. Faculty Tenure Rights and Contractual Obligations

#### 1. Faculty Handbooks as Binding Contracts

**Majority Rule:** Faculty handbooks are **enforceable contracts** in most U.S. jurisdictions.Â¹â¸

The American Association of University Professors (AAUP) has documented that faculty handbooks can have varying degrees of legal enforceability, and in many jurisdictions faculty handbooks are binding contracts, enforceable in court.Â¹â¹

**Three-Element Test for Contractual Status:**Â²â°
1. **Offer:** Handbook language contains a promise clear enough that faculty would reasonably believe an offer was made
2. **Communication:** Statement disseminated to faculty member such that they are aware of contents and reasonably believe it to be an offer
3. **Acceptance:** Faculty member accepts by commencing or continuing to work after learning of policy

**Presumption for MU:** Faculty handbook provisions regarding tenure, dismissal procedures, and shared governance are **presumptively contractual** and binding on both MU and AEH as successor.

#### 2. AAUP Standards and Incorporation by Reference

Many university handbooks incorporate AAUP principles by reference, making them contractually binding.Â²Â¹

**Key AAUP Documents:**
- *1940 Statement of Principles on Academic Freedom and Tenure*
- *Recommended Institutional Regulations on Academic Freedom and Tenure*
- *Statement on Government of Colleges and Universities* (shared governance)

**Judicial Treatment:**
Courts frequently look to AAUP recommended regulations as a guide to interpret university contracts and policies.Â²Â² When a handbook references AAUP standards, those principles become part of the contractual obligations.

#### 3. Tenure Definition and Rights

**Tenure** means:Â²Â³
- Continuous appointment without term limits
- Right to reappointment annually without need for renewal
- Dismissal permitted ONLY for:
  - **Cause** (incompetence, neglect of duty, moral turpitude, insubordination), OR
  - **Financial exigency** (bona fide financial crisis), OR
  - **Bona fide program discontinuance**

**Property Right:**
Tenure creates a constitutionally protected property interest for public university faculty (*Board of Regents v. Roth*, 408 U.S. 564 (1972)).Â²â´ For private universities, tenure is a contractual right enforceable under state contract law.

#### 4. Dismissal for Cause - High Burden of Proof

**AAUP Standard:** Cause for dismissal must be "related, directly and substantially, to the fitness of faculty members in their professional capacities as teachers or researchers."Â²âµ

**Recognized Grounds:**
- **Incompetence:** Demonstrable failure to perform teaching or research duties
- **Neglect of Duty:** Persistent failure to meet classes, grade papers, or perform assigned responsibilities
- **Moral Turpitude:** Criminal conduct or serious ethical violations
- **Insubordination:** Repeated refusal to follow lawful directives

**Procedural Requirements:**
- Written specification of charges
- Hearing before faculty committee (peer review)
- Right to counsel
- Burden of proof on administration
- Written decision with findings
- Appeal rights

**Practical Reality:** Dismissing tenured faculty for cause is **extremely difficult** and time-consuming (often 12-24 months).

#### 5. Financial Exigency Standard

**AAUP Definition:**
"An imminent financial crisis which threatens the survival of the institution as a whole and which cannot be alleviated by less drastic means" than termination of tenured faculty.Â²â¶

**Requirements:**Â²â·
1. **Formal Declaration:** Governing board must formally declare financial exigency
2. **Bona Fide Crisis:** Demonstrated through audited financials showing insolvency or imminent closure
3. **Faculty Participation:** Faculty senate or representative body must have meaningful opportunity to review financial data and recommend alternatives
4. **Less Drastic Means:** Employer must demonstrate good faith efforts to avoid terminations:
   - Hiring freeze
   - Salary freeze or reductions
   - Reduction in non-personnel expenses
   - Program eliminations
5. **Order of Termination:** Non-tenure-track faculty terminated first, then untenured, then tenured (by seniority)
6. **Notice Period:** 12 months for tenured faculty (AAUP standard)
7. **Recall Rights:** If positions reinstated within 3 years, terminated faculty have right of first refusal

**Burden of Proof:** Institution must demonstrate financial exigency through contemporaneous financial records, not post-hoc justifications.

#### 6. Key Case Law on Financial Exigency

**Linn v. Andover-Newton Theological School** (638 F. Supp. 1114, D. Mass. 1986)Â²â¸

*Facts:* Tenured faculty member dismissed due to alleged financial exigency.

*Holding:* Institution breached tenure contract by failing to follow its own internal procedures for declaring financial exigency.

*Principle:* "Where an institution adopts financial exigency guidelines but fails to adhere to them, the court may not uphold a tenured faculty member's dismissal."

**Krotkoff v. Goucher College** (585 F.2d 675, 4th Cir. 1978)Â²â¹

*Holding:* Even without specific language on financial exigency in handbook, there is an implied right in higher education to terminate positions based on declared financial exigency.

*Caveat:* Exigency must be **real and declared**, not merely asserted post-hoc.

**Jacobs v. Mundelein College** (628 N.E.2d 201, Ill. App. 1993)Â³â°

*Facts:* Mundelein College "affiliated" with Loyola University but did not formally declare financial exigency per handbook requirements.

*Holding:* Tenure rights survived affiliation because no financial crisis was declared as required by faculty handbook. "The precise terms of the handbook were operative."

*Critical Principle:* Change of ownership does NOT automatically terminate tenure unless formal financial exigency is declared in accordance with handbook procedures.

**Application to Project Athena:**
- AEH acquiring MU for $780M suggests MU is **NOT in financial exigency**
- Post-acquisition cost-cutting is **NOT financial exigency** (it's business strategy)
- MU's 680 tenured faculty have **contractual right to continued employment** that survives acquisition

### C. Successor Liability for Employment Contracts and Tenure Obligations

#### 1. General Successor Liability Doctrine

**Definition:** Successor liability is "liability (debt or other obligation) that is inherited by a successor entity after a corporate restructuring."Â³Â¹

**Default Rule:** In asset purchases, buyer generally does **NOT** assume seller's liabilities unless expressly assumed.

**Exceptions (When Successor Liability Attaches):**Â³Â²
1. **Express or Implied Assumption:** Buyer agrees to assume liabilities
2. **De Facto Merger:** Transaction functions as merger despite asset purchase structure
3. **Mere Continuation:** Successor is continuation of predecessor
4. **Fraudulent Transfer:** Transaction designed to escape liabilities

#### 2. De Facto Merger Test

Courts apply multi-factor test:Â³Â³
- Continuity of business operations
- Continuity of shareholders/ownership
- Continuity of management and personnel
- Continuity of physical location and assets
- Assumption of liabilities necessary for ongoing operations
- Predecessor ceases operations shortly after sale

**Application to Project Athena:**
- âœ“ Continuity of operations (university continues under AEH)
- âœ“ Continuity of management (likely retaining President, deans)
- âœ“ Continuity of personnel (1,240 faculty, 2,850 staff)
- âœ“ Same physical location (Indiana campus)
- âœ“ MU likely dissolves post-acquisition

**Conclusion:** Transaction will likely be deemed **de facto merger**, triggering successor liability for employment contracts.

#### 3. Successor Liability for Tenure Contracts

**Key Principle:** Courts generally hold that tenure rights **survive change of ownership** and bind the successor institution.Â³â´

When a university is acquired (whether asset purchase or merger), the acquiring entity steps into the shoes of the predecessor and **assumes all contractual obligations to tenured faculty**, including:
- Continued employment
- Academic freedom protections
- Dismissal procedures (cause or financial exigency only)
- Shared governance rights
- Salary and benefit obligations

**Rationale:**
- Tenure contracts are ongoing, not terminable by ownership change
- Faculty reasonably expect tenure to survive institutional transitions
- Public policy favors stability in academic employment
- AAUP principles recognize tenure as commitment "by institution, not by particular administration"

#### 4. *Jacobs v. Mundelein College* - Controlling Precedent

In *Jacobs*, the Illinois Appellate Court held that when Mundelein College affiliated with Loyola University, **tenured faculty contracts survived** the institutional transition because:Â³âµ
- Faculty handbook tenure provisions were contractually binding
- No formal financial exigency had been declared
- Affiliation did not extinguish tenure rights
- Successor institution (Loyola) bound by tenure obligations

**Application:** AEH will be **bound by MU's tenure commitments** to 680 tenured faculty unless:
- MU formally declares financial exigency (unlikely given $780M sale price), OR
- Individual faculty voluntarily resign or are terminated for cause

#### 5. NLRA Successorship Doctrine (Union Context)

While MU faculty are not unionized, NLRA successorship principles provide analogous guidance for employment contracts.

**NLRB v. Burns International Security Services** (406 U.S. 272 (1972))Â³â¶

*Rule:* Successor employer must recognize and bargain with incumbent union if:
1. Substantial continuity of business operations
2. Majority of successor's workforce are predecessor's employees

*Application:* If MU had collective bargaining agreement, AEH would be required to honor it (at least for reasonable period).

**Analogous Principle for Tenure:**
Because tenure contracts are individually negotiated but follow uniform handbook terms, successor liability principles apply similarly. AEH inherits MU's contractual obligations to each tenured faculty member.

#### 6. Practical Implications for AEH

**Cannot Modify Tenure System Unilaterally:**
- Cannot eliminate tenure for existing tenured faculty
- Cannot change dismissal procedures without faculty approval
- Cannot impose new performance standards retroactively
- Cannot convert tenured positions to at-will employment

**Options for AEH:**
1. **Honor Existing Tenure:** Continue employment of all 680 tenured faculty (safest approach)
2. **Negotiate Buyouts:** Offer voluntary separation packages (expensive: $150K-$300K per faculty)
3. **Terminate for Cause:** Individual basis only, requires extensive documentation and hearing process
4. **Declare Financial Exigency:** Requires formal board action, faculty participation, demonstration of institutional crisis (unlikely viable given $780M acquisition price)

**Litigation Risk:**
- Breach of contract claims by terminated tenured faculty

### A. Summary of Legal Conclusions

#### 1. WARN Act Applicability

**CONCLUSION:** American Education Holdings and Midwest University are **subject to federal WARN Act** requirements. No exemption exists for educational institutions or faculty employees. Indiana has no state mini-WARN Act.

**Triggers:** 60-day notice required if:
- 500+ employees laid off (any percentage), OR
- 50+ employees laid off constituting 33% of workforce (1,350 for MU)

**Exposure:** $4M-$8M if mass layoff conducted without proper notice.

#### 2. Tenure Rights and Successor Liability

**CONCLUSION:** AEH will **inherit contractual tenure obligations** for all 680 tenured faculty members. Faculty handbooks are enforceable contracts in most jurisdictions, and successor liability doctrine applies to for-profit acquisitions.

**Key Precedent:** *Jacobs v. Mundelein College* (1993) - tenure rights survived institutional affiliation.

**Implication:** AEH **cannot unilaterally eliminate tenure**, modify dismissal procedures, or terminate tenured faculty without cause or financial exigency (which cannot be claimed given $780M acquisition price).

**Exposure:** $200M-$680M if tenured faculty terminated in breach of contract ($300K-$1M per plaintiff Ã— 680 faculty).

**Litigation Risk:** $350K-$1.7M (back pay, front pay, compensatory damages capped at $300K, attorneys' fees).

**Recommendation:** Settle for $150K-$400K in confidential agreement with release of all claims.

#### 4. Shared Governance Obligations

**CONCLUSION:** Faculty handbook provisions on shared governance are **contractually binding**. AEH must preserve Faculty Senate and consult on academic policies, though final decision-making authority can be reserved to administration.

**Accreditation Risk:** Higher Learning Commission requires meaningful faculty governance. Elimination of Faculty Senate could jeopardize accreditation.

#### 5. Unionization and Collective Bargaining

**CONCLUSION:** Tenured/tenure-track faculty likely qualify as "managerial employees" under *Yeshiva* and cannot unionize. Non-tenure-track faculty (560), graduate student TAs/RAs (200-500), and staff (2,850) are NLRA-covered employees eligible to organize.

**Exposure:** $5M-$15M ongoing (wage increases, reduced management flexibility).

**CONCLUSION:** 403(b) retirement plan must convert to 401(k) upon loss of tax-exempt status. All 401(k) plans subject to ERISA. If MU sponsors defined benefit pension, AEH inherits funding obligations and potential withdrawal liability.
