#### 3. Successor Liability Creates Unlimited Retroactive Exposure

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM-HIGH - Contingent Liability

Section 1094(a)(23) imposes a statutory mandate on acquirers: "In the case of an institution that changes ownership, the new owner shall assume all liabilities of the institution related to the Title IV programs..."

Unlike typical M&A transactions where successor liability is limited by contractual representations, warranties, and indemnification provisions, the HEA imposes successor liability as a FEDERAL STATUTORY OBLIGATION that supersedes any contractual allocation.

American Education Holdings LLC will be statutorily liable for:

1. **Program Review Liabilities:** Any Title IV overpayments, improper disbursements, or compliance failures from pre-acquisition periods. Typical exposure: $500,000-$5 million per program review finding.

2. **Borrower Defense to Repayment Claims:** Former students who attended Midwest University prior to the acquisition may file borrower defense claims alleging institutional misrepresentation (e.g., false job placement rates, misleading accreditation claims, deceptive recruitment). 3. **Closed School Discharge:** If Midwest University closes at ANY time post-acquisition (including due to 90/10 failure), ALL currently enrolled students receive automatic loan discharge. 4. **Return of Title IV Funds:** Any errors in Return of Title IV calculations from pre-acquisition periods discovered in post-acquisition program reviews. Typical exposure: $100,000-$1 million per year of errors.

**No Statute of Limitations:**

Borrower defense claims can be filed at ANY time. There is no statutory limitations period. The Department has established a three-year limitations period in regulations (34 C.F.R. #### 4. Provisional Certification is Virtually Certain

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM-HIGH - Operational Restrictions

Section 1099c(h)(3) authorizes the Secretary to provisionally certify institutions "that have undergone a change of ownership resulting in a change of control, for a period of not more than 3 years."

**Why This is Virtually Certain (95% Probability):**

The Department of Education has adopted a policy of placing ALL for-profit acquisitions of nonprofit institutions on provisional certification. Recent examples:
- Grand Canyon University (for-profit conversion attempt)
- Purdue University Global (acquisition of Kaplan University)
- University of Arizona Global Campus (acquisition of Ashford University)

**Consequences of Provisional Certification:**

1. **Reimbursement Payment Method:** Instead of receiving Title IV funds in advance (normal practice), institution must disburse funds to students FIRST and then seek reimbursement from Department. This creates significant cash flow disadvantage, potentially requiring working capital loan of $10-30 million.

2. **Heightened Monitoring:** Institution must submit monthly or quarterly compliance reports instead of annual reports. This increases administrative burden and costs (estimated $250,000-$500,000 annually for additional staffing and consulting).

3. **Increased Letter of Credit Requirement:** Provisional institutions typically face higher letter of credit requirements than fully certified institutions.

4. **Easier Termination:** Department can terminate provisional certification more easily than full certification. Any compliance failure during provisional period may result in immediate termination rather than opportunity to cure.

5. **No Automatic Recertification:** After provisional period ends (2-3 years), institution must REAPPLY for full certification. This is not automatic. Department conducts comprehensive review and may extend provisional status or impose additional conditions.

**Duration:**

Typical provisional certification for change-of-ownership transactions: 2-3 years (full statutory maximum).

**Mitigation Strategies:**

1. **Budget for Cash Flow Impact:** Model cash flow under reimbursement payment method; secure working capital facility to cover timing gap

2. **Strengthen Compliance Infrastructure:** Hire additional compliance staff BEFORE transaction closes; retain external Title IV compliance consultant

3. **Proactive Department Engagement:** Request pre-acquisition meeting with Department to discuss transition plan; demonstrate commitment to compliance

**Bottom Line:** Provisional certification is virtually inevitable. American Education Holdings LLC must budget for the cash flow and compliance cost impacts.

### Statutory Gaps and Ambiguities Creating Legal Uncertainty

#### Gap 1: No Grace Period for 90/10 Rule

**Issue:** The statute provides NO transition period for institutions converting from nonprofit to for-profit status.

**Impact:** Newly converted institutions face immediate 90/10 compliance obligations despite having NO time to adjust recruitment strategies, financial aid practices, or revenue models.

**Policy Concern:** This may deter acquisitions that could rescue failing nonprofit institutions, as acquirers face existential risk from day one.

**Possible Solutions:**
- **Legislative Amendment:** Congress could amend Â§ 1094(d) to provide one-year grace period for nonprofit-to-for-profit conversions
- **Regulatory Guidance:** Department could issue guidance providing transitional relief (but would likely be challenged as exceeding statutory authority)
- **Current Law:** No grace period exists; acquirers must assume immediate 90/10 compliance obligation

#### Gap 2: Undefined Scope of "Liabilities" in Successor Liability Provision

**Issue:** Section 1094(a)(23) requires new owners to "assume all liabilities... related to the Title IV programs," but does NOT define "liabilities."

**Ambiguities:**
1. Does this include contingent liabilities (borrower defense claims not yet filed)?
2. Does this include liabilities discovered years after the transaction?
3. Does this include liabilities for which the Department has not yet issued final determination?

**Department Interpretation:** Department interprets "all liabilities" to include ALL of the above. However, NO federal court has definitively interpreted this provision, creating uncertainty.

**Constitutional Concern:** Imposing unlimited retroactive liability on an acquirer for conduct it did not commit may raise Fifth Amendment Due Process concerns. However, no court has struck down this provision.

**Impact on Transaction:** Acquirers cannot fully quantify or cap their liability exposure. This creates valuation challenges and may make transactions uninsurable.

#### Gap 3: Tension Between Statutory Mandate and Regulatory Discretion

**Issue:** Many critical requirements are established by statute but implemented through regulations. The interaction between statutory mandates and regulatory discretion creates uncertainty.

**Example:** The statute requires "financial responsibility" (Â§ 1099c(c)(2)) but does NOT specify what this means. Regulations establish the composite score methodology, but the Department periodically revises the regulations.

**Risk for Acquirers:** Regulatory changes during the provisional certification period (up to 3 years post-acquisition) could alter compliance requirements mid-stream, making it impossible for acquirers to assess risk at time of transaction.

### A. HIGHER EDUCATION ACT - INSTITUTIONAL ELIGIBILITY FRAMEWORK

#### 1. Statutory Definitions - 20 U.S.C. Â§ 1001

**Institution of Higher Education** (20 U.S.C. Â§ 1001(a))

The HEA defines "institution of higher education" as an educational institution that:
- Admits as regular students only persons having a high school diploma or equivalent
- Is legally authorized to provide postsecondary education in the state where located
- Provides an educational program for which it awards a bachelor's degree or higher
- Is accredited by a nationally recognized accrediting agency or association
- Has been in existence for at least two years
- Does not have a religious mission (public institutions) or is a nonprofit institution

**Critical Statutory Text:**
> "For purposes of this chapter, the term 'institution of higher education' means an educational institution in any State thatâ€”

### B. THE 90/10 RULE - 20 U.S.C. Â§ 1094(a)(24) AND Â§ 1094(d)

#### 1. Statutory Framework and Requirements

**Primary Statutory Citation:** 20 U.S.C. Â§ 1094(a)(24) and 20 U.S.C. Â§ 1094(d)
**Public Law Source:** Pub. L. No. 105-244 (1998 Amendments); as amended by Pub. L. No. 110-315 (2008); Pub. L. No. 117-2 (American Rescue Plan Act of 2021)
**Current Law Status:** Effective January 1, 2023, VA and DOD education benefits count as "federal education assistance funds"

**Complete Statutory Text - 20 U.S.C. Â§ 1094(a)(24):**

> "In the case of a proprietary institution of higher education (as defined in section 1002(b) of this title), such institution will derive not less than ten percent of such institution's revenues from sources other than Federal funds that are disbursed or delivered to or on behalf of a student to be used to attend such institution (referred to in this paragraph and subsection (d) as 'Federal education assistance funds'), as calculated in accordance with subsection (d)(1), or will be subject to the sanctions described in subsection (d)(2)."

**Calculation Methodology - 20 U.S.C. Â§ 1094(d)(1):**

The statute mandates specific calculation requirements:

1. **Accounting Method:**
   - Must use "cash basis of accounting" (not accrual basis)
   - Exception: Institutional loans made after July 1, 2012, are recognized only when repaid

2. **Revenue Counted:**
   - Only revenue from "tuition, fees, and other institutional charges" for students enrolled in Title IV-eligible programs
   - Includes activities "conducted by the institution that are necessary for the education or training of its students"
   - Must include all programs, not just Title IV-eligible programs

3. **Federal Education Assistance Funds (the "90" side):**
   - All Title IV federal student aid (Pell Grants, Direct Loans, FSEOG, etc.)
   - VA education benefits (Post-9/11 GI Bill, Montgomery GI Bill) - **Added by Pub. L. 117-2 (2021), effective 2023**
   - DOD Tuition Assistance - **Added by Pub. L. 117-2 (2021), effective 2023**
   - Funds paid by students using federal education assistance programs

4. **Non-Federal Revenue Sources (the "10" side):**
   - Private student loans (not federally guaranteed)
   - Institutional scholarships funded by non-federal sources
   - Out-of-pocket payments from students/families
   - Employer tuition reimbursement (non-federal employers)
   - State grant programs (state-funded only)

**Critical 2021 Amendment - Closure of "90/10 Loophole":**

Prior to January 1, 2023, VA and DOD education benefits were counted as "non-federal" revenue, creating a significant loophole that allowed proprietary institutions to aggressively recruit military-connected students without risk of failing the 90/10 Rule. The American Rescue Plan Act of 2021 (Pub. **Legislative Intent:** Congress enacted this change to protect veteran students from predatory recruitment practices by for-profit institutions that viewed military benefits as a workaround to the 90/10 requirement.

#### 1. Statutory Framework - 20 U.S.C. Â§ 1681

#### 3. Private Right of Action - Implied by Supreme Court

**Critical Statutory Omission:**

20 U.S.C. Â§ 1681 **does not contain any express language authorizing a private right of action**. The statute does not state that individuals may file lawsuits for Title IX violations.

**Judicial Creation of Private Right of Action:**

In *Cannon v. University of Chicago*, 441 U.S. 677 (1979), the Supreme Court held that Title IX contains an implied private right of action, allowing individuals to sue educational institutions directly for sex discrimination.

**Supreme Court's Reasoning:**

1. **Congressional Intent:** Title IX was modeled on Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, for which courts had already implied a private right of action
2. **Legislative History:** Congress was aware of Title VI jurisprudence when enacting Title IX
3. **Remedial Purpose:** Private lawsuits necessary to accomplish statutory purpose of protecting individuals from discrimination
4. **No Frustration of Legislative Scheme:** Private enforcement complements administrative enforcement

**Statutory Authority Cited by Court:**

The Supreme Court relied on the statute's remedial purpose and legislative history, not explicit statutory text, to imply the private right of action.

**Subsequent Developments:**

- *Franklin v. Gwinnett County Public Schools*, 503 U.S. 60 (1992): Monetary damages available in Title IX private actions
- *Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School District*, 524 U.S. 274 (1998): Actual notice and deliberate indifference required for damages in teacher-student harassment cases
- *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999): Actual notice and deliberate indifference required for damages in student-on-student harassment cases

**Statutory Consequence:**

Because the private right of action is judicially implied rather than statutorily created, Congress could eliminate it by amending Title IX. However, no such amendment has been enacted, and the private right of action remains firmly established.

---

#### 4. Enforcement Mechanisms - Administrative and Judicial

**Administrative Enforcement - Not Specified in Â§ 1681:**

Section 1681 does not specify how the statute is to be enforced. Section 1682 incorporates enforcement mechanisms from other civil rights laws:

**20 U.S.C. Â§ 1682 - Federal Administrative Enforcement:**

> "Each Federal department and agency which is empowered to extend Federal financial assistance to any education program or activity... is authorized and directed to effectuate the provisions of section 1681 of this title with respect to such program or activity by issuing rules, regulations, or orders of general applicability..."

**Enforcement Process:**

1. Agency investigates alleged violation
2. Agency attempts to secure voluntary compliance
3. If voluntary compliance fails, agency may:
   - Terminate federal financial assistance, OR
   - Use "any other means authorized by law"
4. Termination requires opportunity for hearing
5. Termination must be reported to Congress

**Practical Limitation of Administrative Enforcement:**

Termination of federal financial assistance is a "nuclear option" rarely used. The Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR) typically resolves Title IX complaints through negotiated resolution agreements rather than funding termination.

**Judicial Enforcement:**

The implied private right of action (established in *Cannon*) provides the primary enforcement mechanism. Individuals may sue for:
- Injunctive relief (ordering institution to cease discriminatory practices)
- Monetary damages (compensatory, not punitive)
- Attorney's fees (under fee-shifting statutes)

#### 5. Title IX Athletics Requirements - Statutory vs. Regulatory

**Statutory Text - Â§ 1681(b):**

> "Nothing contained in subsection (a) of this section shall be interpreted to require any educational institution to grant preferential or disparate treatment to the members of one sex on account of an imbalance which may exist with respect to the total number or percentage of persons of that sex participating in or receiving the benefits of any federally supported program or activity, in comparison with the total number or percentage of persons of that sex in any community, State, section, or other area..."

**Interpretation:**

This provision clarifies that Title IX does not require proportional representation based on general population statistics. However, it does not preclude regulations requiring proportional athletic opportunities based on *enrollment* statistics.

**Regulatory Framework (34 C.F.R. Â§ 106.41) - Not Statutory:**

The Department of Education's regulations interpret Title IX to require "equal athletic opportunity" based on a three-part test:
1. Substantial proportionality between athletic participation and enrollment
2. History and continuing practice of program expansion, OR
3. Full and effective accommodation of interests and abilities

**Critical Distinction:**

The three-part test is **regulatory**, not statutory. Challenges to the test must argue that the regulation exceeds statutory authority, not that the statute itself is unconstitutional or problematic.

**Implications for Project Athena:**

Midwest University's Title IX athletics obligations are primarily regulatory, not statutory. The statutory text provides only general prohibition on sex discrimination. Conversion to for-profit status does not alter Title IX athletics obligations.

#### 3. Timely Warning Requirements - Â§ 1092(f)(3)

**Statutory Text:**

3. **Content:** Must aid in prevention of similar occurrences

**Distinction from Emergency Notifications:**

- **Emergency Notifications:** Immediate threats requiring urgent protective action (active shooter, tornado, hazardous materials spill, etc.)

#### 3. Distinction Between VAWA, Clery Act, and Title IX

**Risk Level:** âš ï¸ **CRITICAL - EXISTENTIAL THREAT**

5. **Monitoring Systems:** Implement real-time 90/10 monitoring to track ratio monthly and trigger corrective action

### D. CRITICAL STATUTORY GAPS AND AMBIGUITIES

#### 1. No Grace Period for 90/10 Rule

**Issue:** The statute does not provide any grace period or transition period for institutions converting from nonprofit to for-profit status.

**Policy Argument for Grace Period:**

- Newly converted institutions need time to adjust recruitment and financial aid strategies
- Immediate application creates existential risk that may deter socially beneficial conversions (e.g., rescuing failing nonprofit institutions)
- Regulatory grace periods exist for other compliance areas (e.g., new institutions receive initial cohort default rate exemptions)

**Counterargument:**

- Congress intentionally limited 90/10 Rule to for-profit institutions to prevent excessive reliance on federal funds
- Grace period would create loophole for institutions to convert temporarily, exploit federal funds, and reconvert
- Plain statutory text contains no grace period provision

**Current Law:** No grace period exists in statute or regulations.

---

#### 2. Unclear Scope of "Liabilities" for Successor Liability

**Issue:** Section 1094(a)(23) requires new owners to "assume all liabilities... related to the Title IV programs," but does not define "liabilities."

**Questions:**

1. Does this include contingent liabilities (borrower defense claims that may be filed in the future)?
2. Does this include liabilities arising from pre-acquisition conduct discovered post-acquisition?
3. Does this include liabilities for which the Department has not yet issued a final determination?

**Department Position:**

The Department has interpreted "all liabilities" broadly to include:
- Program review findings issued post-acquisition for pre-acquisition conduct
- Borrower defense claims filed post-acquisition for pre-acquisition enrollment
- Closed school discharge liabilities if institution closes at any time post-acquisition

**No Court Interpretation:**

No federal court has definitively interpreted the scope of Â§ 1094(a)(23). This creates uncertainty for acquirers.

---

#### 3. Constitutional Concerns with Retroactive Liability

**Issue:** Imposing liability on American Education Holdings LLC for Midwest University's pre-acquisition conduct may raise Due Process concerns.

**Constitutional Argument:**

- Fifth Amendment Due Process Clause prohibits arbitrary deprivation of property
- Imposing unlimited liability on a party that did not cause the harm may be unconstitutional
- American Education Holdings LLC had no ability to prevent pre-acquisition violations

**Government Response:**

- Acquirers voluntarily enter Title IV program; participation is a privilege, not a right
- Successor liability is clearly stated in statute; acquirers have notice
- Acquirers can conduct due diligence and negotiate purchase price to account for liabilities
- Purpose is to protect federal fisc and prevent acquirers from buying institutions, stripping assets, and avoiding liabilities

**Current Law:** No court has struck down Â§ 1094(a)(23) as unconstitutional.

#### 2. The 90/10 Rule Presents the Most Significant Statutory Risk

**Critical Finding:** The 90/10 Rule (20 U.S.C. Â§ 1094(a)(24) and Â§ 1094(d)) represents an **existential threat** to the transaction's viability because:

1. **Immediate Applicability:** No grace period or phase-in for newly converted institutions
2. **High Likelihood of Failure:** Private nonprofit universities typically derive 60-80% of revenue from federal sources; post-conversion, this percentage often increases
3. **No Cure Mechanism:** Statute provides no appeal, waiver, or cure process once institution fails for two consecutive years
4. **Automatic Ineligibility:** Loss of Title IV eligibility occurs 30 days after second consecutive failure
5. **Death Spiral Effect:** Loss of Title IV eligibility â†’ enrollment collapse â†’ institution closure â†’ closed school discharge liability ($50-200 million)

**Statutory Text Preventing Cure:**

> "An institution... that fails to meet a requirement of subsection (a)(24) for any 2 institutional fiscal years... shall become ineligible to participate in the programs authorized by this subchapter for a period of not less than 2 institutional fiscal years." (Â§ 1094(d)(2)(A))

The word "shall" is mandatory, not discretionary. The Secretary has no authority to waive this requirement or grant extensions.

**Recommendation:** American Education Holdings LLC must conduct detailed 90/10 modeling before closing the transaction and develop concrete revenue diversification strategies to reduce federal aid dependence below 85% (providing 5% safety margin).

#### 3. Successor Liability Provision Creates Unlimited Retroactive Exposure

**Critical Finding:** Section 1094(a)(23) imposes **unlimited retroactive liability** on American Education Holdings LLC for all Title IV-related liabilities incurred by Midwest University, regardless of:
- Whether American Education Holdings LLC had knowledge of the liabilities
- Whether the purchase agreement allocates liability to the seller
- Whether indemnification or escrow arrangements exist

**Statutory Text:**

> "In the case of an institution that changes ownership, the new owner shall assume all liabilities of the institution related to the Title IV programs..." (Â§ 1094(a)(23))

**Scope of Liability:**

- Program review findings for pre-acquisition periods
- Borrower defense to repayment claims filed by former students (no statute of limitations)
- Closed school discharge liabilities if institution closes post-acquisition
- Return of Title IV funds for pre-acquisition overpayments

**Recommendation:** Conduct extensive Title IV due diligence, including:
- Review of all program reviews conducted in past 10 years
- Analysis of potential borrower defense exposure
- Review of Return of Title IV funds calculations for past 6 years
- Assessment of administrative capability and compliance systems

### B. CRITICAL STATUTORY GAPS AND INTERPRETATION CHALLENGES

#### 1. Absence of Grace Period for 90/10 Rule

**Issue:** The statute provides no transition period for institutions converting from nonprofit to for-profit status, despite the fundamental change in regulatory obligations.

**Policy Concern:** The immediate application of the 90/10 Rule may deter acquisitions that could rescue failing nonprofit institutions, as the acquirer faces immediate existential risk even if the acquisition would ultimately strengthen the institution.

**Congressional Intent:** Legislative history of the 1998 amendments that established the modern 90/10 Rule suggests Congress intended to prevent excessive federal aid dependence by for-profit institutions. However, there is no evidence Congress considered the application to nonprofit-to-for-profit conversions.

**Possible Regulatory Solution:** The Department of Education could potentially issue guidance providing a one-year grace period for newly converted institutions to adjust operations. However, such guidance would likely face legal challenge as exceeding statutory authority, given the plain text contains no grace period provision.

---

#### 2. Undefined Scope of "Liabilities" in Successor Liability Provision

**Issue:** Section 1094(a)(23) requires new owners to "assume all liabilities... related to the Title IV programs," but the statute does not define "liabilities" or specify temporal scope.

**Ambiguities:**

1. **Contingent Liabilities:** Does this include borrower defense claims that have not yet been filed but may be filed in the future?
2. **Undiscovered Liabilities:** Does this include liabilities arising from pre-acquisition conduct discovered years after the transaction?
3. **Unliquidated Liabilities:** Does this include liabilities for which the Department has not yet issued a final determination?

**Department Interpretation:** The Department has consistently interpreted "all liabilities" to include all three categories above. However, no federal court has definitively interpreted this provision.

**Constitutional Concern:** Imposing unlimited retroactive liability on an acquirer for conduct it did not commit may raise Fifth Amendment Due Process concerns. However, no court has struck down this provision as unconstitutional.

---

#### 3. Tension Between Statutory Mandate and Regulatory Discretion

**Issue:** Many critical requirements (90/10 Rule, financial responsibility standards, cohort default rates) are established by statute but implemented through regulations. The interaction between statutory mandates and regulatory discretion creates uncertainty.

**Example:** The statute requires institutions to meet "financial responsibility" standards (Â§ 1099c(c)(2)) but does not specify what those standards are. Regulations establish the composite score methodology, but the Department periodically revises the regulations, creating moving targets for institutions.

**Risk for Acquirers:** Regulatory changes during the provisional certification period (up to 3 years post-acquisition) could alter compliance requirements, making it difficult for acquirers to assess risk at time of transaction.

**Legislative Solution:** Congress could establish more specific statutory standards to reduce regulatory uncertainty. However, this would reduce the Department's flexibility to adapt to changing conditions in higher education.

### D. ALTERNATIVE TRANSACTION STRUCTURES TO CONSIDER

Given the significant statutory risks identified in this analysis, American Education Holdings LLC should consider alternative transaction structures that may reduce regulatory risk:

#### Option 1: Delayed Conversion to For-Profit Status

**Structure:** Acquire Midwest University but maintain nonprofit 501(c)(3) status for first 2-3 years, then convert to for-profit after stabilizing operations.

**Advantages:**
- Avoids immediate 90/10 Rule application
- Allows time to develop revenue diversification strategies
- Reduces letter of credit requirement (nonprofit institutions face lower financial responsibility standards)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires maintaining nonprofit board structure during transition period
- Limits ability to distribute profits to American Education Holdings LLC shareholders
- May be challenged by IRS as sham transaction if conversion was always intended

**Legal Risk:** IRS may revoke 501(c)(3) status retroactively if it determines nonprofit status was maintained solely to avoid Title IV requirements, resulting in excise taxes and penalties.

---

#### Option 2: Maintain Nonprofit Status Permanently Through Controlled Entity

**Structure:** Midwest University remains 501(c)(3) nonprofit, but enters into long-term management agreement with American Education Holdings LLC subsidiary. American Education Holdings LLC receives management fees approximating profit margins but institution retains nonprofit status.

**Advantages:**
- Completely avoids 90/10 Rule (does not apply to nonprofits)
- No letter of credit requirement
- Maintains Title IV eligibility without for-profit restrictions

**Disadvantages:**
- Management fees limited to "reasonable compensation" under IRS rules
- IRS may challenge arrangement as improper private benefit
- State attorneys general may challenge as impermissible transfer of control

**Legal Risk:** This structure has been successfully challenged by regulators in several cases (e.g., University of Arizona/Ashford University, Purdue/Kaplan University initially structured this way but later changed).

---

#### Option 3: Asset Purchase Rather Than Stock Purchase

**Structure:** American Education Holdings LLC purchases Midwest University's assets (facilities, programs, faculty contracts) but does not acquire legal entity or Title IV eligibility. American Education Holdings LLC establishes new for-profit entity and applies for Title IV eligibility as new institution.

**Advantages:**
- Avoids successor liability provision (Â§ 1094(a)(23))
- Avoids assumption of pre-existing Title IV liabilities
- Allows clean start with compliance systems

**Disadvantages:**
- New institution must satisfy 2-year longevity requirement before Title IV eligibility (Â§ 1002(b)(1)(B))
- Loss of accreditation (must seek initial accreditation, which takes 2-5 years)
- Students cannot use Title IV aid during transition period, likely resulting in complete enrollment loss

**Practical Conclusion:** This structure is not viable for institutions dependent on Title IV eligibility.
