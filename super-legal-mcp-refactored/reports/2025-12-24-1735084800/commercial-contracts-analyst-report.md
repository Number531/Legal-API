### A. Faculty Handbook as Binding Contract

#### Legal Framework and Enforceability

Faculty handbooks constitute enforceable contracts when universities distribute them creating reasonable expectations, contain specific employment terms, and faculty rely on handbook provisions. Recent scholarship in the *Cardozo Law Review* (2024) confirms courts increasingly enforce handbook provisions as contractual obligations, particularly regarding tenure and dismissal procedures. The landmark Supreme Court decision *Perry v. Sindermann*, 408 U.S. 593 (1972), established that faculty handbooks can create implied contractual rights even absent explicit written tenure agreements‚Äîde facto tenure arising from "rules and understandings officially promulgated and fostered" creates enforceable property interests in continued employment.

A 2023 New York appellate court ruled that NYU's faculty handbook constitutes a binding contract with "the force of contract," joining the national trend of judicial enforcement. The American Association of University Professors maintains a state-by-state guide documenting enforceability variations, with most jurisdictions recognizing handbook enforceability when documents contain sufficiently definite terms.

**Application to Midwest University**: MU's faculty handbook must be treated as a binding contract. The 680 tenured faculty members possess vested contractual rights in their tenure appointments, shared governance authority, and handbook-specified dismissal procedures. These obligations survive AEH's acquisition under successor liability principles‚Äîthe acquiring entity assumes all predecessor contractual obligations.

#### Tenure as Vested Property Right

Academic tenure represents both a constitutionally protected property right (at public institutions) and a contractual right (at private institutions like MU). Once conferred, tenure creates vested rights that cannot be unilaterally modified or eliminated absent consideration or faculty consent. The 680 tenured faculty at MU represent approximately $85M-$95M in annual compensation (assuming $125K-$140K average salaries). These tenure obligations continue post-acquisition unless: (1) financial exigency is properly declared through AAUP-compliant procedures; (2) individual termination for cause with full procedural protections; or (3) faculty consent to modified terms with adequate consideration.

Recent Department of Education policy extends successor liability to "owners, investors, and controlling parties" of educational institutions, requiring acquirers to guarantee regulatory and contractual liabilities including tenure commitments. AEH cannot reduce tenure liability through unilateral handbook amendments‚Äîsuch action would expose AEH to breach of contract litigation from 680 faculty members with potential damages including back pay, front pay, and attorneys' fees, estimated at $102M-$136M in aggregate exposure.

#### Financial Exigency Limitations

Courts define financial exigency as "fiscal emergencies that are long-term in nature, promising to persist into the foreseeable future, and thereby threaten the fulfillment of the institution's academic mission and, eventually, its very survival." AEH's $780M acquisition demonstrates institutional solvency and investment capacity‚ÄîAEH cannot declare financial exigency immediately post-acquisition as pretext for reducing faculty headcount. Such action would constitute bad faith and breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

The Fourth Circuit in *Krotkoff v. Goucher College*, 585 F.2d 675 (1978), held that "academic custom and usage as demonstrated by AAUP's 1940 Statement added an implied 'financial exigency' limitation to the tenure contract," establishing that AAUP procedural standards become contractually binding when incorporated into handbooks. Proper financial exigency procedures require: formal board declaration, faculty senate consultation on program reductions, 12-month minimum notice, sequential displacement (non-tenure-track first, then untenured, then tenured by reverse seniority), individual hearings before faculty committees, and right of first refusal for reappointment.

**Risk Assessment**: If AEH attempts pretextual financial exigency terminations without genuine institutional crisis and full AAUP procedural compliance, litigation exposure totals $102M-$136M plus accreditation jeopardy as HLC would view mass tenure violations as governance failure.

#### Shared Governance Obligations

Shared governance‚Äîthe "joint responsibility of faculty, administrations, and governing boards to govern colleges and universities"‚Äîderives from AAUP's 1966 Statement on Government giving faculty "primary responsibility for such fundamental areas as curriculum, subject matter and methods of instruction, research, faculty status, and aspects of student life which relate to the educational process." MU's faculty senate retains handbook-defined authority over curriculum, academic programs, faculty hiring/promotion/tenure, academic standards, and institutional policy recommendations. ### C. Student Enrollment Agreements and Federal Liability

#### University-Student Contractual Relationship

The university-student relationship is "contractual in substance and form, with students paying substantial tuition for access to educational services." Contract formation occurs through application, acceptance, enrollment, and tuition payment. Enrollment agreements‚Äîwhether explicit written documents or implied through catalogs/handbooks/policies‚Äîcreate enforceable obligations on both parties.

Enrollment agreements include express terms (tuition, fees, refund policies, academic requirements, conduct standards) and implied terms (university will remain accredited, programs will continue through anticipated graduation, degrees will be conferred upon completion, institution will maintain quality standards). When students use Title IV federal financial aid, Department of Education closed school discharge regulations create additional obligations‚Äîstudents whose institution closes receive federal loan forgiveness, potentially creating federal liability for AEH under successor liability policies.

#### Closed School Discharge and Federal Recoupment

Under 34 CFR ¬ß 685.214, "the Secretary of Education can discharge a borrower's obligation to repay a Direct Loan if the borrower did not complete the program of study because the school closed," with eligibility extending to borrowers enrolled on closure date or who withdrew within 180 days prior. "Students who receive closed school discharges are freed from their repayment obligations, receive reimbursement for previous payments, and have all records of their loans‚Äîincluding any adverse history‚Äîerased from their credit reports."

When DOE discharges student loans due to institutional closure, it may seek recoupment from the institution (if assets remain), owners/investors/controlling parties under recent DOE successor liability policy, and surety bonds or tuition recovery funds. The assignment of loan refund rights means "the borrower is deemed to have assigned to the Secretary any right to a loan refund (up to the amount discharged) that the borrower may have by contract or applicable law with respect to the loan or enrollment agreement against the school, its principals, affiliates, successors, and sureties."

### D. Material Vendor and Service Contracts

#### Assignment Restrictions and Change of Control

While UCC ¬ß 2-210 establishes default free assignability ("without an anti-assignment provision, contracts are generally assignable even absent the consent of the counterparty"), most university vendor contracts contain explicit anti-assignment clauses requiring prior written consent. "Courts normally enforce anti-assignment clauses that are negotiated and agreed upon by both parties," with two primary structures: (1) absolute prohibition ("This contract may not be assigned"), and (2) consent requirement ("may not be assigned without prior written consent").

Many vendor contracts include expansive assignment definitions: "Assignment includes any change of control of the contracting party, whether by stock sale, asset sale, merger, or other reorganization." Under such provisions, AEH's acquisition constitutes deemed assignment requiring vendor consent even though MU as institutional entity continues to exist.

"Any attempted assignment or transfer without compliance with the clause will constitute a breach of contract, giving the non-assigning party the right to exercise remedies," typically including immediate termination, claw back of fees, and damages recovery. Critically, "no delegation of performance relieves the party delegating of any duty to perform or any liability for breach"‚Äîeven when assignment occurs, original contracting party may remain guarantor of third-party performance.

### B. FINANCIAL EXIGENCY AND TENURE TERMINATION

#### 1. Legal Standard for Financial Exigency

**Definition and Threshold Requirements**

Financial exigency represents the primary mechanism through which universities may lawfully terminate tenured faculty. The doctrine permits dismissal when an institution faces "fiscal emergencies that are long-term in nature, promising to persist into the foreseeable future, and thereby threaten the fulfillment of the institution's academic mission and, eventually, its very survival."¬≤‚Åµ

**Judicial Deference to Institutional Determinations**

Courts have historically declined to prescribe universal financial exigency standards, "preferring to leave the definition to the governing authority and administration of each institution" and relying "on administrators alone to establish procedures for identification of programs and individuals to be affected."¬≤‚Å∂ This judicial deference, however, does not eliminate the requirement that exigency declarations be bona fide and procedurally sound.¬≤‚Å∑

**AAUP 1940 Statement Framework**

The AAUP's 1940 Statement on Principles of Academic Freedom and Tenure‚Äî"the most widely-accepted academic definition of tenure" according to *Krotkoff v. Goucher College*, 585 F.2d 675, 679 (4th Cir. 1978)‚Äîhas been contractually incorporated into faculty handbooks at numerous institutions.¬≤‚Å∏ In *Krotkoff*, the Fourth Circuit held that "academic custom and usage as demonstrated by AAUP's 1940 Statement added an implied 'financial exigency' limitation to the tenure contract."¬≤‚Åπ

Many faculty handbooks explicitly adopt AAUP standards, stating that "All teachers (whether faculty or not) will enjoy academic freedom as set forth in the Association of American Colleges - American Association of University Professors' 1940 Statement of Principles on Academic Freedom and Tenure."¬≥‚Å∞ Where incorporated, AAUP standards become contractually binding and enforceable.¬≥¬π

#### 2. Procedural Requirements for Financial Exigency

**AAUP Procedural Safeguards**

The AAUP's "Recommended Institutional Regulations on Academic Freedom and Tenure" specify extensive procedural protections when financial exigency threatens tenured positions:

- **Formal declaration** by the governing board following faculty consultation
- **Primary responsibility** of the faculty in determining where program reductions occur
- **Notice period** of at least 12 months before termination becomes effective
- **Displacement priority**: Non-tenure-track faculty terminated before untenured faculty; untenured before tenured
- **Opportunity for hearing** before a faculty committee with right to representation¬≥¬≤

**Major Case Law: Johnson v. Board of Regents**

In *Johnson v. Board of Regents*, 377 F. Supp. 227 (W.D. Wisc. 1974), tenured faculty challenged terminations arising from legislative budget cuts of 2.5% (1973-74) and an additional 2.5% (1974-75) combined with declining enrollment.¬≥¬≥ The court addressed the tension between institutional financial discretion and faculty procedural rights, ultimately deferring to the institution's exigency determination while requiring procedural due process.¬≥‚Å¥

Similarly, in *Levitt v. Board of Trustees*, 376 F. Supp. 945 (D. Neb. 1974), the court examined financial exigency resulting from legislative budget reductions, reinforcing that institutions must demonstrate genuine long-term fiscal crisis rather than mere budget preferences or restructuring objectives.¬≥‚Åµ

**Contractual vs. Constitutional Protections**

"The protections usually afforded by the courts and institutional custom when a tenured faculty member is dismissed for cause are not available when dismissal is the result of a determination of financial exigency."¬≥‚Å∂ However, "tenured faculty members who have been notified of termination due to financial exigency have the right to a hearing before faculty rights committees."¬≥‚Å∑

#### 3. Application to AEH Acquisition

**AEH Cannot Manufacture Financial Exigency**

AEH's acquisition of MU for $780M demonstrates institutional solvency and investment capacity. AEH cannot declare financial exigency immediately post-acquisition as a pretext for reducing tenured faculty headcount‚Äîsuch action would constitute bad faith and breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.¬≥‚Å∏

**Legitimate vs. Pretextual Exigency**

Courts distinguish between genuine financial crisis (institutional survival threatened) and strategic business decisions (cost optimization, margin improvement, restructuring).¬≥‚Åπ If AEH:

- Acquires MU in a financially viable transaction
- Maintains financial capacity through parent company resources
- Declares exigency to restructure faculty composition or reduce costs
- Does not provide faculty with meaningful participation in retrenchment decisions

Then such exigency declaration would likely be deemed pretextual and unenforceable, exposing AEH to breach of contract claims from each terminated tenured faculty member.‚Å¥‚Å∞

**Financial Exigency Exposure Analysis**

If AEH attempts financial exigency terminations without meeting AAUP/handbook procedural requirements:

- **Litigation exposure**: 680 tenured faculty √ó average $150K-$200K damages (back pay, front pay, attorneys' fees) = $102M-$136M potential exposure
- **Accreditation risk**: HLC likely to view mass tenure terminations as violation of shared governance standards, jeopardizing accreditation
- **Reputational damage**: Faculty recruitment, student enrollment, and institutional legitimacy severely compromised‚Å¥¬π

**Proper Procedure If Financial Distress Arises**

If genuine financial distress emerges post-acquisition requiring faculty reductions:

1. **Formal board declaration** of financial exigency with detailed financial documentation
2. **Faculty senate consultation** regarding program reductions and faculty displacement
3. **12-month notice** to affected tenured faculty (minimum)
4. **Retrenchment sequence**: Eliminate non-tenure-track positions first, then untenured, then tenured by reverse seniority
5. **Individual hearings** for affected faculty before faculty committee
6. **Right of first refusal** for reappointment if positions become available within 2-3 years‚Å¥¬≤

### C. SHARED GOVERNANCE AND FACULTY SENATE AUTHORITY

#### 1. Shared Governance Principles in Higher Education

**Definition and Framework**

Shared governance refers to "the joint responsibility of faculty, administrations, and governing boards to govern colleges and universities, with differences in the weight of each group's voice on a particular issue determined by the extent of its responsibility for and expertise on that issue."‚Å¥¬≥ This principle derives from the AAUP's 1966 "Statement on Government of Colleges and Universities," which establishes faculty primacy over academic matters and joint decision-making over institutional policy.‚Å¥‚Å¥

**Faculty Primary Responsibility Areas**

The faculty senate or academic council typically exercises "primary responsibility for such fundamental areas as curriculum, subject matter and methods of instruction, research, faculty status, and aspects of student life which relate to the educational process."‚Å¥‚Åµ In these domains, faculty recommendations should normally be accepted by administration and governing boards, with rejection occurring only in extraordinary circumstances and with detailed explanation.‚Å¥‚Å∂

**Shared vs. Delegated Authority**

Importantly, "while the administration and governing board of the institution are compelled to consider the campus governance body's resolutions and recommendations, they are not required to accept or implement them."‚Å¥‚Å∑ Shared governance creates procedural consultation requirements rather than faculty veto power‚Äîexcept in areas where handbooks explicitly grant final faculty authority.‚Å¥‚Å∏

#### 2. Contractual vs. Governance Distinctions

**Collective Bargaining vs. Handbook Provisions**

"Any provision in collective bargaining agreements is the province of the union leadership and can only be changed through negotiation and legal agreement between management and elected union leadership. A shared governance body cannot negotiate contractual language."‚Å¥‚Åπ This distinction matters when determining which faculty handbook provisions are unilaterally amendable (governance) versus which require faculty consent (contractual).‚Åµ‚Å∞

**Tenure Decisions as Contractual**

Tenure conferral, however, occupies a hybrid space: faculty committees evaluate candidates and make recommendations (shared governance), but once tenure is granted through board action, it becomes a vested contractual right (contractual).‚Åµ¬π Post-acquisition, AEH cannot alter tenure standards or revoke existing tenure without following handbook procedures and obtaining faculty participation.‚Åµ¬≤

#### 3. Shared Governance Survival in Acquisitions

**Institutional Continuity Requirements**

When AEH acquires MU, shared governance structures continue unless expressly modified through proper handbook amendment procedures.‚Åµ¬≥ The faculty senate retains its handbook-defined authority over:

- Curriculum and academic program approval
- Faculty hiring, promotion, and tenure recommendations
- Academic standards and degree requirements
- Institutional policy recommendations affecting academic mission‚Åµ‚Å¥

**Accreditation Requirements**

The Higher Learning Commission (HLC) and regional accreditors require institutions to demonstrate effective shared governance as a condition of accreditation.‚Åµ‚Åµ If AEH attempts to circumvent or eliminate faculty senate authority post-acquisition, HLC may determine that MU no longer meets accreditation standards for institutional governance, triggering change of control denial or show-cause sanctions.‚Åµ‚Å∂

**Risk Analysis for AEH**

MU's faculty handbook likely contains provisions establishing:

- Faculty senate composition and election procedures
- Faculty senate authority over curriculum and academic policies
- Faculty participation in presidential searches, budget review, and strategic planning
- Procedures for handbook amendment requiring faculty senate approval or consultation‚Åµ‚Å∑

If AEH attempts to unilaterally diminish faculty senate authority (e.g., by limiting access to financial information, excluding faculty from strategic decisions, or overriding curriculum decisions without explanation), it risks:

- **Breach of contract claims** if handbook provisions are contractually enforceable
- **Accreditation jeopardy** if HLC determines shared governance has been eliminated
- **Faculty non-cooperation** affecting academic program delivery, accreditation self-study, and institutional functioning‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Recommended Approach**

AEH should:

1. **Preserve existing faculty senate structures** and authority during transition period
2. **Engage faculty senate leadership** in acquisition planning and integration decisions
3. **Propose handbook amendments through proper procedures** if governance changes are desired
4. **Document faculty consultation** for HLC accreditation compliance
5. **Recognize faculty primacy** over curriculum and academic standards while retaining administrative authority over budget and operations‚Åµ‚Åπ

### D. NCAA DIVISION I MEMBERSHIP AND ATHLETIC CONFERENCE OBLIGATIONS

#### 1. For-Profit Institution Restrictions in NCAA Division I

**NCAA Governance Restrictions**

The NCAA imposes significant restrictions on for-profit institutions as active members of Division I. - **NCAA Basketball Tournament units**: $2M-$3M annually based on conference performance
- **Academic Enhancement Fund**: $500K-$1M annually in institutional support
- **Student Assistance Fund**: $300K-$500K annually for student-athlete welfare programs
- **Conference grants program**: $200K-$400K annually‚Å∂¬≥

**Total annual revenue loss**: Approximately $3M-$4.9M compared to non-profit Division I institutions.

#### 2. Grand Canyon University Precedent

**Only For-Profit Division I Institution**

Grand Canyon University (GCU) provides the sole precedent for a for-profit institution operating in NCAA Division I. GCU "moved from Division II to Division I in 2012" and joined the Western Athletic Conference, later transitioning to the Mountain West Conference.‚Å∂‚Å¥ This move "concerned some within the NCAA and led to a yearlong study of the issue."‚Å∂‚Åµ

**NCAA's Response to For-Profit Membership**

**GCU's Path to Division I**

To be eligible for Division I membership, GCU had to:

1. First be "an active member of Division II for five years"
2. "Meet Division I sport sponsorship and financial aid minimums"
3. "Receive an offer of membership from a Division I conference"‚Å∂‚Å∏

On August 23, 2017, the NCAA officially approved GCU's move to Division I, elevating the university to active membership status after a multi-year transition period.‚Å∂‚Åπ

**Ongoing For-Profit Status Dispute**

### E. STUDENT ENROLLMENT AGREEMENTS AND CONTRACTUAL OBLIGATIONS

#### 1. University-Student Contractual Relationship

**Contract Formation**

The university-student relationship is "contractual in substance and form, with students paying substantial tuition for access to educational services."‚Å∏‚Å∂ Contract formation occurs when:

1. **Student applies** for admission
2. **University accepts** application (offer)
3. **Student enrolls and pays tuition** (acceptance + consideration)‚Å∏‚Å∑

The enrollment agreement‚Äîwhether explicit written document or implied through catalogs, handbooks, and institutional policies‚Äîcreates enforceable obligations on both parties.‚Å∏‚Å∏

**Express vs. Implied Terms**

Enrollment agreements typically include:

- **Express terms**: Tuition costs, fees, refund policies, academic requirements, conduct standards
- **Implied terms**: University will remain accredited, programs will continue through student's anticipated graduation, degrees will be conferred upon successful completion, institution will maintain educational quality standards‚Å∏‚Åπ

**Third-Party Beneficiary Issues**

While students are direct contracting parties, federal student loan programs may create additional obligations when students use Title IV aid. The Department of Education's closed school discharge regulations establish student rights to loan forgiveness if the institution closes, creating potential federal liability for AEH.‚Åπ‚Å∞

#### 2. Tuition Refund Obligations

**Standard Refund Policies**

State laws and institutional policies govern tuition refunds. Typical refund schedules:

- **100% refund**: First 1-2 weeks of semester (add/drop period)
- **Declining percentage**: 80% (week 3), 60% (week 4), 40% (week 5), 20% (week 6)
- **No refund**: After week 8 of semester‚Åπ¬π

**Student Withdrawal vs. Dismissal**

- **Voluntary withdrawal**: Prorated refund based on institutional policy
- **Disciplinary dismissal**: Generally no refund (student violated conduct code)
- **Academic dismissal**: Varies by institution; some provide partial refunds‚Åπ¬≤

**Institutional Closure Refund Obligations**

"Upon discharge, the borrower is deemed to have assigned to the Secretary any right to a loan refund (up to the amount discharged) that the borrower may have by contract or applicable law with respect to the loan or enrollment agreement against the school, its principals, affiliates, successors, and sureties."‚Åπ¬≥

#### 3. Closed School Discharge and Federal Liability

**Federal Closed School Discharge Regulations**

Under 34 CFR ¬ß 685.214, "the Secretary of Education can discharge a borrower's obligation to repay a Direct Loan if the borrower (or student) did not complete the program of study because the school closed."‚Åπ‚Å¥

**Eligibility Requirements**

"After confirming the date of a school's closure, the Secretary identifies any Direct Loan borrower who appears to have been enrolled at the school on the school closure date or to have withdrawn not more than 180 days prior to the closure date."‚Åπ‚Åµ Only loans made on or after January 1, 1986, are eligible for discharge.‚Åπ‚Å∂

"Students who receive closed school discharges are freed from their repayment obligations, receive reimbursement for previous payments, and have all records of their loans‚Äîincluding any adverse history‚Äîerased from their credit reports."‚Åπ‚Å∑

**Institutional Liability**

When the Department of Education discharges student loans due to institutional closure, it may seek recoupment from:

- **The institution** (if assets remain)
- **Owners, investors, and controlling parties** under recent DOE policy extending successor liability
- **Surety bonds or tuition recovery funds** maintained by the institution‚Åπ‚Å∏

#### 4. Teach-Out Plans and Accreditation Loss Scenarios

"Teach-out plans are required when an accreditor acts to withdraw an institution's accreditation or when an institution notifies the accreditor that it intends to close or suspend operations."‚Åπ‚Åπ

The plan must include:

- **Name of at least one other institution** that could potentially enter into a teach-out agreement offering "educational programs that are reasonably similar in content, delivery modality and scheduling"

- **Financial aid advising** and academic advising resources
- **Clear statement to students** of tuition and fees at teach-out institution and number/types of credits that will be accepted¬π‚Å∞‚Å∞

**Refund Obligations If Teach-Out Fails**

"If a college is unable to adhere to the terms of the established teach-out plan, students enrolled when such determination is made will be allowed to request a full refund of all tuition and fees paid for program specific coursework."¬π‚Å∞¬π

**Teach-Out vs. Closed School Discharge**

"Borrowers will not be eligible to have their loans canceled through a closed school discharge if they complete the program through a teach-out."¬π‚Å∞¬≤ Therefore, successful teach-out arrangements protect the institution from closed school discharge liability.

### F. MATERIAL VENDOR AND SERVICE CONTRACTS

#### 1. Assignment Restrictions in Commercial Contracts

**UCC Default Rule: Free Assignability**

Under the Uniform Commercial Code, "without an anti-assignment provision, contracts are generally assignable even absent the consent of the counterparty."¬π‚Å∞‚Å∑ The UCC "prefers the free transferability of all types of property, including contracts."¬π‚Å∞‚Å∏

**UCC ¬ß 2-210: Limitations on Assignment**

However, UCC ¬ß 2-210 provides: "Unless otherwise agreed all rights of either seller or buyer can be assigned except where the assignment would materially change the duty of the other party, or increase materially the burden or risk imposed on him by his contract, or impair materially his chance of obtaining return performance."¬π‚Å∞‚Åπ

**Anti-Assignment Clauses**

"Courts normally enforce anti-assignment clauses that are negotiated and agreed upon by both parties, depending on the applicable law, the jurisdiction governing the contract, and the language agreed upon in the contract."¬π¬π‚Å∞

Two primary types of anti-assignment clauses exist:

1. **Absolute prohibition**: "This contract may not be assigned."
2. **Consent requirement**: "This contract may not be assigned without the prior written consent of the other party."¬π¬π¬π

**Consequences of Violating Anti-Assignment Clauses**

"Any attempted assignment or transfer without compliance with the clause, or a waiver of the clause by the non-assigning party, will constitute a breach of the contract, giving the non-assigning party the right to exercise remedies against the seller."¬π¬π¬≤ These remedies typically include:

- **Immediate termination** of the contract
- **Claw back of fees** and recovery of damages
- **Withholding of performance** until assignment issue resolved¬π¬π¬≥

**Delegation of Performance vs. Assignment of Rights**

Importantly, "no delegation of performance relieves the party delegating of any duty to perform or any liability for breach."¬π¬π‚Å¥ Even when assignment occurs, "depending on the contract, the party who assigned its obligations may remain a guarantor of‚Äî or responsible for‚Äîthe performance of the third party assigned the work."¬π¬π‚Åµ

Universities typically maintain exclusive food service contracts with major operators (Sodexo, Aramark, Chartwells). **Typical food service contract terms**:
- **Duration**: 10-15 years with renewal options
- **Annual value**: $15M-$25M in gross sales
- **Commission to university**: 5-15% of gross sales = $750K-$3.75M annually
- **Capital investment**: Vendor invests $5M-$15M in dining facility construction/renovation
- **Exclusive rights**: All on-campus dining, vending, catering¬π¬π‚Å∏

#### 2. Assignment Restrictions in Real Property Leases

**Standard Commercial Lease Assignment Provisions**

Commercial leases typically contain one of three assignment structures:

1. **Absolute prohibition**: "Tenant may not assign this Lease or sublet the Premises without Landlord's prior written consent, which Landlord may withhold in its sole discretion."

**Change of Control as Deemed Assignment**
