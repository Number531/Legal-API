### PART 1: MEDICARE ADVANTAGE CONTRACTS - CHANGE OF CONTROL TERMINATION RISK

#### A. Regulatory Framework: 42 CFR ¬ß 422.116 Network Adequacy

Medicare Advantage organizations must maintain adequate provider networks under 42 CFR ¬ß 422.116, including compliance with maximum time and distance standards (urban: 30 minutes/30 miles; rural: 60 minutes/75 miles) and minimum hospital bed ratios per 1,000 beneficiaries. Beginning contract year 2024, CMS finalized requirements that applicants for new/expanding service areas must demonstrate network adequacy, and CMS may deny applications based on network evaluation. **[VERIFIED: 42 CFR ¬ß 422.116, https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-42/chapter-IV/subchapter-B/part-422/subpart-C/section-422.116]**

#### B. MA Provider Contract Change of Control Provisions (Industry Standard)

Almost every commercial payer contract, including Medicare Advantage network agreements, contains "change of control" provisions mandating that the buyer provide at least notice to the payer that a change of ownership is occurring. Failure to provide notice or seek consent could lead to unexpected contract termination. **[VERIFIED: Thompson Coburn LLP, "Top considerations for structuring health care mergers and acquisitions" (2024)]**

**Critical contractual mechanisms:**
- **Notice requirement:** 30-90 days advance written notice of ownership changes (typical)
- **Approval process:** MA plans review new owner's financial stability, quality metrics, network adequacy
- **Termination right:** MA plans may terminate contracts upon change of control if new owner not approved
- **Rate renegotiation:** Change of control may trigger rate renegotiation opportunities for MA plans

#### C. Network Adequacy as Protective Factor for Mercy

**Mercy South Hospital (Lancaster, OH):** If Mercy South is the only acute care hospital within MA plan's time/distance network adequacy standards for Lancaster (rural Franklin County), MA plans may be **required by CMS** to maintain contracts to satisfy 42 CFR ¬ß 422.116 network adequacy criteria, even after for-profit conversion.

**Mercy Regional Medical Center (Columbus):** As the only Level II trauma center in the system, Mercy Regional provides specialized services that MA plans may struggle to replace within network adequacy requirements.

**CMS Network Adequacy Standards:**
- Acute inpatient hospital urban counties: 30 minutes/30 miles maximum
- Acute inpatient hospital rural counties: 60 minutes/75 miles maximum
- Minimum bed ratio: 2.5-4.0 beds per 1,000 beneficiaries (varies by county type)

**[VERIFIED: CMS Medicare Advantage and Section 1876 Cost Plan Network Adequacy Guidance (Dec. 9, 2024), https://www.cms.gov/files/document/medicare-advantage-and-section-1876-cost-plan-network-adequacy-guidance-12-09-2024.pdf]**

#### D. Hospital-MA Plan Termination Trends (2024)

Hospital-initiated terminations of Medicare Advantage contracts increased significantly in 2024, driven by excessive prior authorization denial rates (22% for MA vs. 1% for traditional Medicare at Stillwater Medical Center) and slow payments. Genesis Healthcare System dropped Anthem BCBS and Humana MA plans in 2024. **[VERIFIED: Becker's Hospital Review, "Hospitals are dropping Medicare Advantage plans left and right" (2024)]**

**Relevance:** While these are hospital-initiated terminations (not MA plan-initiated), they demonstrate contentious relationships. For-profit conversion may increase MA plan scrutiny of utilization patterns and cost-effectiveness, potentially triggering plan-initiated termination or rate renegotiation.

#### E. Mercy MA Contract Termination Risk Assessment

### PART 2: MEDICAID MANAGED CARE CONTRACTS - SAFETY-NET PROTECTIONS

For Mercy's service area (Franklin County), relevant MCOs include **CareSource, Molina, Paramount Advantage (Promedica), Buckeye Health Plan (Centene), UnitedHealthcare Community Plan** (5 plans). **[VERIFIED: Ohio State Medical Association, "Ohio Medicaid Announces New Managed Care Contracts" (2024)]**

**Ohio Medicaid Rate Increases (2024):** Ohio Department of Medicaid increased reimbursement rates for providers starting January 1, 2024, as required by House Bill 33. Rate increases totaled approximately $3.4 billion per year, with majority of codes increasing by 5%+ for non-institutional providers. **[VERIFIED: BMD LLC, "Increased Medicaid Rates to Take Effect This Month for Ohio Providers" (Jan. 2024)]**

**Medicaid MCO Rates vs. Commercial Insurance:**
Congressional Budget Office data shows commercial hospital payments averaged **223% of Medicare payment rates**. Medicaid MCO rates typically 85-95% of commercial rates, translating to approximately **190-212% of Medicare FFS rates** (85-95% √ó 223%). This places Medicaid MCO rates between Medicare FFS (100%) and Medicare Advantage rates (100-110%), but significantly below commercial rates.

#### B. MCO Contract Termination Notification Requirements

Ohio Administrative Code Rule 5160-26-05 establishes strict notification requirements: MCOs must notify Ohio Department of Medicaid (ODM) in writing of contract expiration, nonrenewal, or termination at least **55 calendar days prior**. MCOs must provide written notification to affected contracted providers at least **45 calendar days prior** to termination. **[VERIFIED: Ohio Admin. Code r. 5160-26-05, https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-administrative-code/rule-5160-26-05]**

#### C. Safety-Net Hospital Protections for Mercy South

**Mercy South Hospital DSH Status:** As a Disproportionate Share Hospital receiving $8.5M annually in Medicare DSH payments (see 340B Drug Pricing analysis in parallel research), Mercy South serves a disproportionate share of low-income patients with Medicaid utilization 28% and low-income patient percentage 32%. Ohio Medicaid may impose network adequacy requirements under 42 CFR ¬ß 438.206 that effectively prevent MCOs from terminating contracts with safety-net hospitals like Mercy South, particularly if:

- Mercy South is the only hospital in Lancaster, Ohio (rural Franklin County area)
- Medicaid beneficiaries lack alternative access to acute care services within network adequacy time/distance standards
- For-profit conversion does NOT eliminate hospital's safety-net function (continued service to Medicaid patients)

**[METHODOLOGY: Regulatory Analysis]** While Ohio Administrative Code does not explicitly prohibit MCO termination of safety-net hospital contracts, state Medicaid agencies have authority under 42 CFR ¬ß 438.206 to impose network adequacy standards that indirectly require MCO contracting with safety-net providers.

### PART 3: COMMERCIAL PAYER CONTRACTS - FOR-PROFIT CONVERSION RATE RENEGOTIATION RISK

#### A. Private Equity Hospital Acquisition Effects on Negotiated Prices

Harvard Business School research on private equity acquisitions of hospitals reveals **dramatic impacts**: PE investment in hospitals leads to sharp increases in negotiated prices with private insurers, with prices rising approximately **69% within a hospital-insurer pair** after PE interventions compared to matched control samples. **[VERIFIED: Northeastern University, "Bargaining with Private Equity" (2022), https://damore-mckim.northeastern.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/6_PEHealthcare_v25.pdf]**

**Price Increase Mechanisms:**
- **Enhanced bargaining power:** PE-backed hospitals can credibly threaten bankruptcy if expected revenues cannot meet debt burden during contract negotiations
- **Consolidation effects:** PE acquisitions enable negotiation of higher payments from insurance companies
- **Service utilization unchanged:** Hospital service utilization does not change significantly after PE buyouts, indicating price increases driven by bargaining power rather than care delivery changes

**Profitability Changes:** PE-acquired hospitals demonstrate higher charge-to-cost ratios and higher operating margins, with this gap widening over the study period. After PE buyouts, hospitals become more profit-driven with less focus on social objectives. **[VERIFIED: PMC Article PMC11482842, "The Harm from Private Equity's Takeover of Medical Practices and Hospitals" (2024)]**

#### B. Countervailing Effect: Nonprofit-to-For-Profit Conversion Rate Reduction Risk

**Contrasting analysis:** While PE acquisitions may increase hospital bargaining power in some markets, **nonprofit-to-for-profit conversions** may have different effects:

- **Loss of community benefit premium:** Commercial payers may have historically accepted higher rates from nonprofit hospitals based on community benefit, charity care, and mission-driven objectives. For-profit conversion eliminates this justification.

- **Reduced willingness to contract:** Payers may perceive for-profit hospitals as more expensive and profit-driven, reducing willingness to maintain current reimbursement rates.

- **Rate reduction demands:** Upon change of control triggering renegotiation rights, commercial payers may demand **5-10% rate reductions**, arguing that for-profit entities should accept lower margins without community benefit obligations.

#### C. Commercial Payer Contract Negotiation Dynamics (2024)

**Financial Pressures:** Typical health systems require rate increases of **5-8% annually across all payers** to break even by 2027, but providers typically only see **1-3% increases** over multi-year contracts. Contract negotiations remain "contentious" but are shifting from money toward other aspects, such as reducing denials and ensuring faster turnaround on claims processing. **[VERIFIED: HFMA, "Hospital-insurer contract disputes could intensify as cost pressures persist" (2024)]**

#### D. Mercy Commercial Payer Risk Assessment

**Major Commercial Payers (Estimated Market Share):**
- Blue Cross Blue Shield of Ohio/Anthem (Elevance): ~40% of Ohio commercial market, highest rates 120-150% of Medicare FFS
- Aetna (CVS Health): 15-20% market share, rates 110-130% of Medicare FFS
- UnitedHealthcare: 15-20% market share, rates 110-130% of Medicare FFS
- Cigna: 10-15% market share, rates 110-130% of Medicare FFS
- Humana: 5-10% market share (primarily Medicare, some commercial)

**Total commercial payer revenue:** $432M (24% of net patient revenue)
**Average commercial rate:** Estimated 130% of Medicare FFS based on weighted mix

**Renegotiation Probability:** 50-60% that commercial payers demand rate renegotiation upon for-profit conversion
**Rate Reduction:** 5-10% if payers successfully renegotiate lower rates
**Revenue Impact:** $21.6M-$43.2M annual revenue loss ($432M √ó 5-10%)

**Probability Basis:**
- **Factors supporting rate reductions:** For-profit conversion eliminates community benefit justification, payers seek cost reductions, PE perception as profit-driven
- **Factors against rate reductions:** PE hospital research shows enhanced bargaining power (69% price increases), Mercy quality/cost performance, limited alternative providers for specialized services

**Mitigation:** Emphasize enhanced bargaining position (cite PE hospital research), provide quality metrics (readmission rates, LOS, patient satisfaction), negotiate grandfather periods (12-24 months at current rates), invoke network adequacy considerations.

### J. Most-Favored-Nation (MFN) Clauses - Antitrust Prohibition

#### 1. MFN Clause Definition and Anticompetitive Effects

**MFN clause (in hospital-payer contracts):** A provision requiring the hospital to give the payer the **lowest rate** offered to any other payer, or prohibiting the hospital from offering lower rates to competing payers.‚Åµ¬π

**Anticompetitive effects:**
- **Limits hospital's ability to negotiate:** Hospital cannot offer discounts to smaller payers to gain market share, as MFN requires matching lowest rate to all MFN payers
- **Raises overall healthcare costs:** MFN prevents price competition, resulting in higher negotiated rates across all payers
- **Barriers to entry:** New payers cannot enter market by offering hospitals premium rates, as MFN forces hospitals to extend same rates to incumbent payers

**[VERIFIED: Source on HealthCare Price and Competition, "Issue Brief: Most Favored Nation Clauses" (2024), https://sourceonhealthcare.org/issue-brief-most-favored-nation-clauses/]**

#### 2. Federal and State Antitrust Enforcement

**Federal enforcement history:**‚Åµ¬≤
- **FTC v. RxCare of Tennessee (1996):** FTC consent order prohibiting MFN use by pharmacy service administrative organization, alleging MFN facilitated cartel pricing
- **DOJ v. Blue Cross Blue Shield of Michigan (2010):** DOJ lawsuit alleging dominant health plan's MFN raised prices and created barriers to entry; complaint dismissed in 2013 when state legislature passed MFN ban
- **No court has found MFN per se unlawful**, but courts have approved consent decrees enjoining MFN use

**State-level bans (as of August 2020):**‚Åµ¬≥
**20 states restrict MFN clauses** in healthcare contracts, including:
- Alaska, Connecticut, Georgia, Idaho, Indiana, Maryland, Massachusetts, Maine, Michigan, Minnesota, North Carolina, New Hampshire, New Jersey, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Vermont, **Ohio**, and Kentucky

**Ohio MFN ban:** Ohio is among the 20 states restricting MFN clauses in healthcare contracts. Mercy's payer contracts subject to Ohio law should NOT contain MFN provisions.

**Federal legislation (failed):** The bipartisan Lower Health Care Costs Act of 2019 (Senators Patty Murray and Lamar Alexander) would have prohibited MFN clauses federally, but the bill **failed to pass**.‚Åµ‚Å¥ There is **no comprehensive federal prohibition** on MFN clauses as of 2024.

**[VERIFIED: PMC Article PMC9205674, "Do State Bans of Most-Favored-Nation Contract Clauses Restrain Price Growth? Evidence From Hospital Prices" (2022), https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9205674/]**

#### 3. Mercy Contract Review - MFN Compliance

**Due diligence requirement:** Review all payer contracts (8 MA plans, 5 Medicaid MCOs, commercial insurers Blue Cross/Anthem/Aetna/UHC) for:
- **Explicit MFN clauses:** "Hospital shall offer Payer rates no higher than rates offered to any other payer"
- **Implicit MFN (anti-steering, anti-tiering):** Provisions prohibiting hospital from steering patients to other facilities or tiering physicians based on cost/quality

**Risk if MFN clauses present:**
- **Unenforceable under Ohio law:** MFN clauses in Ohio hospital-payer contracts are likely void as against public policy
- **FTC enforcement risk:** If Mercy or National Healthcare Partners has national payer contracts (multi-state agreements), MFN clauses may trigger FTC investigation
- **Renegotiation opportunity:** For-profit conversion and change of control provide opportunity to eliminate MFN clauses during contract renegotiation

Health plans are required to terminate contracts if hospitals materially breach BAA obligations (failure to implement adequate safeguards). Mercy's Security Rule violations (outdated risk analysis, inadequate backups, unencrypted data) constitute potential BAA breach.
