## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

National Healthcare Partners LLC's proposed $2.4B acquisition of Mercy Regional Health System presents **two critical tax-related deal issues** that will materially impact transaction economics, financing requirements, and post-closing operations:

1. **Tax-Exempt Status Conversion:** Mercy's 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status terminates upon for-profit acquisition, triggering $36.43M in new annual operating taxes (federal income tax, Ohio state/local taxes), reducing net income by 64% from $72M to $26M annually

2. **Tax-Exempt Bond Redemption:** Mercy's $420M tax-exempt bonds (Mercy Northwest Hospital, 2016-2046) must be redeemed at closing for $428.4M (102% make-whole premium), plus refinancing at taxable rates adds $9.45M annual interest expense

- Precedent: Ohio Supreme Court approved Richmond Heights Hospital conversion with strict conditions (proceeds exclusively for charitable purposes, continuing charity care commitments)
- **Probability of approval:** 90-95% if transaction demonstrates FMV consideration and continuing community benefit; 5-10% denial risk if AG finds inadequate asset dedication

#### 3. Tax-Exempt Bond Redemption - $428.4M Required at Closing

**Mercy Northwest Hospital Bonds:**

- **Maturity:** 2046 (30-year bonds, 22 years remaining)
- **Interest rate:** 4.25% tax-exempt (bondholders receive interest exempt from federal income tax)

**IRC ¬ß 141 Private Activity Bond Test:**
- Bonds are "private activity bonds" if >10% of proceeds used for private business use AND >10% of debt service secured by/derived from private business property
- **Private business use defined:** Use in trade or business of nongovernmental person (for-profit corporation)
- **Mercy Northwest conversion:** 100% of hospital becomes private business use when operated by National Healthcare Partners (for-profit entity)
- **Test failure:** 100% private business use >> 10% threshold ‚Üí bonds lose tax-exempt status immediately upon closing

**Bond Indenture Mandatory Redemption:**
- Typical hospital bond indentures require extraordinary redemption upon events jeopardizing tax-exempt status (change of ownership from nonprofit to for-profit, private business use >10%)
- **Redemption timing:** Within 6 months of disqualifying event (acquisition closing)
- **Failure to redeem:** Retroactive loss of tax-exempt status (bondholders owe back taxes on interest received), bondholder lawsuits, SEC/MSRB enforcement

**Redemption Price:**
- **Make-whole call provision:** Compensates bondholders for lost future tax-exempt income (typical premium 102-105% of principal)

#### 4. Tax Minimization Strategies - Reduce Burden by 45-55% (Year 1)

**Strategy 1: IRC ¬ß 338(h)(10) Election (Asset Acquisition Tax Treatment)**

Current transaction structure is stock purchase (National Healthcare Partners buys Mercy stock). Stock purchases provide **no step-up in asset basis** (Mercy continues depreciating assets at historical basis).

**IRC ¬ß 338(h)(10) election** allows parties to jointly elect to treat stock purchase as asset acquisition for tax purposes only (legal form remains stock purchase):
- **Buyer benefit:** Step-up asset basis to $2.4B (purchase price allocated to assets at FMV)
- **Seller benefit:** Mercy (as nonprofit with $0 tax basis) pays $0 tax on deemed asset sale
- **One level of tax** (vs. double taxation in typical corporate asset sales)

**Purchase Price Allocation (IRC ¬ß 1060 Residual Method):**
- Land: $200M (non-depreciable)
- Buildings: $1,500M (39-year MACRS, annual depreciation $38.5M vs. $25M-$30M historical basis)
- Equipment: $300M (5-7 year MACRS, eligible for bonus depreciation)
- Inventory: $100M (currently deductible COGS)
- Intangibles (contracts, patient relationships): $200M (15-year amortization)
- Goodwill: $100M (15-year amortization)

**Tax benefits:**
- Additional depreciation/amortization: $50M-$100M Years 1-5
- Cumulative tax savings: $50M-$100M √ó 21% = **$10.5M-$21M** (5-year total)

**Trade-offs:**
- **Complexity:** Asset acquisition requires transfer of Medicare provider agreements, state licenses, Joint Commission accreditation, payer contracts (consent/assignment)
- **Transaction costs:** Higher legal/accounting fees
- **Timing:** May delay closing 60-90 days

**Solution:** ¬ß 338(h)(10) election provides asset acquisition tax benefits while maintaining stock purchase legal simplicity (same regulatory approvals as stock deal)

**Strategy 4: REIT Lease-Back (Partial Implementation)**

1. National Healthcare Partners forms REIT subsidiary

- REITs distributing 90%+ income as dividends exempt from federal corporate income tax (IRC ¬ß 856)

**Operating company (Mercy) benefits:**
- **Rent deduction:** $7M-$10M annual rent (7-10% cap rate on $100M property) fully deductible under IRC ¬ß 162

### A. Mercy Regional Health System Tax-Exempt Status

**501(c)(3) Determination:**
- **Original IRS determination:** Issued 1952 (Mercy Regional Hospital recognized as charitable organization under IRC ¬ß 501(c)(3))¬π

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Loss of 501(c)(3) Tax-Exempt Status

#### 1. Automatic Revocation Upon For-Profit Conversion

**Legal Standard:**
IRC ¬ß 501(c)(3) requires that an organization be "organized and operated exclusively for" charitable purposes, and that "no part of the net earnings...inures to the benefit of any private shareholder or individual."‚Åπ When a for-profit entity acquires a non-profit hospital, the acquired entity no longer meets these requirements.

**For-Profit Ownership Triggers Loss of Exemption:**
When National Healthcare Partners (a for-profit PE-backed entity) acquires Mercy, the hospital system will no longer operate "exclusively" for charitable purposes. Instead, it will operate for the benefit of private shareholders (the PE fund investors), which constitutes private inurement prohibited under IRC ¬ß 501(c)(3).¬π‚Å∞

**Revocation Process:**
The IRS does not "automatically" revoke 501(c)(3) status upon ownership change, but the organization's tax-exempt status terminates by operation of law when it ceases to meet the requirements.¬π¬π National Healthcare Partners must:
- Notify the IRS of the change in tax status
- File final Form 990 as a tax-exempt organization
- Begin filing Form 1120 (U.S. Corporation Income Tax Return) as a taxable C corporation¬π¬≤

**Effective Date:**
The revocation is effective on the date of acquisition closing (the date ownership transfers to the for-profit entity).¬π¬≥

#### 2. Ohio Attorney General Approval Required

**Ohio Statutory Requirement:**
Ohio Revised Code ¬ß 109.34 requires nonprofit health care entities to obtain written approval from the Ohio Attorney General before entering into any transaction that transfers at least 20% of assets within a 24-month period.¬π‚Å¥

**Asset Dedication Doctrine:**
Under Ohio law, healthcare nonprofits hold their assets in trust for the benefit of the public. When converting to for-profit status, the nonprofit must ensure that charitable assets are appropriately valued and dedicated to continuing charitable purposes.¬π‚Åµ

**Richmond Heights Hospital Precedent:**
The Ohio Supreme Court approved a nonprofit hospital sale but imposed strict conditions requiring proceeds be used exclusively for charitable purposes and the hospital maintain commitment to providing charity care in the community.¬π‚Å∂ This precedent suggests the Ohio AG will scrutinize:
- Fair market valuation of Mercy's assets ($2.4B purchase price)
- Use of proceeds (if any remain with charitable entity)
- Continuing community benefit obligations post-conversion

#### 3. Step-Up in Asset Basis (Asset vs. Stock Acquisition)

**Stock Acquisition (No Step-Up):**

**Asset Acquisition Alternative (Step-Up Available):**
If the transaction were structured as an asset acquisition, National Healthcare Partners would receive a step-up in asset basis to fair market value ($2.4B purchase price allocated to assets), allowing:¬≤‚Å¥

- Buyer benefit: Step-up in asset basis to $2.4B
- Seller consideration: Mercy (as a non-profit) has $0 tax basis in its assets and pays no tax on the deemed asset sale

#### 4. Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax

**Estimated Annual Federal Income Tax: $15M**

### D. Tax-Exempt Bond Redemption Requirements

#### 1. IRC ¬ß 141 Private Activity Bond Test

**10% Private Business Use Test:**
IRC ¬ß 141(b)(1) provides that a bond is a "private activity bond" if both:
- More than 10% of the proceeds are used for any private business use, AND
- More than 10% of the debt service is secured by or derived from private business property‚Å¥¬≥

**Private Business Use Defined:**
Private business use means use in a trade or business of a nongovernmental person (including for-profit corporations).‚Å¥‚Å¥ When Mercy converts from 501(c)(3) non-profit to a for-profit subsidiary of National Healthcare Partners:
- **100% of Mercy Northwest Hospital** becomes private business use (entire facility operated by for-profit entity)
- The bonds immediately fail the 10% private business use test
- The bonds lose their tax-exempt status‚Å¥‚Åµ

**Modified Rules for 501(c)(3) Bonds:**
Qualified 501(c)(3) bonds receive special treatment under IRC ¬ß 145, which modifies the private activity bond tests. Under these modified rules, use by a 501(c)(3) organization in an activity related to its charitable purposes is treated as governmental use (not private business use).‚Å¥‚Å∂ However, this favorable treatment terminates when the organization ceases to qualify as a 501(c)(3) entity.

#### 2. Bond Indenture Call Provision

**Typical Change of Control Provisions:**
Tax-exempt hospital bond indentures typically require mandatory redemption upon events that jeopardize the tax-exempt status of the bonds, including:‚Å¥‚Å∑
- Change of ownership from non-profit to for-profit entity
- Failure to maintain 501(c)(3) status
- Private business use exceeding 10% of financed property
- Sale or lease of financed property to non-qualified persons

**Remedial Action Requirements:**
When a 501(c)(3) hospital engages in a deliberate action that causes private activity bond tests to be failed (such as converting to for-profit), the organization must cure that action using prescribed remedial actions, which generally consist of redemption or defeasance of bonds.‚Å¥‚Å∏

**Mandatory Redemption Timing:**
Bond documents typically require redemption within 6 months of the disqualifying event (the closing of the acquisition).‚Å¥‚Åπ Failure to redeem timely results in:
- Retroactive loss of tax-exempt status (bondholders owe back taxes on interest received)
- Bondholder lawsuits against issuer and borrower
- Potential SEC/MSRB enforcement action

#### 3. Redemption Price and Make-Whole Premium

**Extraordinary Redemption Provision:**
Tax-exempt bonds commonly include extraordinary optional redemption provisions permitting early par redemptions upon the occurrence of certain specified extraordinary events, such as a "tax call" when interest on the bonds becomes taxable.‚Åµ‚Å∞

**Make-Whole Call Premium:**
While par redemption (100% of principal) is common for tax calls, some bond indentures require a make-whole premium to compensate bondholders for lost future tax-exempt income streams. The make-whole premium typically ranges from **102% to 105%** of principal.‚Åµ¬π

### E. Tax Minimization Strategies

#### 1. Asset Acquisition vs. Stock Acquisition

**Current Transaction Structure:**
Stock purchase (National Healthcare Partners buys Mercy stock, Mercy continues as legal entity with for-profit owner).

**Tax Consequences:**
- **No step-up in asset basis:** Mercy continues to depreciate assets using historical tax basis
- **Limited tax benefits:** Cannot capitalize on accelerated depreciation or bonus depreciation for existing assets

**Asset Acquisition Alternative:**
National Healthcare Partners buys Mercy's assets (hospitals, real estate, equipment, inventory, contracts) rather than stock.

**Tax Benefits of Asset Acquisition:**
- **Step-up in asset basis:** Purchase price ($2.4B) allocated to acquired assets at fair market value‚Åµ‚Å¥
- **Allocation methodology:** IRC ¬ß 1060 residual method allocates purchase price to asset classes in following order:‚Åµ‚Åµ
  1. Cash and cash equivalents
  2. Marketable securities and certificates of deposit
  3. Accounts receivable and other current assets
  4. Inventory
  5. Buildings, land, and equipment
  6. Intangible assets (excluding goodwill)
  7. Goodwill and going concern value (residual)

**Estimated Allocation for Mercy:**
- Land: $200M (non-depreciable)
- Buildings: $1,500M (39-year MACRS)
- Equipment: $300M (5-7 year MACRS, eligible for bonus depreciation)
- Inventory: $100M (currently deductible as cost of goods sold)
- Intangibles (contracts, patient relationships, non-compete): $200M (15-year IRC ¬ß 197 amortization)
- Goodwill: $100M (15-year IRC ¬ß 197 amortization)

**Accelerated Depreciation Benefits:**
- **Buildings:** $1,500M / 39 years = $38.5M annual depreciation (vs. approximately $25M-$30M under historical basis)
- **Equipment:** $300M eligible for bonus depreciation (see Section IV.E.2 below)
- **Intangibles:** $300M ($200M intangibles + $100M goodwill) / 15 years = $20M annual amortization

**Estimated Tax Savings (Years 1-5):**
- Additional depreciation/amortization: $50M-$100M over 5 years
- Tax savings: $50M-$100M √ó 21% federal rate = **$10.5M-$21M cumulative tax savings**

**Trade-Offs:**
- **Complexity:** Asset acquisition requires transfer of:
  - Medicare provider agreements (CMS change of ownership approval for 4 hospitals)
  - State hospital licenses (Ohio Department of Health approval)
  - Joint Commission accreditation
  - Payer contracts (may require consent/assignment)
  - Vendor contracts
- **Transaction costs:** Legal, accounting, and filing fees higher for asset acquisitions
- **Timing:** Asset transfers may delay closing by 60-90 days

**IRC ¬ß 338(h)(10) Election (Hybrid Approach):**
Make joint election to treat stock purchase as asset acquisition for tax purposes only:‚Åµ‚Å∂
- **Legal form:** Stock purchase (simpler regulatory approvals)
- **Tax treatment:** Asset purchase (step-up in basis)
- **Seller consideration:** Mercy (as non-profit) has $0 tax basis, pays $0 tax on deemed asset sale
- **Buyer benefit:** Full step-up to $2.4B allocated basis

**Recommendation:** Pursue IRC ¬ß 338(h)(10) election to obtain asset acquisition tax benefits while maintaining stock purchase legal simplicity.

#### 2. Accelerated Depreciation (IRC ¬ß 168(k) Bonus Depreciation)

**One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) - January 2025:**
The OBBBA permanently restored **100% bonus depreciation** for qualified property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025.‚Åµ‚Å∑

**Qualifying Property:**
- Tangible assets with useful life of 20 years or less
- Includes equipment, machinery, and certain improvements
- Both new and used assets qualify (if not previously used by taxpayer and acquired in arm's-length transaction)‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Application to Mercy Equipment:**
If Mercy is acquired via asset acquisition (or ¬ß 338(h)(10) election) after January 19, 2025:
- **Medical equipment:** $300M allocated basis (MRI/CT/PET scanners, surgical equipment, lab equipment, IT systems)
- **Bonus depreciation:** 100% expensing in Year 1
- **Tax savings:** $300M √ó 21% federal rate = **$63M tax savings in Year 1**

**Phased Down Rates for Pre-OBBBA Property:**
Property acquired before January 20, 2025 follows the prior phase-down schedule:‚Åµ‚Åπ
- 2025: 40% bonus depreciation
- 2026: 20% bonus depreciation
- 2027+: 0% (expires)

**Strategic Timing:**
Close acquisition **after January 19, 2025** to qualify for permanent 100% bonus depreciation (expected closing Q2-Q3 2025 satisfies this requirement).

#### 3. Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) Lease-Back Structure

**Structure Overview:**
1. National Healthcare Partners forms REIT subsidiary
2. REIT purchases Mercy hospital real estate for $600M (buildings + land)
3. REIT leases property back to Mercy (operating company) for fair market value rent
4. Mercy operates hospitals as tenant; REIT owns real estate‚Å∂‚Å∞

**REIT Tax Benefits:**
- **Corporate tax exemption:** REITs that distribute 90%+ of income as dividends are exempt from federal corporate income tax on distributed income (IRC ¬ß 856)‚Å∂¬π
- **Pass-through structure:** Avoids double taxation at corporate and shareholder levels‚Å∂¬≤
- **Depreciation benefits:** REIT depreciates building basis over 39 years, reducing taxable income before distribution

**Operating Company (Mercy) Benefits:**
- **Rent deduction:** Rent payments to REIT are fully deductible operating expenses under IRC ¬ß 162‚Å∂¬≥
- **No interest deduction limitation:** Unlike interest expense (subject to IRC ¬ß 163(j) limitation), rent expense has no cap (other than reasonableness)
- **FMV rent:** $600M real estate √ó 7-10% capitalization rate = $42M-$60M annual rent (deductible)

**Property Tax Implications (Ohio):**
- **REIT ownership generally taxable:** Ohio does not provide blanket property tax exemptions for REIT-owned property‚Å∂‚Å¥
- **Potential assessment reduction:** Carefully structured sale-leaseback may avoid reassessment at higher FMV (maintain $600M assessed value vs. potential increase based on $2.4B enterprise value)
- **Triple-net lease:** Mercy (tenant) pays property taxes, insurance, and maintenance under lease terms

**Estimated Property Tax Savings:**
- If REIT ownership avoids reassessment: $0 savings (still pay $10.8M on $600M assessed value)
- If structured to challenge assessed value: Potential $3M-$5M savings through valuation appeals

**Concerns and Risks:**
- **Recent research:** Hospitals that sold buildings to REITs were more than 5√ó as likely to close or file for bankruptcy as hospitals that kept their real estate‚Å∂‚Åµ
- **Lease obligations:** Long-term triple-net leases (typically 10-15 years) with annual escalators create fixed obligations that reduce financial flexibility‚Å∂‚Å∂
- **Capital expenditures:** Tenant responsible for maintenance and improvements, but landlord (REIT) may restrict major renovations

**Recommendation:** Consider REIT lease-back for outpatient facilities ($100M of $600M total real estate) to test structure while retaining ownership of core hospital campuses ($500M). Provides rent deduction benefits ($7M-$10M annual rent on $100M property, fully deductible = $1.5M-$2M annual tax savings) while mitigating operational risks.

#### 2. Private Inurement Doctrine (IRC ¬ß 501(c)(3))

**Prohibition:**
IRC ¬ß 501(c)(3) requires that "no part of the net earnings...inures to the benefit of any private shareholder or individual."‚Å∑‚Åπ Private inurement occurs when a 501(c)(3) organization's income benefits insiders (physicians, executives, board members) beyond reasonable compensation.‚Å∏‚Å∞

**Kickback Payments as Private Inurement:**
Conduct that violates the False Claims Act, Stark Law, or Anti-Kickback Statute can also constitute private inurement in violation of Section 501(c)(3).‚Å∏¬π The IRS views kickback arrangements as providing excessive private benefit to physician-owners at the expense of the charitable organization's exempt purposes.

**IRS Position:**
The IRS has warned hospitals that joint ventures fostering kickback schemes may jeopardize tax-exempt status.‚Å∏¬≤ Physician-owned ASC arrangements where employed physicians receive ownership returns based on referral volume/value constitute private inurement because:
- Physician-owners receive distributions ($35,000 annually √ó 8 physicians = $280,000/year)
- Distributions are based on referrals (85% of ASC cases come from physician-owners)
- Mercy (the exempt organization) enables the arrangement by employing the physicians and allowing them to own competing ASC

#### 3. Retroactive Revocation Risk
