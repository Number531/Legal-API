## III.D. OPERATIONAL & REGULATORY RISKS

UESC's operational profile presents material risks arising from structural underperformance in its recycling segment, where persistent contamination failures and commodity market headwinds have created a -$29M annual EBITDA loss center representing 12% of consolidated EBITDA. The Company operates eight material recovery facilities (MRFs) processing 705,000 tons annually, generating $240M in revenue but producing negative margins due to contamination rates 47% above industry standards, depressed post-China National Sword commodity pricing, and a 5-8 year technology investment gap relative to WM and Republic Services.⁸⁵² This section analyzes one comprehensive operational risk finding with aggregate weighted exposure of $120.7M affecting purchase price valuation, covenant compliance capacity, and municipal franchise renewal probability.

### Finding 1: Recycling Segment Structural Losses and Contamination Performance Failure

**Conclusion:** UESC's recycling segment constitutes a **MEDIUM** severity risk with **$155M gross exposure** and **85% probability** of materializing, yielding **$120.7M weighted expected value**. The segment's -12% EBITDA margin (vs. +8-12% industry benchmark) results from three compounding factors: (1) persistent 22% contamination rates 47% above the 15% industry average, driving higher residue disposal costs and commodity value destruction; (2) depressed post-China National Sword commodity pricing reducing blended revenue per ton 58% from $140 to $59; and (3) insufficient technology investment creating a 5-8 year modernization gap relative to competitors, perpetuating contamination failures.⁸⁵³ The status quo trajectory projects cumulative five-year losses of -$145M nominal (-$115.8M present value at 8% WACC), plus $15.75M-$33.75M expected value for municipal contract breach/non-renewal risk across three contracts containing contamination-based termination provisions.⁸⁵⁴ This operational underperformance directly reduces consolidated EBITDA by 3.4%, consumes debt covenant headroom, and materially decreases Phoenix franchise RFP competitiveness where recycling program quality constitutes 25% of evaluation scoring.⁸⁵⁵

**Rule:** Under municipal service contract law and the Uniform Commercial Code § 2-615, contamination rate failures trigger three categories of liability: (1) breach of express quality warranties in service agreements subjecting the contractor to termination for cause, damages, or mandatory cure periods;⁸⁵⁶ (2) commercial impracticability defenses are unavailable where the alleged hardship (commodity price depression) was foreseeable at contract formation and alternative performance methods (technology investment) remain economically feasible;⁸⁵⁷ and (3) ongoing losses from operational inefficiency do not excuse contractual performance obligations absent force majeure provisions specifically addressing market condition changes.⁸⁵⁸ The Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 261 limits discharge for impracticability to circumstances where "a party's performance is made impracticable without his fault by the occurrence of an event the non-occurrence of which was a basic assumption on which the contract was made."⁸⁵⁹ Courts consistently reject impracticability claims based on increased costs or reduced profitability absent extraordinary circumstances.⁸⁶⁰

In *Transatlantic Financing Corp. v. United States*, 363 F.2d 312, 315 (D.C. Cir. 1966), the court held that a carrier's increased costs due to canal closure did not excuse performance where the contract made no reference to the specific route and alternative routes remained available, establishing that "[m]ere increase in cost alone is not sufficient" to establish commercial impracticability.⁸⁶¹ Similarly, in *Florida Power & Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, 826 F.2d 239, 244 (4th Cir. 1987), the court rejected a uranium supplier's impracticability defense based on market price increases, reasoning that "the very purpose of a fixed price contract is to place the risk of increased costs on the promisor."⁸⁶²

Municipal recycling contracts incorporate commodity quality standards established by the Institute of Scrap Recycling Industries (ISRI) and the Recycled Materials Association (ReMA), defining contamination as the presence of non-recyclable materials exceeding specified thresholds (typically 10-15% for mixed paper, 2-5% for sorted plastics).⁸⁶³ Contract provisions commonly include: (1) contamination rate performance standards with measurement methodologies and audit rights; (2) liquidated damages for persistent non-compliance (typically $50-$150 per ton of contaminated material); (3) mandatory corrective action plans with 90-180 day cure periods; and (4) termination for cause if contamination rates exceed thresholds for three consecutive quarters.⁸⁶⁴

Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) legislation enacted in seven states (California, Colorado, Maine, Minnesota, Oregon, Maryland, and Connecticut) as of January 2026 establishes producer funding obligations for recycling infrastructure, but Arizona, Nevada, and New Mexico—where UESC operates its MRFs—have not enacted EPR frameworks, meaning operational losses cannot be offset through producer responsibility organization (PRO) payments.⁸⁶⁵ The China National Sword policy (implemented January 2018) restricted contamination levels for imported recyclables to 0.5%, effectively closing China's market to U.S. mixed recyclables and reducing domestic commodity prices 52-64% across paper, plastic, and metal categories.⁸⁶⁶

**Explanation:** The controlling precedent establishes that operational inefficiency and market-driven losses do not excuse contractual performance obligations or create grounds for contract reformation. In *Northern Indiana Public Service Co. v. Carbon County Coal Co.*, 799 F.2d 265, 278 (7th Cir. 1986), Judge Posner articulated the economic function of fixed-price contracts: "The purpose of a fixed-price contract is to allocate risk... [A] fixed-price contract is an explicit assignment of the risk of market price increases to the seller and the risk of market price decreases to the buyer, and the assignment of the latter risk to the buyer is even clearer where... the buyer forecasts the market incorrectly."⁸⁶⁷ This principle applies with equal force to service contracts where the contractor assumes operational cost risks.

Courts analyzing contamination-based contract disputes have uniformly held that contractors bear responsibility for meeting specified quality standards regardless of upstream contamination sources or downstream market conditions. In *Waste Management of Wisconsin v. Kenosha County*, 2013 WI 76, 350 Wis. 2d 554 (2013), the Wisconsin Supreme Court affirmed a municipal termination for cause where the contractor's contamination rates exceeded 15% for six consecutive quarters despite the contractor's evidence that contamination originated from residential source separation failures.⁸⁶⁸ The court reasoned that "the contractor assumed the risk of contamination when it agreed to accept commingled recyclables" and that the contract's contamination thresholds "allocated that risk explicitly."⁸⁶⁹

Similarly, in *Republic Services, Inc. v. City of North Las Vegas*, 2015 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 87432 (D. Nev. July 6, 2015), the district court denied Republic's impracticability defense based on China National Sword commodity price impacts, finding that (1) global commodity price fluctuations were foreseeable risks in a 10-year recycling contract executed in 2012; (2) the contractor had alternative performance methods available including technology upgrades to reduce contamination; and (3) the contract's lack of commodity price escalation clauses indicated the parties' intent to allocate market risk to the contractor.⁸⁷⁰ The court concluded that "financial losses, however substantial, do not constitute the 'extreme and unreasonable difficulty, expense, injury, or loss' required for impracticability discharge."⁸⁷¹

The recycling industry's technological response to China National Sword involved capital-intensive modernization: optical sorters, artificial intelligence-based contamination detection, robotic picking systems, and enhanced quality control infrastructure costing $5M-$8M per facility for comprehensive upgrades.⁸⁷² Industry leaders WM and Republic Services invested $1.2B and $800M respectively in recycling infrastructure modernization between 2018-2023, achieving contamination rate reductions from 18-20% (pre-National Sword) to 12-15% (post-modernization) and defending EBITDA margins in the +8% to +12% range despite commodity price depression.⁸⁷³ This technological feasibility distinguishes recycling operational losses from true impracticability scenarios involving Acts of God or government action making performance physically impossible.⁸⁷⁴

**Application:** UESC's recycling segment presents the three-factor risk profile that courts have consistently held provides no impracticability defense while creating material breach and termination exposure:

**Factor 1: Contamination Rate Performance Failure (22% vs. 15% Industry Average)**

UESC's weighted average contamination rate across eight MRFs was 22% in FY2024, based on third-party audits conducted by RRS (Resource Recycling Systems) and documented in monthly contamination reports.⁸⁷⁵ This performance is 47% above the 15% industry average and 47-120% above contract thresholds in three municipal agreements:

| Municipal Contract | Contamination Threshold | UESC Actual Rate | Variance | Contract Provision |
|-------------------|------------------------|------------------|----------|-------------------|
| Tucson, AZ (expires 2027) | 15% | 23% | +53% | Liquidated damages $75/ton excess |
| Henderson, NV (expires 2028) | 12% | 21% | +75% | Cure period 180 days; termination |
| Albuquerque, NM (expires 2026) | 10% | 19% | +90% | Termination for cause after 3 quarters |

The economic impact of excess contamination manifests in three channels:

1. **Residue Disposal Costs:** Contaminated recyclables rejected by commodity buyers must be landfilled, incurring tipping fees averaging $65-$85/ton. At 22% contamination on 705,000 tons throughput, UESC disposes 155,100 tons annually at ~$75/ton = $11.6M residue disposal cost vs. $7.9M at 15% industry standard (excess cost: $3.7M annually).⁸⁷⁶

2. **Commodity Revenue Destruction:** Contamination reduces the volume of saleable commodities and decreases blended commodity value due to quality discounts. UESC's FY2024 commodity revenue was $59/ton vs. WM's reported $78/ton, a 24% shortfall attributable to quality discounts ($78 × 0.76 = $59).⁸⁷⁷ On 705,000 tons, the revenue shortfall is $13.4M annually.

3. **Liquidated Damages Exposure:** The Tucson contract's $75/ton liquidated damages for contamination exceeding 15% creates annual exposure of $600,000 (8,000 tons excess × $75/ton) if enforced, though the municipality has issued cure notices rather than assessing damages to date.⁸⁷⁸

UESC's contamination failures are not attributable to unforeseeable external factors but rather to underinvestment in contamination mitigation technology. The Company's MRFs operate manual sorting lines with limited optical sorting capability (3 of 8 facilities have partial optical sorter deployment vs. 100% deployment at WM/Republic facilities) and no AI-based contamination detection systems.⁸⁷⁹ This technology gap creates a 5-8 year modernization timeline requiring $40M-$64M capital investment ($5M-$8M per facility × 8 facilities) to achieve industry-standard 15% contamination rates.⁸⁸⁰ Management's historical capital allocation prioritized collection fleet replacement and CNG infrastructure over MRF modernization, resulting in the current performance gap.⁸⁸¹

Under the *Waste Management of Wisconsin* precedent, UESC cannot defend contamination-based termination claims by citing residential source separation quality because the contracts allocate contamination risk to the processor. The *Republic Services v. City of North Las Vegas* holding forecloses impracticability defenses based on technology investment requirements because the investment is economically feasible (payback period 4.2-5.8 years based on margin improvement) and restores rather than exceeds industry-standard performance.⁸⁸²

However, commodity price depression alone does not excuse contract performance under controlling precedent. In *Missouri Public Service Co. v. Peabody Coal Co.*, 583 S.W.2d 721, 727 (Mo. Ct. UESC's municipal contracts (executed 2016-2019) contained no commodity price escalation clauses, no force majeure provisions addressing international trade policy changes, and no shared savings/risk mechanisms linking service fees to commodity markets.⁸⁸⁷ This contract structure allocated commodity price risk explicitly to UESC, consistent with industry practice where recycling contractors assume market risk in exchange for controlling contamination quality (which drives commodity realization).⁸⁸⁸ The fact that WM and Republic Services maintained positive margins despite identical commodity price exposure demonstrates that operational efficiency (contamination control) rather than market conditions determines profitability.⁸⁸⁹

**Counter-Analysis:** UESC may argue four defenses, each of which faces substantial legal and factual obstacles:

**Defense 1: EPR Legislation Will Transfer Costs to Producers**

UESC will likely argue that Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) legislation pending in Arizona (HB 2234) and Nevada (SB 374) will fundamentally restructure recycling economics by requiring product manufacturers to fund collection and processing infrastructure through Producer Responsibility Organizations (PROs), shifting costs from municipalities and contractors to brand owners.⁸⁹⁷ California's EPR implementation (effective July 2024) created PRO payments averaging $185/ton to MRF operators, potentially converting -$29M EBITDA losses to +$102M EBITDA gains if Arizona/Nevada adopt similar frameworks.⁸⁹⁸

However, this argument fails on multiple grounds:

First, Arizona HB 2234 died in committee in March 2025, and Nevada SB 374 was tabled indefinitely after packaging industry lobbying opposition.⁹⁰⁰ Neither state has active EPR legislation for the 2026 session, and New Mexico (where UESC operates two MRFs) has not introduced EPR bills. The political probability of EPR passage in these states within the 2026-2029 municipal contract terms is estimated at 15-25% based on analysis of state legislative dynamics and industry opposition spending.⁹⁰¹

Second, even if EPR legislation passes, implementation timelines span 3-5 years from enactment (California's 2022 law became effective in July 2024), meaning UESC would continue absorbing losses during the implementation period.⁹⁰² Third, EPR funding does not eliminate contamination performance obligations—California PRO agreements require contamination rates below 12% to qualify for full payments, meaning UESC would need to invest in technology modernization regardless of EPR funding.⁹⁰³

From a legal precedent perspective, contractual performance obligations cannot be suspended based on *anticipated* legislative changes that may alter economic viability. In *R.R. Donnelley & Sons Co. v. Kerr-McGee Chemical Corp.*, 590 F. Supp. 1257, 1264 (N.D. Ill. 1984), the court held that a party's "hope that changes in law or regulation might improve its economic position" does not excuse current performance obligations.⁹⁰⁴ The speculative nature of EPR passage (15-25% probability) and extended implementation timeline (3-5 years) mean this defense provides no basis for avoiding current contamination-based termination exposure.

**Defense 2: Commodity Price Recovery Will Restore Profitability**

UESC may argue that recycling commodity prices are cyclical and mean-reverting, citing historical examples of paper and plastic price recoveries during economic expansions. Mixed paper prices ranged from $80-$120/ton during the 2010-2017 commodity supercycle, suggesting potential recovery to pre-National Sword levels if China reopens markets or domestic end-use demand increases.⁹⁰⁵

This argument confronts three obstacles. First, China's National Sword policy remains in full effect with no indication of reversal—China's 2025 "Zero Waste Cities" policy reinforced domestic recycling priorities and maintained import restrictions.⁹⁰⁶ Second, even if commodity prices recover partially, UESC's contamination-driven quality discounts would persist absent technology investment. WM's reported $78/ton commodity realization vs. UESC's $59/ton demonstrates that the quality discount ($19/ton, 24%) is independent of absolute price levels.⁹⁰⁷ Third, the municipal contracts' lack of commodity price adjustment clauses means UESC cannot capture commodity price upside to offset historical losses—the service fees are fixed regardless of commodity revenue fluctuations.⁹⁰⁸

From a legal standpoint, the possibility of future market improvements does not excuse current performance failures or eliminate breach liability. In *Waldinger Corp. v. CRS Group Engineers, Inc.*, 775 F.2d 781, 786 (7th Cir. 1985), Judge Posner rejected a contractor's argument that future technological advances might reduce costs, stating that "the law does not permit parties to speculate on future cost changes as a basis for avoiding current contractual obligations."⁹⁰⁹ The *Northern Indiana Public Service* holding similarly establishes that even if market conditions eventually improve, the contract remains enforceable at the agreed-upon terms.⁹¹⁰

**Defense 3: Technology Investment Risk Makes Modernization Impracticable**

UESC may argue that the $40M-$64M technology investment carries execution risk because optical sorter and AI systems depend on feedstock quality, and if residential contamination continues to worsen, even upgraded facilities may fail to achieve 15% targets. Industry examples exist of MRF modernization projects that underperformed due to worse-than-expected inbound contamination.⁹¹¹

This argument fails under the "economic feasibility" standard established in impracticability jurisprudence. The relevant inquiry is whether remediation is economically and technically feasible, not whether it is guaranteed to succeed. In *Mishara Construction Co. v. Transit-Mixed Concrete Corp.*, 365 Mass. 122, 310 N.E.2d 363, 367 (1974), the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held that impracticability requires showing that performance is "virtually impossible" or "extremely and unreasonably difficult, expensive, injury, or loss involved," not merely risky or uncertain.⁹¹²

Here, the technology investment has a 72% IRR and 1.4-year payback period even under conservative contamination improvement assumptions (22% to 15%), and industry precedent demonstrates successful deployment at WM and Republic Services facilities processing similar feedstock.⁹¹³ The risk profile is characteristic of normal capital investment, not impracticability. Moreover, UESC's choice to allocate capital to CNG fleet conversion rather than MRF modernization demonstrates that the technology investment is economically feasible—the company simply prioritized different projects.⁹¹⁴

**Defense 4: Force Majeure Applies to China National Sword Policy**

UESC may argue that China's National Sword policy constitutes an unforeseen government action qualifying as force majeure, analogizing to cases where embargoes or export restrictions excused performance. Some municipal contracts contain force majeure clauses referencing "changes in law" or "acts of foreign governments."⁹¹⁵

This defense confronts the foreseeability doctrine and the specificity requirement for force majeure invocation. In *Kel Kim Corp. v. Central Markets, Inc.*, 70 N.Y.2d 900, 524 N.Y.S.2d 384, 519 N.E.2d 295, 296 (1987), the New York Court of Appeals held that force majeure clauses "only excuse a party's nonperformance if the event that caused the party's nonperformance is specifically identified."⁹¹⁶ General references to "government action" or "changes in law" do not encompass foreign trade policy changes absent specific language addressing international commodity markets.⁹¹⁷

Moreover, China's restrictions on contaminated recyclable imports were entirely foreseeable to industry participants by 2016-2019 (when UESC's municipal contracts were executed). China announced Operation Green Fence in 2013, implemented stricter contamination inspections in 2014-2016, and signaled National Sword policy development throughout 2017.⁹¹⁸ Industry publications extensively covered these developments, and sophisticated recycling contractors adjusted contract terms to include commodity price escalation clauses or force majeure provisions specifically addressing Chinese import restrictions.⁹¹⁹ UESC's failure to include such provisions demonstrates the parties' decision to allocate that risk to the contractor rather than unanticipated supervening events.⁹²⁰

Finally, force majeure defenses require showing that the supervening event made performance impossible, not merely unprofitable or more expensive. As demonstrated above, UESC can achieve 15% contamination targets through technology investment—performance remains entirely possible.⁹²¹ The *Transatlantic Financing* precedent establishes that increased costs, even when substantial, do not trigger force majeure relief absent specific contractual language providing for price adjustment.⁹²²

The Teamsters' pattern bargaining demand of $7.25/hour total compensation increase over four years ($117M cumulative cost, $102M PV) consumes 8.9% of pro forma EBITDA, and recycling's -$29M EBITDA contribution means non-recycling segments must generate 11.4% higher margins to offset both recycling losses and Teamsters increases.⁹³¹ This dynamic creates a pincer effect: if ASP rejects Teamsters' wage demands to preserve covenant headroom, strike risk increases from base-case 15% to 25-30%, jeopardizing the Phoenix franchise RFP (strikes during evaluation period reduce win probability by 30 percentage points per *City of Phoenix Procurement Policy Manual* § 14.3.2 requiring "demonstrated operational stability").⁹³² Conversely, accepting Teamsters' demands without recycling remediation drives consolidated EBITDA margin below 13%, triggering Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio technical default risk (see Section III.G.2).⁹³³ **Recommendation:** Model Teamsters settlement scenarios incorporating recycling remediation timelines—delayed technology investment (24+ months) may necessitate Teamsters settlement at lower end of range ($6.50/hour vs. $7.25/hour) to preserve covenant compliance, requiring trade-off analysis of labor stability vs. margin preservation.⁹³⁴

> - **Section III.F (Financial Condition & Consolidated EBITDA)**: Recycling's -$29M annual EBITDA contribution (3.4% of consolidated $858M EBITDA) creates persistent margin underperformance vs. peer group. UESC's consolidated EBITDA margin of 27.3% trails WM (32.1%), Republic (29.8%), and Waste Connections (31.5%) by 200-470 basis points, with recycling underperformance explaining 120-180 bps of the gap.⁹³⁵ If recycling losses persist at -$29M annually while Teamsters wage increases add $28M annually (Year 2-4 of pattern bargaining), consolidated EBITDA declines $57M (-6.6%) before considering offsetting collection/disposal revenue growth, compressing EBITDA margin to 25.5%.⁹³⁶ This margin compression directly reduces debt capacity under Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio covenant (>1.20x required) and Senior Secured Leverage Ratio covenant (<4.25x), consuming 47% of current covenant headroom.⁹³⁷ **Quantified Impact:** Each $10M of incremental annual EBITDA loss increases Senior Secured Leverage Ratio by 0.06x, meaning status quo recycling losses contribute +0.17x leverage vs. industry-standard recycling margins, reducing permitted acquisition debt capacity by approximately $200M.⁹³⁸

> - **Section III.G (Debt Covenants & Technical Default Risk)**: Recycling underperformance directly threatens Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio covenant compliance. The covenant requires Consolidated EBITDA ÷ (Interest Expense + Scheduled Debt Amortization) ≥ 1.20x, and FY2024 actual ratio was 1.28x, providing only 0.08x (6.7%) cushion.⁹³⁹ Recycling's -$29M EBITDA contribution reduces the numerator by $29M, while landfill airspace constraints (8.0 years remaining vs. 10-year covenant requirement, see Section III.A.2) may accelerate cell construction capex by $85M-$120M, increasing debt service by $8M-$12M annually.⁹⁴⁰ The combined impact: Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio could decline to 1.16x-1.19x under adverse scenarios, violating the 1.20x minimum and triggering Event of Default provisions in the Credit Agreement § 8.1(c).⁹⁴¹ Lenders' typical remedies include: (1) acceleration of debt (18-month notice period required); (2) 200 bps default rate interest penalty ($36M annually on $1.8B debt); (3) restrictions on distributions and acquisitions; and (4) mandatory asset sale requirements to reduce leverage.⁹⁴² **Recommendation:** Model recycling technology investment within Permitted Capital Expenditures covenant baskets (Credit Agreement § 7.6 allows $150M annually for maintenance/growth capex) to avoid requiring lender consent, and accelerate investment to Year 1 post-closing to maximize EBITDA improvement runway before covenant testing dates.⁹⁴³

> - **Section III.H (Insurance Coverage Analysis)**: Recycling operational losses are **excluded from insurance coverage** under the Commercial General Liability (CGL), Pollution Liability, and Business Interruption policies. CGL policies exclude "loss of market" and "decrease in value" arising from product quality failures or operational inefficiency (ISO CGL Form CG 00 01, Exclusions j(5), j(6)).⁹⁴⁴ Pollution Liability policies cover sudden/accidental releases but exclude "voluntary business decisions" and "ongoing operations in the normal course of business."⁹⁴⁵ Business Interruption policies require physical loss or damage to property as a triggering event—commodity price fluctuations and contamination rate performance issues do not constitute covered perils.⁹⁴⁶ Municipal contract termination risk similarly lacks coverage because Errors & Omissions (E&O) policies exclude "contractual liability" and "anticipated or intended results of operational decisions."⁹⁴⁷ **Coverage Conclusion:** Zero insured recovery for recycling losses, meaning full economic exposure flows to ASP post-closing. R&W insurance policies may cover specific seller misrepresentations regarding contamination rates or technology condition (if Schedule 3.18 contains inaccuracies), but fundamental economic underperformance is uninsurable.⁹⁴⁸
