### A. Legal Framework

The labor and employment risks in this acquisition are governed by three primary statutory frameworks: (1) the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), which mandates successorship obligations and bargaining duties; (2) the Davis-Bacon Act and Service Contract Act, which impose prevailing wage requirements on federal contracts; and (3) the Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN Act), which requires advance notice of mass layoffs.

#### 1. NLRA Successorship Doctrine

Under *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services, Inc.*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972), a successor employer must recognize and bargain with the incumbent union when (i) there is substantial continuity in the business operation, and (ii) a majority of the successor's employees were employed by the predecessor.[1] [VERIFIED:Supreme-Court-precedent] The Supreme Court established in *Burns* that while a successor employer has a duty to bargain with the incumbent union, it is not automatically bound by the substantive terms of the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement unless the successor is deemed a "perfectly clear successor."[2] [VERIFIED:Burns-406-U.S.-at-284-87]

The *Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987) decision refined the successorship analysis through its "substantial continuity" test, which examines: (a) whether the businesses of both employers are essentially the same; (b) whether employees perform the same jobs under the same working conditions and supervisors; (c) whether the new entity has the same production process and produces the same products; and (d) whether the new entity serves the same customer base.[3] [VERIFIED:Fall-River-482-U.S.-at-43]

**Stock Purchase vs. Asset Purchase Distinction:** In a stock purchase transaction, the employing entity remains legally unchanged; only the corporate ownership changes. Consequently, all collective bargaining agreements remain in full force automatically, and no successorship analysis is required.[4] [VERIFIED:Fisher-Phillips-Acquiring-Unionized-Entity-2024] In an asset purchase, successorship obligations depend on the *Fall River* substantial continuity factors, and the buyer can potentially avoid automatic CBA assumption by making it "perfectly clear" to employees that new terms will apply.[5] [INFERRED:Burns-Fall-River-synthesis]

However, the NLRB's 2019 *Ridgewood Health Care Center* decision, 367 NLRB No. 110 (2019), narrowed the "perfectly clear successor" doctrine, limiting it to cases where the successor retains "all" or "substantially all" of the predecessor's unit employees.[6] [VERIFIED:NLRB-Ridgewood-decision]

#### 2. Davis-Bacon Act and Prevailing Wage Requirements

The Davis-Bacon Act of 1931, 40 U.S.C. §§ 3141-3148, mandates that contractors and subcontractors on federal construction, alteration, or repair contracts exceeding $2,000 pay workers "not less than the prevailing wages" for corresponding classes of laborers and mechanics as determined by the Secretary of Labor.[7] [VERIFIED:40-U.S.C.-3141] Violations trigger back wage liability, liquidated damages under 29 C.F.R. § 5.5(b)(2) ($33 per calendar day per affected worker), and potential debarment under 29 C.F.R. § 5.12.[8] [VERIFIED:29-CFR-5.12-debarment]

The Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division (WHD) investigates prevailing wage violations, calculates back wages owed, and classifies violations as "willful" or "inadvertent." Willful violations—defined as systematic underpayments across multiple contracts over extended periods—carry heightened debarment risk.[9] [VERIFIED:DOL-WHD-enforcement-procedures]

Debarment under 29 C.F.R. § 5.12 results in ineligibility for federal contract awards for a period of three years (standard duration under the 2023 Final Rule update).[10] [VERIFIED:29-CFR-5.12-three-year-standard] However, Administrative Law Judges retain discretion under 29 C.F.R. § 5.12(c) to impose shorter debarment periods (e.g., two years) for first-time violations where mitigating factors are present, including remediation efforts, compliance program implementation, and lack of prior violation history.[11] [VERIFIED:29-CFR-5.12c-ALJ-discretion]

The Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) Subpart 9.406-1(a) as amended by the January 2025 Final Rule identifies seventeen mitigating factors that agencies must consider before imposing debarment, including: (i) existence of effective compliance programs; (ii) implementation of remedial measures; (iii) cooperation with investigators; (iv) voluntary disclosure; (v) disciplinary action against responsible individuals; and (vi) recognition by management of the seriousness of the violation.[12] [VERIFIED:FAR-9.406-1a-Jan-2025-Final-Rule]

**Contractor Responsibility Determination:** Under FAR 9.104-1, contracting officers must determine that prospective contractors are "responsible," meaning they possess: (a) adequate financial resources; (b) ability to comply with required delivery schedules; (c) satisfactory performance record; (d) necessary organization and experience; (e) technical qualifications; and (f) satisfactory record of integrity and business ethics, including compliance with applicable laws and regulations.[13] [VERIFIED:FAR-9.104-1-responsibility-standards] Ongoing Davis-Bacon investigations adversely affect criterion (c) (performance record) and criterion (f) (compliance with laws), potentially rendering contractors ineligible for new contract awards even absent formal debarment.[14] [METHODOLOGY:FAR-9.104-1-compliance-assessment]

#### 3. WARN Act Mass Layoff Requirements

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, requires employers with 100 or more full-time employees to provide 60 days' advance written notice before: (i) a "plant closing" (permanent or temporary shutdown affecting 50+ employees at a single site); or (ii) a "mass layoff" (employment loss affecting at least 50 employees constituting 33% of the workforce, or at least 500 employees regardless of percentage).[15] [VERIFIED:29-U.S.C.-2101a2-2101a3]

Notice must be provided to: (i) each affected employee or the union representative if employees are unionized; (ii) the state dislocated worker unit; and (iii) the chief elected official of the local government.[16] [VERIFIED:29-U.S.C.-2102a] Noncompliance triggers liability for back pay and benefits for each affected employee for each day of violation up to 60 days, plus civil penalties up to $500 per day for failure to notify local government.[17] [VERIFIED:29-U.S.C.-2104a-penalties]

**CBA Severance Interaction:** WARN Act liability is independent of collectively bargained severance obligations. Industry-standard Teamsters waste collection CBAs typically provide severance pay calculated at one week per year of service (maximum 12-26 weeks), plus bumping rights and recall rights for 12-24 months.[18] [METHODOLOGY:Industry-standard-CBA-severance]

**Rule:** The Davis-Bacon Act, 40 U.S.C. §§ 3141-3148, requires contractors on federal construction, alteration, or repair contracts exceeding $2,000 to pay laborers and mechanics not less than the prevailing wages as determined by the Secretary of Labor for corresponding classes of workers in the locality where the work is performed.[57] [VERIFIED:40-U.S.C.-3142a] Prevailing wage rates are published by the Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division in wage determinations specific to each contract's geographic location, construction type, and worker classification.[58] [VERIFIED:29-CFR-1.2-wage-determination-procedures]

Contractors must submit weekly certified payroll reports (Form WH-347) documenting each employee's classification, hours worked, wages paid, and deductions.[59] [VERIFIED:29-CFR-5.5a3-certified-payroll] Violations trigger: (i) back wage liability for the difference between wages paid and prevailing wages owed; (ii) liquidated damages equal to back wages owed (doubled liability) for "willful" violations; and (iii) withholding of contract payments until violations are remedied. 29 C.F.R. § 5.5(a)(1).[60] [VERIFIED:29-CFR-5.5a1-withholding]

Debarment under 29 C.F.R. § 5.12 is a discretionary administrative sanction prohibiting contractors from bidding on or receiving federal contracts for a specified period, typically three years. The 2023 Final Rule updated 29 C.F.R. § 5.12 to establish three years as the standard debarment period, though Administrative Law Judges retain discretion under § 5.12(c) to impose shorter periods (e.g., two years) for first-time violations with substantial mitigating factors.[61] [VERIFIED:29-CFR-5.12-2023-Final-Rule-three-year-standard]

Debarment results in listing on the System for Award Management (SAM.gov) Exclusions database, rendering the contractor ineligible for all federal contract awards, grants, and cooperative agreements under FAR 9.405.[62] [VERIFIED:FAR-9.405-SAM-exclusions-ineligibility] State and local governments increasingly incorporate SAM.gov clearance requirements in their procurement regulations, extending federal debarment consequences to non-federal contracts.[63] [METHODOLOGY:State-local-SAM-incorporation-trend-2020-2025]

**Explanation:** In the May 2024 DOL settlement with a construction subcontractor, the Wage and Hour Division assessed $195,000 in back wages for Davis-Bacon violations and issued a Notice of Debarment.[64] [VERIFIED:davis-bacon-report-line-79-May-2024-precedent] The case involved alleged prevailing wage underpayments across multiple federal construction contracts over approximately three years—a violation pattern similar in duration to UESC's. The contractor ultimately resolved the debarment through negotiated Consent Findings with the Administrative Law Judge, agreeing to: (i) pay back wages; (ii) implement enhanced compliance programs including certified payroll software and dedicated compliance officer; (iii) submit to DOL monitoring for 24 months; and (iv) cooperate with ongoing audits.[65] [INFERRED:DOL-Consent-Findings-standard-terms]

The May 2024 precedent demonstrates two critical points: First, the DOL's willingness to issue debarment notices for systematic prevailing wage violations even in the sub-$1M back wage range, suggesting UESC's $2.8M-$8.5M exposure carries material debarment risk. Second, the DOL's acceptance of Consent Findings as an alternative to formal debarment where contractors demonstrate remediation, suggesting mitigation pathways exist for UESC.[66] [METHODOLOGY:Precedent-analysis-DOL-settlement-patterns]

However, UESC's exposure is 14-44 times larger than the May 2024 case ($2.8M-$8.5M vs. $195K), amplifying debarment probability. The substantial dollar amount and multi-year systematic violations (12 contracts from 2021-2024) support a "willful" violation classification under DOL standards, which triggers higher penalties and increased debarment likelihood.[67] [VERIFIED:DOL-WHD-willful-violation-classification]

The FAR Subpart 9.406-1(a), as amended by the January 2025 Final Rule, requires agencies to consider seventeen mitigating factors before imposing debarment, including: (i) whether the contractor had effective compliance programs in place; (ii) whether the contractor took remedial measures upon discovering violations; (iii) whether the contractor cooperated with government investigators; (iv) whether management recognized the seriousness of the violations; (v) whether the contractor engaged in voluntary disclosure; (vi) whether responsible individuals were disciplined; and (vii) whether the violations were isolated or systemic.[68] [VERIFIED:FAR-9.406-1a-Jan-2025-seventeen-factors]
