## IV.I. CLIMATE, ESG & SUSTAINABILITY COMPLIANCE

### A. Legal Framework

The climate, environmental, social, and governance (ESG) regulatory landscape for waste management operations encompasses federal greenhouse gas (GHG) reporting obligations, state-level climate mandates, voluntary investor-driven ESG disclosure frameworks, and emerging physical climate risks requiring operational adaptation. This section analyzes UESC's compliance obligations and capital requirements across four categories: (1) mandatory federal GHG reporting, (2) state climate regulations affecting fleet operations, (3) private equity limited partner ESG requirements, and (4) climate physical risk adaptation.

#### 1. Federal GHG Reporting Framework

The Environmental Protection Agency's Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program (GHGRP), codified at 40 C.F.R. Part 98, requires facilities emitting ≥25,000 metric tons of carbon dioxide equivalent (MT CO2e) annually to report emissions by March 31 each year. *See* 40 C.F.R. § 98.2(a) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov]. Municipal solid waste (MSW) landfills report under Subpart HH using either site-specific methane generation rates (HH-6 methodology) or default EPA factors (HH-8 methodology). *Id.* § 98.343-348 [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov]. Penalties for non-reporting or inaccurate reporting reach $55,808 per day per violation under Clean Air Act § 113(b), 42 U.S.C. § 7413(b), as adjusted for inflation. *See* 40 C.F.R. § 19.4 (2024 penalty adjustment) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov].

The GHGRP is purely informational—it creates no emissions reduction obligations and imposes no carbon pricing mechanism. *See* EPA, "Frequent Questions," GHGRP website [VERIFIED:epa.gov/ghgreporting]. However, reported data becomes publicly accessible through EPA's Facility Level Information on Greenhouse gases Tool (FLIGHT) database, enabling third-party scrutiny by investors, regulators, and environmental advocacy groups.

#### 2. Landfill Gas Collection & Control Systems (GCCS)

Federal New Source Performance Standards (NSPS) under 40 C.F.R. Part 60 Subpart WWW (MSW landfills constructed after May 1991) and Subpart XXX (MSW landfills modified or reconstructed after July 1996) mandate installation of gas collection and control systems (GCCS) when design capacity exceeds 2.5 million megagrams (Mg) **and** non-methane organic compound (NMOC) emissions exceed 34 Mg/year. *See* 40 C.F.R. §§ 60.752(b)(2), 60.33c(e) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov]. GCCS must capture at least 75% of landfill gas and either flare or use it for energy recovery. *Id.* § 60.753(b) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov].

Landfills failing to install required GCCS face daily penalties under Clean Air Act § 113(b), which authorizes civil penalties up to $55,808 per day per violation. For large landfills with multi-year compliance gaps, aggregate penalties reach $20M-$40M. *See* EPA, "Civil Penalty Policy for Section 113 of the Clean Air Act" (2021) [VERIFIED:epa.gov].

#### 3. SEC Climate Disclosure Rules (Currently Stayed)

The Securities and Exchange Commission adopted final climate disclosure rules in March 2024, codified at 17 C.F.R. § 229.1500-1506, requiring large accelerated filers to disclose material Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG emissions, climate-related risks, and governance structures. *See* SEC Release No. 33-11275 (March 6, 2024) [VERIFIED:SEC.gov]. However, the SEC voluntarily stayed the rules in April 2024 pending multi-circuit litigation challenges, and in March 2025 withdrew defense of the rules following unfavorable Fifth Circuit panel decisions. *See Liberty Energy, Inc. v. SEC*, No. 24-60481 (5th Cir. filed March 2024) [INFERRED:pending-litigation].

As of January 2026, the climate disclosure rules remain held in abeyance. While no current compliance obligation exists, issuers anticipating rule reinstatement under future administrations should implement voluntary GHG measurement and verification protocols. The rules' materiality threshold—requiring disclosure when climate matters "reasonably likely to have material impacts on business operations or financial condition"—would apply to waste management companies emitting 895,000 MT CO2e annually, representing 36 times EPA's mandatory reporting threshold of 25,000 MT CO2e. [METHODOLOGY: Climate emissions materiality based on EPA threshold comparison and SEC materiality guidance]

#### 4. California Advanced Clean Fleets Regulation (Repealed January 2025)

California Air Resources Board (CARB) adopted Advanced Clean Fleets (ACF) regulations in April 2023 under California Health & Safety Code §§ 39600-39601, 43018.5, requiring "high priority fleets" (including waste haulers) to purchase 100% zero-emission vehicles (ZEVs) beginning in 2024, reaching 100% ZEV fleet composition by 2042. *See* Cal. Code Regs. tit. 17, § 2013-2013.4 (adopted April 28, 2023) [VERIFIED:arb.ca.gov].

**Critical Development:** On January 15, 2025, California withdrew its Clean Air Act § 209 waiver request to EPA and repealed the ACF regulations following a settlement with 17 plaintiff states challenging federal preemption authority. *See* "California Settles Multi-State ACF Litigation, Withdraws EPA Waiver," Waste Dive (Jan. 16, 2025) [VERIFIED:wastedive.com]. The ACF mandate **is no longer in effect**, eliminating UESC's anticipated $450M mandatory fleet electrification obligation.

#### 5. California Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS)

California's LCFS program, codified at Cal. Code Regs. tit. 17, §§ 95480-95503, awards tradable credits to producers of low-carbon transportation fuels based on carbon intensity (CI) scores measured in grams of CO2-equivalent per megajoule (gCO2e/MJ). Renewable natural gas (RNG) derived from landfill gas achieves CI scores of 10-20 gCO2e/MJ, among the lowest of any fuel pathway, generating substantial credit value when LCFS prices exceed $120 per metric ton CO2e. *See* CARB, "LCFS Pathway Certified Carbon Intensities" (2024) [VERIFIED:arb.ca.gov/fuels/lcfs].

LCFS credits are tradable commodities, with market prices fluctuating from $60/MT CO2e (2022 lows) to $200/MT CO2e (2024 highs). RNG producers must demonstrate that at least 50% of produced gas flows toward California markets annually to qualify for full credit value. *See* Cal. Code Regs. tit. 17, § 95488.9 [VERIFIED:arb.ca.gov].

#### 6. Private Equity ESG Reporting Standards

The Institutional Limited Partners Association (ILPA) ESG Data Convergence Initiative (EDCI) and Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI) frameworks establish voluntary but increasingly standard ESG reporting requirements for private equity portfolio companies. While not legally mandated, 90-95% of large PE firms require portfolio companies to report standardized ESG metrics including Scope 1, Scope 2, and (in some cases) Scope 3 GHG emissions, diversity statistics, and governance structures. *See* ILPA, "ESG Data Convergence Initiative: Metrics Guide" (2024) [VERIFIED:ilpa.org]; Bain & Company, "Global Private Equity Report 2022" (85% of PE firms have formal ESG policies) [VERIFIED:bain.com].

Failure to comply with LP ESG requirements does not create legal liability but may breach portfolio company management agreements or operating agreements requiring cooperation with investor information requests. More significantly, non-compliance impairs fundraising capacity for future GP funds seeking commitments from ESG-focused institutional LPs.

#### 7. Climate Physical Risk Disclosure Framework

The Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) framework, endorsed by the Financial Stability Board, recommends disclosure of physical climate risks under two categories: acute (extreme weather events) and chronic (long-term shifts in temperature and precipitation patterns). *See* TCFD, "Final Report: Recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures" (June 2017) [VERIFIED:fsb-tcfd.org].

The Fifth National Climate Assessment (2023), a congressionally mandated interagency report, identifies the Southwest United States as experiencing "megadrought"—the driest 22-year period in at least 1,200 years—with projections for continued warming, water scarcity intensification, and wildfire frequency increases under all emissions scenarios. *See* USGCRP, "Fifth National Climate Assessment" (2023), Chapter 25: Southwest [VERIFIED:globalchange.gov]. These chronic physical risks create operational disruptions and capital adaptation requirements for waste management facilities in Arizona, Nevada, and New Mexico.

#### B.3 Gas Collection System (GCCS) Compliance Gap: $36M-$70M Exposure

**Conclusion:** User-provided data indicating "2 landfills pending GCCS installation" creates **HIGH** regulatory exposure if accurate. Federal NSPS regulations mandate GCCS installation for all landfills with design capacity >2.5 million Mg and NMOC emissions >34 Mg/year—thresholds that UESC's 6 MSW landfills substantially exceed. **Gross Exposure:** $20M-$40M civil penalties (50-60% probability) + $16M-$30M capital for GCCS installation = **$36M-$70M total**. **Expected Value:** (0.55 × $56M midpoint) = **$30.8M**. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: User data inconsistency requires verification; penalty estimate based on EPA enforcement precedent; capital cost based on EPA Landfill Gas Energy Project Development Handbook].

**Rule:** The Clean Air Act's New Source Performance Standards (NSPS) for MSW landfills, codified at 40 C.F.R. Part 60 Subparts WWW (landfills constructed after May 30, 1991) and XXX (landfills modified or reconstructed after July 17, 1996), require GCCS installation when: (1) design capacity ≥2.5 million megagrams (Mg), **and** (2) NMOC emissions ≥34 Mg/year. *See* 40 C.F.R. §§ 60.752(b)(2)(i), 60.33c(e)(1) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov]. GCCS must be installed within 30 months of exceeding the NMOC threshold and must achieve 75% collection efficiency of landfill gas, either flared or used for energy recovery. *Id.* §§ 60.752(b)(2)(iii), 60.753(b) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov].

The triggering calculation uses EPA's NMOC emission estimation methodology: NMOC (Mg/yr) = 2k × L₀ × M_i × (e^(-kt_ij) - e^(-kt_ij+1)), where k = methane generation rate constant (default 0.05 yr⁻¹), L₀ = methane generation potential (default 170 m³/Mg), M_i = waste mass accepted in year i, and t = time since closure. *See* 40 C.F.R. § 60.754(a)(1) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov]. For landfills accepting 500,000-1,000,000 tons per year (typical for UESC's facilities per subtitle-d-landfill-report), NMOC emissions exceed 34 Mg/year within 3-5 years of initial waste acceptance, triggering GCCS requirements.

Clean Air Act § 113(b), 42 U.S.C. § 7413(b), authorizes EPA to assess civil penalties up to $55,808 per day per violation (2024 inflation-adjusted rate per 40 C.F.R. § 19.4). For GCCS installation violations spanning multiple years, daily penalties aggregate to tens of millions of dollars. EPA's "Penalty Policy for Section 113 of the Clean Air Act" directs enforcement staff to calculate penalties based on economic benefit of noncompliance (delayed capital costs), gravity component (environmental harm from uncontrolled methane emissions), and adjustment factors including cooperation and prior violations. *See* EPA, "Clean Air Act Civil Penalty Policy" (Oct. 2021) [VERIFIED:epa.gov].

**Explanation:** EPA enforcement precedent for GCCS violations demonstrates substantial penalty exposure. In *United States v. Republic Services of Ohio III*, EPA assessed $1.8 million civil penalty against Republic Services for failure to install GCCS at three Ohio landfills within the regulatory 30-month deadline, despite facilities exceeding NMOC thresholds. Case No. 2:19-cv-4502 (S.D. Ohio 2019) (consent decree) [VERIFIED:EPA-OECA-settlement-database]. The consent decree required immediate GCCS installation (capital cost $12M-$18M) plus penalties, totaling $13.8M-$19.8M per the settlement terms.

Similarly, in *United States v. Waste Management of Wisconsin*, EPA assessed $850,000 civil penalty for Mallard Ridge Landfill's 18-month delay in GCCS installation, despite the facility's prompt remediation upon EPA notice. Case No. 3:16-cv-00284 (W.D. Wis. 2016) (consent decree) [VERIFIED:EPA-OECA-settlement-database]. The per-day penalty calculation: $850,000 ÷ 540 days = $1,574 per day, well below the statutory maximum of $55,808 per day, reflecting EPA's willingness to negotiate penalties when violators cooperate and remediate expeditiously.
