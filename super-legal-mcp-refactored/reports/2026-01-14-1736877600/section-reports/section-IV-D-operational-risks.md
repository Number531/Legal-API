### A. Legal Framework

The legal and regulatory framework governing material recovery facility (MRF) operations and recycling services integrates multiple domains of commercial, environmental, and contract law. Unlike traditional environmental compliance obligations that impose permitting requirements and regulatory standards, MRF operations face economic and contractual risks rooted in commodity market dynamics, service contract performance obligations, and material quality standards that have become de facto legal requirements through industry standardization.

**1. Municipal Service Contract Law**

Municipal recycling service agreements constitute executory contracts governed by principles of municipal contract law, public procurement regulations, and performance-based contracting frameworks. These agreements typically incorporate by reference industry specifications such as the Institute of Scrap Recycling Industries (ISRI) standards—rebranded in April 2024 as Recycled Materials Association (ReMA)—which establish internationally recognized commodity quality benchmarks.¹

The enforceability of contamination penalty provisions, processing fee adjustment clauses, and revenue-sharing mechanisms depends on clear contractual language and compliance with applicable state procurement law. Municipal contracts are subject to heightened scrutiny under state constitutional provisions requiring competitive bidding for public contracts exceeding statutory thresholds, typically $25,000-$100,000 depending on jurisdiction.² Arizona Rev. Stat. § 34-201 requires competitive sealed bidding for purchases exceeding $100,000,³ while Nevada Rev. Stat. § 332.065 sets thresholds ranging from $25,000 to $100,000 depending on local ordinance.⁴

**2. Commodity Quality Standards as Contract Terms**

ISRI/ReMA specifications, though technically voluntary industry guidelines, function as incorporated contractual standards when municipal contracts reference "merchantable quality," "industry-standard specifications," or specific ISRI grade designations.⁵ The Uniform Commercial Code (U.C.C.) § 2-314 implied warranty of merchantability applies to commodity sales, requiring goods to be "fit for the ordinary purposes for which such goods are used."⁶ Recyclable materials failing to meet ISRI contamination thresholds—such as Old Corrugated Containers (OCC) exceeding 5% total outthrows or PET plastic exceeding 6% contamination—constitute non-conforming goods subject to buyer rejection under U.C.C. § 2-601.⁷

**3. Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) Legislative Framework**

As of January 2026, seven states have enacted packaging EPR laws: Maine (LD 1541, 2021), Oregon (SB 582, 2022), Colorado (HB 22-1355, 2022), California (SB 54, 2022), Minnesota (2024), Maryland (2024), and Washington (2024).⁸ These statutes impose financial responsibility on product manufacturers to fund recycling infrastructure through producer responsibility organizations (PROs), fundamentally altering MRF economics by providing processing subsidies ranging from $50-$150 per ton.⁹

The absence of EPR legislation in Arizona, Nevada, and New Mexico—UESC's core operating states—means the company cannot access statutory processing subsidies available to competitors operating in EPR-enabled jurisdictions. This creates competitive disadvantage in contract recompete scenarios where competitors can subsidize bid pricing with EPR revenues unavailable to UESC.

**4. China National Sword Policy and International Trade Restrictions**

China's 2018 implementation of the National Sword policy—imposing 0.5% contamination limits on recyclable imports—effectively banned U.S. recycled material exports and triggered permanent structural shifts in commodity markets.¹⁰ Subsequent import restrictions by Southeast Asian nations (Malaysia, Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia) between 2019-2022 eliminated alternative export markets, forcing U.S. MRFs to develop domestic end-markets with significantly higher quality standards.¹¹

While National Sword is foreign policy rather than domestic law, its market consequences function as de facto regulatory requirements: MRFs must achieve contamination rates below 10% to access premium commodity pricing, compared to pre-2018 industry standards of 15-25% contamination.¹² This transformation creates contractual performance risk where MRFs unable to meet post-National Sword quality standards face buyer rejections, price discounts, and municipal contract termination for failure to achieve specified diversion rates.

**5. Contamination as Breach of Contract and Performance Standard**

Municipal recycling contracts increasingly incorporate contamination thresholds as material contract terms, with breach triggering financial penalties, cure periods, or termination rights.¹³ Contamination exceeding 15-20% constitutes substantial impairment of contract value under U.C.C. § 2-612 (installment contracts), permitting municipalities to cancel future performance obligations.¹⁴ Courts analyzing breach of service contracts apply the substantial performance doctrine, requiring contractors to achieve material contract objectives despite minor deficiencies.¹⁵ However, contamination rates exceeding industry benchmarks by 50% or more (e.g., 22% actual vs. 15% industry standard) likely fail the substantial performance test, exposing MRF operators to contract termination and damages claims.

**Rule:** Under U.C.C. § 2-615, failure to perform contractual obligations due to commercially impracticable events may excuse performance if the event was unforeseeable and not caused by the non-performing party's fault.¹⁶ However, market price fluctuations and industry-wide economic conditions generally do not constitute commercial impracticability unless they result in performance costs that are "excessive and unreasonable" beyond all reasonable expectation.¹⁷ The Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 261 provides that performance is excused where "a party's performance is made impracticable without his fault by the occurrence of an event the non-occurrence of which was a basic assumption on which the contract was made."¹⁸ Courts have consistently held that foreseeable market price changes—even significant ones—do not excuse performance obligations.¹⁹

In municipal service contracts, deteriorating financial performance by the contractor does not excuse continued service obligations or permit unilateral price adjustments absent explicit contractual provisions.²⁰ Contract renewal decisions are evaluated under competitive bidding standards, with municipalities required to select the "lowest responsible bidder" that meets service quality specifications.²¹ Contractors unable to offer competitive pricing due to operational inefficiencies face lawful contract non-renewal without legal recourse for lost business expectations.

**Explanation:** Courts have repeatedly rejected commercial impracticability defenses based on market price changes or increased operational costs. In *Transatlantic Financing Corp. v. United States*, the D.C. Circuit held that a shipping company could not invoke impracticability when the Suez Canal closure increased voyage costs by 30%, reasoning that "foreseeability of the risk is not a prerequisite to commercial impracticability" but market risks affecting entire industries are presumed to be allocated to the performing party.²² Similarly, in *Florida Power & Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, the Fourth Circuit rejected a uranium supplier's impracticability defense when global uranium prices increased 1000%, finding that market price volatility was a foreseeable risk in commodities contracts.²³

In the recycling context, post-National Sword commodity price declines have been universally rejected as grounds for excusing MRF contract obligations. Industry reports document that 43% of MRF operators added processing fees to contracts between 2022-2024, but these fee adjustments required contractual amendment or renegotiation—not unilateral imposition.²⁴ Municipalities facing contractor requests for fee increases due to National Sword impacts have successfully insisted on maintaining existing contract terms, forcing MRF operators to either continue performance at a loss or face termination for breach.²⁵

**Application:** UESC's recycling segment losses stem from three compounding factors, each of which is commercially foreseeable and within UESC's control to mitigate:

China's 2018 implementation of 0.5% contamination standards on imported recyclables triggered permanent structural commodity price decline: mixed paper declined 77% (from $65/ton in 2017 to $15/ton in 2018-2019, currently $42/ton in January 2025); OCC cardboard remains 39% below pre-National Sword levels ($73/ton January 2026 vs. $120/ton in 2017); PET plastic hit record lows of 4.19 cents/pound in October 2024 before recovering to 16.44 cents/pound.²⁶ UESC's commodity revenue declined -$16.2M annually (from $28M in 2019 to $11.8M in 2024), a -58% collapse.²⁷

Market analysts project commodity prices will **not recover to pre-National Sword levels within the 5-year acquisition horizon (2026-2031)**: OCC stabilization forecast at $70-$90/ton (vs. $120/ton in 2017); mixed paper at $35-$50/ton (vs. $65/ton); full price recovery probability by 2030 is 30-40% for OCC and 10-15% for mixed paper.²⁸ [METHODOLOGY: NERC Market Watch consensus forecasts; RISI commodity tracking; Waste Dive market analysis January 2025]
