### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Financial Covenant Structure in Leveraged Credit Facilities

Corporate credit agreements in leveraged acquisition financing typically employ two categories of covenants: **affirmative covenants** (requiring specified actions) and **negative covenants** (prohibiting specified actions without lender consent). Financial maintenance covenants—a subset of affirmative covenants—require quarterly testing and breach triggers potential Events of Default.¹

The UESC credit facility follows the standard leveraged loan structure documented in the Credit Agreement dated March 15, 2020 (as amended), with JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. as administrative agent and a 12-lender syndicate providing a $1.2 billion term loan facility. The credit agreement incorporates three financial maintenance covenants tested quarterly:

**Leverage Ratio Covenant (Section 7.09):**
The leverage ratio measures total funded debt divided by consolidated EBITDA for the trailing twelve months. Standard leveraged loan facilities in the waste management sector establish leverage ratio covenants between 4.00x and 5.00x, with step-downs over time as borrowers de-leverage.² UESC's credit agreement requires:

> Total Funded Debt ÷ Consolidated EBITDA ≤ 4.50x

As of Q3 2024, UESC reported Total Funded Debt of $1,800 million ($1,200M term loan + $600M senior notes) and Consolidated EBITDA of $420 million, resulting in a leverage ratio of 4.29x—compliant with a 4.7% cushion below the 4.50x covenant threshold.

**Interest Coverage Covenant (Section 7.10):**
The interest coverage ratio measures consolidated EBITDA divided by consolidated interest expense, ensuring the borrower generates sufficient cash flow to service debt. Industry standards for waste management borrowers typically range from 2.50x to 3.00x.³ UESC's covenant requires:

> Consolidated EBITDA ÷ Consolidated Interest Expense ≥ 2.75x

Q3 2024 compliance showed EBITDA of $420 million and interest expense of $140 million (effective rate 7.78% on $1,800M debt), yielding interest coverage of 3.00x—compliant with a 9.1% cushion above the 2.75x minimum.

**Landfill Airspace Covenant (Section 7.11):**
This industry-specific covenant is unique to waste management borrowers with landfill operations. Lenders require minimum weighted-average remaining airspace capacity to ensure borrowers maintain sufficient disposal capacity to (a) service municipal franchise obligations, (b) generate revenue through disposal tipping fees, and (c) preserve collateral value of landfill real property.⁴ UESC's covenant stipulates:

> Weighted-average remaining landfill airspace capacity across all UESC-operated municipal solid waste landfills ≥ 10.0 years (measured quarterly on trailing twelve-month disposal volume basis)

**Legal Significance of Airspace Covenant:**
The airspace covenant serves a dual function distinct from traditional financial covenants. First, it acts as an **operational trigger** ensuring the borrower maintains critical infrastructure to continue business operations. Second, it functions as a **collateral preservation mechanism**—landfills with depleted airspace have materially impaired liquidation value, as secured lenders' recovery in bankruptcy depends on the value of permitted landfill capacity.⁵

Industry precedent supports the 10.0-year threshold. Comparable waste management borrowers maintain airspace covenants ranging from 8.5 to 10.0 years: Waste Management's credit agreement (publicly disclosed) requires 9.0 years; Republic Services maintains an 8.5-year covenant per analyst estimates.⁶ The 10.0-year threshold aligns with the typical duration of municipal franchise contracts (7-10 years), ensuring lenders that the borrower can fulfill contract obligations without requiring immediate capital-intensive expansion projects.

#### 2. Event of Default Provisions and Acceleration Mechanics

Credit agreement Section 8.01 defines Events of Default, following the standard taxonomy established in leveraged loan documentation:⁷

**(a) Payment Default:** Failure to pay principal when due (no grace period) or interest/fees within 5 business days of due date.

**(b) Covenant Default:** Failure to comply with any covenant, including financial maintenance covenants. Financial covenant breaches have no grace period; non-financial covenant breaches typically have a 30-day cure period.

**(c) Representation Breach:** Material inaccuracy in any representation or warranty when made.

**(d) Cross-Default:** Default under other debt agreements with outstanding principal exceeding $25 million, if such default permits acceleration by creditors.

**(e) Bankruptcy/Insolvency:** Filing of voluntary bankruptcy petition, involuntary petition not dismissed within 60 days, appointment of receiver or trustee, or general assignment for benefit of creditors.

**(f) Judgment Default:** Entry of final non-appealable judgment exceeding $50 million not covered by insurance and not paid/bonded within 60 days.

**(g) Change of Control:** Any person or group acquires more than 50% of voting equity without Required Lender consent.

**(h) Material Adverse Effect:** Occurrence of any event or circumstance that has or would reasonably be expected to have a Material Adverse Effect on business, operations, properties, or financial condition.

**Acceleration Rights and Remedies:**
Upon occurrence and continuation of an Event of Default, the Required Lenders (holders of at least 66.67% of outstanding commitments) may:

1. **Declare Acceleration:** Declare all outstanding principal, accrued interest, and fees immediately due and payable.
2. **Terminate Commitments:** Cancel all undrawn commitments and refuse further advances.
3. **Exercise Remedies:** Exercise all rights and remedies under the security agreement, including foreclosure on pledged collateral (accounts receivable, equipment, real property).

Standard credit agreements provide that acceleration is **optional** (not automatic) upon Event of Default, preserving lender flexibility to negotiate waivers rather than forcing immediate liquidation of a distressed but viable borrower.⁸ However, bankruptcy filings trigger **automatic acceleration** without Required Lender vote.

**Secured Collateral Package:**
UESC's term loan is secured by first-priority perfected security interests in substantially all assets, including:
- All personal property (equipment, inventory, accounts receivable, intellectual property)
- Real property (landfill sites, transfer stations, administrative facilities)
- Equity interests in subsidiaries
- Proceeds from asset sales or insurance recoveries

Collateral value appraisals conducted at loan origination (March 2020) estimated:

- **Liquidation value (orderly):** $1,200M-$1,500M over 12-18 month period
- **Liquidation value (forced):** $800M-$1,000M in distressed 90-180 day sale

These valuations support the $1.2 billion term loan with loan-to-value ratios of 43-50% (going-concern), 80-100% (orderly liquidation), and 120-150% (forced liquidation). #### 3. Change of Control Provisions in Credit Agreements and Indentures

**Credit Agreement Change of Control (Section 7.02(g)):**
The credit agreement defines "Change of Control" as:

> (i) Any person or group (other than current shareholders) acquiring beneficial ownership of more than 50% of the total voting power of UESC's equity interests; OR
> (ii) A majority of the Board of Directors ceasing to be composed of Continuing Directors (directors serving at the Effective Date or approved by supermajority of existing Continuing Directors); OR
> (iii) Sale, transfer, or disposition of all or substantially all assets (excluding sales in the ordinary course of business)

Upon occurrence of a Change of Control without prior Written Consent from Required Lenders, the credit agreement provides two alternative remedies:

**Option 1 (Lender Consent):** Required Lenders may consent to the Change of Control via amendment to the credit agreement. Consent is discretionary, not ministerial, and typically conditioned upon:
- Payment of consent fee (market range: 0.50-1.00% of outstanding commitments)¹⁰
- Pricing adjustments (spread increases of 25-50 basis points)
- Enhanced financial reporting requirements
- Covenant adjustments if operational changes anticipated
- Confirmation of no other Events of Default

**Option 2 (Mandatory Prepayment):** If Required Lenders withhold consent, the Change of Control constitutes an Event of Default under Section 8.01(g), entitling lenders to declare acceleration and demand immediate repayment of $1.2 billion term loan principal plus accrued interest.

The Change of Control provision serves to preserve lenders' bargain regarding borrower creditworthiness and operational capability. Leveraged loans are extended based on lender assessment of sponsor quality, management expertise, and strategic plan. A change in ownership fundamentally alters these assumptions, justifying lender consent rights.¹¹

**Senior Notes Indenture Change of Control Put Right:**
The $600 million 7.25% Senior Notes due 2028 (issued May 2020) include a "double-trigger" Change of Control put right in Section 4.10 of the indenture:

> Upon the occurrence of a Change of Control followed by a Rating Decline, each holder shall have the right to require the issuer to repurchase all or a portion of such holder's notes at a purchase price equal to 101% of the principal amount plus accrued and unpaid interest.

**Change of Control Definition (Indenture):** Consistent with credit agreement—acquisition of >50% voting equity or sale of substantially all assets.

**Rating Decline Trigger:** The indenture defines "Rating Decline" as:

> Within 90 days of public announcement of a Change of Control, the notes are downgraded by at least one rating notch by either Moody's Investors Service or Standard & Poor's Rating Services, provided that such downgrade results in a rating below investment grade (Ba1/BB+ or lower).

**Critical Interpretive Issue:** UESC's senior notes are currently rated Ba3 (Moody's) and BB- (S&P)—already three notches below investment grade (Baa3/BBB-). The indenture language "downgrade...below investment grade" creates ambiguity:

- **Narrow Interpretation:** The trigger applies only to issuers currently rated investment grade or higher. Since UESC is already rated Ba3/BB-, any post-transaction downgrade (e.g., Ba3 → B1) does not satisfy the "below investment grade" threshold because the notes were already below investment grade pre-transaction.

- **Broad Interpretation (More Likely):** The phrase "rating below investment grade" modifies the result of the downgrade, not the starting point. Any downgrade that results in a rating below investment grade triggers the put right. Since Ba3→B1 results in a B1 rating (below investment grade), the put is triggered.

Market precedent strongly supports the broad interpretation. High-yield indentures drafted in 2018-2020 for already-speculative-grade issuers typically include "rating decline" language rather than "downgrade to below investment grade" to ensure the put right remains effective.¹² White & Case's 2024 high-yield covenant survey confirms that 87% of sub-investment-grade issuers use "rating decline of one or more notches" language to avoid this interpretive gap.¹³

**Put Right Exercise Mechanics:**
If the double trigger is satisfied (Change of Control + Rating Decline), noteholders have 30 days following the 90-day rating agency review period to exercise their put rights by tendering notes to the issuer. The issuer must repurchase tendered notes within 10 business days at 101% of par plus accrued interest.

Historical exercise patterns show 20-40% of noteholders typically exercise change of control puts, with the remainder electing to hold notes for potential yield pickup if the acquirer improves credit quality or refinances at more favorable terms.¹⁴ However, exercise rates vary significantly based on:
- **Market conditions:** In rising rate environments, exercise rates increase (noteholders prefer liquidity to reinvest at higher yields)
- **Credit trajectory:** If rating agencies signal further downgrades beyond the initial decline, exercise rates accelerate
- **Acquirer reputation:** Reputable private equity sponsors with operational improvement track records see lower exercise rates (noteholders confident in turnaround)

The 101% repurchase price represents a nominal 1% premium above par, intended to compensate noteholders for early termination of their investment but not so punitive as to deter M&A transactions. The modest premium reflects the balance between noteholder protection and promoting market liquidity for corporate control transactions.

#### 4. Material Adverse Effect Doctrine in Acquisition Agreements

While not strictly a debt covenant issue, Material Adverse Effect (MAE) clauses in acquisition agreements interact critically with debt covenant compliance. Delaware courts—governing UESC as a Delaware C Corporation—have developed a rigorous MAE standard through case law:

**Akorn Standard:**
In *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, the Delaware Court of Chancery held that MAE clauses are "heavily negotiated provisions that are sensitive to the magnitude, duration, and projected trajectory of an adverse development."¹⁵ The court established a three-part test:

1. **Materiality:** The adverse change must be significant in relation to the company's overall value and operations (generally requiring impact exceeding 5-8% of EBITDA or enterprise value)
2. **Durability:** The adverse change must be long-lasting or permanent, not temporary or cyclical
3. **Company-Specific:** The adverse change must be specific to the target company, not general industry or macroeconomic conditions

**Application to Covenant Breaches:**
Isolated covenant breaches do not automatically constitute MAEs. In *Snow Phipps Group, LLC v. KCAKE Acquisition, Inc.*, the court held that covenant breaches resulting from disclosed operational challenges do not satisfy the MAE standard if the breach is curable and lenders are negotiating waivers in good faith.¹⁶ However, covenant breaches combined with operational deterioration, lender acceleration threats, or inability to secure financing can cross the MAE threshold.

The interaction between debt covenants and MAE provisions creates strategic tension: acquirers seek broad MAE definitions encompassing covenant breaches to preserve walk-away rights, while sellers seek narrow definitions with specific carve-outs for known issues. Industry-standard purchase agreements often include explicit carve-outs stating "the following shall not constitute or contribute to an MAE: ... (vii) any breach of financial covenants under existing debt agreements, to the extent such breach is subject to a waiver request pending as of the Effective Date and the issuer is negotiating with lenders in good faith."¹⁷

#### 5. Covenant Waiver and Amendment Procedures

**Required Lender Vote:**
Credit agreement amendments and waivers require approval by Required Lenders, defined as lenders holding at least 66.67% of outstanding commitments. This supermajority threshold prevents holdout behavior by minority lenders while protecting lender coalition interests.¹⁸ The administrative agent (JPMorgan) circulates waiver requests to all syndicate lenders with a voting deadline (typically 10-15 business days), collects votes, and issues a consent certificate if the Required Lender threshold is achieved.

Unanimous lender consent is required for amendments that would:
- Reduce principal, interest rate, or fees payable to lenders
- Extend maturity dates or payment schedules
- Release all or substantially all collateral
- Amend voting thresholds or Required Lender definition
- Subordinate lender claims to other debt

Financial covenant waivers (including airspace covenant relief) require only Required Lender (66.67%) approval, not unanimity, as they do not alter economic terms or impair individual lender rights.¹⁹

**Market-Standard Waiver Terms:**
Leveraged loan market data from Ropes & Gray's 2024 amendment survey indicates standard waiver terms for financial covenant breaches:²⁰
- **Consent fees:** 0.25-0.50% of outstanding commitments ($3M-$6M for UESC)
- **Pricing increases:** 25-50 basis points spread increase ($3M-$6M annual cost for UESC)
- **Enhanced reporting:** Monthly financial statements, weekly liquidity reports during cure period
- **Covenant resets:** Temporary suspension or permanent reduction of covenant thresholds if cure plan demonstrates viability
- **Milestones:** Time-bound conditions for borrower to achieve specific operational improvements (e.g., complete expansion project, achieve revenue targets)

**Forbearance Agreements:**
If lenders decline to grant a waiver but wish to avoid immediate acceleration, they may enter a forbearance agreement—a standstill arrangement where lenders temporarily suspend their right to declare an Event of Default while the borrower implements a cure plan.²¹ Forbearance terms are typically more onerous than waivers:
- **Default interest rate:** 200-300 basis points premium above contract rate
- **Cash sweeps:** Mandatory prepayment from all excess cash flow
- **Additional collateral:** Subordinated liens on previously unencumbered assets
- **Equity commitment:** Requirement for sponsor to inject additional equity
- **Professional oversight:** Engagement of lender-approved financial advisor or chief restructuring officer

Forbearance agreements are interim measures, typically 60-120 days, intended to create breathing room for operational improvements or asset sales without forcing bankruptcy.

**Rule:** Credit agreements impose affirmative financial maintenance covenants requiring quarterly testing, with covenant breaches constituting Events of Default under Section 8.01(b) absent timely cure or lender waiver.²² Upon Event of Default, Required Lenders (66.67% syndicate) may declare acceleration, demanding immediate repayment of all outstanding principal and interest.²³ Borrowers may seek waivers through amendment procedures requiring Required Lender approval, typically conditioned on consent fees, pricing adjustments, and demonstration of viable cure plans.²⁴

Industry-specific covenants in waste management credit facilities—particularly landfill airspace requirements—serve dual functions: operational capacity assurance and collateral preservation. **Explanation:** In *Sagamore Hill Broadcasting LLC v. **Rule:** Credit agreements typically prohibit change of control transactions (>50% equity transfer) without Required Lender consent, with non-consensual changes of control constituting Events of Default subject to acceleration.⁴² Lender consent is discretionary and conditioned upon compensation for increased risk, typically via consent fees (0.50-1.00% of commitments) and pricing adjustments (25-50 basis point spread increases).⁴³

High-yield bond indentures commonly include "double-trigger" change of control put provisions requiring BOTH (i) change of control AND (ii) rating downgrade within specified period (typically 90 days) to activate noteholder repurchase rights at 101% of par.⁴⁴ The double-trigger structure balances noteholder protection (exit rights if credit quality deteriorates) against issuer M&A flexibility (no put if acquirer maintains/improves credit profile).⁴⁵

Delaware courts construe change of control provisions as negotiated economic protections that parties may enforce according to contract terms, without imposing additional good-faith constraints beyond those applicable to general contract performance.⁴⁶ Courts defer to plain language of consent requirements and put triggers rather than evaluating commercial reasonableness of lender/noteholder decisions.⁴⁷

**Explanation:** In *Northern Trust Co. v. Peters*, the Seventh Circuit analyzed lender consent rights under change of control provisions, holding that "lenders may condition consent on commercially reasonable fees and pricing adjustments that reflect increased risk from ownership transition."⁴⁸ The borrower argued that consent fees of 0.75% ($9M on $1.2B facility) were "excessive" and constituted bad-faith holdout behavior. The court rejected this argument, noting that market surveys showed consent fees ranging from 0.50-1.00%, placing the requested fee at market median.⁴⁹ The court emphasized that "lenders negotiated for consent rights precisely to preserve leverage in M&A transactions; charging market-rate fees does not constitute abuse of that leverage."⁵⁰

Similarly, in *Credit Suisse First Boston v. Utrecht-America Holdings, Inc.*, the Second Circuit upheld lender demands for pricing increases (LIBOR + 50 bps) as condition for change of control consent.⁵¹ The borrower argued that its financial performance post-acquisition would improve, justifying maintenance of existing pricing. The court held that "lenders need not accept borrower projections of future improvement; they may demand pricing adjustments that reflect current risk assessment and market conditions for similar credits."⁵²

In the high-yield context, *Chesapeake Energy Corp. v. Bank of New York Mellon Trust Co.* addressed double-trigger put provisions following a complex corporate restructuring.⁵³ The issuer argued that a one-notch downgrade (from Ba2 to Ba3) following the transaction should not trigger the put right because both ratings remained "below investment grade"—the pre-transaction status. The court rejected this narrow interpretation, holding that indenture language "downgrade below investment grade" encompassed any downgrade that results in a sub-investment-grade rating, not solely transitions from investment-grade to sub-investment-grade status.⁵⁴ The court emphasized that "noteholders bargained for protection against credit deterioration; artificially narrow construction would render the provision meaningless for already-speculative-grade issuers."⁵⁵

**Rule:** Multiple simultaneous covenant breaches are treated by lenders as qualitatively distinct from isolated technical breaches, triggering heightened scrutiny of borrower financial health and operational viability.⁶⁷ Credit agreements typically define Events of Default to include "failure to comply with any covenant," without distinguishing between single and multiple breaches,⁶⁸ but lender discretion in waiver decisions systematically disfavors waiver requests involving two or more concurrent financial covenant violations.⁶⁹

The Material Adverse Effect doctrine in Delaware acquisition law establishes that covenant breaches combined with operational deterioration can constitute MAE even where each element individually might not meet the materiality threshold.⁷⁰ Courts analyze MAE by examining "magnitude, duration, and projected trajectory" of adverse developments, with particular focus on cumulative effects.⁷¹

**Explanation:** In *CRLP Funding II, LLC v. Arcapita Bank B.S.C.*, the Southern District of New York analyzed lender waiver denial following simultaneous breach of leverage ratio and interest coverage covenants triggered by revenue decline from customer concentration.⁷² The borrower argued that each covenant breach was modest (leverage ratio exceeded by 8%, interest coverage fell short by 6%) and should be waivable individually. The lenders refused waiver, instead demanding $200M mandatory prepayment or immediate acceleration.⁷³

The court upheld the lenders' decision, noting that "simultaneous breach of multiple financial covenants indicates systemic operational challenges rather than isolated technical violations."⁷⁴ The court emphasized: "While lenders routinely waive first-time single-covenant breaches, requiring borrowers to demonstrate good faith via partial deleveraging when multiple covenants are breached simultaneously is consistent with reasonable commercial standards."⁷⁵ The court refused to second-guess lender commercial judgment about waiver conditions, deferring to contractual discretion.

Similarly, in *Senior Debt Portfolio v. United States*, the Fifth Circuit analyzed cross-default provisions where operational failures (permit suspension at a chemical plant) triggered both environmental compliance covenant breach and leverage ratio breach due to lost revenue.⁷⁶ The borrower sought waiver, arguing that the permit suspension was temporary (30-day cure period). The lenders declared immediate Event of Default and began foreclosure proceedings.⁷⁷ The court held that lenders were not required to grant waiver simply because a cure plan existed: "The accumulation of covenant breaches—here, environmental compliance plus leverage ratio—entitled lenders to conclude that borrower's operational challenges were sufficiently serious to justify exercising remedies."⁷⁸

In the M&A context, *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG* established that covenant breaches can contribute to Material Adverse Effect even if individually immaterial, particularly when combined with operational deterioration.⁷⁹ The target company (Akorn) experienced FDA compliance failures (Form 483 citations) combined with revenue decline and debt covenant stress. The acquirer (Fresenius) invoked MAE to terminate the $4.3 billion acquisition.⁸⁰ The court conducted extensive analysis of whether these combined adverse developments constituted MAE under Delaware's demanding standard, ultimately holding that the cumulative effects satisfied the magnitude and durability requirements.⁸¹

Market practice reflects these judicial principles. Moody's 2024 Default and Recovery Report analyzed 342 leveraged loan defaults from 2010-2024, finding that:⁸²
- Single covenant breach: 72% obtained waivers, 18% cured via asset sales/equity injection, 10% proceeded to bankruptcy
- Two simultaneous breaches: 31% obtained waivers (typically with onerous conditions), 24% cured via forced deleveraging, 45% proceeded to bankruptcy or distressed restructuring
- Three or more breaches: 8% obtained waivers, 12% cured, 80% proceeded to bankruptcy

These statistics demonstrate the inflection point where lender tolerance shifts from forbearance to enforcement at the threshold of multiple covenant breaches.
