#### 5. Successor Liability and Corrective Action Transfer

**RCRA Citizen Suit Authority (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 6972)**: RCRA authorizes private citizen suits against facility operators for: (1) violations of RCRA permits, standards, or regulations [¬ß 6972(a)(1)(A)], and (2) imminent and substantial endangerment to health or environment from hazardous waste handling [¬ß 6972(a)(1)(B)]. Prevailing plaintiffs recover attorney fees, creating incentive for litigation.

**Operator Liability**: RCRA's "operator" liability applies to "the person responsible for the overall operation of a facility." Courts interpret this broadly to include current owners with operational control, management companies, and even parent corporations exercising pervasive control over subsidiaries. Unlike CERCLA's retroactive "owner or operator" liability, RCRA citizen suits generally require a present violation‚Äîbut imminent endangerment claims can reach past contributors if contamination presents continuing threat.

**Successor Liability Doctrines**: While the general rule is that asset purchasers do not assume seller liabilities, four common-law exceptions apply: (1) express or implied assumption, (2) de facto merger, (3) mere continuation, and (4) fraud on creditors. Courts examine factors including continuity of management, same physical location, common equity ownership, assumption of ordinary business debts, and seller dissolution post-sale.

**RCRA-Specific Precedents**: Courts have held that companies can have RCRA "operator" liability even if they became involved with a facility **after closure**, if they assumed environmental obligations, provided financial guarantees, or took active roles in fulfilling prior owner/operator obligations. Post-bankruptcy successors have been held liable for pre-bankruptcy contamination under RCRA corrective action orders.

**Application to Project Titan**:
- **Stock Purchase/Merger Structure** (likely): UESC continues as same legal entity; all RCRA permits, corrective action obligations, and liabilities transfer automatically; no permit transfer approval required
- **Asset Purchase Structure** (less likely): RCRA permits must be transferred with EPA approval under 40 C.F.R. ¬ß 270.40 (6-12 month process); corrective action obligations transfer with the facility by operation of law; successor liability risk if transaction qualifies as de facto merger or mere continuation

**Corrective Action as Ongoing Obligation**: Sierra Vista's $15-25M remaining corrective action obligation is not a discrete past liability but an **ongoing permit condition** under RCRA ¬ß 3004(u). Corrective action orders "run with the land"‚Äîthey attach to the facility, not merely to the original violator. The renewed RCRA permit will incorporate corrective action as a binding condition on "the permittee" (whoever holds the permit post-acquisition). This obligation **cannot be disclaimed** in an asset purchase; it transfers by operation of law.

**Citizen Suit Risk Post-Acquisition**: The 450 public comments received during permit renewal (with environmental justice concerns) indicate organized community opposition. Citizen suit risk factors include:
- Ownership changes often trigger suits (new owner perceived as deeper pockets)
- Ongoing corrective action creates continuing basis for imminent endangerment claims
- Future permit violations trigger violation-based claims
- OSHA PSM violations cited as evidence of systemic non-compliance

**Quantified Risk**:

- **Defense Costs**: $500K-$1.5M (2-3 year litigation)

### B. Groundwater Monitoring Requirements (40 C.F.R. Part 264, Subpart F)

#### 1. Regulatory Framework for Permitted TSDFs

#### 2. Three-Phase Groundwater Monitoring Program

For permitted TSDFs, a groundwater monitoring program consists of three distinct phases, each with escalating requirements:¬π‚Å∏

**Phase 1: Detection Monitoring (40 C.F.R. ¬ß 264.98)**

The owner/operator must monitor for **indicator parameters** (e.g., specific conductance, total organic carbon, or total organic halogen), waste constituents, or reaction products that provide a reliable indication of the presence of hazardous constituents in groundwater.¬π‚Åπ

**Objective**: Detect releases before significant groundwater contamination occurs.

**Phase 2: Compliance Monitoring (40 C.F.R. ¬ß 264.99)**

Whenever hazardous constituents under ¬ß 264.93 from a regulated unit are detected at a compliance point under ¬ß 264.95, the owner/operator must institute a **compliance monitoring program**.¬≤‚Å∞ The owner/operator must monitor groundwater to determine whether regulated units are in compliance with the **groundwater protection standard** under ¬ß 264.92.¬≤¬π

**Groundwater Protection Standard**: The standard requires that hazardous constituents not exceed:
- Maximum concentration limits (MCLs) for drinking water, or
- Alternate concentration limits (ACLs) established by EPA, or
- Background levels for constituents without MCLs

**Phase 3: Corrective Action (40 C.F.R. ¬ß 264.100)**

Whenever the groundwater protection standard under ¬ß 264.92 is exceeded, the owner/operator must institute a **corrective action program**.¬≤¬≤ The owner/operator must:

1. Take corrective action to ensure regulated units comply with the groundwater protection standard
2. Implement a corrective action program that **prevents hazardous constituents from exceeding concentration limits** at the compliance point by:
   - Removing the hazardous waste constituents, or
   - Treating them in place¬≤¬≥

3. Conduct corrective action to remove or treat hazardous constituents that exceed concentration limits in groundwater:
   - Between the compliance point and downgradient property boundary
   - Beyond the facility boundary where necessary to protect human health and the environment¬≤‚Å¥

#### 3. Statistical Analysis Requirements

EPA has issued extensive guidance on statistical analysis of groundwater monitoring data at RCRA facilities.¬≤‚Åµ The regulations require statistically significant increases above background levels to trigger compliance monitoring, and exceedances of groundwater protection standards to trigger corrective action.

### D. RCRA Corrective Action (Section 3004(u) Authority)

### F. Successor Liability Under RCRA

#### 1. RCRA Citizen Suit Authority (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 6972)

#### 2. "Operator" Liability Definition

Under RCRA regulations, a facility "operator" is defined as "the person responsible for the overall operation of a facility."‚Å∂‚Å¥ Courts have interpreted this broadly to include:
- Current owners who exercise operational control
- Management companies or contractors with day-to-day operational authority
- Parent corporations that exercise pervasive control over subsidiary operations‚Å∂‚Åµ

**Critical Distinction from CERCLA**: Unlike CERCLA's "owner or operator" liability (which can apply to past owners), RCRA citizen suits under ¬ß 6972(a)(1)(A) generally require a present violation. **Imminent and Substantial Endangerment**: Courts have allowed ¬ß 6972(a)(1)(B) claims against former owners/operators, noting that the imminent endangerment provision reaches continued pollution arising from past RCRA violations, even if the defendant no longer owns or operates the facility.‚Å∂‚Å∏

#### 4. Successor Corporation Liability in Asset Purchases

**General Rule**: The traditional rule is that where one company sells or otherwise transfers its assets to another company, the latter is **not liable** for the debts and liabilities of the transferor.‚Å∂‚Åπ

**Four Common-Law Exceptions**:‚Å∑‚Å∞
1. The buyer **expressly or impliedly assumes** the seller's liabilities
2. The transaction constitutes a **de facto merger**
3. The buyer is a **mere continuation** of the seller
4. The transaction was undertaken to **defraud creditors**

**De Facto Merger Factors**: Courts consider:‚Å∑¬π
- Continuity of management, personnel, and operations
- Same physical location and general business
- Common equity ownership between buyer and seller
- Assumption of seller's ordinary course business trade debt
- Seller's dissolution or cessation of operations following the sale

**Mere Continuation Factors**: Courts consider:‚Å∑¬≤
- Same shareholders, directors, and officers
- Only one corporation remains after transaction
- Same business conducted from same location
- Same assets and employees

#### 5. RCRA-Specific Successor Liability Precedents

**Operator Liability for Post-Closure Obligations**: Courts have ruled that a company that did not become involved with a hazardous waste TSDF until **after the facility was closed** can have liability as an "operator" under RCRA if it:‚Å∑¬≥
- Assumed environmental obligations in the transaction
- Provided financial guarantees or indemnification for environmental liabilities
- Took an active role in fulfilling existing environmental obligations of the prior owner/operator

**Post-Bankruptcy Successor Liability**: Courts have held that a company that emerged from Chapter 11 bankruptcy was required to undertake cleanup ordered by EPA under RCRA for contamination caused by its **pre-bankruptcy predecessor**.‚Å∑‚Å¥ A successor to a post-bankruptcy entity may be held responsible for cleanup obligations associated with pre-bankruptcy operations.‚Å∑‚Åµ

**RCRA Corrective Action Liability**: In *Williams Mullen* case analysis, courts have addressed whether RCRA corrective action liability can be imposed on a company that never owned or operated the facility, concluding that operational control or assumption of environmental obligations can create liability even without formal ownership.‚Å∑‚Å∂
