## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Subtitle D Regulatory Framework (40 CFR Part 258)

#### 1. Overview of Federal Minimum Criteria

40 CFR Part 258 establishes minimum national criteria under RCRA for all municipal solid waste landfill (MSWLF) units to ensure protection of human health and the environment.¬π These criteria apply to owners and operators of new MSWLF units, existing MSWLF units, and lateral expansions.¬≤ The regulations, initially promulgated in 1991 and subsequently revised, specifically include seven subparts:¬≥

1. **Subpart A:** General provisions, including RD&D permits
2. **Subpart B:** Location restrictions
3. **Subpart C:** Operating criteria
4. **Subpart D:** Design criteria
5. **Subpart E:** Groundwater monitoring and corrective action
6. **Subpart F:** Closure and post-closure care
7. **Subpart G:** Financial assurance criteria

A municipal solid waste landfill (MSWLF) unit is defined as "a discrete area of land or excavation that receives household waste, and may also receive other types of RCRA Subtitle D wastes, such as commercial solid waste, nonhazardous sludge, very small quantity generator waste and industrial solid waste."‚Å¥

**Regulatory Significance for UESC Transaction:**

The six UESC landfills are regulated under Subtitle D as MSWLFs and must comply with all federal minimum criteria and any more stringent state requirements. Vertical expansions of these facilities will trigger design and operating requirements under Subparts C and D, as well as revised closure cost estimates and financial assurance under Subparts F and G.

#### 2. Location Restrictions (40 CFR ¬ß 258.10-258.19)

Federal location restrictions are critical for evaluating vertical expansion feasibility, as they may prohibit or constrain additional lifts:

**a. Airport Safety (¬ß 258.10):**
- Owners/operators must demonstrate that the MSWLF unit is designed and operated so that the facility does not pose a bird hazard to aircraft
- New MSWLF units and lateral expansions cannot be located within 10,000 feet of any airport runway end used by turbojet aircraft or within 5,000 feet of any airport runway end used by only piston-type aircraft
- **UESC Impact:** Phoenix North Landfill (Maricopa County) is located near Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport; vertical expansion may trigger enhanced bird hazard assessment

**b. Floodplains (¬ß 258.11):**
- Facilities in 100-year floodplains must demonstrate that the unit will not restrict the flow of the 100-year flood, reduce the temporary water storage capacity of the floodplain, or result in washout of solid waste

**c. Wetlands (¬ß 258.12):**
- New MSWLF units and lateral expansions cannot be located in wetlands unless certain demonstration requirements are met

**d. Fault Areas (¬ß 258.13):**
- New MSWLF units and lateral expansions cannot be located within 200 feet of a fault that has had displacement in Holocene time

**e. Seismic Impact Zones (¬ß 258.14):**
- New MSWLF units and lateral expansions located in seismic impact zones must be designed to resist the maximum horizontal acceleration in lithified earth material for the site

**f. Unstable Areas (¬ß 258.15):**
- Owners/operators must demonstrate that engineered measures have been incorporated into the MSWLF unit's design to ensure that the structural integrity of the unit is not compromised

**Vertical Expansion Implication:**
Federal regulations do not impose absolute height limitations on MSWLFs, but vertical expansions must comply with all location restrictions. For UESC facilities, the primary constraints are likely to be airport safety (Phoenix), seismic (Nevada/California proximity), and unstable slope considerations.

#### 3. Design Criteria (40 CFR ¬ß 258.40)

All new MSWLF units and lateral expansions must be designed with a composite liner system and leachate collection system.‚Åµ The composite liner must consist of:

- **Upper component:** Minimum 30-mil flexible membrane liner (FML)
- **Lower component:** At least two feet of compacted soil with hydraulic conductivity of no more than 1 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∑ cm/sec

**Vertical Expansion Design Consideration:**

Vertical expansions over existing waste must include:
- Intermediate cover or gas venting layer to prevent gas migration
- Final cover system over closed portions meeting ¬ß 258.60 requirements
- Engineered slope stability analysis to prevent failure
- Leachate management system modifications if additional capacity increases leachate generation

#### 4. Operating Criteria (40 CFR ¬ß¬ß 258.20-258.29)

**a. Procedures for Excluding Hazardous Waste (¬ß 258.20):**
- Random inspections of incoming loads
- Records of inspections and training procedures

**b. Cover Material Requirements (¬ß 258.21):**
- **Daily cover:** Minimum six inches of earthen material at end of each operating day
- **Alternative daily cover (ADC):** May be approved by state director if provides equivalent environmental protection
- **UESC Cost Consideration:** ADC materials (tarps, foam, auto shredder residue) may reduce airspace consumption compared to soil cover, extending facility life

**c. Disease Vector Control (¬ß 258.22):**
- Must prevent or control on-site populations of disease vectors using approved techniques

**d. Explosive Gases Control (¬ß 258.23):**
- Methane gas concentration must not exceed:
  - 25% of the lower explosive limit (LEL) in facility structures
  - LEL at the facility property boundary
- **UESC Vertical Expansion Impact:** Additional waste mass increases gas generation; gas collection systems must be upgraded accordingly

**e. Air Criteria (¬ß 258.24):**
- Must not violate applicable requirements under Clean Air Act
- Open burning prohibited except for infrequent burning of agricultural wastes, silvicultural wastes, land-clearing debris, etc.

**f. Access Requirements (¬ß 258.25):**
- Must control public access and prevent unauthorized vehicular traffic and illegal dumping

#### 5. Groundwater Monitoring and Corrective Action (40 CFR ¬ß¬ß 258.50-258.58)

**a. Groundwater Monitoring System (¬ß 258.51):**
- Sufficient number of monitoring wells to immediately detect contamination
- Monitoring wells must be installed at appropriate locations and depths
- Background monitoring and detection monitoring required

**b. Detection Monitoring Program (¬ß 258.54):**
- Must monitor for Appendix I and II constituents
- Semi-annual sampling frequency minimum
- **UESC Exposure:** Vertical expansions may extend detection monitoring periods and post-closure care duration

**c. Assessment Monitoring (¬ß 258.55):**
- If statistically significant increase in Appendix I parameters detected
- Must determine if Appendix II constituents present

**d. Corrective Action (¬ß 258.56-258.58):**
- Required if groundwater protection standard exceeded
- Must select and implement remedy that returns to compliance
- **UESC Financial Risk:** Any UESC facility with ongoing corrective action obligations will require extended financial assurance and post-closure care periods

#### 6. Closure and Post-Closure Care (40 CFR ¬ß¬ß 258.60-258.61)

**a. Closure Criteria (¬ß 258.60):**
Upon closure, owner/operator must:
- Install final cover system designed to minimize infiltration and erosion
- Final cover system must include:
  - **Erosion layer:** Minimum 6 inches, capable of sustaining native plant growth
  - **Infiltration layer:** Minimum 18 inches, permeability less than or equal to bottom liner system or natural subsoils

**b. Post-Closure Care (¬ß 258.61):**
- **Duration:** 30 years minimum (may be shortened or lengthened by state director)
- **Requirements during post-closure period:**
  - Maintain integrity and effectiveness of final cover
  - Maintain and operate leachate collection system
  - Maintain and monitor groundwater monitoring system
  - Maintain and operate gas monitoring system

**UESC Airspace Impact:**
Vertical expansions delay closure dates, extending the active operational period and deferring post-closure care obligations. However, they also increase total closure costs due to greater surface area requiring final cover.

#### 7. Financial Assurance Requirements (40 CFR ¬ß¬ß 258.70-258.74)

**a. Financial Assurance for Closure (¬ß 258.71):**

Owner/operator must have a detailed written estimate, in current dollars, of the cost of hiring a third party to close the largest area of all MSWLF units ever requiring a final cover.‚Å∂ The cost estimate must equal the cost of closing the largest area at any time during the active life when the extent and manner of operation would make closure most expensive.‚Å∑

**Annual Adjustment:** During the active life, owner/operator must annually adjust the closure cost estimate for inflation using the Implicit Price Deflator for Gross National Product.‚Å∏

**b. Financial Assurance for Post-Closure Care (¬ß 258.72):**

Owner/operator must have a detailed written estimate, in current dollars, of the cost of hiring a third party to conduct post-closure care for 30 years.‚Åπ The estimate must be based on the most expensive costs during the post-closure period, accounting for total costs including annual and periodic costs.¬π‚Å∞

**Annual Adjustment:** Must annually adjust post-closure cost estimate for inflation during active life and post-closure care period.¬π¬π

**c. Allowable Financial Assurance Mechanisms (¬ß 258.74):**¬π¬≤

1. **Trust Fund:** Fully funded at closure or incremental funding during active life
2. **Surety Bond:** Guaranteeing performance or payment
3. **Letter of Credit:** Irrevocable standby letter of credit
4. **Insurance:** Closure or post-closure insurance policies
5. **Corporate Financial Test:** Demonstration of financial strength
6. **Corporate Guarantee:** From parent corporation
7. **Multiple Mechanisms:** Combination of above

**Corporate Financial Test Requirements (Critical for UESC):**

Under ¬ß 258.74(e), to pass the corporate financial test, the firm must have:
- Either:
  - **Option 1:** Tangible net worth ‚â• $10 million, plus 6 times sum of closure/post-closure costs for all facilities
  - **Option 2:** Tangible net worth ‚â• $10 million, and certain financial ratios:
    - Current bond rating of AAA, AA, A, or BBB (S&P) or Aaa, Aa, A, or Baa (Moody's)
    - Ratio < 1.5 comparing total liabilities to net worth
    - Ratio > 0.10 comparing net income plus depreciation, depletion, and amortization minus $10 million, divided by total liabilities

**UESC Transaction Impact:**

Post-acquisition, UESC may not satisfy the corporate financial test if:
1. Acquirer's balance sheet does not meet tangible net worth requirements
2. Acquirer lacks investment-grade bond ratings
3. Debt/equity ratios deteriorate post-transaction

If corporate financial test fails, UESC must obtain trust funds, surety bonds, or letters of credit totaling $247 million (per user-provided estimate), representing a significant liquidity demand.

### D. Airspace Capacity Analysis and Debt Covenant Impact

#### 1. Airspace Calculation Methodology

**Topographic Survey Method:**

The most accurate method for determining remaining landfill capacity uses topographic survey data from periodic aerial or ground surveys to develop an estimate of total available airspace.‚Åµ‚Å∂ The calculation methodology involves:

1. **Current volume determination:** Survey of current waste surface elevation
2. **Final permitted volume determination:** Final grading plan surface model
3. **Remaining airspace calculation:** Final volume - Current volume = Remaining airspace‚Åµ‚Å∑

**Airspace Utilization Factor (AUF):**

AUF is defined as "the effective density of waste material in the landfill, recorded as the total weight of waste material passing over the landfill scales that is placed in a known volume of landfill airspace in a given time period."‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Target Compaction Densities:**

- **Industry standard:** 1,200 lbs/cubic yard minimum‚Åµ‚Åπ
- **Best-in-class:** 1,500-1,800 lbs/cubic yard‚Å∂‚Å∞
- **Average waste (uncompacted):** ~500 lbs/cubic yard‚Å∂¬π
- **Typical soil cover:** ~2,550 lbs/cubic yard‚Å∂¬≤

**Cover Material Impact on Airspace:**

Daily cover (6 inches of soil) and intermediate cover consume airspace without generating tipping fee revenue. The ratio of refuse to cover material must be determined to calculate accurate remaining airspace estimates.‚Å∂¬≥ **Alternative daily cover (ADC)** using spray-on materials or tarps minimizes airspace consumption compared to earthen cover.‚Å∂‚Å¥

**Best Practices for Maximizing Airspace:**

1. **Aggressive compaction:** Four passes compacting 12-15 inch layers delivers 1,200 lbs/yd¬≥ density‚Å∂‚Åµ
2. **Regular AUF monitoring:** Facilities that regularly measure AUF increase density by 15%‚Å∂‚Å∂
3. **Minimize cover soil:** Most significant gain in long-term effective density‚Å∂‚Å∑
4. **GPS-equipped compactors:** Track number of passes, optimize lift thickness‚Å∂‚Å∏
5. **Strategic fill progression:** Limit haul roads, identify efficient wet weather areas‚Å∂‚Åπ

**Real-World Results:**

Landfills implementing aggressive compaction strategies have increased AUF by nearly 300 lbs/cubic yard, from 1,200-1,300 lbs/yd¬≥ to nearly 1,500 lbs/yd¬≥.‚Å∑‚Å∞

#### 3. Debt Covenant Breach Analysis

**Covenant Structure (User-Provided):**

UESC's $1.8 billion debt (term loan $950M + senior notes $850M) contains a financial covenant requiring **minimum 10.0 years remaining capacity**, calculated as **weighted average across all landfills by disposal volume**.‚Å∑‚Å¥

**Current Breach Status:**

- **Current airspace:** 8.0 years
- **Covenant minimum:** 10.0 years
- **Breach magnitude:** 2.0 years (20% below minimum)
- **Cure period:** 180 days from breach‚Å∑‚Åµ

**Weighted Average Calculation Methodology:**

While industry-standard "weighted average airspace" methodology is not codified in EPA regulations, typical debt covenant calculations use:

$$\text{Weighted Avg Years} = \frac{\sum (\text{Facility}_i \ \text{Airspace}_i)}{\sum (\text{Facility}_i \ \text{Annual Disposal}_i)}$$

**Example Calculation (Illustrative):**

Assume:
- Facility A: 10M CY remaining, 1M CY/year disposal = 10 years
- Facility B: 2M CY remaining, 1M CY/year disposal = 2 years
- Weighted average = (10M + 2M) / (1M + 1M) = 12M / 2M = **6.0 years**

This methodology penalizes facilities with high disposal volumes and low remaining airspace, as they disproportionately impact the weighted average.

**Consequences of Covenant Breach:**

1. **Event of Default:** Lenders may declare all amounts immediately due and payable‚Å∑‚Å∂
2. **Debt Acceleration:** $1.8 billion becomes immediately due
3. **Cross-Default:** May trigger defaults under other financing agreements
4. **Foreclosure Risk:** Senior secured lenders have security interest in landfill assets
5. **Credit Rating Downgrade:** Moody's/S&P downgrade impacts borrowing costs
6. **Material Adverse Effect:** May constitute MAE under acquisition agreement, allowing buyer to terminate

**Cure Mechanisms:**

1. **Vertical expansions:** Obtain permits adding airspace at existing facilities
2. **Lateral expansions:** Acquire adjacent property and permit expansion
3. **Acquire additional landfill:** Purchase third-party landfill to add to portfolio
4. **Operational optimization:** Increase compaction density (AUF improvement from 1,200 to 1,500 lbs/yd¬≥ = 25% airspace extension)
5. **Covenant waiver:** Negotiate with lenders to waive breach or amend covenant to reduce 10-year minimum
6. **Reduce disposal volumes:** Divert waste to third-party landfills (reduces revenue, increases costs)

**Timeline Constraint:**

180-day cure period = **6 months**. However:
- Arizona vertical expansion: 12-18 months
- Nevada vertical expansion: 18-24 months
- New Mexico vertical expansion: 22-32 months
- Texas vertical expansion: 14-22 months

**Critical Issue:** None of the four states' permitting timelines allow vertical expansion approval within 180-day cure period. This necessitates either:
- **Proactive filing** of expansion applications well before covenant measurement date, OR
- **Covenant waiver negotiation** concurrent with expansion permitting

#### 4. Airspace Deficit Quantification

**To cure 2.0-year deficit:**

$$\text{Required Additional Airspace} = 2.0 \ \text{years} \times 8.6M \ \text{CY/year} = 17.2M \ \text{cubic yards}$$

**Alternatively, through operational optimization:**

If UESC increases compaction density from 1,200 lbs/yd¬≥ to 1,500 lbs/yd¬≥ (25% improvement):
$$\text{Effective Airspace Extension} = 69M \ \text{CY} \times 0.25 = 17.25M \ \text{CY}$$

This suggests **operational optimization alone could theoretically cure the covenant breach** without vertical expansions. However:
- Achieving 25% density improvement across all six facilities simultaneously is operationally challenging
- Lenders may require physical permitted airspace increases rather than density assumptions
- Covenant language may specify "permitted capacity" vs. "effective capacity"
