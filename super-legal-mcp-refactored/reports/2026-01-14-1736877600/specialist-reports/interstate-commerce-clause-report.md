### Key Takeaways

**1. Robust Constitutional Protection (45+ Years of Consistent Precedent)**

The dormant Commerce Clauseâ€”implicit in Article I, Section 8, Clause 3 of the U.S. Constitutionâ€”prohibits states from discriminating against or excessively burdening interstate commerce. In the waste management context, the Supreme Court has developed a clear three-tier framework:

**Tier 1 - Per Se Invalid (Strict Scrutiny)**: State laws that facially discriminate against interstate commerce based on waste origin are presumptively unconstitutional unless the state proves (a) a legitimate local purpose unrelated to economic protectionism, AND (b) no nondiscriminatory alternatives exist. Applies to:
- Out-of-state waste import bans (*Philadelphia*, *Fort Gratiot*)
- Differential tipping fees for out-of-state waste (*Oregon Waste Systems*)
- Flow control ordinances requiring disposal at privately-owned facilities (*Carbone*)

**Tier 2 - Pike Balancing (Intermediate Scrutiny)**: Non-discriminatory regulations that incidentally burden interstate commerce are evaluated under *Pike v. Bruce Church*: upheld if benefits to local health/safety/welfare outweigh interstate commerce burden. Applies to:
- Flow control ordinances requiring disposal at publicly-owned facilities (*United Haulers*)
- Neutral regulations with incidental interstate effects

**Tier 3 - Market Participant Exception**: States acting as market participants (buyers/sellers) rather than regulators are exempt from Commerce Clause scrutiny (not applicable to UESC operations).

This framework provides UESC with **predictable, enforceable constitutional rights** to transport waste across state lines without discriminatory state restrictions.

**5. Flow Control to Publicly-Owned Facilities: The One Constitutional Exception**

**Critical Distinction from *C & A Carbone* (1994) to *United Haulers* (2007)**:

In *C & A Carbone, Inc. v. Town of Clarkstown*, 511 U.S. 383 (1994), the Supreme Court struck down a flow control ordinance requiring all waste to be processed at a **privately-operated** transfer station before leaving the municipality. The Court held this discriminated against interstate commerce by "hoarding solid waste...for the benefit of the preferred processing facility" and depriving out-of-state competitors of market access.

However, in *United Haulers Ass'n, Inc. v. Oneida-Herkimer Solid Waste Management Authority*, 550 U.S. 330 (2007), the Supreme Court **upheld** a flow control ordinance requiring waste disposal at a **publicly-owned** facility operated by a public benefit corporation. The Court distinguished *Carbone* based on the public vs. private ownership of the designated facility:

> "The only salient difference from *Carbone* is that the laws at issue here require haulers to bring waste to facilities owned and operated by a state-created public benefit corporation...Disposing of trash has been a traditional government activity for years, and laws that favor the government in such areasâ€”but treat every private business, whether in-state or out-of-state, exactly the sameâ€”do not discriminate against interstate commerce."

The Court applied the less stringent *Pike* balancing test (rather than strict scrutiny) and held that incidental burdens on interstate commerce did not outweigh benefits to local citizens.

**Implications for UESC**:
- **Unconstitutional**: Phoenix, Las Vegas, or other municipalities cannot require disposal at UESC's privately-owned landfills or transfer stations (*Carbone*)

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Supreme Court Dormant Commerce Clause Jurisprudence: Interstate Waste Transport

#### 1. *City of Philadelphia v. New Jersey*, 437 U.S. 617 (1978) - Foundational Precedent

**Facts**: In 1974, New Jersey enacted legislation prohibiting the importation of most "solid or liquid waste which originated or was collected outside the territorial limits of the State." [VERIFIED: Supreme Court opinion] Private landfill operators and the City of Philadelphia challenged the statute as violating the dormant Commerce Clause.

**Holding**: The Supreme Court, in a 7-2 decision authored by Justice Stewart, struck down New Jersey's waste import ban. The Court held that:

1. **Waste is an Article of Commerce**: "All objects of interstate trade merit Commerce Clause protection and none is excluded from the definition of 'commerce' at the outset." Solid waste qualifies as an article of interstate commerce regardless of its negative characteristics or environmental hazards.

2. **Facial Discrimination**: New Jersey's statute facially discriminated against interstate commerce by treating out-of-state waste differently based solely on its origin.

3. **Environmental Purpose Insufficient**: The Court rejected New Jersey's environmental protection rationale, holding that "[t]he State's ultimate purpose may not be accomplished by discriminating against articles of commerce coming from outside the State unless there is some reason, apart from their origin, to treat them differently."

**Legal Standard**: States cannot discriminate against articles of interstate commerce absent a legitimate local purpose that cannot be served by nondiscriminatory alternatives. Protectionist measures that isolate in-state markets from interstate competition are per se invalid.

**Citation**: *City of Philadelphia v. New Jersey*, 437 U.S. 617, 626-29 (1978) [CourtListener ID: 109644] [VERIFIED].

**Relevance to UESC**: This foundational precedent establishes that any Nevada, Arizona, New Mexico, Texas, or California statute that facially bans or restricts waste imports from other states based solely on origin is presumptively unconstitutional. UESC's 450,000 tons/year of Las Vegas waste transported to California landfills and 120,000 tons/year of California waste imports to Arizona/Nevada landfills are protected interstate commerce activities.

---

#### 2. *Fort Gratiot Sanitary Landfill, Inc. v. Michigan Department of Natural Resources*, 504 U.S. 353 (1992) - County-Level Waste Import Restrictions

**Facts**: Michigan's Solid Waste Management Act authorized counties to prohibit acceptance of out-of-state, out-of-county, and out-of-state waste unless explicitly authorized in the receiving county's solid waste management plan. St. Clair County denied Fort Gratiot Landfill's application to accept out-of-state waste.

**Holding**: The Supreme Court (7-2, Justice Stevens) struck down Michigan's Waste Import Restrictions as facially discriminatory:

1. **Protectionism**: "The Waste Import Restrictions unambiguously discriminate against interstate commerce and are appropriately characterized as protectionist measures that cannot withstand Commerce Clause scrutiny."

2. **County-Level Discrimination**: The restrictions "authorize each county to isolate itself from the national economy" and "afford local waste producers complete protection from competition from out-of-state producers seeking to use local disposal areas."

3. **Health and Safety Burden**: Because the law discriminated on its face, Michigan bore the burden of proving it furthered health and safety concerns that could not be adequately served by nondiscriminatory alternatives. Michigan failed to provide any valid health and safety reason for limiting out-of-state waste acceptance.

**Citation**: *Fort Gratiot Sanitary Landfill, Inc. v. Michigan Dep't of Nat. Res.*, 504 U.S. 353, 359-67 (1992) [CourtListener ID: 112817] [VERIFIED].

**Relevance to UESC**: County-level waste import restrictions (e.g., Maricopa County, Clark County, or other Nevada counties where UESC operates) that discriminate based on waste origin are unconstitutional. UESC can challenge any local ordinance that restricts its ability to transport waste across state lines for disposal optimization.

#### 3. *C & A Carbone, Inc. v. Town of Clarkstown*, 511 U.S. 383 (1994) - Flow Control to Private Facilities

**Facts**: Clarkstown, New York enacted a flow control ordinance requiring all nonhazardous solid waste within the town to be processed at a privately-operated transfer station before leaving the municipality. The ordinance charged a $81/ton tipping fee, significantly higher than out-of-state disposal costs. C&A Carbone, a private recycler, sought to ship non-recyclable waste to cheaper out-of-state processors.

**Holding**: The Supreme Court (6-3, Justice Kennedy) struck down the flow control ordinance:

1. **Protectionism**: "The flow control ordinance is a financing measure, and revenue generation is not a local interest that can justify discrimination against interstate commerce."

2. **Hoard Resources**: The ordinance "hoard[s] solid waste, and the demand to get rid of it, for the benefit of the preferred processing facility."

3. **Market Access**: The ordinance "deprives competitors, including out-of-state firms, of access to a local market."

**Citation**: *C & A Carbone, Inc. v. Town of Clarkstown*, 511 U.S. 383, 389-94 (1994) [CourtListener ID: 117883] [VERIFIED].

**Relevance to UESC**: Flow control ordinances in Phoenix, Las Vegas, Albuquerque, or other UESC-served municipalities that mandate disposal at specific privately-owned facilities (including UESC's own landfills if mandated) are unconstitutional. UESC benefits from this precedent by maintaining flexibility to optimize disposal routes across state lines based on tipping fees and operational efficiency.

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION**: This holding applies only to flow control to **private facilities**. See *United Haulers* (below) for the public facility exception.

#### 4. *Oregon Waste Systems, Inc. v. Department of Environmental Quality of Oregon*, 511 U.S. 93 (1994) - Differential Tipping Fees

**Facts**: Oregon imposed a $2.25/ton surcharge on disposal of solid waste generated in other states, while charging only $0.85/ton for in-state wasteâ€”a discriminatory differential of approximately 3:1.

**Holding**: The Supreme Court (7-2, Justice Thomas) struck down the differential surcharge as facially discriminatory:

1. **Facial Discrimination**: The surcharge "subjected waste from other States to a fee almost three times greater than the $0.85 per ton charge imposed on in-state waste."

2. **Origin-Based**: "The statutory determinant for whether the fee applies is whether or not the waste was generated out of state."

3. **Compensatory Theory Rejected**: Oregon's argument that the higher fee compensated for additional costs imposed by out-of-state waste was rejected. The Court held that "[t]he alleged compensatory aim of the surcharge ha[d] no bearing on whether it was facially discriminatory."

**Citation**: *Oregon Waste Systems, Inc. v. Dep't of Envtl. Quality of Or.*, 511 U.S. 93, 99-101 (1994) [CourtListener ID: 117834] [VERIFIED].

**Relevance to UESC**: Any Nevada, Arizona, New Mexico, Texas, or California landfill that charges differential tipping fees based on waste origin (in-state vs. out-of-state) violates the Commerce Clause. UESC benefits from uniform pricing across state lines. If UESC encounters differential pricing that disadvantages its interstate waste flows, constitutional challenge is warranted.

**Financial Impact**: UESC transports 450,000 tons/year to California ($35/ton vs. $55/ton Nevada). If California imposed a discriminatory surcharge (e.g., $20/ton differential), UESC would face additional costs of $9M annually. Such surcharge would be unconstitutional under *Oregon Waste Systems*.

#### 5. *United Haulers Association, Inc. v. Oneida-Herkimer Solid Waste Management Authority*, 550 U.S. 330 (2007) - Public Facility Flow Control

**Facts**: Oneida and Herkimer Counties (New York) enacted flow control ordinances requiring all solid waste to be delivered to a publicly-owned waste processing facility operated by a public benefit corporation. Private haulers challenged the ordinances, arguing they violated *Carbone* by restricting interstate commerce. The public facility charged $86/ton, while out-of-state disposal was available for $37-55/ton.

**Holding**: The Supreme Court (6-3, Chief Justice Roberts) **upheld** the flow control ordinances, distinguishing *Carbone*:

1. **Public vs. Private**: "The only salient difference from *Carbone* is that the laws at issue here require haulers to bring waste to facilities owned and operated by a state-created public benefit corporation." The Court found this difference "constitutionally significant."

2. **Traditional Government Function**: "Disposing of trash has been a traditional government activity for years, and laws that favor the government in such areasâ€”but treat every private business, whether in-state or out-of-state, exactly the sameâ€”do not discriminate against interstate commerce."

3. **Pike Balancing Test**: Applying the less stringent *Pike* balancing test (for non-discriminatory regulations), the Court held that "any incidental burden [the ordinances] may have on interstate commerce does not outweigh the benefits they confer on the citizens of Oneida and Herkimer Counties."

**Citation**: *United Haulers Ass'n, Inc. v. Oneida-Herkimer Solid Waste Mgmt. Auth.*, 550 U.S. 330, 334-47 (2007) [CourtListener ID: 145741] [VERIFIED].

**Relevance to UESC**: Flow control ordinances requiring disposal at **publicly-owned** facilities (e.g., municipal landfills, county-operated transfer stations) are constitutional even if they burden interstate commerce. This creates a critical distinction:

- **Unconstitutional**: Phoenix, Las Vegas, or other municipalities requiring disposal at UESC's privately-owned facilities (*Carbone*).
- **Constitutional**: Phoenix, Las Vegas, or other municipalities requiring disposal at city-owned or county-operated facilities (*United Haulers*).

**Strategic Risk**: If Phoenix, Las Vegas, or other UESC-served municipalities establish publicly-owned waste processing facilities and mandate flow control to those facilities, UESC cannot constitutionally challenge such ordinances. This represents a competitive threat if municipalities seek to vertically integrate waste management operations.

**Arizona/Nevada Context**: Arizona and Nevada municipalities generally rely on private waste haulers and privately-owned landfills. Flow control to publicly-owned facilities is less common but represents a potential market disruption risk.

### B. Summary of Legal Framework: Three-Tier Analysis

#### Tier 1: **Per Se Invalid** (Strict Scrutiny)
Regulations that facially discriminate against interstate commerce based on waste origin are presumptively unconstitutional unless the state proves:
1. Legitimate local purpose unrelated to economic protectionism, AND
2. No nondiscriminatory alternatives exist to achieve that purpose.

**Applies to**:
- Out-of-state waste import bans (*Philadelphia*, *Fort Gratiot*)
- Differential tipping fees based on waste origin (*Oregon Waste Systems*)
- Flow control to privately-owned facilities (*Carbone*)

#### Tier 2: **Pike Balancing Test** (Intermediate Scrutiny)
Regulations that do not facially discriminate but burden interstate commerce are evaluated under *Pike v. Bruce Church, Inc.*, 397 U.S. 137 (1970):
- Upheld if: Benefits to local health/safety/welfare outweigh burden on interstate commerce
- Struck down if: Burden on interstate commerce is "clearly excessive in relation to the putative local benefits"

**Applies to**:
- Flow control to publicly-owned facilities (*United Haulers*)
- Neutral regulations with incidental interstate effects

#### Tier 3: **Market Participant Exception**
States acting as market participants (buyers/sellers) rather than regulators are exempt from Commerce Clause scrutiny.
- Example: State purchases waste disposal services for state-generated waste (not applicable to UESC operations)
