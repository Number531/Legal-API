### Material Adverse Effect Risk Assessment

**MAE Definition (Standard M&A Agreement):**
"Material Adverse Effect" means any event, circumstance, change, or effect that, individually or in the aggregate, has had or would reasonably be expected to have a material adverse effect on (a) the business, operations, results of operations, assets, liabilities, or financial condition of the Company and its subsidiaries, taken as a whole, or (b) the ability of the Company to consummate the transactions contemplated by this Agreement.

**Legal Standard (*Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, 2018 Del. Ch. LEXIS 325):**
Delaware Chancery Court held that MAE provisions are "backstops" protecting acquirers from unknown events that substantially threaten the target's overall earnings potential in a "durationally-significant manner." Vice Chancellor Laster emphasized:
- "Short-term hiccups in earnings should not suffice; the Material Adverse Effect should be material when viewed from the longer-term perspective of a reasonable acquirer"
- "The important consideration is whether there has been an adverse change in the target's business that is consequential to the company's long-term earnings power over a commercially reasonable period"
- *Akorn* was first Delaware case in history to find MAE occurred (affirmed by Delaware Supreme Court December 7, 2018), establishing high bar for MAE claims

**Application to UESC Debt Covenant Scenarios:**

| Scenario | MAE Probability | Analysis |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| **Airspace Covenant Breach Alone** | **10-15% (LOW)** | Single technical covenant breach with 180-day cure period does NOT constitute MAE because: (1) Known issue disclosed to acquirer in due diligence; (2) Viable cure plan via landfill expansions (Superstition Ridge approved, Desert View 50-60% approval probability); (3) Does not "substantially threaten overall earnings potential" â€” landfills continue operating, revenue unaffected; (4) Analogous to *Williams Cos. v. | **Airspace Breach + Waiver Denied + Debt Acceleration Threat** | **70-85% (HIGH)** | Lender threats to accelerate $1.8B debt constitute MAE for four reasons: (1) **Ability to Consummate Transaction Impaired** â€” Lenders refusing consent to change of control prevent acquirer from obtaining clear title, satisfying prong (b) of MAE definition; (2) **Financial Condition Impact** â€” Pending debt acceleration and cross-default to all debt instruments represent material adverse change in financial condition; (3) **Duration** â€” Debt acceleration is not "short-term hiccup" but rather fundamental capital structure crisis requiring bankruptcy or distressed refinancing; (4) **Acquirer Reasonable Expectations** â€” Acquirer reasonably expected to assume manageable $1.8B debt, not debt in Event of Default status |

### D. Event of Default Provisions and Acceleration Consequences

#### 1. Events of Default Under Credit Agreement

Standard leveraged loan credit agreements define the following Events of Default:

**(a) Payment Defaults:**
- Failure to pay principal when due (immediate Event of Default, no cure period)

- Failure to pay fees or other amounts within 10 business days (grace period)

**(b) Covenant Defaults:**
- Breach of financial maintenance covenants (leverage, interest coverage, **airspace**) â† **CURRENT BREACH**
- **Cure Period:** Typically 30-60 days to cure or obtain waiver
- **UESC Status:** 180-day cure period for airspace covenant (expires March 29, 2025)

**(c) Representation and Warranty Defaults:**
- Material misrepresentation in loan documents, compliance certificates, financial statements

**(d) Cross-Default and Cross-Acceleration:**
- Default under other debt agreements >$50M threshold

**(e) Bankruptcy and Insolvency Events:**
- Voluntary bankruptcy filing, involuntary petition not dismissed within 60 days
- Appointment of receiver, custodian, or trustee
- Assignment for benefit of creditors

**(f) Material Adverse Effect:**
