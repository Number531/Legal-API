### D. Municipal Recycling Contract Structures and Risk Allocation

#### 1. Evolution of MRF Contract Models Post-National Sword

The China National Sword policy fundamentally transformed municipal recycling contract economics. Pre-2018, many MRFs operated under **revenue-sharing models** where commodity sales fully or partially offset processing costs. Post-National Sword, the industry shifted dramatically toward **fee-for-service models** where municipalities pay guaranteed processing fees regardless of commodity prices.â¶â¸

**Fee-for-Service Model (Dominant Post-2018):**
Larger MRF operators have "almost ubiquitously adopted a fee-for-service model" in recent years, and this model is "likely to be the norm going forward."â¶â¹ Survey data shows:
- **43% of MRF operators** added processing fees to contracts within the last 36 months (2022-2024)â·â°
- **32% of recycling coordinators and government officials** now pay processing fees to MRF operatorsâ·Â¹

**Revenue Sharing Models (Legacy Contracts):**
Revenue share is "a percentage of the revenue generated by the Vendor through the sale of Recyclable Material," which may "fully or partially offset the processing fee."â·Â² Revenue is typically calculated based on:
- **Actual sales** (transparent, but volatile)
- **Market indices** (preferred by vendors for predictability)â·Â³

**Hybrid Models:**
Revenue shares are commonly structured so municipalities receive **100% of revenue until processing fees are covered**, after which surplus revenue is split (e.g., 75/25, 60/40).â·â´ Example: Rumpke Waste & Recycling operates a 75/25 split (75% to municipality, 25% to vendor) in the Midwest for cities sharing in commodity price upside.â·âµ

**Illustrative Bid Comparison:**
A municipality might receive two bids:
- **Bid A:** $98/ton processing fee + 80% revenue share
- **Bid B:** $78/ton processing fee + 40% revenue shareâ·â¶

At current commodity prices ($85/ton average), Bid B provides better economics ($78 fee - $34 revenue share = $44 net) versus Bid A ($98 - $68 = $30 net).

#### 2. Key Contract Elements and Quality Standards

The Recycling Partnership identifies **11 most important elements** of MRF contracts:â·â·
1. **Processing fees** ($/ton paid to MRF)
2. **Revenue sharing** (commodity value allocation)
3. **Material value determination** (indices, actual sales, pricing transparency)
4. **Acceptable materials mix** (contamination thresholds, prohibited items)
5. **Material audits** (third-party verification of tonnage and quality)
6. **Material contamination/quality** (residue rates, reject load standards)
7. **MRF performance** (throughput capacity, downtime allowances)
8. **Rejected loads and residue disposal** (who pays for contaminated loads?)
9. **Education and outreach support** (vendor assistance with contamination reduction)
10. **Contingencies** (force majeure, market collapse provisions)
11. **Reporting and communication** (tonnage reports, commodity sales transparency)

**Contamination Penalties:**
Modern contracts increasingly include **contamination penalty clauses** where municipalities pay surcharges if inbound contamination exceeds specified thresholds (e.g., >15% contamination triggers $10-$25/ton penalty).

**Price Reopener Clauses:**
Contracts may include provisions allowing processing fee adjustments when commodity prices fall below specified levels, protecting MRF operators from prolonged market downturns.â·â¸
