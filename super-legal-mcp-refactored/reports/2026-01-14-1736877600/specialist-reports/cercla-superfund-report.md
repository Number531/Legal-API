### CERCLA Liability Framework and Statutory Basis

#### Strict, Joint and Several, Retroactive Liability

CERCLA imposes one of the most stringent environmental liability regimes in federal law. Under 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a), four categories of parties are strictly liable for cleanup costs:
1. **Current owners/operators** (¬ß 9607(a)(1))
2. **Past owners/operators at time of disposal** (¬ß 9607(a)(2))
3. **Arrangers** (generators who arranged for disposal) (¬ß 9607(a)(3))
4. **Transporters** who selected disposal sites (¬ß 9607(a)(4))

Liability is **strict** (no fault required), **joint and several** (any one PRP can be held liable for entire cleanup cost), and **retroactive** (applies to disposal activities predating CERCLA's 1980 enactment). *Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. United States*, 556 U.S. 599, 614 (2009).

**Critical Implication for Stock Purchase:**
In a stock acquisition, the buyer becomes the "current owner" under ¬ß 9607(a)(1) and automatically assumes all CERCLA liabilities - known and unknown - regardless of contractual indemnification provisions. Indemnification provides a **private remedy against the seller** but does not bind EPA. If EPA sues the buyer post-closing, the buyer must pay EPA first, then seek reimbursement from the seller (bearing seller insolvency risk).

#### Section 107 vs. Section 113: Cost Recovery vs. Contribution

CERCLA provides two distinct remedies for allocating cleanup costs among PRPs, with dramatically different consequences:

**Section 107(a) Cost Recovery (Joint and Several Liability):**
- EPA can sue any PRP to recover its entire cleanup costs
- Burden on defendant to prove harm is "divisible" to avoid joint and several liability (*Burlington Northern*)
- 6-year statute of limitations from initiation of remedial action
- Defendant faces full exposure plus "orphan share" if co-PRPs are insolvent
- **Currently applicable to UESC at Tucson Airport site**

**Section 113(f) Contribution (Several Liability Only):**
- Available to PRPs who settle with EPA or have been sued under ¬ß 106/¬ß 107
- Court applies equitable factors ("Gore Factors") to allocate shares among all PRPs
- 3-year statute of limitations from judgment or settlement
- Settling PRPs receive contribution protection under ¬ß 113(f)(2) - **orphan share risk eliminated**
- **UESC's strategic goal: Convert Tucson from ¬ß 107 to ¬ß 113 via settlement**

*United States v. Atlantic Research Corp.*, 551 U.S. 128 (2007) (establishing ¬ß 107 vs. ¬ß 113 framework).

#### EPA Enforcement Mechanisms

**Unilateral Administrative Orders (UAOs) - Section 106:**
EPA can issue UAOs compelling PRPs to conduct cleanup when there is "imminent and substantial endangerment." Recipients face a "Hobson's choice":
- **Comply** with the UAO (even if disputed), OR
- **Refuse** and face up to **$78,376 per day** civil penalties (2024 inflation-adjusted) plus **treble damages** if refusal is deemed "willful" without "sufficient cause"

42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9606(a), ¬ß 9607(c)(3). UESC received a 2019 UAO at Motorola 52nd Street and complied, triggering ¬ß 113(f)(1) contribution rights against 46 other PRPs.

### Insurance Coverage Analysis: 95-98% Uninsured Exposure

#### CGL Pollution Exclusion Evolution

**Pre-1973:** Standard occurrence-based CGL policies with no pollution exclusion ‚Üí **Full coverage** for gradual pollution
**1973-1985:** "Sudden and accidental" pollution exclusion ‚Üí Coverage only if pollution unexpected/unintended (extensive litigation over meaning of "sudden")
**Post-1986:** **Absolute pollution exclusion** ‚Üí No coverage for any pollution (sudden, gradual, intentional, or accidental)

*Morton International, Inc. v. General Accident Insurance Co.*, 629 A.2d 831 (N.J. 1993) (pre-1986 case law holding insurers estopped from enforcing "sudden and accidental" exclusion for unintentional gradual pollution).

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. CERCLA Statutory and Regulatory Framework

#### 1. CERCLA Liability Provisions (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607)

**Statutory Basis:**
CERCLA, enacted in 1980 and codified at 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9601 et seq., establishes one of the most stringent environmental liability regimes in federal law. Section 9607(a) imposes strict, joint and several, and retroactive liability on four categories of potentially responsible parties (PRPs).¬π

**Four PRP Categories (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)):**

1. **Current Owners/Operators** (¬ß 9607(a)(1)): The current owner and operator of a vessel or facility
2. **Past Owners/Operators** (¬ß 9607(a)(2)): Any person who at the time of disposal of any hazardous substance owned or operated any facility at which such hazardous substances were disposed of
3. **Arrangers** (¬ß 9607(a)(3)): Any person who by contract, agreement, or otherwise arranged for disposal or treatment of hazardous substances owned or possessed by such person
4. **Transporters** (¬ß 9607(a)(4)): Any person who accepts or accepted any hazardous substances for transport to disposal or treatment facilities selected by such person¬≤

**Nature of CERCLA Liability:**

CERCLA imposes **strict liability**, meaning fault or negligence need not be proven. Liability attaches regardless of whether the disposal practices complied with regulations at the time.¬≥ Under common law principles incorporated into CERCLA, liability is presumed to be **joint and several** unless the harm is divisible.‚Å¥

**Joint and Several Liability - *Burlington Northern* Framework:**

In *Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. United States*, 556 U.S. 599 (2009), the Supreme Court held that while CERCLA does not expressly provide for joint and several liability, Congress intended the scope of liability to be determined from "traditional and evolving principles of common law."‚Åµ Under Restatement (Second) of Torts ¬ß 433A, apportionment is proper when there is "a reasonable basis" for determining the contribution of each cause to a single harm.‚Å∂

**Critical Implication for UESC:**
If UESC cannot prove divisibility of harm at each Superfund site, it faces joint and several liability for the entire cleanup cost, subject only to contribution claims against other PRPs under ¬ß 113(f). Given that UESC is alleged to be a generator/transporter (not the site owner/operator), proving divisibility will be challenging, particularly where multiple PRPs contributed similar wastes (TCE, VOCs) to commingled groundwater plumes.

#### 2. Arranger Liability - *Burlington Northern* Test

**Supreme Court Holding:**
In *Burlington Northern*, 556 U.S. at 610-11, the Supreme Court narrowed "arranger" liability under ¬ß 9607(a)(3), holding that an entity qualifies as an arranger "only when it takes intentional steps to dispose of a hazardous substance."‚Å∑ The Court rejected a strict liability approach that would hold parties liable merely because disposal was a "foreseeable byproduct" of a legitimate transaction.‚Å∏

**Application to UESC:**
UESC is alleged to have been a generator/transporter of TCE-contaminated waste at the Motorola 52nd Street and Tucson Airport sites between 1985-1995. The critical factual question is whether UESC:
- Intentionally arranged for disposal of waste at these sites, OR
- Engaged in legitimate transactions where disposal was merely a foreseeable byproduct

EPA will argue UESC selected the disposal sites and contracted for waste hauling, establishing arranger liability. UESC may defend by demonstrating it engaged third-party waste haulers who independently selected disposal sites (though this defense is weakened if UESC retained disposal site selection authority).

#### 3. Operator Liability - *United States v. Bestfoods*

**Supreme Court Framework:**
In *United States v. Bestfoods*, 524 U.S. 51 (1998), the Supreme Court established two paths to parent corporation liability under CERCLA:‚Åπ

1. **Derivative Liability**: When the corporate veil may be pierced, a parent corporation may be charged with derivative liability for its subsidiary's actions
2. **Direct Operator Liability**: A corporate parent that actively participated in and exercised control over the operations of the facility itself may be held directly liable as an "operator"

**Operator Standard:**
An operator must manage, direct, or conduct operations specifically related to the leakage or disposal of hazardous waste, or decisions about compliance with environmental regulations.¬π‚Å∞ General oversight of a subsidiary's business operations is insufficient; the parent must exercise control over the facility's environmental compliance functions.

**Application to UESC:**
This framework is critical for assessing successor liability for UESC's 14 acquired companies (1995-2022). If UESC merely acquired stock in entities that operated facilities, it may avoid operator liability unless it exercised day-to-day control over environmental compliance at those facilities. However, if UESC acquired assets and directly operated facilities, operator liability attaches regardless of corporate structure.

#### 4. Section 107 Cost Recovery vs. Section 113 Contribution

**Statutory Distinction:**
CERCLA provides two pathways for recovering cleanup costs from other PRPs, with significantly different procedural and substantive consequences established in *United States v. Atlantic Research Corp.*, 551 U.S. 128 (2007).¬π¬π

**Section 107(a) Cost Recovery Actions:**
- Available to "any person" who incurs response costs
- Imposes joint and several liability on defendant PRPs
- 6-year statute of limitations from initiation of remedial action¬π¬≤
- Plaintiff can recover full costs from any defendant, leaving allocation to contribution counterclaims
- Burden on defendant to prove divisibility (*Burlington Northern*)

**Section 113(f) Contribution Actions:**
- Available only to parties who have been sued under ¬ß 106 or ¬ß 107(a), or who have settled with the government
- Liability is several only (equitable allocation)
- 3-year statute of limitations from date of judgment or settlement¬π¬≥
- Court applies equitable factors (Gore Factors) to allocate shares among all PRPs
- Plaintiff recovers only equitable share, not full amount

**Critical Tactical Implication for UESC:**
At the Tucson Airport site, EPA filed a ¬ß 107 cost recovery lawsuit against UESC in 2022. This means:
- UESC faces joint and several liability for EPA's entire cleanup costs
- UESC can file ¬ß 113(f) contribution counterclaims against the other 89 PRPs
- UESC's ultimate liability depends on: (1) whether it can prove divisibility, and (2) equitable allocation in contribution among all viable PRPs
- Orphan share exposure: If other PRPs are insolvent/defunct, UESC may bear their shares under joint and several liability

**Settlement Incentive:**
UESC should prioritize settlement negotiations at Tucson Airport to convert from joint and several liability (¬ß 107) to fixed several liability (¬ß 113 settlement), capping exposure and eliminating orphan share risk.

### B. EPA Enforcement Mechanisms and Settlement Framework

#### 1. Unilateral Administrative Orders (UAOs) - Section 106

**Statutory Authority:**
CERCLA ¬ß 106(a) (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9606(a)) authorizes EPA to issue unilateral administrative orders (UAOs) compelling PRPs to conduct cleanup activities when EPA determines there is an "imminent and substantial endangerment" to public health or the environment.¬π‚Å¥

**UAO Enforcement Leverage:**
UAOs create extraordinary enforcement leverage for EPA because recipients face a "Hobson's choice":¬π‚Åµ
1. **Comply** with the UAO and conduct cleanup (even if the recipient disputes liability)
2. **Refuse** and face severe penalties:
   - Up to **$78,376 per day** civil penalties under ¬ß 106(b)(1) (2024 inflation-adjusted)¬π‚Å∂
   - **Treble damages** under ¬ß 107(c)(3) if non-compliance is deemed "willful" without "sufficient cause"¬π‚Å∑

**"Sufficient Cause" Defense:**
The Seventh and Ninth Circuits have held that "sufficient cause" exists when a PRP has a "reasonable, good-faith basis" for refusing compliance.¬π‚Å∏ A PRP need not ultimately prevail on its defense; it need only demonstrate "reasonable grounds to contest" the UAO.¬π‚Åπ However, courts apply this defense narrowly.

**Application to UESC:**
At the Motorola 52nd Street site, UESC was named in a 2019 UAO. UESC's compliance with that UAO was legally required to avoid treble damages, even if UESC disputed its allocation percentage. This compliance triggers ¬ß 113(f)(1) contribution rights against the other 46 PRPs.

#### 2. Section 107 Cost Recovery Lawsuits

**EPA Cost Recovery Actions:**
Under ¬ß 107(a)(4)(A), EPA can sue PRPs to recover costs the government has already incurred in conducting cleanups. These lawsuits impose **joint and several liability**, meaning EPA can recover its entire cleanup costs from any one defendant, leaving that defendant to pursue contribution claims against other PRPs.¬≤‚Å∞

**Tucson Airport Case Study:**
EPA filed a ¬ß 107 cost recovery lawsuit against UESC and 88 other PRPs in 2022 at the Tucson Airport site. Current EPA cost estimate: **$340M**. UESC's strategic options:
1. **Settle**: Negotiate fixed allocation percentage (estimated 5-15% = $17M-$51M)
2. **Litigate**: Face joint and several liability for full $340M, with burden on UESC to prove divisibility and pursue contribution against 88 other PRPs
3. **Risk**: Orphan share exposure if other PRPs are insolvent

**Recommended Strategy:**
Settlement is strongly advisable. Every dollar of cleanup costs EPA incurs while litigation proceeds increases UESC's joint and several exposure. Early settlement also caps liability and provides contribution protection under ¬ß 113(f)(2).

#### 3. Settlement Types and De Minimis Buyouts

**CERCLA ¬ß 122(g) De Minimis Settlements:**
EPA offers expedited settlement to PRPs whose contribution to site contamination is minimal in **volume and toxicity**. Standard de minimis criteria:¬≤¬π
- **Volumetric threshold**: 0.07% to 10% of total waste at site (site-specific)
- **Toxicity adjustment**: Non-hazardous or low-toxicity waste receives premium discount
- **Cash-out payment**: Typically 100-120% of volumetric allocation to account for transaction costs and orphan share contribution

**Typical Settlement Percentages (Industry Data):**
- **De minimis PRPs (< 1% allocation):** Settle for 110-130% of volumetric share
- **Small PRPs (1-5% allocation):** Settle for 100-115% of volumetric share
- **Major PRPs (> 20% allocation):** Settle for 90-105% of volumetric share (due to orphan share risk)

**Application to UESC:**
- **Motorola 52nd Street:** 2.3% allocation = potential de minimis settlement at ~$3.2M (110% √ó 2.3% √ó $125M)
- **Tucson Airport:** 5-15% allocation = too large for de minimis; standard settlement likely
- **Albuquerque South Valley:** 40-60% allocation as former operator = major PRP, significant orphan share risk

#### 4. Consent Decrees and Contribution Protection

**Section 113(f)(2) Contribution Protection:**
A PRP that enters into a consent decree or administrative settlement with EPA obtains **contribution protection** against claims by non-settling PRPs.¬≤¬≤ This is a critical benefit of settlement.

**Matters Addressed:**
The consent decree specifies "matters addressed" - the scope of liability resolved by the settlement. Contribution protection extends only to those matters. PRPs should negotiate broad "matters addressed" language to maximize protection.¬≤¬≥

**Practical Implication for UESC:**
If UESC settles the Tucson Airport site for $30M (mid-range of $17M-$51M estimate), UESC is fully protected from contribution claims by the other 88 PRPs. Without settlement, UESC faces contribution counterclaims if EPA obtains a judgment against UESC first and UESC then sues other PRPs under ¬ß 113(f)(1).

### C. Successor Liability in M&A Transactions

#### 1. Stock Purchase vs. Asset Purchase - Environmental Liability Transfer

**General Rule - Asset Purchases:**
Under traditional corporate law, an asset purchaser does **not** acquire the seller's liabilities unless one of four exceptions applies:¬≤‚Å¥
1. **Express or implied assumption** of liabilities by contract
2. **De facto merger** between buyer and seller
3. **Mere continuation** of seller's business
4. **Fraudulent conveyance** to escape liabilities

**Stock Purchase - Automatic Liability Transfer:**
In a stock purchase, the buyer acquires the entity that owns or operates the contaminated property. CERCLA ¬ß 107(a)(1) imposes strict liability on "current owners and operators," regardless of whether the current owner caused the contamination.¬≤‚Åµ **No exceptions exist for innocent purchasers** who acquire stock (except for limited bona fide prospective purchaser defenses under ¬ß 107(r), which require extensive due diligence and no affiliation with prior owners).¬≤‚Å∂

**Critical Implication for UESC Acquisition:**
If American Sustainability Partners acquires UESC via **stock purchase** (as anticipated), ASP automatically becomes liable as the "current owner" under ¬ß 107(a)(1) for:
- All three known Superfund sites
- Any unknown Superfund sites where UESC or its 14 acquired companies are later designated as PRPs
- Ongoing corrective action obligations (even if unrelated to Superfund)

**Rationale for Stock Purchase Despite Liability:**
Buyers typically prefer stock purchases for environmental services companies despite liability transfer because:
- **Operational continuity**: Preserves contracts, permits, franchises (critical for UESC's $320M Phoenix franchise)
- **Tax efficiency** (if ¬ß 338(h)(10) election available): Achieves tax benefits of asset sale with legal form of stock sale
- **Valuation**: Environmental liabilities are disclosed and priced into purchase price adjustment or indemnification

#### 2. Asset Purchase - Successor Liability Exceptions

**De Facto Merger Test:**
Courts apply a multi-factor test to determine whether an asset purchase constitutes a de facto merger, triggering successor liability:¬≤‚Å∑
- Continuity of management, personnel, physical location, assets, and general business operations
- Continuity of shareholders (seller's shareholders receive buyer's stock as consideration)
- Cessation of ordinary business and dissolution of seller shortly after sale
- Assumption of seller's obligations necessary for uninterrupted business continuity

**"Substantial Continuity" Test - *United States v. Mexico Feed & Seed***:
The Eighth Circuit held that successor liability attaches when there is "substantial continuity" of the enterprise, considering eight factors:¬≤‚Å∏
1. Retention of same employees
2. Retention of same supervisory personnel
3. Same production facilities in same location
4. Same products
5. Same customers
6. Same trade name
7. Continuity of assets
8. Continuity of general business operations

**Knowledge Requirement:**
Most circuits require that the asset purchaser had **knowledge** of the potential CERCLA liability **and** continued the polluting activity.¬≤‚Åπ If the purchaser had no notice and the seller ceased operations before the sale, successor liability generally does not attach.

**Application to UESC's 14 Acquired Companies (1995-2022):**
UESC acquired 14 companies over 27 years. Successor liability analysis depends on:
- **Structure**: Asset purchase or stock purchase?
- **Continuity**: Did UESC retain former owner's employees, facilities, operations?
- **Knowledge**: Did UESC conduct Phase I/II environmental assessments before acquisition?
- **Post-acquisition operations**: Did UESC continue hazardous waste disposal practices?

**Data Room Discovery Required:**

- Purchase structure (stock vs. asset)
- Environmental representations and warranties
- Environmental indemnification provisions

If any acquisitions were structured as asset purchases with substantial continuity, UESC may have successor liability for contamination caused by those predecessors.

#### 3. Contractual Allocation - Limits on Transferring Environmental Liability

**General Rule - Government Not Bound:**
Private parties **cannot** transfer CERCLA liability by contract in a manner that binds the government.¬≥‚Å∞ Even if the acquisition agreement allocates environmental liabilities to the seller, EPA can still pursue the buyer as the "current owner" under ¬ß 107(a)(1).

**Indemnification - Private Remedy Only:**
Indemnification provisions in acquisition agreements provide a **private remedy** between buyer and seller, but do not eliminate the buyer's direct liability to EPA.¬≥¬π If EPA sues the buyer, the buyer must:
1. Pay EPA's cleanup costs
2. Then seek indemnification from seller (if contractual right exists)
3. Bear risk of seller's insolvency or bankruptcy

**Escrow and Holdbacks:**
Sophisticated acquirers address environmental liability through:
- **Purchase price escrow** (5-10% of purchase price held 5-10 years)
- **Environmental liability cap** in indemnification (often 25-50% of purchase price)
- **Survival period** for environmental reps (typically 5-10 years vs. standard 18-24 months)
- **Representations & Warranties (R&W) insurance** with environmental sublimit

### D. PRP Allocation Methodologies and Orphan Share Risk

#### 1. Gore Factors for Equitable Allocation

**Section 113(f) Contribution Framework:**
When multiple PRPs are held jointly and severally liable, courts apply equitable factors to allocate responsibility among them. The six "Gore Factors" (named after Senator Al Gore) dominate allocation analysis:¬≥¬≤

1. **Ability to distinguish contribution**: Can each PRP's waste be separated from others?
2. **Volume of waste**: Quantity of hazardous substances contributed by each PRP
3. **Toxicity of waste**: Relative toxicity/risk profile of each PRP's waste
4. **Degree of involvement**: Generation, treatment, storage, disposal activities
5. **Waste management practices**: Compliance with regulations, care taken
6. **Cooperation with authorities**: Responsiveness to EPA, participation in cleanup

**Advanced Methodologies - Risk-Based Allocation:**
Recent cases have applied **risk magnitude** methodologies using GIS mapping and Monte Carlo analysis to allocate cleanup costs based on:¬≥¬≥
- **Remedy driver chemicals (RDCs)**: Which contaminants drive cleanup costs?
- **Spatial distribution**: Geographical extent of each PRP's contamination
- **Remediation difficulty**: Cost to remediate each PRP's waste stream
- **Correlation analysis**: Matching waste streams to specific PRPs using waste manifests

**Application to UESC Sites:**

**Motorola 52nd Street (2.3% allocation):**
- EPA allocated based on **volumetric analysis** of TCE waste transported 1988-1992
- UESC's 2.3% allocation appears reasonable given 47 PRPs and predominantly volumetric methodology
- Limited toxicity adjustment (TCE is uniform contaminant across all PRPs)

**Tucson Airport (5-15% estimated range):**
- EPA conducting waste-in analysis (ongoing)
- 89 PRPs including major defense contractors (Raytheon, Davis-Monthan AFB)
- UESC alleged transporter role (lower allocation than generator/operator)
- **Toxicity adjustment risk**: Aviation fuel waste may have higher remediation costs than industrial solvents

**Albuquerque South Valley (40-60% as former operator):**

#### 2. Orphan Share Exposure and Non-Settling PRP Risk

**Definition of Orphan Shares:**
"Orphan shares" are allocation percentages attributed to PRPs who are **insolvent, defunct, or judgment-proof**.¬≥‚Å¥ Under joint and several liability, orphan shares are reallocated among **viable PRPs** (those capable of paying).

**Reallocation Mechanisms:**

**Section 107 Actions (Joint and Several Liability):**
If EPA sues under ¬ß 107 and obtains a judgment for $340M against UESC and 88 other PRPs, but 40 PRPs are insolvent, UESC faces:¬≥‚Åµ
- **Initial allocation**: 10% = $34M
