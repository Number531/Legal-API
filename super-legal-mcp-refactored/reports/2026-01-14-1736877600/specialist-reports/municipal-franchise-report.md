### Case Law: Franchise Termination and Asset Stranding

**Material Breach Standards:**

Under Arizona contract law, "a material breach goes to the heart of the agreement and may justify termination, while a minor breach may only warrant damages."² Material breach examples in solid waste franchises: persistent missed pickups (>1% rate), safety violations (OSHA citations, spills), financial insolvency (failure to maintain insurance, bankruptcy), service abandonment (3+ days without notice), fraud/criminal conduct. Standard cure period: 10-60 days written notice.

**Competitive Bidding Requirements for Exclusive Franchises:**

Kennedy v. Prichard, 499 So. 2d 409 (Ala. 1986): Alabama Supreme Court held "an exclusive contract for wrecker service which failed to comply with the bid law was void as an unconstitutional grant of a special privilege."³ Beavers v. County of Walker, 645 So. 2d 1365 (Ala. 1994): "When a municipality will grant an exclusive right to a private business that will result in a benefit in excess of the bid law amount, then the right to conduct that exclusive business within the corporate limits must be bid."⁴

**Phoenix franchise ($320M annual revenue) far exceeds any bid law threshold; Arizona Constitution Article XIII, § 6 prohibits exclusive franchises that divest municipalities of regulatory control; Phoenix franchise renewal requires competitive RFP under A.R.S. § 34-201.**

**Asset Stranding and Compensation:**

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION:** Municipal solid waste franchise agreements typically do NOT require municipality to purchase franchisee assets upon natural expiration (as opposed to termination for convenience). Standard provision:

> "Upon expiration or termination of this Agreement, Contractor shall remove all Contractor-owned equipment and vehicles from the Service Area. Municipality has no obligation to compensate Contractor for capital investments or stranded assets."

**Exception:** If municipality terminates for convenience (without cause, prior to expiration), some agreements require fair market value compensation for undepreciated assets. Arizona franchise termination statute (A.R.S. § 28-4457) provides compensation framework for motor vehicle dealer franchises applicable by analogy: goodwill value, fair market value of special tools/equipment acquired within 3 years, reimbursement for facility upgrades within 3 years.⁵

**Phoenix Application:**
- **Scenario 1 (Natural Expiration Dec 31, 2029)**: Phoenix issues competitive RFP; UESC loses to Republic Services. Phoenix has NO OBLIGATION to compensate UESC for stranded $185.4M in assets. UESC must liquidate or redeploy; likely $85-110M write-down.
- **Scenario 2 (Early Termination Upon Change of Control Denial 2026)**: If Phoenix denies consent and treats acquisition as breach triggering termination, legal ambiguity whether this constitutes "termination for convenience" entitling UESC to asset buyback. Likely litigation; settlement range $50-100M compensation.

**Non-Renewal vs. Termination for Cause:**

Weller v. City of Gadsden, 138 Ala. 195 (1904): "A valid contract (franchise) entered into by a municipality cannot be repudiated at the whim of the governing body."⁶ However, Craig D. Corp. v. Atlantic-Richfield Co., 112 Wn 2d 574 (1993): "Franchisees are not insulated against adverse economic change upon non-renewal."⁷

**UESC has NO LEGAL RIGHT to renewal of Phoenix franchise upon December 31, 2029 expiration.** Phoenix may negotiate renewal, issue competitive RFP, or municipalize services. UESC's only remedy if franchise awarded to competitor: liquidate or redeploy $185.4M in assets; no compensation from Phoenix.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Arizona Municipal Franchise Legal Framework

#### 1. Constitutional Foundation: Arizona Constitution Article XIII

**Article XIII, Section 6 - Franchise Regulation**

Arizona Constitution Article XIII, Section 6 provides the constitutional foundation for municipal franchise regulation:

> "No grant, extension, or renewal of any franchise or other use of the streets, alleys, or other public grounds, or ways, of any municipality shall divest the state or any of its subdivisions of its or their control and regulation of such use and enjoyment; nor shall the power to regulate charges for public services be surrendered; and no exclusive franchise shall ever be granted."¹

**Key Constitutional Principles:**
- **No Perpetual Franchises**: The state and municipalities retain control and regulation over franchise rights, preventing permanent divestiture of regulatory authority
- **Rate Regulation Retained**: Municipalities cannot surrender the power to regulate charges for public services, even through franchise agreements
- **Prohibition on "Exclusive" Franchises (Literal Interpretation)**: While Section 6 states "no exclusive franchise shall ever be granted," this provision has been interpreted narrowly in practice; municipalities commonly grant de facto exclusive service territories for solid waste collection through zoning and franchise agreements, with exclusivity justified by operational efficiency and public health objectives²

**Practical Application to Solid Waste:**
- Arizona municipalities structure solid waste franchises as "non-exclusive" but designate service territories where only one franchisee operates, avoiding the literal constitutional prohibition while achieving operational exclusivity
- The Phoenix exclusive franchise ($320M annual revenue) likely operates under this framework: designated service area with single franchisee, but structured as "non-exclusive" to comply with Article XIII, Section 6

#### 2. Statutory Framework: Arizona Revised Statutes Title 34

**A.R.S. § 34-201 - Municipal Procurement and Competitive Bidding**

A.R.S. § 34-201 governs "Notice of intention to receive bids and enter contract; procedure; doing work without advertising for bids; county compliance."³ This statute applies to Arizona counties, cities, and towns for public works contracts including solid waste franchise agreements.

**Key Requirements:**

**Bid Security (A.R.S. § 34-201(A)):**
> "Every proposal shall be accompanied by a certified check, cashier's check or surety bond for ten percent of the amount of the bid as a guarantee that the contractor will enter into a contract to perform the proposal in accordance with the plans and specifications."⁴

**Surety Bond Standards:**
- Surety bonds must be executed by a surety company holding a certificate of authority to transact surety business in Arizona issued by the Director of the Department of Insurance and Financial Institutions pursuant to A.R.S. Title 20, Chapter 2, Article 1
- This requirement ensures financial capacity of bidders and protects municipalities from contractor default

#### 4. Standard Municipal Franchise Agreement Terms (Industry Analysis)

Based on EPA guidance and industry research, typical municipal solid waste franchise agreements include the following provisions:¹⁰

**Assignment and Change of Control Provisions:**
- **Prohibition on Assignment Without Consent**: "Contractor shall not assign this Agreement to any other entity without the Municipality's prior written consent, which consent may be withheld in Municipality's sole discretion"¹¹
- **Change of Control as Deemed Assignment**: "Any change in ownership or control of Contractor, including sale of majority equity interest, merger, or acquisition, shall be deemed an assignment requiring Municipality's prior written consent"¹²
- **Continued Liability**: "Even when assignment is permitted, Contractor remains primarily responsible for performance and liable for any breach"¹³

- Each municipality has discretion to withhold consent, though unreasonable withholding may be challenged
- Standard practice: Acquirer seeks pre-closing consent letters from municipalities, often requiring demonstrations of financial capacity, operational competence, and service continuity plans

**Termination Rights:**

**Termination for Cause:**
- Municipalities may terminate for contractor failure, service violations, or material breach
- **Notice and Cure Period**: Typically 10-60 days written notice with opportunity to cure¹⁴
- Material breach examples: persistent missed pickups (>1% miss rate), safety violations, financial insolvency, failure to maintain required insurance
- **No Cure for Certain Violations**: Fraud, criminal conduct, abandonment of service may trigger immediate termination

**Termination for Convenience:**
- Many franchises allow municipality to terminate without cause upon specified notice (e.g., 24 months) plus compensation for undepreciated assets
- **Fair Market Value Compensation**: Municipality must pay franchisee for trucks, containers, and other assets stranded by termination
- **Estimated Phoenix FMV**: 850 residential/commercial trucks @ $180K average = $153M book value, less accumulated depreciation = $85-110M FMV

**Termination Upon Expiration:**
- Franchises typically have defined terms (5-10 years) with optional renewal periods
- **No Automatic Renewal**: Expiration requires competitive RFP or negotiated renewal
- **Transition Obligations**: Outgoing franchisee must cooperate with successor for 60-90 days

**Exclusive Franchise Provisions:**
- Municipalities grant exclusive rights to "qualified solid waste enterprises for collection and recovery of solid waste from all residential, industrial and commercial premises" within defined service territory¹⁵
- Exclusivity limited to 5-10 year terms, with extensions subject to performance review
- **Public Health Justification**: Exclusive franchises prevent "double running" of routes, reduce truck traffic, improve service accountability

**Performance Standards:**
- Missed pickup thresholds (typically 0.5% of scheduled pickups allowed)
- Customer complaint rates (typically <5 complaints per 1,000 customers monthly)
- Fleet age and environmental standards (maximum 10-year vehicle age; CNG or electric vehicle percentages)
- Safety record (OSHA violations, traffic accidents, spills)
- Recycling contamination rates (typically <15% contamination threshold)

**Rate Adjustment Mechanisms:**
- **Consumer Price Index (CPI) Adjustments**: Annual adjustments tied to CPI-U or regional CPI
- **Fuel Surcharges**: Pass-through for diesel fuel price increases above baseline
- **Disposal Fee Adjustments**: Pass-through for landfill tipping fee increases
- **Scope Changes**: Adjustments for added services (e.g., organics collection, bulky item pickup)
- **Regulatory Compliance Costs**: Pass-through for new mandates (e.g., California Advanced Clean Fleets rule)

**Capital Investment Obligations:**
- Franchisees must invest in trucks, containers, facilities to meet service requirements
- **Asset Stranding Risk**: If franchise not renewed, franchisee bears risk of unrecovered capital investments
- **Phoenix Example**: UESC likely invested $150-200M in trucks, containers, facilities for Phoenix operations; non-renewal in 2029 could strand $85-110M in undepreciated assets

### C. Case Law: Municipal Franchise Terminations & Non-Renewals

#### 1. Material Breach Standards

**Arizona Contract Law Principles:**

Under Arizona contract law, "a material breach goes to the heart of the agreement and may justify termination, while a minor breach may only warrant damages."³⁴ A material breach is one that "deprives the non-breaching party of the benefit they expected from the contract."³⁵

**Termination Rights:**
"The right to terminate or cancel the contract if the other party has significantly breached it relieves both parties of their future obligations under the agreement."³⁶

**Cure Period Requirements:**

Federal courts applying Arizona law have held that "a contractual notice requirement applies only to defaults that could reasonably be cured within the time allowed; it does not preclude immediate termination for a 'vital breach' that could not be cured within the time period."³⁷

**Application to Municipal Solid Waste Franchises:**

Municipal code provisions typically provide that "failure by franchisee to provide necessary solid waste disposal service or otherwise comply with the provisions of applicable regulations shall be grounds for termination, modification, suspension or revocation of the franchise."³⁸ Standard cure period: 10-60 days written notice identifying deficiencies.³⁹

**Material Breach Examples in Solid Waste Franchises:**
- **Persistent Missed Pickups**: >1% miss rate over consecutive months
- **Safety Violations**: OSHA citations; hazardous material spills; traffic accidents caused by negligent drivers
- **Financial Insolvency**: Failure to maintain required insurance coverage; bankruptcy filing; inability to pay disposal fees
- **Service Abandonment**: Failure to provide service for 3+ consecutive days without notice
- **Fraud or Criminal Conduct**: Bribery of municipal officials; illegal dumping; falsification of recycling diversion reports

#### 2. Competitive Bidding Requirements for Exclusive Franchises

**Constitutional Foundation:**

Several state courts have held that exclusive municipal franchises must comply with competitive bidding requirements or risk constitutional invalidation.

**Kennedy v. Prichard, 499 So. 2d 409 (Ala. 1986):**⁴⁰

The Alabama Supreme Court held "an exclusive contract for wrecker service which failed to comply with the bid law was void as an unconstitutional grant of a special privilege."

**Beavers v. County of Walker, 645 So. 2d 1365 (Ala. 1994):**⁴¹

Alabama Supreme Court held that "when a municipality will grant an exclusive right to a private business that will result in a benefit to the private business in excess of the bid law amount, then the right to conduct that exclusive business within the corporate limits must be bid."

**Application to Phoenix Franchise:**

Arizona Constitution Article XIII, § 6 prohibits exclusive franchises that divest the state or municipalities of regulatory control.⁴² Municipalities structure solid waste franchises as "non-exclusive" with designated service territories to comply with constitutional requirements while achieving operational exclusivity.

Phoenix franchise ($320M annual revenue) far exceeds any bid law threshold, requiring competitive RFP under A.R.S. § 34-201 for initial award and renewals.

#### 3. Franchise Termination Compensation: Fair Market Value of Assets

**Arizona Franchise Termination Statute (Motor Vehicle Dealers):**

A.R.S. § 28-4457 governs termination of motor vehicle dealer franchises and provides compensation framework applicable by analogy to other franchise contexts:⁴³

**Fair Market Value Components:**
- "Goodwill value of the franchisee's franchise in the community"
- "Fair market value of all undamaged special tools, equipment and signage required by the franchisor and acquired within three years before termination"
- "Reimbursement for facility upgrades and renovations required by the franchisor within three years before termination"
- "Payment equal to twelve months' rent at fair market rental value for the real property"

**California Franchise Relations Act (Broader Protection):**⁴⁴

If a franchisor "terminates or fails to renew a franchisee in violation of the law, the aggrieved franchisee may recover 'the fair market value of the franchised business and franchise assets,' as well as any other damages."

California law requires franchisors to purchase "all inventory, supplies, equipment, fixtures, and furnishings purchased or paid for" by the franchisee if those purchases were made in accordance with franchise agreement requirements.⁴⁵

**Application to Phoenix Franchise Non-Renewal:**

#### 4. Non-Renewal vs. Termination for Cause: Key Distinctions

**Weller v. City of Gadsden, 138 Ala. 195 (1904):**⁴⁶

"A valid contract (franchise) entered into by a municipality cannot be repudiated at the whim of the governing body of the municipality." The city had entered a 30-year franchise with plaintiff to construct a water works system. A subsequent council, before construction began, passed an ordinance repealing the franchise. The court upheld the franchise, finding the city could not unilaterally repudiate a vested contractual right.

**Application:**

- **Mid-Term Termination Without Cause**: If municipality terminates franchise prior to expiration without demonstrating material breach, franchisee may challenge termination as breach of contract and seek damages (lost profits for remaining term + asset stranding).

- **Non-Renewal Upon Expiration**: If franchise expires naturally at end of term and municipality elects not to renew (or awards franchise to competitor through RFP), franchisee has **no contractual right to renewal** unless agreement contains automatic renewal provisions. "Franchisees are not insulated against adverse economic change" (Craig D. Corp. v. Atlantic-Richfield Co., 112 Wn 2d 574 (1993)).⁴⁷

**Phoenix Franchise Non-Renewal Risk (December 2029):**

UESC has **no legal right to renewal** of Phoenix franchise upon December 31, 2029 expiration. Phoenix may elect to:
1. Negotiate renewal with UESC (avoid RFP process)
2. Issue competitive RFP and award to UESC or competitor based on evaluation criteria
3. Municipalize services (convert to city employees; eliminate private franchise)

UESC's only remedy if Phoenix awards franchise to Republic Services: liquidate or redeploy $185.4M in stranded assets; no compensation from Phoenix unless agreement contains unusual "asset buyback upon non-renewal" provision (unlikely).

#### 5. Lost Profits and Royalties in Franchise Termination Cases

**McAlpine (AAMCO case):**⁴⁸

Court awarded "lost future royalties for ten franchises for a period of four years based on wrongful termination." This remedy available only when franchisor wrongfully terminates franchise prior to expiration in breach of agreement.

**Measure of Damages (Wrongful Termination):**

Franchisee may recover:
- **Lost Profits**: Net income franchisee would have earned over remaining franchise term, discounted to present value
- **Asset Stranding**: Fair market value of undepreciated assets less salvage value
- **Transition Costs**: Costs incurred to wind down operations (employee severance, lease termination penalties)

**Application to Phoenix:**

If Phoenix wrongfully terminates UESC franchise prior to December 31, 2029 expiration **without demonstrating material breach**, UESC's damages:

**Lost Profits (2026-2029, if terminated early upon change of control):**
- $320M annual revenue × 28-32% EBITDA margin = $89.6-102.4M annual EBITDA
- 3.5 years remaining (mid-2026 to Dec 2029) × $96M average EBITDA = $336M gross lost profits
- Discounted to present value @ 8% discount rate = $288M NPV
- Less mitigation (UESC's duty to mitigate by redeploying assets to other markets) = $200-250M net damages

**Asset Stranding:** $85-110M (as calculated above)

**Total Damages (Wrongful Early Termination):** $285-360M

**HOWEVER:** If Phoenix terminates **for cause** (material breach) OR allows franchise to expire naturally (December 31, 2029), UESC has **no damages claim** for lost profits or asset stranding.
