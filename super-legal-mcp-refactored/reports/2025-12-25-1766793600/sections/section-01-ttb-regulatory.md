# SECTION I: FEDERAL TTB REGULATORY COMPLIANCE

The $1.2 billion acquisition of Artisan Spirits & Brewing Company, LLC (ASBC) presents material compliance requirements under the Federal Alcohol Administration Act and Internal Revenue Code administered by the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB). This section analyzes permit transfer procedures for ASBC's five production facilities (three breweries, one distilled spirits plant, one winery), formula approval requirements affecting 42 spirits formulations, Certificate of Label Approval (COLA) re-application requirements for 280 approved labels, and tied house law compliance affecting 18 taprooms and Oregon self-distribution operations. The analysis identifies one **CRITICAL** regulatory exposure: an ongoing TTB investigation into ASBC's Single Barrel Bourbon product using toasted wood staves during aging, presenting $1.45 million to $2.66 million in probable financial exposure (70-80% likelihood of formula denial) based on controlling bourbon standards of identity and established TTB precedent. ## A. TTB Regulatory Framework and Jurisdiction

The TTB exercises plenary authority over alcohol beverage production, labeling, and distribution pursuant to the Federal Alcohol Administration Act (FAA Act), codified at 27 U.S.C. § 201 *et seq*., and Internal Revenue Code provisions governing distilled spirits (26 U.S.C. §§ 5001-5692), wine (26 U.S.C. §§ 5041-5392), and beer (26 U.S.C. §§ 5051-5417).¹ The agency's implementing regulations appear at 27 C.F.R. Parts 1-40, establishing comprehensive qualification requirements for permit holders, standards of identity for alcohol beverage products, labeling approval procedures, tax obligations, and three-tier distribution system compliance.² TTB jurisdiction extends to all aspects of ASBC's operations across five production facilities and 18 consumer-facing taprooms.

The FAA Act authorizes TTB to regulate industry members "engaged in business as a distiller, brewer, rectifier, blender, or other producer, or as an importer or wholesaler" of beverage alcohol.³ ASBC qualifies as an industry member operating under three distinct permit categories: brewer (three facilities producing 525,000 barrels annually), distiller (DSP-OR-20145 producing 100,000 proof gallons annually), and winery/meadery (Hood River, Oregon facility).⁴ Each permit category imposes distinct qualification requirements, operational restrictions, tax obligations, and compliance monitoring protocols that survive ownership changes only through formal TTB-approved transfer procedures.⁵

Recent TTB modernization efforts—including comprehensive labeling regulation reorganization (2020-2022), establishment of American Single Malt Whisky standards of identity (effective January 19, 2025), brewery qualification streamlining (2022), and winery permit modernization (2024)—demonstrate the agency's commitment to regulatory flexibility while maintaining strict adherence to statutory product standards and consumer protection mandates.⁶ Notably absent from this modernization wave: any amendment to bourbon whisky standards of identity codified at 27 C.F.R. § 5.143, which maintain absolute prohibition on adding "coloring, flavoring, or blending materials" to products designated as bourbon.⁷ This regulatory stasis creates material risk for ASBC's wood stave-finished bourbon production, as analyzed in Section I.C below.

## B. Permit Transfer Requirements: Non-Transferability and Successor Qualification Mandates

### 1. Federal Alcohol Administration Act Basic Permits

TTB basic permits issued under the FAA Act are categorically non-transferable by operation of federal regulation. The controlling provision, 27 C.F.R. § 1.44, provides: "No basic permit shall be leased, sold, or otherwise voluntarily transferred, and, in the event of such lease, sale, or other voluntary transfer, such basic permit shall automatically terminate thereupon."⁸ This automatic termination rule applies whenever "actual or legal control of the permittee is acquired" whether directly or indirectly, through stock ownership or other means.⁹ The regulation creates a 30-day grace period: if the transferee or permittee files an application for a new basic permit within 30 days of the ownership change, the outstanding permit continues in effect until TTB takes final action on the new application.¹⁰

The $1.2 billion acquisition of ASBC triggers the automatic termination provision, requiring the acquirer to file new basic permit applications for all five ASBC production facilities within 30 days of closing to maintain operational continuity.¹¹ Failure to file within this 30-day window results in immediate permit termination and cessation of all production, bottling, and distribution activities pending new permit issuance.¹² This creates quantifiable revenue exposure: with annual production of 525,000 barrels of beer and 100,000 proof gallons of spirits generating estimated annual revenue of $120-150 million, each month of production shutdown represents $10-12.5 million in lost revenue opportunity.¹³

Concurrent with permit applications, acquirer must notify TTB of all changes in ownership, management, and control pursuant to 27 C.F.R. § 1.42, including names and addresses of all new persons participating in ownership, management, or control of the business.¹⁴ For corporate acquirers, this extends to officers, directors, and any persons owning or controlling more than 10% of voting stock.¹⁵ TTB uses this information to conduct background investigations examining criminal history, prior alcohol beverage law violations, tax compliance, and financial qualifications to operate under federal permit.¹⁶

## C. Bourbon Standards of Identity: Toasted Wood Stave Investigation (CRITICAL RISK)

### 1. Regulatory Framework for Bourbon Whisky

Federal standards of identity for bourbon whisky appear at 27 C.F.R. § 5.143(c), relocated from former 27 C.F.R. § 5.22 during TTB's 2020-2022 labeling regulation reorganization (substantive requirements unchanged).⁵² Bourbon whisky is defined as whisky produced:

1. At not exceeding 160° proof from a fermented mash of not less than 51% corn grain;
2. Stored at not more than 125° proof;
3. In charred new oak containers.⁵³

"Straight bourbon whisky" requires storage in charred new oak containers for two years or more.⁵⁴ These production requirements are mandatory but not alone sufficient for bourbon designation. The critical regulatory prohibition appears in the standards of identity for whisky generally: **"No coloring, flavoring, or blending materials may be used in the production of spirits designated as 'bourbon whisky' in accordance with § 5.22(b)(1)(i) or 'straight' whisky."**⁵⁵

This prohibition is absolute. Unlike vodka (which may be treated with sugar and citric acid) or whisky specialties (which permit harmless coloring, flavoring, or blending materials), bourbon whisky admits zero additives beyond water reduction for bottling proof.⁵⁶ The regulatory text provides no exceptions, safe harbors, or de minimis thresholds. Any addition of coloring, flavoring, or blending materials categorically disqualifies a product from bourbon designation, requiring reclassification as a whisky specialty with mandatory label disclosures.⁵⁷

### 2. Treatment with Wood: Regulatory Classification as "Flavoring"

The term "coloring, flavoring, or blending material" explicitly includes wood treatment beyond standard barrel aging. Section 5.39(c) addresses "Treatment with wood," specifying that distilled spirits treated with wood (including oak chips, staves, or other wood additives) during distillation or storage—other than through normal contact with the oak container in which the spirits are stored—require label disclosure stating "colored and flavored with wood (insert chips, slabs, etc., as appropriate)" as part of the class and type designation.⁵⁸

TTB treats wood staves inserted into barrels during aging as "added HCFBM (harmless coloring, flavoring, or blending materials)" subject to formula approval requirements.⁵⁹ Products receiving wood treatment during aging must be labeled as whiskey specialties rather than bourbon, with front-label disclosure of the wood treatment process.⁶⁰ TTB allows producers to reference "bourbon" on specialty whiskey labels "as long as it is followed with the statement of what was done to it that made it no longer Bourbon," with this disclosure appearing on the front label.⁶¹ Example compliant labels include: "Bourbon Whiskey Finished with Oak Staves" or "Bourbon Whiskey - Colored and Flavored with Wood Staves."⁶²

### 3. Maker's Mark 46 Precedent: Definitive TTB Position on Wood Staves

The dispositive precedent for ASBC's bourbon formula issue is Maker's Mark 46, a premium whiskey product using **seared French oak staves** inserted into barrels during the aging process—a production method directly analogous to ASBC's Single Barrel Bourbon using **toasted wood staves**.⁶³ Despite being produced by one of the most prominent bourbon distilleries using traditional bourbon mash bill and aging in new charred oak barrels, Maker's Mark 46 is **not labeled as bourbon** and cannot be marketed as bourbon under TTB regulations.⁶⁴

Maker's Mark 46 is classified as a **"Whisky Finished with Oak Staves"** and falls under the whiskey specialty category at 27 C.F.R. § 5.39(c).⁶⁵ The product label explicitly discloses the wood stave finishing process, stating "Finished with French Oak Staves" as part of the class and type designation.⁶⁶ This classification reflects TTB's definitive position that wood staves—whether seared, toasted, or otherwise treated—constitute "added flavoring" prohibited under bourbon standards of identity.⁶⁷

The Maker's Mark 46 precedent is particularly instructive because:
- The base spirit qualifies as bourbon before stave insertion (≥51% corn, new charred oak aging, proper proof limits)
- The staves are oak (the same wood species required for bourbon barrels)
- The staves undergo heat treatment (searing) similar to barrel charring
- Maker's Mark is an established, well-capitalized distiller with sophisticated regulatory counsel⁶⁸

If TTB required Maker's Mark—a flagship bourbon brand with substantial regulatory expertise—to reclassify a stave-finished product as whiskey specialty, TTB will almost certainly apply the same standard to ASBC's toasted stave bourbon.⁶⁹ No published TTB rulings, industry guidance, or regulatory amendments since Maker's Mark 46's introduction suggest any relaxation of this classification requirement.⁷⁰

### 4. ASBC Single Barrel Bourbon: Factual Background

TTB received a competitor complaint in September 2024 alleging that ASBC's Single Barrel Bourbon product violates bourbon standards of identity by using **toasted wood staves** during the aging process to enhance flavor profile.⁷¹ The complaint alleges these staves constitute "added flavoring" explicitly prohibited under 27 C.F.R. § 5.143 for bourbon-designated products.⁷² TTB opened a formal investigation following the complaint, with determination expected during Q1 2026.⁷³

ASBC maintains that the toasted wood staves are part of the permitted "new charred oak barrel" aging process rather than separate added flavoring.⁷⁴ This position lacks regulatory support. The bourbon standard requires aging "in charred new oak containers"—a singular container requirement, not a hybrid container-plus-supplemental-wood approach.⁷⁵ The regulatory text provides no exception for additional oak contact beyond the container itself, and TTB's treatment with wood provisions at § 5.39(c) explicitly classify such supplemental wood contact as "flavoring" requiring special labeling.⁷⁶

Current Single Barrel Bourbon inventory totals **$4 million** at cost, representing finished goods inventory available for sale plus aging barrel inventory.⁷⁷ All units in this inventory were produced using the toasted wood stave method under investigation, creating uniform exposure across the entire product line.⁷⁸ ASBC filed TTB Form 5100.51 (Application for and Certification/Exemption of Formula) for the Single Barrel Bourbon formula, which TTB approved at the time of initial production.⁷⁹ The current investigation represents a potential formula approval revocation based on alleged misclassification of the product as bourbon rather than whiskey specialty.⁸⁰

### 7. Criminal Penalty Exposure (26 U.S.C. § 5687)

In addition to civil penalties under the FAA Act, criminal penalties apply under the Internal Revenue Code. - Fined not more than $1,000, or
- Imprisoned not more than one year, or

TTB rarely pursues criminal prosecution for standards of identity violations absent evidence of intentional fraud or consumer harm.¹¹¹ ASBC's prior formula approval and absence of evidence suggesting intentional misbranding make criminal prosecution unlikely (probability <5%).¹¹² However, the statutory maximum $1,000 fine per violation remains a theoretical exposure point for each improperly labeled product unit.¹¹³ With $4 million inventory potentially representing 40,000-80,000 bottles (depending on product mix of 750ml vs. 1L packaging), theoretical maximum criminal penalty exposure reaches $40-80 million if TTB pursued per-bottle prosecution—an extreme scenario without precedent in TTB enforcement history.¹¹⁴

## D. Certificate of Label Approval (COLA) Re-Application Requirements

### 1. COLA Non-Transferability Upon Ownership Change

TTB regulations define the COLA "certificate holder" as the permittee or brewer whose name, address, and basic permit number, plant registry number, or brewer's notice number appears on the approved TTB Form 5100.31.¹²⁴ Because all ASBC COLAs identify ASBC as the certificate holder with ASBC's permit information, these approvals cannot be transferred to the acquiring entity following the ownership change.¹²⁵

When proprietorship change occurs, "the certificate holder information (name, address, permit number) on the original COLA is no longer accurate," requiring the new proprietor to file new COLA applications with updated permit holder information.¹²⁶ This requirement extends to all **280 approved COLAs** in ASBC's product portfolio, covering beer labels (breweries), spirits labels (distillery), and wine/mead labels (winery).¹²⁷

TTB's COLA re-application requirement following ownership change serves important regulatory purposes:
- Verifies new permit holder qualifications and compliance status
- Ensures label accuracy reflecting current responsible party for regulatory violations
- Creates opportunity for TTB to review labels under current regulatory standards
- Establishes new certificate holder's responsibility for label compliance¹²⁸

Unlike permit transfers (which may be filed conditionally pre-closing), COLA applications require active permit status, creating sequential dependency: acquirer must receive permit approvals before filing COLA applications.¹²⁹ This sequence extends total timeline for full operational continuity to 120-180 days post-closing (60-90 days for permits plus 60-90 days for COLAs).¹³⁰

### 2. COLA Re-Application Process and Timeline

TTB accepts COLA applications via two methods:
- **COLAs Online electronic system** (https://www.ttb.gov/colasonline/) - preferred method, faster processing
- Paper TTB Form 5100.31 submission (duplicate copies required) - legacy method, slower processing¹³¹

Electronic submissions via COLAs Online typically process in **15-45 days** depending on application completeness, label complexity, formula approval status, and current TTB workload.¹³² Paper submissions experience longer processing times of 30-90 days.¹³³ For ASBC's 280 COLA re-applications, exclusive use of electronic submission is essential to minimize timeline exposure.¹³⁴

## E. Tied House Law Compliance: Three-Tier System Requirements

### 1. Federal Tied House Prohibition Framework

The FAA Act Section 105(b)(3), codified at 27 U.S.C. § 205, prohibits producers and wholesalers from inducing retailers to purchase products to the exclusion of competitors—the anti-"tied house" provision designed to prevent vertical integration that dominated the pre-Prohibition alcohol industry.¹⁵⁷ TTB's implementing regulations at 27 C.F.R. Part 6 elaborate this prohibition, defining unlawful tied house practices including:

- Direct or indirect financial interest in retail establishments
- Furnishing, giving, renting, lending, or selling equipment, fixtures, signs, or supplies to retailers
- Extending credit beyond lawful limits
- Paying for advertising on behalf of retailers
- Providing free products beyond permissible samples
- Any inducement to purchase products to the exclusion of competitors¹⁵⁸

The regulations define "industry members" subject to these prohibitions as persons engaged in business as distillers, brewers, rectifiers, blenders, other producers, importers, or wholesalers of distilled spirits, wine, or malt beverages.¹⁵⁹ ASBC qualifies as an industry member operating production facilities, subjecting all ASBC operations to federal tied house restrictions.¹⁶⁰

The tied house prohibition serves three policy objectives:
1. **Competition Protection:** Prevents producers from controlling retail shelf space and consumer access
2. **Consumer Protection:** Ensures retailer independence in product selection serving consumer interests
3. **Tax Compliance:** Maintains clear separation between regulated tiers facilitating tax collection and enforcement¹⁶¹

Penalties for tied house violations include:
- Federal: Basic permit suspension or revocation, cease and desist orders, civil penalties under 27 U.S.C. § 207
- Criminal: Fines and potential imprisonment for willful violations
- State: License suspension/revocation, state fines, exclusion from state markets¹⁶²

### 2. On-Premise Consumption Exception: ASBC's 18 Taprooms

Federal regulations provide critical exception to tied house prohibitions for on-premise consumption at production facilities. Section 25.25 of 27 C.F.R. authorizes brewers to use brewery premises for other purposes upon TTB approval, including "operation of a tavern on brewery premises."¹⁶³ This exception permits producers to maintain 100% ownership interest in retail operations co-located with production facilities, selling directly to consumers without violating tied house prohibitions.¹⁶⁴

ASBC operates **18 taprooms** serving beer, spirits, and wine directly to consumers.¹⁶⁵ These taprooms likely qualify for the on-premise consumption exception if they are:
- **Co-located** with ASBC production facilities (same physical location or adjacent properties)
- Licensed as part of the brewery/distillery/winery premises rather than separate retail locations
- Operating under the production facility's TTB permit rather than separate wholesale/retail permits¹⁶⁶

**Critical Unknown:** The research materials do not specify whether all 18 taprooms are co-located with ASBC's five production facilities (3 breweries, 1 distillery, 1 winery/meadery) or whether some taprooms operate at separate locations distant from production.¹⁶⁷ If any taprooms operate as standalone retail locations not co-located with production:

- **Federal tied house issue:** Producer financial interest in off-premises retailer violates 27 C.F.R. Part 6
- **Remediation required:** Corporate restructuring to separate taproom ownership from production entities, or closure of off-premises taprooms
- **State law variation:** Some states permit producer-owned retail locations under specific statutory exceptions; state-by-state analysis required¹⁶⁸

**Severity Rating: LOW** (assuming all 18 taprooms are co-located with production facilities and qualify for federal exception).¹⁶⁹

**Probability Assessment:** 5-10% probability that one or more taprooms operate as off-premises retail locations creating tied house compliance issue; 90-95% probability all taprooms qualify for on-premise exception.¹⁷⁰

**Recommendation:** During due diligence, obtain:
- TTB license documentation for each taproom showing classification as on-premise retail or production premises extension
- Lease agreements or property records confirming co-location with production facilities
- State ABC licenses showing license classification for each taproom¹⁷¹

If any off-premises taprooms are identified, assess remediation cost (corporate restructuring legal fees estimated $50,000-$150,000) versus closure cost (lost revenue from affected locations).¹⁷²

### 3. Self-Distribution Exception: Oregon Direct-to-Retailer Sales

ASBC self-distributes **15% of Oregon production volume** (approximately 10,875 barrels annually based on 72,500 barrels total Oregon brewery production) directly to retailers without using third-party wholesaler intermediaries.¹⁷³ This practice raises tied house compliance questions under both federal and state three-tier system laws.

**Federal Law Analysis:**

Federal tied house regulations do not categorically prohibit brewery self-distribution.¹⁷⁴ The FAA Act treats brewers as manufacturers, and federal law authorizes limited self-distribution (direct sales to retailers without wholesaler intermediary) subject to state law restrictions.¹⁷⁵ Federal tied house prohibitions target producer financial interests in **retailers** and provision of things of value creating exclusivity arrangements; direct sales from producer to retailer constitute normal commercial transactions absent exclusivity inducements.¹⁷⁶

**State Law Dependency:**

Oregon state law governs whether ASBC's self-distribution complies with Oregon's three-tier system. State three-tier laws vary significantly:
- Some states follow federal model permitting brewery self-distribution for producers under specified volume thresholds
- Other states require strict three-tier separation with mandatory wholesaler intermediary
- Threshold limits vary (e.g., California permits self-distribution for breweries producing <60,000 barrels annually; Oregon limits unknown from research)¹⁷⁷

**Critical Unknown:** Research materials do not specify:
- Oregon statutory provisions governing brewery self-distribution
- Volume thresholds or production capacity limits for self-distribution qualification
- Geographic restrictions on self-distribution within Oregon
- Entity type restrictions (LLC vs. corporation vs. sole proprietor)
- Whether ASBC holds Oregon self-distribution license or operates under standard brewery license¹⁷⁸

**Risk if Oregon Law Prohibits Self-Distribution at ASBC's Volume:**

- **License Violation:** Operating without proper wholesale distribution license; OLCC (Oregon Liquor and Cannabis Commission) enforcement action
- **Civil Penalties:** Oregon administrative fines for three-tier violations
- **License Suspension:** Potential brewery license suspension during compliance remediation
- **Forced Transition:** Requirement to engage third-party distributor for all retail sales, losing 15% direct-to-retailer margin (estimated $500,000-$1 million annual margin impact based on approximately 10,875 self-distributed barrels × $25-40 per barrel margin advantage)¹⁷⁹

**Severity Rating: MEDIUM** (if Oregon law prohibits self-distribution at current volume, remediation requires operational restructuring with quantifiable margin impact).¹⁸⁰

**Probability Assessment:** 35-45% probability that Oregon law prohibits or restricts self-distribution at ASBC's current production volume, requiring remediation; 55-65% probability self-distribution is fully compliant under Oregon law.¹⁸¹ This assessment reflects:
- Most states permit limited brewery self-distribution below threshold limits
- Oregon's reputation as brewery-friendly regulatory environment
- Absence of evidence in research materials suggesting ASBC operates self-distribution unlawfully
- However, 350,000-barrel Portland brewery capacity may exceed typical small brewery self-distribution thresholds¹⁸²

**Recommendation:** Retain Oregon alcohol beverage counsel to provide legal opinion addressing:

**Priority 9 (HIGH): Bourbon Production Cessation**
- **Action:** Immediately halt all Single Barrel Bourbon production using toasted wood staves pending TTB formula determination
- **Rationale:** Continued production during investigation increases inventory at risk and may be viewed negatively by TTB if formula ultimately denied
- **Alternative:** Continue producing bourbon WITHOUT toasted wood staves (traditional bourbon process), creating compliant inventory for sale during investigation period
- **Timeline:** Maintain production halt until Q1 2026 TTB determination or earlier if expedited approval received²⁰⁴

- **Action:** Monitor Federal Register for final rule on Alcohol Facts labeling (proposed January 17, 2025)

## I. Conclusion

Federal TTB regulatory compliance requirements for the ASBC acquisition present one CRITICAL risk (bourbon formula investigation, $1.45M-$2.66M exposure at 70-80% probability), two HIGH risks (permit transfer operational continuity, $5.75M-$11.5M revenue exposure at 30-40% probability if not properly managed; COLA re-application burden, $280K-$560K certain cost), and multiple MEDIUM and LOW risks aggregating to $2.38 million to $4.62 million probability-weighted expected exposure across all categories. With structured pre-closing planning—including expedited bourbon formula determination request, 90-120 day advance permit filing, surety bond pre-qualification, and Oregon three-tier compliance opinion—these risks are manageable within the scope of a $1.2 billion acquisition. The bourbon formula issue represents genuine regulatory uncertainty requiring either pre-closing TTB resolution or substantial escrow protection ($2.66 million for 18 months) to allocate risk appropriately between parties. All other TTB compliance matters are procedural requirements with quantifiable costs and timelines amenable to detailed implementation planning and purchase agreement covenant structuring.
