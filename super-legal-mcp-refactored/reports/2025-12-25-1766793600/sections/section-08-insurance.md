#### 2. Coverage Trigger Analysis — Occurrence-Based Policy

General liability policies employ either occurrence-based or claims-made coverage triggers. The standard ISO CGL form CG 00 01 is an occurrence-based policy that covers "bodily injury" or "property damage" that occurs during the policy period, regardless of when the claim is made or lawsuit is filed.⁸ The occurrence trigger contrasts with claims-made policies commonly used for professional liability, directors and officers, and employment practices liability coverage, which require both the wrongful act and the claim to occur during the policy period (or extended reporting period).⁹

The Smith v. ASBC litigation involves bodily injury that occurred in September 2023 when patron David Jones, allegedly served alcohol while visibly intoxicated at the Target's taproom, drove his vehicle while impaired and caused a collision that injured plaintiff Smith. The lawsuit was filed in July 2024—ten months after the underlying accident. Under occurrence-based coverage, the controlling date for determining coverage is the date of the occurrence (the September 2023 accident), not the date the lawsuit was filed (July 2024).¹⁰ If the Target's general liability policy was in force on the September 2023 accident date, coverage is triggered for all resulting claims, demands, and lawsuits arising from that occurrence, regardless of when those claims are made—including claims made years after the policy expires.¹¹

The occurrence definition under the 1973 ISO CGL form is "an accident, including continuous or repeated exposure to conditions, which results in bodily injury or property damage neither expected nor intended from the standpoint of the insured."¹² The September 2023 drunk driving accident constitutes a classic insured "occurrence" under CGL policy terms. The accident was unintended from the Target's perspective—the Target did not intend or expect that Jones would drive while intoxicated and cause third-party injury. The fact that the Target's alleged negligent service of alcohol to a visibly intoxicated patron was a proximate cause of the accident does not convert the event into an intentional act outside the occurrence definition. Negligent conduct, even gross negligence, falls within the occurrence-based coverage grant so long as the resulting injury was not intended or expected by the insured.¹³

**Coverage Conclusion**: The Smith v. ASBC claim is covered under the Target's occurrence-based GL policy. The September 2023 accident date triggers coverage under any GL policy in force at that time. The July 2024 lawsuit filing date is irrelevant to the coverage trigger analysis.

#### 3. Self-Insured Retention and Defense Cost Allocation

The Target's GL policy contains a $100,000 self-insured retention (SIR), not a deductible. The distinction between SIR and deductible structures is critical to understanding the Target's obligations under the policy and the timeline for insurer involvement in the Smith litigation.

A self-insured retention requires the insured to pay the first specified amount of loss—including both defense costs and indemnity—before the insurer has any obligation to defend or indemnify.¹⁴ The insured effectively acts as its own primary insurer up to the SIR amount, retaining complete responsibility for claims investigation, defense counsel selection and management, settlement negotiations, and payment of all defense costs and settlement amounts until the SIR is exhausted.¹⁵ Only after the insured has paid the full SIR amount (whether through defense costs alone, indemnity alone, or a combination of both) does the insurer's duty to defend and indemnify attach.¹⁶

In contrast, a deductible structure provides for immediate insurer defense from the first dollar. Under a deductible policy, the insurer assumes control of the claim defense, selects defense counsel, pays all defense costs and settlement amounts, and then seeks reimbursement from the insured for the deductible amount (typically limited to indemnity payments, not defense costs) after the claim is resolved.¹⁷ The insured's financial obligation under a deductible is reimbursement after the fact, whereas the insured's obligation under an SIR is payment before insurer involvement.

The $100,000 SIR in the Target's GL policy requires the Target to:

- Pay all attorneys' fees, expert witness fees, deposition costs, and litigation expenses
- Manage settlement negotiations and evaluate settlement offers
- Fund any settlement or judgment amounts
- Track all defense and indemnity costs toward the $100,000 SIR threshold
- Continue self-funding until aggregate defense costs plus indemnity reach $100,000

**Post-Exhaustion Phase (Insurer Obligations):**
- Assume control of defense once $100,000 SIR is exhausted
- Select defense counsel (subject to approval requirements in policy)
- Pay all further defense costs outside and in addition to policy limits
- Evaluate settlement offers and make coverage decisions
- Fund settlements or judgments up to $5 million per occurrence limit

#### 5. Duty to Defend Analysis — Eight Corners Rule

The insurer's duty to defend is determined under the "eight corners rule" (also termed the "four corners rule"), which examines only (1) the four corners of the complaint and (2) the four corners of the insurance policy.²¹ Under this rule, if the allegations in the complaint potentially or possibly state a claim within the scope of coverage under the policy, the insurer must defend the entire action, even if some claims are not covered, some allegations are groundless, false, or fraudulent, or the insurer believes it will ultimately owe no indemnity.²²

The duty to defend is broader than the duty to indemnify. An insurer may have a duty to defend claims that ultimately prove to be uncovered, so long as the initial complaint allegations potentially state a covered claim.²³ The insurer cannot deny its duty to defend based on extrinsic evidence outside the complaint demonstrating that coverage does not apply—at least 33 states now permit extrinsic evidence in duty-to-defend analysis, but only to trigger or broaden coverage, not to deny it.²⁴ An insurer seeking to deny its duty to defend based on a policy exclusion must demonstrate that the complaint allegations, read liberally, cannot possibly state a covered claim under any theory.²⁵

#### 6. Oregon Dram Shop Statutory Framework

Oregon Revised Statutes § 471.565 establishes civil liability for commercial alcohol providers who serve visibly intoxicated patrons. The statute provides that providers of alcoholic beverages are liable for injuries to third parties caused by intoxicated persons where the provider knew or should have known that the person being served was visibly intoxicated.²⁶ Oregon's dram shop law imposes liability on commercial establishments that serve alcohol to persons exhibiting visible signs of intoxication, where that service is a proximate cause of subsequent injury to third parties.²⁷

The Oregon statutory framework establishes three elements for dram shop liability: (1) the defendant establishment served alcoholic beverages to a person, (2) at the time of service, the person exhibited visible signs of intoxication that were or should have been apparent to the establishment's employees, and (3) the service of alcohol was a proximate cause of injury to a third party.²⁸ Unlike some jurisdictions that require proof that the defendant's service of alcohol was the cause of intoxication (rather than merely a contributing cause), Oregon's statute requires only that the service was a proximate cause—one of potentially multiple causes—of the ultimate injury.²⁹

Oregon's dram shop law allows plaintiffs to pursue punitive damages against commercial alcohol providers where service to a visibly intoxicated patron constitutes gross negligence.³⁰ Gross negligence under Oregon law requires a showing that the defendant's conduct was a reckless and conscious disregard of a known risk of harm to others.³¹ In the dram shop context, gross negligence may be established where the establishment: (1) served multiple drinks over an extended period to a patron exhibiting obvious and severe intoxication (e.g., inability to stand, incoherent speech, falling asleep at the bar), (2) served alcohol after the patron stated an intent to drive, (3) had received prior warnings or complaints about over-service, or (4) violated internal alcohol service policies prohibiting service to visibly intoxicated patrons.³²

The applicability of punitive damages to the Smith litigation depends on the specific facts regarding Jones's level of visible intoxication and the Target's employees' awareness and response. If discovery reveals that Jones exhibited only moderate signs of intoxication (e.g., slightly slurred speech, mild unsteadiness) and employees reasonably believed he would not drive, punitive damages may not be viable. If discovery reveals Jones exhibited severe intoxication (e.g., stumbling, unable to walk steadily, speech incomprehensible), employees discussed his obvious impairment, and the Target continued serving alcohol despite clear danger, punitive damages become more likely.

Oregon is a minority jurisdiction that permits insurance coverage for punitive damages as a matter of public policy. The Oregon Supreme Court has held that insurance coverage for punitive damages does not violate Oregon public policy and such damages are insurable under Oregon law.³³ However, the fact that Oregon law permits such coverage does not mean the Target's insurance policy provides it. Most commercial liability insurance policies expressly exclude coverage for punitive damages, fines, and penalties through contract terms, even in jurisdictions where such coverage would be legally permissible.³⁴

The Target must immediately review its GL policy terms to determine whether punitive damages are excluded. The standard ISO CGL form CG 00 01 does not contain a punitive damages exclusion in the base form, but insurers frequently add punitive damages exclusions through endorsement.³⁵ If the Target's policy contains a punitive damages exclusion, and Smith amends the complaint to seek punitive damages or a jury awards punitive damages at trial, the Target will be personally liable for those damages without insurance coverage. Punitive damage awards in Oregon dram shop cases can range from $100,000 to $1 million or more depending on the egregious nature of the over-service and the defendant's financial condition.³⁶

See Section VIII.A.4 (Punitive Damages Coverage), supra at ¶¶ 109-127, analyzing punitive damages risk in Smith litigation and potential GL policy exclusions creating uninsured exposure.

#### 2. Claims-Made-and-Reported Trigger

EPL policies employ a claims-made-and-reported coverage trigger, which requires that both the claim and the notice to the insurer occur during the policy period.⁴⁷ This trigger structure differs fundamentally from occurrence-based coverage (such as the Target's general liability policy) and creates strict timing requirements for coverage to apply.

If the EEOC charge was filed in November 2024 but the Target did not report it to the EPL insurer until February 2025, and the policy renewed on January 1, 2025, coverage would be denied because the claim was not reported during the policy period in which it was made (assuming no extended reporting period or other policy provisions addressing late reporting).⁴⁹

#### 3. D&O vs. EPL Coverage Distinction

Directors and officers liability insurance and employment practices liability insurance address overlapping but distinct risks. D&O insurance provides coverage for individual directors and officers (and in some policies, the entity itself under "Side C" coverage) against claims alleging mismanagement, breach of fiduciary duty, failure of corporate governance, and regulatory violations.⁵² D&O policies typically cover claims brought by shareholders (derivative suits and securities class actions), regulatory agencies (SEC, FTC, DOJ investigations), and in some cases, creditors in bankruptcy.⁵³ D&O policies generally exclude employment-related claims unless the policy includes a specific EPL endorsement providing entity coverage for employment practices.⁵⁴

EPL insurance provides coverage for the company entity (and in some policies, individual managers and supervisors) against claims by employees or former employees alleging employment-related wrongful acts, including discrimination based on protected characteristics (race, sex, age, disability, religion, national origin), harassment (sexual harassment, hostile work environment), retaliation (for protected activity such as filing discrimination complaints, reporting illegal conduct, taking FMLA leave), wrongful termination (discharge in violation of public policy, constructive discharge), and in some policies, wage and hour violations.⁵⁵

#### 1. D&O Coverage for Regulatory Investigations

### D. Excess and Umbrella Liability Coverage

#### 1. Excess Coverage Tower Structure

Excess liability insurance sits above primary insurance policies and provides additional limits once the primary policy limits are exhausted.⁷⁴ A typical commercial insurance tower consists of: (1) primary layer (the Target's $5 million GL policy), (2) first excess layer (typically $5 million–$10 million excess of the primary), and (3) additional excess layers (potentially $10 million–$25 million or more for large commercial risks).⁷⁵

Excess policies are typically "follow form" policies that mirror the terms, conditions, definitions, and exclusions of the underlying primary policy.⁷⁶ The excess insurer does not become obligated to pay until the full limits of the underlying primary policy are exhausted—either through payment of a judgment or settlement, or through erosion of aggregate limits by multiple claims during the policy period.⁷⁷ Once the underlying limits are exhausted, the excess policy responds on the same terms as the primary policy.⁷⁸

The Smith v. ASBC dram shop litigation presents exposure of $1.85 million–$3.85 million, which falls comfortably within the Target's $5 million primary GL policy limit. Excess coverage is not required for this claim under base case exposure assumptions. However, excess coverage becomes material in two scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Punitive Damages Award**

If the Smith litigation proceeds to trial and the jury awards punitive damages in addition to compensatory damages, total liability could exceed $5 million. Oregon dram shop cases with egregious over-service facts (serving obviously intoxicated patrons who exhibit severe impairment, continuing service after warnings, serving patrons who stated intent to drive) have generated punitive damages awards of $500,000–$2 million.⁷⁹ A verdict structure of $3.5 million compensatory damages + $2 million punitive damages = $5.5 million total would exceed the primary $5 million limit by $500,000, creating uninsured exposure to the Target if no excess coverage exists (and assuming punitive damages are not excluded from the primary policy).

**Scenario 2: Aggregate Limit Erosion**

CGL policies contain both a per occurrence limit ($5 million for the Target's policy) and an annual aggregate limit (typically $10 million for policies with $5 million per occurrence limits).⁸⁰ The aggregate limit is the maximum the insurer will pay for all claims during the policy period, regardless of the number of occurrences. If the Target faces multiple dram shop claims, slip-and-fall claims, product liability claims, or other GL claims during the same policy year, those claims collectively erode the aggregate limit. Once the aggregate limit is exhausted, the insurer has no further obligation to defend or indemnify any additional claims during that policy period, even if those claims are otherwise covered.⁸¹

If the Target has additional pending or potential GL claims (other taproom injuries, product liability, premises liability), the Smith claim may erode a substantial portion of the aggregate limit, leaving limited coverage for subsequent claims. Excess coverage would "drop down" to provide coverage once the primary aggregate is exhausted.

**Excess Coverage Verification Priority**: The Target should immediately verify whether excess liability coverage exists above the $5 million primary GL layer. If no excess coverage exists, the Target should evaluate the cost and availability of $10 million–$25 million excess coverage. Typical pricing for first-layer excess ($5 million excess of $5 million primary) for a craft beverage manufacturer with favorable loss history is $10,000–$30,000 annual premium.⁸²

#### 2. Umbrella Liability Distinction

Umbrella liability policies differ from straight excess policies in two material respects: (1) breadth of coverage and (2) drop-down provisions.⁸³ An umbrella policy provides excess coverage over multiple underlying policies (general liability, automobile liability, employer's liability) and may provide broader coverage than the underlying policies, filling gaps where the underlying policies do not respond.⁸⁴

**Breadth of Coverage**: An umbrella policy may cover claims that are excluded from or not addressed by the underlying policies, subject to a "self-insured retention" that applies when the umbrella provides primary coverage.⁸⁵ For example, if the Target's GL policy excludes employment practices claims (which it does under standard CGL forms), but the umbrella policy does not contain the same exclusion, the umbrella may provide coverage for employment practices claims subject to a self-insured retention (e.g., $100,000 or $250,000).

**Drop-Down Coverage**: Umbrella policies may "drop down" to provide primary coverage when an underlying policy does not respond due to aggregate limit exhaustion, policy cancellation, or insurer insolvency.⁸⁶ If the Target's GL aggregate limit is exhausted by multiple claims during the policy period, an umbrella policy with drop-down provisions would provide first-dollar coverage for subsequent GL claims (subject to the umbrella's drop-down retention).

### E. Notice and Cooperation Compliance

#### 1. Notice Requirements and Timing

All liability insurance policies contain conditions requiring the insured to provide prompt notice of claims, occurrences, or circumstances that may give rise to claims.⁸⁷ The notice requirement serves multiple purposes: enabling the insurer to conduct timely investigation while evidence and witnesses are available, allowing the insurer to participate in early settlement negotiations, and preserving the insurer's subrogation rights against third parties.⁸⁸

The Target's GL policy likely requires notice "as soon as practicable" after the insured becomes aware of an occurrence or claim.⁸⁹ This standard does not specify a precise deadline but requires notice within a reasonable time under the circumstances. For lawsuits, best practice is to provide notice to the insurer within 3–7 days of service of the complaint. For EEOC charges, best practice is to provide notice within 7–14 days of receipt of the charge. For occurrences that may give rise to future claims (e.g., the September 2023 drunk driving accident before any lawsuit was filed), notice should be provided within 30 days of the occurrence.

#### 2. Late Notice Defense and Prejudice Requirement

The majority of U.S. jurisdictions apply a "notice-prejudice rule" that precludes insurers from denying coverage based on late notice unless the insurer demonstrates it suffered actual prejudice as a result of the delay.⁹⁰ Under the notice-prejudice rule, even if the insured's notice was unreasonably late, the insurer cannot avoid its coverage obligations unless it proves that the delay materially impaired its ability to investigate the claim, defend the insured, or preserve subrogation rights.⁹¹

Demonstrable prejudice typically requires showing:
- Key evidence was destroyed or became unavailable due to the delay
- Material witnesses died, relocated, or lost memory of relevant facts
- The claim settled or judgment was entered without the insurer's involvement
- The insurer lost subrogation rights against third parties
- The delay prevented the insurer from conducting an independent investigation⁹²

A minority of jurisdictions treat timely notice as a strict condition precedent to coverage, allowing insurers to deny coverage based on late notice regardless of prejudice.⁹³ These "strict notice" states hold that failure to provide timely notice voids coverage even if the insurer suffered no harm from the delay.

**Oregon's Position**: Research indicates Oregon likely follows the majority notice-prejudice rule, though specific Oregon Supreme Court precedent definitively establishing the standard was not identified in the specialist report. Under the notice-prejudice framework, even if the Target delayed notice to the GL insurer beyond July 2024, the insurer would be required to prove actual prejudice to deny coverage. Given the Smith litigation timeline (accident September 2023, lawsuit July 2024, case currently in early stages with no trial date set), the insurer would face difficulty proving prejudice. Evidence remains available, witnesses are accessible, no judgment has been entered, and the insurer can conduct a full investigation and participate in defense strategy.

**Recommendation**: The Target should immediately verify the exact date it provided notice to its GL insurer of the Smith lawsuit. If notice was provided within 30 days of service (July-August 2024), no late notice issue exists. If notice was delayed beyond August 2024, the Target should document the reasons for the delay and assess prejudice risk. Even if notice was delayed, Oregon's likely application of the notice-prejudice rule substantially reduces the risk of coverage denial.

#### 3. Cooperation Clause Requirements

All liability insurance policies contain cooperation clauses requiring the insured to cooperate with the insurer in the investigation and defense of claims.⁹⁴ The cooperation clause is a condition precedent to coverage—material breach of the cooperation obligation can result in complete forfeiture of coverage, even for otherwise covered claims.⁹⁵

Standard cooperation clause requirements include:
- Providing the insurer with all relevant documents and information
- Attending depositions and providing testimony as required
- Assisting in the investigation of the claim
- Appearing at trial and testifying if necessary
- Refraining from making admissions of liability or settling without insurer consent
- Responding promptly to insurer requests for information⁹⁶

Breach of the cooperation clause typically requires showing: (1) the insured failed to cooperate, (2) the failure was material (not merely technical or inadvertent), and (3) the failure prejudiced the insurer's ability to defend the claim.⁹⁷ Courts require substantial, not incidental, failure to cooperate before permitting coverage denial.⁹⁸ Examples of material breach include: the insured refusing to attend a deposition, the insured providing false information to the insurer, the insured concealing relevant documents, or the insured refusing to testify at trial.⁹⁹

**Target's Obligations in Smith Litigation**: Once the Target's $100,000 SIR is exhausted and the insurer assumes control of the defense, the Target must fully cooperate with the insurer and insurer-appointed defense counsel. This includes: making Target employees available for depositions, producing all relevant documents (employee training records, alcohol service policies, incident reports, Jones's service records from September 15, 2023), responding to interrogatories, and preparing Target representatives for deposition and trial testimony. Failure to cooperate—for example, if a key Target employee (the server who served Jones) refuses to attend deposition—could provide grounds for the insurer to deny coverage.

#### 4. No Voluntary Payments and Settlement Authority

CGL policies universally prohibit the insured from making voluntary payments, assuming obligations, or incurring expenses without the insurer's consent, except at the insured's own cost.¹⁰⁰ This "no voluntary payments" condition prevents the insured from settling claims or admitting liability in ways that prejudice the insurer's defense rights or bind the insurer to settlement terms it did not approve.¹⁰¹

**Application During SIR Period**: While the Target's $100,000 SIR remains unexhausted, the Target controls the claim and may settle without insurer consent because the insurer has no duty to defend or indemnify until the SIR is exhausted. If the Target settles the Smith claim for $90,000 while only $50,000 of the SIR has been exhausted through defense costs, the Target can do so without insurer approval—the entire settlement falls within the SIR, and the insurer has no financial stake.

**Application After SIR Exhaustion**: Once the SIR is exhausted and the insurer assumes the defense, the Target cannot settle without the insurer's prior written consent. If the Target settles the Smith claim for $2 million without insurer approval after the SIR is exhausted, the insurer can deny indemnity for the settlement on grounds that the insured violated the no-voluntary-payments condition. The insurer may still owe the Target defense costs incurred up to the point of the unauthorized settlement, but the insurer would not be obligated to pay the $2 million settlement amount.

**Recommendation**: The Target should coordinate closely with its GL insurer on settlement strategy for the Smith litigation. ### F. Bad Faith Exposure and Insurer Settlement Obligations

#### 1. Third-Party Bad Faith Standards

Oregon law imposes on liability insurers a duty to exercise good faith and due care in investigating and settling third-party claims against their insureds.¹⁰² Oregon Revised Statutes § 746.230 establishes standards for unfair claim settlement practices, requiring insurers to conduct reasonable investigations and act in good faith when evaluating settlement opportunities.¹⁰³ An insurer that breaches its duty of good faith in handling a third-party claim may be liable to its insured for bad faith, including liability for any excess judgment (judgment exceeding policy limits) that results from the insurer's bad faith refusal to settle within limits.¹⁰⁴

The landmark Oregon case *Moody v. Oregon Community Credit Union* (2022-2023) established that violations of ORS 746.230 can provide the basis for negligence per se claims against insurers.¹⁰⁵ The case significantly expanded insurer liability for bad faith claim handling in Oregon, permitting insureds to pursue tort claims for insurer misconduct in addition to contract-based coverage claims.¹⁰⁶

**Duty to Settle Within Limits**: When a third-party claimant offers to settle a claim within the insured's policy limits, and the facts indicate the claim may result in an excess judgment if tried, the insurer has a duty to accept the settlement offer to protect the insured from personal liability for an excess verdict.¹⁰⁷ The insurer must give equal consideration to the insured's interests and the insurer's own interests in evaluating settlement offers—the insurer cannot reject a reasonable settlement offer within policy limits solely to minimize its own payout, knowing that rejection exposes the insured to personal liability for an excess verdict.¹⁰⁸

**Excess Verdict Liability**: If the insurer wrongfully refuses a reasonable settlement demand within policy limits, and the case proceeds to trial resulting in a verdict that exceeds policy limits, the insurer may be liable to the insured for the full amount of the judgment—including the excess portion above policy limits.¹⁰⁹ For example, if Smith offers to settle for $4.5 million (within the $5 million policy limits), the insurer rejects the offer without reasonable justification, and the jury returns a verdict of $6.5 million, the insurer could be liable to the Target for the full $6.5 million judgment (not just the $5 million policy limit) on a bad faith claim.¹¹⁰

**Smith v. ASBC Bad Faith Risk Assessment**: The risk of bad faith liability in the Smith litigation is LOW under current exposure estimates. The claim exposure of $1.85 million–$3.85 million falls comfortably within the $5 million policy limit, leaving substantial cushion for settlement within limits. Bad faith risk would increase materially only in two scenarios:

**Scenario 1**: Smith's damages prove substantially higher than estimated (e.g., severe permanent disability, lifetime medical care needs, loss of earning capacity) and Smith demands $4.8 million to settle. If the insurer rejects this demand believing it can achieve a better result at trial, but the jury returns a $6 million verdict, bad faith exposure arises.

**Scenario 2**: Punitive damages are awarded at trial, pushing the total verdict above $5 million. If Smith offered to settle for compensatory damages only ($3.5 million) and waive punitive damages claims, but the insurer rejected that offer, and the jury awards $3.5 million compensatory + $2.5 million punitive = $6 million total, the insurer's decision to reject the within-limits offer could constitute bad faith.

Given the current exposure profile and substantial policy limit headroom, the Target faces minimal bad faith risk from its insurer's settlement decisions. However, the Target should monitor settlement negotiations and consult with coverage counsel if the insurer adopts an unreasonably aggressive litigation posture that exposes the Target to excess verdict risk.
