### A. FTC Act Section 5 Legal Framework

#### 1. Statutory Authority and Three-Part Deception Test

The Federal Trade Commission Act Section 5(a) prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce."³ The FTC applies a three-part test to determine whether advertising is deceptive: (1) a representation, omission, or practice that is likely to mislead; (2) from the perspective of a consumer acting reasonably under the circumstances; and (3) the representation, omission, or practice is material to consumer decision-making.⁴

For health claims, the FTC requires "competent and reliable scientific evidence" defined as "tests, analyses, research, or studies that (1) have been conducted and evaluated in an objective manner by experts in the relevant field and (2) are generally accepted in the profession to yield accurate and reliable results."⁵ Randomized controlled human clinical trials (RCTs) constitute the gold standard for substantiating health benefit claims.⁶

#### 2. FTC Policy on Alcohol Health Claims (1994)

The FTC issued a seminal policy statement in 1994 addressing health claims for alcoholic beverages, determining that unqualified health claims for alcohol are inherently deceptive because: (1) alcohol is a known carcinogen classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) as Group 1 (carcinogenic to humans); (2) no net health benefit from moderate consumption has been established by competent and reliable scientific evidence; (3) any claimed benefit is outweighed by countervailing harms including cardiovascular disease, liver damage, addiction risk, and increased cancer incidence; and (4) health claims fail to disclose material risks to consumers.⁷

The FTC's position remains that health claims for alcoholic beverages are **per se deceptive** and lack adequate substantiation regardless of the level or quality of scientific evidence presented.⁸ This per se rule reflects the policy determination that the known harms of alcohol consumption preclude any representation suggesting net health benefits, even if isolated components (such as antioxidants in hops or resveratrol in wine) might possess beneficial properties when consumed in non-alcoholic forms.⁹

#### 3. Application to ASBC's Posts

**Post 1 (Instagram, March 2024):** "Artisan IPA contains antioxidants from hops — enjoy the health benefits of moderate beer consumption! #craftbeer #healthy"

**Legal Analysis:** This post contains an express unsubstantiated health claim ("health benefits of moderate beer consumption"). The claim fails the FTC's three-part deception test on all elements: (1) the representation expressly states health benefits exist; (2) a reasonable consumer would interpret this as meaning beer consumption provides net health benefits; and (3) health claims are inherently material to consumer purchasing decisions.¹⁰ The claim lacks competent and reliable scientific evidence because no RCTs demonstrate net health benefits from beer consumption, and the representation omits material information about alcohol's known health risks.¹¹

**Post 2 (Facebook, April 2024):** "Our hops are rich in polyphenols, which support heart health. Drink responsibly and live well! #hophealth"

**Legal Analysis:** This post contains a specific organ-related health claim ("support heart health") that is both unsubstantiated and misleading. While hops do contain polyphenols, and some research suggests polyphenols in certain plant foods may have cardiovascular benefits, these findings do not establish that consuming polyphenols in alcoholic beverages provides net cardiovascular benefit.¹² The inclusion of "Drink responsibly and live well!" does not cure the deceptive claim because it implies that responsible drinking of this product supports cardiovascular wellness, reinforcing the underlying misrepresentation.¹³

**Conclusion:** Both posts definitively violate FTC Act Section 5. The violations are per se deceptive under the FTC's 1994 alcohol policy and cannot be substantiated regardless of the scientific evidence ASBC might present regarding antioxidants or polyphenols in hops.¹⁴

### C. Corrective Advertising Requirements and Cost Exposure

#### 1. Legal Standard for Corrective Advertising

Corrective advertising is an equitable remedy warranted when "challenged ads substantially contributed to the development and maintenance of a false belief that lingers in the minds of a substantial portion of consumers."³³ The Supreme Court has recognized that corrective advertising or affirmative disclosures that prevent future deception or correct past deception do not raise First Amendment concerns, provided they do not exceed what is necessary to remedy the deceptive practice.³⁴

The FTC typically requires corrective advertising to reach an audience comparable to the original deceptive advertisements, ensuring that consumers exposed to the false claims also receive the corrective message.³⁵ The required language must clearly and unambiguously inform consumers that prior claims were unsubstantiated or false.³⁶

#### 2. Precedents in Alcoholic Beverage Cases

**Canandaigua Wine Company (Cisco, 1991):** The FTC obtained a consent order requiring Canandaigua to modify packaging and labeling for Cisco, a fortified wine product whose packaging misrepresented the alcohol content as comparable to wine coolers when in fact a single bottle contained alcohol equivalent to five servings of 80-proof vodka.³⁷ Multiple consumers suffered alcohol poisoning based on the misleading packaging.³⁸ The consent order required corrective labeling and packaging modifications but did not mandate a mass media corrective advertising campaign, likely because the deception related to product design rather than advertising claims.³⁹

**Phusion Projects (Four Loko, 2014):** The modified consent order required label disclosures when products exceeded specific alcohol content thresholds and resealability requirements for oversized containers.⁴⁰ Like Canandaigua, the remedy focused on prospective product modifications rather than retrospective corrective advertising, distinguishing product design cases from advertising claim cases.⁴¹

**Distinguishing ASBC:** Unlike Canandaigua and Four Loko, which involved ongoing product design issues requiring structural remedies, ASBC's violations stem from discrete advertising claims in two social media posts that were promptly deleted. The FTC may determine that corrective advertising is appropriate to dispel any lingering false beliefs among the estimated 50,000 consumers exposed to the posts.⁴²

### D. Beverage Alcohol Code (BAC) Compliance Framework

#### 1. Industry Self-Regulation Structure and FTC Oversight

The alcoholic beverage industry operates under voluntary self-regulatory codes administered by three trade associations: the Distilled Spirits Council of the United States (DISCUS) for spirits, the Beer Institute for beer and malt beverages, and the Wine Institute for wine.⁴⁸ These codes establish advertising and marketing standards that member companies agree to follow, creating an industry-wide framework for responsible marketing practices.⁴⁹

**Legal Status and FTC Position:** While BAC Code compliance is technically voluntary, the FTC actively monitors industry adherence and treats BAC Code violations as evidence of potential FTC Act Section 5 violations.⁵⁰ The FTC has stated: "The FTC encourages industry members to comply with codes of conduct relating to alcohol advertising and marketing written by the Distilled Spirits Council of the United States (DISCUS), the Beer Institute, and the Wine Institute."⁵¹ This statement signals that BAC Code compliance serves as a safe harbor, while violations may trigger FTC scrutiny.⁵²

**Enforcement Mechanism:** BAC Code enforcement is primarily complaint-driven, with trade associations investigating member violations and potential sanctions including public censure, suspension, or expulsion from the association.⁵³ More significantly, BAC Code violations provide evidentiary support for FTC enforcement actions, as demonstrated by ASBC's case where the health claims violated both FTC standards and BAC Code provisions.⁵⁴

#### 2. BAC Code Health Claims Prohibition

**DISCUS Code of Responsible Practices (2022):** "Beverage alcohol advertising and marketing materials should not make any health-related claims or health-related statements, including specific health claims or general health claims."⁵⁵

**Beer Institute Advertising Code:** "Beer advertising and marketing materials should not contain or refer to health-related claims."⁵⁶

**TTB Parallel Prohibition:** The Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB) regulations similarly prohibit "the appearance on labels or in advertisements of any health-related statement, including a specific health claim, that is untrue in any particular or tends to create a misleading impression."⁵⁷ This creates overlapping federal regulatory authority, with FTC jurisdiction over advertising and TTB jurisdiction over labeling, though both agencies typically coordinate enforcement to avoid duplicative proceedings.⁵⁸

**ASBC Violation Analysis:**
- Post 1: "enjoy the health benefits of moderate beer consumption" → Direct general health claim (prohibited)
- Post 2: "support heart health" → Specific organ-related health claim (prohibited)
- Post 2: "Drink responsibly and live well!" → Implied health/wellness claim (arguably prohibited)

**Conclusion:** ASBC's posts violated BAC Code health claims prohibitions, TTB advertising regulations, and FTC substantiation standards, creating a trifecta of regulatory non-compliance.⁵⁹

#### 3. Adult Audience Placement Requirements (73.6%/73.8% Standard)

The BAC Code requires that alcohol advertising placements ensure at least 71.6% to 73.8% of the audience is of legal drinking age, with the specific threshold varying by industry segment and year of adoption.⁶⁰

**Historical Standard (2011-2022):** 71.6% of audience must be 21+ years old, based on 2010 Census data.⁶¹

**Current Standards (Updated 2022-2024):**
- **Beer Institute / Brewers Association:** 73.6% of audience must be 21+ (updated to reflect 2020 Census data)⁶²
- **DISCUS (Distilled Spirits):** 73.8% of audience must be 21+ (updated 2022)⁶³

**Rationale for Adjustment:** The updated thresholds reflect the 2020 Census showing a higher proportion of adults in the U.S. population, requiring advertisers to demonstrate a correspondingly higher percentage of adult audience composition.⁶⁴

**Platform Compliance:** Instagram and Facebook both meet the 73.6% adult demographic threshold based on platform-wide user composition as of 2024.⁶⁵ However, the BAC Code requires more than platform-wide compliance; it mandates **age-gating** on brand social media pages to actively verify that users are of legal drinking age before viewing alcohol advertising content.⁶⁶

**Age-Gating Requirement:** DISCUS and Beer Institute codes require that "placements in digital media where there is dialogue between a user and a brewer may only be made where a user confirms they are of legal drinking age through disclosure of a user's full birth date or other active confirmation prior to viewing an advertisement."⁶⁷ The FTC has similarly recommended that age gates on company websites and social media pages should require consumers to enter their full date of birth rather than simply checking a box certifying legal age.⁶⁸

**ASBC Compliance Gap:** If ASBC's Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, TikTok, and YouTube brand pages lack age-gating requiring full birth date verification, this constitutes an **ongoing BAC Code violation** that must be remedied immediately.⁶⁹ Age-gating implementation is a technical matter that can be accomplished within 1-2 weeks using built-in platform features or third-party age verification tools (such as Agechecker.net or Jumio).⁷⁰

#### 4. Additional BAC Code Compliance Requirements

**Prohibitions on Appeals to Minors:** BAC Code provisions prohibit advertising content that has primary appeal to persons under legal drinking age, including: cartoon characters or imagery appealing to children; school settings or playground imagery; toys, games, or activities primarily associated with minors; teen or pre-teen slang; celebrities, athletes, or influencers with primary appeal to underage audiences; and characters or imagery depicting persons who appear to be under 21.⁷¹

**Responsible Depiction Requirements:** The codes prohibit depictions of excessive drinking, intoxication, loss of control, drinking games encouraging excessive consumption, chugging or rapid consumption, drinking and driving, drinking in connection with dangerous activities, and suggestions that social, professional, educational, athletic, or sexual success results from drinking.⁷²

**Social Media-Specific Requirements:** Alcohol companies are liable for health claims in user-generated content (UGC) that they repost or share on brand channels.⁷³ Under 16 C.F.R. Part 255 (FTC Guides Concerning Use of Endorsements and Testimonials), companies adopt customer testimonials they repost, becoming liable for any unsubstantiated claims contained in those posts.⁷⁴ Additionally, influencer marketing requires clear and conspicuous disclosure of material connections between the brand and paid endorsers.⁷⁵

**ASBC Immediate Compliance Actions Required:**
1. Implement age-gating on all social media brand pages (Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, TikTok, YouTube)
2. Establish user-generated content monitoring protocols; never repost customer content containing health claims
3. Review all influencer agreements to ensure compliance with 16 C.F.R. Part 255 disclosure requirements
4. Provide FTC Disclosures 101 guidance to all paid endorsers and brand ambassadors
5. Conduct comprehensive audit of all advertising materials (digital, print, outdoor, point-of-sale) for BAC Code compliance

### I. Draft Remediation Language

While the FTC health claims investigation does not require draft contract language (as it involves regulatory resolution rather than transaction terms), ASBC should implement the following compliance policies as remediation measures:

#### Social Media Advertising Policy — Health Claims Prohibition

**Policy Effective Date:** [Date]

**Scope:** This policy applies to all social media posts, advertising creative, promotional materials, influencer content, and user-generated content reshared by Artisan Spirits & Brewing Company on any platform.

**Prohibited Content — Health Claims:**

ASBC shall not publish, sponsor, or reshare any content containing:

1. **General Health Claims:** Statements suggesting that consumption of ASBC products provides health benefits, promotes wellness, supports healthy lifestyle, or contributes to overall health (e.g., "enjoy the health benefits of moderate beer consumption," "healthy choice," "wellness in every sip").

2. **Specific Health Claims:** Statements referencing specific organs, body systems, diseases, or health conditions (e.g., "supports heart health," "good for digestion," "boosts immunity," "reduces risk of [disease]").

3. **Nutrient Function Claims:** Statements claiming that ingredients in ASBC products (including antioxidants, vitamins, minerals, polyphenols, probiotics, or other bioactive compounds) provide health benefits when consumed in alcoholic beverages (e.g., "antioxidants from hops," "probiotics for gut health," "Vitamin C for immunity").

4. **Implied Health Claims:** Indirect suggestions of health benefits through imagery (athletes, fitness activities, medical symbols), taglines ("drink responsibly and live well"), or endorsements from health professionals.

**Permissible Statements:**

ASBC may make truthful, non-misleading statements regarding:
- Ingredient sourcing and quality (e.g., "brewed with Pacific Northwest hops," "organic barley")
- Production methods and heritage (e.g., "small-batch distilled," "traditional brewing methods")
- Sensory characteristics (e.g., "smooth finish," "citrus notes," "full-bodied flavor")
- Awards and recognitions from beverage industry competitions
- Responsible drinking messages mandated by law or recommended by industry associations

**Pre-Approval Requirement:**

All social media posts, advertising creative, promotional materials, and influencer content must receive written approval from ASBC's Compliance Officer or designee before publication. Approval must be documented with approver name, date, and content version reviewed.

**User-Generated Content:**

ASBC shall not reshare, repost, or amplify user-generated content containing any prohibited health claims. Social media team members shall monitor brand mentions and customer posts daily, flagging any content containing health claims for compliance review.

**Violation Consequences:**

Employees who violate this policy are subject to disciplinary action up to and including termination. External agencies or influencers who violate this policy may have contracts terminated and payment withheld until violations are corrected.

**Training Requirement:**

All marketing staff, social media managers, external agencies, distributors involved in local marketing, and brand ambassadors must complete BAC Code compliance training annually and certify understanding of this policy.
