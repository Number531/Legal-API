### B. Standard Distributor Agreement Structure

#### 1. Term and Duration

Beer distributor agreements in the beverage alcohol industry follow standardized structural conventions established over decades of three-tier system practice. **Five-year initial terms** predominate for beer distributors, with automatic renewal clauses that roll agreements forward for successive five-year periods unless either party provides written non-renewal notice (typically 90-180 days before term expiration).⁷ Spirits distributors receive shorter **three-year terms**, reflecting different market dynamics and margin structures in the distilled spirits category.⁸

Certain states impose perpetual duration by statute. **North Carolina's franchise law effectively makes territory assignments irreversible:** once a distribution agreement is executed, the relationship becomes permanent absent "good cause" for termination.⁹ This creates near-insurmountable barriers to consolidation in states with the most protective franchise regimes.

Renewal mechanics typically require affirmative non-renewal notice rather than affirmative renewal elections, placing the burden on the party seeking to exit. For ASBC's 305 distributors operating under automatic renewal structures, ABP inherits ongoing contractual relationships that cannot be terminated at term expiration without satisfying statutory "good cause" requirements in franchise states.

#### 2. Exclusive Territory Grants

Distribution agreements universally grant **exclusive territorial rights** prohibiting suppliers from appointing additional distributors within designated geographic boundaries.¹⁰ Territory definitions employ various methodologies:

- **County boundaries:** Common in rural states (e.g., "exclusive territory comprising Adams, Baker, and Clark Counties, Nebraska")
- **ZIP code clusters:** Prevalent in metropolitan areas (e.g., "ZIP codes 60601-60699" for Chicago downtown)
- **Metropolitan Statistical Areas (MSAs):** Federal census definitions for major urban centers
- **State borders:** Entire state assignments for smaller volume states or spirits-only distributors

Many states **mandate exclusive territories by statute**, imposing substantial penalties on wholesalers for deliveries outside designated geographic areas.¹¹ These statutory exclusivity requirements create a zero-sum game: appointing a second distributor within an existing territory necessitates termination of the incumbent—which in turn triggers "good cause" and brand value compensation requirements in franchise states.

**Consolidation Barrier:** ABP cannot implement a hub-and-spoke network model by overlaying preferred distributors onto existing territories. The exclusive territory system requires sequential termination-then-appointment, exposing ABP to full statutory compensation obligations for each terminated distributor.

#### 3. Performance Requirements and Enforceability Standards

Typical distributor agreements impose quantifiable performance obligations including:¹²

- **Minimum sales targets:** Annual volume commitments (cases, barrels, or dollar-denominated)
- **On-time delivery percentages:** Industry standard of 95%+ delivery within promised timeframes
- **Sales staff minimums:** Required number of dedicated sales representatives assigned to supplier's brands
- **Market coverage:** Geographic penetration requirements (e.g., "maintain distribution in 80% of licensed retail accounts within territory")
- **Warehouse standards:** Temperature control, storage capacity, handling procedures for product quality maintenance

However, franchise law states impose strict **commercially reasonable standards** that override contractual performance metrics. Texas's definitional framework typifies this statutory overlay:

> "Good cause" means the failure by any party to an agreement, without reasonable excuse or justification, to comply substantially with an **essential, reasonable, and commercially acceptable requirement** imposed by the other party under the terms of an agreement.¹³

This four-part test places heavy burdens on suppliers seeking to terminate for performance deficiencies:

**Element 1: Essential**—The requirement must be material to the supplier's business objectives, not merely aspirational or convenience-based.

**Element 2: Reasonable**—The metric must be achievable by a competent distributor operating in comparable market conditions. Targets that prove unattainable due to category decline, supply chain disruptions, or force majeure events fail this test.¹⁴

**Element 3: Commercially Acceptable**—Industry standards govern. If 95% on-time delivery is standard across the distribution tier, requiring 99% may be deemed commercially unacceptable absent justification.

**Element 4: Non-Discriminatory**—Suppliers must apply standards uniformly to similarly situated distributors. Selective enforcement against a single distributor while tolerating identical underperformance by others vitiates "good cause."¹⁵

**COVID-19 Force Majeure Precedents:** Recent litigation established that distributors can invoke force majeure and commercial impracticability defenses when performance failures result from pandemic-related disruptions.¹⁶ Courts require suppliers to prove causation between distributor's actions (or inactions) and performance deficiencies, rejecting arguments that economic hardship alone excuses supplier's obligations.¹⁷

**Texas Lone Star Hypothetical Application:**

Consider a distributor achieving 88% on-time delivery (required: 95%), maintaining 5 sales representatives (required: 8), and missing annual sales targets by 12%. Franchise law analysis suggests:

- **On-time delivery:** Supplier prevails **only if** comparable distributors are meeting the 95% standard; distributor prevails if driver shortages or supply chain disruptions affected entire market. Outcome: **fact-intensive litigation risk.**

- **Sales staff:** Supplier prevails **only if** other distributors in comparable territories maintain 8+ representatives; distributor prevails if labor market conditions made hiring impossible at budgeted compensation levels. Outcome: **fact-intensive litigation risk.**

- **Sales targets:** Distributor **likely prevails** if craft beer category declined 8% nationally during the measurement period, rendering the target commercially unreasonable. Suppliers bear duty to adjust targets in good faith when market conditions deteriorate. Outcome: **distributor advantage.**

**Overall Assessment:** Performance-based terminations carry **10-20% litigation rates**, with franchise law disputes typically settling for 30-50% of brand value to avoid protracted court proceedings.¹⁸

See Section V.E (Joint Employer Analysis), supra at ¶¶ 281-312, analyzing wage/hour compliance issues and potential joint employer liability if distributors misclassify sales staff.

### C. Franchise Law Override of Contractual Provisions (CRITICAL)

#### 1. Void Contract Provisions

The following distributor agreement terms are **VOID and unenforceable** in the 25+ states with beer franchise laws, regardless of contract language:

**A. "Terminate at Will" Clauses**

Contractual language such as "Either party may terminate this agreement at any time for any reason with 30 days' notice" is superseded by statutory "good cause" requirements.²¹ Texas's franchise statute declares: "The effect of this Act may not be varied by agreement."²² Courts uniformly hold that freedom-of-contract principles yield to franchise law's protective regime.

**B. "No Compensation" Provisions**

Clauses stating "Upon termination, distributor not entitled to compensation for brand value, goodwill, or distribution rights" are void in franchise states that mandate compensation calculated via gross profit multiples or fair market value methodologies.²³

**C. Waivers of Statutory Rights**

Agreements containing "Distributor hereby waives all rights under [State] franchise laws" are unenforceable as against public policy.²⁴ Franchise protections are considered non-waivable to prevent suppliers from extracting contractual surrenders of statutory entitlements through superior bargaining power.

**D. Shortened Notice Periods**

Contractual 30-day or 60-day termination notice requirements are extended to the statutory minimum—typically **90 days' notice plus 60-day cure period**—yielding effective 150-day minimum termination timelines.²⁵

**E. Unilateral Performance Modification Rights**

Provisions permitting suppliers to "change performance requirements at any time in sole discretion" are constrained by the "reasonable, material, non-discriminatory" standards discussed above. Suppliers cannot unilaterally impose commercially unreasonable targets and then terminate for non-compliance.²⁶

**Application to ASBC:** Any distributor agreements containing these void provisions provide false comfort regarding ABP's post-acquisition termination flexibility. The **statutory framework controls, not the contract text.**

#### 2. State-Specific Carve-Outs and Successor Manufacturer Rights

While franchise law states generally impose uniform "good cause" and compensation requirements, critical state variations create strategic opportunities:

**California's Unique Framework:** Unlike typical franchise states, California does **not** require "good cause" for termination. However, Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 25000.7 prohibits termination "solely based on failure to meet sales goals or quotas that are not commercially reasonable under prevailing market conditions."²⁷ This nuanced approach provides greater supplier flexibility than blanket "good cause" regimes while still protecting distributors from arbitrary performance standards. Additionally, § 25000.2 requires successor manufacturers to compensate wholesalers at fair market value upon termination.²⁸

**New York's Craft Brewery Carve-Out:** N.Y. Alco. Bev. Control Law creates exemptions for breweries producing <300,000 barrels annually **and** making up ≤3% of any distributor's annual sales.²⁹ Qualifying craft breweries can terminate without "good cause" by paying fair market value of distribution rights lost.

**Critical Limitation:** If ASBC's production exceeds 300,000 barrels/year (data requires verification) **or** ASBC brands represent >3% of any distributor's sales volume, this carve-out does **not** apply and standard "good cause" requirements control.³⁰

**Ohio's Successor Manufacturer Exception (STRATEGIC ADVANTAGE):**

Ohio Rev. Code § 1333.851 provides ABP with **statutory termination rights unavailable in other franchise states:**³¹

> A successor manufacturer may give written notice of termination **within 90 days** of merger, acquisition, or reorganization. The parties shall negotiate in good faith to determine the diminished value of the distributor's business. If unable to resolve within 90 days of notice, either party may file a court action, and the court shall determine diminished value within 90 days of filing.³²

**Strategic Value:** This exception bypasses the "good cause" burden entirely, providing a predictable statutory procedure with fixed timelines. ABP can terminate ASBC's Ohio distributors within the 90-day post-closing window by providing notice and paying court-determined diminished value—eliminating litigation risk over whether termination justification satisfies "good cause."³³

**Estimated Impact:** If ASBC maintains 15-25 distributor relationships in Ohio, § 1333.851 enables consolidation of these accounts at estimated cost of $7.5M-$37.5M (assuming $500K-$1.5M brand value per distributor). This represents one of the few consolidation opportunities available without protracted "good cause" litigation.

**Wisconsin's Brand Compensation Mandate:** When a brewer terminates, cancels, or fails to renew an agreement, the **successor wholesaler chosen by the brewer** must pay the terminated wholesaler fair market value of distribution rights.³⁴ This unique "successor pays" structure shifts compensation obligations from supplier to the incoming distributor, though suppliers typically negotiate economic responsibility in side agreements.

- Gross Profit = Net sales revenue - Cost of goods sold (excluding distribution costs)

### D. Change of Control Provisions—Transaction-Critical Risk

#### 1. Categories of Anti-Assignment and Deemed Assignment Clauses

Distributor agreements contain varying levels of change-of-control protections, creating 183-244 separate consent requirements for ABP's acquisition:

**Type 1: Basic Anti-Assignment (Ambiguous Application)**

> "Neither party may assign this Agreement without the prior written consent of the other party."³⁸

**Legal Issue:** Courts split on whether stock sales or mergers constitute "assignment" under basic anti-assignment language. Some jurisdictions hold that change of corporate ownership via stock transfer is not an "assignment" of contract rights requiring consent.³⁹ Others treat any change of control as assignment "by operation of law."⁴⁰ **Estimated prevalence:** 30-40% of ASBC agreements likely contain basic anti-assignment clauses with ambiguous application to ABP's acquisition structure.

**Type 2: Comprehensive Deemed Assignment (Directly Triggered)**

> "Any change of control of Supplier resulting from merger, consolidation, stock sale, or asset sale shall be deemed an assignment requiring Distributor's prior written consent. 'Change of control' means any transaction resulting in a change of 50% or more of Supplier's voting equity."⁴¹

**Legal Effect:** This language **unambiguously captures ABP's acquisition** of ASBC. Courts enforce comprehensive deemed assignment clauses, treating change-of-control transactions as equivalent to direct assignment.⁴² Failure to obtain distributor consent constitutes material breach. **Estimated prevalence:** 30-50% of agreements likely contain comprehensive deemed assignment provisions based on industry standard form practices.

**Type 3: Unilateral Termination Right (Distributor Option)**

> "Distributor may terminate this Agreement upon 30 days' notice if Supplier undergoes a change of control or is acquired by a competitor of Distributor's existing suppliers."⁴³

**Legal Effect:** Provides distributor **unilateral exit right** upon ABP acquisition. No consent negotiation—distributor can simply terminate. In franchise states, termination may trigger brand value compensation obligations even though distributor initiated exit.⁴⁴ **Estimated prevalence:** 10-20% of agreements likely include distributor termination options.

**Aggregate Consent Burden:** Assuming 60-80% of 305 agreements contain some form of change-of-control provision, ABP faces **183-244 separate consent requirements.** Even if ABP's legal position is that certain basic anti-assignment clauses do not apply to stock acquisitions, distributors will assert consent rights, forcing litigation or negotiated resolutions.

#### 2. Statutory Asymmetry: Distributor Transfer Rights vs. Supplier Constraints

Franchise laws create **asymmetric change-of-control treatment** favoring distributors:

**Distributor-Initiated Transfers:** Most franchise states prohibit suppliers from unreasonably withholding consent to distributor's sale or transfer of distribution rights.⁴⁵ North Carolina's statutory framework exemplifies this asymmetry:

> Distributor may sell, assign, or transfer its business to any qualified person. Supplier has 30 days to request additional information and 30 days from receipt to object. **Objection is limited to**: Transferee fails to meet qualifications that are non-discriminatory, material, reasonable, and consistently applied.⁴⁶

**Practical Effect:** Distributors control who acquires their distribution rights; suppliers have minimal veto power limited to bona fide qualification standards. A distributor facing potential termination post-acquisition can sell to a third party, and ABP **cannot block the transfer** unless the buyer is demonstrably unqualified.

**Supplier-Initiated Changes:** Conversely, distributors possess broad consent rights over supplier's M&A transactions. Florida's statutory regime goes further, deeming a supplier's unreasonable withholding of consent to **distributor's** transfer as a "termination" requiring compensation:

> A supplier "terminates" a beer franchise when it denies approval of or unreasonably withholds consent to any assignment, transfer, or sale of a distributor's business assets or stock.⁴⁷

**Strategic Implication:** Distributors are **statutorily empowered** to extract concessions as the price of consenting to ABP's acquisition. Holdout distributors can demand:
- Contract term extensions (additional 2-3 years)
- Performance requirement reductions (10-15% target decreases)
- Marketing support increases ($0.25-$0.50 per case)
- Territory expansions or additional brand assignments
- Most-favored-nation rate parity clauses

#### 3. "Subterfuge" Doctrine Risk—Punitive Damages Exposure

Courts scrutinize whether "performance" terminations are **pretexts for consolidation.** If terminations cluster immediately post-acquisition and involve distributors previously meeting contractual requirements, courts may find "consolidation as subterfuge" triggering:⁵¹

- Full brand value compensation (no reduction for alleged performance deficiencies)
- **Punitive damages** equal to 2-3x compensatory damages
- Attorneys' fees awards to prevailing distributors
- Injunctive relief blocking further terminations

**Red Flag Indicators:**⁵²
- Timing: Terminations within 6-12 months of acquisition
- Pattern: Multiple terminations across different states simultaneously
- Internal documents: Emails or presentations referencing "consolidation strategy" or "rationalization plan"
- Pre-termination performance: Distributors meeting 80-90% of targets suddenly terminated for "performance"

**Risk Mitigation:** Any termination strategy must be supported by **rigorous, contemporaneous performance documentation** demonstrating legitimate "good cause" unrelated to consolidation objectives. Even then, 10-20% of terminated distributors typically file litigation, forcing costly defense regardless of substantive merits.

#### 4. "Grow Into" Strategy (Strategy B)—Recommended Alternative

Rather than immediate termination-based consolidation, the **"Grow Into" model** redirects resources to preferred partners while allowing natural market forces to consolidate over 5-10 years:⁵³

**Year 1-2: Designation of Strategic Partners**
- Identify 50 distributors as "strategic partners" based on performance, market coverage, financial strength
- **No terminations announced**—all 305 relationships maintained
- New product launches assigned exclusively to strategic partners
- Marketing fund allocations shift toward strategic partners (enhanced support)

**Year 3-5: Selective Consolidation and Natural Attrition**
- Terminate bottom 20% performers (60 distributors) with documented good cause
- Exercise Ohio-style successor manufacturer statutory rights where available (15-25 distributors)
- Natural attrition: 15-20% of non-strategic distributors retire, sell businesses voluntarily, or exit due to declining volume⁵⁴
- **Estimated cost:** $30M-$90M compensation over 3-5 years

**Year 6-10: Market-Driven Consolidation Completion**
- Strategic partners acquire non-strategic distributors through market transactions (ABP not directly involved)
- Remaining non-strategic distributors operate with static/declining volume as strategic partners capture growth
- Performance-based terminations of chronic underperformers (documented deficiencies)
- **Estimated cost:** $0-$30M additional compensation

**Total "Grow Into" Strategy Cost:** $30M-$120M over 10 years

**Cost Savings vs. Immediate Consolidation:** $63M-$415M (net savings of 34-78%)

**Payback Period:** 1.8-12 years (median: 4.5 years) vs. 18-54 years for immediate consolidation

**Additional Strategic Advantages:**
- Minimal litigation risk (natural attrition requires no terminations)
- No "consolidation as subterfuge" exposure (market forces drive outcomes)
- Preservation of distributor relationships during transition (avoids antagonizing entire network)
- Flexibility to adjust strategic partner designations based on performance evolution

**Recommendation:** ABP should **abandon immediate consolidation strategy** and adopt "Grow Into" model as official post-acquisition integration plan. This single strategic decision reduces quantified risk exposure by **$110M-$282M in expected value terms** (probability-weighted).⁵⁵

### H. Conclusion: Strategic Imperatives for Distributor Network Management

The comprehensive analysis of ASBC's 305-distributor network reveals five critical findings that must guide ABP's post-acquisition integration strategy:

**Finding 1: Immediate Consolidation Is Commercially Infeasible**

The $183M-$535M cost of consolidating from 305 to 50 distributors within 2-3 years produces unacceptable payback periods of 18-54 years. Franchise law requirements for "good cause," brand value compensation, and extended termination timelines (90-day notice + 60-day cure) combine with 10-20% litigation rates to render aggressive consolidation economically irrational.⁵⁹

**Finding 2: "Grow Into" Strategy Delivers Superior Risk-Adjusted Returns**

Phasing consolidation over 5-10 years through strategic resource allocation, natural attrition, and market-driven transactions reduces costs by 34-78% ($63M-$415M savings) while achieving comparable long-term outcomes. This patient approach avoids "consolidation as subterfuge" litigation risk and preserves critical distributor relationships during the transition period.⁶⁰

**Finding 3: Change-of-Control Consent Risk Is Transaction-Critical**

With 60-80% of agreements likely containing some form of consent requirement, ABP faces 183-244 separate consent negotiations. The Top 10 distributors (72% of volume) must provide unconditional consent as a **mandatory closing condition**—any alternative exposes ABP to material business continuity risk if major distribution partners exit post-closing.⁶¹

**Finding 4: Spirits Distributors Offer Consolidation Opportunity**

The 125 spirits distributors operate under materially weaker franchise protections (only 10-15 states extend franchise laws to spirits vs. 25+ for beer), enabling targeted consolidation at one-third the cost of equivalent beer distributor terminations. **Recommendation:** Prioritize spirits distributor consolidation in Years 2-3 post-closing while maintaining stability in the beer distribution network.⁶²

**Finding 5: Ohio Successor Manufacturer Exception Provides Tactical Advantage**

Ohio Rev. Code § 1333.851's statutory termination procedure allows ABP to exit 15-25 Ohio distributor relationships within 90 days of closing by paying court-determined diminished value—bypassing "good cause" litigation entirely. This rare exception should be exercised promptly within the statutory 90-day window.⁶³

**Ultimate Recommendation:** ABP should **formally abandon immediate consolidation strategy,** adopt "Grow Into" model as official integration policy, budget $30M-$120M for phased consolidation over 10 years, and prioritize pre-closing consent procurement from Top 50 distributors representing 80%+ of volume. This strategic pivot eliminates $110M-$282M of quantified risk exposure while achieving long-term distribution network optimization objectives.
