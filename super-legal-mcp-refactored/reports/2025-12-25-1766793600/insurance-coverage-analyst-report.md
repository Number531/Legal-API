**State Variation:**
Some states view timely notice as a core requirement and consider coverage void if notice is late, even when insurer suffered no prejudice (strict notice states).¬≤¬π

**Oregon's Position:**
Research indicates Oregon likely follows the **notice-prejudice rule** (majority approach), but specific Oregon case law should be confirmed.

**What Constitutes Prejudice:**
- Inability to investigate claim properly because notice provided only after important evidence destroyed

- Lost opportunity to settle claim within policy limits
- Inability to preserve subrogation rights¬≤¬≤

**Smith v. ASBC Application:**

ASBC must fully cooperate with insurer-appointed defense counsel once SIR exhausted and insurer assumes defense.

#### 4. Voluntary Payments and Settlement

**No Voluntary Payments Rule:**
Standard CGL policy: Insured cannot **settle without insurer's consent** (or admit liability, make voluntary payments)

**Consequences of Unauthorized Settlement:**
- If ASBC settles Smith case without insurer approval ‚Üí Insurer can **deny indemnity** (refuse to reimburse settlement amount)
- **Exception:** Insurer may still owe defense costs incurred prior to unauthorized settlement

**Settlement Authority:**
- **Before SIR exhausted:** ASBC controls settlement (paying within $100K SIR)
- **After SIR exhausted:** Insurer assumes control, ASBC needs insurer consent for settlement

**Recommendation:** ASBC should not settle Smith claim without insurer's advance written consent once insurer involved in defense.

### C. DUTY TO DEFEND AND RESERVATION OF RIGHTS

#### 1. Duty to Defend ‚Äî Eight Corners Rule

**Eight Corners Rule (Four Corners Rule):**
An insurer's duty to defend is assessed based **only on**:
1. **Four corners of the complaint** (allegations in plaintiff's lawsuit)
2. **Four corners of the insurance policy** (coverage grants and exclusions)

No extrinsic evidence may be considered when determining duty to defend.¬≤¬≥

**Broader Than Duty to Indemnify:**
The duty to defend is **broader** than the duty to indemnify. If complaint allegations **potentially** or **possibly** state a covered claim, insurer must defend **entire action**, even if some claims are not covered.¬≤‚Å¥

**Smith v. ASBC Application:**
- **Complaint allegations:** ASBC served alcohol to visibly intoxicated patron (Jones), Jones drove drunk, caused accident, injured Smith
- **Policy coverage:** Dram shop coverage for bodily injury caused by serving alcohol to intoxicated patrons
- **Duty to defend:** Complaint allegations **clearly state covered claim** ‚Üí Insurer has **duty to defend** ‚úì

**Extrinsic Evidence:**
At least 33 states now allow extrinsic evidence when determining duty to defend, but typically **only to trigger coverage**, not deny it.¬≤‚Åµ Insurer may not rely on extrinsic evidence to deny duty to defend.

#### 2. Reservation of Rights

**When Issued:**
If insurer has **potential coverage defenses** (exclusion may apply, policy limits issue, notice concerns), insurer defends under **reservation of rights** ‚Äî preserves right to deny coverage later if exclusion applies or facts develop showing no coverage.

**Effect:**
- Insurer provides defense now
- Insurer preserves right to deny indemnity later
- **Creates conflict of interest** between insurer and insured

**Smith v. ASBC ‚Äî Potential Reservation Issues:**
- If punitive damages sought ‚Üí Insurer may reserve rights on punitive damages coverage (typically excluded)
- If prior knowledge exclusion potentially applies ‚Üí Insurer may reserve rights
- **Likely:** Insurer will defend without reservation (dram shop coverage clearly applies, no apparent exclusions)

#### 3. Independent Counsel (Cumis Counsel)

**Cumis Counsel Doctrine:**
When insurer defends under reservation of rights, **conflict of interest** exists between insurer (wants to deny coverage) and insured (wants full coverage). In such cases, insured may have right to **independent counsel at insurer's expense** (Cumis counsel, named after *San Diego Navy Fed. Credit Union v. Cumis Ins. Society*, 162 Cal. App. 3d 358 (1984)).¬≤‚Å∂

**Oregon's Position:**
Oregon **has not fully adopted** Cumis counsel doctrine for general cases. Idaho and Oregon apparently either do not require Cumis counsel, or courts have not squarely addressed the issue.¬≤‚Å∑ Instead, Oregon requires all carrier-retained counsel to consider the insured the "primary client" and counsel's "dominant concern."¬≤‚Å∏

**Exception ‚Äî Environmental Claims:**
Oregon has specific statute (ORS 465.483) requiring **independent counsel** for environmental claims when insurer defends under reservation of rights.¬≤‚Åπ

**Smith v. ASBC Application:**
- If insurer defends without reservation ‚Üí No conflict, no Cumis counsel issue
- If insurer defends under reservation ‚Üí ASBC likely cannot demand independent counsel under Oregon law (unless environmental claim exception applies)
- **Alternative:** ASBC can demand panel counsel treat ASBC as "primary client" under Oregon standard

### E. EXCESS AND UMBRELLA LIABILITY COVERAGE

#### 1. Insurance Tower Structure

**Primary Layer:**

- **Typical structure:** $5M primary + $10M excess = **$15M total coverage**

#### 2. Follow Form vs. Stand-Alone Excess

**Follow Form Excess:**
A follow form excess liability policy sits above primary insurance and **mimics its terms, conditions, definitions, exclusions, and endorsements**, responding once primary limits exhausted.‚Å¥¬≤ Excess policies typically "follow form" and follow exactly the coverages of the underlying policy, stacking excess limit on top of primary limit.‚Å¥¬≥

**Umbrella Liability:**
An umbrella liability policy provides excess coverage over **multiple underlying policies** (GL, auto, employer's liability) and may offer **broader protection** than underlying policies.‚Å¥‚Å¥

**Drop Down Coverage:**
In some cases, umbrella policy will **"drop down"** to provide primary coverage when underlying policy doesn't respond, as long as loss would have been covered under umbrella's terms.‚Å¥‚Åµ Once aggregate limit exhausted by prior claims, umbrella pays first dollar up to umbrella aggregate limit.‚Å¥‚Å∂

#### 3. Exhaustion Requirements

**Zeig Rule (Exhaustion Default):**
The American Law Institute's Restatement describes the Zeig rule as the **"exhaustion default rule"** for excess insurance.‚Å¥‚Å∑ This does not mean underlying insurers must actually pay ‚Äî it means their **obligation to do so must be finally determined**.‚Å¥‚Å∏

**Smith v. ASBC Application:**
- **If verdict exceeds $5M:** (e.g., $7M verdict)
  - Primary GL pays: $5M
  - Excess policy pays: $2M (if excess coverage exists)
- **If settlement within $5M:** Excess policy not triggered

**Recommendation:**
- Verify whether ASBC has excess liability coverage above $5M primary
- If not, ASBC exposed to catastrophic verdict exceeding $5M (low probability given $1.85M-$3.85M exposure estimate, but possible if punitive damages awarded)
- **Risk mitigation:** Confirm excess coverage in place; if not, consider purchasing excess layer
