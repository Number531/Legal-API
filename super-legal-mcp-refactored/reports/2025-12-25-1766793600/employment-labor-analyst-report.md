**Background:**

ASBC terminated Gonzalez in **August 2024** (approximately 2 weeks post-complaint). Gonzalez filed EEOC charge in **October 2024** alleging national origin discrimination and retaliation.

**Prima Facie Retaliation Case:**
- **Protected activity:** Wage complaint protected under FLSA ¬ß 215(a)(3) and Colorado COMPS Order
- **Adverse action:** Termination
- **Causal link:** **2-week temporal proximity** between complaint and termination

**Legal Analysis:**
Courts consistently find 2-week temporal proximity "very close" and sufficient to establish prima facie retaliation case. Lower courts have specifically held that less than two weeks creates strong inference of causation. Post-*Nassar* (2013), temporal proximity alone insufficient to rebut employer's stated reason, but **temporal proximity combined with other evidence** (inconsistent explanations, procedural deviations) may defeat summary judgment.

**2025 Legal Development:**
Justice Thomas and Justice Gorsuch (March 2025) questioned whether McDonnell Douglas framework "distorts" analysis by requiring plaintiffs to "win" at summary judgment stage. Eleventh Circuit now requires "big picture" approach‚Äîeven if employer's explanation legitimate, **other evidence supporting retaliation inference** may survive summary judgment.

**Pretext Vulnerability:**

1. Customer complaints (3 complaints June-July 2024)
2. Cash register discrepancies ($350 shortage over 6 months)
3. Tardiness (late 8 times in 2024)

**Critical questions:**

All alleged tasks meet DOL standard for compensable time. Under *Steiner v. Bartender prep/cleanup clearly satisfies this standard‚Äîcannot bartend without stocked supplies, clean glassware, operational POS system.

If off-the-clock work pushed employees over 40 hours/week OR 12 hours/day, ASBC owes **overtime rate (1.5√ó)**, not straight time. Colorado 2024 update clarified tipped employee overtime rate is 1.5√ó minimum wage ($11.79 √ó 1.5 = $17.69/hour), **excluding tips** from calculation.

### D. DRAM SHOP LIABILITY: SMITH V. ASBC (MEDIUM-HIGH RISK)

**Plaintiff's Allegation:**
ASBC taproom staff inadequately trained, failed to recognize Jones' visible intoxication, over-served alcohol, resulting in damages to Smith.

**Oregon Dram Shop Law (ORS 471.565):**
Servers **NOT liable** for damages caused by intoxicated patrons UNLESS plaintiff proves by **clear and convincing evidence**:
1. Server served alcohol to **visibly intoxicated** patron
2. Service was **substantial factor** in causing injury
3. Injury was **foreseeable**
4. Plaintiff did **NOT substantially contribute** to patron's intoxication

**NO Statutory Safe Harbor for Training:**
ORS 471.565 provides NO provision stating "completion of OLCC training = no liability." Training evidence relevant to **comparative negligence analysis** (did ASBC act reasonably?), but not dispositive.

**Constitutional Issue (*Bonner v. American Golf Corp.*):**
Oregon Supreme Court held ORS 471.565(1) would be **unconstitutional** if server served alcohol to patron who reached point of **involuntary intoxication** (loss of reason/volition). If Jones reached involuntary intoxication state, ASBC's statutory shield may erode.

**ASBC Defense Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of supplemental training beyond OLCC minimum**
   - No role-playing scenarios for refusing service to difficult patrons
   - No practice in high-pressure situations (busy taproom, impatient customers)

2. **No documented cut-off policy or incident logs**
   - Absence of documentation = Plaintiff argues no intervention occurred
   - No paper trail showing staff attempted to cut off Jones or call manager

3. **No annual refresher training**
   - OLCC certification valid 5 years = long gap between training sessions
   - Skills/knowledge atrophy over time without reinforcement

**Supplemental Training Recommendations:**
To strengthen dram shop defense:
- **Role-playing scenarios:** Practice refusing service, handling confrontations
- **Manager supervision protocols:** Train managers to monitor staff, intervene when uncertain
- **Mandatory incident logs:** Document patron name, time, intoxication signs, actions taken
- **Annual refreshers:** Reinforce intoxication recognition beyond 5-year OLCC cycle

**Exposure Range:**
Depends on injury severity (Smith's damages):
- **Minor injury:** $100,000 - $250,000
- **Moderate injury:** $500,000 - $1,500,000
- **Severe injury/death:** $2,000,000 - $7,000,000+

**Insurance:** ASBC's liquor liability policy limits critical. Typical craft brewery policies: $1M-$2M per occurrence. If exposure exceeds policy limits, ASBC personally liable for excess.

### C. DRAM SHOP LIABILITY AND TRAINING DEFENSE

#### 1. Smith v. ASBC Context

**Plaintiff's Allegation:**
ASBC's taproom staff were **inadequately trained**, failed to recognize Jones' visible intoxication, and **over-served** alcohol, resulting in damages.

**ASBC's Asserted Defense:**
Staff completed Oregon Alcohol Server Education (OLCC-required training), demonstrating reasonable care.

**Key Legal Question:**
Does completion of mandatory state alcohol server training provide sufficient liability protection in dram shop litigation?

---

#### 2. Oregon Dram Shop Law: ORS 471.565

**Statutory Framework:**

**ORS 471.565(1):** A patron who **voluntarily consumes** alcoholic beverages does not have a cause of action against the server, **even if beverages are served while the patron is visibly intoxicated**.‚Å¥¬≤

**ORS 471.565(2):** Servers are **not liable** for damages caused by intoxicated patrons **UNLESS** the plaintiff proves by **clear and convincing evidence**:
1. The server served alcohol to a **visibly intoxicated** patron
2. The server's service was a **substantial factor** in causing plaintiff's injury
3. The injury was **foreseeable**
4. The plaintiff did **not substantially contribute** to the patron's intoxication‚Å¥¬≥

**Oregon Supreme Court Constitutional Clarification:**
In *Bonner v. American Golf Corp.*, the Oregon Supreme Court held that ORS 471.565(1) would be **unconstitutional** if applied when:
- A server serves alcohol to a visibly intoxicated person who has reached the point at which **further consumption is involuntary** (loss of reason and volition)
- The distinction hinges on **voluntary vs. involuntary intoxication**‚Å¥‚Å¥

**Key Implication:** If Jones had reached a state of involuntary intoxication, ASBC's liability shield may erode.

---

#### 3. Does Server Training Provide Liability Protection?

**Oregon Statutory Analysis:**
ORS 471.565 provides **NO statutory safe harbor** for server training completion. The statute does not state "completion of OLCC training = no liability."‚Å¥‚Åµ

**However:**
- **Comparative negligence analysis:** Training evidence is relevant to whether ASBC acted reasonably
- **Jury consideration:** Did staff receive reasonable training to recognize intoxication signs and intervene appropriately?
- **Standard of care:** What training would a reasonable establishment provide?‚Å¥‚Å∂

**OLCC-Required Training as Minimum Standard:**
- Oregon law mandates OLCC Alcohol Server Education
- Completion demonstrates **minimum compliance** with state standard
- BUT: May not suffice if **additional training** reasonably necessary given context (high-volume taproom, late-night service)

---

#### 4. Training Content Required to Defend Dram Shop Claims

**A. Recognize Intoxication Signs**

OLCC-trained servers must reasonably recognize **observable indicators of intoxication**:‚Å¥‚Å∑
- **Physical signs:** Slurred speech, bloodshot eyes, unsteady gait, impaired coordination
- **Behavioral signs:** Aggressive behavior, impaired judgment, emotional volatility, slowed reaction time
- **Cognitive signs:** Confusion, disorientation, difficulty focusing

**Training Deficiency Risk:**
If ASBC's staff failed to recognize Jones' visible intoxication despite OLCC training:
- Plaintiff argues training was **inadequate** (insufficient practice/reinforcement)
- Plaintiff argues training was **not applied** (lack of management oversight)

**B. Intervention Techniques**

Effective training must include **how to cut off service** to intoxicated patrons:
- Polite but firm refusal of service
- Offer food and water
- Call taxi/rideshare or arrange safe transportation
- **NEVER allow visibly intoxicated patron to drive**
- Involve manager if patron becomes belligerent

**C. Documentation Procedures**

**CRITICAL DEFENSE ELEMENT:**
Establishments with **incident documentation policies** strengthen dram shop defense:‚Å¥‚Å∏
- **Cut-off logs:** Document patron name, time, observable intoxication signs, refusal of service
- **Manager notification:** Require manager approval/documentation of service refusals
- **Creates paper trail:** Demonstrates establishment took reasonable steps to prevent over-service

**ASBC Documentation Gap Assessment:**

- Was there any documentation of Jones' intoxication level or intervention attempts?
- **Absence of documentation** = Plaintiff argues no intervention occurred

#### 2. FLSA "Integral and Indispensable" Standard

**Compensable Time Principle:**
Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), compensable time includes **all hours during which a non-exempt employee is suffered or permitted to work**, meaning time the employer requires or allows an employee to work, regardless of whether the work is authorized, scheduled, or requested.‚Å¥‚Åπ

**"Integral and Indispensable" Test:**
If an employee performs tasks before or after their scheduled shift that are **integral and indispensable** to their primary job duties (e.g., setting up equipment, logging into system, changing into protective gear), that time is considered compensable.‚Åµ‚Å∞

**Steiner v. Mitchell Foundation:**
*Steiner v. Mitchell* (1956) established that time workers spend on activities performed before or after regular working hours, on or off the production floor, is compensable under the FLSA if the activities are **integral and indispensable parts** of the principal activity of the worker's employment.‚Åµ¬π

#### 3. Are Gonzalez's Prep/Cleanup Tasks Compensable?

**Analysis of Bartender Duties:**

| Task | Integral & Indispensable? | Compensable? | Rationale |
|------|---------------------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Stocking bar** (beer, liquor, garnishes) | **YES** | **YES** | Cannot bartend without stocked supplies |
| **Cutting garnishes** (limes, lemons, oranges) | **YES** | **YES** | Essential preparation for cocktail service |
| **Setting up POS system** | **YES** | **YES** | Cannot process orders/payments without POS |
| **Washing glassware** (post-shift) | **YES** | **YES** | Maintaining clean glassware integral to operations |
| **Restocking supplies** (post-shift) | **YES** | **YES** | Ensures readiness for next shift |
| **Cashing out register** | **YES** | **YES** | Required reconciliation of sales |

**Conclusion:** All tasks Gonzalez identified are **integral and indispensable** to bartending. ASBC **must compensate** for this time.‚Åµ¬≤

**DOL Guidance:**
In general, any activity an employee performs that **benefits their employer should be paid**.‚Åµ¬≥ Employers are responsible for preventing off-the-clock work and must enforce policies that prohibit it. Ignoring unauthorized work does not excuse the employer from compensating for it.‚Åµ‚Å¥

#### 4. Colorado COMPS Order Overtime Rules

**Colorado Minimum Wage (2024-2025):**
- **Non-tipped employees:** $14.81/hour (2025)‚Åµ‚Åµ
- **Tipped employees:** $11.79/hour (2025) + tips (employer must make up difference if tips don't reach minimum)‚Åµ‚Å∂

**Tipped Employee Definition (2024 Update):**
Employees are considered "tipped employees" when they regularly receive **more than $1.64 per hour in tips** averaged over any pay period (replaces previous $30/month threshold).‚Åµ‚Å∑

**Overtime Calculation (Critical Update):**
Colorado updated its "regular rate of pay" rule to **expressly exclude tips from regular rates**, clarifying that "a tipped minimum wage employee's overtime rate is 1.5 times minimum wage, **not** 1.5 times the sum of minimum wage plus their tips."‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Colorado Overtime Triggers:**
Employers must pay **1.5 times** employee's regular rate of pay for hours past:
- **(A) 40 hours per week**, OR
- **(B) 12 hours per day**, OR
- **(C) 12 consecutive hours**
‚Äî **Whichever results in higher pay**.‚Åµ‚Åπ

**Off-the-Clock Work Violation:**
If Gonzalez worked 15-30 min/shift prep/cleanup unpaid, and these hours pushed her **over 40 hours/week or 12 hours/day**, ASBC owes **overtime rate** (not straight time) for those hours.

### E. WARN ACT ANALYSIS

#### 1. WARN Act Overview

**Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 2101):**
The WARN Act requires covered employers to provide **60 days advance written notice** to employees prior to any **plant closing** or **mass layoff**.‚Å∂‚Å∑

**Purpose:**
Provide employees and communities time to prepare for job loss and seek alternative employment or retraining.

---

#### 2. WARN Act Applicability

**Employer Coverage:**
WARN Act applies to employers with:
- **100 or more full-time employees**, OR
- **100 or more employees** (full-time and part-time) who work **aggregate 4,000+ hours/week**‚Å∂‚Å∏

**ASBC Status:**
- **1,850 employees** ‚Üí **WARN Act APPLIES**

---

#### 3. Triggering Events

**A. Plant Closing:**
Permanent or temporary shutdown of a **single site of employment** OR one or more **facilities/operating units** within a single site that results in **50 or more employees** losing employment during any 30-day period.‚Å∂‚Åπ

**B. Mass Layoff:**
Reduction in force at a single site of employment during any 30-day period that results in employment loss for:
- **50-499 employees** if they constitute **at least 33% of active workforce**, OR
- **500 or more employees** (regardless of percentage)‚Å∑‚Å∞

---

#### 4. ABP Acquisition: Potential WARN Triggers

**Post-Acquisition Restructuring Scenarios:**

| Scenario | WARN Trigger? | Notice Required? | Exposure |
|----------|---------------|------------------|----------|
| **Close 1+ breweries** (50+ employees) | **YES** (plant closing) | 60 days | Back pay + benefits (60 days √ó affected employees) |
| **Close 3+ taprooms** (50+ employees total at single site) | **YES** (plant closing) | 60 days | Same |
| **Layoff 50-499 employees** (33%+ of site workforce) | **YES** (mass layoff) | 60 days | Same |
| **Layoff 500+ employees** (any percentage) | **YES** (mass layoff) | 60 days | Same |
| **Consolidate corporate functions** (eliminate 50+ roles at HQ) | **YES** (plant closing) | 60 days | Same |

**Key Consideration:**
"Single site of employment" analyzed separately‚Äîcannot aggregate layoffs across multiple locations unless under common operational control.‚Å∑¬π

---

#### 5. Seller vs. Buyer WARN Liability in Acquisitions

**Critical Rule:**
In a **business sale**, WARN Act responsibility splits between seller and buyer:

**A. Seller's Responsibility:**
The seller is responsible to provide notice for any plant closing or mass layoff **up to and including the effective date of the sale**.‚Å∑¬≤

**B. Buyer's Responsibility:**
The purchaser is responsible for providing notice for any plant closing or mass layoff that occurs **after the effective date of the sale**.‚Å∑¬≥

---

#### 6. Asset Sale vs. Stock Sale Distinction

**Asset Sale:**
When a case involves simply a **sale of assets**, as opposed to the sale of a business as a going concern, the **seller retains WARN Act notice responsibility** because the seller is the party actually closing the plant that results in the employment loss.‚Å∑‚Å¥

**Going Concern Sale (Stock Purchase):**
If the sale is on a **going-concern basis**, it is presumed that the sale involves the hiring of the seller's employees **unless something indicates otherwise**, whether or not the sale agreement expressly provides for retention of the seller's employees.‚Å∑‚Åµ

**ABP-ASBC Transaction:**
- If **asset purchase**: ASBC (seller) responsible for pre-closing layoffs
- If **stock purchase**: ABP may inherit ASBC employees; ABP responsible for post-closing layoffs

---

#### 7. WARN Act 60-Day Notice Requirements

**Who Receives Notice:**

**A. Affected Employees (or Union Representatives):**
- Written notice to each affected employee individually
- If union represents employees, notice to union representative‚Å∑‚Å∂

**B. State Dislocated Worker Unit:**
- Colorado: Colorado Department of Labor and Employment
- Oregon: Oregon Employment Department
- North Carolina: NC Division of Workforce Solutions
- Washington: Washington Employment Security Department
- California: California Employment Development Department‚Å∑‚Å∑

**C. Local Government:**
- Chief elected official of local government where employment site located

**Notice Content:**
- Name and address of employment site
- Contact information of company official
- Expected date of layoff/closure
- Job titles and number of affected employees‚Å∑‚Å∏

---

#### 8. WARN Act Exceptions (Reducing Notice Period)

**A. Faltering Company:**
Employer actively seeking capital/business that would avoid/postpone shutdown, and employer reasonably believed that giving notice would preclude obtaining capital/business.‚Å∑‚Åπ

**B. Unforeseeable Business Circumstances:**
Layoffs caused by business circumstances that were **not reasonably foreseeable** at the time 60-day notice would have been required.‚Å∏‚Å∞
- **Third Circuit Standard:** Requires layoffs be **probable**, not merely **possible**‚Å∏¬π

**C. Natural Disaster:**
Flood, earthquake, drought, storm, fire, or similar natural disaster.‚Å∏¬≤

---

#### 9. WARN Act Penalties for Non-Compliance

**Employer Liability:**
Employers who violate the WARN Act by failing to provide the required notice may be liable for:

**A. Back Pay:**
- **Up to 60 days** back pay for each affected employee
- Calculated from date employment loss occurred

**B. Benefits:**
- Value of benefits (health insurance, retirement contributions) for **up to 60 days**

**C. Civil Penalties:**
- **Up to $500 per day** for failure to notify local government (not to exceed 60 days = $30,000 max)‚Å∏¬≥

**D. Attorney's Fees:**
- Prevailing employees entitled to reasonable attorney's fees
