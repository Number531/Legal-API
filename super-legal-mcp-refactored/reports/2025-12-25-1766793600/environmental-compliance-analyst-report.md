#### 5. CERCLA Successor Liability (CRITICAL RISK â€” Phase I ESAs Missing)

**Most critical environmental risk identified:** No evidence that ASBC has conducted **Phase I Environmental Site Assessments (ESAs)** at any of its 5 facilities. Under CERCLA Â§ 107(a)(1), current property owners are **strictly, jointly, and severally liable** for environmental contamination regardless of fault. Asset purchasers inherit this liability under **federal common law successor liability doctrine** applied by most federal circuits.

**Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser (BFPP) Defense (CERCLA Â§ 107(r)):**
Acquirer can avoid CERCLA liability if:
1. **All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI) conducted** â€” Phase I ESA per ASTM E1527-21 standard (effective Feb. 13, 2024)
2. Purchaser **not affiliated** with any potentially responsible party (PRP)
3. Appropriate care exercised, precautions taken against releases
4. Full cooperation with response actions, compliance with institutional controls

**WITHOUT PHASE I ESAs: BFPP DEFENSE UNAVAILABLE.** Acquirer assumes unlimited, joint and several liability for pre-existing contamination, including:
- Underground storage tank (UST) leaks (petroleum hydrocarbons)
- Historical solvent disposal (chlorinated solvents, VOCs)
- Metals contamination (lead, arsenic, chromium)
- Off-site disposal liability (Superfund contribution claims from other PRPs)

**Brewery/Distillery Contamination Concerns (Lower-Risk Profile):**
- Modern craft facilities (post-2000) have minimal contamination risk compared to heavy manufacturing
- Primary wastes are organic (spent grains, yeast, stillage) â€” non-hazardous
- Natural gas fuel (no fuel oil USTs)
- Smaller chemical inventories

**However:** Historical uses of property (pre-brewery/distillery), neighboring contamination, or legacy USTs could result in significant liability. **Phase I ESA is only method to identify these risks before acquisition.**

**Mandatory Pre-Closing Action:**
Commission **Phase I ESAs for all 5 ASBC facilities** (Portland brewery, Portland distillery, Denver brewery, Asheville brewery, Hood River meadery).

**Cost & Timeline:**

- **Total Phase I budget: $15,000-$30,000 (5 facilities)**
- Timeline: 2-4 weeks per facility (can be conducted in parallel)

**Penalty/Liability Exposure:** CERCLA cleanup costs can range from $100,000 (minor petroleum release) to $10M+ (Superfund site). **Unlimited joint and several liability** for contamination â€” any PRP can be liable for entire cleanup cost. Phase I ESA investment ($15K-$30K) is de minimis compared to potential CERCLA exposure.

### C. DISTILLERY STILLAGE DISPOSAL

#### 1. Stillage Characteristics and Regulatory Status

**Portland Distillery Operations (100,000 proof gallons/year):**

**Stillage Generation:**
- Volume: 10-15 gallons stillage per gallon spirits produced
- Annual production: **530,000-795,000 gallons/year** stillage
- Characteristics:
  - BOD: 30,000-60,000 mg/L (100-300x higher than brewery wastewater)
  - High solids content (spent grains, yeast)
  - pH: 3.5-4.5 (acidic)
  - Temperature: 80-95Â°C when discharged from still

**Why Stillage Cannot Be Sewered:**
- BOD load would overwhelm POTW treatment capacity
- Example: 650,000 gallons stillage at 45,000 mg/L BOD = 244,000 lbs BOD/year
- Equivalent to wastewater from population of 11,000 people
- Would require extensive (cost-prohibitive) pretreatment: multi-stage anaerobic/aerobic digestion, evaporation

#### 2. Oregon Beneficial Reuse Regulations

**Agricultural Use Exclusion (OAR 340-093-0030):**

Oregon DEQ exempts "industrial food processing wastes and spent brewery grains fed to livestock" from solid waste regulation when:
- Materials used "on land in agricultural operations and the growing or harvesting of crops and the raising of fowls or animals"
- Application at or below **agronomic rates** (nutrient uptake capacity of crops/pasture)Â¹Â²

**Regulatory Coordination:**
- Oregon DEQ and Oregon Department of Agriculture signed Memorandum of Understanding (May 2017) for coordinating agricultural material use oversightÂ¹Â³
- No solid waste permit required for qualifying beneficial reuse
- No volumetric caps specified in regulations

**Applicability to Distillery Stillage:**
- Stillage not explicitly named in Agricultural Use Exclusion (unlike "spent brewery grains")
- High organic content (protein, minerals) valuable for livestock nutrition
- Industry practice: Distillery stillage widely used as cattle feed supplement (dairy and beef operations)

#### 3. Disposal/Beneficial Reuse Options

**Option A: Livestock Feed (Recommended for Cost and Compliance):**

**Advantages:**
- **No disposal cost** (farmers typically accept free or pay nominal fee)
- Qualifies for Oregon Agricultural Use Exclusion (no DEQ permit)
- Stillage contains 20-30% protein (dry basis), valuable for dairy cattle
- Reduces farmer feed costs ($0.10-$0.15/gallon equivalent feed value)

**Requirements:**
- Farm must have adequate land for agronomic application (nutrient management)
- Transportation logistics: Tanker truck delivery 2-3x per week
- Storage at farm: Concrete bunker or lagoon (stillage ferments/degrades if stored >5 days)

**Estimated Costs:**
- Transportation: $0.05-$0.10/gallon (100 miles radius) = $26,500-$79,500/year
- No disposal fee (beneficial reuse)

**Option B: Land Application (Agricultural Fertilizer):**

**Regulatory Requirements:**
- Oregon DEQ Land Application Permit may be required (depends on application rate, site characteristics)
- Agronomic rates: Typically 5,000-15,000 gallons/acre/year (based on nitrogen loading)
- For 650,000 gallons: Requires 43-130 acres application area

**Costs:**
- Permit: $5,000-$10,000 initial, $2,000-$5,000 annual
- Transportation + spreading: $0.15-$0.30/gallon = $97,500-$238,500/year

**Option C: Waste Hauler (Least Preferred â€” Expensive):**

**Process:**
- Contract liquid waste hauler to pump and transport stillage to wastewater treatment facility or composting operation

**Costs:**
- Typical rates: $0.10-$0.30/gallon including hauling and disposal
- Annual cost: $53,000-$238,500 for 530,000-795,000 gallons

**Option D: On-Site Treatment (Capital Intensive):**

**Technologies:**
- Anaerobic digestion with biogas recovery: $500K-$1.5M capital, $50K-$100K annual operating
- Evaporation system: $300K-$800K capital, high energy costs ($80K-$150K/year natural gas/electricity)
