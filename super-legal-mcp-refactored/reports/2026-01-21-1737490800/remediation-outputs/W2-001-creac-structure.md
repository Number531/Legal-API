### 3. Explanation

Surplus notes are subordinated debt instruments issued by life insurers that receive favorable regulatory treatment under statutory accounting principles. Unlike traditional debt, surplus notes are classified as surplus (not liabilities) on statutory balance sheets and receive 100% credit toward Total Adjusted Capital for RBC purposes.

The tax treatment of surplus notes follows general debt principles under IRC § 163(a), which permits deduction of "all interest paid or accrued within the taxable year on indebtedness." The IRS has consistently ruled that surplus notes constitute debt for federal income tax purposes despite their equity-like treatment under statutory accounting. *Metropolitan Life Insurance Co. v. State insurance department approval is required for both surplus note issuance and each subsequent interest payment. Regulators evaluate whether interest payments impair statutory surplus or RBC ratios, and typically approve payments when the insurer maintains RBC >200% and interest coverage ratio >2.0× (statutory net income ÷ interest expense).

Alternative capital structures include subordinated debt (receives only 25% RBC credit), preferred stock (receives limited credit depending on terms), and common equity (receives 100% credit but no tax deduction). The capital structure optimization analysis must consider RBC efficiency, tax benefits, and cost of capital.
