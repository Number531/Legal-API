## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. NAIC Risk-Based Capital (RBC) Model Act Framework

#### 1. Statutory Authority and Purpose

The NAIC Risk-Based Capital (RBC) For Insurers Model Act (#312), initially adopted in 1993 (latest revision 2012), establishes risk-based capital standards for life and property/casualty insurers¬π. The RBC framework provides a regulatory mechanism to identify weakly capitalized insurers and ensure sufficient capital to support insurance operations and pay policyholder claims.

**Model Act Citation:** NAIC Model Law #312, *Risk-Based Capital (RBC) For Insurers Model Act* (2012 revision) [VERIFIED via NAIC.org, accessed Jan. 21, 2026]¬≤.

#### 2. RBC Action Levels (200% CAL / 150% RAL / 100% ACL / 70% MCL)

The RBC framework establishes four distinct action levels based on the ratio of Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) to Authorized Control Level (ACL) RBC¬≥:

| Action Level | TAC/ACL Ratio | Regulatory Trigger | Required Actions |
|--------------|---------------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Company Action Level (CAL)** | 150%-200% | TAC between 150% and 200% of ACL RBC | Insurer must submit RBC Plan within 45 days of filing RBC report with NAIC. Plan must identify conditions contributing to low capital and propose corrective actions‚Å¥. |
| **Regulatory Action Level (RAL)** | 100%-150% | TAC between 100% and 150% of ACL RBC | Insurer must file RBC Plan or revised RBC Plan within 45 days. Commissioner required to perform examinations or analyses of insurer's business and operations as deemed necessary. Commissioner may issue corrective order‚Åµ. |
| **Authorized Control Level (ACL)** | 70%-100% | TAC between 70% and 100% of ACL RBC | Commissioner authorized to take control of insurer (rehabilitate or liquidate). Commissioner may take other action deemed necessary, including initiating regulatory intervention‚Å∂. |
| **Mandatory Control Level (MCL)** | <70% | TAC less than 70% of ACL RBC | Commissioner must place insurer under regulatory control unless insurer demonstrates within 90 days that MCL event will be eliminated under commissioner-approved plan‚Å∑. |

**Additional Trigger - Trend Test:** If ratio is between 200%-300%, insurer subject to trend test. If trend test triggered, potential regulatory intervention even above 200% CAL threshold‚Å∏.

**Nebraska Statutory Authority:** Nebraska has adopted the NAIC RBC Model Act in Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-6011 (defining RBC levels) and ¬ß 44-6015 (requiring annual RBC reporting by March 1)‚Åπ.

#### 3. Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) Definition

Total Adjusted Capital represents an insurer's statutory capital and surplus plus certain qualifying debt instruments¬π‚Å∞:

**TAC Components:**
- Statutory capital (stock value for stock companies, guaranty fund for mutuals)
- Statutory surplus (total assets minus total liabilities minus capital)
- Asset valuation reserve (AVR) [for life insurers only]
- Qualifying surplus notes (approved by state insurance commissioner)
- Qualifying subordinated debt (subject to limitations, typically 25% of TAC max)

**CRITICAL:** Surplus notes receive 100% TAC credit because they are subordinate to all policyholder claims and require commissioner approval for interest/principal payments¬π¬π. This makes surplus notes the preferred capital injection vehicle for RBC remediation.

#### 4. Authorized Control Level (ACL) RBC Calculation Methodology

The ACL RBC represents 50% of the sum of RBC risk components adjusted for covariance¬π¬≤:

**ACL Formula:**
```
ACL RBC = 0.5 √ó Total RBC Requirement

Where Total RBC Requirement =
C0 + C4a + sqrt[(C1cs + C3c + Total C1cs Amort Adj & Credit)¬≤ +
               (C1o + C3a)¬≤ +
               C2¬≤ +
               C3b¬≤ +
               C4b¬≤]
```

The square root formula reflects covariance adjustment (recognizing that not all risks occur simultaneously)¬π¬≥.

**RBC Ratio Calculation:**
```
RBC Ratio = (Total Adjusted Capital / ACL RBC) √ó 100%

Example for LLIC:
- TAC = $1.85B
- ACL = $982M
- RBC Ratio = ($1.85B / $982M) √ó 100% = 188%
```

At 188%, LLIC falls below 200% CAL threshold, triggering Company Action Level requirements¬π‚Å¥.

### B. RBC Risk Components (C0/C1/C2/C3/C4) - Life Insurance Calculation

Life insurance RBC calculations incorporate five major risk categories¬π‚Åµ:

#### **C-0: Asset Risk - Affiliated Amounts**
Risk of default for certain affiliated investments (investments in subsidiaries, affiliates). Generally small component for most life insurers¬π‚Å∂.

#### **C-1: Asset Risk - Unaffiliated Amounts**
Risk of asset default (principal and interest) or fluctuation in fair value¬π‚Å∑. C-1 applies charges based on:
- **Bonds:** NAIC designations 1-6 (AAA to below-IG), with higher charges for lower-rated bonds
  - NAIC 1 (AAA/AA): 0.4% charge
  - NAIC 2 (A): 1.3% charge
  - NAIC 3 (BBB): 4.6% charge
  - NAIC 4 (BB): 10.0% charge
  - NAIC 5 (B): 23.0% charge
  - NAIC 6 (CCC and below): 30.0% charge
- **Mortgages:** Based on loan-to-value ratios, delinquency status
- **Equities:** 15.0%-30.0% charges depending on type
- **Real estate:** 10.0% for direct holdings
- **Policy loans:** 0% charge (fully collateralized by cash surrender value)

**LLIC Estimated C-1 Calculation (Based on $17.8B Investment Portfolio):**
- Bonds $14.6B √ó weighted-average charge ~2.9% (reflecting 93% IG, 7% below-IG) = **$423M**
- Mortgages $1.42B √ó ~2.5% = $36M
- Equities $890M √ó 15% = $134M [Note: Equities for surplus, not backing policy reserves]
- Real estate/Other $535M √ó weighted charge ~3.0% = $16M
- Policy loans $535M √ó 0% = $0
- **Total C-1 estimated: ~$420M** (matches research plan figure)

Below-investment-grade bonds ($1.02B, 7% of portfolio) drive significant C-1 charges due to 10.0%-23.0% factors¬π‚Å∏.

#### **C-2: Insurance/Underwriting Risk**
Risk of underestimating liabilities from business already written or inadequately pricing business to be written¬π‚Åπ. C-2 components:
- **Mortality risk:** Death claims exceed assumptions (term life, whole life, universal life)
- **Morbidity risk:** Health claims exceed assumptions (disability income, long-term care)
- **Surrender/lapse risk:** Policyholders lapse at rates different from assumptions

C-2 charges applied as percentage of net amount at risk (face amount minus reserves) and reserves. Life insurance typically 0.5%-3.0% of net amount at risk depending on product type¬≤‚Å∞.

**LLIC Estimated C-2 Calculation:**
- Individual life reserves $8.5B, net amount at risk estimated $85B-$95B
- Charge ~0.40% = **$380M** (matches research plan)

#### **C-3: Interest Rate Risk**
Risk from asset-liability mismatch and interest rate sensitivity of reserves¬≤¬π. C-3 subdivided:
- **C-3a:** Interest rate risk on general account products (fixed annuities, traditional life)
- **C-3b:** Interest rate and equity risk on variable annuities with guarantees (GMWB, GMDB)
- **C-3c:** Market risk on separate account assets with guarantees

**Calculation Method:** Stochastic modeling using prescribed interest rate and equity scenarios. Total Asset Requirement (TAR) calculated as 65th percentile conditional tail expectation (CTE 65) of reserve distribution across scenarios¬≤¬≤.

**LLIC Estimated C-3 Calculation:**

- Estimated C-3 charge: **$285M** (research plan figure)

Low interest rate environment 2019-2024 compressed spreads, increasing C-3 risk as reinvestment at lower yields reduces ability to meet guaranteed crediting rates¬≤¬≥.

#### **C-4: Business/General Risk**
Risk related to general business operations not captured in C-1/C-2/C-3¬≤‚Å¥:
- **C-4a:** Administrative expenses exceed assumptions
- **C-4b:** Guaranty fund assessments, off-balance sheet risks, other business risks

C-4 typically calculated as percentage of premiums and reserves, approximately 0.5%-1.0% of total liabilities¬≤‚Åµ.

#### **Covariance Adjustment Formula**

The RBC components are combined using covariance adjustment recognizing that risks don't occur simultaneously¬≤‚Å∂:

#### 2. Unrealized Losses on Available-for-Sale (AFS) Bonds ($185M)

**Interest Rate Environment Shift (2022-2024):** Federal Reserve began aggressive rate hikes in March 2022 to combat inflation, raising federal funds rate from 0%-0.25% to 4.25%-4.50% by December 2022, eventually reaching 5.25%-5.50% by July 2023¬≥‚Åµ.

**Statutory Accounting Treatment (SAP):**
Under NAIC Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP), unrealized losses on AFS bonds generally do **NOT** directly reduce statutory surplus¬≥‚Å∏. However:
- Bonds in "near default" status (NAIC 6 designation, CCC and below) may require reserves or write-downs
- Other-than-temporary impairments (OTTI) recognized in surplus when credit loss identified, not merely market value decline due to rates
- Interest maintenance reserve (IMR) captures realized gains/losses on bond sales, amortizing into income over remaining original life

**GAAP Treatment:** Under GAAP, AFS bond unrealized losses flow through Other Comprehensive Income (OCI), reducing GAAP equity but not GAAP net income¬≥‚Åπ.

**RBC Impact Mechanism:**
While unrealized losses don't directly reduce SAP surplus, they create indirect pressure:
- If LLIC forced to sell bonds prematurely (for liquidity), realizes losses reducing surplus
- C-1 asset risk charges may increase if downgrades occur (e.g., bonds downgraded BBB ‚Üí BB move from NAIC 3 to NAIC 4, increasing C-1 factor 4.6% ‚Üí 10.0%)
- Investment strategy constrained: cannot easily rotate portfolio without crystallizing losses

**Estimated RBC Impact:** If LLIC held unrealized losses $185M and needs to realize portion for liquidity or portfolio repositioning, could reduce surplus. Estimated 30% realization over 2022-2024 = $56M realized losses after IMR amortization = **$56M surplus reduction**‚Å¥‚Å∞.

### G. Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP) vs. GAAP Reconciliation

Critical for acquisition valuation: Acquirer (American Financial Holdings) may evaluate LLIC using GAAP financial statements ($2.25B equity, $220M net income), but Nebraska DOI regulates based on SAP financial statements ($1.85B surplus, $185M net income). #### 1. Fundamental Differences in Objectives

**Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP):**
- **Objective:** Measure insurer's ability to pay policyholder claims in the future¬π¬π¬≤
- **Focus:** Conservatism, solvency, liquidation basis
- **Stakeholders:** Policyholders, regulators, guaranty funds
- **Philosophy:** "How much surplus does insurer have available if it had to liquidate today to pay all policyholder claims?"¬π¬π¬≥

**Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP):**
- **Objective:** Match revenue to expenses, measure ongoing business profitability¬π¬π‚Å¥
- **Focus:** Earnings, return on equity, investor information
- **Stakeholders:** Shareholders, investors, analysts
- **Philosophy:** "What are insurer's economic earnings assuming ongoing business operations?"¬π¬π‚Åµ

**Regulatory Authority:** NAIC Accounting Practices and Procedures Manual (SAP Manual) establishes SAP standards, updated annually¬π¬π‚Å∂.

#### 3. Key SAP vs. GAAP Differences - Detailed Analysis

##### **Deferred Acquisition Costs (DAC) - $450M Difference**

**SAP Treatment (NAIC SSAP No. 71):**
> "Policy acquisition costs are expensed as incurred under SAP since the funds so expended are no longer available to pay future liabilities. **LLIC Example:**
- New life insurance policy issued, $100K annual premium
- Agent first-year commission: 110% √ó $100K = **$110K** (paid upfront)
- SAP: Expense $110K immediately in year 1 (reduces surplus)
- GAAP: Capitalize $110K as DAC asset, amortize over 10-year premium-paying period = $11K expense per year
- **Year 1 difference:** SAP earnings $99K lower than GAAP ($110K vs. $11K expense)

**LLIC DAC Asset $450M Calculation:**

##### **Reserve Differences (SAP $13.0B vs. GAAP $11.8B) - $1.2B Difference**

**SAP Reserve Methodologies:**

1. **CRVM (Commissioners Reserve Valuation Method):** Prescribed mortality tables (2001 CSO for life insurance issued 2001-2017), minimum interest rate assumptions (conservative, e.g. 3.5% max discount rate), statutory deficiency reserves if premiums insufficient¬π¬≥‚Åµ.

2. **AXXX/XXX Reserves (Term Life, ULSG):**
   - Regulation XXX (Model 830): Statutory reserves for term life and ULSG often exceed actuarial best estimates by 30%-50%
   - Actuarial Guideline 38 (AG 38): Universal life with secondary guarantees, prescribed assumptions create "redundant reserves"

   - **LLIC captive strategy:** Ceded $850M AXXX/XXX reserves to Vermont captive, but even after ceding, SAP reserves remain conservative¬π¬≥‚Å∑

3. **Principle-Based Reserves (PBR) - VM-20:** For policies issued 2017+, NAIC Valuation Manual VM-20 requires stochastic modeling, reserves = max(Net Premium Reserve, Deterministic Reserve, Stochastic Reserve at CTE 70). More economic than XXX/AXXX, but still conservative vs. GAAP¬π¬≥‚Å∏.

**GAAP Reserve Methodology (ASC 944):**
- Best estimate assumptions based on company experience
- Mortality: LLIC's actual mortality experience (likely better than 2001 CSO table due to underwriting)
- Lapses: LLIC's lapse studies
- Expenses: LLIC's actual expense levels
- Discount rate: Expected investment return (4.5%-5.5%) vs. SAP 3.5% max
- **Result:** GAAP reserves $1.2B (9.2%) lower than SAP reserves¬π¬≥‚Åπ
