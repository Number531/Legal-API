### Overview of Nebraska Market Conduct Examination

The Nebraska DOI, as LLIC's domiciliary regulator, conducted a comprehensive market conduct examination covering the three-year period from January 1, 2021, through December 31, 2023. The examination scope included seven functional areas: (1) sales practices; (2) underwriting procedures; (3) claims handling; (4) advertising and marketing materials; (5) producer licensing and appointment; (6) complaint handling; and (7) policyholder services. This comprehensive scope aligns with NAIC Market Regulation Handbook standards for life insurance market conduct examinations and represents the most extensive regulatory review conducted of LLIC's operations in the past five years (Nebraska's minimum domestic insurer examination frequency).

The examination was conducted pursuant to Nebraska's Insurers Examination Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-5901 to 44-5910), which requires examiners to observe the guidelines and procedures set forth in the NAIC Market Regulation Handbook. Nebraska participates in the NAIC's coordinated multistate market conduct examination program, and 12 states participated in the 2022 coordinated examination of LLIC with Nebraska serving as the lead state (Iowa, Kansas, Missouri, Colorado, Wyoming, Montana, South Dakota, North Dakota, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, and Nebraska).

**Exit Conference and Final Report Timeline.** The Nebraska DOI conducted an exit conference with LLIC management in November 2024 to present preliminary findings. LLIC submitted a written response to the preliminary findings in December 2024, and the Nebraska DOI is expected to issue the final examination report in Q1 2025 (estimated December 2024 – February 2025). This timeline is consistent with typical examination completion periods of 60-90 days after the exit conference and provides 4-6 months for resolution before the anticipated Q3 2025 acquisition closing.

### Finding 1: Sales Illustrations Violations (5 Violations)

**Nature of Violations.** The Nebraska DOI identified five violations involving life insurance sales illustrations in which LLIC agents showed policy illustrations with non-guaranteed elements (dividends on participating whole life products, credited interest rates on universal life and indexed universal life products) without adequately disclosing the distinction between guaranteed and non-guaranteed policy elements. Specifically:

- **Omission of Guaranteed Column (3 violations):** Agents provided illustrations showing only the current non-guaranteed column without the required side-by-side guaranteed column, in violation of Nebraska Regulation § 210-72 implementing NAIC Model Regulation 582.
- **Inadequate Disclosure (2 violations):** Agents provided illustrations that included the required disclosure language in footnote form but failed to display it prominently, rendering the disclosure ineffective in conveying that "values shown are not guaranteed."

**Applicable Regulatory Framework.** Nebraska Regulation § 210-72 (Life Insurance Illustrations) implements the NAIC Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation (Model Regulation 582). The regulation requires that basic illustrations (ledgers or proposals used in the sale of life insurance) show **both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements** at the same durations, clearly indicate which elements are guaranteed versus nonguaranteed, and include prominent disclosure language stating that projected values are not guaranteed and actual results may vary. The regulation's purpose is to ensure that illustrations do not mislead purchasers of life insurance and to make illustrations more understandable by requiring side-by-side comparison of contractually guaranteed benefits versus current but non-guaranteed projections.

**Consumer Harm Assessment.** The Nebraska DOI preliminarily concluded that no substantive consumer harm occurred from these violations. All policyholders received their policy contracts with full and compliant disclosure of guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements upon delivery. No consumer complaints were filed regarding the sales illustrations. The violations were characterized as technical compliance deficiencies (failure to follow required illustration format) rather than substantive misrepresentations likely to deceive purchasers.

**Estimated Fines and Corrective Actions.** Based on comparable state market conduct examination precedents, estimated fines range from **$25,000 to $50,000** ($5,000-$10,000 per violation). Mitigating factors supporting the lower range include: (a) no consumer harm; (b) first-time violations for the specific agents involved; and (c) prompt corrective action implemented by LLIC before the final examination report. LLIC's corrective actions totaled **$750,000**: enhanced agent training ($500,000 for development and mandatory delivery of an 8-hour CE course to all 650 captive agents and 8,500 independent producers) and compliance illustration software ($250,000 for a pre-submission review tool that automatically generates guaranteed/non-guaranteed columns and prevents printing of non-compliant illustrations).

### Finding 2: Replacement Forms Violations (12 Violations)

**Nature of Violations.** The Nebraska DOI identified 12 violations involving life insurance policy replacements in which LLIC agents failed to provide required replacement notices to policyholders who surrendered existing policies (from another carrier or an old LLIC policy) and purchased new LLIC policies. Specifically:

- **Form Not Provided (8 violations):** Agents failed to provide the statutorily required "Notice Regarding Replacement" form at all.
- **Untimely Delivery (4 violations):** Agents provided the notice after application submission, rather than before or at the time of application as required.
- **Lack of Comparison Documentation (12 violations):** Policy files contained no documented comparison of existing versus proposed policy benefits, costs, surrender charges, or loss of accumulated values.

**Applicable Regulatory Framework.** Nebraska Title 210, Chapter 19 (Life Insurance and Annuity Replacement Regulation), promulgated pursuant to Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-1533 and 44-1525, requires agents to provide a "Notice Regarding Replacement" to applicants **before or at the time of application**. The notice must: (1) be signed by both applicant and producer; (2) list all policies proposed to be replaced; (3) provide comparison information showing premiums, benefits, surrender charges, and loss of guarantees; and (4) inform the applicant of the 30-day free-look right to return the replacement policy and receive an unconditional full refund. The regulation is designed to protect purchasers by ensuring informed decision-making in replacement transactions.

**Consumer Harm Assessment.** The Nebraska DOI preliminarily concluded that limited consumer harm occurred. While replacement may have been suitable in some cases based on documented needs analysis, consumers were not provided the required comparison information showing surrender charges on existing policies ($5,000-$15,000 in some cases), loss of benefits such as guaranteed death benefit or premium rates, new contestability period (2 years), and new suicide exclusion period (2 years). The absence of required notice deprived consumers of the statutory 30-day free-look period to unconditionally rescind potentially unsuitable replacement transactions.

**Estimated Fines and Corrective Actions.** Because replacement violations involve consumer harm (deprivation of informed decision-making and potential financial loss), fines are typically higher than technical compliance violations. Estimated fines range from **$60,000 to $120,000** ($5,000-$10,000 per violation). Aggravating factors include: (a) consumer harm present (potential unsuitable replacements with financial loss); (b) deprivation of statutory protections (30-day free-look period); and (c) pattern concern (12 violations over 3 years suggests systemic supervision deficiency). LLIC's corrective actions totaled **$100,000** (implementation) + **$150,000 annually** (ongoing): revised replacement procedures ($100,000) requiring pre-application form completion, home office suitability review, and rejection authority; and quarterly compliance audits ($150,000 annually) reviewing random sample of 200 replacement applications per year.

### Finding 3: Claims Processing Violations (3 Violations)

**Nature of Violations.** The Nebraska DOI identified 3 violations involving death claims paid more than 60 days after receipt of the death certificate, in violation of Nebraska Statute § 44-502(12) requiring payment within 2 months of receipt of due proof of death. Specific cases:

- **Case 1 (75 days):** Beneficiary dispute – LLIC delayed payment while investigating conflicting designations; should have paid undisputed portion within 60 days and interpleaded disputed portion.
- **Case 2 (80 days):** Medical records verification – Medical records received day 40, but review delayed until day 80 due to claims examiner workload backlog.
- **Case 3 (90 days):** Administrative backlog – New claims examiner training and staffing shortages; no reasonable cause for delay.

**Applicable Regulatory Framework.** Nebraska Statute § 44-502(12) requires death benefit payment "not later than two months" after receipt of due proof of death. Nebraska Regulation § 210-61-008 (Unfair Life, Sickness and Accident Claims Settlement Practices) requires insurers to acknowledge claims within 15 days and provide written explanation if unresolved after 15 days, with updates every 30 days if investigation exceeds 30 days. Violations constitute unfair claims settlement practices subject to regulatory enforcement under Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-1536 to 44-1544.

**Consumer Harm Assessment.** The Nebraska DOI found that beneficiaries suffered financial hardship from delayed receipt of death benefits. Beneficiaries (surviving spouses and children) needed proceeds for living expenses, funeral costs, and debt payments, and filed complaints with the Nebraska DOI regarding the delays. This represents direct consumer harm requiring restitution.

**Estimated Fines, Interest Penalties, and Corrective Actions.** Estimated fines range from **$15,000 to $30,000** ($5,000-$10,000 per violation). In addition to regulatory fines, LLIC paid **$8,500** in retroactive interest to beneficiaries at 9% per annum from day 61 until payment date (covering delays of 15-30 days on claims totaling $850,000 face amount). Total claims violations exposure: **$23,500-$38,500** (fines + interest). LLIC's corrective actions totaled **$250,000 annually**: implemented 45-day internal deadline (vs. 60-day statutory) to create buffer, escalation procedures for claims pending >45 days, and hired 2 additional claims examiners to reduce backlog.

### FINRA Examination Follow-Up Risk

**Dual Regulatory Jurisdiction for Variable Products.** If Nebraska's sales illustrations violations include variable universal life (VUL) or variable annuity (VA) illustrations, FINRA may initiate a parallel examination of Liberty Life Securities LLC (LLIC's broker-dealer subsidiary) under FINRA Rule 2210 (Communications with the Public) and Rule 2111 (Suitability). Variable product illustrations are subject to both state insurance regulation (Nebraska Regulation § 210-72) and FINRA's securities regulation. If illustrations violated Nebraska's requirement to show guaranteed and non-guaranteed columns, the same illustrations likely violated FINRA's fair and balanced communications standard.

**FINRA Cause Examination Probability:** 30-40% probability that FINRA initiates a targeted "cause examination" of Liberty Life Securities LLC if Nebraska findings specifically involve VUL/VA illustrations or VA replacements. This probability is conditional on the specific product types involved in the Nebraska violations (requires data room verification).

**Estimated FINRA Exposure:** $50,000-$150,000 (AWC settlement fine for systemic supervision deficiencies) + $100,000-$200,000 annually (enhanced supervision procedures, including principal pre-approval of all VUL/VA applications and quarterly compliance reviews). Total FINRA exposure: **$150,000-$350,000** (conditional on VUL/VA involvement).

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Nebraska Market Conduct Examination Framework

#### 1. Statutory Authority and Procedural Standards

Nebraska's market conduct examination authority is codified in Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-5901 to 44-5910, known as the Insurers Examination Act.¹ The statute grants the Nebraska Department of Insurance (DOI) broad authority to examine the affairs of any insurance company licensed in the state to ensure compliance with Nebraska insurance laws and regulations.²

**Examination Standards.** Nebraska examiners must observe the guidelines and procedures set forth in the Market Regulation Handbook (formerly the Market Conduct Examiners Handbook) adopted by the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC).³ The NAIC Market Regulation Handbook provides standardized examination procedures covering sales practices, illustrations, replacement forms, and claims handling, all based on NAIC model laws and regulations adapted to individual state requirements.⁴

**Record Retention Requirements.** Every company subject to examination must retain market conduct records for five years following the completion of a transaction relating to the insurance business.⁵ "Market conduct records" include all books, records, accounts, papers, documents, and computer recordings relating to transactions with insureds, claimants, insurance producers, and other business affairs.⁶

**Examination Frequency.** The minimum examination frequency for domestic insurers in Nebraska is five years,⁷ though the Director may conduct more frequent examinations when deemed necessary or expedient.⁸

**Multistate Coordination.** Nebraska participates in the NAIC's coordinated multistate market conduct examination program.⁹ As of 2024, 51 jurisdictions participate in the Market Conduct Annual Statement (MCAS), a mandatory filing that collects market-related data for centralized analysis by the NAIC and participating state regulators.¹⁰ The data is shared among all participating states to enable both national and state-level analysis, facilitating coordinated regulatory actions when patterns of concern are identified.¹¹

### B. Finding 1: Sales Illustrations Violations (5 Violations)

#### 2. Applicable Regulatory Framework

**Nebraska Regulation § 210-72 (Life Insurance Illustrations).** Title 210, Chapter 72 of the Nebraska Administrative Code implements the NAIC Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation (Model Regulation 582).²⁴ The regulation's stated purpose is to ensure that illustrations do not mislead purchasers of life insurance and to make illustrations more understandable.²⁵

**Basic Illustration Requirements.** A "basic illustration" is defined as a ledger or proposal used in the sale of a life insurance policy that shows both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements.²⁶ Nebraska Regulation § 210-72-004 requires that basic illustrations:

- Display both guaranteed and non-guaranteed elements at the same durations;²⁷
- Clearly indicate which elements are guaranteed and which are nonguaranteed;²⁸
- Include required disclosure language stating that "values shown are not guaranteed" and that actual results may vary;²⁹
- Show a zero for any guaranteed element if no guaranteed benefit or value is available at a duration for which a non-guaranteed benefit or value is shown.³⁰

**Disciplined Current Scale Standard.** Non-guaranteed elements shown in illustrations must reflect a "disciplined current scale" that is reasonably based on actual recent historical experience, as certified annually by an illustration actuary designated by the insurer.³¹ The disciplined current scale must reflect only actions that have already been taken or events that have already occurred, and may not include projected trends of improvements or assumed improvements beyond the illustration date.³²

**Delivery and Record Retention.** A copy of the basic illustration signed by the applicant and agent must be delivered to the applicant at the time of application or policy delivery, and must be retained by the insurer until three years after the policy is no longer in force.³³

#### 2. Applicable Regulatory Framework

**Nebraska Title 210, Chapter 19 (Life Insurance and Annuity Replacement Regulation).** Nebraska's replacement regulation is promulgated pursuant to Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-1533 and 44-1525, and is designed to protect the interests of life insurance and annuity purchasers by establishing minimum standards of conduct to be observed in replacement transactions.⁵⁵

**Notice Regarding Replacement Requirements.** The regulation requires that the agent provide a "Notice Regarding Replacement" to the applicant that:⁵⁶

- Is signed by both the applicant and the producer attesting that the notice has been read aloud by the producer or that the applicant did not wish the notice to be read aloud;⁵⁷

**Insurer Obligations.** The replacing insurer must:⁶²

- Maintain evidence that a replacement notice was provided;
- Verify that a comparison of existing and proposed coverage was conducted;
- Retain copies of replacement notices and comparison documents for at least five years after the policy is no longer in force.⁶³

### C. FINRA Examination Follow-Up Risk

#### 1. Dual Regulatory Jurisdiction for Variable Products

**Variable Products Implicated.** If Nebraska's sales illustrations violations include variable universal life (VUL) or variable annuity (VA) illustrations showing non-guaranteed credited interest rates without adequate disclosure, FINRA may view the state insurance examination findings as triggering a need for parallel FINRA examination of Liberty Life Securities LLC (LLIC's broker-dealer subsidiary).¹¹⁶

**FINRA Rule 2210 (Communications with the Public).** Variable product sales materials, including illustrations, are subject to FINRA's communications standards.¹¹⁷ FINRA Rule 2210 requires that communications with the public be:

- Based on principles of fair dealing and good faith;
- Fair and balanced, providing a sound basis for evaluating the facts;
- Not omitting material facts or qualifying information that would cause the communication to be misleading.¹¹⁸

If VUL/VA illustrations showed only non-guaranteed columns without guaranteed columns, this could constitute a FINRA Rule 2210 violation in addition to the Nebraska insurance regulation violation.¹¹⁹

**FINRA Rule 2111 (Suitability).** If replacement form violations involved variable annuity replacements (1035 exchanges from existing fixed annuities to new variable annuities), FINRA may examine whether the replacements satisfied suitability obligations, particularly if customers incurred new surrender charges or increased fees without adequate disclosure of the tradeoffs.¹²⁰
