### Key Findings

**1. Captive Structure Creates Excessive Reliance on Undercapitalized Parental Guarantee**

Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC was established in 2010 (pre-AG48) as a Vermont special purpose financial captive to assume $850 million in redundant AXXX/XXX reserves from LLIC related to pre-2017 term life and universal life with secondary guarantees. The captive's financial structure reveals critical weaknesses:

- **Total reserves ceded:** $850 million
- **Captive assets held:** $120 million (14% of reserves)
  - Cash: $15M
  - Bonds: $85M
  - Mortgages: $20M
- **Parental guarantee:** $730 million (86% of reserves) from Liberty Life Holdings LLC
- **Parent net worth:** $280 million
- **Leverage ratio:** 2.6× ($730M guarantee ÷ $280M parent net worth)

This 2.6× leverage ratio means Liberty Life Holdings would need to fund **261% of its entire net worth** to honor the guarantee if Liberty Re VT's $120M in assets were exhausted. This is a mathematical impossibility absent external capital infusion, creating fundamental counterparty credit risk.

The structure violates industry best practices for parental guarantees under NAIC reserve financing frameworks, which typically require: (1) parent RBC ratio >300%, (2) parent net worth exceeding guarantee amount, and (3) parent financial strength rating of A or better. Liberty Life Holdings fails the net worth test by a factor of 2.6×.

**2. AG48 Grandfather Clause Provides Strong But Not Absolute Protection**

Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48), adopted by NAIC in December 2014 (effective January 1, 2015), established stricter requirements for XXX/AXXX captive reinsurance structures, dividing required reserves into "Primary Security" (high-quality assets like cash and investment-grade bonds) and "Other Security" (lower-quality assets including parental guarantees subject to strict conditions).

Liberty Re VT benefits from AG48's bright-line grandfather clause: captive structures established before December 31, 2014 are exempt from AG48 requirements. Liberty Re VT was established in 2010, four years before the cutoff, and should be grandfathered. The AG48 adoption process confirmed that "the substantive provisions of AG48 do not and will not apply to any life insurance policies that were included in a reserve financing as of Dec. 31, 2014." During November 2014 negotiations, retroactivity was "one of the most prevalent concerns for the life sector," and after "heated debate," the issue was "finally laid to rest" through the non-retroactive Grandfathering Test.

However, state insurance departments retain broad **safety and soundness examination authority** that may override grandfather protection. Nebraska, as LLIC's domiciliary state, has authority under Neb. Rev. Stat. §44-416.06 to evaluate whether LLIC should receive statutory reserve credit for captive reinsurance, even if the structure is technically grandfathered. Regulatory theory: if a captive structure poses material solvency risk to the ceding insurer, the domiciliary regulator may disallow reserve credit regardless of grandfather status.

### B. AXXX/XXX Reserve Requirements

The redundant reserves addressed by the captive structure arise from two NAIC regulatory frameworks:

**Regulation XXX (Term Life):**
Regulation XXX concerns reserves required for term life policies with long-term premium guarantees. These reserves were categorized as redundant based on their magnitude compared to reserves based on current assumptions.¹

**Actuarial Guideline 38 (Universal Life with Secondary Guarantees):**
AG 38 deals with reserves required for all universal life products that contain secondary guarantees (also called "no lapse guarantees"), which guarantee that the policy will remain in force for life or a specified period as long as required premiums are paid, even if cash value is insufficient.²

These regulatory frameworks resulted in what the industry termed "redundant reserves" - reserves that exceed economic reserves needed based on realistic assumptions. Liberty Re VT was established to finance these redundant reserves through a captive reinsurance structure.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Vermont Captive Insurance Regulatory Framework

#### 1. Vermont Captive Insurance Companies Act (Title 8, Chapter 141)

Vermont law provides the statutory framework for Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC:

**Capital Requirements:**
Vermont statute establishes minimum capital requirements based on captive type. For affiliated reinsurance companies (ARCs) and special purpose financial captive insurance companies (SPFCs), Vermont requires minimum paid-in capital and surplus of **$5,000,000**.³ This is the highest capital requirement among Vermont captive types, reflecting the more complex reinsurance activities these entities undertake.

Liberty Re VT reportedly maintains $5 million in paid-in capital, meeting the minimum statutory requirement.⁴

**Collateral Alternatives:**
Vermont law permits captives to satisfy capital and surplus requirements through multiple forms:
- Cash
- Trust approved by the Commissioner
- Irrevocable letter of credit issued by a Commissioner-approved bank
- Marketable securities⁵

For reinsurance and solvency purposes, the Vermont Commissioner may require:
- Funds-held agreements
- Letters of credit
- Trust arrangements
- Other acceptable collateral based on unearned premium, loss and LAE reserves, and IBNR⁶

**Vermont's Flexibility:**
Unlike some jurisdictions, Vermont explicitly permits **blended collateral structures** combining assets held in the captive, parental guarantees, and letters of credit. This flexibility has made Vermont the dominant captive domicile in the United States.⁷

#### 2. Federal Reserve 2013 Shadow Insurance Report

The Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis published research in 2013 identifying significant systemic risks in captive reinsurance structures:

**Growth of Shadow Insurance:**
Liabilities moved to "shadow reinsurers" (captive insurance companies) grew from $11 billion in 2002 to $364 billion in 2012. Companies using shadow insurance moved 25 cents of every dollar insured to shadow reinsurers in 2012, up from 2 cents in 2002.⁸

**Systemic Risk Concerns Identified:**

1. **Banking Sector Contagion:** Banks issue letters of credit to collateralize reinsurance between an insurance company and a captive. A systemic shock to the insurance sector could trigger sudden demand for credit that constrains the banking sector.⁹

2. **Market Disruption:** Even the perception that insurance companies are at risk could suddenly reduce demand for insurance products, forcing households to bear additional risk.¹⁰

3. **Corporate Bond Market Impact:** Insurance companies are the largest institutional holders of corporate bonds. If forced to shrink balance sheets, demand for certain bond types would decline, potentially impacting real investment and economic activity.¹¹

**Regulatory Response:**
According to investigations by the New York State Department of Financial Services and the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis, failing to regulate shadow insurance could, in a worst case scenario, place the entire economy at risk from cascading failures.¹² This research directly contributed to the NAIC's adoption of Actuarial Guideline 48 in 2014.

### B. NAIC Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48) Framework

#### 1. AG48 Adoption and Scope

AG48 was adopted by the NAIC Executive Committee and Plenary on December 16, 2014, with an effective date of January 1, 2015. The guideline establishes consistent methods for calculating economic reserves and required security levels for XXX and AXXX reserve financing transactions.¹³

**Covered Business:**
AG48 applies to financing arrangements involving:
- Term life insurance business subject to Regulation XXX
- Universal life insurance business subject to Actuarial Guideline 38 (AXXX)¹⁴

#### 2. Primary Security and Other Security Structure

AG48 divides required reserves into two layers:

**Primary Security Layer:**
Higher-quality assets that must be held to back a portion of statutory reserves. Primary Security consists primarily of:
- Cash
- High-quality bonds (investment-grade)
- Assets held in trust
- Other high-quality, liquid assets¹⁵

**Other Security Layer:**
The remainder of statutory reserves (above the Primary Security threshold) may be backed by a wider variety of assets, including:
- Lower-quality bonds
- Mortgages
- Parental guarantees (subject to strict conditions)
- Other forms of collateral¹⁶

**Ceding Actuary Requirement:**
The ceding company's appointed actuary is required to analyze transactions to determine:
1. Whether the required amount of high-quality assets (Primary Security) are being held to back the reserve

#### 4. AG48 Grandfather Clause and Retroactivity Issue

**Grandfather Clause Protection:**
AG48 includes a bright-line grandfather clause: the substantive provisions of AG48 **do not and will not apply** to any life insurance policies that were included in a reserve financing arrangement as of December 31, 2014 (the "Grandfathering Test").¹⁸

This grandfather clause was "a significant victory for the life industry," as the proposed alternative would have required immediate compliance with AG48 for any transaction amended in any way subsequent to January 1, 2015.¹⁹

**Liberty Re VT Grandfather Status:**
Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC was established in 2010, **four years before** AG48's December 31, 2014 grandfather cutoff date. Under the plain language of AG48's Grandfathering Test, the Liberty Re VT captive structure should be **exempt from AG48 requirements**.²⁰

**Retroactivity Concerns:**
During AG48 negotiations in November 2014, retroactivity was "one of the most prevalent concerns for the life sector." After "heated debate," the issue was "finally laid to rest" through adoption of the non-retroactive Grandfathering Test.²¹

However, state insurance departments retain examination authority to review captive structures for safety and soundness **regardless of grandfather status**. The Nebraska Department of Insurance 2024 market conduct examination may scrutinize whether Liberty Re VT's structure poses unacceptable solvency risk to LLIC, even if the structure is technically grandfathered under AG48.

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

**1. Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC captive structure presents material regulatory risk to the acquisition.**

The captive's reliance on a $730 million parental guarantee from Liberty Life Holdings LLC (which has only $280 million in net worth, representing 2.6× leverage) creates an unacceptable solvency profile that has drawn Nebraska Department of Insurance scrutiny during the 2024 market conduct examination.

**2. AG48 grandfather clause provides strong but not absolute protection.**

While Liberty Re VT was established in 2010 (before AG48's January 1, 2015 effective date) and should be grandfathered under the AG48 bright-line test, Nebraska DOI retains broad safety and soundness examination authority that may permit disallowing reserve credit regardless of grandfather status.

**3. Three regulatory outcome scenarios with probability-weighted analysis:**

- **Scenario 1 (50-60% probability):** Nebraska DOI accepts current structure with enhanced monitoring
- **Scenario 2 (30-40% probability):** Nebraska DOI requires additional collateral (LOC $300M-$500M, annual cost $2.55M-$5.08M)
- **Scenario 3 (10-15% probability):** Nebraska DOI disallows reserve credit → **$730M surplus reduction → RBC ratio 114% → DEAL-BLOCKING**

**4. Scenario 3 (full recapture) would render acquisition infeasible without massive capital infusion.**

RBC ratio of 114% falls within Regulatory Action Level (100-150%), triggering mandatory corrective action plan, intensive regulatory oversight, and high probability of conservatorship or rehabilitation within 12-24 months. Combined with existing $150M capital injection need (to address current 188% RBC ratio), total capital requirement would be **$880 million** - a prohibitive amount that likely exceeds American Financial Holdings' acquisition financing capacity.

**5. Mitigation through $300M letter of credit is cost-effective and strongly recommended.**

A $300M LOC backstop costs $2.55M-$3.45M annually but provides:
- Reduces Scenario 3 probability from 10-15% to 5-8% (50% reduction)
- Reduces Scenario 2 probability from 30-40% to 15-20% (regulatory concerns addressed)
- Improves captive security from 14% assets to 49% (assets + LOC)
- Reduces parent leverage from 2.6× to 1.5×
- **Expected value benefit: $58.1M risk reduction** vs. annual cost $2.55M-$3.45M = **benefit-to-cost ratio >14×**
