### Case Overview

**Thompson v. Liberty Life Insurance Company** [HYPOTHETICAL SCENARIO] is a pending class action filed in Nebraska District Court (August 2023) alleging indexed universal life (IUL) insurance misrepresentation, omissions, and Nebraska Consumer Protection Act violations. The case involves **850 IUL policyholders** (ages 35-65) who purchased policies during 2015-2020 based on sales illustrations projecting **8.5% annual index crediting rates**, but actual performance averaged only **4.2%** (2015-2023) due to cap rate reductions (12% → 10% in 2020), low interest rates, and increased hedging costs. Aggregate cash value shortfall totals **$85M-$125M** ($100K-$200K per policyholder), with punitive damages exposure of **2-3× compensatory** ($170M-$375M total potential exposure if case proceeds to trial).

**Procedural Status:** Motion to dismiss denied (all three counts survive pleading stage), discovery substantially complete (50,000+ pages produced, executive/agent depositions conducted, expert reports exchanged), class certification likely, mediation currently scheduled Q2 2025. **Strategic recommendation:** Accelerate mediation to Q1 2025 for pre-closing resolution.

### Key Legal Findings

#### Count 1: Misrepresentation

**Plaintiffs' Theory:** LLIC misrepresented likely IUL performance by illustrating **8.5% crediting rates** based on 90-year historical S&P 500 returns (1926-2015 average: 10%, less 1.5% adjustment for caps/floors = 8.5%) without adjusting for post-2008 low interest rate environment. Federal Reserve zero-rate policy (2008-2021) and increased hedging costs (option purchases) rendered 8.5% rate unsustainable, yet LLIC continued illustrating this rate through 2020.

**Evidence Supporting Plaintiffs:**
- Actual crediting rates 2015-2023 averaged **4.2%** (50% below illustrated 8.5%)
- LLIC reduced cap rates from 12% to 10% in 2020 (confirms original rates unsustainable)
- Nebraska DOI 2024 market conduct exam cited **5 illustration violations** (non-guaranteed elements disclosure deficiencies)
- 850 policyholders suffered aggregate **$85M-$125M cash value shortfall** (illustrated vs. actual)
- Discovery may reveal internal communications acknowledging 8.5% rate was aggressive given market conditions

**LLIC's Defenses:**
- **NAIC Regulation 582 compliance:** Illustrations used two-column format (guaranteed vs. non-guaranteed), included required disclosure language ("values not guaranteed, actual results may vary"), and were certified by Illustration Actuary per ASOP No. 24
- **Historical reasonableness:** 8.5% rate based on 90-year S&P 500 average (10%) with reasonable adjustments for IUL mechanics (caps/floors/participation)
- **Industry standards:** Other major carriers illustrated similar rates 2015-2020 (Pacific Life 7.5-8.5%, Nationwide 8.0-9.0%, Prudential 7.75-8.25%)
- **Unforeseeable events:** 2020 pandemic crash (-34% S&P decline March 2020) and prolonged zero-rate environment were not predictable when policies issued 2015-2020

**Strength Assessment:** **Moderate liability risk.** Plaintiffs have sympathetic facts (consumers lost retirement savings) and expert testimony (8.5% excessive for low-rate environment), but LLIC has credible regulatory compliance defense. Jury trial outcome uncertain (40-60% plaintiff win probability).

#### Count 2: Omissions (Failure to Disclose)

**Plaintiffs' Theory:** LLIC failed to adequately disclose that **cap rates, floor rates, and participation rates were subject to change** and likely to decline in low interest rate environment. Policy illustrations held caps constant at 12% over entire 30-year projection period, creating misleading impression that caps were stable. Policy contracts disclosed LLIC's right to change caps (page 18), but illustrations did not reflect this variability.

**Material Omission Standard:** Under Nebraska consumer protection law, material omission occurs when information withheld is significant to reasonable consumer's decision and consumer would have acted differently if fully informed. Cap rate changes directly impact cash value accumulation (LLIC's 2020 reduction from 12% to 10% = ~20% reduction in credited returns when S&P gains exceed cap).

**Evidence Supporting Plaintiffs:**
- Illustrations showed caps held constant at 12% for 30 years
- Actual cap reduction to 10% in 2020 (demonstrates caps not stable as illustrated)
- Hedging cost increases in low-rate environment were predictable (LLIC actuaries would have known option premiums increase when interest rates fall)
- Reasonable consumer (age 35-65, non-insurance professional) would not understand technical contract language on page 18 regarding cap change rights

**LLIC's Defenses:**
- **Contract disclosure:** Policy page 18 clearly states LLIC "reserves right to change Cap Rate, Floor Rate, and Participation Rate" subject to guaranteed minimums (0% floor, 2% cap, 25% participation)
- **Regulatory compliance:** NAIC Regulation 582 does not require illustrations to show variable caps (illustrated values are "current assumptions," not projections of parameter changes)
- **Annual notices:** Policyholders received annual statements showing actual credited rates and current caps (notice of cap reduction in 2020)
- **No duty to predict future:** LLIC had no duty to predict future cap reductions at time of policy issuance (2015-2020), caps were 12% when policies sold

**Strength Assessment:** **Moderate-to-high liability risk.** Courts increasingly skeptical of "fine print" contract disclosures when illustrations create contrary impression. Omissions claim stronger than misrepresentation claim because it avoids NAIC 582 compliance defense (focuses on disconnect between illustration assumptions and economic reality).

#### Count 3: Nebraska Consumer Protection Act

**Statutory Basis:** Nebraska Uniform Deceptive Trade Practices Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 87-301 to 87-306) prohibits:
- Representing that goods/services have characteristics they do not have (§ 87-302(a)(5))
- Representing goods/services are of particular standard/quality/grade if they are of another (§ 87-302(a)(7))
- Advertising goods/services with intent not to sell as advertised (§ 87-302(a)(9))

**Application:** IUL illustrations represented policies would achieve 8.5% crediting (robust cash value accumulation suitable for retirement funding) but actually achieved 4.2% crediting (significantly lower accumulation, many policies at risk of lapse). Reasonable consumers were deceived into purchasing IUL policies unsuitable for stated purpose.

**Remedies:** Nebraska UDTPA provides private right of action for actual damages, injunctive relief, attorney fees, and costs. Punitive damages available under separate Nebraska statute (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 25-21,188) for "willful and reckless or malicious conduct" (typically 2-3× compensatory).

**Strength Assessment:** **High likelihood of surviving summary judgment.** Consumer protection act claims have low pleading threshold, and LLIC's NAIC 582 compliance does not immunize from state consumer protection liability (regulatory compliance relevant but not dispositive). However, proving "willful" conduct required for punitive damages is challenging given LLIC's good faith regulatory adherence.

#### 3. Attorney Fees and Costs

**Statutory Basis:**
Nebraska Consumer Protection Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 87-303(3)): "A person who suffers damages as a result of a violation... may... recover... the costs of the action, including reasonable attorney's fees."¹³³

**Class Action Attorney Fee Standards:**

**Percentage-of-Fund Method:**¹³⁴
Federal courts typically award class counsel 25-33% of total settlement fund:¹³⁵
- **Conservative:** 25% × $40M settlement = **$10M**
- **Mid-Range:** 28-30% × $40M settlement = **$11.2M-$12M**

**Factors Courts Consider (Goldberger Factors):**¹³⁶
1. Time and labor expended by counsel
2. Magnitude and complexities of litigation
3. Risk of non-payment (contingency risk)
4. Quality of representation
5. Benefit obtained for class
6. Awards in similar cases

**Lodestar Cross-Check:**¹³⁷
Courts also calculate "lodestar" (hours worked × reasonable hourly rates) and compare to percentage award:
- Class counsel hours: 5,000-8,000 hours (multiple attorneys, paralegals, experts)¹³⁸
- Blended hourly rate: $400-$600/hour¹³⁹
- Lodestar: $2M-$4.8M
- Multiplier: Percentage award ÷ lodestar = 2.1-6.0× (typical for contingency class actions)¹⁴⁰
