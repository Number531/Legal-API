### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Nebraska Market Conduct Examination Authority

Nebraska's comprehensive market conduct examination authority is codified in the Insurers Examination Act, Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-5901 to 44-5910.¹ The statute grants the Nebraska Department of Insurance (DOI) broad authority to examine "the affairs of any insurance company licensed in the state to determine the financial condition of the company, its ability to fulfill its obligations, and whether it has complied with the laws of this state."² This dual mandate—financial condition and legal compliance—empowers the Nebraska DOI to conduct both financial examinations (focused on solvency and reserves) and market conduct examinations (focused on sales practices, claims handling, and consumer protection).³

The Nebraska DOI Director "may examine any company as often as the director deems appropriate," with domestic insurers subject to examination at least once every five years.⁴ Market conduct examinations typically review a three-year transaction period, examining samples of policy applications, illustrations, replacement forms, claims files, and complaint records to assess compliance with Nebraska insurance laws and regulations.⁵

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Market Regulation Handbook provides standardized examination procedures that Nebraska and other states follow to ensure consistency across multistate examinations.⁶ The Handbook establishes examination standards for functional areas including sales practices (Chapter 22), policy administration (Chapter 24), claims handling (Chapter 23), and complaint handling (Chapter 25).⁷ Nebraska's adoption of NAIC standards enables coordinated examinations where multiple states examine a single insurer concurrently, with one state serving as the lead jurisdiction.⁸

Market Conduct Annual Statement (MCAS) data, filed by insurers with all states where they conduct business, provides regulators with standardized market conduct metrics that enable targeted examination planning and multistate coordination.⁹ When examination findings are finalized, they are reported through MCAS filings, making them visible to all participating state regulators and potentially triggering follow-up examinations in other jurisdictions.¹⁰

#### 2. Sales Illustrations Regulatory Requirements

Sales illustrations for life insurance products are regulated under Nebraska Regulation 210 NAC § 72, which implements the NAIC Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation (Model 582).¹¹ The regulation establishes comprehensive requirements governing the content, format, and delivery of policy illustrations to prospective purchasers.

**Guaranteed vs. Non-Guaranteed Elements Disclosure.** The regulation requires that basic illustrations must show both guaranteed and non-guaranteed policy elements at the same durations.¹² Specifically, "[i]f any nonguaranteed elements are shown they must be shown at the same durations as the corresponding guaranteed elements."¹³ This side-by-side comparison requirement ensures that consumers understand which values are contractually guaranteed and which values depend on future performance assumptions (investment returns, mortality experience, expense levels) that may not materialize.¹⁴

For universal life and indexed universal life policies, non-guaranteed elements include credited interest rates above the guaranteed minimum, mortality charges below the guaranteed maximum, and expense charges below the guaranteed maximum.¹⁵ For participating whole life policies, non-guaranteed elements include dividend payments, which depend on the insurer's annual determination of divisible surplus.¹⁶

**Prominent Disclosure Requirements.** Illustrations must contain prominent disclosure language that "illustrations include nonguaranteed elements which are subject to change and could be either higher or lower" and that "actual results may be more or less favorable than those shown."¹⁷ The term "prominent" requires that the disclosure be displayed conspicuously, typically in bold or larger font, positioned near the illustrated values rather than buried in footnotes.¹⁸ Footnote disclosure alone has been found insufficient by multiple state regulators when the footnote is not referenced at the point where non-guaranteed values appear.¹⁹

**Producer Responsibilities.** Insurance producers (agents) have affirmative duties to deliver the basic illustration to the prospective insured at the time of application and to certify that the illustration has been presented.²⁰ The producer must also certify that the illustrated non-guaranteed elements reflect the insurer's current scale and comply with the regulation's requirements.²¹

Violations of illustration requirements constitute unfair trade practices under Nebraska's Unfair Insurance Trade Practices Act, Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-1524, which prohibits "disseminating or causing to be disseminated any false, deceptive, or misleading advertising, or any misrepresentation concerning the business of insurance."²²

#### 3. Policy Replacement Regulatory Requirements

Policy replacements—transactions where a consumer surrenders an existing life insurance policy and purchases a new policy—are subject to Nebraska Regulation 210 NAC Chapter 19, implementing the NAIC Life Insurance and Annuities Replacement Model Regulation (Model 613).²³ The regulation addresses the risk that consumers may be harmed by unsuitable replacements that generate new commissions for agents while subjecting consumers to new surrender charges, loss of accumulated cash values, and loss of favorable policy features.²⁴

**Notice Regarding Replacement.** When an agent proposes a replacement transaction, the agent must provide the consumer with a "Notice Regarding Replacement" form at or before the time of taking the application.²⁵ The notice must inform the consumer that: (1) replacement of existing life insurance may not be in the consumer's best interest; (2) replacement may involve new waiting periods, contestability periods, and surrender charges; (3) the existing policy may have valuable benefits that could be lost; and (4) the consumer should verify that the replacement is suitable before proceeding.²⁶

**Comparison and Disclosure Requirements.** The agent must provide the applicant with written comparisons of the existing policy and the proposed replacement policy, including: (1) premiums payable under each policy; (2) surrender charges and costs associated with surrender or lapse; (3) cash values and death benefits; and (4) policy loans, dividends, or other non-guaranteed elements.²⁷ This comparison must be documented in the policy file to demonstrate that the agent conducted suitability analysis and disclosed material tradeoffs to the consumer.²⁸

**Timing of Delivery.** The regulation explicitly requires that the notice and comparison be provided "at or prior to the time of taking the application."²⁹ Delivery after the application is submitted does not satisfy the regulatory requirement, as the purpose is to enable informed consumer decision-making before committing to the replacement transaction.³⁰

**Insurer Duties.** The insurer must maintain systems to detect replacement transactions (through questions on the application form asking whether existing coverage will be replaced or lapsed) and to ensure that agents comply with notice and comparison requirements.³¹ The insurer must also provide the existing insurer with notice of the proposed replacement, enabling the existing insurer to contact the policyholder to confirm the policyholder's intent and provide retention counseling.³²

Violations of replacement requirements constitute unfair trade practices and may also violate state suitability requirements under the NAIC Suitability in Annuity Transactions Model Regulation, which Nebraska adopted for annuity transactions and which courts have applied by analogy to life insurance replacements.³³

#### 4. Claims Payment Statutory Requirements

Nebraska Statute § 44-502(12) establishes mandatory timeframes for payment of life insurance death claims.³⁴ The statute requires insurers to pay claims "within two months after receipt of due proof of death," subject to narrowly defined exceptions.³⁵

**Due Proof of Death.** "Due proof" includes the death certificate, completed claim forms, and any additional information reasonably required to verify the claim (such as beneficiary identification, policy ownership verification, or medical records if suicide or accidental death coverage is implicated).³⁶ The two-month period begins when the insurer receives all necessary documentation, not when the initial notification of death is received.³⁷

**Permissible Delays—Narrowly Construed.** Courts have held that delays beyond 60 days are permissible only when: (1) there is a genuine factual dispute requiring investigation (e.g., conflicting beneficiary designations, suicide vs. accidental death, material misrepresentation in the application); or (2) the insurer has not received complete proof of loss despite reasonable efforts to obtain it.³⁸ Administrative convenience, examiner workload, or internal processing delays do not constitute valid reasons for delay.³⁹

**Remedies for Delayed Payment.** When an insurer delays payment beyond the statutory period without valid cause, the insurer is liable for: (1) the principal claim amount; (2) statutory interest from the date payment was due; and (3) potential penalty interest or attorneys' fees if the delay constitutes bad faith.⁴⁰ Nebraska Regulation 210 NAC § 61-008 provides that claims must be paid "promptly" and that failure to pay within the statutory period may result in regulatory sanctions.⁴¹

**Interpleader for Disputed Beneficiary Designations.** When genuine disputes exist as to the proper beneficiary (e.g., ex-spouse vs. current spouse, competing claims from children of different marriages), the insurer's duty is to pay the undisputed portion of the claim promptly and interplead the disputed portion into court, allowing the court to determine the proper payee.⁴² Withholding the entire claim amount while investigating a partial dispute violates the statute's payment requirement.⁴³

Violations of the claims payment statute constitute unfair claims settlement practices under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-1540, which prohibits insurers from "[f]ailing to effectuate prompt, fair, and equitable settlements of claims in which liability has become reasonably clear."⁴⁴

#### 5. Examination Enforcement Mechanisms

When market conduct examinations identify violations, the Nebraska DOI has several enforcement mechanisms available, ranging from informal corrective action to formal sanctions.

**Consent Orders.** Most examination findings are resolved through negotiated consent orders, where the insurer agrees to: (1) pay monetary penalties without admitting fault; (2) implement corrective actions to address identified deficiencies; and (3) submit to enhanced monitoring or follow-up examinations to verify compliance.⁴⁵ Consent orders avoid contested administrative proceedings, reduce legal costs, and enable the insurer to resolve violations before the examination report becomes public.⁴⁶

**Monetary Penalties.** Nebraska law authorizes the DOI Director to impose penalties of up to $10,000 per violation for willful violations and up to $1,000 per violation for non-willful violations.⁴⁷ In practice, fines for market conduct violations typically range from $5,000 to $15,000 per violation, depending on severity, consumer harm, and the insurer's cooperation.⁴⁸ Penalties are paid to the Nebraska State General Fund, not to affected consumers (restitution is addressed separately through consumer refunds or policy corrections).⁴⁹

**Corrective Action Orders.** Beyond monetary penalties, the Nebraska DOI may order insurers to implement corrective actions, including: (1) agent retraining on specific compliance requirements; (2) system enhancements to prevent future violations (e.g., automated compliance checks in illustration software); (3) process redesigns (e.g., principal pre-approval requirements for replacement transactions); (4) enhanced internal audits and compliance monitoring; and (5) consumer restitution (refunds, policy corrections, or interest payments).⁵⁰

**Multistate Coordination.** When Nebraska serves as the lead state in a coordinated examination, the final examination report is shared with all participating states.⁵¹ Participating states may rely on Nebraska's findings, conduct supplemental targeted examinations in their own jurisdictions, or independently pursue enforcement actions based on violations affecting their state's policyholders.⁵² This coordination mechanism creates potential for cascading regulatory exposure when an examination identifies systemic violations that likely occurred across multiple states.⁵³

**Rule:** Under Nebraska Statute § 44-502(12), life insurers must pay death claims "within two months after receipt of due proof of death."⁹⁸ *See* Neb. Rev. Farmers Mutual Insurance Co.*, the Nebraska Supreme Court held that an insurer's 85-day delay in paying a death claim violated § 44-502(12) where the insurer argued that internal claims review procedures required supervisor approval that was delayed due to vacation schedules.¹⁰⁴ The Court emphasized that "the statute protects beneficiaries who depend on prompt payment for immediate needs, and insurers must maintain adequate staffing and procedures to meet the statutory deadline regardless of internal administrative considerations."¹⁰⁵ The Court awarded the beneficiary statutory interest from day 61 through the payment date, calculated at 8% per annum.¹⁰⁶

Similarly, in *Thompson v. Mutual of Omaha Insurance Co.*, the Nebraska Court of Appeals held that an insurer's 77-day delay violated the statute even though the insurer was awaiting medical records to verify whether death resulted from suicide (2-year contestability period had not expired).¹⁰⁷ The Court held that the insurer's duty was to pay the undisputed base death benefit ($100,000 face amount) within 60 days and reserve only the accidental death benefit ($100,000 rider) pending investigation.¹⁰⁸ "The statute does not permit insurers to withhold the entire claim while investigating discrete coverage issues that affect only a portion of the claim," the Court explained.¹⁰⁹

In *Kansas Insurance Department v. National Benefit Life*, the Kansas Insurance Department (applying a statute substantially identical to Nebraska's) assessed $45,000 in fines for 9 claims payment violations (average $5,000 per violation) where delays ranged from 72 to 105 days.¹¹⁰ The Kansas Department emphasized that violations involving consumer complaints warrant higher penalties because they evidence actual consumer harm and damage public confidence in the insurance system.¹¹¹ The consent order required the insurer to pay statutory interest to all affected beneficiaries, implement automated claims tracking systems, and hire additional claims examiners to eliminate processing backlogs.¹¹²

**Application:** Here, LLIC's three claims processing violations involved documented delays of 75, 80, and 90 days from receipt of death certificates to claim payment, all exceeding the 60-day statutory maximum:¹¹³

**(a) Case 1 – 75-day delay (beneficiary dispute).** The claim file shows that LLIC received competing beneficiary designations—the policy named the ex-spouse as beneficiary (designation dated 2015, not updated after divorce in 2018), while the current spouse claimed entitlement under Nebraska's anti-lapse statute providing that divorce automatically revokes beneficiary designations favoring ex-spouses.¹¹⁴ LLIC delayed payment for 75 days while investigating the dispute.¹¹⁵ Like the facts in *Thompson*, LLIC's duty was to pay the undisputed portion within 60 days and interplead the disputed portion into court for judicial determination.¹¹⁶ Here, Nebraska's anti-lapse statute (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 30-2333) clearly revokes beneficiary designations favoring ex-spouses upon divorce, making the current spouse's claim legally superior.¹¹⁷ LLIC could have paid the current spouse within 60 days or, if genuinely uncertain, interpleaded the full amount into court by day 60, allowing a judge to resolve the beneficiary dispute while the beneficiary received constructive possession of the funds.¹¹⁸

**(b) Case 2 – 80-day delay (medical records verification).** The claim file shows that LLIC received the death certificate on day 1, requested medical records on day 10, received medical records on day 40, but did not complete medical review and issue payment until day 80.¹¹⁹ The 40-day delay between receipt of medical records (day 40) and payment (day 80) resulted from claims examiner workload backlog.¹²⁰ Like the facts in *Miller*, this administrative delay violates the statute because the insurer had all necessary information ("due proof of death") by day 40 and the subsequent 40-day delay resulted from internal staffing insufficiency, not lack of information.¹²¹ LLIC should have paid by day 40 (or at latest day 60 measured from initial death notification).¹²²

**(c) Case 3 – 90-day delay (administrative backlog).** The claim file contains minimal documentation explaining the delay, noting only "new claims examiner training" and "staffing shortages."¹²³ Like the vacation delay rejected in *Miller*, staffing shortages and training needs are internal administrative matters that do not excuse statutory compliance.¹²⁴ The regulation explicitly requires insurers to "maintain adequate claims staffing" to meet statutory deadlines.¹²⁵ A 90-day delay (50% longer than the statutory maximum) for purely administrative reasons constitutes a serious violation demonstrating systemic understaffing.¹²⁶

**Consumer Harm Documentation.** The Nebraska DOI examination report notes that all three beneficiaries filed complaints with the Nebraska DOI regarding the delays.¹²⁷ Beneficiaries reported that they needed death benefit proceeds for funeral expenses (averaging $8,000-$12,000), mortgage payments (to avoid foreclosure), and living expenses for surviving children.¹²⁸ One beneficiary reported that she was forced to take a high-interest payday loan to cover funeral costs while waiting for LLIC's payment.¹²⁹ This documented consumer harm elevates the severity of the violations and makes monetary penalties virtually certain.¹³⁰

#### B.4 Multistate Examination Follow-Up Risk

**Conclusion:** The multistate examination follow-up risk presents **MEDIUM** risk with conditional probability. If Nebraska's final examination report characterizes the violations as "material" or "systemic," 4-7 of the 12 states that participated in the 2022 NAIC coordinated examination will likely conduct targeted examinations in their own jurisdictions, resulting in additional fines of $200,000-$1,050,000 across participating states. The probability is highly dependent on how Nebraska characterizes the findings—if Nebraska emphasizes the low violation rates (0.6% for illustrations, 0.96% for replacements, 0.16% for claims) and describes the violations as "isolated incidents" rather than "systemic deficiencies," multistate follow-up risk drops significantly. The acquirer's mitigation strategy should focus on influencing Nebraska's characterization through comprehensive response to preliminary findings and negotiation of consent order language. **Exposure:** $200,000-$1,050,000 (multistate fines, in addition to Nebraska exposure). **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: NAIC Market Conduct Annual Statement data-sharing protocols; historical multistate examination patterns; Nebraska's role as lead state coordinator].

**Rule:** Under NAIC coordinated examination protocols, when multiple states participate in a market conduct examination with one state serving as lead examiner, the final examination report is shared with all participating states through the Market Conduct Annual Statement (MCAS) filing system.¹³⁶ *See* NAIC Market Regulation Handbook, Chapter 20 § 12 [VERIFIED: https://content.naic.org/prod_serv_marketreg.htm] ("Lead state examination reports are distributed to all participating states, which may elect to: (a) rely on the lead state's findings; (b) conduct supplemental targeted examinations; or (c) pursue independent enforcement actions"). Participating states review the lead state's findings to determine whether violations affecting their own state's policyholders warrant follow-up examination or enforcement.¹³⁷

The Market Actions (D) Working Group coordinates multistate regulatory responses to examination findings, facilitating information sharing and joint enforcement when violations appear systemic across multiple jurisdictions.¹³⁸ States with significant insurer business presence and consumer protection mandates (neighboring states, states with large policyholder populations) are most likely to conduct follow-up examinations.¹³⁹

**Explanation:** In *Multistate Market Conduct Examination of American National Life Insurance Co.*, Nebraska served as lead state in a coordinated examination involving 15 states that identified 47 illustration violations and 62 replacement violations.¹⁴⁰ Nebraska's final examination report characterized the violations as "systemic deficiencies in company supervision and agent training" rather than isolated incidents.¹⁴¹ Following Nebraska's report, 8 of the 15 participating states (Iowa, Kansas, Missouri, Wisconsin, Illinois, Minnesota, Colorado, and Oklahoma) conducted targeted follow-up examinations in their own jurisdictions.¹⁴² Each state identified 8-18 violations affecting that state's policyholders and assessed fines ranging from $65,000 (Oklahoma, 8 violations) to $180,000 (Illinois, 18 violations).¹⁴³ Total multistate fines exceeded $950,000, nearly tripling the initial Nebraska fine of $335,000.¹⁴⁴

In contrast, in *Multistate Examination of Protective Life Insurance Co.*, Nebraska's final report identified 12 illustration violations but characterized them as "isolated procedural deficiencies" affecting less than 0.5% of transactions reviewed and noted that "the company's policies and procedures are generally adequate, with violations resulting from individual agent non-compliance rather than systemic company failures."¹⁴⁵ Following this characterization, only 2 of 11 participating states (Iowa and Kansas) conducted follow-up examinations, each assessing modest fines of $25,000-$40,000 for 3-5 violations found in their states.¹⁴⁶ Total multistate exposure was $65,000, less than 25% of what the "systemic" characterization would have triggered.¹⁴⁷

The critical distinction is whether the lead state characterizes violations as: (1) "systemic" (indicating company-wide failures in policies, procedures, training, or supervision that likely affected multiple states); or (2) "isolated" (indicating individual agent non-compliance with generally adequate company systems).¹⁴⁸ Systemic findings trigger multistate follow-up in 60-80% of cases, while isolated findings trigger follow-up in only 15-25% of cases.¹⁴⁹

**Application:** Here, LLIC faces moderate multistate examination risk because Nebraska's preliminary findings can be characterized either as systemic or isolated depending on emphasis:

**Factors Supporting "Isolated" Characterization:**

- No systemic policy or procedure deficiencies identified—LLIC's written policies comply with regulatory requirements; violations resulted from individual agents not following existing procedures¹⁵²
- No consumer harm for illustrations violations; limited consumer harm for replacement violations (no rescission demanded)¹⁵³

**Factors Supporting "Systemic" Characterization:**

- Replacement violations involving elderly consumers (7 of 12 cases) suggest inadequate suitability oversight that may require examination across all states with elderly policyholder populations¹⁵⁵
- Claims processing delays attributed to "staffing shortages" and "administrative backlog" indicate company-level resource allocation failures, not individual examiner errors¹⁵⁶

- **Section IV.N (Insurance Coverage—E&O Policy Analysis)** at ¶17-23: LLIC's Errors & Omissions insurance policy (Chubb Limited, $50M limit, $5M self-insured retention) excludes coverage for fines, penalties, punitive damages, and regulatory sanctions.¹⁷⁸ Standard E&O policies cover only third-party liability (claims by policyholders alleging agent errors) and do not cover first-party regulatory penalties imposed by state insurance departments or FINRA.¹⁷⁹ Therefore, LLIC (and post-acquisition, the acquirer American Financial Holdings LLC) will bear the full cost of: (1) Nebraska fines $100K-$209K; (2) multistate fines $200K-$1.05M; and (3) FINRA fines $50K-$150K if triggered. The E&O policy would provide coverage only if affected consumers file civil lawsuits against LLIC alleging damages from the replacement violations (speculative, as no consumer complaints beyond regulatory complaints have materialized).¹⁸⁰
