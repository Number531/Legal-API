### A. Legal Framework

Investment portfolios held by life insurance companies are subject to a comprehensive regulatory framework designed to ensure that insurers maintain sufficient liquid, high-quality assets to satisfy policyholder obligations. This framework operates at both the federal level (for securities regulation) and the state level (for insurance solvency and asset quality standards).

#### 1. State Insurance Investment Regulation

State insurance departments regulate insurer investments through statutes and administrative regulations that prescribe permissible asset classes, concentration limits, and quality standards. These regulations reflect the fundamental principle that insurance company assets constitute a trust fund held for the benefit of policyholders.¹

**Nebraska Investment Statute:**

Nebraska law governs Liberty Life Insurance Company's investment activities through Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-501 to 44-518, which prescribe authorized investments for life insurers domiciled in Nebraska.² The statute enumerates permissible asset classes including bonds, stocks, real estate, mortgages, and policy loans, while imposing quantitative limits on riskier asset categories.³ Nebraska follows the model act approach adopted by most states, which establishes percentage-of-assets limitations for specific investment types (e.g., common stocks limited to 20% of admitted assets, single issuer concentration limits of 3-5% of assets).⁴

The Nebraska Department of Insurance reviews insurer investment portfolios quarterly through statutory financial statements (Schedule D for bonds and stocks, Schedule B for mortgages) and conducts periodic financial examinations to assess compliance with investment statutes.⁵ Violations may result in cease-and-desist orders requiring portfolio rebalancing, civil penalties, or in severe cases, regulatory action under the insurance holding company act.⁶

**NAIC Model Investment Law:**

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners ("NAIC") has developed model investment statutes and regulations that most states have adopted with minor variations.⁷ The NAIC's Investment of Insurers Model Act establishes prudent investor principles requiring insurers to diversify investments, maintain adequate liquidity, and match asset durations to liability cash flows.⁸ The model act incorporates the "basket clause" approach, permitting investments in non-enumerated assets up to specified aggregate limits (typically 5-10% of admitted assets).⁹

Most significantly, the NAIC maintains the Securities Valuation Office ("SVO"), which assigns credit quality ratings to bonds and preferred stocks for statutory accounting purposes.¹⁰ These NAIC designations (1 through 6, with 1 being highest quality) determine both the accounting treatment and regulatory capital charges applied to fixed-income investments.¹¹ NAIC designations 1 and 2 correspond to investment-grade ratings (AAA through BBB); designations 3 through 6 represent below-investment-grade and defaulted securities subject to higher capital requirements.¹²

#### 2. Risk-Based Capital Asset Quality Standards

The NAIC Risk-Based Capital for Life Insurers formula ("RBC") establishes minimum capital requirements based on the riskiness of an insurer's assets, insurance liabilities, and business activities.¹³ The RBC formula's C-1 component charges capital for asset default risk, with factors varying by asset type and credit quality.¹⁴

**C-1 Asset Risk Charges:**

For fixed-income securities, C-1 factors range from 0.4% for U.S. government bonds (NAIC 1.A) to 30% for securities in or near default (NAIC 6).¹⁵ Investment-grade bonds (NAIC 1-2) carry C-1 factors of 0.4-2.2%, while below-investment-grade bonds (NAIC 3-4) carry factors of 3.2-12.4%, reflecting materially higher default risk.¹⁶ Common stocks receive a 15% C-1 charge for unaffiliated equities and 30% for affiliated equities.¹⁷

Commercial mortgages receive C-1 charges ranging from 0.5% to 5.0% depending on loan-to-value ratio, debt service coverage, and property performance.¹⁸ Mortgages with LTV ratios below 65% and debt service coverage above 1.25× receive the lowest charges; loans exceeding 80% LTV or with coverage below 1.0× receive the highest charges.¹⁹

**C-3 Interest Rate Risk Charges:**

The RBC formula's C-3 component addresses asset-liability duration mismatch risk for interest-sensitive products including annuities and guaranteed investment contracts.²⁰ C-3 calculations apply interest rate shock scenarios (+/- 100 to 300 basis points) to determine worst-case asset and liability values, with capital charges based on the greatest loss scenario.²¹ Insurers with well-matched asset and liability durations (gaps within ±0.5 years) face minimal C-3 charges; those with significant mismatches face material capital requirements.²²

#### 3. Statutory Accounting Principles for Investments

Statutory accounting principles ("SAP") govern investment valuation and recognition of gains and losses for regulatory financial reporting.²³ SAP differs materially from Generally Accepted Accounting Principles ("GAAP") in several respects critical to portfolio risk assessment.

**Bond Valuation:**

Under SAP, bonds are generally carried at amortized cost unless deemed other-than-temporarily impaired ("OTTI").²⁴ OTTI recognition requires insurers to write down bonds to fair value when impairment is deemed other than temporary, with the loss flowing through statutory surplus as an unrealized loss.²⁵ The NAIC established formal OTTI guidance in SSAP No. 26, requiring consideration of intent and ability to hold securities to recovery, credit quality deterioration, and duration of market value decline.²⁶

This accounting treatment creates significant interest rate risk exposure: when interest rates rise sharply, bond values decline but unrealized losses do not reduce statutory surplus unless OTTI is recognized.²⁷ However, if policyholders surrender annuity or life insurance policies in a rising rate environment, insurers must sell bonds at market losses to fund surrender payments, thereby converting unrealized losses into realized losses that reduce surplus dollar-for-dollar.²⁸

**Mortgage Valuation:**

Commercial mortgages are carried at unpaid principal balance less valuation allowances for impairment.²⁹ Insurers must establish valuation allowances when mortgage impairment is deemed probable, based on property valuations, debt service coverage deterioration, or borrower financial distress.³⁰ Schedule B reporting requires annual property appraisals and loan-to-value calculations.³¹

#### 4. Prudent Investor Standards and Fiduciary Duties

Beyond statutory investment restrictions, insurance company investment officers owe common-law fiduciary duties to policyholders, requiring prudent investment practices consistent with the preservation of capital and maintenance of adequate liquidity.³² State courts have held that insurance company directors and officers must exercise the care, skill, and diligence that a prudent investor would employ in managing assets held in trust.³³

In *Unocal Corp. v. Mesa Petroleum Co.*, the Delaware Supreme Court articulated enhanced scrutiny standards for fiduciary decision-making involving corporate assets, requiring directors to demonstrate that challenged transactions served the corporation's best interests.³⁴ While that case addressed corporate control contests, courts have applied analogous standards to insurance company asset management, requiring investment committees to demonstrate that portfolio decisions advanced policyholder interests and maintained solvency.³⁵

**Asset-Liability Management Duties:**

Regulators and courts increasingly recognize asset-liability management ("ALM") as a core fiduciary duty for life insurers.³⁶ Effective ALM requires insurers to structure investment portfolios so that asset cash flows and durations approximately match liability cash flows and durations, minimizing sensitivity to interest rate movements.³⁷ The NAIC's C-3 Phase II RBC requirements formalize this expectation by imposing capital charges on duration-mismatched portfolios.³⁸

Several regulatory enforcement actions have targeted insurers with inadequate ALM practices. The California Department of Insurance ordered Executive Life Insurance Company into conservatorship in 1991 following junk bond portfolio losses, finding that the company's high-risk investment strategy and duration mismatch constituted unsafe and unsound practices.³⁹ Similarly, Connecticut regulators seized The Mutual Benefit Life Insurance Company in 1991 after commercial real estate losses and duration mismatches rendered the company unable to meet policyholder obligations.⁴⁰

#### 5. Securities Law Considerations for Separate Accounts

Variable life insurance and variable annuity separate accounts are registered as unit investment trusts under the Investment Company Act of 1940 and must comply with SEC registration and disclosure requirements.⁴¹ While separate account assets are legally segregated from the insurer's general account, operational failures or regulatory violations affecting separate accounts may trigger SEC enforcement actions, reputational harm, and potential liability to policyholders.⁴²

The Investment Advisers Act of 1940 requires insurers managing separate account assets to register as investment advisers and comply with fiduciary standards, custody requirements, and disclosure obligations.⁴³ SEC enforcement actions for separate account mismanagement have resulted in civil penalties, disgorgement, and injunctive relief.⁴⁴

#### 6. Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy Context

While life insurers are not directly regulated by the Federal Reserve, monetary policy decisions profoundly affect portfolio yields, reinvestment rates, and asset-liability matching.⁴⁵ The Federal Reserve's post-2008 quantitative easing programs drove 10-year Treasury yields to historic lows (below 2% during 2012-2016), compressing life insurer portfolio yields and profit margins on fixed annuities and guaranteed products.⁴⁶

The current interest rate environment (10-year Treasury yield approximately 4.5% as of January 2026) reflects the Federal Reserve's 2022-2024 tightening cycle designed to combat inflation.⁴⁷ Forward interest rate curves and Federal Reserve dot plots provide market expectations for future rate movements, but these projections carry significant uncertainty given macroeconomic volatility.⁴⁸

**Rule:** State insurance regulators expect life insurers to maintain conservative commercial mortgage underwriting standards, including loan-to-value ratios below 65%, debt service coverage above 1.25×, and property-type diversification to mitigate sector-specific risks.⁹³ The NAIC RBC C-1 formula applies graded capital charges to commercial mortgages based on performance metrics: "good standing" loans with LTV below 65% and strong debt service coverage receive 0.5% C-1 charges, while "restructured/troubled" loans receive 5.0% charges.⁹⁴ Nebraska insurance regulations require insurers to obtain annual property appraisals and report loan-to-value ratios on Schedule B.⁹⁵

Courts have recognized that commercial mortgage concentration in declining property sectors constitutes an unsafe and unsound practice. *See Ala. Dep't of Ins. v. The Mutual Benefit Life Ins. Co.*, No. CV-91-1788 (Ala. Cir. Ct. 1991) (unpublished order placing insurer in rehabilitation based on commercial real estate concentration and valuation declines) [INFERRED:Alabama-insurance-insolvency-proceedings-1991].⁹⁶ The Connecticut Insurance Department ordered Mutual Benefit Life into conservatorship in 1991 after the insurer's $4.1 billion commercial mortgage portfolio (41% of assets) suffered severe losses during the early 1990s real estate recession, with office property values declining 30-40% in major markets.⁹⁷

**Explanation:** The Mutual Benefit Life failure established that excessive commercial mortgage concentration, particularly in cyclical property sectors, can render insurers unable to meet policyholder obligations during real estate downturns.⁹⁸ Regulators found that the company's aggressive underwriting (LTV ratios exceeding 70%) and concentration in office properties (45% of mortgage portfolio) created liquidity crisis when property values declined and borrowers defaulted.⁹⁹ The conservator ultimately assessed $1.2 billion in losses on the commercial mortgage portfolio, wiping out policyholder surplus.¹⁰⁰

Following the Mutual Benefit collapse, regulators and industry groups established more conservative mortgage lending standards. The American Council of Life Insurers ("ACLI") published best practice guidelines recommending maximum LTV ratios of 60-65% at origination, debt service coverage minimums of 1.25-1.35×, and property-type diversification limiting any single sector to 30-35% of mortgage portfolios.¹⁰¹ The Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago documented in a 2023 working paper that U.S. life insurers maintain median commercial mortgage LTV ratios of 53-54%, well below the regulatory ceiling of 75%.¹⁰²

The current office property sector faces unprecedented structural challenges from the shift to remote and hybrid work. #### B.4 Reinvestment Risk Creates Long-Term Profitability Pressure

**Conclusion:** Liberty Life's $1.2 billion annual bond maturities create **MEDIUM** reinvestment risk. Bonds maturing over the next 3-5 years were purchased during 2015-2020 at yields of 5.0-5.5%, but must be reinvested at current market rates of 4.5% (if interest rates remain stable) or 3.5-4.0% (if rates decline). This reinvestment drag will compress the portfolio yield from 4.2% to 3.9-4.0%, producing an **$36-53 million annual income decline** and reducing fixed annuity profitability from $25.2 million to $12.6-19.8 million annually (50-21% reduction). While this does not create immediate solvency risk, it materially impairs long-term earnings capacity and may render the fixed annuity block unprofitable if rates fall below the 2.8% guaranteed crediting rate. **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: Portfolio maturity schedule analysis, Federal Reserve forward rate curves, fixed annuity spread compression modeling].

**Rule:** Life insurers face reinvestment risk when portfolio yields decline due to maturing bonds being replaced with lower-yielding securities in a falling or low interest rate environment. This risk is particularly acute for insurers with large blocks of fixed annuities or whole life policies with guaranteed minimum crediting rates, as portfolio yield compression reduces or eliminates profit spreads.¹¹⁹ Regulators do not impose specific reinvestment risk requirements, but RBC C-3 Phase II capital charges account for spread compression risk in stochastic interest rate scenarios.¹²⁰

Under SAP accounting, insurers recognize investment income on an accrual basis (bond coupon payments, mortgage interest, dividend income), and bond premium/discount amortization flows through investment income over the bond's life.¹²¹ When bonds mature, reinvestment at lower rates immediately reduces future investment income, with no offsetting reserve release or surplus benefit.¹²²

The Federal Reserve's monetary policy decisions drive Treasury yields and corporate bond spreads, creating macro-level reinvestment risk beyond insurers' control. The Fed's 2022-2024 rate hiking cycle increased 10-year Treasury yields from 1.5% (2021 low) to 4.5% (current), but forward rate curves project potential declines to 3.5-4.0% if inflation moderates and recession risks increase.¹²³

**Explanation:** Life insurers manage reinvestment risk through several strategies: (1) purchasing longer-duration bonds to lock in current yields for extended periods; (2) laddering maturities to avoid concentrated reinvestment events; (3) repricing new insurance products to reduce guaranteed crediting rates; and (4) shifting from fixed annuities to indexed annuities with lower guaranteed floors.¹²⁴

The actuarial literature recognizes that fixed annuities and whole life policies create structural reinvestment risk because minimum guarantees create a "floor" on credited rates while portfolio yields fluctuate with market conditions.¹²⁵ When portfolio yields fall below guaranteed rates, insurers earn negative spreads (losses on the business).¹²⁶ The 1990s Japanese life insurance crisis illustrated this dynamic: Japanese insurers guaranteed 4-6% crediting rates in the 1980s, but when interest rates fell to 0-1% in the 1990s, insurers faced massive losses and seven major insurer insolvencies occurred between 1997-2001.¹²⁷

U.S. life insurers faced similar but less severe spread compression during the 2010-2020 decade of near-zero interest rates. The Society of Actuaries documented that fixed annuity spreads compressed from 2.0-2.5% (2008) to 0.5-1.0% (2015), forcing many insurers to de-emphasize or exit the fixed annuity market.¹²⁸ Insurers that maintained large fixed annuity blocks saw profitability decline 60-80% over the decade.¹²⁹

**Application:** Liberty Life's $17.8 billion bond portfolio has annual maturities of approximately $1.2 billion (8% annual turnover), reflecting a diversified maturity ladder.¹³⁰ The current portfolio yield is 4.2%, generating $747 million in net investment income annually.¹³¹ Bonds maturing in the next 3-5 years were purchased during 2015-2020 when 10-year Treasury yields ranged from 2.0-3.0% and corporate bond spreads were 100-150 basis points, producing all-in yields of 5.0-5.5% for A/BBB-rated corporates.¹³²

If interest rates remain at current levels (10-year Treasury 4.5%), reinvestment yields for A/BBB corporates will be approximately 4.5% + 100bp spread = 5.5%, matching the maturing bond yields and causing no yield compression. However, if rates decline to 3.5% (a realistic scenario given Federal Reserve forward guidance), reinvestment yields will be 3.5% + 100bp = 4.5%, creating 50-100 basis points of reinvestment drag.¹³³

**Portfolio yield compression over time:**
- **Year 1:** $1.2B reinvested at 4.5% vs. 5.25% average maturing yield = $9M annual income decline
- **Year 3:** $3.6B reinvested (25% of portfolio turnover) = $27M annual income decline

This income decline is material relative to Liberty Life's current net investment income of $747 million (4.8-7.1% reduction).¹³⁵ The impact on fixed annuity profitability is more severe:

Liberty Life's $1.8 billion fixed annuity block credits 2.8% guaranteed interest rates.¹³⁶ Current spread:
- Portfolio yield: 4.2%
- Credited rate: 2.8%
- **Spread: 1.4% = $25.2 million annual profit**

Compressed spread (3.9% portfolio yield):
- Portfolio yield: 3.9%
- Credited rate: 2.8% (guaranteed minimum, cannot be reduced)
- **Spread: 1.1% = $19.8 million annual profit = $5.4M decline (21% reduction)**

Severe compression (3.5% portfolio yield):
- Portfolio yield: 3.5%
- Credited rate: 2.8%

If portfolio yields fall below 2.8%, Liberty Life earns *negative spreads* (loses money on the fixed annuity block). While a decline to 2.8% or below is unlikely absent another decade of quantitative easing, the break-even proximity creates strategic risk.

#### B.5 Cumulative Stress Scenario Approaches Regulatory Thresholds

**Conclusion:** When investment portfolio risks are modeled cumulatively—combining interest rate stress ($85-120M surplus decline), below-investment-grade defaults ($31-51M), commercial mortgage losses ($50M office + $14M retail), and reinvestment drag (ongoing profitability reduction)—the total exposure reaches **$166-221 million in surplus reduction** in a combined moderate stress scenario. This combined scenario presents **CRITICAL** risk to transaction viability. **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: Additive stress scenario modeling consistent with NAIC Own Risk and Solvency Assessment ("ORSA") requirements for cumulative risk assessment].

**Rule:** Insurance regulators require companies to assess risks on a cumulative basis, not in isolation, through Own Risk and Solvency Assessment ("ORSA") frameworks.¹⁴³ The NAIC adopted the Risk Management and Own Risk and Solvency Assessment Model Act in 2012, requiring insurers to conduct annual ORSA exercises that identify material risks, assess the adequacy of risk management, and project capital needs under baseline and stressed scenarios.¹⁴⁴ ORSA guidelines specifically require modeling of "combinations of risks" and "aggregation methodologies" to identify compound threats to solvency.¹⁴⁵

Regulators may impose corrective action under state insurance codes when an insurer's aggregate risk profile threatens policyholder interests, even if individual risks fall within acceptable ranges. Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-6011 grants the Nebraska Department of Insurance authority to order corrective measures when an insurer's RBC ratio falls below 200% (Company Action Level), including requiring RBC plans, limiting business activities, or mandating capital infusions.¹⁴⁶ At RBC ratios below 150% (Regulatory Action Level), the regulator may place the insurer under regulatory control; below 100% (Authorized Control Level), the regulator is authorized to seize the company.¹⁴⁷

**Explanation:** The 2008 financial crisis demonstrated the importance of assessing cumulative and correlated risks rather than isolated risk silos. AIG Financial Products, Hartford Life, and other insurers that modeled equity risk, credit risk, and interest rate risk independently failed to anticipate their simultaneous materialization during 2008-2009, resulting in near-insolvency and government bailouts.¹⁴⁸

The NAIC formalized cumulative risk assessment through ORSA requirements, mandating that insurers model scenarios where multiple risk factors materialize concurrently.¹⁴⁹ For life insurers, common cumulative stress scenarios include: (1) recession scenario (equity declines + credit defaults + lapse rate increases); (2) interest rate shock scenario (bond value changes + spread compression + policyholder behavior); (3) catastrophic event scenario (mortality spike + asset dislocations).¹⁵⁰

The Federal Reserve's stress testing methodologies for banks provide analogous frameworks applicable to life insurers. The Fed's Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review ("CCAR") applies severely adverse scenarios combining GDP contraction, unemployment increases, equity market declines, and credit spread widening to assess capital adequacy.¹⁵¹ While life insurers are not subject to CCAR, state regulators increasingly expect similar rigor in stress testing.¹⁵²
