### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Vermont Captive Insurance Regulatory Framework

Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC operates as a special purpose financial captive (SPFC) under Vermont Statutes Annotated Title 8, Chapter 141, which establishes comprehensive regulatory standards for captive insurance companies domiciled in Vermont.¹ Vermont has emerged as the leading domestic captive domicile in the United States, with over 1,100 licensed captives as of 2024, due to its flexible regulatory framework and specialized captive insurance division within the Vermont Department of Financial Regulation (DFR).²

Vermont law defines a "special purpose financial captive" as a captive insurance company formed or licensed under Vermont captive statutes to provide financing or reinsurance relating to insurance or reinsurance securitization, including catastrophe bonds, weather derivatives, and life insurance reserve financing transactions.³ Unlike traditional captive insurance companies that primarily insure risks of their parent organizations or affiliated entities, SPFCs serve as financing vehicles to assume specific blocks of reserves or risks through structured transactions.⁴

**Capital and Surplus Requirements:**
Vermont law requires captive insurance companies to maintain minimum capital and surplus of $250,000, though the Commissioner of the Vermont DFR may prescribe additional capital and surplus based on the type, volume, and nature of insurance business transacted.⁵ For SPFCs engaged in life insurance reserve financing—such as assuming AXXX/XXX redundant reserves—Vermont regulators typically require demonstration of adequate assets and collateral to support ceded reserves, though the specific requirements are tailored to each transaction through regulatory approval processes.⁶

**Regulatory Oversight:**
Vermont DFR exercises ongoing supervision of captive insurers through annual financial statement filings, triennial financial examinations, and quarterly financial reporting requirements for SPFCs.⁷ The Department conducts risk-based examinations focusing on solvency, reserve adequacy, investment portfolio quality, and compliance with approved business plans.⁸

**Statutory Reserve Credit Standards:**
For a ceding insurer domiciled in a state other than Vermont (such as Liberty Life Insurance Company domiciled in Nebraska) to receive statutory reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to a Vermont captive, the captive must either: (1) be authorized or accredited in the ceding insurer's state of domicile, or (2) post collateral acceptable to the domiciliary state regulator under credit for reinsurance standards.⁹ Vermont captives are generally unauthorized reinsurers in most states, necessitating collateral arrangements such as trust accounts, letters of credit, or parental guarantees to secure reserve credit.¹⁰

#### 2. NAIC Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48) Framework

Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48) was adopted by the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Executive Committee and Plenary on December 16, 2014, with an effective date of January 1, 2015.¹¹ AG48 establishes consistent methods for calculating economic reserves and required security levels for XXX and AXXX reserve financing transactions involving affiliated reinsurers, including captive insurance companies.¹²

**Purpose and Policy Objectives:**
AG48 responded to regulatory concerns that life insurers were using affiliated captive reinsurance structures to reduce statutory reserve requirements without corresponding reduction in economic risk.¹³ Under pre-AG48 practices, life insurers could cede redundant AXXX/XXX reserves (reserves exceeding economic reserves based on realistic assumptions) to affiliated captives, which would back those reserves with parental guarantees or letters of credit rather than high-quality liquid assets.¹⁴ This practice, sometimes characterized as "shadow insurance," raised solvency concerns that captives lacked sufficient resources to pay claims if the parental guarantee proved uncollectible.¹⁵

**Primary Security and Other Security Classification:**
AG48 divides required reserves into two layers with differentiated collateral requirements:¹⁶

*Primary Security:* A baseline amount of reserves that must be backed by high-quality assets including cash, investment-grade bonds (NAIC designations 1 or 2), assets held in trust meeting specified criteria, and limited other qualifying assets.¹⁷ The Primary Security amount is calculated using prescribed formulae based on policy characteristics, mortality assumptions, and interest rate scenarios.¹⁸

*Other Security:* Reserves exceeding the Primary Security threshold may be backed by a broader range of collateral, including lower-rated bonds, equity investments, and parental guarantees subject to certain conditions.¹⁹ For parental guarantees to qualify as acceptable Other Security, industry practice (though not explicitly codified in AG48's publicly available provisions) typically requires the guarantor to maintain: (a) Risk-Based Capital (RBC) ratio exceeding 300% of Company Action Level, (b) net worth exceeding the guaranteed amount, and (c) financial strength rating of A or better from a recognized rating agency.²⁰

**AG48 Grandfather Clause:**
AG48 includes a critical grandfather provision: the substantive requirements of AG48 do not and will not apply to any life insurance policies that were included in a reserve financing arrangement as of December 31, 2014 (the "Grandfathering Test").²¹ This bright-line test provides legal certainty for captive structures established before AG48's adoption.²²

During AG48's development, retroactivity was identified as "one of the most prevalent concerns for the life sector," and after "heated debate," the issue was "finally laid to rest" through adoption of the non-retroactive Grandfathering Test, which the industry characterized as "a significant victory."²³ The alternative proposal—which would have required immediate AG48 compliance for any transaction amended after January 1, 2015—was rejected in favor of the grandfathering approach.²⁴

**State Examination Authority Notwithstanding Grandfather Status:**
While AG48's grandfather clause exempts pre-2015 captive structures from AG48's substantive requirements, state insurance departments retain examination authority to review captive structures for safety and soundness under general insurance code provisions.²⁵ Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-416.06 grants the Nebraska Director of Insurance authority to examine affiliated reinsurance arrangements and determine whether reserve credit is appropriate based on solvency considerations.²⁶ This examination authority exists independently of AG48 compliance and may be exercised even for grandfathered structures if the regulator determines the arrangement poses unacceptable solvency risk to the ceding insurer.²⁷

#### 3. Nebraska Credit for Reinsurance Standards

Nebraska law follows the NAIC Credit for Reinsurance Model Law framework, codified in Nebraska Revised Statutes §§ 44-416.01 through 44-416.11.²⁸ These provisions govern when a Nebraska-domiciled insurer may take statutory reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to another insurer.

**Authorized vs. Unauthorized Reinsurers:**
For reinsurance ceded to an authorized reinsurer (licensed in Nebraska or holding a Certificate of Authority), the ceding insurer may take full reserve credit without additional collateral requirements.²⁹ However, for reinsurance ceded to an unauthorized reinsurer—such as a Vermont captive not licensed in Nebraska—the ceding insurer may take reserve credit only if the assuming reinsurer posts security in one of the following forms:³⁰

(a) **Trust Account:** Assets deposited in a qualified U.S. financial institution trust account for the benefit of the ceding insurer, consisting of cash, securities, or other qualified investments.³¹

(b) **Letter of Credit:** An irrevocable, clean letter of credit issued by a qualified U.S. financial institution or foreign bank meeting specified criteria, payable upon presentation of sight draft by the ceding insurer.³²

(c) **Funds Withheld:** The ceding insurer withholds assets otherwise payable to the reinsurer as security for the reinsurer's obligations.³³

(d) **Parental Guarantee:** A guarantee issued by the reinsurer's parent company meeting creditworthiness standards prescribed by the Director of Insurance, typically requiring the parent to maintain specified financial strength ratings and capital levels.³⁴

**Regulatory Discretion on Collateral Adequacy:**
The Nebraska Director of Insurance retains discretion to determine whether collateral arrangements are "adequate" to protect the ceding insurer's policyholders, considering factors such as: (i) quality and liquidity of collateral assets, (ii) creditworthiness of guarantors, (iii) overall solvency impact on the ceding insurer, and (iv) whether the reinsurance arrangement presents undue concentration or counterparty risk.³⁵

#### 4. NAIC Risk-Based Capital (RBC) Framework

The NAIC Risk-Based Capital for Life and Health Insurers Model Act establishes minimum capital requirements based on the risks assumed by life insurance companies.³⁶ Each insurer calculates its Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) and Authorized Control Level Risk-Based Capital (ACL), with regulatory action levels triggered at specific ratios:³⁷

| RBC Ratio | Action Level | Regulatory Consequence |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| **200%** | Company Action Level (CAL) | Company must submit RBC Plan to regulator within 45 days |
| **150%** | Regulatory Action Level (RAL) | Regulator may order corrective actions, including capital infusion, dividend restrictions, asset sales |
| **100%** | Authorized Control Level (ACL) | Regulator is authorized (but not required) to seize company through rehabilitation or conservatorship |
| **70%** | Mandatory Control Level (MCL) | Regulator must seize company |

Nebraska has adopted the NAIC RBC Model Act through Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-6011, applying these thresholds to all Nebraska-domiciled life insurers.³⁸ Liberty Life Insurance Company's current RBC ratio of 188% falls below the 200% Company Action Level, requiring submission of an RBC Plan (filed November 2024 proposing $150 million capital injection to restore RBC to 204%).³⁹

**Critical Interaction with Captive Recapture Risk:**
If Nebraska DOI disallows statutory reserve credit for the Liberty Re VT captive reinsurance, LLIC would be required to recapture $850 million in reserves and re-establish them on its statutory balance sheet.⁴⁰ With Liberty Re VT holding only $120 million in assets, the net impact would be a $730 million surplus reduction ($850M reserves less $120M assets returned).⁴¹ This would reduce LLIC's RBC ratio from 188% to approximately 114%, falling into the Regulatory Action Level (100-150%) zone where Nebraska DOI has broad authority to impose corrective measures or initiate conservatorship proceedings.⁴²

**Counter-Analysis:** LLIC may argue that proactively implementing a $300M LOC before receiving Nebraska DOI's final examination report (expected Q1 2025) is premature and imposes unnecessary costs, particularly if the final report ultimately accepts the current structure (Scenario 1, 50-60% probability).⁹⁴ The annual LOC cost of $2.55M-$3.45M represents 1.4-1.9% of LLIC's statutory net income and would reduce dividend capacity to the parent holding company.⁹⁵ However, this argument fails to account for: (1) the magnitude of downside risk in Scenario 3 (deal-blocking $880M capital requirement), (2) the timing imperative—if Nebraska issues a final report requiring collateral strengthening in Q1 2025, there may be insufficient time before Q3 2025 target closing to negotiate and implement an LOC, potentially requiring closing delay or purchase price renegotiation, and (3) the strong positive signal that proactive LOC implementation sends to Nebraska DOI, substantially increasing probability of favorable regulatory treatment.⁹⁶ Given the asymmetric risk profile (limited cost vs. potential deal-blocking exposure), the LOC represents prudent risk management and should be implemented as a **condition precedent to closing**.⁹⁷
