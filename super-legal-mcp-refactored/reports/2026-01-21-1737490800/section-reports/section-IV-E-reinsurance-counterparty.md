### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. NAIC Credit for Reinsurance Model Law

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Credit for Reinsurance Model Law establishes the framework governing when an insurer may take statutory reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to another entity.1 This framework is critical to Liberty Life Insurance Company's capital structure, as LLIC has ceded $850 million in reserves to Global Reassurance Ltd. and an additional $850 million in redundant AXXX/XXX reserves to its Vermont captive, Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC.

Under the Model Law, reinsurance credit is available only if the assuming reinsurer satisfies one of three categories: (1) authorized reinsurers licensed in the ceding insurer's state of domicile, (2) accredited reinsurers meeting specified financial standards, or (3) unauthorized reinsurers posting qualifying collateral equal to 100% of the ceding insurer's reinsurance recoverables.2 Nebraska has adopted the Model Law framework through Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-416.01 et seq., governing LLIC's ability to claim reserve credit for its reinsurance program.3

**Authorized Reinsurers:** Swiss Re and Munich Re are authorized reinsurers, licensed to transact reinsurance business in Nebraska.4 Because they are authorized, LLIC may take full statutory reserve credit for amounts ceded to these reinsurers without requiring collateral, provided the reinsurers maintain minimum financial strength ratings and surplus levels established by Nebraska Department of Insurance regulations.5 The Nebraska DOI continuously monitors authorized reinsurers' financial condition through quarterly financial statement filings, annual examinations, and coordination with reinsurers' domiciliary state regulators.6

**Unauthorized Reinsurers:** Global Reassurance Ltd., domiciled in Cayman Islands, is not licensed in Nebraska and therefore classified as an unauthorized reinsurer.7 For LLIC to claim statutory reserve credit for the $850 million in reserves ceded to Global Re, the reinsurer must post qualifying collateral in an amount equal to 100% of LLIC's reinsurance recoverables.8 Qualifying collateral includes: (a) letters of credit issued by qualifying banks, (b) trust accounts funded with cash or securities meeting specified investment quality standards, (c) funds withheld arrangements where LLIC retains premiums pending claims settlement, or (d) any combination thereof.9

Global Re has posted an $850 million letter of credit issued by Barclays Bank to satisfy the collateral requirement.10 The letter of credit must be "clean" (payable on demand without conditions), irrevocable during its term, and issued by a bank meeting Nebraska DOI's qualifying bank standards (typically rated A or better by major credit rating agencies and subject to regulation by U.S. federal or state banking authorities or equivalent international standards).11

#### 2. Actuarial Guideline 48 and Captive Reinsurance Regulation

Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48), adopted by the NAIC in December 2014 and effective January 1, 2015, imposed stricter requirements on captive reinsurance arrangements used by life insurers to finance redundant reserves required under Regulation XXX (term life) and Actuarial Guideline AXXX (universal life with secondary guarantees).12 Prior to AG48, insurers commonly ceded redundant reserves to affiliated or third-party captive reinsurers capitalized with minimal assets and substantial parental guarantees, creating counterparty credit risk if the parent proved unable to fund the guarantee.13

AG48 divided required collateral into two categories: "Primary Security" and "Other Security."14 Primary Security consists of high-quality assets including cash, investment-grade securities, and secured loans, and must equal at least a specified percentage of required reserves (typically 50-75% depending on reserve type).15 Other Security includes lower-quality assets such as parental guarantees, surplus notes, and below-investment-grade securities, subject to strict conditions including parent financial strength requirements (RBC ratio exceeding 300%, net worth exceeding guarantee amount, and minimum A financial strength rating).16

**Grandfather Clause:** AG48 included a critical grandfather provision: captive reinsurance arrangements established before January 1, 2015, are not subject to AG48's Primary Security and Other Security requirements, provided the terms of the pre-existing arrangement are not materially modified post-2015.17 Material modifications triggering loss of grandfather status include increasing ceded reserves beyond the original treaty limits, weakening collateral quality, or extending the treaty term beyond the original expiration date.18

Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC was established in 2010, five years before AG48's effective date.19 This pre-2015 establishment date qualifies Liberty Re VT for grandfather status, exempting it from AG48's heightened collateral requirements—provided LLIC has not materially modified the treaty terms since 2015.20 However, as discussed below, the Nebraska Department of Insurance has questioned whether the captive's reliance on an undercapitalized parental guarantee constitutes a safety and soundness concern that supersedes AG48 grandfather protection under the DOI's general examination authority.21

#### 3. Nebraska Statutory Framework for Reinsurance and Captives

Nebraska Insurance Code § 44-416.01 through § 44-416.17 implement the NAIC Credit for Reinsurance Model Law, establishing detailed requirements for reserve credit, collateral adequacy, and ongoing monitoring.22 Nebraska Regulation 56 supplements the statutory framework with specific requirements for letters of credit, trust agreements, and financial strength ratings for authorized reinsurers.23

Nebraska DOI's market conduct examination authority, codified at Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-5901 through 44-5910, grants the Director of Insurance broad power to examine insurers domiciled in Nebraska "as often as the director deems appropriate" to determine financial condition, ability to fulfill obligations, and compliance with law.24 This examination authority extends to reviewing the adequacy of reinsurance arrangements, including captive structures, even if those arrangements technically comply with AG48 or other specific regulations.25 If the Director determines that a reinsurance arrangement poses material risk to the insurer's solvency or policyholders' interests, the Director may order corrective action including additional collateral, treaty modification, or reserve recapture, notwithstanding technical compliance with reinsurance credit regulations.26

The interplay between AG48 grandfather protection and Nebraska DOI's general safety and soundness authority creates legal uncertainty: does the grandfather clause create a substantive right preventing Nebraska from requiring captive strengthening, or merely establish a regulatory presumption that pre-2015 structures are acceptable absent specific evidence of inadequacy?27 Industry precedent and legal commentary suggest the latter interpretation—regulators retain authority to require corrective action if a grandfathered structure creates material solvency risk, but the burden of proof shifts to the regulator to demonstrate specific inadequacy rather than mere failure to meet current standards.28

#### 4. Reinsurance Recoverables and Statutory Accounting

Under Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP), specifically Statement of Statutory Accounting Principles No. 61R (SSAP 61R), reinsurance recoverables are recorded as admitted assets on an insurer's statutory balance sheet, representing amounts the ceding insurer expects to collect from assuming reinsurers.29 Recoverables include: (a) amounts recoverable from reinsurers for paid claims (amounts already paid by the ceding insurer to policyholders and billed to reinsurers), (b) reserves held by reinsurers on unpaid claims, and (c) unearned premium reserves held by reinsurers.30

For LLIC, the statutory balance sheet as of Q3 2024 includes approximately $850 million in reinsurance recoverables from Global Reassurance Ltd. related to the quota share term life treaty, plus additional recoverables from Swiss Re (modified coinsurance on universal life), Munich Re (excess reinsurance on group life), and Liberty Re VT (AXXX/XXX redundant reserves).31 These recoverables are offset by corresponding reductions in policy reserve liabilities, producing net capital relief equal to the ceded reserves minus any required Schedule F provisions for uncollectible reinsurance.32

**Schedule F Provisions:** Under SAP, insurers must establish provisions (reserve deductions) for reinsurance recoverables from unauthorized or financially impaired reinsurers.33 Schedule F of the NAIC Annual Statement categorizes reinsurance recoverables by the financial strength and collateral status of each assuming reinsurer, requiring provisions ranging from 0% (fully collateralized or highly rated authorized reinsurers) to 100% (unauthorized reinsurers without collateral or reinsurers in rehabilitation or liquidation).34

For Global Re's $850 million recoverable, LLIC currently establishes a 0% Schedule F provision because the recoverable is fully collateralized by the Barclays letter of credit.35 If the letter of credit were to lapse without replacement, LLIC would be required to establish a Schedule F provision based on Global Re's uncollateralized financial strength—likely 20-50% given Global Re's A- (Excellent) rating from A.M. Best but unauthorized status, resulting in a $170 million to $425 million charge to statutory surplus.36

#### 5. Recapture Provisions in Reinsurance Treaties

Reinsurance treaties typically include recapture provisions allowing either the ceding insurer or the assuming reinsurer to terminate the treaty and return ceded business to the ceding insurer under specified conditions.37 Common recapture triggers include: (a) voluntary recapture by the ceding insurer after a specified period (e.g., 10 years) if business proves more profitable than expected, (b) mandatory recapture if the assuming reinsurer's financial strength rating falls below a specified threshold, (c) recapture upon change of control of either party, or (d) recapture if collateral requirements are not maintained.38

**Global Re Quota Share Recapture Terms:** The Global Reassurance Ltd. treaty permits LLIC to voluntarily recapture ceded policies after 10 years from issue date, with policies issued in 2020 becoming eligible for recapture in 2030.39 Recapture requires LLIC to provide 180-day advance written notice and pay a recapture settlement amount equal to the statutory reserves for recaptured policies, calculated using LLIC's current reserve methodology, less a recapture fee (typically 2-5% of reserves to compensate the reinsurer for lost future profits).40

**Vermont Captive Recapture Risk:** Unlike the voluntary Global Re recapture, the Liberty Re VT captive structure faces potential involuntary recapture if Nebraska DOI disallows statutory reserve credit due to concerns about parental guarantee adequacy or AG48 compliance.41 Involuntary recapture would require LLIC to restore $850 million in reserves to its statutory balance sheet, reducing surplus by $730 million (the gap between ceded reserves and the $120 million in captive assets available to LLIC).42

**Rule:** Under Nebraska Insurance Code § 44-416.09, a ceding insurer may claim statutory reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to an unauthorized reinsurer only if the reinsurer posts qualifying collateral "in an amount equal to the statutory reserves and other reinsurance obligations," maintained continuously throughout the term of the reinsurance agreement.43 If the collateral becomes inadequate or non-qualifying at any time, the ceding insurer must either obtain replacement collateral within the treaty's cure period (typically 30-60 days) or establish a Schedule F provision for the uncollateralized recoverable amount, charged directly to statutory surplus.44 The Nebraska Director of Insurance may disallow reserve credit entirely if collateral adequacy concerns persist beyond the cure period, requiring the ceding insurer to recapture ceded reserves and restore them to its statutory balance sheet.45

Courts have held that the duty to maintain qualifying collateral is a continuing obligation of the reinsurer, not merely a one-time requirement at treaty inception. In *In re Integrity Insurance Co.*, the Colorado District Court held that when an unauthorized reinsurer's letter of credit expired without renewal, the ceding insurer was required to establish Schedule F provisions within 90 days or face regulatory sanctions for overstating admitted assets.46 Similarly, in *National Union Fire Insurance Co. v. Amerisure Mutual Insurance Co.*, the Seventh Circuit emphasized that collateral adequacy must be evaluated continuously, and the ceding insurer bears the ultimate risk if the reinsurer fails to maintain required collateral.47

**Explanation:** Letters of credit for reinsurance collateral are typically issued with one-year terms and renewed annually, requiring the issuing bank to consent to each renewal based on the bank's ongoing assessment of the reinsurer's creditworthiness and the bank's own risk appetite for reinsurance exposures.48 Industry data from the Reinsurance Association of America indicates that approximately 5-8% of reinsurance letters of credit are not renewed at expiration due to: (a) deterioration in the reinsurer's financial strength or credit rating, (b) the issuing bank's decision to reduce reinsurance exposure or exit the reinsurance LOC business, (c) increased LOC pricing making renewal uneconomical for the reinsurer, or (d) regulatory changes affecting the bank's capital requirements for LOC issuance.49

In the 2008-2010 financial crisis, major banks including Citibank, Bank of America, and Barclays significantly reduced their reinsurance LOC portfolios, declining to renew letters of credit for Bermuda and Cayman Islands-domiciled reinsurers even when the reinsurers remained financially sound, causing material disruption to ceding insurers who depended on those LOCs for statutory reserve credit.50 More recently, Basel III capital requirements imposed higher capital charges on banks for LOC exposures, making reinsurance LOCs less profitable for banks and increasing the risk of non-renewal.51

#### B.2 Vermont Captive Parental Guarantee Enforceability and Recapture Risk

**Conclusion:** The Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC captive structure presents **CRITICAL** risk with potential deal-blocking consequences. The captive's reliance on a $730 million parental guarantee from Liberty Life Holdings LLC (a parent with only $280 million in net worth, creating 2.6× leverage) violates industry best practices and may trigger Nebraska DOI corrective action requiring additional collateral or full recapture of $850 million in reserves. If Nebraska disallows reserve credit and requires recapture, LLIC's statutory surplus would decline by $730 million, reducing the RBC ratio from 188% to 114% (or from 204% post-capital-injection to 129%), falling dangerously close to the 100% Authorized Control Level at which Nebraska DOI may seize the company. The probability of Nebraska requiring some form of corrective action is estimated at 40-55%, with full recapture (deal-blocking scenario) at 10-15% probability. **Exposure:** $73 million to $365 million (probability-weighted, wide range reflects uncertainty in Nebraska DOI's ultimate decision and parent's ability to fund the guarantee if called). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Nebraska DOI November 2024 exit conference preliminary findings; AG48 regulatory framework; Liberty Life Holdings financial statements; industry precedent for captive structures with excessive parental guarantee leverage].

**Rule:** Actuarial Guideline 48, effective January 1, 2015, established requirements for captive reinsurance arrangements used to finance redundant Regulation XXX and Actuarial Guideline AXXX reserves, dividing required collateral into "Primary Security" (high-quality assets) and "Other Security" (including parental guarantees subject to strict parent financial strength requirements).77 While AG48 included a grandfather clause exempting captive arrangements established before January 1, 2015, from these heightened requirements, the grandfather protection is not absolute.78 State insurance regulators retain general safety and soundness examination authority under statutes such as Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-5901 to 44-5910, permitting the Director of Insurance to require corrective action if a reinsurance arrangement—even if grandfathered under AG48—poses material risk to the insurer's solvency or policyholders' interests.79

The NAIC's Financial Analysis Handbook provides guidance to state regulators on evaluating parental guarantees supporting captive reinsurance, recommending that regulators consider: (a) the parent's financial strength rating (A or better preferred), (b) the parent's RBC ratio (300% or higher preferred to ensure capacity to fund the guarantee without itself becoming impaired), (c) the ratio of the guarantee amount to parent net worth (1.0× or lower preferred to ensure parent can fund from existing capital), and (d) the parent's liquidity and access to capital markets (ability to raise funds quickly if guarantee is called).80 Guarantees exceeding parent net worth by more than 2.0× are considered "highly leveraged" and subject to enhanced regulatory scrutiny.81

Courts have consistently upheld state regulators' authority to require additional collateral or disallow reserve credit for reinsurance arrangements that pose safety and soundness concerns, even if those arrangements comply with specific regulatory provisions such as AG48. In *American Council of Life Insurers v. Nebraska Department of Insurance*, the Nebraska Supreme Court held that the Director's examination authority includes broad discretion to evaluate the substance of reinsurance transactions, not merely formal compliance with reinsurance credit regulations.82 Similarly, in *In re Rehabilitation of Midland Insurance Co.*, the Texas Court of Appeals affirmed the Insurance Commissioner's authority to disallow reserve credit for an affiliated reinsurance arrangement where the affiliated reinsurer lacked sufficient assets to support the ceded reserves, notwithstanding technical compliance with Texas reinsurance statutes.83

**Explanation:** Captive reinsurance structures became common in the life insurance industry following the adoption of Regulation XXX (2000) and Actuarial Guideline AXXX (2003), which imposed highly conservative reserve requirements for term life and universal life with secondary guarantees that many actuaries and industry participants considered redundant relative to actual expected claims.84 Rather than hold these redundant reserves on their statutory balance sheets (consuming capital and reducing RBC ratios), insurers ceded the reserves to affiliated captive reinsurers, typically special purpose financial captives (SPFCs) domiciled in Vermont, South Carolina, or other captive-friendly jurisdictions.85

The typical pre-AG48 captive structure involved: (1) the ceding insurer establishing a captive reinsurer as a wholly-owned subsidiary or affiliate, (2) ceding redundant reserves to the captive through a reinsurance treaty, (3) the captive holding minimal assets (often 10-20% of ceded reserves) invested in cash or high-quality bonds, (4) the captive's parent or another affiliate providing a parental guarantee for the balance of reserves (often 80-90% of ceded reserves), and (5) the ceding insurer claiming statutory reserve credit for the full amount ceded, thereby improving its RBC ratio and capital position.86 This structure allowed insurers to arbitrage the difference between statutory reserve requirements (based on conservative regulatory assumptions) and economic reserve requirements (based on realistic actuarial assumptions), freeing capital for business growth or shareholder distributions.87

The NAIC adopted AG48 in response to regulatory concerns that captive structures with minimal assets and substantial parental guarantees created counterparty credit risk—if the parent proved unable or unwilling to fund the guarantee when called, the ceding insurer would face sudden reserve recapture and capital depletion.88 AG48's Primary Security requirement (50-75% of reserves backed by high-quality assets) and Other Security parent financial strength requirements (RBC >300%, net worth exceeding guarantee, A rating or better) were designed to ensure that captives had sufficient resources to honor their reinsurance obligations without complete reliance on parental guarantees.89

However, AG48's grandfather clause created a two-tier regulatory framework: captives established before January 1, 2015, continued operating under pre-AG48 standards (minimal assets, substantial parental guarantees), while new captives faced stricter requirements.90 This created potential inequity and ongoing regulatory scrutiny of grandfathered structures, particularly where parental guarantees substantially exceeded parent net worth.91

In recent years, several state insurance departments have challenged grandfathered captive structures on safety and soundness grounds, notwithstanding AG48 grandfather status. In 2018, the New York Department of Financial Services (DFS) required a New York-domiciled life insurer to strengthen its Vermont captive arrangement (established in 2012, pre-AG48) by injecting $250 million in additional capital into the captive, reducing parental guarantee reliance from 3.5× parent net worth to 1.2×.92 The insurer challenged the DFS order, arguing AG48 grandfather protection prevented retroactive application of AG48 standards, but the New York Supreme Court upheld DFS's authority, holding that the grandfather clause did not eliminate DFS's general examination authority to require corrective action for structures posing solvency risk.93

Similarly, in 2020, the Vermont Department of Financial Regulation (DFR) issued guidance clarifying that Vermont-domiciled captives must maintain "adequate financial resources" to meet their obligations, and that excessive reliance on parental guarantees—even for pre-AG48 grandfathered captives—could result in Vermont requiring additional collateral or capital injections.94 While Vermont as captive domicile regulator generally defers to ceding insurers' domiciliary state regulators on reserve credit determinations, Vermont's guidance signaled increasing regulatory scrutiny of undercapitalized captives.95

**Conclusion:** LLIC's modified coinsurance treaty with Swiss Re (covering $1.6 billion in universal life reserves) and yearly renewable term excess treaty with Munich Re (covering group life claims exceeding $250,000 per life) present **LOW** counterparty credit risk. **Rule:** Under Nebraska's implementation of the NAIC Credit for Reinsurance Model Law, ceding insurers may claim full statutory reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to authorized reinsurers without requiring collateral, provided the authorized reinsurer maintains minimum financial standards established by regulation.146 Nebraska Regulation 56 requires authorized reinsurers to maintain: (a) minimum surplus of $50 million, (b) financial strength rating of B+ or better from A.M. Best or equivalent rating from S&P or Moody's, and (c) compliance with ongoing quarterly financial reporting requirements.147

Swiss Re and Munich Re, as authorized reinsurers domiciled in Switzerland and Germany respectively but licensed to transact reinsurance business in Nebraska, satisfy these requirements with substantial surplus levels ($40+ billion for Swiss Re, $35+ billion for Munich Re) and Superior financial strength ratings (AA- or better).148 The authorized reinsurer framework reflects regulatory confidence that large, highly-rated reinsurers pose minimal counterparty credit risk and that requiring collateral would be unnecessary and economically inefficient.149

However, authorized reinsurer status is not perpetual or unconditional. If an authorized reinsurer's financial condition deteriorates materially—measured by declining surplus, weakening RBC ratio, or rating downgrades—the Nebraska DOI may: (a) require the reinsurer to post collateral for existing reinsurance recoverables, (b) suspend the reinsurer's authorization to accept new reinsurance from Nebraska-domiciled insurers, or (c) in extreme cases, require ceding insurers to recapture reserves or establish Schedule F provisions.150 The trigger for such action is typically a downgrade below investment grade (BBB-) or a regulatory intervention by the reinsurer's domiciliary regulator.151

For Swiss Re's modified coinsurance treaty, the treaty structure provides additional protection to LLIC: under modified coinsurance (mod-co), the ceding insurer retains legal ownership of the investment assets backing the ceded reserves, with the reinsurer assuming only the mortality and expense risk.152 If Swiss Re were to default, LLIC would still hold the $1.6 billion in investment assets (bonds, mortgages, etc.) supporting the universal life policies, limiting LLIC's loss to the difference between actual reserve requirements and the value of retained assets.153 This contrasts with traditional coinsurance (used in the Global Re treaty) where the reinsurer assumes both reserves and assets, creating full counterparty exposure if the reinsurer defaults.154

**Explanation:** Swiss Reinsurance Company Ltd. (Swiss Re) is one of the world's largest and most financially stable reinsurers, with total assets exceeding $200 billion, shareholder equity exceeding $40 billion, and a diversified global business spanning life, property-casualty, and specialty reinsurance.155 Swiss Re maintains AA- financial strength ratings from A.M. Best and S&P Global, reflecting "superior" ability to meet ongoing insurance obligations and "very strong" capitalization.156

A.M. Best's rating methodology for reinsurers evaluates five key factors: (1) balance sheet strength (capitalization, reserve adequacy, asset quality), (2) operating performance (underwriting profitability, return on equity, earnings stability), (3) business profile (market position, diversification, competitive advantages), (4) enterprise risk management (risk controls, governance, catastrophe modeling), and (5) financial flexibility (access to capital markets, liquidity, dividend capacity).157 Swiss Re scores favorably on all five factors, with particular strength in balance sheet and business profile categories.158

Historical default rates for AA-rated reinsurers are exceptionally low. A.M. Best's "Impairment Review" covering 1977-2023 data shows that fewer than 1% of AA-rated reinsurers experienced impairment (defined as regulatory intervention, missed claim payments, or downgrade to non-investment grade) within a five-year period.159 The primary causes of reinsurer impairment historically have been: (a) catastrophic losses exceeding reserve and capital capacity (e.g., Hurricane Katrina in 2005, COVID-19 pandemic in 2020), (b) severe investment losses (e.g., 2008 financial crisis mortgage-backed securities writedowns), (c) reserve strengthening for long-tail liabilities (e.g., asbestos and environmental claims), or (d) fraud or management misconduct.160

Swiss Re's exposure to these impairment drivers appears limited: (1) Swiss Re maintains robust catastrophe reinsurance reserves and retrocessional protection (reinsurance of reinsurance) limiting net retention on any single catastrophic event to less than $5 billion, (2) Swiss Re's investment portfolio is heavily weighted toward investment-grade sovereign and corporate bonds with minimal exposure to distressed assets, (3) Swiss Re's life reinsurance reserves are calculated using conservative actuarial assumptions and subject to annual independent actuarial review, and (4) Swiss Re's corporate governance includes rigorous internal controls, external audit by PricewaterhouseCoopers, and regulatory oversight by the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA).161

Munich Re presents an even stronger credit profile than Swiss Re, with AA financial strength ratings (one notch higher than Swiss Re's AA-) and similar scale ($250+ billion in total assets, $35+ billion in shareholder equity).162 Munich Re's historical performance includes continuous profitability since 1970 (with exception of 2001 due to 9/11 terrorism losses and 2002 equity market declines), making it one of the most stable reinsurers globally.163

**Application:** LLIC's modified coinsurance treaty with Swiss Re cedes 50% of indexed universal life (IUL) policy reserves issued between 2018 and 2024, totaling approximately $1.6 billion in ceded reserves as of Q3 2024.164 Under the mod-co structure, LLIC retains legal ownership of the investment assets backing these reserves (estimated $1.6 billion in bonds, mortgages, and other fixed-income securities), with Swiss Re assuming only the mortality risk (death claims), lapse risk (policyholder behavior), and expense risk (policy administration costs).165

Each quarter, LLIC calculates the required statutory reserves for the IUL policies using Nebraska's standard valuation law and actuarial guidelines, then: (1) reports the reserve amount to Swiss Re, (2) Swiss Re credits LLIC with a "reinsurance allowance" (effectively a payment from Swiss Re to LLIC) equal to the change in ceded reserves, and (3) LLIC invests the reinsurance allowance in its general account portfolio, with investment returns flowing to LLIC rather than Swiss Re.166 This structure provides three layers of protection to LLIC:

**Layer 1—Asset Retention:** LLIC retains full legal ownership of $1.6 billion in investment assets, so Swiss Re default would not cause LLIC to lose these assets.167

**Layer 2—Reserve Calculation Continuity:** Even if Swiss Re defaulted, LLIC would continue holding reserves calculated under Nebraska statutory requirements; the only impact would be loss of Swiss Re's reinsurance allowance (effectively, LLIC would need to fund future reserve increases from its own capital rather than receiving reinsurance credits from Swiss Re).168

**Layer 3—Experience Refund Offset:** The treaty includes an experience refund provision: if actual mortality experience on the ceded IUL block is favorable (fewer death claims than expected), Swiss Re refunds a portion of underwriting profit to LLIC; if experience is unfavorable, Swiss Re absorbs the excess losses without charging LLIC.169 This asymmetric risk-sharing reduces LLIC's downside exposure.170

#### B.4 Aggregate Reinsurance Concentration and Cumulative Stress Scenario Risk

**Conclusion:** When analyzed individually, LLIC's four reinsurance treaties present a manageable risk profile: Global Re poses HIGH risk ($76.5M-$114.8M weighted exposure), Liberty Re VT poses CRITICAL risk ($73M-$109.5M weighted exposure but potentially deal-blocking if full recapture required), and Swiss Re and Munich Re pose LOW risk ($8M-$36M combined). **Rule:** While insurance regulations evaluate reinsurance counterparty risk on a treaty-by-treaty basis, rating agencies and sophisticated investors increasingly scrutinize aggregate reinsurance concentration risk—the potential for multiple reinsurance exposures to materialize simultaneously during periods of economic or regulatory stress.184 The NAIC Financial Analysis Handbook recommends that state regulators calculate a "reinsurance concentration ratio" by dividing total reinsurance recoverables by statutory surplus; ratios exceeding 100% indicate that a ceding insurer's solvency is substantially dependent on reinsurer performance, and ratios exceeding 200% indicate excessive concentration risk.185

Courts have recognized that seemingly independent reinsurance counterparty risks may be correlated during systemic stress events. In *In re Midland Insurance Co.*, the Texas Court of Appeals noted that the ceding insurer's reliance on four separate reinsurers (none of which individually posed material default risk) became problematic when economic downturn simultaneously impaired all four reinsurers' investment portfolios, forcing the ceding insurer to establish Schedule F provisions for all four recoverables in the same quarter—creating a $400 million surplus reduction that triggered regulatory seizure.186 The court held that the Insurance Commissioner properly considered aggregate reinsurance concentration, not merely individual treaty risks, in determining that the insurer posed a hazard to policyholders.187

**Explanation:** LLIC's reinsurance program as of Q3 2024 includes approximately $3.3 billion in total reinsurance recoverables across four treaties:188

- Global Reassurance Ltd. (quota share term life): $850 million
- Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC (AXXX/XXX captive): $850 million (net $730M after captive assets)
- Swiss Re (modified coinsurance IUL): $1.6 billion
- Munich Re (YRT excess group life): Minimal balance (<$10 million)

LLIC's reinsurance concentration ratio is approximately 178% ($3.3B recoverables ÷ $1.85B surplus), exceeding the NAIC's 100% guideline and approaching the 200% excessive concentration threshold.189 This ratio indicates that LLIC's solvency is heavily dependent on all four reinsurers performing as expected—if multiple reinsurers simultaneously experience stress, LLIC's statutory surplus could be rapidly depleted.190

Correlation analysis requires identifying common risk factors that could cause multiple reinsurers to fail simultaneously:

**Common Risk Factor 1—Macroeconomic Recession:** A severe recession (similar to 2008-2009 or worse) would impact multiple reinsurers through: (a) investment portfolio losses (corporate bond defaults, commercial mortgage foreclosures, equity declines), (b) reserve strengthening (if low interest rates force reinsurers to increase reserves for long-term guarantees), and (c) rating downgrades (rating agencies downgrade reinsurers experiencing capital depletion).191 Global Re, Swiss Re, and Munich Re all hold substantial investment portfolios and could experience simultaneous downgrades if recession severity exceeds their stress test assumptions.192

**Common Risk Factor 2—Regulatory Tightening:** If state insurance regulators collectively tighten standards for reinsurance collateral (e.g., NAIC adopts more stringent revisions to AG48 or Credit for Reinsurance Model Law), both Global Re's LOC and Liberty Re VT's parental guarantee could face simultaneous challenges—Global Re might be required to increase LOC collateral from 100% to 120% of recoverables, while Liberty Re VT might lose grandfather status and be required to comply with AG48 Primary Security standards.193

**Common Risk Factor 3—Life Insurance Industry Mortality Shock:** A pandemic significantly more severe than COVID-19 (e.g., mortality rates increasing by 50-100% above baseline for 2-3 years) would impact Global Re (quota share term life), Swiss Re (IUL mortality risk), and Munich Re (group life excess claims) simultaneously, causing all three to experience reserve strengthening and potential capital depletion.194

The specialist report estimates 5-10% probability of the combined stress scenario (not 10% × 15% × 2.5% = 0.375%, because the events are positively correlated—recession increases probability of all three simultaneously).201 Using 7.5% midpoint probability:

- 7.5% × $1.145B surplus reduction = **$85.9M weighted exposure for combined scenario**

This $85.9M weighted exposure for the combined stress scenario is incremental to the individual treaty exposures calculated in sections B.1-B.3 above (which total $157.5M to $260.3M). However, to avoid double-counting, the total aggregate exposure should not simply sum individual exposures plus combined scenario—instead, the combined scenario represents the tail risk beyond individual treaty risks.202

- **Classification:** Hybrid (combines one-time events with potential multi-year impacts)
- **Methodology:** Tail Risk Analysis (focus on low-probability, high-severity combined scenario)
- **Calculation:**
  - Combined stress scenario: 7.5% × $1.145B = $85.9M
  - Alternatively, using probability distribution: P90 (90th percentile worst-case) approximately $600M surplus impact at 10% probability = $60M weighted
  - **Result: $60M to $85.9M incremental tail risk** beyond individual treaty exposures
- **Discount Rate Basis:** Not applicable (one-time event within 5-year horizon)

**Counter-Analysis:** The acquirer and seller may argue that the combined stress scenario probability of 5-10% is overstated because it assumes perfect positive correlation between Global Re LOC non-renewal, Liberty Re VT recapture, and Swiss Re downgrade—but these events may be partially independent. For example, Nebraska DOI's decision on Liberty Re VT is driven primarily by regulatory policy (AG48 application, parental guarantee enforceability) rather than macroeconomic conditions, so a recession that triggers Global Re and Swiss Re stress may not necessarily increase Liberty Re VT recapture probability.203

This counter-analysis has merit: the three events are not perfectly correlated (correlation coefficient likely 0.3-0.5 rather than 1.0), suggesting that simple multiplication of individual probabilities (10% × 15% × 2.5% = 0.0375% or 3.75 basis points) understates the risk, but assuming 100% correlation (5-10% combined probability) may overstate it.204 A more sophisticated approach would use copula modeling or Monte Carlo simulation to estimate the joint probability distribution, likely yielding a combined scenario probability of 2-5% rather than 5-10%.205

However, even at 2-5% combined probability, the severity of the outcome (RBC falling to 72-87%, triggering mandatory Nebraska DOI seizure) justifies treating this as a material risk requiring mitigation. The acquirer cannot prudently proceed with a transaction that has even a 2-5% probability of regulatory seizure within the first five years post-closing.206

**Mitigation for Aggregate Concentration Risk:**
1. **Implement Global Re LOC Renewal Confirmation:** Obtain written commitment from Barclays (or arrange replacement LOC) for 5-year renewal through 2030, reducing Global Re risk from 10-15% to <5%.207
2. **Implement Liberty Re VT Strengthening:** Arrange $300M-$500M LOC or capital injection into captive pre-closing, reducing recapture risk from 10-15% to <5%.208
3. **Diversify Reinsurance Counterparties:** Post-closing, solicit proposals from alternative reinsurers (Hannover Re, SCOR, RGA) for new business production, reducing concentration with Global Re and Swiss Re over time.209
4. **Establish Reinsurance Monitoring Framework:** Quarterly Board-level reporting on aggregate reinsurance recoverables, collateral adequacy, rating changes, and stress scenario modeling (recession, pandemic, regulatory tightening).210

With these mitigations implemented, the combined stress scenario probability declines from 5-10% to approximately 1-2%, and even if the scenario occurs, the severity is reduced (e.g., if Global Re and Liberty Re VT both have backstop LOCs, the surplus reduction is limited to Swiss Re Schedule F provision of $160M, yielding post-stress RBC of 173% rather than 72%).211
