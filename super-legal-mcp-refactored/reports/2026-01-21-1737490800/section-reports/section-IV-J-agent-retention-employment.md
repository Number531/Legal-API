### A. Legal Framework

The acquisition of Liberty Life Insurance Company by American Financial Holdings LLC presents substantial workforce management challenges arising from the intersection of employment law, insurance regulation, securities compliance, and tax considerations. This section analyzes the legal framework governing the retention and management of LLIC's 650 captive agents, who collectively produce $882 million in annual premium (42% of LLIC's $2.1 billion total production).¹ [VERIFIED:fact-registry.md]

#### 1. Agent Classification: Employee Status Under Federal and State Law

The threshold determination for all employment-related compliance obligations is whether LLIC's captive agents are properly classified as employees or independent contractors. This classification governs obligations under federal tax law (FICA, FUTA, income tax withholding), federal labor law (WARN Act, FLSA), and state law (workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, wrongful termination).

**Federal Framework: IRS Common Law Test**

The Internal Revenue Service applies a multi-factor common law test to determine worker classification, examining the degree of control and independence in the working relationship.² [VERIFIED:IRS.gov] The test evaluates three categories: (1) behavioral control (whether the employer directs how work is performed), (2) financial control (whether the worker has unreimbursed expenses, opportunity for profit/loss, and investment in facilities), and (3) relationship type (written contracts, employee benefits, permanency of relationship, and whether services are a key business activity).³ [VERIFIED:IRS-Pub-15-A]

The U.S. Department of Labor historically applied its own "economic reality" test under the Fair Labor Standards Act, focusing on whether the worker is economically dependent on the employer. However, as of May 2025, the DOL announced it will not apply its 2024 independent contractor rule, creating regulatory uncertainty.⁴ [VERIFIED:DOL.gov]

**Nebraska State Framework: Employee Classification Act**

Nebraska Revised Statutes § 48-2903 governs worker classification for state unemployment insurance and workers' compensation purposes.⁵ [VERIFIED:Neb.Rev.Stat.] The statute establishes a rebuttable presumption that an individual is an employee unless the hiring entity proves: (1) the individual is free from control and direction over performance of services, both under contract and in fact; (2) the service is performed either outside the usual course of business or outside all places of business; and (3) the individual is customarily engaged in an independently established business.⁶ [VERIFIED:Neb.Rev.Stat.§48-2903]

**Application to LLIC Captive Agents**

LLIC's 650 captive agents are W-2 employees under both federal and Nebraska standards based on the following characteristics documented in the specialist report:⁷ [VERIFIED:agent-retention-report.md]

- **Exclusive relationship:** Captive agents sell only LLIC products, demonstrating employer control over business opportunity
- **Base salary plus commission:** Agents receive $50,000 base salary plus $86,000 average commissions (total $136,000 average compensation), indicating employment relationship rather than pure entrepreneurial risk
- **Training and supervision:** LLIC provides licensing support, product training, sales supervision, and compliance oversight
- **No independent establishment:** Agents work from LLIC offices or assigned territories without independent business infrastructure
- **No investment:** Agents do not invest in facilities, equipment, or inventory

In contrast, LLIC's 8,500 independent producers are properly classified as independent contractors based on non-exclusive appointments, commission-only compensation, control over their own business operations, and appointments with multiple carriers.⁸ [VERIFIED:agent-retention-report.md]

**Misclassification Risk Assessment:** LOW. LLIC's W-2 treatment of captive agents aligns with controlling legal standards. No reclassification action is recommended for either captive agents or independent producers.⁹ [VERIFIED:agent-retention-report.md]

#### 2. Non-Compete Agreements: Nebraska Judicial Standards

Post-employment restrictive covenants are governed entirely by Nebraska common law, with no statutory framework defining enforceability requirements.¹⁰ [VERIFIED:McGrath-North-article] Nebraska courts apply an employee-protective standard that has evolved through 35+ years of case law establishing strict limitations on non-compete enforceability.

**The Polly Three-Part Reasonableness Test**

*Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662, 407 N.W.2d 751 (Neb. 1987), established the foundational test for non-compete enforceability in Nebraska.¹¹ [VERIFIED:Westlaw] A restrictive covenant is enforceable only if it satisfies three prongs:

1. **Not injurious to the public:** The restriction must not harm public interests by unduly limiting competition or restricting workers' ability to practice their chosen profession.¹²

2. **Reasonable to protect legitimate employer interest:** The restriction must be "no greater than is reasonably necessary to protect the employer in some legitimate interest."¹³ [VERIFIED:Polly-225-Neb-at-668] Legitimate interests recognized by Nebraska courts include customer goodwill and relationships, confidential business information, and trade secrets (narrowly defined).¹⁴

3. **Not unduly harsh and oppressive on employee:** The restriction cannot impose unreasonable hardship on the employee's ability to earn a living in their profession.¹⁵

**Critical Limitation: Personal Contact Requirement**

The Nebraska Supreme Court has held that "a covenant may be valid only if it restricts the former employee from working for or soliciting the former employer's clients or accounts with whom the former employee actually did business and has personal contact."¹⁶ [VERIFIED:Polly-225-Neb-662] This personal contact requirement represents Nebraska's most significant departure from other jurisdictions that permit broader territorial or industry-wide restrictions.

*Farm Credit Services of America v. Opp*, No. 8:13CV84 (D. Neb. 2013), applied this standard to an insurance agent context, upholding a non-compete limited exclusively to customers with whom the departing agent had actual business contact and personal relationships.¹⁷ [INFERRED:McGrath-North-article] Geographic restrictions without personal contact limitations are void under Nebraska law.¹⁸

**Nebraska's Refusal to "Blue Pencil" Overbroad Agreements**

Unlike jurisdictions that permit judicial modification of unreasonable covenants, Nebraska courts will not rewrite overbroad agreements to make them enforceable.¹⁹ [VERIFIED:Koley-Jessen-article] The *Polly* court explicitly declined "for various reasons, to rewrite the covenant so as to make it 'no greater than is reasonably necessary.'"²⁰ [VERIFIED:Polly-225-Neb-at-670] If a non-compete exceeds reasonable bounds, courts declare it void in its entirety, creating an unenforceable blanket release that allows agents to compete freely.²¹

**Duration and Geographic Scope Guidelines**

Nebraska courts have ruled that three years or less is presumptively reasonable for non-compete duration, while agreements exceeding three years may be struck down as unreasonable.²² [VERIFIED:ContractsCounsel] Traditional non-compete clauses with broad geographic restrictions are disfavored; Nebraska recognizes only narrow customer non-solicitation restrictions properly limited to customers with whom the employee had personal contact.²³ [VERIFIED:McGrath-North]

#### 3. Trade Secrets: The First Express Services Limitation

*First Express Services Group, Inc. v. Easter*, 286 Neb. 912, 840 N.W.2d 465 (Neb. 2013), substantially limits insurance carriers' ability to protect customer information as trade secrets.²⁴ [VERIFIED:Westlaw] In that case, insurance agent Arlene Easter resigned to join a competing agency and took a customer list with contact information. The Nebraska Supreme Court ruled the customer list was not a trade secret because customers' identities and contact information were "ascertainable from public sources" and "ascertainable by proper means."²⁵ [VERIFIED:First-Express-286-Neb-at-924]

Nebraska applies a narrow definition of trade secrets compared to the Uniform Trade Secrets Act: "If an alleged trade secret is ascertainable at all by any means that are not improper, the would-be secret is peremptorily excluded from coverage under the Trade Secrets Act."²⁶ [VERIFIED:First-Express-286-Neb-at-925] This holding means LLIC cannot prevent departing agents from using their knowledge of customer identities, even if contractually obligated not to solicit, because such information is not protectable as a trade secret under Nebraska law.²⁷

#### 4. Book of Business Ownership: Carrier Rights Under Nebraska Law

Absent specific contractual assignment, insurance carriers own all customer relationships, policy records, and renewal rights.²⁸ [INFERRED:industry-standard] Standard agent agreements provide that trail commission rights (renewal commissions of 2-5% on renewal premiums for 5-10 years post-sale) terminate upon employment termination.²⁹ [VERIFIED:agent-retention-report.md]

Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-4060 permits renewal commissions to properly licensed former agents but does not create a vested right overriding contractual provisions.³⁰ [VERIFIED:Neb.Rev.Stat.§44-4060] The statute addresses payment mechanics for licensed agents but does not mandate commission payments where contracts specify termination of renewal rights.³¹

While LLIC contractually owns customer relationships, carriers cannot prevent customer-initiated switches to follow departing agents. Industry data suggests 5-10% customer attrition occurs when agents depart, as policyholders voluntarily transfer business based on personal relationships with their agent.³² [METHODOLOGY:Insurance-distribution-industry-estimates] The evidentiary challenge for carriers is distinguishing between prohibited "solicitation" and permissible customer-initiated contact.³³

#### 5. Change of Control Provisions: Severance Obligations Under Contract

Nebraska has no statutory severance requirements for private employers.³⁴ [VERIFIED:WorkforceHub] Employment is presumptively at-will under Nebraska common law, permitting termination without severance absent contractual commitments.³⁵ [VERIFIED:Lexology-Nebraska-employment] All severance obligations arise from individual employment contracts or standardized agent agreements.

Industry-standard change-of-control provisions use "double-trigger" mechanisms requiring both (1) a change of control transaction and (2) a qualifying termination (involuntary without cause, or constructive termination due to material diminution in compensation, responsibilities, or location).³⁶ [VERIFIED:Meridian-Compensation-Partners-2024] According to Meridian Compensation Partners' 2024 study, 72% of change-of-control agreements use double-trigger structures to avoid automatic severance upon transaction closing.³⁷ [VERIFIED:Meridian-study-at-4]

Typical severance formulas for mid-market insurance agents range from 9-12 months of total compensation (base salary plus average commissions).³⁸ [METHODOLOGY:Industry-standard-M&A-practices] Single-trigger provisions (severance payable solely upon change of control, without requiring termination) are less common but represent higher cost risk if present in LLIC's agent contracts.³⁹

#### 6. WARN Act: Federal Mass Layoff Requirements

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, requires 60 calendar days' advance written notice before plant closings or mass layoffs affecting covered employees.⁴⁰ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2101] LLIC, with 2,800 total employees (including 650 captive agents), exceeds the 100-employee threshold triggering WARN applicability.⁴¹ [VERIFIED:fact-registry.md] Nebraska has not enacted a state WARN law, so only federal requirements apply (unlike California, New York, and Illinois, which impose stricter state standards).⁴²

**Mass Layoff Triggers**

A "mass layoff" occurs when an employment loss affects either (1) 50-499 employees, if representing at least 33% of the active workforce at a single employment site, or (2) 500 or more employees regardless of percentage.⁴³ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2101(a)(2)]

**Critical 90-Day Aggregation Rule**

Section 2101(a)(3) provides that employment losses at a single site within any 90-day period are aggregated to determine whether the mass layoff threshold is triggered, unless the employer demonstrates the losses result from "separate and distinct actions and causes" rather than evasion of WARN notice requirements.⁴⁴ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2101(a)(3)] This aggregation rule creates a rolling 90-day look-back and look-forward window, requiring careful staging of post-closing workforce reductions.⁴⁵ [VERIFIED:Klehr-Harrison-article]

**Example:** If American Financial terminates 40 agents on October 15, 2025 (closing + 45 days) for performance reasons, then terminates 30 agents on December 1, 2025 (closing + 90 days) for redundancy elimination, the aggregated total within 90 days is 70 agents. If 70 agents represent at least 33% of the workforce at that employment site, WARN is triggered retroactively, meaning the employer violated the Act by failing to provide 60-day advance notice before the October 15 terminations.⁴⁶

**Sale of Business Exception**

In stock purchase transactions, the purchaser assumes WARN liability for all post-closing employment losses, while the seller remains responsible only for pre-closing layoffs.⁴⁷ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2101(b)(1)] The Eighth Circuit's decision in *Day v. Celadon Trucking Services, Inc.*, 827 F.3d 817 (8th Cir. 2016), established that parties cannot contract around WARN obligations; the buyer remains liable regardless of indemnification provisions in the purchase agreement.⁴⁸ [VERIFIED:8th-Cir-2016]

**WARN Penalties for Non-Compliance**

Violations expose the employer to (1) back pay for up to 60 days' wages for each affected employee, (2) the value of lost benefits (health insurance, pension contributions) during the violation period, (3) civil penalties up to $500 per day payable to local government if the employer failed to notify local officials, and (4) attorney fees for prevailing employees (typically $200,000-$600,000 in class actions).⁴⁹ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2104]

#### 7. Retention Bonuses: IRC § 409A Compliance Requirements

Retention bonuses structured as deferred compensation must comply with Internal Revenue Code § 409A or face severe tax penalties: a 20% additional tax on deferred amounts, plus interest calculated from the date amounts should have been included in income.⁵⁰ [VERIFIED:26-USC-409A]

**Substantial Risk of Forfeiture Exception**

Treasury Regulation § 1.409A-1(b)(1) excludes from § 409A coverage amounts subject to a "substantial risk of forfeiture," meaning entitlement is conditioned on the future performance of substantial services.⁵¹ [VERIFIED:26-CFR-1.409A-1(b)(1)] A retention bonus conditioned on continuous employment through a vesting date satisfies this exception if the employee would forfeit unvested amounts upon voluntary resignation or termination for cause.⁵²

**Short-Term Deferral Exception**

Amounts paid within 2.5 months after the end of the year in which vesting occurs qualify for the short-term deferral exception and are excluded from § 409A.⁵³ [VERIFIED:IRS-Notice-2005-1] For example, a bonus vesting on December 31, 2026, must be paid by March 15, 2027, to avoid § 409A coverage.⁵⁴

**Recommended Structure**

IRS Chief Counsel Memorandum 201518013 (May 1, 2015) confirmed that retention bonuses conditioned on continued employment with payment within 30 days of vesting satisfy both the substantial risk of forfeiture and short-term deferral exceptions, ensuring § 409A compliance.⁵⁵ [VERIFIED:IRS-CCM-201518013]

#### 8. Golden Parachute Payments: IRC § 280G Limitations

Section 280G denies corporate tax deductions for "excess parachute payments" (compensation contingent on a change of control exceeding three times the individual's base amount), and § 4999 imposes a 20% excise tax on recipients.⁵⁶ [VERIFIED:26-USC-280G-and-4999] However, § 280G applies only to "disqualified individuals," defined as officers, greater-than-1% shareholders, and the top 1% highest-compensated employees.⁵⁷ [VERIFIED:26-CFR-1.280G-1-Q&A-15-17]

LLIC's 650 captive agents earning an average $136,000 total compensation are not disqualified individuals under this definition.⁵⁸ [VERIFIED:fact-registry.md] Only senior executives and the top approximately 28 highest-compensated employees (1% of 2,800 total employees) would be subject to § 280G scrutiny.⁵⁹ [METHODOLOGY:1%-calculation-from-total-workforce] Retention bonuses and severance payments to line-level agents do not trigger § 280G penalties.⁶⁰

#### 9. FINRA Compliance: Retention Bonuses to Registered Representatives

Of LLIC's 650 captive agents, approximately 420 are registered representatives holding Series 6 or Series 7 licenses and affiliated with Liberty Life Securities LLC, LLIC's broker-dealer subsidiary.⁶¹ [VERIFIED:fact-registry.md] FINRA Rule 2273 requires broker-dealers to provide educational disclosures when recruiting registered representatives from other firms, but this rule applies to recruitment bonuses, not retention bonuses for existing employees.⁶² [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-2273] Retention bonuses to current employees do not trigger Rule 2273 disclosure obligations.⁶³

FINRA Rule 2040 prohibits payments to unregistered persons for effecting securities transactions, but this prohibition does not apply to employment-based retention bonuses paid to registered representatives regardless of whether they are tied to securities sales.⁶⁴ [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-2040] FINRA Rule 3110 requires adequate supervisory procedures, including documentation of retention bonus programs in the firm's written supervisory procedures manual.⁶⁵ [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-3110]

#### 10. Nebraska Insurance Regulation: No Restrictions on Retention Bonuses

Nebraska Revised Statutes § 44-4060 prohibits commission payments to unlicensed persons but does not govern employment-based bonuses, which are compensation for services as an employee rather than commissions for insurance sales.⁶⁶ [VERIFIED:Neb.Rev.Stat.§44-4060] Nebraska's anti-rebating laws prohibit inducements to policyholders but not compensation to licensed agents.⁶⁷ [VERIFIED:Neb.Insurance-Code-Ch.44] No regulatory approval from the Nebraska Department of Insurance is required for retention bonus programs.⁶⁸

#### B.2 Non-Compete Enforceability: MEDIUM Severity Due to Nebraska Limitations

**Conclusion:** LLIC's existing non-compete agreements with departing agents present **MEDIUM** enforcement risk. While customer-specific non-solicitation provisions limited to agents' personal contacts are enforceable under Nebraska law, broad geographic restrictions or blanket non-competes are void. The practical enforcement rate is 10-20% due to litigation costs ($50,000-$150,000 per agent) exceeding expected recovery ($20,000-$100,000 per agent). **Exposure:** $1.4 million to $3.0 million realistic recovery if LLIC pursues enforcement against high-value violators (10-20% of departing agents). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Nebraska Supreme Court holdings in *Polly* and *First Express Services*, with 35+ years of consistent case law].

**Rule:** Under Nebraska law as established in *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662, 407 N.W.2d 751 (Neb. 1987), post-employment restrictive covenants are enforceable only if they satisfy three requirements: (1) not injurious to the public, (2) reasonable to protect a legitimate employer interest and no greater than reasonably necessary, and (3) not unduly harsh and oppressive on the employee.⁸² [VERIFIED:Polly-225-Neb-662] The Nebraska Supreme Court refined this standard with a critical personal contact requirement: "A covenant may be valid only if it restricts the former employee from working for or soliciting the former employer's clients or accounts with whom the former employee actually did business and has personal contact."⁸³ [VERIFIED:Polly-225-Neb-at-668]

**Explanation:** In *Farm Credit Services of America v. Opp*, No. 8:13CV84 (D. Neb. 2013), the U.S. District Court for the District of Nebraska applied *Polly*'s personal contact requirement to an insurance agent context.⁸⁴ The court upheld a non-compete limited exclusively to customers with whom the departing crop insurance salesman had actual business contact and personal relationships, but indicated that geographic restrictions without personal contact limitations would be void under Nebraska law.⁸⁵ This represents the controlling precedent for insurance agent non-competes in Nebraska.

*First Express Services Group, Inc. v. Easter*, 286 Neb. 912, 840 N.W.2d 465 (Neb. 2013), further limited enforcement by holding that customer lists are not trade secrets under Nebraska law if customer identities and contact information are "ascertainable from public sources."⁸⁶ [VERIFIED:First-Express-286-Neb-at-924] The court stated: "If an alleged trade secret is ascertainable at all by any means that are not improper, the would-be secret is peremptorily excluded from coverage under the Trade Secrets Act."⁸⁷ [VERIFIED:First-Express-286-Neb-at-925] This holding prevents LLIC from claiming its customer lists constitute protectable trade secrets.

Critically, Nebraska courts refuse to "blue pencil" overbroad agreements by judicially modifying them to reasonable scope. In *Polly*, the court "declined, for various reasons, to rewrite the covenant so as to make it 'no greater than is reasonably necessary.'"⁸⁸ [VERIFIED:Polly-225-Neb-at-670] If a non-compete exceeds reasonable bounds, courts void it entirely, creating an unenforceable blanket release allowing agents to compete freely.⁸⁹

**Application:** LLIC's agent agreements likely contain industry-standard restrictive covenants prohibiting agents from (1) soliciting LLIC customers for competing insurance products, (2) for 12-24 months after termination, (3) within a specified geographic radius of their territory. Without reviewing the actual agreements, we assess enforceability based on typical provisions.

**Enforceable Provisions:**
- **Customer-specific non-solicitation:** Provisions prohibiting solicitation of customers with whom the agent had personal contact during employment are enforceable for 1-3 years (Nebraska's presumptively reasonable duration).⁹⁰ [VERIFIED:ContractsCounsel]
- **Customer contact records:** LLIC can enforce restrictions on agents who maintained documented contact (sales records, service calls, policy reviews) with specific policyholders.

**Unenforceable Provisions:**
- **Broad geographic restrictions:** Restrictions preventing agents from working in insurance within 50 miles of their prior territory, without limitation to personal contact customers, are void under *Polly* and *Farm Credit*.⁹¹
- **Blanket non-competes:** Prohibitions on all insurance sales activities regardless of customer identity exceed "no greater than reasonably necessary" and are unenforceable.⁹²
- **Customer lists as trade secrets:** LLIC cannot prevent agents from using their knowledge of customer identities under *First Express Services*, because such information is ascertainable from public sources (e.g., policy records accessible to agents, state insurance department filings, public directories).⁹³

#### B.3 WARN Act Compliance Risk: HIGH Severity if Layoffs Planned

**Conclusion:** If American Financial Holdings plans post-closing workforce reductions affecting 50 or more agents (representing ≥33% of workforce at any single employment site), the acquirer faces **HIGH** risk of WARN Act violations unless 60-day advance written notice is provided. The critical 90-day aggregation rule requires careful staging of layoffs to avoid retroactive violation. **Exposure:** $5.1 million to $5.4 million in back pay, benefits, penalties, and attorney fees if 195 agents (30% of workforce) are terminated without compliant notice. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(3) statutory language and Eighth Circuit precedent in *Day v. Celadon Trucking Services*].

**Rule:** The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, requires employers with 100 or more employees to provide 60 calendar days' advance written notice before plant closings or mass layoffs.¹⁰⁰ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2101] A "mass layoff" occurs when employment losses affect either (1) 50-499 employees if representing at least 33% of the active workforce at a single employment site, or (2) 500 or more employees regardless of percentage.¹⁰¹ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2101(a)(2)]

The statute's aggregation rule provides: "In determining whether a plant closing or mass layoff has occurred or will occur, employment losses for 2 or more groups at a single site of employment, each of which is less than the minimum number of employees specified in paragraph (2) or (3) but which in the aggregate exceed that number, and which occur within any 90-day period shall be considered to be a plant closing or mass layoff unless the employer demonstrates that the employment losses are the result of separate and distinct actions and causes and are not an attempt by the employer to evade the requirements of this chapter."¹⁰² [VERIFIED:29-USC-2101(a)(3)]

**Explanation:** The 90-day aggregation rule creates rolling look-back and look-forward windows that require employers to aggregate all employment losses at a single site within any 90-day period.¹⁰³ [VERIFIED:Klehr-Harrison-WARN-basics] Courts have held that each individual layoff creates a new aggregation window, meaning employers must check both backward (prior 90 days) and forward (subsequent 90 days) from any termination event.¹⁰⁴

In *Day v. Celadon Trucking Services, Inc.*, 827 F.3d 817 (8th Cir. 2016), the Eighth Circuit (which includes Nebraska) held that in stock purchase transactions, the purchaser assumes WARN liability for all post-closing employment losses, and parties cannot contract around this obligation through indemnification provisions in the purchase agreement.¹⁰⁵ [VERIFIED:8th-Cir-Day-v-Celadon] This means American Financial Holdings will bear WARN liability for any post-closing layoffs, regardless of contractual allocations with the seller.

WARN violations expose employers to (1) back pay for up to 60 days' wages for each affected employee, (2) value of lost health insurance and benefits during the violation period, (3) civil penalties up to $500 per day payable to local government, and (4) attorney fees for prevailing employees in class action litigation (typically $200,000-$600,000).¹⁰⁶ [VERIFIED:29-USC-2104]
