## II. DELAWARE M&A PRECEDENT - MATERIAL ADVERSE EFFECT ANALYSIS

### A. Legal Framework and Seminal Cases

#### 1. *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, 2018 WL 4719347 (Del. Ch. Oct. 1, 2018)

**Citation:** *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, C.A. No. 2018-0300-JTL, 2018 WL 4719347 (Del. Ch. Oct. 1, 2018) (Laster, V.C.), *aff'd*, 198 A.3d 724 (Del. 2018).

**Procedural Posture:** Post-trial decision following Fresenius's termination of $4.75 billion merger agreement.

**Facts:**
- April 2017: Fresenius Kabi AG agreed to acquire Akorn, Inc. for $4.75 billion ($34 per share)
- Second quarter 2017: Akorn's business performance "fell off a cliff"
- Year-over-year revenue decreased 25%
- Year-over-year operating income dropped 105%
- Year-over-year earnings per share dropped 113%
- EBITDA fell by 86%
- Analyst valuations dropped from approximately $32 per share to a range of $5 to $12 per share
- Akorn attributed decline to loss of a key contract and unexpected competition
- April 22, 2018: Fresenius delivered termination notice

**Holdings:**
This was the **first time** a Delaware court held that a seller had suffered an MAE, entitling the buyer to terminate an acquisition transaction. Vice Chancellor Travis Laster found three independent bases for termination:

1. **General MAE**: Akorn suffered a Material Adverse Effect under the merger agreement's MAE provision
2. **Regulatory Compliance Breach**: Akorn's representations regarding regulatory compliance were untrue and the magnitude would reasonably be expected to result in an MAE
3. **Ordinary Course Breach**: Akorn materially breached its obligation to operate in the ordinary course of business between signing and closing

**MAE Legal Standard:**
The Court articulated the following framework:
- **"Material"** = "matters" to a reasonable buyer in the industry
- **"Durationally Significant"** = not temporary or a short-term blip; must affect long-term value
- An MAE generally must be "durationally significant" and represent "sustained, adverse changes fundamentally altering the company"
- The Court found a MAE can result from a "dramatic, unexpected and company-specific downturn"

**Key Language (at *82-*83):**
> "To qualify as material, the adverse effect must be material when viewed from the longer-term perspective of a reasonable acquiror... Short-term hiccups in earnings should not suffice; rather the Material Adverse Effect should substantially threaten the overall earnings potential of the target in a durationally-significant manner."

**Relevance to SkyWest Transaction:**
*Akorn* establishes that a dramatic, sudden revenue decline from loss of key customers or contracts can constitute an MAE where:
- The decline is substantial (here, 25% revenue drop, 86% EBITDA drop)
- The decline is company-specific rather than industry-wide
- The decline has durationally significant impacts on long-term earnings potential
- The decline represents a fundamental change to the business model

**Subsequent History:** Affirmed by Delaware Supreme Court, 198 A.3d 724 (Del. 2018).

#### 2. *IBP, Inc. v. Tyson Foods, Inc.*, 789 A.2d 14 (Del. Ch. 2001)

**Citation:** *IBP, Inc. v. Tyson Foods, Inc.*, 789 A.2d 14 (Del. Ch. 2001).

**Procedural Posture:** Trial on buyer's attempt to terminate merger agreement; seller sought specific performance.

**Facts:**
- January 1, 2001: Tyson Foods agreed to acquire IBP, Inc.
- Post-signing: IBP disclosed disappointing fourth quarter 2000 results and first quarter 2001 results
- Tyson argued these results constituted a MAC (Material Adverse Change), entitling it to terminate
- March 29, 2001: Tyson filed action in Arkansas seeking rescission/termination based on fraud and breach of warranty

**Holding:**
Vice Chancellor (now Chief Justice) Leo E. Strine, Jr. **rejected Tyson's MAC claim** and ordered specific performance, requiring Tyson to complete the merger.

**MAE Legal Standard:**
The Court established foundational MAE principles:
- MAC clause is a "backstop" protecting buyer from unknown or unsuspected developments
- Must threaten or permanently damage the company's overall earnings for a **significant period of time**
- Given a long-term outlook, a "short-term hiccup" in earnings does **not** qualify as material
- Single quarter earnings miss â‰  fundamental change to business

**Key Language (at 68):**
> "In the relatively short period between signing and closing, the target's business may suffer any number of adverse developments. If the contractual language swept away the buyer's obligation to close based merely on events having some adverse impact on the target, even an acquisition-hungry purchaser would have substantial reason to question its decision during the period leading up to closing."

**Court's Analysis:**
The Court found IBP remained "a consistently but erratically profitable company that is struggling to implement a strategy that will reduce the cyclicality of its earnings." The poor quarters did not represent a fundamental transformation of the business.

**Relevance to SkyWest Transaction:**
*IBP* establishes that temporary earnings declines, even significant ones, do **not** constitute an MAE unless they represent permanent, structural damage to the business. This is directly relevant to analyzing:
- Short-term pilot strikes (30 days): Likely insufficient under *IBP*
- Temporary operational disruptions
- Single-quarter revenue misses

**Precedential Value:** HIGH - foundational MAE case cited in virtually all subsequent Delaware MAE litigation.

#### 3. *Channel Medsystems, Inc. v. Boston Scientific Corp.*, 2019 WL 6896462 (Del. Ch. Dec. 18, 2019)

**Citation:** *Channel Medsystems, Inc. v. Boston Scientific Corp.*, C.A. No. 2018-0673-AGB, 2019 WL 6896462 (Del. Ch. Dec. 18, 2019) (Bouchard, C.).

**Procedural Posture:** Post-trial decision; first Delaware MAE case decided after *Akorn*.

**Facts:**
- November 1, 2017: Boston Scientific agreed to acquire Channel Medsystems, developer of Cerene medical device
- Late December 2017: Channel discovered its VP of Quality Assurance falsified documents, including FDA submissions
- Fraudulent conduct involved approximately $2.6 million stolen from Channel
- May 11, 2018: Boston Scientific sent termination notice claiming MAE
- March 28, 2019: FDA approved Cerene (before trial)

**Holding:**
Chancellor Andre G. Bouchard ruled Boston Scientific **failed to prove** an MAE and ordered specific performance.

**Court's Analysis:**
The Court found that *Akorn* did **not** change the traditional MAE framework. Buyers claiming an MAE continue to face a **heavy burden**:
- Must demonstrate actual or reasonably expected MAE as of termination date
- FDA's subsequent approval of Cerene undermined claim of lasting regulatory harm
- Court found implausible Boston Scientific's contentions that an MAE would reasonably be expected

**Key Language:**
> "Akorn provides a clear example that a MAE can result from a 'dramatic, unexpected and company-specific downturn,' which continues to be a high threshold."

**Relevance to SkyWest Transaction:**
*Channel Medsystems* confirms that *Akorn* was not a relaxation of MAE standards. The case demonstrates that buyers must prove **actual material harm with durational significance**, not merely speculative future problems.

**Precedential Value:** HIGH - first post-*Akorn* interpretation, confirming traditional framework applies.

#### 4. *Bardy Diagnostics, Inc. v. Hill-Rom, Inc.*, C.A. No. 2021-0175-JTL (Del. Ch. July 9, 2021)

**Citation:** *Bardy Diagnostics, Inc. v. Hill-Rom, Inc.*, C.A. No. 2021-0175-JTL, 2021 WL 2877084 (Del. Ch. July 9, 2021) (Laster, V.C.).

**Procedural Posture:** Post-trial decision on buyer's MAE defense.

**Facts:**
- January 2021: Hill-Rom agreed to acquire Bardy Diagnostics for $350 million plus earnout
- Bardy's sole product: Carnation Ambulatory Monitor (patch detecting heart arrhythmias)
- Post-signing: Medicare dramatically reduced reimbursement rate for Bardy's product
- Hill-Rom sought to terminate based on MAE; Bardy sought specific performance

**Holding:**
Vice Chancellor Laster ruled the Medicare reimbursement reduction did **not** constitute an MAE and ordered Hill-Rom to close.

**Durational Significance Analysis:**
The Court's analysis focused on whether effects were durationally significant:
- Hill-Rom's own projections: Bardy would not be profitable until 3 years post-acquisition
- Hill-Rom acknowledged 5+ years would be durationally significant
- Only 2-year period potentially affected by rate decrease
- **Conclusion:** Insufficient durational significance

**Key Language:**
> "The Delaware courts have consistently held that, to constitute an MAE, an event must have effects that are both sufficiently material and have durational significance (i.e., affect the long-term value of the company)."

**MAE Provision Interpretation:**
Court also held that even if event was material and durationally significant, the merger agreement's MAE carve-outs **excluded** this type of regulatory change from constituting an MAE.

**Relevance to SkyWest Transaction:**
*Bardy* provides critical guidance on **durational significance**:
- Effects measured in **years, not months**
- Buyer's own long-term projections matter
- 2-year impact period insufficient for durational significance
- 5+ year impact = durationally significant

This is directly relevant to analyzing pilot strike scenarios:
- 30-day strike: Insufficient duration
- 90-day strike: Possibly insufficient unless causes lasting customer/contract loss

**Precedential Value:** HIGH - most recent detailed analysis of "durational significance" requirement.

### B. Application to SkyWest Transaction: Loss of United CPA (46% of Revenue)

#### Scenario: United Airlines Withholds Change-of-Control Consent; CPA Terminates

**Factual Premise:**
- United CPA represents 46% of SkyWest's revenue ($850M of $1.85B annually)
- CPA contains change-of-control consent provision
- United withholds consent following announcement of GAH acquisition
- CPA terminates effective 90 days after change of control
- SkyWest loses $850M annual revenue stream permanently

#### Legal Analysis Under Delaware MAE Precedent

**1. Materiality Analysis (Does Loss "Matter" to Reasonable Buyer?)**

Under *Akorn*'s framework, the question is whether the loss "matters" to a reasonable buyer in the regional airline industry.

**Application:**
- Loss of 46% of total revenue is **objectively material** to any buyer
- $850M annual revenue loss from single customer = fundamental business transformation
- Regional airline business model depends on stability of capacity purchase agreements
- United CPA was specifically identified as key asset in transaction valuation

**Conclusion:** Loss of United CPA meets "materiality" prong under *Akorn*.

**Supporting Authority:**
- *Akorn*: 25% revenue decline + 86% EBITDA decline = material (2018 WL 4719347, at *82)
- *Bardy*: Court assumed materially adverse for purposes of analysis (2021 WL 2877084, at *15)

---

**2. Durational Significance Analysis (Does Loss Have Lasting Impact?)**

Under *Akorn* and *Bardy*, adverse effect must be "durationally significant" - affecting long-term value measured in years, not months.

**Application:**
- Loss of United CPA is **permanent** - contract terminated, not temporarily suspended
- SkyWest cannot immediately replace 46% of capacity with other airline partners
- Industry capacity agreements negotiated over months/years, not weeks
- Revenue loss continues indefinitely until replacement capacity secured (if ever)
- This is **not** a "short-term hiccup" (per *IBP*)

**Temporal Analysis:**
- *IBP*: Single quarter earnings miss = insufficient duration
- *Bardy*: 2-year revenue impact = insufficient; 5+ years = durationally significant
- **United CPA loss**: Permanent structural change to business model

**Conclusion:** Loss of United CPA meets "durational significance" prong.

**Counterargument:** Buyer (GAH) might argue SkyWest could replace United capacity with other airline partners within 2-3 years. However:
- Burden on buyer to prove MAE, so uncertainty favors seller
- *Akorn*: Even speculation about recovery must be "realistic" (2018 WL 4719347, at *85)
- Regional airline market consolidation limits replacement opportunities
- Customer concentration risk was known, but **actual loss** transforms risk into reality

---

**3. "Company-Specific" vs. "Industry-Wide" Analysis**

Delaware courts distinguish between company-specific adverse events (may constitute MAE) and industry-wide downturns (typically excluded by MAE provisions).

**Application:**
- Loss of United CPA is **company-specific** to SkyWest
- Caused by GAH's acquisition (change of control), not industry conditions
- Other regional carriers retain their CPAs
- Not attributable to regulatory changes, economic downturn, or force majeure

**Conclusion:** Company-specific event under *Akorn* framework.

### C. Application to SkyWest Transaction: ALPA Pilot Strike

- Strike lasts 30 days
- SkyWest loses $152M revenue (8.2% of annual revenue)

**1. Materiality**
- $152M revenue loss is material in absolute terms
- 8.2% of annual revenue is significant but not catastrophic
- Question: Does temporary loss "matter" to long-term buyer?

**2. Durational Significance**
- 30-day strike = **temporary disruption**
- Under *IBP*: "short-term hiccup" insufficient for MAE

- Strike ends, operations resume, revenue returns to normal

*IBP* rejected MAC claim based on single quarter earnings miss. Court emphasized:
- Long-term strategic buyers focus on sustained earnings capacity
- Temporary disruptions, even material ones, do not transform the business
- Company remains fundamentally the same post-disruption

**Application to 30-day strike:**
- Strike is **more temporary** than *IBP*'s quarterly earnings decline
- SkyWest retains same fleet, pilots (post-strike), customer contracts
- Business fundamentally unchanged after strike resolution

**Conclusion:** 30-day strike does **not** meet durational significance requirement.

**Probability 30-Day Strike Constitutes MAE: 5-10%**

**Rationale:**
- Clearly fails durational significance test under *IBP* and *Bardy*
- Temporary operational disruption, not fundamental business change
- Only scenario where 30-day strike = MAE: If causes **permanent** customer/contract loss (see analysis below)

**2. Durational Significance: Key Question**

The durational significance analysis depends on whether strike causes **permanent** harm:

**Scenario A: Strike Ends, Operations Resume Normally (No Permanent Harm)**

**3. Distinguishing "Strike Itself" from "Consequences of Strike"**

Critical distinction:
- **Strike duration** (90 days): Temporary, insufficient for MAE
- **Permanent consequences** (customer loss): Durationally significant, may constitute MAE

**Case Law Support:**
- *Bardy*: "Effects must affect the long-term value of the company" (2021 WL 2877084)
- *Akorn*: Court focused on "sustained, adverse changes" not temporary events (2018 WL 4719347)

**Application:**
If 90-day strike causes United to terminate CPA (permanent $850M revenue loss), the MAE analysis shifts from "temporary strike" to "permanent customer loss." The **cause** (strike) may be temporary, but the **effect** (lost CPA) is durationally significant.

## IV. RAILWAY LABOR ACT - SINGLE CARRIER DETERMINATIONS

### A. Statutory Framework: Railway Labor Act

#### 45 U.S.C. Â§ 152, Ninth - NMB Jurisdiction

**Citation:** 45 U.S.C. Â§ 152, Ninth (Railway Labor Act).

**Statutory Text:**
The Railway Labor Act (RLA) grants the National Mediation Board (NMB) exclusive jurisdiction over representation disputes in the airline industry. When two airlines merge or integrate operations, the NMB determines whether they constitute a "single carrier" for purposes of collective bargaining.

**Purpose:**
Single carrier determination triggers:
1. **Representation election**: Combined workforce votes on union representation
2. **Seniority integration**: Pilot, flight attendant, mechanic seniority lists must be integrated
3. **Unified collective bargaining**: Single union represents craft/class across merged entity

**NMB Authority:**
- Determine whether merger creates "single transportation system"
- Certify union representation for combined carrier
- Oversee integration of collective bargaining agreements

---

### B. NMB Single Carrier Test

#### Legal Standard

The NMB evaluates whether two airlines constitute a single carrier based on **operational integration**, not mere corporate ownership.

**Key Factors (NMB Policy):**

1. **Centralized Labor Relations**
   - Common management making labor policy decisions
   - Unified HR/labor relations structure
   - Integrated grievance procedures

2. **Common Management and Supervision**
   - Single CEO and executive team
   - Integrated operational management
   - Shared strategic planning

3. **Operational Integration**
   - Shared facilities (maintenance bases, crew bases, gates)
   - Common equipment and fleet
   - Integrated scheduling and dispatch
   - Unified customer-facing operations (single brand)

4. **Interchangeability of Employees**
   - Employees work across both pre-merger entities
   - Cross-utilization of pilots, flight attendants, mechanics
   - Shared training programs

5. **Functional Integration**
   - Combined operations under single operating certificate
   - Integrated safety management systems
   - Unified quality assurance programs

**Test is Fact-Specific:**
NMB examines totality of circumstances. No single factor is determinative.

---

**Citation:** *Addington v. US Airline Pilots Ass'n*, 606 F.3d 1174 (9th Cir. 2010).

**Facts:**

**Citation:** *Beckington v. American Airlines, Inc.*, 926 F.3d 549 (9th Cir. 2019).

- USAPA enjoined from participating in McCaskill-Bond seniority integration **except** to advocate for Nicolau Award
- Seniority integration arbitration to proceed under McCaskill-Bond

**Significance:**
- Confirms arbitration awards under Allegheny-Mohawk/McCaskill-Bond are **binding**
- Unions cannot unilaterally abandon prior arbitration awards
- Seniority disputes can take **10+ years** to resolve through litigation

### B. Supreme Court Precedent: *Young v. United Parcel Service*

#### *Young v. United Parcel Service, Inc.*, 575 U.S. 206 (2015)

**Citation:** *Young v. United Parcel Service, Inc.*, 575 U.S. 206, 135 S. Ct. 1338 (2015).

**Facts:**
- Peggy Young worked as UPS driver; job required lifting packages up to 70 pounds
- Young became pregnant; doctor advised lifting restriction: 20 pounds (first half of pregnancy), 10 pounds (second half)
- Young requested light duty accommodation
- **UPS's Policy**: Light duty limited to three categories:
  1. Employees with on-the-job injuries
  2. Employees who lost DOT certification
  3. Employees with ADA-covered disabilities
- Pregnancy did not qualify under any category
- UPS told Young she could not work while under lifting restriction
- Young forced to take unpaid leave

**Legal Framework (PDA):**
PDA's second clause requires employers to treat pregnant employees the same as other employees "similar in their ability or inability to work."

**Issue:**
Did UPS violate PDA by denying light duty to pregnant employee while providing light duty to other employees with similar lifting restrictions?

---

**Supreme Court Holding (Justice Breyer, 6-3):**

The Court established a **modified McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting framework** for PDA claims:

**Step 1: Plaintiff's Prima Facie Case**
Plaintiff must show:
1. Belongs to protected class (pregnant)
2. Sought accommodation
3. Employer did not accommodate her
4. Employer **did accommodate others "similar in their ability or inability to work"**

**Step 2: Employer's Burden**
Employer must articulate a "legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason" for denying accommodation.

**Step 3: Pretext Analysis**
Plaintiff can reach jury by providing sufficient evidence that:
1. Employer's policies impose a **significant burden** on pregnant workers, **AND**
2. Employer's "legitimate, nondiscriminatory" reasons are **not sufficiently strong** to justify the burden

**Key Language (at 229):**
> "The employer cannot simply say that it is more expensive or less convenient to add pregnant women to the category of those ('similar in their ability or inability to work') whom the employer accommodates."

**Outcome:**
Supreme Court vacated Fourth Circuit's summary judgment for UPS and remanded for further proceedings. Case subsequently settled.

---

**Relevance to SkyWest EEOC Case:**
*Young* establishes that employers must justify denying pregnancy accommodations provided to other employees with similar work restrictions. If SkyWest provided light duty to flight attendants with non-pregnancy-related restrictions but denied it to pregnant flight attendant, SkyWest faces high risk of liability under *Young* framework.

**Precedential Value:** HIGH - controlling Supreme Court precedent for PDA claims; informs PWFA interpretation.
