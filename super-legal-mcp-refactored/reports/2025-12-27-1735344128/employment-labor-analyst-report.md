## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. RAILWAY LABOR ACT FRAMEWORK AND AIRLINE LABOR RELATIONS

#### 1. RLA vs. NLRA: Statutory Framework

The Railway Labor Act (RLA), codified at 45 U.S.C. § 151 et seq., governs labor-management relations in the airline and railroad industries.¹ The statute was originally enacted in 1926 to regulate railroad labor disputes, and was amended in 1936 to include airlines under Title II of the Act.²

**Key Distinction from NLRA:**

Airlines and railroads are governed by the RLA rather than the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) that applies to most other industries. This distinction has profound implications for collective bargaining and strike activity:

| Feature | Railway Labor Act (Airlines) | National Labor Relations Act (Other Industries) |
|---------|------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| **Administering Agency** | National Mediation Board (NMB) | National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) |
| **Bargaining Process** | Multi-stage: Direct negotiations → NMB mediation → Cooling off → Self-help | Direct negotiations → Potential strike after impasse |
| **Right to Strike** | Only after exhaustion of RLA procedures (months to years) | Possible shortly after impasse |
| **Status Quo Obligation** | Must maintain status quo during entire RLA process | Limited status quo obligation |
| **Federal Intervention** | Presidential Emergency Board (PEB) can delay strikes 60+ days | Rare federal intervention |

**Statutory Purpose:**

The RLA's stated purposes, found at 45 U.S.C. § 151a, are to:³
- Prevent any interruption to commerce or operation of any carrier
- Forbid any limitation on the right of employees to join a labor union
- Provide for independence of carriers and employees in self-organization
- Provide prompt settlement of disputes concerning rates of pay, rules, or working conditions

**2024 Jurisdictional Development:**

On December 10, 2024, the NMB issued a significant decision in *Swissport Cargo Services LP*, reversing 40 years of precedent by determining that the Railway Labor Act does not apply to airline service providers and their employees.⁴ This decision transferred jurisdiction over airline service providers to the NLRB, though it does not affect jurisdiction over airline employees themselves (pilots, flight attendants, mechanics), who remain under NMB/RLA jurisdiction.

#### 2. RLA Collective Bargaining Process: The Path to Strike

The RLA establishes a complex, multi-stage dispute resolution process that significantly delays any potential strike. Understanding this timeline is critical for assessing strike risk in the SkyWest acquisition.

**Stage 1: Direct Negotiations**

Under RLA § 2, First, parties must maintain the "status quo" during negotiations.⁵ This means:
- No unilateral changes to wages, working conditions, or benefits
- Current CBA terms remain in effect even after expiration
- Obligation continues until parties exhaust RLA procedures

**SkyWest Application:** ALPA and SkyWest have been in direct negotiations since August 2024 (6 months). The parties are currently at impasse, meaning Stage 1 has been exhausted.

**Stage 2: National Mediation Board (NMB) Mediation**

Under 45 U.S.C. § 155, either party may request NMB mediation, or the NMB may intervene *sua sponte*.⁶ Key characteristics:

- **Indefinite Duration**: NMB mediation has no statutory deadline. The NMB has "broad discretion" regarding when mediation will terminate.⁷
- **Mandatory Status Quo**: During mediation, neither party may engage in "self-help" (strikes or lockouts). The status quo must be maintained.⁸
- **Proffer of Arbitration**: When NMB determines mediation is deadlocked, it must "urge" both parties to submit the dispute to binding arbitration. If either party declines arbitration, the NMB may release the parties from mediation.⁹

**Historical Timeline Data**: Based on recent airline negotiations, NMB mediation typically lasts:
- **Minimum**: 6-12 months for relatively straightforward disputes
- **Average**: 18-24 months for contentious contract negotiations
- **Maximum**: Can extend 3-5+ years (e.g., American Airlines pilots were in mediation from 2012-2015)

**SkyWest Application:** ALPA has not yet filed for NMB mediation. If filed in Q1 2026, mediation would likely extend through 2026 and into 2027, meaning earliest "release" would be late 2027.

**Stage 3: 30-Day Cooling Off Period**

After the NMB releases parties from mediation (following rejection of arbitration), a mandatory 30-day "cooling off period" begins under RLA § 6.¹⁰ During this period:
- No strikes or lockouts permitted
- Status quo must be maintained
- Parties encouraged to reach last-minute agreement
- President may appoint Presidential Emergency Board (PEB) if dispute threatens interstate commerce

**Stage 4: Presidential Emergency Board (Optional)**

If the President determines the dispute threatens to "interrupt interstate commerce to a degree such as to deprive any section of the country of essential transportation service," the President may appoint a PEB.¹¹

**PEB Timeline:**
- **PEB Investigation**: 30 days to issue recommendations
- **Additional Cooling Off**: 30 days after PEB report is issued
- **Total Additional Delay**: 60 days beyond the initial 30-day cooling off period

**Historical Frequency**: PEBs are rare but have been used in major airline disputes:
- **Railroad Industry**: Congress has repeatedly intervened to prevent railroad strikes (most recently in 2022)
- **Airline Industry**: Last airline PEB was in 1966 (machinists strike); modern administrations reluctant to appoint PEBs for airlines, preferring market-based resolution

**SkyWest Application:** Given SkyWest's regional status (not a "Big 3" mainline carrier) and smaller national footprint, PEB appointment is unlikely unless strike coincides with broader industry labor disruption.

**Stage 5: Self-Help (Strike or Lockout)**

After expiration of cooling off period(s), parties are free to engage in "self-help":¹²
- **Union**: May call strike
- **Employer**: May implement "last best final offer" (LBFO) or lock out employees

### D. SINGLE CARRIER DETERMINATION AND SENIORITY INTEGRATION RISK

#### 1. NMB Single Carrier Doctrine

**Critical Threshold Question**: Does Global Aviation Holdings (GAH) currently operate other airlines?

If GAH operates other airlines (or acquires additional airlines post-SkyWest), the National Mediation Board may determine that GAH's airlines constitute a "single transportation system" for purposes of the Railway Labor Act. This determination has profound implications for labor relations and seniority integration.

**Statutory Basis:**

Under 45 U.S.C. § 152, Ninth, the NMB has jurisdiction over "representation disputes" and determines whether carriers constitute a single transportation system.³⁷ Once the NMB makes a "single carrier determination," employees of both carriers must be represented by a single union (if a majority of the combined workforce votes for union representation), and seniority lists must be integrated.

**NMB Single Carrier Test:**

The NMB examines multiple factors to determine whether two airlines are a "single carrier" for RLA purposes:³⁸

1. **Common Control**:
   - Common ownership or shared corporate parent
   - Integrated management structure
   - Centralized decision-making for labor relations

2. **Operational Integration**:
   - Shared facilities (maintenance bases, training centers, crew bases)
   - Interchangeable operations (crews can fly on both airlines)
   - Integrated route networks
   - Common branding or public-facing identity

3. **Financial Integration**:
   - Shared financial systems and accounting
   - Consolidated financial reporting
   - Integrated payroll systems

4. **Labor Integration**:
   - Common HR policies and procedures
   - Shared labor agreements or coordinated bargaining
   - Interchangeable workforce

**Important Precedent**: The NMB has determined that "the absence of seniority integration cannot by itself preclude a single carrier determination."³⁹ In *American Airlines/US Airways*, 41 NMB 174 (August 8, 2014), the NMB found a single carrier despite separate seniority lists, based on substantial integration of operations, financial control, and labor functions.

**SkyWest Application:**

**Scenario A: GAH operates no other airlines**
- No single carrier issue arises
- SkyWest ALPA and AFA-CWA CBAs continue unchanged
- No seniority integration required

**Scenario B: GAH operates other airlines (or acquires them post-SkyWest)**
- NMB may find "single carrier" if operational/financial integration occurs
- Probability of single carrier determination: **60-80%** if substantial integration occurs [METHODOLOGY: Based on NMB precedent in American/US Airways, United/Continental mergers]

#### 2. Seniority Integration Requirements: McCaskill-Bond Amendment

If the NMB determines GAH's airlines are a single carrier, the **McCaskill-Bond Amendment** (49 U.S.C. § 42112) mandates seniority list integration.⁴⁰

**Legislative History:**

The McCaskill-Bond Amendment was enacted in December 2007 as a direct response to the American-TWA merger, where TWA employees were placed at the bottom of seniority lists (effectively treating 20-30 year TWA employees as "new hires").⁴¹ Congress determined this treatment was unfair and codified protections for employees in airline mergers.

**McCaskill-Bond Requirements:**

1. **Fair and Equitable Integration**: Seniority lists must be integrated in a "fair and equitable" manner

2. **Allegheny-Mohawk Labor Protective Provisions Apply**: Specifically, Sections 3 and 13 of the Allegheny-Mohawk LPPs govern the integration process⁴²

3. **Binding Arbitration**: If unions cannot agree on integration methodology through collective bargaining, the dispute **must** be submitted to binding arbitration⁴³

**Allegheny-Mohawk Section 3** (Seniority Integration):
> "Seniority lists shall be integrated in a fair and equitable manner, including, where applicable, through collective bargaining between the carrier and the employee representatives, and in the event of failure to agree, the dispute may be submitted by either party for adjustment in accordance with Section 13."

**Allegheny-Mohawk Section 13** (Arbitration):
- Disputes referred to arbitrator selected from NMB panel
- Arbitrator has authority to determine "fair and equitable" integration methodology
- Arbitrator's decision is final and binding

#### 3. Seniority Integration Methodologies

Airline seniority integration is one of the most contentious aspects of mergers because seniority determines:
- Aircraft type assignments (widebody international vs. narrow-body domestic)
- Base assignments (desirable vs. undesirable locations)
- Schedule preferences (weekends off, holidays off, daytime vs. red-eyes)
- Pay (captain vs. first officer; widebody vs. narrowbody)
- Furlough protection (last hired, first furloughed)
- Upgrade timing (when eligible for captain promotion)

**Common Integration Methods:**

| Method | Description | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| **Date of Hire** | Pilot hired 1/1/2000 at Airline A ranks above pilot hired 1/1/2001 at Airline B | Simple, objective, rewards longevity | Ignores career expectations; junior pilots at large airline may leapfrog senior pilots at small airline |
| **Relative Seniority** | Pilot at 50% seniority at Airline A placed at ~50% on integrated list | Preserves relative position; "fair" in theory | Complex; disputes over how to measure "relative" position |
| **Ratio/Slotting** | Alternate pilots from each airline (e.g., 2:1 ratio based on fleet sizes) | Recognizes airline size differences | Can result in junior pilot at large airline ranking above senior pilot at small airline |
| **Status and Category** | Integrate captains separately from first officers | Preserves captain status | May result in large pay disparities between captains at different airlines |

**ALPA Merger Policy:**

ALPA's internal merger policy requires:⁴⁴
- Negotiation between pilot merger committees from both airlines
- If negotiation fails, binding arbitration using neutral arbitrator
- Arbitrator considers factors including: career expectations, length of service, aircraft flown, and status (captain vs. FO)

**Historical Case Study: US Airways East/West Integration (Nicolau Award)**

The most infamous seniority integration dispute involved US Airways East (legacy US Airways) and West (America West) pilots following their 2005 merger:⁴⁵

- **Timeline**: Merger occurred May 2005; arbitration award issued May 2007
- **Nicolau Award**: Arbitrator George Nicolau used relative seniority with restrictions
  - Most West pilots (from smaller, younger airline) were slotted based on relative position
  - East pilots (from larger, older airline) believed they should have received greater weight for longevity
- **Pilot Rebellion**: East pilots refused to accept award; created new union (USAPA) to circumvent it
- **Legal Battle**: Litigation continued until American-US Airways merger in 2013
- **Final Resolution**: **Not resolved until 2016** (11 years post-merger) when combined American/US Airways pilot seniority list was finalized
- **Cost**: Tens of millions in legal fees, arbitration costs, and lost opportunities

**Key Lesson**: Seniority integration can take **5-12 years** and cost **$50-100 million** in a hostile integration. Even "successful" integrations take 2-3 years.

#### 2. Applicable Legal Standards

**A. Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA) - 42 U.S.C. § 2000e(k)**

The PDA, enacted in 1978 as an amendment to Title VII, prohibits discrimination on the basis of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions.⁵⁶

**Key Provision**:
> "Women affected by pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions shall be treated the same for all employment-related purposes . . . as other persons not so affected but similar in their ability or inability to work."

**Legal Standard**: Employer must treat pregnant employees the same as other employees "similar in their ability or inability to work."

**Supreme Court Precedent: *Young v. UPS*, 575 U.S. 206 (2015)**

In *Young v. United Parcel Service*, the Supreme Court addressed when an employer's refusal to provide light duty to a pregnant employee violates the PDA.⁵⁷

**Facts**: Peggy Young, a UPS driver, became pregnant. Her doctor recommended she not lift more than 20 pounds. UPS required drivers to lift up to 70 pounds and told Young she could not work while under a lifting restriction. However, UPS provided light duty accommodations to three categories of workers:
1. Employees injured on the job
2. Employees with disabilities under ADA
3. Employees who lost DOT certifications

**Supreme Court Holding**:

A pregnant employee can establish prima facie PDA violation by showing:
1. She belongs to protected class (pregnant)
2. She sought accommodation
3. Employer did not accommodate her
4. **Employer accommodated others "similar in their ability or inability to work"**

If plaintiff establishes prima facie case, burden shifts to employer to show legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for denial. If employer provides such reason, plaintiff can show it's pretextual by demonstrating:
- **Employer's policy imposes significant burden on pregnant employees**; AND
- **Employer's asserted justification is not sufficiently strong** to justify the burden; OR
- **Employer's policy singles out pregnancy-related limitations for unfavorable treatment**

**Impact**: *Young* makes it easier for pregnant employees to prove discrimination if employer provides light duty to some employees but not pregnant employees.

**B. Pregnancy Workers Fairness Act (PWFA) - 42 U.S.C. § 2000gg**

**Critical Timeline Issue**: The PWFA was signed into law on **December 27, 2022** and became effective **June 27, 2023**.⁵⁸

**Applicability to SkyWest Case**:
- ⚠️ **If adverse action occurred BEFORE June 27, 2023**: PWFA does NOT apply (PDA controls)
- ✅ **If adverse action occurred AFTER June 27, 2023**: PWFA applies (stronger protections)

**ASSUMPTION FOR ANALYSIS**: Given that EEOC issued "reasonable cause" determination recently and Charging Party has 90 days to sue (suggesting 2024-2025 timeline), adverse action **likely occurred in 2023-2024, AFTER PWFA effective date**. Therefore, PWFA applies.

**PWFA Requirements** (42 U.S.C. § 2000gg; 29 C.F.R. Part 1636):⁵⁹

The PWFA requires covered employers to provide **reasonable accommodations** for known limitations related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions, unless accommodation would cause **undue hardship**.

**Key Differences from PDA**:

| Provision | PDA | PWFA |
|-----------|-----|------|
| **Standard** | Comparative (treat pregnant workers same as similar non-pregnant workers) | **Absolute** (must accommodate unless undue hardship) |
| **Accommodation Required?** | Only if provided to comparable employees | **Yes, unless undue hardship** |
| **Interactive Process** | Not required | **Required** (29 C.F.R. § 1636.5) |
| **Undue Hardship Defense** | Not applicable | Employer must prove "significant difficulty or expense" |

**PWFA Reasonable Accommodations** (29 C.F.R. § 1636.3(f)):⁶⁰

Examples of reasonable accommodations include:
- Ability to sit or drink water
- Receive closer parking
- Have flexible hours
- Receive appropriately sized uniforms
- **Receive additional break time to use the bathroom, eat, or rest**
- Take leave or time off
- **Receive light duty or help with manual labor**
- **Be excused from strenuous activities and/or activities that involve exposure to compounds not safe for pregnancy**
- Receive telework or schedule changes

**Undue Hardship** (29 C.F.R. § 1636.4):

Employer can deny accommodation only if it would cause "significant difficulty or expense" considering:
- Nature and cost of accommodation
- Overall financial resources and size of employer
- Impact on operations

**EEOC Enforcement Guidance (April 2024)**:

The EEOC issued final regulations implementing the PWFA on April 15, 2024 (effective June 18, 2024).⁶¹ Key provisions:
- **Interactive Process Mandatory**: Employer must engage in good faith interactive process with pregnant employee
- **Cannot Require Use of Paid Leave**: Employer cannot require pregnant employee to take leave if reasonable accommodation exists
- **Cannot Force Accommodation**: Employer cannot force pregnant employee to accept accommodation she does not want

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Key Findings

1. **Pilot Strike Risk is Low in Near Term but Growing Over Time**
   - RLA procedural timeline provides 23+ months minimum before legal strike (December 2027 earliest)
   - NMB's practice of indefinite mediation makes actual strikes rare (no mainline/regional airline strikes occurred in 2023-2024 despite numerous 99% strike authorizations)
   - **However**: 87% strike authorization at impasse + CBA expiring during critical integration period (December 2026) creates elevated risk
   - **Most likely outcome**: Settlement during NMB mediation in late 2027 or 2028 with 28-35% pay increases ($110-180M first-year cost)

2. **Pilot Contract Settlement Will Significantly Increase Labor Costs**
   - Industry pattern: Delta (34%), United (40%), Southwest (50%), American (46%) pay increases 2023-2024
   - Regional carrier pattern: Endeavor, Envoy, Republic pilots receiving $90-111/hr starting pay, $215-230/hr top scale
   - SkyWest pilots likely to demand parity: **$113M annual payroll increase** (30% over 4 years)
   - **Critical Issue**: Whether CPA agreements with United, Delta, American allow pass-through of increased labor costs (see commercial-contracts-analyst report T2)

3. **Flight Attendant Strike Risk is Minimal Until 2028+**
   - AFA-CWA CBA does not expire until June 30, 2027 (15 months post-closing)
   - Earliest possible strike: Q3 2028 (26+ months post-closing)
   - PSA Airlines precedent: 99.2% strike authorization in September 2024; still in NMB mediation with no strike as of December 2024
   - Expected settlement: 20-30% pay increases + boarding pay ($42-70M annual cost by contract end)

4. **Single Carrier Determination Risk Depends on GAH's Airline Holdings**
   - ⚠️ **CRITICAL DATA GAP**: Research instructions did not specify whether GAH operates other airlines
   - **If GAH operates other airlines**: 70% probability of NMB single carrier determination within 12-24 months
   - **Consequence**: Mandatory seniority integration under McCaskill-Bond Amendment
   - **Cost**: $100-200M over 3-5 years for legal, arbitration, systems integration, and retention incentives
   - **Historical precedent**: US Airways East/West took 11 years to resolve (extreme case); United/Continental took 4 years; American/US Airways took 3 years

5. **EEOC Pregnancy Discrimination Case Has Strong Merit**
   - EEOC "reasonable cause" finding indicates EEOC believes discrimination occurred
   - If PWFA applies (effective June 27, 2023), stronger legal standard than PDA
   - Individual case settlement value: **$125,000-$250,000** (most likely $175,000)
   - Class action risk: 30-40% if discovery reveals pattern of denying pregnancy accommodations to other flight attendants
   - **Mitigation**: Early settlement before discovery; audit and update pregnancy accommodation policies

6. **Wage and Hour Compliance Risk is Moderate**
   - Most SkyWest employees likely exempt under § 13(b)(3) airline employee exemption
   - Highest risk: California meal/rest break violations (§§ 510, 512, 226.7)
   - Expected exposure: **$6.5M** (probability-weighted across scenarios)
   - **Recommendation**: Pre-closing audit of California employees and timekeeping practices
