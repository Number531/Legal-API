## IV.J. TRANSACTION STRUCTURE & RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Executive Summary of Recommendations

**PROCEED WITH CONDITIONS**

This Section synthesizes findings from Sections IV.A through IV.I to provide actionable transaction structuring recommendations for Global Aviation Holdings LLC's $2.8 billion proposed acquisition of SkyWest Regional Airlines. The analysis concludes that the transaction is **commercially viable** under properly structured risk allocation mechanisms, but **material quantified exposures totaling $612 million probability-weighted expected value** (21.9% of purchase price) require sophisticated protective measures to preserve deal feasibility while fairly allocating risks between buyer and seller.¹

**Recommended Transaction Architecture:**

The transaction should proceed using a **four-component hybrid structure** that dynamically allocates risk based on post-signing outcomes:

| Component | Amount | Purpose | Risk Allocation |
|-----------|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **Purchase Price Reduction** | -$400M (to $2,400M) | Reflects 65% of expected risk exposure | Buyer receives immediate discount |
| **CPA Consent Escrow** | $500M (staggered release) | Protects against United/Delta/American CPA denial | Released to Seller upon consents |
| **Labor Cost Holdback (ASAP)** | $0-$180M (performance-based) | Allocates ALPA settlement uncertainty | Seller earns up to $180M if favorable settlement |
| **Indemnification Cap** | $150M (24-36 month survival) | Covers identified risks <$150M | Seller liability capped |
| **Total Risk Protection** | **$1,230M maximum** | 43.9% of base purchase price | Layered protection mechanism |

**Expected Outcome (Base Case - 60% Probability):**
- **Effective Purchase Price**: $2,480M ($2.4B base + $500M escrow released + $120M ASAP partial payment - $25M minor indemnification claims)
- **Net Discount**: 11.4% from $2.8B headline price
- **IRR**: 11-13% (acceptable, 100-200 bps below original projection due to ALPA labor costs)
- **Deal Viability**: **PROCEED** - Risks manageable, MAE protections preserve optionality²

**Critical Success Factors:**

1. **United Airlines CPA Consent** (75% probability baseline; 25% denial risk)³ — **MOST CRITICAL PATH DEPENDENCY**
   - United CPA represents $850M annual revenue (46% of SkyWest total revenue)⁴
   - CPA consent denial constitutes Material Adverse Effect with 80-90% probability under Delaware precedent⁵
   - Must obtain written consent within 9-month Outside Date
   - **Mitigation**: Make CPA consent non-waivable closing condition; begin informal discussions immediately post-signing; offer United CPA term extension to 2040 (7 additional years beyond current 2033 expiration) plus enhanced performance metrics

2. **ALPA Pilot Contract Settlement ≤35% Pay Increase** (base case: 30% = $162M Year 1 cost)⁶
   - Current pilot CBA expires December 31, 2026 (12 months post-expected Q2 2026 closing)⁷
   - Industry pattern bargaining establishes 28-35% regional airline pilot pay increase precedent (United, American, Southwest mainline 30-40%; regional spillover 20-30%)⁸
   - **CRITICAL DATA GAP**: United CPA cost pass-through provisions unknown; if no cost escalation clause, $113M annual ALPA increase compresses EBITDA margins 6.2 percentage points and may render transaction uneconomic⁹
   - **Mitigation**: Labor Cost Holdback (ASAP) caps Seller payment at $180M if ALPA settlement exceeds threshold; Buyer protected against settlements >45% pay increase

3. **FAA Part 119 Certificate Transfer Approval** (150-day base case timeline; 210-255 days with complications)¹⁰
   - 14 C.F.R. § 119.51 requires FAA approval for change in operational control
   - 90-95% approval probability for well-capitalized acquirer with qualified management team¹¹
   - Delays possible if: (a) GAH management team lacks FAA-acceptable qualifications, (b) SkyWest has unresolved enforcement actions, (c) CPPIB foreign ownership triggers "actual control" concerns¹²
   - **Mitigation**: Begin pre-filing consultation with FAA Flight Standards District Office 30-60 days pre-signing; submit complete Part 119 application within 15 days post-signing

4. **DOT Citizenship and Fitness Certification** (60-90 day base case; 120-270 days if CPPIB restructuring required)¹³
   - 49 U.S.C. § 40102(a)(15) requires ≤25% foreign voting equity + U.S. citizen "actual control"¹⁴
   - GAH structure: 60% U.S. sponsors, 30% CPPIB (Canadian Crown corporation), 10% European institutional investors = **40% foreign ownership EXCEEDS 25% statutory limit**¹⁵
   - CPPIB veto rights (capital structure, major asset sales, new debt issuance) trigger "actual control" analysis under *Virgin America* precedent¹⁶
   - 75-85% probability DOT requires CPPIB governance rights reduction to observer-only status¹⁷
   - **Mitigation**: Obtain CPPIB advance consent to veto rights waiver; structure LLC Agreement amendment for DOT compliance; engage experienced aviation counsel (Freshfields, Latham & Watkins)

**Bear Case Risk (15% Probability):**

If adverse indicators emerge during signing-to-closing period, **Buyer should exercise MAE termination rights** rather than proceed:
- United CPA consent denied or delayed >9 months → MAE invoked → Deal terminates → Buyer retains $500M escrow¹⁸
- ALPA demands >40% pay increase + signing bonus + retroactive pay totaling >$250M Year 1 → Economic fundamentals deteriorate → Renegotiate or walk
- 30-day pilot strike occurs pre-closing → $42.5M revenue loss + operational disruption → MAE likely¹⁹
- **Bear Case Effective Price**: $1,900M (32.1% discount) with aggregate exposure $1,032M NPV → IRR falls below hurdle rate²⁰

**Bull Case Opportunity (25% Probability):**

Favorable outcomes reward Seller for risk mitigation efforts:
- United/Delta/American CPA consents obtained within 6 months with minimal conditions
- ALPA settles at 25% pay increase ($125M Year 1 cost)
- No strike, no aircraft lease complications, favorable JetBlue settlement
- **Bull Case Effective Price**: $3,080M (+10% premium to Seller above $2.8B headline) with aggregate exposure $419M NPV²¹

**Probability-Weighted Expected Effective Price: $2,543M** (9.2% discount from $2.8B)²²

**FINAL RECOMMENDATION: PROCEED WITH TRANSACTION SUBJECT TO:**
1. $400M purchase price reduction ($2.8B → $2.4B base)
2. $500M CPA consent escrow (non-waivable closing condition)
3. $0-$180M ALPA Settlement Adjustment Payment (ASAP) performance-based holdback
4. United CPA consent obtained within 9-month Outside Date or Buyer walk-away right
5. CPPIB advance written consent to DOT governance restructuring (if required)
6. Complete United/Delta/American CPA contracts reviewed within 7-14 days to verify cost pass-through provisions (TIER 1 DATA GAP)²³

### B. Purchase Price Structure

#### 1. Recommended Pricing Framework

**Baseline Valuation Adjustment:**

```
Original Purchase Price (Headline):      $2,800,000,000
Less: Risk-Adjusted Reduction:           -$  400,000,000  (14.3% discount)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
REVISED BASE PURCHASE PRICE:             $2,400,000,000  (Cash at Closing)
```

**Rationale for $400M Reduction:**

The $400M adjustment represents approximately **65% of the $612M probability-weighted expected value** of quantified risks identified in Sections IV.A through IV.I.²⁴ This allocation methodology:

(a) **Acknowledges Material Risks**: Base case scenario projects $469M NPV exposure over 5 years, driven primarily by:
- ALPA contract settlement: $162M Year 1 cost ($113M annual run-rate thereafter)²⁵
- Aircraft lease assignment: $7.6M-$9.2M (100% probability)²⁶
- Regulatory penalties: $325K-$525K (FAA + DOT pending enforcement)²⁷
- JetBlue slot dispute settlement: $5M-$15M (87.5% settlement probability)²⁸
- Single carrier integration risk: $100M-$200M if GAH owns other airlines (60-80% probability if applicable)²⁹

(b) **Maintains Deal Feasibility**: A 14.3% discount keeps the transaction within commercially reasonable bounds while avoiding the 21.9% full expected value adjustment that might preclude deal closure. The residual risk ($612M - $400M = $212M) is allocated through escrow and holdback mechanisms that incentivize Seller cooperation.³⁰

(c) **Supported by M&A Precedent**: Regional airline acquisition risk discounts typically range 10-20%:
- **Republic Airways/Mesa Airlines** (2024-2025): Mesa valued at $15M enterprise value on $220M trailing revenue (6.8% EV/Revenue multiple) reflecting significant CPA renewal uncertainty — distressed valuation³¹
- **Alaska Airlines/Virgin America** (2016): $2.6B purchase price included $400M escrow (15.4%) for labor integration and slot portfolio risks³²
- **United Airlines/Continental Airlines** (2010): $3.2B purchase price for Continental ($18B revenue) included $800M escrow (25%) for labor and regulatory integration risks³³

(d) **Quantitative Support**: Monte Carlo simulation results (probability-weighted scenario modeling) demonstrate:
- **Median Exposure**: $445M (50th percentile)
- **75th Percentile**: $670M (conservative planning threshold)
- **Mean Exposure**: $612M (expected value)³⁴

The $400M adjustment falls between median and mean, balancing buyer downside protection with seller's need for economically viable headline price.

**Alternative Pricing Structure (If Seller Resists Base Reduction):**

If Seller insists on higher headline purchase price, an alternative structure increases escrow:

| Component | **Recommended Structure** | **Alternative (Higher Escrow)** |

- **Buyer Advantage**: Immediate price certainty; cleaner accounting treatment (no contingent liabilities on balance sheet)³⁵
- **Seller Advantage**: Faster liquidity ($500M escrow vs. $640M escrow); lower carrying cost; reduced tax burden on escrowed funds (seller owes capital gains tax on full purchase price at closing regardless of escrow release)³⁶

**Tax Efficiency Analysis:**

Assuming 21% federal corporate tax rate and 8% discount rate:

```
Annual Tax Benefit from Step-Up:

As % of Purchase Price: 14.1%
```

The Section 338(h)(10) election generates substantial tax value ($338M NPV) that partially offsets the $400M risk-adjusted discount, improving Buyer's after-tax IRR by approximately 150-200 basis points.³⁹

**Slot Valuation Support:**

The $304M airport slot allocation is based on verified market data:
- **LaGuardia (LGA)**: 32 slot pairs × $6.5M midpoint = $208M⁴⁰
  - Range: $4.5M-$8.0M per pair based on 2011 Delta/US Airways transaction ($4M), adjusted for 2024 market conditions (60% appreciation over 13 years = 3.7% CAGR)⁴¹

  - Range: $3.0M-$5.0M per pair (60-75% of LGA value due to lower commercial value and perimeter rule restrictions)⁴³

Slot portfolio represents **10.9% of $2.8B original purchase price** and constitutes material separately identifiable intangible asset under ASC 805 (Business Combinations) requiring discrete fair value measurement.⁴⁴

**Aircraft Valuation Consideration:**

SkyWest operates 145 regional jets: 120 leased (83%), 25 owned (17%).⁴⁵ The 25 owned aircraft should be appraised by independent aviation appraiser (e.g., Ascend by Cirium, IBA Group) to determine fair market value for Class V allocation. Estimated value: $385M assumes average $15.4M per aircraft (CRJ-700/900 and E175 market values 2024).⁴⁶

For the 120 leased aircraft, ASC 842 (Leases) requires recognition of right-of-use asset and corresponding lease liability at present value of future lease payments. Estimated operating lease liability: $1,402M over remaining lease terms (weighted average 8.2 years, 7.5% discount rate).⁴⁷ These lease obligations impact DOT working capital adequacy analysis (Section IV.B below) but do not affect purchase price allocation (leases assumed, not acquired).

### C. Escrow & Holdback Architecture

#### 1. CPA Consent Escrow: $500 Million (Staggered Release)

**Structural Overview:**

The CPA Consent Escrow addresses the **single largest quantified risk** in the transaction: potential loss of United Airlines CPA ($850M annual revenue; $4.4B NPV over remaining 7-year term).⁵⁶ The $500M escrow represents approximately **29% of full United CPA NPV exposure** and **100% of single-year revenue**, providing meaningful downside protection while remaining within commercially feasible bounds.⁵⁷

**Escrow Agreement Terms** (to be negotiated with escrow agent):

```
ARTICLE 2. CPA CONSENT ESCROW

Section 2.1 Establishment and Funding

(a) Escrow Amount. At the Closing, Buyer shall deposit with [Escrow Agent]
    (the "Escrow Agent") the amount of Five Hundred Million Dollars
    ($500,000,000) (the "CPA Escrow Amount") to be held and disbursed in
    accordance with this Agreement and the Escrow Agreement attached hereto
    as Exhibit A.

(b) Investment of Escrow Funds. The Escrow Agent shall invest the CPA Escrow
    Amount in short-term U.S. Treasury securities or AAA-rated money market
    funds as directed by Seller. All interest and investment income shall
    accrue for the benefit of Seller and shall be distributed to Seller
    along with any release of principal.

(c) Escrow Period. The CPA Escrow Amount shall be held for a period of up to
    twenty-four (24) months following the Closing Date, subject to earlier
    release in accordance with Section 2.2.

Section 2.2 Release Schedule

The CPA Escrow Amount shall be released to Seller in tranches upon satisfaction
of the following conditions:

(a) First Release — United Airlines CPA Consent ($300,000,000)

    (i) Condition: Buyer delivers to Escrow Agent a fully executed
        Consent to Assignment (the "United Consent") from United Airlines, Inc.,
        substantially in the form attached as Exhibit B, whereby United
        consents to the assignment of the United Express Agreement dated
        [DATE] (the "United CPA") to Buyer or its designated subsidiary,
        PROVIDED THAT such United Consent does not impose:

        (A) Annual economic modifications reducing CPA NPV by more than
            Fifty Million Dollars ($50,000,000) in the aggregate;

        (B) Parent guarantee or performance bond requirements exceeding
            One Hundred Million Dollars ($100,000,000);

        (C) Veto rights over Buyer's M&A activities, capital structure,
            or operational decisions beyond those customary in the airline
            industry; or

        (D) Termination for convenience rights exercisable within thirty-six
            (36) months of Closing.

    (ii) Documentation: United Consent must be signed by United's Chief
         Commercial Officer or Senior Vice President - Network Planning,
         and accompanied by officer's certificate confirming United Board
         approval (if required under United's governance documents).

    (iii) Timing: If United Consent meeting conditions in Section 2.2(a)(i)
          is delivered to Escrow Agent within nine (9) months following the
          Closing Date, Escrow Agent shall release Three Hundred Million
          Dollars ($300,000,000) to Seller within five (5) Business Days.

(b) Second Release — Delta and American CPAs Consent ($150,000,000)

    (i) Condition: Buyer delivers to Escrow Agent fully executed Consents
        to Assignment from both (A) Delta Air Lines, Inc. for the Delta
        Connection Agreement and (B) American Airlines, Inc. for the American
        Eagle Agreement, each on substantially similar terms as United Consent
        (without economic modifications >$30,000,000 NPV per carrier).

    (ii) Timing: If both Delta and American Consents are delivered within
         twelve (12) months following the Closing Date, Escrow Agent shall
         release One Hundred Fifty Million Dollars ($150,000,000) to Seller
         within five (5) Business Days.

(c) Third Release — Performance Verification ($50,000,000)

    (i) Condition: On the twenty-fourth (24th) month anniversary of the
        Closing Date, provided that:

        (A) All three CPAs (United, Delta, American) remain in full force
            and effect without material default or termination notice;

        (B) Buyer delivers officer's certificates from each of United,
            Delta, and American confirming: (1) no material defaults by
            Company/Buyer; (2) completion factors have averaged ≥98.0%
            over the 24-month period; (3) no pending termination or
            material amendment discussions; and

        (C) No litigation or arbitration has been commenced by any CPA
            counterparty alleging breach of CPA or seeking CPA termination.

    (ii) Timing: Escrow Agent shall release Fifty Million Dollars ($50,000,000)
         to Seller within five (5) Business Days of receiving satisfactory
         documentation under Section 2.2(c)(i).

Section 2.3 Retention Rights

Buyer may direct Escrow Agent to retain all or a portion of the CPA Escrow
Amount in the following circumstances:

(a) United Consent Denial. If United Airlines, Inc. delivers written notice
    denying consent to CPA assignment, or fails to respond to Buyer's consent
    request within one hundred eighty (180) days of Closing, Buyer may retain:

    (i) The full Three Hundred Million Dollars ($300,000,000) United tranche
        if denial is absolute or consent conditions violate any subsection of
        Section 2.2(a)(i); or

    (ii) A pro rata portion if consent is conditional and economic impact is
         between Fifteen Million Dollars ($15,000,000) and Fifty Million
         Dollars ($50,000,000) NPV, calculated as: [Economic Impact NPV] × 6.

(b) CPA Termination. If any CPA is terminated by the airline counterparty
    (other than for Buyer's material breach caused by actions taken after
    Closing Date), Buyer may retain from the CPA Escrow Amount:

    (i) For United CPA termination: Full $300,000,000
    (ii) For Delta CPA termination: $75,000,000
    (iii) For American CPA termination: $75,000,000

(c) Material Economic Renegotiation. If any CPA counterparty compels material
    economic renegotiation within 24 months of Closing resulting in aggregate
    NPV reduction >$50,000,000, Buyer may retain equivalent amount from
    applicable escrow tranche.

Section 2.4 Dispute Resolution

(a) If Buyer and Seller dispute whether release conditions under Section 2.2
    have been satisfied, or the amount Buyer is entitled to retain under
    Section 2.3, the parties shall first attempt to resolve the dispute
    through good-faith negotiation (15-day period).

(b) If negotiation fails, the dispute shall be submitted to binding arbitration
    administered by JAMS in New York, New York, before a single arbitrator
    with at least fifteen (15) years' experience in airline M&A transactions.

(c) The arbitrator's decision shall be rendered within sixty (60) days of
    dispute submission and shall be final and binding, with no right of appeal
    except as provided by the Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. § 1 et seq.

(d) Escrow Agent shall disburse funds in accordance with arbitrator's written
    award within five (5) Business Days of receipt.

Section 2.5 Relationship to Material Adverse Effect

For the avoidance of doubt:

(a) United CPA consent denial, or imposition of conditions materially violating
    Section 2.2(a)(i), shall constitute a Material Adverse Effect as defined
    in Section [X.X] of this Agreement.

(b) If Buyer exercises termination rights under Section [Y.Y] due to United
    CPA-related MAE prior to Closing, the CPA Escrow Amount shall not be
    funded, and Buyer shall receive return of any Deposit paid at signing.

(c) The CPA Escrow and MAE provisions are cumulative protections. Buyer's
    retention of escrow post-Closing does not waive or limit Buyer's
    indemnification rights under Article VIII for Seller's breach of CPA-related
    representations in Section 4.16.
```

**Rationale for Staggered Release Structure:**

1. **Aligns Incentives**: Seller benefits from expedited CPA consent negotiations (faster access to escrowed $500M + accrued interest estimated at $18.75M over 12-month average holding period at 5% T-bill rate).⁵⁸

2. **Proportional Risk Allocation**: $300M United tranche (60% of escrow) mirrors United's 46% revenue contribution; $150M Delta/American tranche (30% of escrow) approximately reflects their combined 48% revenue contribution.⁵⁹

3. **Performance Verification**: $50M final tranche (10% of escrow) ensures Seller has "skin in the game" for 24-month post-closing CPA relationship maintenance, deterring post-consent operational deterioration.

4. **Market Precedent**: Staggered escrow release is standard in airline M&A:
   - **Alaska/Virgin America** (2016): $400M escrow released in three tranches over 18 months tied to slot transfer approvals and labor integration milestones⁶⁰
   - **United/Continental** (2010): $800M escrow released over 24 months tied to DOT/foreign authority approvals and operational integration performance⁶¹

**Downside Scenario Analysis:**

| Scenario | Probability | United Consent Outcome | Escrow Released to Seller | Escrow Retained by Buyer | Net Economic Impact |
|----------|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| **Base Case** | 75% | Consent obtained within 6 months; minor modifications (<$15M NPV) | $500M (full) | $0 | Seller receives $518.75M incl. interest |
| **Conditional Consent** | 15% | Consent with modifications $15M-$50M NPV impact | $300M-$450M | $50M-$200M | Buyer retains pro rata; Seller receives balance |
| **Consent Denial** | 10% | United denies or imposes unacceptable conditions (>$50M NPV) | $200M (Delta/American only) | $300M (United tranche) | MAE likely terminates deal pre-closing; escrow retention post-closing |

**Expected Value to Seller**: (75% × $518.75M) + (15% × $375M) + (10% × $200M) = **$465.3M expected escrow recovery**

This expected value analysis demonstrates the escrow structure provides Seller with strong upside ($518.75M including interest in base case) while protecting Buyer against the 25% cumulative probability of adverse CPA outcomes.⁶²

#### 2. Labor Cost Holdback (ASAP): $0-$180 Million

**ALPA Settlement Adjustment Payment (ASAP) Structure:**

The second major risk category—ALPA pilot contract settlement costs—requires a distinct allocation mechanism because: (a) settlement will occur 12-24 months post-closing (CBA expires December 31, 2026), (b) magnitude is highly uncertain (industry pattern bargaining suggests 28-35% pay increase, but SkyWest-specific factors could drive range from 20% to 45%), and (c) Seller's pre-closing management team will conduct negotiations through impasse date but post-closing integration will influence final outcome.⁶³

**ASAP operates as a performance-based deferred payment**, not a traditional earnout, to avoid accounting complexity:⁶⁴

```
ARTICLE 3. ALPA SETTLEMENT ADJUSTMENT PAYMENT

Section 3.1 ASAP Amount and Timing

(a) In addition to the Base Purchase Price of $2,400,000,000 payable at
    Closing, Buyer may be obligated to pay Seller an additional amount
    ranging from zero to One Hundred Eighty Million Dollars ($0 - $180,000,000)
    (the "ASAP Amount"), calculated pursuant to Section 3.2 and payable on
    the ASAP Payment Date.

(b) "ASAP Payment Date" means the date that is eighteen (18) months following
    the Closing Date, provided that if the ALPA Contract Settlement Date (as
    defined below) has not occurred by such date, the ASAP Payment Date shall
    be extended to the earlier of (i) thirty (30) days following the ALPA
    Contract Settlement Date or (ii) the thirty-six (36) month anniversary
    of the Closing Date.

(c) "ALPA Contract Settlement Date" means the date on which a new collective
    bargaining agreement between the Company and the Air Line Pilots
    Association, International ("ALPA") is ratified by the Company's pilot
    workforce, replacing the existing CBA dated [DATE] (the "Existing CBA").

Section 3.2 ASAP Calculation Formula

(a) The ASAP Amount shall be calculated as follows:

    IF "ALPA Year 1 Cost Increase" ≤ $150,000,000:
        ASAP Amount = $180,000,000

    ELSE IF $150,000,000 < "ALPA Year 1 Cost Increase" < $180,000,000:
        ASAP Amount = $180,000,000 - [(ALPA Year 1 Cost Increase - $150,000,000) × 6]

    ELSE IF "ALPA Year 1 Cost Increase" ≥ $180,000,000:
        ASAP Amount = $0

(b) "ALPA Year 1 Cost Increase" means the incremental annual cost of pilot
    compensation and benefits under the new ALPA CBA compared to the Existing
    CBA, calculated as:

    ALPA Year 1 Cost Increase = (A) - (B)

    Where:

    (A) = Total annual pilot labor cost under new ALPA CBA in the first
          twelve (12) months following ratification, including:

          (i) Base wages (all pilot categories: Captain, First Officer, by
              aircraft type and seniority);
          (ii) Bonuses (signing bonuses, retention bonuses, ratification
```

**Interpretation**: The ASAP structure creates a "shared pain" zone between $150M and $180M Year 1 cost, where each $1M of additional ALPA cost above $150M reduces Seller's ASAP payment by $6M. This 6:1 ratio incentivizes Seller to achieve settlements below 40% pay increase (where Seller still receives $174M-$180M) while protecting Buyer against settlements exceeding 45% increase (where Buyer bears majority of excess cost).⁶⁵

**Comparison to Employment Agreement Earnouts:**

ASAP differs from typical earnout structures in several critical respects:

1. **Tax Treatment**: ASAP is treated as **deferred purchase price** (capital gain to Seller upon receipt) rather than contingent earnout (potentially ordinary income).⁶⁶

2. **Accounting Treatment**: Under ASC 805, ASAP is recorded as **contingent consideration liability** on Buyer's balance sheet at estimated fair value, with subsequent adjustments to goodwill (rather than P&L).⁶⁷

3. **Single Determination Date**: Unlike multi-year earnouts tied to financial performance, ASAP settles once based on objective ALPA contract terms (reduces dispute risk).

4. **Inverse Correlation**: Unlike typical earnouts where seller earns more if performance improves, ASAP pays Seller more if ALPA costs are *lower* (aligning Seller incentive with Buyer's economic interest).

**CRITICAL DATA GAP ALERT:**

The ASAP structure assumes that increased ALPA costs are **economically absorbed by Buyer** (i.e., margins compress unless operational efficiencies offset labor inflation). However, if the United/Delta/American CPAs contain **cost pass-through provisions** allowing SkyWest to increase rates when pilot wages rise, the economic impact to Buyer may be neutral or minimal.⁶⁸

**URGENT PRE-SIGNING ACTION ITEM**: Within 7-14 days, Buyer's counsel must obtain complete United, Delta, and American CPA contracts to analyze:
- Whether "Rate Adjustment" or "Cost Escalation" clauses permit pass-through of labor cost increases
- Magnitude of permissible adjustments (full cost pass-through vs. capped at CPI or other index)
- Timing of adjustments (contemporaneous with CBA ratification vs. annual true-up)
- Whether adjustments are subject to airline counterparty approval or negotiation

If CPAs provide full cost pass-through, the ASAP structure may be unnecessary (ALPA cost is economically neutral). If CPAs provide zero cost pass-through, Buyer should increase the ASAP cap from $180M to $250M or further reduce base purchase price.⁶⁹

**Indemnification Cap Analysis:**

The $150M cap (5.4% of $2.4B base purchase price) is positioned at the **25th percentile of M&A market standards** (10-25% caps typical for transactions $1B-$5B).⁷¹ The below-market cap reflects:

1. **Layered Risk Allocation**: The $500M CPA escrow and $180M ASAP already address the two largest risk categories; indemnification covers residual/"tail" risks.

2. **Representation Quality**: If Seller's due diligence responses and disclosure schedules are comprehensive and accurate, indemnification claims should be minimal (<$50M aggregate expected value based on identified risk inventory).⁷²

3. **24-Month Survival**: Shorter-than-market survival period (24 months general vs. 18-36 month market norm) reduces Seller's exposure window, justifying lower cap percentage.⁷³

**Basket Analysis:**

The $12M basket (0.5% of base purchase price) represents a **"tipping basket"** (once threshold exceeded, indemnification applies from first dollar) rather than **deductible** (which would exclude first $12M from recovery). Tipping baskets are buyer-favorable and increasingly common in sponsor-backed M&A transactions.⁷⁴

The $12M threshold approximates:
- Aircraft lease assignment cost overruns: Base estimate $8.4M; $12M basket allows $3.6M cushion
- JetBlue settlement: $5M-$15M range; $12M basket excludes claim unless settlement exceeds $12M (unlikely) or combined with other breaches
- Regulatory penalties: $0.4M aggregate (FAA + DOT); de minimis relative to basket
- EEOC individual settlement: $0.13M-$0.25M; de minimis
- Wage-hour violations: $1.5M-$3.1M expected value ($600K-$1.2M probability-weighted); falls below basket unless class action emerges

**Conclusion**: The $12M basket effectively excludes nuisance claims and routine operational exposures, reserving indemnification for material undisclosed liabilities or aggregated breach scenarios.⁷⁵

### E. Material Adverse Effect Definition

The **Material Adverse Effect (MAE)** definition is the most heavily negotiated provision in M&A agreements because it allocates the risk of adverse developments between signing and closing.⁸⁸ For this transaction, the MAE definition must:

1. **Protect Buyer** against United CPA consent denial, prolonged labor strikes, and company-specific operational failures;
2. **Protect Seller** against industry-wide developments (fuel price spikes, recession, regulatory changes affecting all airlines);
3. **Provide Certainty** through specific carved-out categories to minimize post-signing litigation over MAE interpretation.

**Recommended MAE Definition** (refined from Delaware case law precedent):⁸⁹

```
Section 10.1 Material Adverse Effect

"Material Adverse Effect" means any event, change, circumstance, occurrence,
or effect (each, an "Event") that, individually or in the aggregate with all
other Events, has had or would reasonably be expected to have a material
adverse effect on (a) the business, financial condition, assets, liabilities,
or results of operations of the Company and its Subsidiaries, taken as a whole,
or (b) the ability of Seller to consummate the transactions contemplated by
this Agreement; PROVIDED, HOWEVER, that none of the following shall be deemed
to constitute, and none of the following shall be taken into account in
determining whether there has been or would be, a Material Adverse Effect:

(i) Events affecting the United States economy generally, or global economic
    or financial market conditions generally, including changes in interest
    rates, credit markets, or currency exchange rates;

(ii) Events affecting the airline industry generally (including regional
     airlines), including changes in:

     (A) Fuel prices, insurance costs, or other input costs affecting the
         airline industry broadly;
     (B) Passenger demand for air travel (leisure or business);
     (C) Airport access fees, air traffic control charges, or other
         governmental aviation taxes or fees;
     (D) Competitive conditions (e.g., new entrant airlines, capacity
         increases by competitors);

(iii) Changes in law, regulation, or regulatory policy affecting the airline
      industry generally, including:

      (A) FAA regulations regarding aircraft certification, pilot training,
          maintenance requirements, or safety standards applicable to all
          Part 121 air carriers;
      (B) DOT regulations regarding consumer protection, slot allocation,
          or fitness requirements applicable to all certificated carriers;
      (C) EPA or state environmental regulations regarding aircraft
          emissions, noise, or fuel standards;
      (D) Department of Labor, NLRB, or NMB regulations regarding labor
          relations, unionization, or collective bargaining applicable to
          all airlines;

(iv) Changes in GAAP or SAB accounting principles or interpretations thereof;

(v) Acts of terrorism, war (whether or not declared), sabotage, military
    action, cyberattacks, or natural disasters (hurricanes, earthquakes,
    pandemics), except to the extent any such Event has a disproportionate
    impact on the Company compared to other similarly situated regional
    air carriers;

(vi) The announcement or pendency of this Agreement or the transactions
     contemplated hereby, including:

     (A) Loss of employees, customers, suppliers, or business partners
         resulting from announcement of the transaction;
     (B) Compliance with covenants in this Agreement requiring Seller to
         operate the Company in the ordinary course or restricting certain
         actions between signing and closing;
     (C) Actions taken (or omitted) with Buyer's prior written consent;

(vii) Failure to meet financial projections, forecasts, or budgets, PROVIDED
      that the underlying causes of such failure may be considered in
      determining whether an MAE has occurred (if not otherwise excluded
      by subsections (i)-(vi) or (viii)-(x));

(viii) Changes in the Company's stock price or credit ratings, PROVIDED that
       the underlying causes of such changes may be considered in determining
       whether an MAE has occurred (if not otherwise excluded);

(ix) Changes in labor costs resulting from industry-wide pattern bargaining
     or changes in labor laws applicable to the airline industry generally,
     INCLUDING:

     (A) Wage increases for pilots, flight attendants, mechanics, or other
         employee groups resulting from "pattern bargaining" whereby regional
         airline labor unions negotiate agreements based on settlements at
         other regional carriers;

     (B) Changes in federal or state minimum wage laws, overtime regulations
         (e.g., Department of Labor overtime threshold adjustments), or
         employee classification standards (e.g., independent contractor
         vs. employee tests);

     (C) Changes in retirement benefit requirements (e.g., PBGC premium
         increases, pension funding rules, 401(k) match requirements);

     PROVIDED THAT this exclusion shall NOT apply to labor costs resulting
     from events or circumstances unique to the Company or its labor relations
     (e.g., unusually high pilot attrition, adverse NLRB or NMB rulings
     specific to the Company, single-carrier integration with another GAH
     airline resulting from NMB determination);

(x) Changes in capacity purchase agreement rates or terms resulting from
    industry-wide repricing affecting multiple regional carriers (e.g., if
    United, Delta, and American simultaneously renegotiate CPA rates with all
    regional partners due to mainline financial distress or industry
    restructuring), PROVIDED THAT this exclusion shall NOT apply to CPA
    changes specific to the Company (e.g., United terminates Company's CPA
    but maintains CPAs with other regional carriers).

**NOTWITHSTANDING THE FOREGOING**, the exceptions in clauses (i), (ii), (iii),
(v), (ix), and (x) shall not apply to the extent any Event described therein
has a materially disproportionate adverse effect on the Company relative to
other participants in the regional airline industry (the "Disproportionate
Impact Exception"). For purposes of determining disproportionate impact, the
Company shall be compared to regional air carriers operating similar aircraft
types (50-76 seat regional jets) under capacity purchase agreements with
mainline carriers.

**SPECIFIC INCLUSIONS** (events that SHALL be deemed to constitute or contribute
to an MAE notwithstanding any other provision of this definition):

(A) **United Airlines CPA Loss**: If United Airlines, Inc. (i) denies consent
    to assignment of the United Express Agreement, (ii) terminates the United
    Express Agreement (other than due to Buyer's material breach or actions
    taken with Buyer's consent), or (iii) imposes consent conditions that
    violate Section 7.2(a)(iii)(A)-(E), such Event SHALL constitute a Material
    Adverse Effect without regard to any other factor, representation, or
    qualitative determination.

    **Rationale**: United CPA represents 46% of Company revenue ($850M annually;
    $4.4B NPV over remaining term through 2033).⁹⁰ Delaware precedent in
    *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG* establishes that revenue losses
    exceeding 40% of total revenue create an 80-90% probability of MAE
    finding.⁹¹ Including United CPA loss as specific inclusion eliminates
    litigation risk and provides Buyer certainty.

(B) **Pilot Strike Exceeding 30 Days**: If ALPA-represented pilots engage in
    a lawful strike (following NMB release and expiration of 30-day cooling-off
    period under the Railway Labor Act, 45 U.S.C. § 156) that lasts more than
    thirty (30) consecutive days, such Event SHALL constitute a Material
    Adverse Effect.

    **Rationale**: A 30-day strike results in $40M-$45M revenue loss plus
    operational disruption likely triggering CPA termination rights (United/Delta/
    American CPAs typically include force majeure or persistent service failure
    termination provisions).⁹² The 30-day threshold balances: (a) Buyer's need
    for protection against extended strikes, and (b) Seller's argument that
    short-duration strikes (1-3 days) are not material.

(C) **Delta and American CPA Loss (Combined)**: If both (i) Delta Air Lines, Inc.
    denies consent to assignment of the Delta Connection Agreement or terminates
    such agreement, AND (ii) American Airlines, Inc. denies consent to assignment
    of the American Eagle Agreement or terminates such agreement, such combined
    Event SHALL constitute a Material Adverse Effect.

    **Rationale**: Combined Delta + American revenue is $889M annually (48% of
    total).⁹³ Loss of both CPAs simultaneously (10-15% probability scenario)
    would be similarly devastating as United CPA loss alone. However, loss of
    only one (Delta OR American) should NOT automatically constitute MAE
    (allows Seller to argue Company remains viable with United + one other CPA).

(D) **Regulatory Certificate Suspension or Revocation**: If the FAA suspends
    or revokes the Company's Part 119 Air Carrier Certificate, or issues an
    Emergency Order pursuant to 49 U.S.C. § 44709 grounding all or substantially
    all of the Company's fleet for more than fifteen (15) consecutive days,
    such Event SHALL constitute a Material Adverse Effect.

(E) **Airport Slot Forfeiture**: If the Company loses, or is required by the
    FAA to return or transfer, more than twenty percent (20%) of its LaGuardia
    Airport or Reagan National Airport slot portfolio (i.e., >6 LGA slot pairs
    or >5 DCA slot pairs), due to (i) FAA enforcement action alleging slot
    misuse under 14 C.F.R. § 93.227 or § 93.323, or (ii) airport authority
    condemnation or slot reallocation, such Event SHALL constitute a Material
    Adverse Effect.

    **Rationale**: Slot portfolio valued at $304M (10.9% of purchase price).⁹⁴
    Loss of >20% of slots ($60M+ value) materially impairs Company's competitive
    position and route network.

**BURDEN OF PROOF**: Seller shall bear the burden of proving that an Event falls
within one of the exceptions in clauses (i)-(x). Buyer shall bear the burden of
proving that an Event has a "materially disproportionate adverse effect" under
the Disproportionate Impact Exception.

**QUANTITATIVE MATERIALITY THRESHOLD**: For purposes of this definition, an
Event shall be presumed (rebuttably) to have a "material adverse effect" on
the Company's business, financial condition, or results of operations if such
Event causes or would reasonably be expected to cause:

(i) A decline in the Company's EBITDA of more than thirty percent (30%)
    measured on a trailing twelve-month basis compared to the twelve-month
    period ending [DATE] (the "Reference Period"), sustained for a period
    of at least ninety (90) consecutive days; OR

(ii) A decline in the Company's annual revenue of more than twenty percent (20%)
     compared to the Reference Period, sustained for at least ninety (90)
     consecutive days; OR

(iii) Incurrence of liabilities (not reflected on the Company's balance sheet
      as of [DATE]) exceeding Fifty Million Dollars ($50,000,000) in the
      aggregate, excluding liabilities arising from Events described in
      exceptions (i)-(x) above.

**Effect of Presumption**: If Buyer establishes that an Event meets one of the
quantitative thresholds in (i)-(iii), Seller may rebut the presumption by
demonstrating that the Event, when considered qualitatively in context of the
Company's business and industry, does not rise to the level of "material adverse
effect" as that term has been interpreted by the Delaware Court of Chancery.⁹⁵
```

**Critical MAE Definition Features**:

1. **United CPA as Specific Inclusion**: Removes uncertainty; United consent denial = automatic MAE = Buyer walk-away right.⁹⁶

2. **Pattern Bargaining Carve-Out (Clause ix)**: Protects Seller against argument that 30% pilot pay increase (industry-wide pattern) constitutes MAE. BUT disproportionate impact exception allows Buyer to argue MAE if SkyWest's ALPA settlement is materially worse than industry (e.g., 45% increase when peers average 28-30%).⁹⁷

3. **CPA Repricing Exclusion (Clause x)**: If United/Delta/American simultaneously reduce CPA rates for ALL regional carriers due to mainline financial distress, Seller not responsible (industry-wide event). BUT if United selectively reduces SkyWest's rates or terminates SkyWest CPA while maintaining others, Seller IS responsible (company-specific event).⁹⁸

4. **Quantitative Thresholds**: 30% EBITDA decline, 20% revenue decline, or $50M new liability presumption creates rebuttable materiality standard, reducing litigation over subjective "materiality" determinations.⁹⁹

5. **Strike Threshold (30 Days)**: Balances risks. 2-5% probability of strike within 12 months; if strike occurs, historical data suggests 40-60% resolve within 1-15 days, 30-40% extend 30-90 days, 5-10% exceed 90 days.¹⁰⁰ The 30-day threshold allows short strikes to occur without MAE (preserving deal) while protecting Buyer against extended strikes that destroy CPA relationships.

**Negotiation Dynamics**:

Seller will resist the **Specific Inclusions** (clauses A-E), arguing they create "hair-trigger" MAE that removes deal certainty. Seller's counter-proposal will likely:
- Remove United CPA as automatic MAE; force Buyer to prove 46% revenue loss constitutes MAE under general definition
- Increase strike threshold from 30 days to 60 or 90 days
- Remove quantitative thresholds (argue materiality is qualitative, not mechanical)

**Buyer's Response**: Buyer should stand firm on United CPA specific inclusion (non-negotiable) but may compromise on:
- Strike threshold: 45 days (split difference between 30 and 60)
- Quantitative thresholds: Increase to 35% EBITDA / 25% revenue (but resist removing entirely)
- Delta/American combined loss: Remove as specific inclusion (argue under general definition instead)

**Tax and Accounting Impact of MAE Provision**:

If Buyer exercises MAE termination rights, the transaction unwinds:
- Buyer receives return of Deposit ($140M) + reimbursement of transaction expenses (legal, accounting, financing fees) up to negotiated cap ($15M-$25M)
- Neither party pays break-up fee (MAE is a "no-fault" termination right)
- Seller retains Company but must disclose MAE event in subsequent SEC filings if publicly traded (potential stock price impact)
- Buyer loses transaction costs ($20M-$40M sunk costs in due diligence, financing, regulatory filings)

**Strategic Rationale for RWI:**

1. **Seller Benefit — Limited Exposure**: RWI reduces Seller's indemnification exposure from $150M (direct indemnification cap) to effectively $0 after 24-month survival period (Buyer pursues claims against insurance, not Seller). This increases deal certainty for Seller and may justify accepting lower purchase price.¹⁰²

2. **Buyer Benefit — Extended Coverage**: Buyer obtains 6-year coverage (vs. 24-month standard survival) for Fundamental Representations, particularly valuable for:
   - Tax matters (statute of limitations is 3-6 years)
   - CPA contracts (long-term obligations through 2033)
   - ERISA compliance (DOL lookback periods 3-6 years)¹⁰³

3. **Dual-Layer Protection**: Combining $150M RWI policy with $150M seller indemnification cap creates $300M aggregate protection (though seller indemnification terminates at 24 months for general reps).

4. **CPA-Specific Value**: The $100M CPA sublimit supplements the $500M CPA Escrow. If United CPA consent is obtained (escrow released to Seller) but United subsequently terminates CPA due to undisclosed breach (e.g., Seller misrepresented United's satisfaction with performance), Buyer recovers from RWI policy.¹⁰⁴

**RWI Policy Limitations for This Transaction:**

1. **Forward-Looking Risks NOT Covered**: The two largest risks—(a) United CPA consent denial and (b) ALPA contract settlement costs—are forward-looking events occurring post-Closing, NOT breaches of representations. RWI does NOT cover these risks (hence the CPA Escrow and ASAP mechanisms).¹⁰⁵

2. **Labor Cost Inflation**: RWI generally excludes "changes in law or economic conditions affecting insured post-closing." The $162M ALPA Year 1 cost increase is not a breach of Seller's representations (Seller accurately disclosed CBA expiration date and impasse status); it's an anticipated future event. RWI does NOT cover this cost.¹⁰⁶

3. **Premium Cost**: $5.25M premium (3.5% of $150M limit) is a sunk cost borne by Buyer, paid at policy inception. If no claims materialize, Buyer loses the premium.

4. **Retention Erosion**: Every claim reduces the $150M limit. If Buyer makes three $30M claims (totaling $90M in covered losses), the remaining available limit is $60M for subsequent claims.

**RWI vs. Direct Indemnification Comparison:**

| Feature | Seller Indemnification (Section C.3) | RWI Policy |
|---------|-------------------------------------|------------|
| **Coverage Amount** | $150M cap | $150M limit |
| **Survival Period** | 24 months (general reps) | 6 years |
| **Retention/Basket** | $12M tipping basket | $12M retention (SIR) |
| **Claims Process** | Negotiate with Seller; arbitration if dispute | File claim with Insurer; arbitration if denied |
| **Recovery Timing** | 30-90 days (if Seller liquid); years if litigation | 60-120 days typical payout |
| **Seller Exposure** | Up to $150M direct liability | $0 (insurance responds) |
| **Buyer Cost** | $0 (Seller funds escrow) | $5.25M premium |
| **Tax Treatment** | Indemnification payment = purchase price adjustment (reduces capital gain) | Insurance proceeds = tax-free recovery |

**Recommendation**: **Pursue RWI policy as supplement, not substitute, for direct indemnification.** The combined structure (direct indemnification for 24 months + RWI for 6 years) provides Buyer with best protection while allowing Seller to market "limited escrow, no long-tail liability" transaction to its stakeholders.¹⁰⁷
