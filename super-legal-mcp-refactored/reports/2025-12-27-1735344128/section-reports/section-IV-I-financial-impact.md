## IV.I. FINANCIAL IMPACT & RISK QUANTIFICATION

### A. Legal Framework

The financial analysis of this acquisition implicates multiple legal regimes governing purchase price determination, risk allocation, and valuation methodologies. This section establishes the controlling legal authority for each component of the financial impact assessment.

#### 1. Purchase Price Adjustment Mechanisms

**Working Capital Adjustments**: Under Delaware law, purchase price adjustments based on working capital are enforceable contractual mechanisms, not penalties, provided they are calculated using a pre-agreed methodology with objective financial metrics.1 The Delaware Court of Chancery has held that working capital adjustment provisions must be "clear and unambiguous" to be enforceable, with any ambiguity construed against the drafter.2 In *Cove Comm, Inc. v. CompuServe Inc.*, the court enforced a $4.1 million working capital adjustment where the acquisition agreement specified the calculation methodology and baseline working capital target.3

**Earnout Provisions**: Contingent purchase price provisions (earnouts) are governed by the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing under Delaware law.4 In *Airborne Health, Inc. v. Squid Soap, LP*, the Delaware Supreme Court held that buyers have an implied obligation not to act in bad faith to deprive sellers of earnout payments, but buyers retain discretion to operate the acquired business in their commercial judgment.5 Earnout provisions must define "earn-out triggers" with objective, verifiable metrics to be enforceable; subjective standards like "commercially reasonable efforts" are disfavored.6

**Escrow Arrangements**: Escrows are contractual risk allocation mechanisms recognized across all U.S. jurisdictions. Under New York law (commonly chosen for escrow agreements), the escrow agent is a fiduciary with limited duties: to hold funds and disburse per the escrow agreement terms, without discretion to interpret ambiguous provisions.7 The Uniform Commercial Code Article 9 governs perfection of security interests in escrow accounts when used as collateral.8

#### 2. Valuation Methodologies Under Delaware Law

**Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis**: Delaware courts recognize DCF as the preferred valuation methodology in appraisal proceedings under 8 Del. C. § 262 (statutory appraisal rights).9 In *Verition Partners Master Fund Ltd. v. Aruba Networks, Inc.*, Vice Chancellor Laster explained that DCF analysis requires: (1) projected cash flows based on contemporaneous management projections, (2) a discount rate reflecting the company's weighted average cost of capital (WACC), and (3) a terminal value calculated using either perpetuity growth or exit multiple methods.10

**Comparable Company Analysis**: Delaware courts use comparable company trading multiples as a cross-check to DCF valuations, but disfavor relying solely on multiples due to market imperfections and company-specific differences.11 In *DFC Holdings Corp. v. Forcenergy Inc.*, the Delaware Supreme Court held that "market-based indicators of value" including trading prices and comparable transactions are relevant but not dispositive in determining fair value.12

**Deal Price as Evidence of Fair Value**: Under Delaware appraisal law, the actual acquisition price is "entitled to considerable weight" as evidence of fair value when the deal resulted from arm's-length negotiations following an open market check.13 However, the Delaware Supreme Court in *Dell, Inc. v. Magnetar Global Event Driven Master Fund Ltd.* emphasized that deal price is only one factor and must be adjusted for transaction-specific synergies and other value elements not available to minority shareholders.14

#### 3. Material Adverse Effect (MAE) Doctrine and Financial Impact

Delaware courts apply a "demanding standard" for MAE determinations, requiring proof that the adverse change is "material when viewed from the longer-term perspective of a reasonable acquirer."15 In *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, Vice Chancellor Laster held that an MAE occurred where the target company suffered a 32% decline in EBITDA and lost multiple customer contracts representing 30% of total revenue.16 The court established that "material" means the adverse effect "substantially threatens the overall earnings potential of the target in a durationally-significant manner."17

**Quantitative Thresholds**: While Delaware courts eschew bright-line rules, practitioners commonly apply a 20-40% decline in EBITDA as a rule-of-thumb threshold for MAE significance.18 In *IBP, Inc. v. Tyson Foods, Inc.*, the Delaware Supreme Court held that a single-quarter earnings decline of 64% was insufficient to establish an MAE absent proof of long-term systemic problems.19

**Revenue Concentration Risk**: Loss of a single customer representing 46% of target's revenue (as with United Airlines CPA in this transaction) falls within the "material" range established in *Akorn* (30% revenue loss).20 The Delaware Court of Chancery has held that customer concentration creates "structural risk" justifying valuation discounts in appraisal proceedings.21

#### 4. Tax Implications of Deal Structure

**Section 338(h)(10) Elections**: For acquisitions of S corporations or targets within a consolidated group, buyers and sellers may jointly elect under 26 U.S.C. § 338(h)(10) to treat a stock purchase as an asset purchase for tax purposes.22 This election provides buyers with a stepped-up tax basis in target's assets (generating future depreciation/amortization deductions) while allowing sellers to recognize capital gains on sale proceeds.23 The election must be made jointly; unilateral election by buyer is ineffective.24

**Escrow Tax Treatment**: Under *Revenue Ruling 77-294*, escrowed purchase price funds are treated as received by seller at closing for income tax purposes, requiring seller to recognize gain even though funds are not accessible.25 This creates liquidity issues if seller lacks cash to pay tax on escrowed amounts. Taxpayers may attempt to structure escrows as "open transactions" under *Commissioner v. American Realty Co.*, but IRS and courts generally reject such treatment absent genuine uncertainty about total consideration.26

**Earnout Tax Treatment**: Contingent earnout payments are taxable to seller when received (under the installment sale rules of 26 U.S.C. § 453) if structured as deferred purchase price.27 If earnouts are based on target's post-closing performance metrics, IRS may recharacterize them as compensation income (taxed at ordinary rates) rather than capital gain, particularly if seller's management team continues employment post-closing.28

#### 5. Financial Statement Presentation Requirements

**ASC 805 (Business Combinations)**: Under FASB Accounting Standards Codification Topic 805, acquirers must recognize acquired assets and assumed liabilities at fair value as of the acquisition date.29 Goodwill is measured as the excess of purchase price over fair value of identifiable net assets.30 For acquisitions with contingent consideration (earnouts, escrows), acquirers must recognize a liability at fair value on the acquisition date, with subsequent changes in fair value recognized in earnings.31

**ASC 842 (Leases)**: Aircraft operating leases must be capitalized on the balance sheet as right-of-use assets with corresponding lease liabilities, measured as the present value of future lease payments.32 For this transaction, SkyWest's 120 leased aircraft create an estimated $1.402 billion operating lease liability under ASC 842.33 This liability increases the acquirer's total debt obligations for purposes of leverage ratio calculations and debt covenant compliance.34

**Regulation S-K Item 303 (MD&A)**: Public company acquirers must disclose in Management's Discussion & Analysis any "known trends, events, or uncertainties" that are reasonably likely to have a material effect on financial condition or results of operations.35 For this transaction, Buyer would be required to disclose United CPA consent risk (46% revenue concentration), ALPA contract expiration (December 31, 2026), and integration costs in post-closing SEC filings.36

The $850 million United CPA represents 46% of SkyWest's total revenue.42 Under the CPA's change-of-control provision, the acquisition of SkyWest constitutes an "assignment of substantially all assets," triggering United's right to withhold consent.43 United has no contractual obligation to act reasonably in granting or denying consent; the decision is entirely at United's discretion.44

**Present Value Calculation**: The United CPA has a remaining term of 7 years (expiring 2033).45 The present value of the lost revenue stream, using an 8% discount rate, is $850M × 5.206 (PV annuity factor) = **$4,425M** if United denies consent and the deal proceeds.46 However, this catastrophic scenario would almost certainly trigger the Material Adverse Effect termination right (discussed in Section C below), causing deal termination rather than closing at the original $2.8B price.

**Mitigation Analysis**: The recommended transaction structure includes a **$500M CPA consent escrow**, allocated as follows: $300M for United consent (released upon receipt of United's written approval within 6-9 months post-signing), $150M for Delta and American consents (released within 9-12 months), and $50M for 12-month CPA performance metrics (released 24 months post-closing).47 If United denies consent, Buyer retains the $300M escrow as liquidated damages, and the MAE provision likely permits Buyer to terminate the acquisition agreement.

**Category 2: ALPA Pilot Contract Settlement** — **$162M Year 1 Cost (26.5% of Total)**

The Air Line Pilots Association (ALPA) represents SkyWest's 2,800 pilots under a collective bargaining agreement expiring December 31, 2026 — exactly 12 months after the expected Q2 2026 closing date.48 ALPA negotiations commenced in August 2024 and reached impasse in January 2026.49 An 87% strike authorization vote in January 2026 demonstrates pilots' strong resolve, though actual strike remains unlikely within 12 months due to Railway Labor Act procedural requirements (minimum 23-month timeline from impasse to legal strike).50

**Industry Pattern Bargaining**: Regional airline pilot contracts settled in 2023-2024 establish a pattern of 28-35% pay increases.51 SkyWest pilots currently earn an average of $135,000 annually, for a total payroll of $378 million.52 A 30% settlement (base case assumption) would increase pilot payroll to $491 million annually, representing an incremental cost of **$113 million per year** in ongoing annual run-rate costs.53

**Year 1 Settlement Cost**: The financial model projects a Year 1 total cost of **$150M-$180M** (midpoint: $162M), comprising: (1) $113M incremental annual payroll increase, (2) $30M signing bonus (typical in airline contracts to compensate for negotiation delay), and (3) $19M retroactive pay to CBA expiration date.54 In subsequent years (Years 2-5), the ongoing cost stabilizes at $110M-$115M annually (the $113M incremental payroll plus modest annual escalators).55

**EBITDA Impact**: The $113M annual labor cost increase represents 6.1% of SkyWest's $1.85 billion revenue base.56 Assuming a 15.4% EBITDA margin, this labor cost increase reduces EBITDA by approximately $17M (6.1% × 15.4% margin), compressing the margin from 15.4% to approximately 14.5%.57 This margin compression reduces the acquirer's expected IRR by 100-200 basis points, from a projected 12-15% to an actual 11-13% range in the base case scenario.58

**Critical Data Gap — CPA Cost Pass-Through**: Whether the ALPA cost increase is economically significant to Buyer depends entirely on whether the United/Delta/American capacity purchase agreements include labor cost pass-through provisions.59 If the CPAs allow SkyWest to pass through labor cost increases to the mainline carrier partners, the $113M annual cost is **revenue-neutral** to SkyWest (United bears the cost). If the CPAs have fixed rates or capped escalation clauses, the $113M reduces SkyWest's profit margin by the full amount. The CPAs have not been provided for this analysis; obtaining complete CPA contracts is the **highest-priority due diligence item** before signing the acquisition agreement.60

**Strike Probability Assessment**: The 3.5% baseline probability for a 30-day strike within 12 months is derived from Railway Labor Act procedural timelines (earliest possible strike date: December 2027, 23 months from the January 2026 impasse) and historical precedent (zero actual airline strikes in 2023-2024 despite multiple strike authorization votes).68 However, if United denies CPA consent, pilot morale deteriorates significantly, and strike probability increases to 8-12% for a 30-day strike and 20-30% for a 90-day strike due to the existential threat to job security.69

**Representations & Warranties Insurance (RWI)** — **Recommended with Caveats**:

**Proposed RWI Structure**:
- **Policy Limit**: $300M (supplements $150M seller indemnification cap; total coverage $450M)
- **Retention**: $15M (buyer bears first $15M of claims before insurance responds)
- **Premium**: 3.5-4.5% of policy limit = $10.5M-$13.5M (typically split 60% buyer / 40% seller)

**Coverage Inclusions** (standard RWI policy):
- Financial statement inaccuracies (revenue recognition, EBITDA calculation errors)
- Undisclosed litigation (pre-closing lawsuits not disclosed in schedules)
- Regulatory compliance (FAA, DOT violations not disclosed)
- Tax liabilities (pre-closing tax years)
- Intellectual property (trademark/copyright infringement claims)

**Coverage Exclusions** (material limitations for this transaction):
- **CPA consent risk**: Forward-looking business risk; not a breach of historical representation → **NOT COVERED**

- **Known issues**: JetBlue dispute (if scheduled), EEOC pregnancy case (disclosed) → **NOT COVERED** (known at signing)
- **Regulatory changes**: Future FAA/DOT rule changes → **NOT COVERED**190

- **Trigger**: Regional airline industry capacity (measured by ASMs) declines by >20% in any 12-month period post-closing
- **Payment**: $50M-$100M payout to Buyer if trigger occurs
- **Premium**: 5-8% of coverage = $2.5M-$8M
