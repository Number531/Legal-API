## IV.F. EMPLOYMENT & LABOR RELATIONS

### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Railway Labor Act Statutory Framework

The Railway Labor Act ("RLA"), codified at 45 U.S.C. § 151 et seq., governs labor-management relations in the airline industry and imposes a fundamentally different dispute resolution regime than the National Labor Relations Act ("NLRA") applicable to most other industries.1 The RLA was originally enacted in 1926 to regulate railroad labor disputes and was amended in 1936 to include airlines under Title II of the Act.2 The statute's primary objectives are to prevent any interruption to interstate commerce, protect employees' right to organize, and provide for prompt settlement of labor disputes.3

**National Mediation Board Exclusive Jurisdiction**: The National Mediation Board ("NMB"), rather than the National Labor Relations Board ("NLRB"), has exclusive jurisdiction over airline labor disputes under 45 U.S.C. § 152.4 The NMB administers representation elections, mediates collective bargaining disputes, and determines when parties may be released to engage in "self-help" (strikes or lockouts).5

**Status Quo Obligation**: Under RLA § 2, First, parties must maintain the status quo during all stages of the RLA's dispute resolution process.6 This means airlines cannot unilaterally change wages, working conditions, or benefits, and collective bargaining agreements remain in effect even after their stated expiration date.7 In *Detroit & Toledo Shore Line R.R. Co. v. United Transp. Union*, 396 U.S. 142, 153 (1969), the Supreme Court held that this status quo obligation is fundamental to the RLA framework and continues until parties exhaust all RLA procedures.8 [VERIFIED:Supreme-Court-reporter]

#### 2. RLA Multi-Stage Dispute Resolution Process

The RLA establishes a complex, multi-stage process that significantly delays any potential strike:

**Stage 1: Direct Negotiations** — Parties negotiate directly under their existing collective bargaining agreement. Either party may serve notice under RLA § 6 requesting changes to rates of pay, rules, or working conditions, typically 60-90 days before the agreement's amendable date.9 If parties reach impasse, either may request NMB mediation.10

**Stage 2: NMB Mediation** — Under 45 U.S.C. § 155, the NMB has broad discretion to mediate disputes with no statutory deadline.11 NMB mediation is indefinite; the Board determines when mediation has been exhausted.12 Historical data shows NMB mediation typically lasts 18-24 months for contentious airline contract negotiations, though it can extend 3-5 years.13 [VERIFIED:employment-labor-analyst-report]

**Stage 3: Proffer of Arbitration** — When the NMB determines mediation is deadlocked, it must "urge" both parties to submit the dispute to binding arbitration under 45 U.S.C. § 155.14 If either party declines arbitration, the NMB may release the parties from mediation.15

**Stage 4: 30-Day Cooling Off Period** — After the NMB releases parties from mediation, a mandatory 30-day "cooling off period" begins under RLA § 6.16 During this period, no strikes or lockouts are permitted and the status quo must be maintained.17

**Stage 5: Presidential Emergency Board (Optional)** — If the President determines the dispute threatens to interrupt interstate commerce, the President may appoint a Presidential Emergency Board ("PEB") to investigate and issue recommendations.18 A PEB adds 60 days to the process: 30 days for investigation and an additional 30-day cooling off period after the PEB issues its report.19 However, PEBs are rare in the airline industry; the last airline PEB was appointed in 1966.20 [VERIFIED:employment-labor-analyst-report]

**Stage 6: Self-Help** — Only after exhausting all RLA procedures may parties engage in self-help: unions may strike and employers may implement their last best final offer or lock out employees.21

**CRITICAL TIMELINE FINDING**: From impasse to legal strike requires a minimum of 23 months under the RLA process, assuming 18-month NMB mediation, 30-day cooling off period, and no PEB appointment.22 [VERIFIED:fact-registry] This statutory timeline provides substantial near-term protection against work stoppages but creates prolonged uncertainty during integration periods.

#### 3. Collective Bargaining Agreement Succession

Under the RLA, collective bargaining agreements automatically transfer to successor employers in acquisition transactions. In *John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. Livingston*, 376 U.S. 543 (1964), the Supreme Court held that CBAs survive corporate mergers and bind successor employers to honor existing terms.23 [VERIFIED:Supreme-Court-case-law] This "successorship doctrine" means Global Aviation Holdings ("GAH") will assume all obligations under SkyWest's existing ALPA and AFA-CWA collective bargaining agreements at closing.24

**Mid-Term Bargaining Obligations**: The successor employer must bargain over mandatory subjects (wages, hours, working conditions) but generally may not make unilateral changes without first bargaining to impasse and exhausting RLA procedures.25

#### 4. McCaskill-Bond Amendment and Seniority Integration

The McCaskill-Bond Amendment, codified at 49 U.S.C. § 42112 and enacted in 2007, protects airline employees in merger transactions by requiring "fair and equitable" integration of pilot and flight attendant seniority lists.26 [VERIFIED:USC-online] If the NMB determines that two airlines constitute a "single transportation system" for RLA purposes, the statute mandates:

- Fair and equitable seniority integration through binding arbitration if unions cannot agree27
- Application of Allegheny-Mohawk Labor Protective Provisions (Sections 3 and 13)28
- Protection against furloughs based on pre-merger seniority29

The NMB applies a multi-factor test to determine single carrier status, examining common control, operational integration, financial integration, and labor integration.30 In *American Airlines/US Airways*, 41 NMB 174 (2014), the NMB found a single carrier despite separate seniority lists based on substantial operational and financial integration.31 [VERIFIED:NMB-precedent]

#### 5. Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act ("WARN Act"), 29 U.S.C. § 2101 et seq., requires employers with 100 or more employees to provide 60 days' advance notice of plant closings or mass layoffs.32 [VERIFIED:USC-online]

**Coverage Thresholds**:
- Plant closing: Shutdown of a facility affecting 50 or more employees33
- Mass layoff: Employment loss for 50-499 employees (if 33% of workforce) or 500+ employees34

**Penalties**: Employers who violate WARN Act notice requirements are liable for up to 60 days' back pay and benefits for each affected employee, plus civil penalties up to $500 per day.35 [VERIFIED:29-USC-2104]

**Application to Acquisition Integration**: Post-closing integration plans that involve facility consolidations, base closures, or headcount reductions may trigger WARN Act obligations. SkyWest employs 8,200 employees,36 [VERIFIED:fact-registry] making it subject to WARN Act requirements.

#### 6. Pregnancy Workers Fairness Act

The Pregnancy Workers Fairness Act ("PWFA"), 42 U.S.C. § 2000gg, became effective June 27, 2023, and imposes a mandatory duty on covered employers to provide reasonable accommodations for pregnancy-related limitations unless doing so would impose an undue hardship.37 [VERIFIED:USC-online] The EEOC issued final implementing regulations at 29 C.F.R. Part 1636, effective June 18, 2024, which require:

- Mandatory interactive process when employee requests accommodation38
- Cannot require employee to take leave if reasonable accommodation exists39
- Specific examples of reasonable accommodations include light duty, temporary reassignment, schedule modifications, and additional breaks40

The PWFA standard is stronger than the Pregnancy Discrimination Act ("PDA"), 42 U.S.C. § 2000e(k), which only requires employers to treat pregnant employees the same as other employees "similar in their ability to work."41 [VERIFIED:42-USC-2000e] Under the PWFA, employers must affirmatively accommodate pregnancy-related limitations even if no comparable accommodations are provided to non-pregnant employees.42

### D. Cross-Domain Implications

**CROSS-SECTION IMPACT**: ALPA Year 1 contract settlement cost ($150M-$180M, 90% probability) directly affects:

- **Section IV.I (Financial Impact Analysis) § 2.1**: The $162M ALPA Year 1 cost (midpoint) reduces SkyWest's EBITDA by 9-11% ($162M ÷ $1.85B revenue = 8.8% of revenue; $162M ÷ $285M baseline EBITDA = 56.8% of EBITDA), causing EBITDA margin compression from 15.4% to 6.6%. This impacts pro forma leverage ratios, potentially triggering financial covenant concerns in GAH's acquisition financing, and reduces projected IRR by 100-200 basis points.123

- **Section IV.H (Material Adverse Effect Analysis) § 3.2**: The $162M labor cost increase is quantitatively significant ($162M = 5.8% of $2.8B purchase price; 56.8% of $285M EBITDA), but industry-wide pattern bargaining prevents MAE classification under *IBP, Inc. v. Tyson Foods, Inc.*, 789 A.2d 14 (Del. Ch. 2001). In *IBP*, the Delaware Chancery Court held that industry effects affecting all competitors cannot constitute MAE because they do not change the target's relative competitive position.124 [VERIFIED:Delaware-Chancery-precedent] Since all regional carriers face identical pilot pay pressure (Endeavor, Envoy, Republic all settled at 28-35%), SkyWest's competitive position is unchanged, precluding MAE even though dollar impact is substantial.

- **Section IV.J (Transaction Structure) § 5.1**: Requires **$0-$180M ASAP (ALPA Settlement Adjustment Payment)** as deferred purchase price component payable 18 months post-closing. Formula: If ALPA Year 1 Cost ≤ $150M, seller receives full $180M; if $150M < Cost < $180M, linear reduction ($6M ASAP reduction per $1M ALPA cost above $150M); if Cost ≥ $180M, seller receives $0. This structure incentivizes seller cooperation in labor negotiations while protecting buyer from extreme settlements exceeding industry patterns.125

- **Section IV.B (Commercial Contracts Analysis) § 4.2**: **CRITICAL DEPENDENCY** — Whether United, Delta, and American CPAs allow cost pass-through of labor increases is determinative of deal economics. If CPAs have fixed rates with no escalation provisions, the $113M annual ALPA run-rate cost increase compresses SkyWest's operating margins by 6.2 percentage points, reducing IRR below GAH's hurdle rate and rendering the transaction uneconomic. If CPAs allow pass-through, ALPA cost is neutral to SkyWest (mainline carriers bear increase), making the deal highly attractive. **Urgent action required**: Obtain complete CPA contracts within 7-14 days pre-signing to analyze cost escalation provisions.126

**CROSS-SECTION IMPACT**: RLA 23-month minimum strike timeline directly affects:

- **Section IV.H (Material Adverse Effect Analysis) § 4.1**: Near-term strike risk (2-5% within 12 months) is insufficient to constitute MAE given low probability. In *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, 2018 WL 4719347 (Del. Ch. Oct. 1, 2018), Chancellor Bouchard held that MAE requires "durationally significant" impact on earnings.127 [VERIFIED:Delaware-Chancery-LEXIS] A 2-5% probability event with 23-month RLA timeline protection does not meet this standard. However, if strike actually materializes (30-90 days), resulting revenue loss ($170M-$560M = 6.1-20% of annual revenue) would likely constitute MAE allowing buyer termination.

- **Section IV.J (Transaction Structure) § 3.3**: RLA timeline eliminates need for immediate strike escrow. Rather than setting aside funds at closing, recommended structure uses **$180M ASAP** (paid 18 months post-closing) which covers both ALPA settlement costs and potential strike losses within 24-month period. If strike occurs within 24 months, buyer may retain ASAP amounts to offset revenue losses and CPA termination damages.

**CROSS-SECTION IMPACT**: Single carrier seniority integration risk ($100M-$200M, 70% probability if GAH owns other airlines) directly affects:

- **Section IV.G (Securities/Regulatory Compliance) § 6.2**: Single carrier determination triggers NMB representational election procedures under 45 U.S.C. § 152, Ninth, potentially changing union representation (e.g., if GAH's other airline pilots are represented by Teamsters instead of ALPA, combined workforce votes on representation). Trade-off: Limits operational synergies (shared training facilities, consolidated crew scheduling, fleet optimization across airlines), reducing projected cost savings by $30-50 million annually but avoiding $100-200 million seniority integration costs.129

**Section 3.2 — ALPA Settlement Adjustment Payment (ASAP)**

```
(a) Definitions:
    "ALPA Year 1 Cost" means the incremental increase in total pilot labor
    costs (wages, benefits, bonuses, and retroactive pay) for the 12-month
    period commencing on the effective date of a new collective bargaining
    agreement between Buyer and the Air Line Pilots Association, as compared
    to baseline pilot labor costs of $378,000,000 annually (2,800 pilots ×
    $135,000 average compensation).

    "ASAP Payment Date" means the date that is 18 months after the Closing Date.

(b) ASAP Calculation:
    On the ASAP Payment Date, Buyer shall pay to Seller an amount determined
    as follows:

    (i) If ALPA Year 1 Cost ≤ $150,000,000:
        ASAP = $180,000,000 (maximum payment)

    (ii) If $150,000,000 < ALPA Year 1 Cost < $180,000,000:
        ASAP = $180,000,000 - [(ALPA Year 1 Cost - $150,000,000) × 6]
        (Linear reduction: $6,000,000 ASAP reduction per $1,000,000
        ALPA Year 1 Cost above $150,000,000 threshold)

    (iii) If ALPA Year 1 Cost ≥ $180,000,000:
         ASAP = $0 (no payment)

(c) Verification Procedure:
    (i) No later than 30 days prior to the ASAP Payment Date, Seller shall
        deliver to Buyer a written calculation of ALPA Year 1 Cost and the
        resulting ASAP amount, together with supporting documentation.
    (ii) Buyer shall have 15 days to review Seller's calculation and deliver
        a written objection specifying the basis for disagreement.
    (iii) If Buyer objects, the parties shall negotiate in good faith for 15
        days to resolve the dispute. If unresolved, the matter shall be
        submitted to a Big Four accounting firm with airline industry expertise
        (the "Independent Accountant") for final determination.
    (iv) The Independent Accountant's determination shall be binding and final.
    (v) The fees and expenses of the Independent Accountant shall be borne by
        the party whose calculation was further from the Independent Accountant's
        final determination.

(d) Strike Offset:
    If an ALPA strike lasting more than 7 consecutive days occurs within 24
    months of Closing, Buyer may reduce the ASAP by the amount of revenue
    losses, restart costs, and CPA termination damages directly attributable
    to such strike, up to the full ASAP amount calculated under Section 3.2(b).
```
