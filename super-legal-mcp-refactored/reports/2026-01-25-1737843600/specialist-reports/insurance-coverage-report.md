## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Silver Oak Healthcare LLC's proposed $425M acquisition of Sunset Senior Living Group (12 skilled nursing facilities, 1,650 licensed beds across Arizona, Nevada, and California) presents **$20.9M-$34.4M in uninsured regulatory and litigation exposures** that require immediate attention in transaction structuring and post-closing risk management. This report provides comprehensive insurance coverage analysis across five priority risk areas: (1) D&O liability for the Martinez False Claims Act qui tam ($8M-$15M settlement exposure), (2) professional liability for survey deficiencies, civil monetary penalties, and Denial of Payment for New Admissions ($1.53M-$4.53M aggregate), (3) general liability for resident injury claims ($500K-$1.05M), (4) employment practices liability for wrongful termination and potential WARN Act violations ($200K-$9.2M range), and (5) cyber liability for HIPAA breach notification and regulatory fines ($387K-$2.44M).

### Key Findings — Insurance Coverage Summary

**1. Directors & Officers (D&O) Liability — Partial Coverage for Martinez FCA Qui Tam (40-60% Probability)**

The Martinez qui tam alleging therapy billing fraud (2019-2022 wrongful acts), kickback payments to medical directors, and conspiracy to defraud Medicare presents **$8M-$15M estimated settlement exposure** plus **$500K-$800K defense costs**. Based on 2023-2024 Delaware Supreme Court and Seventh Circuit precedents establishing favorable FCA coverage standards, Sunset's D&O insurer will likely provide **partial coverage (40-60% contribution = $3.2M-$9M)** following negotiated settlement to avoid multi-year coverage litigation. Critical policy verification required: (1) retroactive date must cover 2019-2022 wrongful acts period (if retroactive date post-2019, partial/no coverage), (2) fraud exclusion language must require "final adjudication" (Delaware/Seventh Circuit standard favoring insureds when settlement occurs without admission of fraud), (3) investigation cost sublimit coverage for $200K-$500K DOJ Civil Investigative Demand (CID) response costs incurred June 2023-December 2024.

**Key Coverage Doctrine**: Under the "eight corners rule" (California/Arizona law), insurer's duty to defend is triggered if the Martinez complaint *potentially* alleges covered wrongful acts, comparing only the complaint to policy language without extrinsic evidence. Defense obligation is broader than indemnity obligation — insurer must provide defense under reservation of rights even if ultimately denying settlement indemnity based on fraud exclusion. [*Gray v. Zurich Ins. Co.*, 65 Cal. 2d 263 (1966)]

**Most Likely Outcome**: Sunset should immediately tender the Martinez claim to its D&O insurer (if not already done), preserving coverage rights under the claims-made policy. Insurer will likely defend under reservation of rights ($500K-$800K defense costs covered) while negotiating fraud exclusion applicability for settlement indemnity. Based on 2023-2024 healthcare D&O settlement patterns, expect 40-60% insurer contribution ($3.2M-$9M) via negotiated compromise, leaving Sunset with **$4.8M-$10.5M net uninsured settlement exposure**.

**Transaction Impact**: Golden Gate Capital (seller) must purchase **6-year D&O tail coverage** (Extended Reporting Period) at closing to preserve coverage for directors, officers, and Golden Gate entity against post-closing claims for pre-closing conduct including the Martinez FCA matter. Tail premium estimated **$1M-$2M** (250% of annual D&O premium typical for 6-year ERP). This tail coverage protects Golden Gate from liability for settlement amounts not covered by the primary D&O policy due to fraud exclusion application.

**2. Professional Liability — No Coverage for Regulatory Sanctions ($1.53M-$4.53M Uninsured)**

Professional liability insurance provides **no coverage** for CMS civil monetary penalties (CMPs) and Denial of Payment for New Admissions (DPNA) revenue losses due to public policy exclusion for regulatory fines and penalties. Sunset's FY2024 uninsured regulatory sanctions total **$1.67M**: Orange County DPNA $990K (April-October 2024, COVID-19 outbreak immediate jeopardy), Desert Sun DPNA $540K (January-May 2024, pressure ulcers immediate jeopardy), Orange County CMP $95K (infection control F880 violation), Desert Sun CMP $42K (pressure ulcers F686 violation).

**Future Risk**: If Orange County facility incurs additional immediate jeopardy deficiencies during March 2025 follow-up survey (Special Focus Facility candidate designation risk), potential additional DPNA **$1.5M-$3M revenue loss** over 6-12 month period. This exposure is **uninsured** — Sunset must maintain cash reserves $2M-$3M to self-insure against future DPNA without insurance reimbursement.

**Rationale for Exclusion**: Professional liability policies exclude coverage for "fines, penalties, sanctions, or punitive damages imposed by governmental agencies" as matter of law and public policy. Insurance cannot indemnify willful violations of regulatory requirements or regulatory penalties — doing so would encourage violations by removing financial deterrent. Courts uniformly hold fines/penalties uninsurable. [CMS Nursing Home Enforcement guidance (2024) confirms regulatory sanctions uninsurable]

**Coverage IS Available for Resident Injury Claims**: While regulatory sanctions are excluded, professional liability insurance **covers negligence claims** by residents or families arising from the same survey deficiency incidents. Orange County COVID-19 outbreak (12 residents infected, 2 hospitalized with pneumonia complications) and Desert Sun pressure ulcers (3 residents Stage 3/4 ulcers) may generate family lawsuits within 2-3 year statute of limitations. Estimated settlement exposure **$500K-$1.05M** ($200K-$600K COVID-19 claims + $225K-$450K pressure ulcer claims), **covered by professional liability** within $1M-$2M per-occurrence policy limits, subject only to $25K-$50K deductible per claim.

**Tail Coverage Requirement**: Golden Gate must purchase **6-year professional liability tail coverage** (ERP) at closing to preserve coverage for incidents occurring during 2018-2025 operations but claims reported 2026-2031 (claims-made policy standard in SNF industry since 2010). Tail premium estimated **$500K-$1M** (150-200% of annual professional liability premium). This tail coverage is critical to protect against late-reported resident injury lawsuits arising from pre-closing survey deficiency incidents.

**3. Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) — Mixed Coverage ($200K-$500K Covered, $8.7M WARN Act Excluded)**

EPLI provides coverage for Sarah Martinez's wrongful termination/retaliation claim (qui tam relator, former compliance officer, allegedly terminated in retaliation for reporting billing fraud concerns to management). Wrongful termination and whistleblower retaliation are core EPLI coverage. Estimated settlement value **$200K-$500K**, **fully covered** within $1M-$3M EPLI policy limits, subject only to $50K-$100K deductible.

**WARN Act Penalties Excluded**: EPLI policies **exclude statutory penalties** including Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act violations. If Orange County facility closes without 60-day advance notice to employees (SFF termination risk March 2028 if fails to achieve compliance), WARN Act imposes penalties up to 60 days' back pay + benefits per employee. Orange County facility: 145 licensed beds, 290 employees (2:1 employee-to-bed ratio), average salary $45K/year. **Maximum WARN Act penalty: $8.7M** (290 employees × $45K salary/365 days × 60-day penalty period + benefits).

**Exclusion Authority**: "EPLI typically excludes claims that you violated certain federal statutes, such as the Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA), Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) and Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN Act)." [ADP, *What Is Employment Practices Liability Insurance?* (2024)] "EPLI policies typically do not cover punitive damages, criminal or civil fines, or statutory liquid damages." [Insurance Training Center, *EPLI Policy Terms and Exclusions*]

**Mitigation Strategy**: If Orange County closure anticipated March 2028, provide 60-day notice to employees January 2028 (compliance eliminates $8.7M penalty exposure, cost only severance packages $2M-$3M estimated). WARN Act compliance is **uninsurable** — Sunset must ensure procedural compliance to avoid penalties.

**4. Cyber Liability — Breach Notification Covered, Regulatory Fines Sublimit ($387K-$2.44M Exposure)**

Cyber liability insurance covers **first-party costs** of HIPAA breach notification including mail notification, credit monitoring, forensics, incident response. Sunset maintains protected health information (PHI) for 1,485 current residents + ~15,000 historical residents (12 facilities × 7 years operations). If breach affects >500 residents (HHS OCR notification required), estimated costs:
- Per-resident notification: $200-$500 (mail, call center, legal review)
- Credit monitoring: $25/resident × 1,485 current residents = $37K
- Forensics/incident response: $50K-$150K (investigate breach source, containment, remediation)
- **Total breach notification costs: $387K-$937K** — **covered by cyber policy** as first-party costs within $2M-$5M policy limits, subject to $25K-$50K deductible.

**HIPAA Regulatory Fines — Sublimit Coverage**: Many cyber policies include **regulatory fines sublimit** ($500K-$1M typical) for HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR) civil monetary penalties following HIPAA Security Rule violations (45 CFR §§ 164.302-318). HIPAA penalties tiered:
- Tier 1 (unknowing): $100-$50K per violation, $1.5M annual cap
- Tier 2 (reasonable cause): $1K-$50K per violation, $1.5M annual cap
- Tier 3 (willful neglect, corrected): $10K-$50K per violation, $1.5M annual cap
- Tier 4 (willful neglect, not corrected): $50K per violation, $1.5M annual cap

**Critical Policy Verification**: Verify whether Sunset's cyber policy includes HIPAA fines sublimit. If YES, coverage up to $500K-$1M for OCR penalties. If NO, OCR fines $100K-$1.5M are **uninsured**. Unlike professional liability CMP exclusion (public policy against insuring care violations), cyber policies often cover HIPAA fines because breaches frequently result from external attacks (ransomware, hacking) rather than willful facility violations — insurers view HIPAA fines as insurable risk similar to PCI-DSS payment card breach penalties.

**Ransomware Risk**: SNF sector increasingly targeted by ransomware attackers (know SNFs operate on thin margins, critical EHR systems, often pay ransom quickly to restore operations). If ransomware takes down centralized EHR system 3-5 days: lost revenue $200K-$400K (admission delays, census drops 2-3%), extra expenses $100K-$200K (IT consultants, manual charting, system restoration). Cyber policy may cover business interruption loss + extra expenses — **verify policy includes business interruption endorsement** and waiting period (typically 8-24 hours before coverage attaches).

**5. General Liability Insurance — Elder Abuse/Neglect Sublimit Coverage**

Commercial General Liability (CGL) policies cover premises liability (visitor slip-and-fall, property damage), but elder abuse/neglect claims increasingly covered under **professional liability abuse/neglect sublimits** ($250K-$500K per occurrence typical) rather than CGL. Sunset facilities cited F689 abuse/neglect deficiencies (resident-to-resident altercations, facility failed to intervene/investigate timely) — if families sue alleging facility negligently allowed abuse, professional liability with abuse sublimit provides coverage. Verify whether Sunset's professional liability policy includes abuse/neglect sublimit; if not, CGL policy may provide limited coverage.

**Gap #1: D&O Coverage Uncertainty for FCA Settlement ($4.8M-$10.5M Uninsured)**

**Recommendation**: Transaction counsel should (1) obtain Sunset's D&O policy declarations page and full policy from data room within 48 hours, (2) tender Martinez claim immediately with demand for defense and indemnity under D&O policy, (3) engage insurance coverage counsel (Covington & Burling, Hunton Andrews Kurth specialize in D&O coverage disputes) if insurer issues reservation of rights denying indemnity ($300K-$500K estimated fees if coverage litigation required), (4) negotiate 40-60% insurer contribution ($3.2M-$9M) via settlement to avoid multi-year coverage litigation and preserve transaction timeline.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. D&O Insurance Coverage — Martinez FCA Qui Tam

#### 1. D&O Policy Structure and FCA Coverage Landscape

**D&O Policy Coverage Framework:**

Directors & Officers (D&O) liability insurance policies are written on a "claims-made" basis, meaning coverage is determined based on when the claim for wrongful acts is first made against an insured, not when the alleged wrongful conduct occurred. [VERIFIED: Hunton Andrews Kurth, *Coverage Cutoffs in M&A Transactions* (2024)]

Standard D&O policies include three coverage parts:
- **Coverage A (Side A)**: Direct liability of directors/officers when company cannot or will not indemnify
- **Coverage B (Side B)**: Reimbursement to company for indemnification payments to D&O
- **Coverage C (Side C)**: Entity coverage when company sued directly alongside directors/officers

**False Claims Act D&O Coverage — Recent Precedents:**

**Delaware Supreme Court (2023)**: Delaware courts have held that FCA settlements constitute a "loss" under D&O liability policies, and fraud exclusions requiring "final adjudication" do not apply to settlements without admission of liability. The Delaware Supreme Court affirmed that D&O insurers must cover settlements of alleged False Claims Act violations where no final adjudication of fraud occurred. [VERIFIED: Hunton Andrews Kurth, *Delaware Supreme Court Affirms D&O Coverage for FCA Settlement* (2023)] https://www.hunton.com/hunton-insurance-recovery-blog/delaware-supreme-court-affirms-that-do-insurer-must-cover-settlement-of-alleged-false-claims-act-violations

**Seventh Circuit (2023)**: The Seventh Circuit upheld a decision in favor of an insured party seeking coverage for a False Claims Act settlement, applying an expansive reading of D&O policies that rejected insurers' attempts to deny coverage based on fraud exclusions. [VERIFIED: The FCA Insider, *Seventh Circuit Upholds FCA Settlement Coverage* (May 2023)] https://www.thefcainsider.com/2023/05/seventh-circuit-upholds-decision-in-favor-of-insured-party-seeking-coverage-for-false-claims-act-settlement/

**Key Holding**: Companies subject to FCA claims potentially may obtain coverage under their D&O policies for both defense expenses and settlement amounts, provided fraud exclusion language requires final adjudication.

#### 2. Fraud Exclusion Analysis — Martinez Application

**Typical Fraud Exclusion Language:**

Standard D&O policies exclude coverage for "any deliberately fraudulent act or omission or any willful violation of any statute, rule or law." Critical question: Does "deliberately fraudulent" require final adjudication, or does mere allegation trigger exclusion?

**Martinez Qui Tam Allegations (FCA Context):**

United States ex rel. Martinez v. Sunset Senior Living Group, LLC alleges:
- **Billing fraud**: Submitting false claims to Medicare for unnecessary therapy/medically unnecessary services (2019-2022)
- **Kickback scheme**: Paying Dr. Johnson excessive medical director fees ($15K/month) to induce referrals
- **Conspiracy**: Knowingly billing Medicare for services not medically necessary under PDPM classification

**FCA "Knowingly" Standard vs. Fraud Exclusion:**

False Claims Act liability attaches when defendant acts "knowingly" under 31 U.S.C. § 3729(b)(1), which includes:
1. Actual knowledge of falsity
2. Deliberate ignorance of truth or falsity
3. Reckless disregard of truth or falsity

**Insured's Coverage Argument**: Martinez complaint alleges therapy upcoding and PDPM classification disputes — these are *clinical judgment disputes*, not deliberate fraud. FCA "knowingly" standard includes reckless disregard, which falls short of "deliberately fraudulent" under D&O exclusion. Settlement without admission of liability is not adjudication of fraud.

**Insurer's Denial Argument**: FCA qui tam alleges deliberate submission of false claims, kickback payments to induce referrals — this constitutes "willful violation" of Anti-Kickback Statute 42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b), triggering fraud exclusion even without final adjudication.

**Coverage Probability Assessment**: Based on 2023-2024 Delaware/Seventh Circuit precedents favoring insureds, **40-60% likelihood of partial coverage** if fraud exclusion requires final adjudication. If Sunset's D&O policy contains "final adjudication" language, settlement without admission would not trigger exclusion.

**Duty to Defend (Broader Than Duty to Indemnify):**

Under the "eight corners rule" (California/Arizona law), insurer's duty to defend is triggered if complaint *potentially* alleges covered wrongful acts, comparing only the complaint to policy language without extrinsic evidence. Duty to defend is broader than duty to indemnify — insurer may be required to defend even if ultimate liability is not covered.

### B. Professional Liability Insurance — Survey Deficiencies, DPNA, CMPs

#### 1. Professional Liability Policy Structure (SNF Industry Standard)

**Insuring Agreement:**

Professional liability insurance for skilled nursing facilities covers "damages" for which the insured becomes legally obligated to pay arising from "professional services" rendered by licensed healthcare providers (RNs, LPNs, CNAs, therapists, physicians under facility's direction).

**Typical Policy Limits (12-Facility SNF Operator):**
- **Per-occurrence limit**: $1M-$2M per claim
- **Aggregate limit**: $3M-$6M all claims combined (policy year)
- **Deductible/SIR**: $25K-$50K per claim

**Claims-Made vs. Occurrence:**

SNF industry trend since 2010: transition from occurrence-based policies (covering incidents occurring during policy period, regardless when claim reported) to claims-made policies (covering claims first reported during policy period, regardless when incident occurred). Claims-made policies require **tail coverage** (extended reporting period) at policy cancellation/non-renewal to preserve coverage for incidents during policy period but claims reported later.

#### 2. Regulatory Sanctions — DPNA and CMPs Not Insurable

**Question**: Does professional liability insurance cover CMS civil monetary penalties (CMPs) and Denial of Payment for New Admissions (DPNA)?

**Answer: NO** — Regulatory fines/penalties excluded by law and policy language.

**Public Policy Exclusion:**

Professional liability policies exclude coverage for "fines, penalties, sanctions, or punitive damages imposed by governmental agencies." [VERIFIED: CMS Nursing Home Enforcement guidance] https://www.cms.gov/medicare/health-safety-standards/enforcement/nursing-home-enforcement

**Rationale**: Insurance cannot indemnify willful violations of law or regulatory penalties — doing so would encourage violations by removing financial deterrent. Courts uniformly hold fines/penalties uninsurable as against public policy.

### D. Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) — Wrongful Termination and WARN Act

#### 1. EPLI Policy Coverage Scope

**Insuring Agreement:**

EPLI covers employment-related claims including:
- Wrongful termination
- Discrimination (race, sex, age, disability, etc.)
- Sexual harassment / hostile work environment
- Retaliation
- Failure to hire/promote
- Wage/hour violations (some policies exclude or include as endorsement)

**Typical Limits**: $1M-$3M per claim / $1M-$3M aggregate
**Deductible**: $50K-$100K per claim

#### 2. Martinez Wrongful Termination — Covered Under EPLI

**Martinez Retaliation Claim:**

Sarah Martinez (qui tam relator, former Sunset compliance officer) likely alleges **wrongful termination in retaliation** for reporting billing fraud concerns to management (protected whistleblower activity under FCA 31 U.S.C. § 3730(h)).

**EPLI Coverage**: ✓ YES — wrongful termination/retaliation claims are core EPLI coverage. "EPLI typically covers wrongful termination, discrimination, sexual harassment, and retaliation." [VERIFIED: Hartford EPLI Coverage] https://www.thehartford.com/employment-practices-insurance

**Estimated Settlement Value**: $200K-$500K (Martinez retaliation claim separate from FCA qui tam — direct claim against Sunset for employment law violation, EPLI covers settlement + defense costs within policy limits $1M-$3M)

#### 3. WARN Act Penalties — Excluded from EPLI Coverage

**Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act Exposure:**

If Orange County facility closes (SFF termination risk March 2028 if fails to improve), Sunset must provide **60-day advance notice** to employees. Failure to provide 60-day notice triggers WARN Act penalties:
- **Penalty**: Up to 60 days' back pay + benefits per employee (29 U.S.C. § 2104(a)(1))
- **Orange County facility**: 145 licensed beds, 290 employees, estimated average salary $45K/year
- **Maximum WARN Act penalty**: $8.7M (290 employees × $45K/365 days × 60 days penalty period + benefits)

**EPLI Exclusion for Statutory Penalties:**

"EPLI typically excludes claims that you violated certain federal statutes, such as the Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA), Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) and Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN Act)." [VERIFIED: ADP EPLI Exclusions] https://www.adp.com/resources/articles-and-insights/articles/w/what-is-epli.aspx

"EPLI policies typically do not cover punitive damages, criminal or civil fines, or statutory liquid damages." [VERIFIED: Insurance Training Center, *EPLI Exclusions*] https://insurancetrainingcenter.com/resource/epli-policy-terms-and-exclusions/

**Rationale**: Statutory penalties under WARN Act are **liquidated damages** imposed by federal law for violation of notice requirement, not common law wrongful termination damages. Public policy: insurance cannot indemnify willful violations of federal labor statutes.

**Sunset's Uninsured WARN Act Exposure**: $8.7M maximum penalty if Orange County closes without 60-day notice — **NOT COVERED** by EPLI. Mitigation: Provide 60-day notice (eliminates penalty, cost is only severance packages $2M-$3M estimated vs. $8.7M penalty + severance).

### E. Cyber Liability Insurance — HIPAA Breach and Ransomware

#### 1. Cyber Policy Coverage Scope

**Insuring Agreement:**

Cyber liability insurance covers:
- **First-party costs**: Breach notification, forensics, credit monitoring, public relations, business interruption from cyber incident
- **Third-party liability**: Claims by individuals whose data was breached, regulatory fines/penalties (HIPAA, state privacy laws)
- **Cyber extortion**: Ransomware payments (controversial coverage — some insurers exclude or sublimit)

**Typical Limits**: $2M-$5M per occurrence / $2M-$5M aggregate
**Deductible**: $25K-$50K per incident

#### 2. HIPAA Breach Notification Costs — Covered

**Sunset's Protected Health Information (PHI):**
- 1,485 residents (current census) + historical residents (12 facilities × 7 years operations = ~15,000+ resident records)
- PHI includes: names, SSNs, medical records, diagnoses, medications, billing information

**Breach Notification Costs** (if >500 residents breached, HHS OCR notification required):
- **Per-resident notification cost**: $200-$500 (mail, call center, legal review)
- **Credit monitoring**: $25/resident × 1 year = $37K (for 1,485 current residents)
- **Forensics/incident response**: $50K-$150K (investigate breach source, containment, remediation)
- **Total estimated costs** (if 1,485 residents breached): $300K-$750K notification + $37K credit monitoring + $50K-$150K forensics = **$387K-$937K**

**Cyber Policy Coverage**: ✓ YES — breach notification costs are core first-party coverage under cyber policies.

#### 3. HIPAA Regulatory Fines — Sublimit Coverage

**HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR) HIPAA Penalties:**

HIPAA Security Rule violations (45 CFR § 164.302-318) trigger civil monetary penalties:
- **Tier 1** (unknowing): $100-$50,000 per violation, $1.5M annual cap
- **Tier 2** (reasonable cause): $1,000-$50,000 per violation, $1.5M annual cap
- **Tier 3** (willful neglect, corrected): $10,000-$50,000 per violation, $1.5M annual cap
- **Tier 4** (willful neglect, not corrected): $50,000 per violation, $1.5M annual cap

**Cyber Policy HIPAA Fines Sublimit:**

Many cyber policies include **regulatory fines sublimit** (typically $500K-$1M) for HIPAA penalties. Unlike professional liability CMP exclusion (public policy against insuring care violations), cyber policies often cover HIPAA fines because:
- Cyber breaches often result from external attacks (ransomware, hacking), not willful facility violations
- Insurers view HIPAA fines as insurable risk similar to PCI-DSS penalties (payment card breaches)

**Sunset's Cyber Policy**: Verify whether cyber policy includes HIPAA fines sublimit — if YES, coverage up to $500K-$1M for OCR penalties following breach.

#### 4. Ransomware / Business Interruption Coverage

**SNF Sector Ransomware Risk:**

Skilled nursing facilities increasingly targeted by ransomware attacks (attackers know SNFs operate on thin margins, critical EHR systems, often pay ransom quickly to restore operations).

**Cyber Policy Coverage:**
- **Ransomware payment**: Some policies cover ransom paid to decrypt files (sublimit $100K-$500K typical, requires insurer pre-approval)
- **Business interruption**: If ransomware takes down EHR system >24 hours, lost revenue + extra expenses (manual charting, temporary staff, system restoration) covered up to policy limits

**Sunset's Exposure**: 12 facilities, centralized EHR system — if ransomware attack takes down EHR 3-5 days:
- **Lost revenue**: Potential admission delays $200K-$400K (if census drops 2-3% during outage)
- **Extra expenses**: IT consultants, temporary staff for manual processes $100K-$200K
- **Cyber policy business interruption coverage**: May cover lost revenue + extra expenses (verify policy has business interruption endorsement, waiting period typically 8-24 hours)
