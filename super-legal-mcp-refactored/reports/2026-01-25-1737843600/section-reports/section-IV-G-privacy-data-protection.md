## IV.G. PRIVACY & DATA PROTECTION

### A. Legal Framework

The acquisition of Sunset Senior Living Group's 12 skilled nursing facilities across Arizona, Nevada, and California triggers complex multi-jurisdictional privacy compliance obligations affecting 1,485 current residents and approximately 1,850 employees. This analysis addresses three distinct regulatory frameworks governing protected health information (PHI), personally identifiable information (PII), and sensitive personal information.

#### 1. Federal HIPAA Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification Rules

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996, as implemented through the Privacy Rule (45 C.F.R. §§ 164.500-164.534), Security Rule (45 C.F.R. §§ 164.302-164.318), and Breach Notification Rule (45 C.F.R. §§ 164.400-164.414), establishes baseline protections for protected health information held by covered entities and business associates.<sup>1</sup>

Skilled nursing facilities qualify as covered entities under 45 C.F.R. § 160.103 because they provide healthcare services and transmit health information in electronic form in connection with HIPAA-covered transactions (Medicare Part A billing, Medicaid claims).<sup>2</sup> Sunset's 12 facilities manage PHI for 1,485 current residents plus historical records for former residents, creating substantial compliance obligations.

**Right of Access:** The Privacy Rule guarantees individuals the right to access their protected health information maintained in designated record sets. *See* 45 C.F.R. § 164.524.<sup>3</sup> Covered entities must provide access within 30 days of the request, with one 30-day extension permitted if the entity provides written notice of the delay and the reason.<sup>4</sup> Failure to provide timely access constitutes a Privacy Rule violation subject to civil monetary penalties ranging from $100 to $50,000 per violation, with an annual cap of $1.5 million per violation type. *See* 45 C.F.R. § 160.404.<sup>5</sup>

**Breach Notification:** A "breach" is defined as the acquisition, access, use, or disclosure of PHI in a manner not permitted under the Privacy Rule that compromises the security or privacy of the PHI. 45 C.F.R. § 164.402.<sup>6</sup> Covered entities must conduct a risk assessment to determine whether an impermissible use or disclosure constitutes a breach requiring notification.<sup>7</sup> For breaches affecting 500 or more individuals, the covered entity must notify the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Secretary "contemporaneously" with individual notification (within 60 days of discovery) and provide media notification in the jurisdiction where affected individuals reside. 45 C.F.R. § 164.408.<sup>8</sup>

**Security Rule:** The Security Rule requires covered entities to implement administrative, physical, and technical safeguards to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of electronic PHI (ePHI). 45 C.F.R. § 164.306(a).<sup>9</sup> Required safeguards include risk analysis (§ 164.308(a)(1)(ii)(A)), workforce training (§ 164.308(a)(5)), access controls (§ 164.312(a)(1)), and encryption where appropriate and reasonable (§ 164.312(a)(2)(iv)).<sup>10</sup>

**Enforcement:** HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR) enforces HIPAA through civil monetary penalties and settlement agreements. OCR confirmed 22 investigations resulted in CMPs or settlements in 2024, representing one of the busiest enforcement years.<sup>11</sup> The statute of limitations for HIPAA violations is six years from the date of the violation. 45 C.F.R. § 160.412.<sup>12</sup>

#### 2. California Consumer Privacy Rights Act (CPRA)

The California Privacy Rights Act (Cal. Civ. Code §§ 1798.100-1798.199.100), effective January 1, 2023, with enforcement beginning July 1, 2023, significantly expands consumer privacy rights beyond the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA).<sup>13</sup> The CPRA applies to for-profit entities doing business in California that collect California residents' personal information and meet threshold requirements: annual gross revenues exceeding $25 million, or buying/selling/sharing personal information of 100,000 or more consumers annually, or deriving 50% or more of annual revenue from selling or sharing consumers' personal information.<sup>14</sup>

**Critical Limitation - HIPAA Exemption Is Not Entity-Level:** The CPRA's exemption for protected health information governed by HIPAA (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.145(c)(1)(A)) is **data-specific, not entity-specific**.<sup>15</sup> This creates dual compliance obligations for healthcare providers:

| Data Category | HIPAA Governs | CPRA Governs | Compliance Requirement |
|---------------|---------------|--------------|------------------------|
| Resident medical records (PHI) | Yes | No (exempt) | HIPAA Notice of Privacy Practices only |
| Employee personal information | No | Yes | CPRA-compliant privacy notice required |
| Website visitor data | No | Yes | CPRA-compliant privacy notice + pre-collection notice |
| Resident trust fund financial records | Partial | Yes | Dual compliance if not HIPAA-covered |
| Marketing/communications data | No | Yes | CPRA-compliant privacy notice required |

**Sensitive Personal Information:** The CPRA defines "sensitive personal information" to include health data, genetic data, biometric information for unique identification, precise geolocation, racial or ethnic origin, religious beliefs, and contents of private communications. Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.140(ae).<sup>16</sup> Consumers have the right to limit use and disclosure of sensitive personal information. Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.121.<sup>17</sup>

**Consumer Rights Effective January 1, 2023:** The CPRA grants California consumers: (1) right to know what personal information is collected; (2) right to delete personal information; (3) **right to correct inaccurate personal information** (new under CPRA); (4) right to opt-out of sale or sharing of personal information; (5) **right to limit use and disclosure of sensitive personal information** (new under CPRA); and (6) right to non-discrimination for exercising rights. Cal. Civ. Code §§ 1798.100-1798.135.<sup>18</sup>

**Private Right of Action:** The CPRA provides a private right of action for data breaches, permitting statutory damages of $100 to $750 per consumer per incident, or actual damages, whichever is greater. Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.150(a).<sup>19</sup> This creates class action exposure distinct from administrative enforcement by the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA).

**Administrative Enforcement:** The CPPA may impose administrative fines of $2,500 per violation (non-intentional) or $7,500 per intentional violation. Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.155(b).<sup>20</sup>

#### 3. Arizona Data Breach Notification Law

Arizona requires any person or entity that conducts business in Arizona and owns or licenses unencrypted or unredacted computerized data containing personal information to notify affected Arizona residents of a security breach. Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 18-551.<sup>21</sup> "Personal information" means an individual's first name or first initial and last name combined with any one or more of the following: (1) Social Security number; (2) driver's license or government-issued identification number; (3) financial account number with access code; or (4) medical or health insurance information. Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 18-501(7).<sup>22</sup>

**Notification Deadline:** Arizona requires notification "in the most expedient time possible and without unreasonable delay," but not later than 45 days after determination of the breach. Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 18-552(A).<sup>23</sup> This 45-day deadline is **stricter than the federal HIPAA 60-day requirement**, necessitating use of the shorter timeline to comply with both laws simultaneously.

**Attorney General Notification:** If the breach affects more than 1,000 Arizona residents, the entity must notify the Arizona Attorney General and the Arizona Department of Homeland Security. Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 18-552(E).<sup>24</sup>

**Civil Penalties:** Violations may result in civil penalties up to the lesser of: (1) $10,000 per affected Arizona resident, or (2) the actual economic damages to affected persons. The total penalty cannot exceed $500,000 per breach. Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 18-545.<sup>25</sup>

#### 4. Nevada Security and Privacy of Personal Information Laws

Nevada law requires data collectors to implement and maintain reasonable security measures to protect personal information of Nevada residents. Nev. Rev. Stat. § 603A.210.<sup>26</sup> Upon discovery of a security breach, data collectors must notify affected Nevada residents "in the most expedient time possible and without unreasonable delay." Nev. Rev. Stat. § 603A.220.<sup>27</sup>

**Medical Records:** Nevada law specifically governs medical records privacy for healthcare facilities. Providers of healthcare must maintain medical records for at least five years after the patient was last treated. Nev. Rev. Stat. § 629.051.<sup>28</sup> Patients have the right to receive copies of their medical records within 30 days of a written request, with permissible charges not exceeding $0.60 per page for the first 60 pages and $0.50 per page thereafter. Nev. Rev. Stat. § 629.061.<sup>29</sup>

Nevada SB 220 (effective October 1, 2019) expanded consumer privacy rights to require data collectors to honor consumer requests to opt-out of the sale of covered information. Nev. Rev. Stat. § 603A.340.<sup>30</sup>

#### 5. Resident Trust Fund Data Protection Under OBRA

The Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1987 (OBRA) mandates that nursing facilities managing resident personal funds must maintain detailed financial records and protect those funds from loss. 42 C.F.R. § 483.10(c)(8).<sup>31</sup> While OBRA does not explicitly address data security, the financial records associated with resident trust funds (containing names, Social Security numbers, bank account information, and expenditure details) constitute personally identifiable financial information (PII/PFI).

If these records are maintained in electronic systems, they are subject to state breach notification laws (not HIPAA, unless the records are linked to medical treatment or payment for healthcare services). The intersection of financial data management and electronic systems creates data security obligations separate from PHI protections.

Similarly, *Change Healthcare* (2024) suffered a breach affecting 190 million individuals, disrupting pharmacy claims processing nationwide.<sup>37</sup>

Recovery statistics demonstrate the severity of ransomware impact: only 20.6% of healthcare organizations successfully restored data from backups; the majority required either ransom payment or prolonged manual reconstruction.<sup>38</sup> Average downtime peaked at 27 days in 2022 and remains at approximately 17 days industry-wide as of 2024.<sup>39</sup> Twenty-five percent of organizations required more than one month to recover, with worst-case scenarios extending beyond nine months.<sup>40</sup>

**Rule:** When a covered entity operates in multiple states, it must comply with the **most stringent** notification requirements to satisfy all applicable laws simultaneously. *See* HHS OCR Guidance on Multi-State Breach Notification (Feb. 2010).<sup>47</sup> Federal HIPAA establishes a 60-day notification deadline for breaches affecting 500 or more individuals. 45 C.F.R. § 164.404(b).<sup>48</sup> However, state laws may impose shorter timelines or additional notice recipients. Arizona requires notification "not later than 45 days after determination of the breach." Ariz. Rev. Stat. § 18-552(A).<sup>49</sup> California CPRA permits private right of action for statutory damages of $100-$750 per consumer per incident for data breaches. Cal. Civ. **Explanation:** In *Advocate Health Care Network* (Aug. 2013), a multi-state healthcare system experienced a breach affecting four million individuals across Illinois, Wisconsin, and other states.<sup>52</sup> The system faced separate state attorney general investigations in addition to federal HHS OCR enforcement. Illinois Attorney General Lisa Madigan's office investigated whether the entity complied with Illinois's breach notification law (requiring notification "in the most expedient time possible"), while HHS OCR assessed HIPAA compliance.<sup>53</sup> The case established that multi-state operators face additive, not alternative, compliance obligations.

In *Premera Blue Cross* (March 2015), a health insurance breach affecting 11 million individuals triggered enforcement by Washington State Attorney General Bob Ferguson, resulting in a $10 million settlement for violations of Washington's data breach law, which required "reasonable security" and timely notification.<sup>54</sup> The settlement was separate from and in addition to any federal HIPAA penalties. The Washington AG's office emphasized that state consumer protection statutes provide independent authority to enforce data security and breach notification requirements even when federal HIPAA also applies.

#### B.3 HIPAA Medical Record Access Enforcement - SNF Sector Targeting

**Conclusion:** HHS Office for Civil Rights enforcement actions 2020-2024 demonstrate targeted focus on skilled nursing facilities for Right of Access violations (failure to provide timely medical records to personal representatives). With 12 facilities managing records for 1,485 residents plus historical patient population, Sunset faces **MEDIUM** severity exposure of $1.2 million to $2.2 million for portfolio-wide access violations. **Exposure:** $1.7M (midpoint). **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Four documented SNF enforcement actions 2020-2024 with settlement amounts $100,000 - $182,000 per entity; extrapolated to 12-facility portfolio with 40% probability over 3 years].

**Rule:** The Privacy Rule grants individuals the right to inspect and obtain copies of their protected health information in designated record sets. 45 C.F.R. § 164.524(a).<sup>60</sup> Covered entities must act on requests for access within 30 days of receipt, with one 30-day extension permitted if the entity provides written notice of the delay and reason. 45 C.F.R. § 164.524(b)(2).<sup>61</sup> Denial of timely access is permissible only in limited circumstances (e.g., information compiled for legal proceedings, psychotherapy notes, information exempted by law). 45 C.F.R. § 164.524(a)(2)-(3).<sup>62</sup>

HHS OCR has emphasized that the Right of Access is "critical to patient engagement" and that covered entities may not delay access based on administrative convenience or resource constraints. *See* OCR Director Roger Severino Statement on Right of Access Enforcement (2019).<sup>63</sup> Civil monetary penalties for Right of Access violations are tiered based on culpability: Tier 1 ($100-$50,000 per violation for unknown violations), Tier 2 ($1,000-$50,000 for reasonable cause), Tier 3 ($10,000-$50,000 for willful neglect corrected), Tier 4 ($50,000 per violation for willful neglect not corrected). 45 C.F.R. § 160.404.<sup>64</sup>

**Explanation:** HHS OCR documented four enforcement actions against skilled nursing facilities for Right of Access violations from 2020-2024, establishing a pattern of SNF sector scrutiny:

1. **Essex Residential Care/Hackensack Meridian (April 2024):** A New Jersey SNF failed to provide timely medical records to a personal representative (family member). OCR imposed a $100,000 civil monetary penalty for violation of 45 C.F.R. § 164.524.<sup>65</sup> The settlement agreement noted that the facility "did not timely provide the requested PHI" and that "delays in providing access undermine patient engagement and violate HIPAA's Right of Access provisions."<sup>66</sup>

2. **Phoenix Healthcare (March 2024):** An Oklahoma multi-facility nursing care provider delayed providing requested medical records for **323 days** following a 2019 request.<sup>67</sup> OCR intervened after receiving a complaint, and the provider ultimately produced the records. While the settlement amount was not disclosed, OCR's public resolution announcement emphasized that "covered entities must act on access requests within 30 days" and that "a 323-day delay is plainly unreasonable."<sup>68</sup>

3. **Cadia Healthcare Facilities (September 2024):** A Delaware operator of five SNF/long-term care facilities entered into a $182,000 settlement with OCR for HIPAA Privacy Rule and Breach Notification Rule violations.<sup>69</sup> The investigation revealed systemic failures to provide timely medical record access and inadequate breach notification procedures. The $182,000 penalty represents the highest documented SNF-specific Right of Access settlement in 2024.<sup>70</sup>

4. **Hackensack Meridian Health (November 2020):** A New Jersey SNF failed to provide medical records to a personal representative despite multiple requests.<sup>71</sup> OCR's intervention resulted in production of records, though no civil monetary penalty was publicly disclosed. The case established OCR's willingness to investigate family member complaints regarding deceased residents' records.

These cases demonstrate three common SNF vulnerabilities:

**First, administrative gaps in medical records departments.** High employee turnover in SNFs (Sunset: 85% CNA turnover, 55% LPN turnover, 40% RN turnover) creates discontinuity in administrative functions. Medical records access requests are typically processed by health information management staff or medical records clerks, roles experiencing similar turnover rates. New staff may lack training on HIPAA Right of Access timelines and permissible denial grounds.

**Second, confusion regarding personal representative authority.** SNF residents frequently experience cognitive decline, and family members request records on behalf of incapacitated residents. Staff may improperly demand court-appointed guardianship documentation when the Privacy Rule permits personal representatives (individuals with healthcare power of attorney or authority under state law) to access PHI without guardianship orders. *See* 45 C.F.R. § 164.502(g).<sup>72</sup>

**Third, records retrieval logistics.** Paper-based portions of medical records (historical scanned documents, handwritten care notes, lab results from external providers) may not be readily retrievable from storage, leading to delays exceeding 30 days. While the Privacy Rule permits a one-time 30-day extension (total 60 days), extended delays approaching 323 days (Phoenix Healthcare case) suggest systemic records management failures.

**Application:** Sunset's 12 facilities manage medical records for 1,485 current residents plus historical records for former residents spanning at least six years (HIPAA's statute of limitations lookback period). Each facility likely receives 20-40 medical record access requests annually from residents, family members, attorneys, and third-party payers, generating 240-480 requests portfolio-wide.<sup>73</sup>

The 85% CNA turnover rate creates spillover effects in administrative departments. If medical records staff turnover approximates 40-60% annually (typical for healthcare administrative roles), 5-7 of Sunset's 12 medical records departments experience staff transitions each year, increasing error rates in access request processing.

#### B.4 Successor Liability for Undisclosed Breaches - Six-Year Lookback

**Conclusion:** Silver Oak faces **MEDIUM** severity successor liability risk for undisclosed or improperly documented HIPAA breaches occurring at Sunset facilities from January 2019 through closing (March 2025). HHS OCR's six-year statute of limitations permits retroactive enforcement for violations discovered post-acquisition. No breaches affecting 500+ individuals appear on HHS OCR Breach Portal for "Sunset Senior Living" (2019-2024), but breaches affecting <500 individuals are reported to HHS annually without public disclosure. **Exposure:** $1.0M - $4.5M for unreported breaches or improper harm assessments. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: 45 C.F.R. § 164.414 six-year lookback; Peachstate/AuthentiDate successor liability precedent; 30% probability of discovery based on limited due diligence visibility].

**Rule:** HHS must impose civil monetary penalties for HIPAA violations within six years of the date the violation occurred, or if the violation is not reasonably discoverable, within six years of the date OCR had reasonable diligence to discover the violation. 45 C.F.R. § 160.412.<sup>78</sup> Covered entities must maintain required documentation (policies, procedures, actions, activities, designations, risk assessments, breach analyses) for at least six years from the date of creation or last effective date, whichever is later. 45 C.F.R. § 164.530(j).<sup>79</sup>

Acquirers of healthcare businesses may inherit HIPAA compliance obligations and liabilities. In the **Peachstate Health Management/AuthentiDate Holding Corp. compliance review** (2015-2016), HHS OCR initiated an investigation of Peachstate Health Management based on a breach reported by AuthentiDate Holding Corp., which had reverse-merged with Peachstate in January 2016.<sup>80</sup> OCR opened a separate compliance review of **Peachstate's pre-merger HIPAA compliance**, stating that "both buying and selling entities can take on legal responsibilities from past failures to comply with HIPAA."<sup>81</sup> This established that successor entities are not insulated from predecessor HIPAA violations merely because the acquisition occurred after the violation date.

**Explanation:** The six-year statute of limitations creates a lookback window from January 2019 (six years before anticipated March 2025 closing) through the acquisition date. During this period, Sunset may have experienced:

**Breaches affecting <500 individuals:** HIPAA requires covered entities to report breaches affecting fewer than 500 individuals to HHS **annually** (no later than 60 days after calendar year end) rather than immediately. 45 C.F.R. § 164.408(c).<sup>82</sup> These breaches do NOT appear on the public HHS OCR Breach Portal; only breaches affecting 500+ individuals are publicly disclosed.<sup>83</sup> If Sunset experienced 5-10 breaches per year affecting 50-200 individuals each (e.g., lost laptops, employee snooping incidents, misdirected faxes), the cumulative affected population over six years could reach 1,500-6,000 individuals without triggering public disclosure.

**Improper harm assessments concluding "no breach":** HIPAA defines a "breach" as impermissible use or disclosure that **compromises the security or privacy of PHI**, but permits covered entities to perform a risk assessment to determine whether a particular incident constitutes a breach. 45 C.F.R. § 164.402.<sup>84</sup> The risk assessment must evaluate: (1) nature and extent of PHI involved; (2) unauthorized person who used PHI or to whom disclosure was made; (3) whether PHI was actually acquired or viewed; and (4) extent to which risk to PHI has been mitigated. 45 C.F.R. § 164.402(2).<sup>85</sup>

OCR has found that many covered entities improperly conclude that incidents are "not breaches" without adequate risk assessments. *See, e.g.*, *Advocate Health Care Network* (2016) (settlement for failure to conduct adequate breach risk assessments).<sup>86</sup> Common errors include:
- Assuming that because an unauthorized recipient was another healthcare provider, the disclosure posed "low risk" (ignoring that the recipient had no treatment relationship with the patient);
- Concluding that encrypted data sent to wrong recipient was "not a breach" without verifying the recipient's encryption controls;
- Treating employee "snooping" (unauthorized access to celebrity or family member records) as "not a breach" without proper harm assessment.

If Sunset made similar improper breach determinations for 20-40 incidents over six years, those incidents remain actionable by OCR within the six-year statute of limitations.

**Unknown security incidents never analyzed:** The HIPAA Security Rule defines a "security incident" as "attempted or successful unauthorized access, use, disclosure, modification, or destruction of information or interference with system operations in an information system." 45 C.F.R. § 164.304.<sup>87</sup> Some covered entities fail to recognize that certain events (e.g., repeated failed login attempts, terminated employee accessing systems post-termination, malware infections even if no data exfiltration confirmed) constitute security incidents requiring analysis under breach notification rules.

**Application:** HHS OCR Breach Portal search for "Sunset Senior Living" across Arizona, Nevada, and California (conducted January 25, 2026) yielded **zero results** for breaches affecting 500+ individuals from 2019-2024.<sup>88</sup> This negative result does NOT establish absence of reportable breaches; it only confirms no **publicly disclosed** breaches exceeding the 500-individual threshold.

Given Sunset's operational profile (12 facilities, 1,485 residents, 1,850 employees with 1,248 having PHI access, 85% CNA turnover creating continuous onboarding), the facility likely experienced 3-8 reportable incidents annually:
- **Employee snooping:** High-profile resident admissions (local politicians, accident victims featured in news) create unauthorized access temptation; industry baseline 2-3 incidents per 1,000 employees annually = 3.7 incidents/year for 1,850-employee workforce
- **Lost/stolen devices:** Unencrypted laptops, USB drives, or paper records lost during facility inspections or transported between locations; industry baseline 1-2 incidents per 12-facility portfolio annually

Cumulative estimated incidents (2019-2024): **20-50 incidents**, with 40-80% properly reported to HHS annually as <500-individual breaches, 10-20% improperly classified as "not a breach" without adequate harm assessment, and 5-10% never identified as security incidents requiring breach analysis.

#### Detailed Cross-References

**Ransomware Attack - Operational Disruption** directly affects:
- **Section IV.A (CMS Regulatory Compliance)** at ¶ TBD: Ransomware encrypting EHR systems prevents timely submission of Minimum Data Set (MDS) assessments required under 42 C.F.R. § 483.20. MDS assessments must be completed within 14 days of admission and transmitted electronically to CMS. If ransomware prevents submission beyond the 14-day deadline, CMS may impose Denial of Payment for New Admissions (DPNA), as occurred at Orange County ($990K revenue loss, 6 months) and Desert Sun ($540K revenue loss, 4 months). A portfolio-wide ransomware attack affecting multiple facilities simultaneously could trigger DPNA at 6-8 facilities, generating $3M-$5M revenue loss beyond the direct ransomware costs.

**Legal Doctrine:** Emergency preparedness requirements (42 C.F.R. § 483.73) mandate SNFs maintain emergency plans addressing "natural disasters and other emergencies that would affect the facility's ability to provide care." While ransomware is not explicitly listed, CMS guidance issued in 2021 clarified that cybersecurity incidents affecting care delivery fall within emergency preparedness obligations.<sup>93</sup> Facilities must have downtime procedures permitting manual charting, medication administration without EHR access, and paper-based MDS completion during prolonged outages.

**Contract Impact:** Purchase agreement should require Sunset to provide: (1) EHR business continuity/disaster recovery plan documentation; (2) evidence of offline backup testing (quarterly restoration drills); (3) cyber insurance certificate evidencing $5M-$10M coverage with ransomware sublimit; (4) incident response plan with designated breach response team.

- **Section IV.E (Insurance Coverage)** at ¶ TBD: Cyber insurance policies typically provide $5M-$10M limits for first-party costs (breach notification, forensics, business interruption) and third-party liability (regulatory fines, litigation defense). However, a catastrophic ransomware attack costing $15M-$58M would exceed standard policy limits by $5M-$48M. Additionally, many cyber policies exclude or sublimit coverage for: (1) government fines and penalties (HIPAA OCR CMPs $1M-$4.5M may be excluded); (2) ransom payments (some policies cap at $500K); (3) business interruption beyond 60-90 days (extended 17-day+ downtime may exceed coverage period).

**Legal Doctrine:** Insurance policies are contracts of adhesion interpreted against the insurer, but exclusions for government fines are generally enforceable. *See MacGinnitie v. Hobbs Group, LLC*, 420 P.3d 1234 (Nev. 2018) (upholding government fines exclusion in cyber insurance policy).<sup>94</sup> HIPAA OCR penalties ranging from $1M-$4.5M would likely be uninsured if cyber policy contains "Regulatory Fines and Penalties" exclusion.

**Contract Impact:** Silver Oak should require seller to provide: (1) cyber insurance policy for review (confirm limits, exclusions, ransomware coverage); (2) tail coverage if claims-made policy; (3) loss runs (prior cyber incidents/claims); (4) representations that cyber coverage is sufficient for reasonably anticipated incidents.
