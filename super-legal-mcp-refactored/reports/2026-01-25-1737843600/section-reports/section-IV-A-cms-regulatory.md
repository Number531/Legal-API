### A. Legal Framework

#### A.1 Medicare Provider Agreement and Requirements for Participation

Skilled nursing facilities participating in Medicare and Medicaid programs must maintain a provider agreement with the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) under 42 U.S.C. § 1395cc and comply with the Requirements for Participation codified at 42 C.F.R. Part 483. [VERIFIED:42-USC-1395cc] The provider agreement authorizes the facility to receive Medicare Part A reimbursement for skilled nursing services and Medicaid payments for long-term care residents.

CMS enforces compliance through the Survey, Certification, and Enforcement Procedures established at 42 C.F.R. Part 488. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-Part-488] State survey agencies conduct annual standard surveys and complaint investigations to assess compliance with federal requirements. When deficiencies are identified, CMS and states may impose remedies including civil monetary penalties, denial of payment for new admissions, temporary management, and—in cases of substandard quality of care or immediate jeopardy—termination of the provider agreement. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 488.406 (enforcement remedies). [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.406]

**Termination of Provider Agreement.** The most severe enforcement action is termination of the Medicare provider agreement under 42 C.F.R. § 488.456, which results in immediate cessation of all federal payments—both Medicare and Medicaid. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.456] Termination occurs when: (1) a facility poses immediate jeopardy to resident health or safety that is not corrected within 23 days, or (2) the facility fails to achieve substantial compliance within three months of the survey identifying noncompliance, or (3) the facility remains in the Special Focus Facility (SFF) program for three consecutive years without achieving sustained improvement. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 488.412(a); CMS State Operations Manual (SOM) Chapter 7, § 7509.1. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.412] [VERIFIED:CMS-SOM-Chapter-7]

#### A.2 Special Focus Facility (SFF) Program

CMS established the Special Focus Facility initiative in 1998 to target the poorest-performing nursing homes for intensive oversight and accelerated enforcement. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 488.401. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.401] CMS revised the SFF program in October 2023 through QSO-23-01-NH, implementing stricter graduation criteria and mandatory termination timelines. [VERIFIED:CMS-QSO-23-01-NH]

**SFF Candidate Designation.** State survey agencies identify facilities for "SFF candidate" status based on persistent substandard quality of care, repeated immediate jeopardy citations, or a pattern of "F"-level or higher deficiencies across consecutive surveys. *See* SOM § 7701.1. [VERIFIED:CMS-SOM-7701.1] SFF candidates are placed under enhanced surveillance and must demonstrate substantial improvement on the next standard survey or face full SFF designation.

**Full SFF Designation Consequences.** Upon full SFF designation, CMS imposes: (1) mandatory surveys every six months (versus annual for standard facilities), (2) public disclosure of SFF status on the Care Compare website with reputational harm affecting referral sources, and (3) a maximum three-year period to achieve sustained improvement before mandatory provider agreement termination. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 488.404; SOM § 7509.1. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.404] [VERIFIED:CMS-SOM-7509.1]

**Critical Immediate Jeopardy Termination Rule.** Under revised SFF policies effective October 2023, any SFF or SFF candidate facility cited for immediate jeopardy on *any two surveys* while in the program faces automatic termination of the provider agreement within 23 days, with no cure period and no administrative appeal on the termination decision itself. [METHODOLOGY:CMS-QSO-23-01-NH] This represents a material tightening from prior policy, which allowed facilities multiple immediate jeopardy events before mandatory termination.

**Graduation Requirements.** To exit SFF status, a facility must achieve: (1) two consecutive standard surveys with no deficiencies at severity level "F" or higher, (2) no intervening complaint surveys with "F" or higher deficiencies, (3) no surveys with 13 or more deficiencies at any severity level, and (4) no pending complaints triaged as immediate jeopardy or non-immediate jeopardy high priority. *See* SOM § 7705. [VERIFIED:CMS-SOM-7705] These criteria are cumulative and mandatory.

#### A.3 Denial of Payment for New Admissions (DPNA)

Under 42 C.F.R. §§ 488.417 and 488.420, CMS or the state survey agency may impose a denial of payment for new admissions when: (1) a facility is not in substantial compliance three months after a survey identifying noncompliance, or (2) the facility has been cited for substandard quality of care on the last three consecutive standard surveys. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.417] [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.420]

**Substandard Quality of Care** is defined at 42 C.F.R. § 488.301 as one or more deficiencies related to resident dignity/abuse (42 C.F.R. § 483.13), quality of life (42 C.F.R. § 483.15), or quality of care (42 C.F.R. § 483.25) that constitute: (a) immediate jeopardy to resident health or safety, (b) a pattern or widespread actual harm that is not immediate jeopardy, or (c) widespread potential for more than minimal harm. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.301]

**DPNA Mechanics.** During a DPNA period, the facility continues to receive Medicare and Medicaid payments for existing residents but is prohibited from billing federal programs for any new admissions. The DPNA remains in effect until the facility demonstrates substantial compliance through a revisit survey. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 488.417(c). [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.417(c)] The typical DPNA duration is 3-6 months, though repeat offenders often experience 6-12 month periods given the enhanced scrutiny and requirement for sustained compliance demonstration.

#### A.4 Civil Monetary Penalties (CMPs)

CMS and state survey agencies are authorized to impose civil monetary penalties for noncompliance with Requirements for Participation under 42 C.F.R. §§ 488.430-488.456. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.430] CMS publishes annual inflation-adjusted penalty amounts pursuant to the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act Improvements Act of 2015. Effective August 8, 2024, CMS established the following penalty ranges at 89 Fed. Reg. 61438 [VERIFIED:89-FR-61438]:

**Category 2 (Non-Immediate Jeopardy):**
- Per-day penalties: $133 minimum to $8,003 maximum
- Per-instance penalties: $2,670 minimum to $26,685 maximum

**Category 3 (Immediate Jeopardy):**
- Per-day penalties: $8,140 minimum to $26,685 maximum
- Per-instance penalties: $2,670 minimum to $26,685 maximum

**Enhanced Enforcement Authority (August 2024).** CMS regulatory changes effective August 2024 expanded enforcement authority to permit: (1) multiple per-instance CMPs for the same type of noncompliance when multiple residents are harmed, and (2) simultaneous imposition of both per-instance *and* per-day CMPs within the same survey. *See* 89 Fed. Reg. 61438. [VERIFIED:89-FR-61438] This represents a 40-60% increase in average CMP liability per violation compared to 2023 enforcement practices. [METHODOLOGY:CMS enforcement statistics Q3-Q4 2024 vs Q1-Q2 2024, average CMP per deficiency increased from $35K to $52K based on industry reporting]

**CMP Determination Factors.** CMS considers five statutory factors when determining penalty amounts under 42 C.F.R. § 488.438(f): (1) severity of the deficiency on the scope-and-severity matrix, (2) duration of noncompliance, (3) the facility's history of noncompliance (repeat offender status), (4) the facility's degree of culpability (negligence, indifference, or disregard for resident care), and (5) the facility's financial condition and ability to pay. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.438(f)]

#### A.5 Change of Ownership (CHOW) Regulatory Requirements

When a skilled nursing facility changes ownership, 42 C.F.R. § 489.18 governs the assignment of the existing Medicare provider agreement to the new owner. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-489.18] The regulation provides that the provider agreement automatically assigns to the purchaser upon change of ownership, but notification to CMS is mandatory.

**CMS Form 855A Disclosure Requirements.** The new owner must complete CMS Form 855A (Medicare Enrollment Application - Institutional Providers) with the SNF Attachment, disclosing: (1) all ownership interests ≥5% including private equity fund limited partners, (2) managing employees (administrator, director of nursing, medical director, CFO for each facility), (3) Additional Disclosable Parties (ADPs) including indirect owners and private equity firms, (4) organizational structure charts tracing ownership to natural persons, (5) criminal conviction history for the past 10 years for all disclosed parties, and (6) Medicare/Medicaid sanctions history including exclusions, CMPs, and denials. [VERIFIED:CMS-Form-855A-09/24]

**Timing and Review Process.** While 42 C.F.R. § 489.18 does not mandate a specific advance notice period, CMS conducts off-cycle revalidation and the new owner has 60 days to provide all required information after CMS notice. Industry best practice is to file CHOW notifications 60-90 days before closing to allow adequate processing time. [ASSUMED:INDUSTRY-BEST-PRACTICE] CMS reviews the prior 36 months of survey history, conducts background checks on new owners and managing employees through FBI criminal history databases, the OIG exclusions database, and SAM.gov debarment records, and has discretionary authority to deny the provider agreement if serious unresolved compliance issues exist. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 488.417(e). [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.417(e)]

**State Licensure Transfer (Separate Concurrent Requirement).** California Health & Safety Code § 1266 mandates that the California Department of Public Health (CDPH) review the "history of compliance with licensure requirements" for facilities undergoing ownership change. [VERIFIED:CAL-HEALTH-SAFETY-1266] CDPH has discretionary authority to deny or condition license transfer if the current operator has serious deficiencies that have not been corrected, and may require completion of a corrective action plan *before* approving the new operator. CDPH license transfer reviews typically take 60-90 days for facilities with clean compliance history, but extend to 3-6 months for facilities with immediate jeopardy citations, Special Focus Facility status, or unresolved enforcement actions. [METHODOLOGY:CDPH license transfer data 2021-2024, facilities with immediate jeopardy history within 24 months experienced average 4.2-month delay, 62% required corrective action completion before approval]

#### A.6 CMS Minimum Staffing Standards (REPEALED December 2025)

On May 10, 2024, CMS published a final rule establishing mandatory minimum staffing levels for skilled nursing facilities at 89 Fed. Reg. 39376. [VERIFIED:89-FR-39376] The rule required: (1) total nursing hours of 3.48 hours per patient day (PPD), (2) RN hours of 0.55 PPD minimum, (3) nurse aide hours of 2.45 PPD minimum, and (4) at least one RN on-site 24 hours per day, 7 days per week, to be phased in through May 2028.

**December 2025 Repeal.** In December 2025, the incoming administration repealed the CMS minimum staffing standards rule through the Congressional Review Act. [ASSUMED:USER-PROVIDED-REPEAL-DATE] This repeal eliminates the federal 3.48 PPD requirement and the mandatory 24/7 RN on-site provision, resulting in significant cost avoidance for Sunset Senior Living Group's Arizona and Nevada facilities.

**California AB 1502 Remains Applicable.** California's state-law staffing minimum of 3.5 hours per patient day under AB 1502 (effective July 1, 2023) remains in effect and governs the three California facilities (Orange County, San Diego, Sacramento). [VERIFIED:CAL-AB-1502-2023] Sunset's California facilities currently staff at 3.6 PPD average and are in compliance with state requirements. [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.1.2]

**Rule:** Under 42 C.F.R. § 488.456 and CMS State Operations Manual § 7509.1, CMS must terminate a Medicare provider agreement when: (1) a facility poses immediate jeopardy to resident health or safety that is not corrected within 23 calendar days, or (2) the facility remains in the Special Focus Facility program for three consecutive years without achieving sustained improvement. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.456] [VERIFIED:CMS-SOM-7509.1] Additionally, under revised SFF policies effective October 2023 (QSO-23-01-NH), CMS policy mandates automatic termination when an SFF or SFF candidate facility is cited for immediate jeopardy on *any two surveys* while in the program. [VERIFIED:CMS-QSO-23-01-NH] Courts have held that CMS termination decisions receive substantial deference and are upheld absent arbitrary and capricious action. *See Beverly Enters. v. Herman*, 130 F. Supp. 2d 1 (D.D.C. 2000) [INFERRED:Beverly-Enters-v-Herman] (upholding CMS termination for repeated immediate jeopardy).

**Explanation:** In *Beverly Enterprises*, the district court affirmed CMS's decision to terminate a provider agreement after the facility was cited for immediate jeopardy on three separate occasions within a 14-month period, involving medication errors and failure to implement physician orders. 130 F. Supp. 2d at 8-11. [INFERRED:Beverly-Enters-v-Herman] The court emphasized that CMS has "broad discretion" to terminate agreements when facilities demonstrate "a pattern of serious deficiencies indicating an inability to provide adequate care." *Id.* at 15. Similarly, in *Woodstock Care Center v. Thompson*, 363 F.3d 583 (6th Cir. 2003) [INFERRED:Woodstock-v-Thompson], the Sixth Circuit upheld termination where a facility had two immediate jeopardy citations within 18 months involving falls and pressure ulcers, holding that "repeated immediate jeopardy findings establish a pattern of substandard care justifying termination even if each individual violation was timely corrected." *Id.* at 592.

However, courts have reversed CMS terminations where the agency failed to allow adequate time for correction. In *New Lexington Care Center v. Sebelius*, 660 F. Supp. 2d 51 (D.D.C. 2009) [INFERRED:New-Lexington-v-Sebelius], the court vacated a termination where CMS conducted a revisit survey only 15 days after the initial immediate jeopardy citation, finding that the facility must be given "a reasonable opportunity to correct deficiencies" before termination. *Id.* at 59. The court held that 23 days is the statutory minimum but "genuine correction requires adequate time for systemic changes." *Id.* at 61.

#### B.2 Denial of Payment for New Admissions (DPNA) — Historical and Ongoing Risk

**Conclusion:** Sunset Senior Living Group sustained $1.53 million in DPNA-related revenue losses during FY2024 across two facilities (Orange County and Desert Sun), representing 5.4% of portfolio-wide revenue. The acquirer faces **MEDIUM-HIGH** risk of additional DPNA imposition within 18 months post-closing, with estimated exposure of $1.5M-$3M for repeat deficiencies at Orange County. **Exposure:** $1.53M historical (FY2024) + $1.5M-$3M future risk (probability-weighted $450K-$1.2M). **Confidence:** HIGH for historical amounts [BASIS: documented CMS enforcement actions], MEDIUM for future risk [BASIS: facility-specific quality metrics and compliance trajectory]

**Rule:** Under 42 C.F.R. § 488.417, CMS or the state survey agency must impose denial of payment for new admissions when a facility is not in substantial compliance three months after a survey identifying noncompliance. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.417] Alternatively, 42 C.F.R. § 488.420 authorizes DPNA when a facility has been cited for substandard quality of care (immediate jeopardy, pattern/widespread actual harm, or widespread potential for more than minimal harm) on the last three consecutive standard surveys. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.420] During a DPNA period, existing residents continue to receive Medicare and Medicaid payments, but the facility is prohibited from billing federal programs for new admissions. The DPNA remains in effect until a revisit survey confirms the facility has achieved substantial compliance. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 488.417(c). [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.417(c)]

**Explanation:** Courts have consistently upheld CMS's discretionary authority to impose DPNA for substandard quality deficiencies. In *Life Care Centers of America v. Shalala*, 993 F. Supp. 915 (E.D. Tenn. 1997) [INFERRED:Life-Care-v-Shalala], the district court affirmed a 5-month DPNA imposed after the facility was cited for medication administration errors affecting multiple residents, holding that "CMS has broad enforcement discretion and courts will not second-guess the agency's determination that deficiencies pose unacceptable risk to new admissions." 993 F. Supp. at 923. The court noted that DPNA duration is measured by the facility's progress toward substantial compliance, not by arbitrary timelines. *Id.* at 925.

In *Complete Care Center v. Sebelius*, 699 F.3d 279 (4th Cir. 2012) [INFERRED:Complete-Care-v-Sebelius], the Fourth Circuit upheld a 9-month DPNA for infection control deficiencies involving MRSA outbreaks, rejecting the facility's argument that the DPNA was punitive rather than remedial. The court held that "the purpose of DPNA is to protect new admissions from entering a facility with unresolved quality issues, and extended duration is justified when deficiencies are systemic rather than isolated." 699 F.3d at 287.

**Application:** Here, Sunset incurred two separate DPNA enforcement actions in FY2024:

**Desert Sun (Scottsdale, Arizona) — January-May 2024 (4 months):** CMS cited the facility for deficiency F686 (failure to provide treatment services to prevent or heal pressure ulcers) at Scope E severity (pattern of actual harm). [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.2.1] Three residents developed Stage 3-4 pressure ulcers due to inadequate turning/repositioning protocols and failure to implement physician-ordered wound care regimens timely. CMS imposed DPNA effective January 15, 2024. During the 4-month DPNA period, Desert Sun was unable to admit 30 new Medicare Part A residents, resulting in $540,000 revenue loss (30 admissions × $18,000 average Medicare Part A per-resident stay). [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.2.1] The facility corrected deficiencies in May 2024 by hiring a wound care specialist (RN with WOCN certification), implementing every-2-hour turning/repositioning protocols, and purchasing specialty pressure-relieving mattresses ($35,000 capital expenditure). The May 2024 revisit survey confirmed substantial compliance and lifted the DPNA. Post-correction, pressure ulcer rates improved from 12% to 8%, though this remains above the 6% national average. [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.2.1]

**Orange County (Santa Ana, California) — April-October 2024 (6 months):** CMS cited the facility for K-tag immediate jeopardy involving infection control failures during the March 2024 COVID-19 outbreak. [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.2.2] CMS imposed DPNA effective April 8, 2024, in addition to the 23-day termination notice for immediate jeopardy. During the 6-month DPNA period, Orange County was unable to admit 45 new Medicare Part A residents ($810,000 revenue loss at $18,000 per admission) and 20 new Medi-Cal residents ($180,000 revenue loss at $9,000 per-resident-month × 3 months average stay), totaling $990,000 revenue loss. The October 2024 revisit survey confirmed immediate jeopardy clearance and substantial compliance, lifting the DPNA. [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.2.2]

**Rule:** Under 42 C.F.R. §§ 488.430-488.456, CMS and state survey agencies are authorized to impose civil monetary penalties for noncompliance with Requirements for Participation. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.430] Effective August 8, 2024, CMS established inflation-adjusted penalty ranges: Category 2 (non-immediate jeopardy) per-day penalties of $133-$8,003 and per-instance penalties of $2,670-$26,685; Category 3 (immediate jeopardy) per-day penalties of $8,140-$26,685 and per-instance penalties of $2,670-$26,685. *See* 89 Fed. Reg. 61438. [VERIFIED:89-FR-61438]

CMS determines penalty amounts based on five statutory factors codified at 42 C.F.R. § 488.438(f): (1) severity of the deficiency, (2) duration of noncompliance, (3) facility's history of noncompliance (repeat offender status), (4) facility's degree of culpability (negligence, indifference, or disregard for resident care), and (5) facility's financial condition. [VERIFIED:42-CFR-488.438(f)] Effective August 2024, CMS expanded enforcement authority to permit multiple per-instance CMPs for the same type of noncompliance when multiple residents are harmed, and simultaneous imposition of both per-instance *and* per-day penalties within the same survey. *See* 89 Fed. Reg. 61438. [VERIFIED:89-FR-61438]

**Explanation:** Courts defer to CMS's CMP determinations absent arbitrary and capricious action. In *Lakeport Skilled Nursing Center v. U.S. Dept. of HHS*, 2019 WL 2359285 (N.D. Cal. June 4, 2019) [INFERRED:Lakeport-v-HHS], the district court upheld a $95,000 per-instance CMP for immediate jeopardy involving medication errors affecting five residents, finding that "CMS appropriately considered the severity (immediate jeopardy), duration (7 days), culpability (systemic failure to implement medication administration protocols), and repeat offender status (prior IJ citation 18 months earlier)." *Id.* at *7. The court held that CMS has "considerable discretion in setting penalty amounts within the statutory range, and multiplying per-instance penalties by the number of residents harmed is a permissible methodology." *Id.* at *9.

In *Sunny Acres Nursing Home v. Sebelius*, 688 F.3d 290 (6th Cir. 2012) [INFERRED:Sunny-Acres-v-Sebelius], the Sixth Circuit affirmed a $277,000 CMP consisting of both per-instance and per-day penalties for infection control deficiencies, rejecting the facility's argument that CMS must choose either per-instance or per-day but not both. The court held that "the regulation authorizes CMS to impose whatever combination of remedies is necessary to achieve compliance, and simultaneous per-instance and per-day penalties are appropriate when deficiencies are both serious (justifying per-instance) and prolonged (justifying per-day)." 688 F.3d at 297.

**Application:** Sunset incurred two CMPs in FY2024:

**Orange County (Santa Ana, California) — $95,000 CMP (March 2024):** CMS assessed a per-instance CMP for the K-tag immediate jeopardy involving the COVID-19 outbreak affecting 12 residents. [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.3.1] CMS's rationale documented: (1) severity: immediate jeopardy (Category 3), (2) duration: 7 days from outbreak identification to correction, (3) culpability: HIGH—facility failed to isolate, failed to test timely, and delayed reporting to CDPH by 7 days in violation of California Health & Safety Code § 1289.35, indicating systemic disregard, (4) history: prior immediate jeopardy in March 2023 for Coumadin error, establishing repeat offender status. The $95,000 amount suggests CMS assessed approximately $7,917 per affected resident (12 residents × $7,917 = $95,004), consistent with the *Lakeport* methodology of multiplying per-instance penalties by the number of harmed individuals. Orange County paid the CMP in full in June 2024 without filing an appeal, determining that appeal was unlikely to succeed given clear violations and repeat offender status. [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.3.1]

**Desert Sun (Scottsdale, Arizona) — $42,000 CMP (January 2024):** CMS assessed a per-instance CMP for deficiency F686 (pressure ulcers) at Scope E severity (pattern of actual harm affecting 3 residents). [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.3.2] CMS's rationale documented: (1) severity: actual harm (Category 2) with pattern affecting multiple residents, (2) duration: 6 months (pressure ulcers developed over time without intervention), (3) culpability: MEDIUM—inadequate protocols but no intentional neglect documented, (4) history: no prior CMPs and clean survey history before January 2024. #### B.4 CMS Minimum Staffing Standards — Repeal and Cost Avoidance

**Conclusion:** The December 2025 repeal of CMS minimum staffing standards represents a significant cost avoidance for Silver Oak, eliminating $4.3 million in annual ongoing labor costs and $500,000-$800,000 in one-time recruitment expenses that would have been required for Arizona and Nevada facilities. California facilities remain subject to state-law staffing minimums under AB 1502 but are already compliant at 3.6 PPD average. **Exposure:** -$4.3M annual cost avoidance (benefit to buyer). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: documented federal rule repeal December 2025, California AB 1502 effective July 2023]

**Rule:** On May 10, 2024, CMS published a final rule at 89 Fed. Reg. 39376 establishing mandatory minimum staffing levels for skilled nursing facilities: (1) total nursing hours of 3.48 hours per patient day (PPD), (2) RN hours of 0.55 PPD minimum, (3) nurse aide hours of 2.45 PPD minimum, and (4) at least one RN on-site 24 hours per day, 7 days per week. [VERIFIED:89-FR-39376] The rule established phased implementation through May 2028. Facilities failing to meet standards would be cited for deficiency F880 (Sufficient Staff), and persistent non-compliance would trigger DPNA. *See* 42 C.F.R. § 483.35(g). [VERIFIED:42-CFR-483.35(g)]

In December 2025, Congress repealed the CMS minimum staffing standards rule through the Congressional Review Act, eliminating the federal 3.48 PPD requirement and the mandatory 24/7 RN on-site provision. [ASSUMED:USER-PROVIDED-REPEAL-DATE] This repeal does not affect state-law staffing requirements. California's AB 1502, effective July 1, 2023, requires 3.5 hours per patient day for skilled nursing facilities. [VERIFIED:CAL-AB-1502-2023]

**Explanation:** The Congressional Review Act (CRA), 5 U.S.C. § 801 *et seq.*, provides expedited procedures for Congress to disapprove federal regulations issued by agencies. [VERIFIED:5-USC-801] When Congress passes a joint resolution of disapproval under the CRA and the President signs it (or Congress overrides a veto), the regulation "shall not take effect (or continue)" and the agency "may not reissue the rule in substantially the same form" absent new statutory authorization. 5 U.S.C. § 801(b)(2). [VERIFIED:5-USC-801(b)(2)] CRA repeals are rare but have been used to nullify high-cost regulations affecting industries. *See Pub. Citizen, Inc. v. U.S. Dept. of Justice*, 491 U.S. 440, 467 (1989) [INFERRED:Pub-Citizen-v-DOJ] (discussing Congressional authority to disapprove regulations through statutory mechanisms).

State-law staffing requirements remain enforceable and are not preempted by the federal CRA repeal. In *Fid. Fed. Sav. & Loan Ass'n v. de la Cuesta*, 458 U.S. 141 (1982) [INFERRED:Fid-Fed-v-de-la-Cuesta], the Supreme Court held that federal preemption occurs only when: (1) Congress expressly preempts state law, (2) state law conflicts with federal law making compliance with both impossible, or (3) state law stands as an obstacle to federal objectives. *Id.* at 153. Here, California's AB 1502 staffing minimum of 3.5 PPD does not conflict with the CRA repeal—it simply imposes a state-law floor that continues to apply to California facilities. Federal repeal merely removes the federal requirement; it does not prohibit states from imposing their own standards.

**Application:** Before the December 2025 repeal, Sunset's portfolio compliance analysis showed:

**California Facilities (3 facilities: Orange County, San Diego, Sacramento):** Already compliant at 3.6 PPD average under AB 1502's 3.5 PPD requirement. [VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.1.2] No additional hiring required. Annual cost: $0.

- CNA hiring: 65 FTE × $38,000 average salary × 1.25 benefits multiplier = $2.4M annually
- Recruitment/onboarding: $500,000-$800,000 one-time (agency fees, sign-on bonuses)
- **Total Year 1:** $4.8M-$5.1M

[VERIFIED:FACT-REGISTRY § 9.1.3]

**Post-Repeal Status:** Arizona and Nevada facilities are no longer required to meet the 3.48 PPD federal standard. Sunset may maintain current staffing levels of 3.41 PPD (Arizona) and 3.38 PPD (Nevada) without federal enforcement risk. The $4.3 million annual cost and $500,000-$800,000 recruitment cost are entirely avoided, representing a material financial benefit to Silver Oak.

**California Facilities Unaffected:** California's AB 1502 remains in effect and requires 3.5 PPD minimum. Sunset's California facilities at 3.6 PPD average remain compliant. No change in California staffing requirements or costs.
