### A. Legal Framework

Insurance coverage analysis for the Project Asclepius transaction requires evaluation of Sunset Senior Living Group's existing policies against material risks identified in due diligence. The legal framework governing insurance coverage involves state contract law (policy interpretation), federal and state regulatory requirements (insurable vs. uninsurable risks), and industry-specific standards for healthcare providers.

#### 1. Claims-Made vs. Occurrence Policies

Directors & Officers (D&O) liability insurance and professional liability insurance in the healthcare sector predominantly utilize **claims-made** coverage structures, wherein coverage is triggered based on when a claim is first made against the insured during the policy period, not when the underlying wrongful act occurred.1 This contrasts with occurrence-based policies, which cover incidents occurring during the policy period regardless of when claims are reported.

The significance for M&A transactions is substantial: claims-made policies require Extended Reporting Period (ERP) endorsements—commonly called "tail coverage"—at policy termination or change of control to preserve coverage for pre-transaction incidents that generate post-closing claims.2 Without tail coverage, a claim filed after closing for pre-closing conduct would fall into a coverage gap: the acts occurred during the prior policy period, but the claim was made after the policy expired.

**Policy Interpretation Standards**: Insurance policies are contracts interpreted under state law. Most courts apply the "reasonable expectations" doctrine, construing ambiguities against the insurer (contra proferentem).3 The "eight corners rule" governs duty to defend determinations—the insurer compares only the complaint allegations to the policy language without extrinsic evidence.4

#### 2. Fraud Exclusions in D&O Policies

Standard D&O policies exclude coverage for "deliberately fraudulent acts" or "willful violations of law."5 The critical interpretive question is whether such exclusions require final adjudication of fraud or apply based on mere allegations. Recent appellate decisions establish a pro-insured trend:

The **Delaware Supreme Court** (2023) held that D&O fraud exclusions requiring "final adjudication" do not bar coverage for settled False Claims Act cases where the insured settles without admitting fraud.6 Similarly, the **Seventh Circuit** (2023) rejected insurers' attempts to invoke fraud exclusions for FCA settlements, holding that allegations of "knowing" violations under 31 U.S.C. § 3729(b)(1)—which includes reckless disregard—do not constitute "deliberate fraud" sufficient to trigger exclusions absent final adjudication.7

These decisions create a pathway for D&O coverage of FCA settlements in healthcare contexts, particularly where the policy includes "final adjudication" language and the settlement includes no admission of fraud.

#### 3. Public Policy Exclusions—Fines and Penalties

Courts uniformly hold that regulatory fines, civil monetary penalties, and statutory penalties are **uninsurable as against public policy**.8 The rationale is that allowing insurance indemnification for punitive governmental sanctions would eliminate the deterrent effect and encourage regulatory violations.

This doctrine excludes coverage for:
- CMS Civil Monetary Penalties (CMPs) imposed under 42 C.F.R. § 488.430 for survey deficiencies
- Denial of Payment for New Admissions (DPNA) revenue losses (not damages, but lost revenue from regulatory sanctions)
- WARN Act statutory penalties under 29 U.S.C. § 2104(a)(1)
- OSHA fines and penalties
- Statutory liquidated damages (distinguished from compensatory damages)

Professional liability policies explicitly exclude "fines, penalties, sanctions imposed by governmental agencies" consistent with this public policy rule.9

**Exception—Cyber Liability HIPAA Fines**: Some cyber liability insurers include limited sublimits ($500K-$1M) for HIPAA regulatory fines, reasoning that cybersecurity breaches often result from external attacks rather than willful facility violations.10 This represents a narrow exception where insurers view the fines as arising from insurable risk (data breach) rather than uninsurable regulatory noncompliance.

#### 4. Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) Coverage Scope

EPLI policies cover employment-related claims including wrongful termination, discrimination, sexual harassment, retaliation, and failure to hire/promote.11 Coverage extends to both damages awarded to employees and defense costs. Standard exclusions include:

- Statutory penalties (WARN Act, FLSA liquidated damages)12
- ERISA violations
- OSHA citations
- Criminal fines
- Intentional torts (assault, battery)

The wrongful termination and retaliation coverage is particularly relevant where employees assert whistleblower protection claims under federal statutes such as the False Claims Act's anti-retaliation provision, 31 U.S.C. § 3730(h).

#### 5. Tail Coverage Requirements in M&A Transactions

Healthcare M&A transactions involving claims-made policies require tail coverage to protect both the seller and buyer from coverage gaps. Industry standards for tail coverage include:13

- **Duration**: 6-year ERP for both D&O and professional liability (matching or exceeding statute of limitations for underlying claims: medical malpractice 2-4 years, securities/governance claims 3-6 years)
- **Premium**: 150-250% of annual premium (D&O tail typically 200-250%, professional liability 150-200%)
- **Limits**: Tail policy limits must match the expiring policy limits
- **Allocation**: Seller typically pays tail premium as part of representations and warranties regarding adequate insurance coverage

The economic impact is significant: for a 12-facility SNF operator with $5M-$10M D&O limits and $3M-$6M professional liability aggregate, estimated tail premium is $1.5M-$3M total.

#### B.2 D&O Defense Costs and Investigation Sublimit Coverage

**Conclusion:** Sunset's D&O insurer has a **duty to defend** the Martinez FCA qui tam litigation, providing coverage for estimated defense costs of $500K-$800K under reservation of rights. **Rule:** Under the "eight corners rule" applied in California, Arizona, and Nevada (states where Sunset operates facilities), an insurer's duty to defend is triggered if the complaint potentially alleges covered wrongful acts, comparing only the four corners of the complaint to the four corners of the policy without extrinsic evidence.28 The duty to defend is broader than the duty to indemnify—the insurer must defend even if the ultimate liability is not covered, so long as the complaint alleges at least one potentially covered claim.29

D&O policies typically provide that defense costs are paid in addition to policy limits (outside limits) or reduce policy limits (inside limits).30 Many D&O policies include a **regulatory investigation cost endorsement** (also called DIC—Defense and Investigation Cost coverage) providing a sublimit ($500K-$1M typical) for legal fees incurred responding to government investigations, subpoenas, and Civil Investigative Demands prior to formal charges or complaints.31

**Explanation:** Courts universally hold that D&O insurers must defend FCA qui tam actions under reservation of rights, even where the insurer believes the fraud exclusion may ultimately bar indemnity coverage.32 The reasoning is that FCA complaints allege "Wrongful Acts" of corporate officers (submission of false claims, conspiracy to defraud) that fall within the insuring agreement's grant of coverage. The fraud exclusion is an affirmative defense to indemnity, not a bar to the duty to defend.

The insurer's defense under reservation of rights preserves its ability to: (1) deny indemnity coverage if fraud is ultimately established, and (2) seek recoupment of defense costs paid if the fraud exclusion applies.33 However, the practical effect is that the insurer bears defense costs during the litigation, reducing the insured's immediate cash outflow.

For regulatory investigation costs prior to complaint filing, coverage depends on whether the policy includes a DIC endorsement. Many modern D&O policies include this coverage in response to increasing government investigations that impose substantial legal costs before formal charges materialize. The sublimit is separate from main policy limits, providing additional capacity.

**Rule:** Professional liability policies for skilled nursing facilities cover "damages" for which the insured becomes legally obligated to pay arising from "professional services" rendered by licensed healthcare providers.38 Standard exclusions include "fines, penalties, sanctions, or punitive damages imposed by governmental agencies."39

Courts uniformly hold that regulatory fines and penalties are **uninsurable as against public policy** because insurance indemnification would eliminate the deterrent effect of governmental sanctions and encourage regulatory violations.40 This doctrine applies to:

The distinction between insurable and uninsurable losses is critical: professional liability covers **damages awarded to injured residents** (negligence claims, wrongful death, abuse/neglect settlements), but not governmental sanctions for regulatory noncompliance.

This is particularly acute in healthcare, where penalties aim to protect vulnerable nursing home residents from substandard care.

CMS guidance explicitly states that CMPs are **personal to the facility** and cannot be reimbursed through Medicare cost reports or insurance proceeds.42 DPNA is characterized as a restriction on revenue (the facility cannot bill for new admissions), not a damage award—professional liability policies cover legal liability for damages, not business interruption or lost revenue from regulatory sanctions.

Some courts have recognized a narrow exception for penalties that are **compensatory rather than punitive** in nature, but this exception rarely applies to CMS sanctions, which are explicitly designed as deterrents for noncompliance.43

#### B.7 Tail Coverage Transaction Requirement—$1.5M-$3M Cost

**Conclusion:** The Project Asclepius transaction requires seller (Golden Gate Capital) to purchase Extended Reporting Period (ERP) "tail coverage" for both D&O and professional liability claims-made policies at closing, with estimated aggregate cost of $1.5M-$3M. Tail coverage is **essential** to preserve coverage for pre-closing incidents that generate post-closing claims. Failure to procure tail coverage creates a coverage gap exposing both seller and buyer to uninsured pre-closing liabilities. **Exposure:** $0 if seller pays (transaction standard); $1.5M-$3M if buyer pays (requires purchase price reduction). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Industry standard M&A practice verified by multiple sources].

**Rule:** Claims-made insurance policies require Extended Reporting Period (ERP) endorsements—"tail coverage"—when policies are canceled or not renewed to preserve coverage for wrongful acts occurring during the policy period but claims reported after policy expiration.75 Without tail coverage, a coverage gap arises: the incident occurred while the policy was in force (triggering the occurrence requirement for occurrence policies), but the claim was made after the policy expired (failing the claims-made trigger).

Industry standards for tail coverage in healthcare M&A transactions:76
- **Duration**: 6 years (matching or exceeding statutes of limitations for underlying claims)
- **Premium**: 150-250% of annual premium
  - D&O: 200-250% of annual premium
  - Professional liability: 150-200% of annual premium
- **Limits**: Tail policy limits must match expiring policy limits
- **Allocation**: Seller pays as part of representations and warranties regarding adequate insurance

**Explanation:** The economic rationale for seller-paid tail coverage is that the seller's representations and warranties regarding operations and liabilities extend post-closing. If a claim arises post-closing for pre-closing conduct (e.g., resident injury lawsuit filed in 2027 for February 2024 pressure ulcers, or Martinez FCA settlement negotiated in 2026 for 2019-2022 wrongful acts), the seller needs insurance coverage to fund defense and indemnification obligations.

From the buyer's perspective, tail coverage protects against successor liability. In healthcare transactions, even asset purchases can expose buyers to liabilities for pre-closing conduct under doctrines such as:
- Medicare/Medicaid successor liability
- WARN Act employer succession
- Continuing violations (HIPAA breaches spanning pre- and post-closing)

Six-year tail coverage duration is industry standard because it exceeds most statutes of limitations:77
- Medical malpractice: 2-4 years (varies by state)
- FCA: 6 years, 31 U.S.C. § 3731(b)
- Securities claims: 3-6 years
- HIPAA breach reporting: 6-year lookback for OCR investigations

**Application:** Here, Sunset's estimated tail coverage costs are:

**Professional Liability Tail Coverage:**
