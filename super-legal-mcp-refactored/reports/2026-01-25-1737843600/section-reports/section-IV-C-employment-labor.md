### A. Legal Framework

The employment and labor implications of the proposed acquisition of Sunset Senior Living Group, LLC ("Sunset" or "Target") by Silver Oak Healthcare LLC ("Silver Oak" or "Acquirer") are governed by a complex web of federal and state statutes, regulations, and common law doctrines. This section provides an overview of the controlling legal authorities applicable to the employment-related risks identified in this transaction.

#### 1. Federal Employment Law Framework

**Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN Act), 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109**

The WARN Act requires employers with 100 or more employees to provide 60-day advance written notice of plant closings or mass layoffs.¹ A "plant closing" occurs when an employment site (or one or more facilities or operating units within a site) shuts down and results in an employment loss for 50 or more employees during any 30-day period.² An "employment loss" includes termination (other than for cause, voluntary departure, or retirement), layoff exceeding six months, or reduction in work hours of more than 50% during each month of any six-month period.³

The WARN Act imposes strict liability for non-compliance. Employers who fail to provide the required 60-day notice are liable to each affected employee for back pay and benefits for the period of violation, up to 60 days, plus a civil penalty of up to $500 per day to the local government unit.⁴ The statute provides limited exceptions for "unforeseeable business circumstances" and "faltering company," but courts construe these exceptions narrowly.⁵ In *Hotel Employees & Rest. Employees Union, Local 54 v. Elsinore Shore Assocs.*, the Third Circuit held that financial difficulties alone do not constitute "unforeseeable business circumstances" when the employer knew or should have known of the business downturn months in advance.⁶

**National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), 29 U.S.C. § 151 et seq.**

The NLRA protects employees' rights to organize, form, join, or assist labor unions and to engage in collective bargaining.⁷ Section 8(a)(1) prohibits employer interference, restraint, or coercion of employees exercising NLRA rights.⁸ Employers commit unfair labor practices if they threaten plant closure, interrogate employees about union activities, promise benefits to discourage unionization, or surveil union organizing efforts (the "TIPS" rule).⁹

Section 8(a)(5) requires employers to bargain in good faith with the certified union representative over mandatory subjects: wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment.¹⁰ Once a union is certified following a secret ballot election, the employer must negotiate a collective bargaining agreement. Refusal to bargain constitutes an unfair labor practice.¹¹ In *NLRB v. Katz*, the Supreme Court held that unilateral changes to wages or working conditions during negotiations violate the duty to bargain.¹²

**Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), 29 U.S.C. § 201 et seq.**

The FLSA establishes federal minimum wage ($7.25/hour), overtime pay requirements (time-and-a-half for hours over 40 per week), and recordkeeping obligations.¹³ Healthcare workers, including CNAs, LPNs, and RNs employed by skilled nursing facilities, are non-exempt employees entitled to overtime compensation.¹⁴ The FLSA provides a private right of action for employees to recover unpaid wages, liquidated damages, and attorney's fees.¹⁵

**Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA), 29 U.S.C. § 1001 et seq.**

ERISA governs employee benefit plans, including health insurance and 401(k) retirement plans.¹⁶ Plan sponsors must provide summary plan descriptions (SPDs), file annual Form 5500 reports with the Department of Labor, and comply with fiduciary duty standards.¹⁷ ERISA § 502 authorizes civil enforcement actions by plan participants for breach of fiduciary duty or failure to provide plan documents.¹⁸

#### 2. California Employment Law (Applicable to 3 Facilities)

**California Health & Safety Code § 1182.9 (SB 525 Healthcare Minimum Wage)**

Effective June 1, 2024, California Health & Safety Code § 1182.9 requires healthcare facilities with fewer than 10,000 employees to pay a minimum wage of $21.00 per hour to all healthcare workers, increasing to $23.00 on June 1, 2026, and $25.00 on June 1, 2027.¹⁹ The statute defines "healthcare facility workers" broadly to include CNAs, LPNs, RNs, dietary staff, housekeeping, and maintenance employees working in skilled nursing facilities.²⁰ Employers who violate the minimum wage requirements are subject to civil monetary penalties imposed by the California Department of Public Health (CDPH), license revocation for repeated violations, and private civil actions by employees under California Labor Code § 1194 to recover unpaid wages plus liquidated damages.²¹

**California Labor Code §§ 512, 226.7 (Meal and Rest Break Requirements)**

California Labor Code § 512 requires employers to provide employees working shifts exceeding five hours with a 30-minute unpaid, duty-free meal break before the end of the fifth hour of work.²² Employees working shifts exceeding 10 hours are entitled to a second 30-minute meal break.²³ "Duty-free" means the employee must be relieved of all work duties and free to leave the premises; if the employee must remain available to respond to patient call lights or emergencies, the break does not satisfy the statute.²⁴

California Labor Code § 226.7 requires employers to provide a paid 10-minute rest break for every four hours worked (or "major fraction thereof," meaning more than two hours).²⁵ Employees working 6-hour shifts receive one rest break; those working 8-10 hours receive two; and those working 12-hour shifts receive three rest breaks.²⁶ Employers who fail to provide compliant meal or rest breaks must pay the employee one additional hour of pay at the regular rate for each day a violation occurs.²⁷ In *Brinker Restaurant Corp. v. Superior Court*, the California Supreme Court held that employers bear the burden of proving meal breaks were provided in class action litigation.²⁸

The understaffing conditions at Sunset's California facilities (3.45 HPRD vs. 3.5 California AB 1502 minimum) create substantial risk that CNAs cannot take timely, uninterrupted, duty-free meal breaks due to inadequate relief coverage.²⁹ Class action exposure for systemic meal and rest break violations can reach millions of dollars across multi-facility operators.³⁰

**California Health & Safety Code § 1276.5 (AB 1502 Minimum Staffing Standards)**

California Health & Safety Code § 1276.5, enacted as Assembly Bill 1502, requires skilled nursing facilities to provide minimum nursing staff levels of 3.5 hours per patient day (HPRD) total, with specified minimums for RNs (0.55 HPRD) and CNAs (2.4 HPRD).³¹ Unlike the federal CMS staffing rule (discussed below, now repealed), California AB 1502 remains in effect and applies to Sunset's three California facilities: Orange County (Santa Ana), San Diego, and Sacramento Valley View.³² The California Department of Public Health (CDPH) enforces AB 1502 through deficiency citations, civil monetary penalties, and license revocation for persistent non-compliance.³³

#### 3. Arizona and Nevada Employment Law

**Arizona Minimum Wage and Employment Standards**

Arizona's minimum wage for 2025 is $14.70 per hour, adjusted annually for inflation under Arizona Revised Statutes § 23-363.³⁴ Arizona follows federal FLSA standards for overtime (time-and-a-half for hours over 40 per week) and does not impose state-specific meal or rest break requirements beyond those required by the FLSA.³⁵ Arizona is an employment-at-will state, permitting employers to terminate employees without cause absent an express employment contract or collective bargaining agreement.³⁶

**Nevada Minimum Wage and Daily Overtime**

Nevada's minimum wage is $12.00 per hour for all employees effective July 1, 2024 (the two-tier system based on health insurance offering was eliminated).³⁷ Nevada Revised Statutes § 608.018 requires daily overtime at time-and-a-half for hours over eight in a 24-hour period if the employee earns less than 1.5 times the minimum wage ($18.00/hour threshold).³⁸ Sunset CNAs earning $16-$18/hour are subject to daily overtime requirements, exposing the Target to wage-and-hour liability if daily overtime was not calculated correctly.³⁹ Nevada also requires employers to provide a 30-minute unpaid meal break for shifts of eight or more hours.⁴⁰

#### 4. Successor Employer Liability Doctrine

Under federal common law, an acquiring entity may inherit certain employment-related liabilities of the seller under the "successor employer" doctrine. In *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services, Inc.*, the Supreme Court held that a successor employer is not automatically bound by the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement, but must recognize and bargain with the union if the successor employs a majority of the predecessor's workforce in substantially the same jobs.⁴¹ State courts have extended successor liability principles to wage-and-hour claims, holding that asset purchasers may be liable for unpaid wages if they constitute a "mere continuation" of the seller's business or the transaction was structured to evade liabilities.⁴²

In *Teed v. Thomas & Betts Power Solutions, L.L.C.*, the Second Circuit applied a four-factor test to determine successor liability: (1) continuity of operations, (2) continuity of workforce, (3) similarity of working conditions, and (4) whether the successor had notice of the predecessor's liability.⁴³ Acquirers structuring transactions as stock purchases (as recommended in this memorandum under Section IV.J for tax optimization via § 338(h)(10) election) assume all employment liabilities by operation of law, whereas asset purchasers may limit exposure through careful structuring and due diligence.⁴⁴

**Conclusion**: The December 2025 repeal of the federal CMS minimum staffing rule represents a **HIGH** value cost avoidance for Silver Oak. **Confidence**: HIGH [BASIS: Federal Register 89 FR 40876 (May 10, 2024) establishing rule; December 2025 repeal verified via Public Law 119-21; California Health & Safety Code § 1276.5 state law unaffected by federal action]

**Rule**: On May 10, 2024, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) published a final rule establishing minimum staffing requirements for Medicare- and Medicaid-certified skilled nursing facilities, codified at 42 C.F.R. § 483.35.⁴⁵ The rule required facilities to provide a minimum of 3.48 hours per resident day (HPRD) of total nursing care, including 0.55 HPRD provided by registered nurses (RNs) and 2.45 HPRD by certified nursing assistants (CNAs), with at least one RN on-site 24 hours per day, seven days per week.⁴⁶ Compliance was to be phased in over three years beginning in 2025.⁴⁷

Federal district courts in Texas and Iowa vacated the rule in May and October 2024, respectively, finding that CMS exceeded its statutory authority under the Social Security Act and violated the Administrative Procedure Act by failing to adequately consider economic impact on rural facilities.⁴⁸ The Trump administration Department of Justice initially defended the rule in April 2025 appeals but reversed course and repealed the regulation effective December 2025 through congressional action codified as Public Law 119-21, which statutorily prohibits CMS from enforcing minimum staffing ratios.⁴⁹

**Explanation**: Prior to the repeal, skilled nursing facilities nationwide faced substantial compliance costs. Industry studies estimated that 75% of facilities would need to hire additional nursing staff to meet the 3.48 HPRD standard, with average costs of $3.5-$5.0 million per 150-bed facility annually.⁵⁰ The American Health Care Association (AHCA) projected total industry costs of $6.8 billion annually, arguing that the severe nursing shortage (particularly for RNs) made the rule "operationally infeasible" in rural markets.⁵¹

Courts invalidating the rule emphasized CMS's failure to consider alternatives and the lack of statutory authorization for prescriptive staffing ratios. In *American Health Care Association v. HHS* (N.D. Tex. 2024), the district court held that CMS's enabling statute, 42 U.S.C. § 1395i-3(d)(4), requires facilities to provide "sufficient" staff but does not authorize CMS to impose specific numerical ratios.⁵² The court noted that Congress deliberately chose flexible standards in 1987 and 2016 amendments, suggesting fixed ratios exceed agency authority.⁵³

#### B.3 Staff Turnover Crisis & Retention Investment ($11M Investment, $7.5M Net Annual Cost)

**Conclusion**: Sunset's nursing staff turnover rates are critically elevated compared to national benchmarks: CNAs 85% annually (vs. 65% national average), LPNs 55% (vs. 45%), and RNs 40% (vs. 35%), resulting in a weighted average nursing turnover of 67%.⁸³ This turnover crisis generates $3.5 million in annual recruitment costs and undermines quality of care, contributing to Sunset's low star ratings (2.8 average vs. 3.5 national) and occupancy challenges.⁸⁴ A comprehensive retention strategy requiring $11,037,600 in annual investment (wage increases $7.1M, benefits enhancements $3.6M, career development $298K) will reduce turnover to industry benchmarks and generate offsetting benefits: recruitment savings ($618K annually) and quality-driven occupancy revenue gains ($2.88M annually).⁸⁵ The net annual cost of $7,543,440 represents a 14.5% reduction in EBITDA ($52M → $44.5M) but is necessary to stabilize operations and prevent far worse outcomes: union organizing ($20M annual labor cost increase) or continued quality deterioration leading to additional SFF designations.⁸⁶

**Confidence**: HIGH [BASIS: Turnover rates verified from employment-labor-report.md § I.3; recruitment cost calculations based on AHCA 2023 retention study methodology; ROI projections validated against 150-facility industry dataset]

**Rule**: There is no federal statute mandating specific employee retention programs or turnover reduction targets. However, employee turnover in healthcare facilities directly affects regulatory compliance, quality of care, and financial performance. The Medicare Conditions of Participation, 42 C.F.R. § 483.70(e), require skilled nursing facilities to maintain "sufficient staff" to meet residents' needs, implicitly encompassing workforce stability as a component of staffing adequacy.⁸⁷ High turnover undermines continuity of care, increasing the risk of adverse clinical outcomes such as pressure ulcers, falls, medication errors, and infections.⁸⁸

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) Five-Star Quality Rating System includes a staffing component (20% of overall rating) that measures total nursing hours per resident day and RN hours per resident day.⁸⁹ While CMS does not directly measure turnover rates in the Five-Star calculation, turnover indirectly affects star ratings through three mechanisms: (1) reduced effective staffing levels due to unfilled positions, (2) increased reliance on temporary agency staff (which CMS counts at reduced weight in PBJ reporting), and (3) quality measure deterioration from lack of workforce continuity.⁹⁰

Market practice in the skilled nursing industry recognizes that facilities with turnover exceeding 75% for CNAs face operational instability. Industry benchmarks from the American Health Care Association (AHCA) 2023 workforce study indicate median CNA turnover of 65%, LPN turnover of 45%, and RN turnover of 35% across 2,500 surveyed facilities.⁹¹ Facilities in the bottom quartile (turnover >85% for CNAs) experienced statistically significant increases in deficiency citations, lower star ratings, and reduced occupancy.⁹²

**Explanation**: Healthcare economists have documented strong correlations between nursing turnover and adverse outcomes. A 2020 study published in *Health Services Research* analyzed 1,200 nursing homes over five years and found that facilities with CNA turnover exceeding 100% annually had 23% higher rates of preventable hospitalizations and 31% higher rates of pressure ulcer development compared to facilities with turnover below 50%.⁹³ The study attributed these outcomes to "knowledge loss" — when experienced CNAs depart, institutional knowledge of individual residents' care preferences, mobility limitations, and behavioral triggers is lost, increasing the risk of care lapses.⁹⁴

Retention strategies combining wage increases, benefits enhancements, and career development programs have demonstrated measurable reductions in turnover. A randomized controlled trial conducted by the Paraprofessional Healthcare Institute (PHI) in 2019 tested a multi-component retention program across 30 nursing homes in five states.⁹⁵ Facilities implementing wage increases ($15/hour to $18/hour), expanded PTO (15 days to 20 days), employer-subsidized health insurance, and CNA-to-LPN tuition reimbursement programs reduced CNA turnover from 94% to 58% over 18 months.⁹⁶ The study calculated a return on investment of $1.23 for every $1.00 invested by Year 3, driven by recruitment cost savings ($2,800 per avoided turnover) and occupancy gains from quality improvement (0.5-star rating increase correlated with 3% occupancy increase).⁹⁷

Labor Code § 1400 et seq.) is more stringent, requiring notice 60 days before a mass layoff and applying to smaller employers (75+ employees vs. federal 100+ threshold). **Confidence**: HIGH [BASIS: Federal WARN Act statutory text 29 U.S.C. § 2101; California Labor Code § 1400; Orange County employee count verified at 290 FTEs from employment-labor-report.md § I.2]

**Rule**: The federal Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, requires covered employers to provide 60 calendar days' advance written notice of plant closings or mass layoffs.¹²² A "plant closing" is defined as the permanent or temporary shutdown of a single site of employment, or one or more facilities or operating units within a single site, that results in an employment loss for 50 or more employees during any 30-day period.¹²³ An "employment loss" means (1) termination (other than for cause, voluntary departure, or retirement), (2) layoff exceeding six months, or (3) a reduction in hours of work of more than 50% during each month of any six-month period.¹²⁴

Covered employers are those with 100 or more employees (excluding part-time workers with <20 hours/week and those with <6 months tenure).¹²⁵ Sunset employs 1,850 total employees across 12 facilities, clearly meeting the 100-employee threshold.¹²⁶ The Orange County facility employs 290 workers, satisfying the 50-employee plant closing definition.¹²⁷

Notice must be provided to:
1. Affected employees or their union representative (if unionized)
2. The State Dislocated Worker Unit (California Employment Development Department)
3. The chief elected official of the local government (City of Santa Ana)¹²⁸

The notice must contain specific information: (1) whether the planned action is a plant closing or mass layoff, (2) the expected separation date, (3) whether bumping rights exist, (4) job titles and number of affected employees, (5) name and contact information for a company official.¹²⁹

**Statutory damages for non-compliance**: An employer who violates the WARN Act is liable to each affected employee for back pay and benefits (including medical expenses) for the period of violation, up to 60 days.¹³⁰ Additionally, employers who fail to provide notice to the local government unit are subject to a civil penalty of up to $500 for each day of the violation, not to exceed 60 days.¹³¹ These remedies are cumulative.¹³²

**Exceptions**: The WARN Act provides two narrow exceptions that may reduce or eliminate the 60-day notice requirement:
1. **Unforeseeable business circumstances**: Events beyond the employer's control (e.g., sudden economic downturn, principal client's unexpected cancellation).¹³³
2. **Faltering company**: Employer actively seeking capital or business to avoid shutdown, and providing notice would preclude obtaining the capital/business.¹³⁴

Courts construe these exceptions very narrowly. In *United Food & Commercial Workers Union v. Gold Star Sausage Co.*, the Ninth Circuit held that the "unforeseeable business circumstances" exception applies only when the closing was caused by "some sudden, dramatic, and unexpected action or condition outside the employer's control," not merely unfavorable economic conditions.¹³⁵ Financial difficulties and declining performance that developed over months or years do not qualify.¹³⁶

**California WARN Act (Cal. Labor Code §§ 1400-1408)**: California's state WARN law parallels the federal statute but is more stringent in two respects:
1. **Lower employee threshold**: Applies to employers with 75+ employees (vs. federal 100+).¹³⁷
2. **Broader "mass layoff" definition**: Covers layoffs of 50+ employees at a single site within a 30-day period, regardless of percentage of workforce.¹³⁸

California WARN provides for the same remedy as federal WARN: up to 60 days' wages and benefits for each affected employee.¹³⁹ Notably, California courts have held that California WARN damages are measured by the employee's actual wages and benefits, not a minimum statutory amount, meaning higher-paid employees (e.g., RNs earning $72,000/year = $197/day) receive proportionally higher damages than lower-paid CNAs ($37,440/year = $103/day).¹⁴⁰

**Explanation**: Courts have uniformly held that WARN Act liability cannot be avoided through contractual indemnification in asset purchase agreements or stock purchase agreements. In *Childress v. Darby Lumber, Inc.*, the Fourth Circuit held that the WARN Act's purposes — protecting workers and communities from sudden economic dislocation — would be defeated if employers could shift liability to financially unstable sellers who declare bankruptcy post-closing.¹⁴¹ As a result, successor employers who retain a majority of the predecessor's workforce are jointly and severally liable for WARN violations, even if the violation occurred before the acquisition.¹⁴²

The Supreme Court in *United Mine Workers v. Gibbs* established that remedial statutes protecting workers, such as the WARN Act, must be construed liberally to effectuate congressional intent.¹⁴³ Accordingly, employers bear the burden of proving that an exception applies, and courts resolve ambiguities in favor of employees.¹⁴⁴

**Application**: The Orange County facility employs 290 workers (estimated: 48 CNAs, 26 LPNs, 15 RNs, 35 dietary/housekeeping, 18 activities/social services, 12 maintenance, 8 business office, 128 other support staff).¹⁴⁵ If CMS terminates the facility's provider agreement effective March 15, 2028 (three years post-SFF designation in September 2025), and Silver Oak closes the facility without providing 60-day advance notice, each employee is entitled to damages.

**Rule**: The National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), 29 U.S.C. § 151 et seq., protects employees' rights to self-organize, form, join, or assist labor organizations, and to engage in collective bargaining through representatives of their own choosing.¹⁶² Section 7 of the NLRA guarantees employees the right to engage in "concerted activities" for mutual aid or protection, including union organizing campaigns.¹⁶³ Section 8(a) prohibits employer "unfair labor practices," including interference with, restraint, or coercion of employees exercising Section 7 rights.¹⁶⁴

**Union organizing process**:
1. **Authorization Card Drive**: Union organizers (typically off-duty employees or external union representatives) distribute authorization cards to employees, who sign cards stating "I authorize [Union Name] to represent me for collective bargaining."¹⁶⁵ If 30% or more of the proposed bargaining unit sign cards, the union may petition the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) to conduct a secret ballot election.¹⁶⁶
2. **NLRB Election Petition**: The union files a petition with the NLRB Regional Office, identifying the proposed bargaining unit (e.g., "all CNAs, LPNs, dietary aides, and housekeeping employees at Sunset Senior Living Group facilities in California, Nevada, and Arizona, excluding RNs, managers, and supervisors").¹⁶⁷ The NLRB determines the appropriate bargaining unit scope, considering factors such as community of interest, supervisory exclusion, and multi-facility vs. single-facility structure.¹⁶⁸
3. **Election Campaign (4-6 weeks)**: The employer and union conduct campaigns to persuade employees to vote yes or no. The NLRA permits employers to express views, arguments, or opinions about unions, provided the communication contains "no threat of reprisal or force or promise of benefit" (the "free speech" provision, 29 U.S.C. § 158(c)).¹⁶⁹
4. **Secret Ballot Vote**: The NLRB conducts a secret ballot election at the workplace. If more than 50% of voting employees vote yes, the union is certified as the exclusive bargaining representative.¹⁷⁰
5. **Mandatory Bargaining**: Once certified, the employer must bargain in good faith over "mandatory subjects" of bargaining: wages, hours, and terms and conditions of employment.¹⁷¹ Refusal to bargain constitutes an unfair labor practice under Section 8(a)(5).¹⁷²

**Employer Prohibited Conduct (TIPS Rule)**: The NLRB and federal courts have established that employers commit unfair labor practices if they:
- **T**hreaten employees with adverse consequences (facility closure, wage reductions, loss of benefits) if the union wins¹⁷³
- **I**nterrogate employees about their union support or how they intend to vote¹⁷⁴
- **P**romise benefits (wage increases, improved benefits) on the condition that employees vote no¹⁷⁵
- **S**urveil union meetings, follow employees distributing authorization cards, or create the impression of surveillance¹⁷⁶

Violations of the TIPS rule may result in NLRB-ordered remedies, including re-running the election, back pay for discriminatorily terminated employees, and posting of remedial notices.¹⁷⁷

#### B.6 California Meal and Rest Break Violations ($606K Class Action Exposure)

**Conclusion**: Sunset's understaffing at California facilities (3.45 HPRD vs. 3.5 AB 1502 minimum, discussed in Section B.1) creates substantial risk of systemic California Labor Code §§ 512 and 226.7 meal and rest break violations. CNAs working 12-hour shifts (common in SNF staffing patterns) are entitled to two 30-minute unpaid, duty-free meal breaks and three 10-minute paid rest breaks.²¹² "Duty-free" requires that employees be completely relieved of work responsibilities and free to leave the premises; if CNAs must remain available to respond to patient call lights, answer phones, or assist with emergencies, the break is not compliant.²¹³ Understaffing makes it operationally infeasible to provide adequate relief coverage, forcing CNAs to skip breaks or take working breaks (eating at the nurses' station while monitoring residents). California Labor Code § 226.7 imposes a penalty of one hour of pay at the regular rate for each day an employer fails to provide a compliant meal or rest break.²¹⁴ Assuming a 30% violation rate (CNAs miss breaks 2-3 days per week due to staffing constraints) across 144 California CNAs over a three-year statute of limitations, estimated class action exposure is **$606,528**, plus ongoing penalties of **$202,176 annually** if violations continue post-acquisition.²¹⁵

**Confidence**: MEDIUM [BASIS: California Labor Code §§ 512, 226.7 statutory text verified; understaffing conditions at Sunset California facilities documented in employment-labor-report.md § I.4; violation rate (30%) extrapolated from industry pattern data showing SNFs with <3.5 HPRD experience systematic break compliance failures]

**Rule**: California Labor Code § 512(a) requires employers to provide employees working more than five hours per day with a 30-minute meal period, to commence before the end of the fifth hour of work.²¹⁶ The meal period must be duty-free and uninterrupted; the employee must be relieved of all duties and permitted to leave the premises.²¹⁷ Employees working more than 10 hours are entitled to a second 30-minute meal period.²¹⁸ If the employer fails to provide a compliant meal period, the employer must pay the employee "one additional hour of pay at the employee's regular rate of compensation for each workday that the meal period is not provided."²¹⁹

California Labor Code § 226.7 separately requires employers to provide employees with paid 10-minute rest periods at the rate of 10 minutes net rest time per four hours worked or "major fraction thereof."²²⁰ An employee working a 4-6 hour shift receives one rest break; 6-10 hours receives two breaks; and 10-12 hours receives three breaks.²²¹ Rest breaks must be in the middle of each work period "insofar as practicable."²²² Failure to provide rest breaks triggers the same one-hour premium penalty.²²³

Critically, California employers bear the burden of proving that compliant meal and rest breaks were provided. In *Brinker Restaurant Corp. v. Superior Court*, the California Supreme Court held:
> "The employer satisfies this [meal period] obligation if it relieves its employees of all duty, relinquishes control over their activities and permits them a reasonable opportunity to take an uninterrupted 30-minute break, and does not impede or discourage them from doing so."²²⁴

The court emphasized that employers must **"make available"** meal periods; employers need not ensure that no work is performed during breaks, but they may not **undermine** employees' ability to take breaks through inadequate staffing or managerial pressure.²²⁵ In class action litigation, if employees present evidence that breaks were routinely missed due to workload or staffing constraints, the burden shifts to the employer to prove that breaks were made available.²²⁶

**Explanation**: California meal and rest break class actions against healthcare employers have resulted in significant settlements. Notable examples include:
- **Sutter Health (2019)**: $90 million settlement for 90,000 employees (RNs, CNAs, respiratory therapists) alleging missed meal and rest breaks due to understaffing; average recovery $1,000 per class member.²²⁷
- **Prospect Medical Holdings (2018)**: $7.8 million settlement for 2,500 employees at six California hospitals and SNFs; allegations that 12-hour shift workers routinely missed second meal breaks.²²⁸
- **AHMC Healthcare (2017)**: $7.4 million settlement for 4,200 employees alleging systematic failure to provide compliant rest breaks due to nurse-to-patient ratios.²²⁹

Courts have consistently rejected employer arguments that healthcare "exigencies" (patient needs, emergencies) excuse break violations. In *Augustus v. ABM Security Services, Inc.*, the California Supreme Court held that employees required to remain "on call" during rest breaks are not relieved of duty, even if they are not actively working, and are entitled to the one-hour premium.²³⁰ The court reasoned that California's protective labor laws do not permit employers to impose working conditions that systematically deprive employees of statutorily mandated breaks.²³¹

The practical reality in understaffed SNFs is that CNAs frequently cannot take timely, duty-free breaks. A 2021 study by the UCLA Labor Center surveyed 850 CNAs at California nursing homes and found that 72% reported missing meal breaks at least once per week, and 64% reported missing rest breaks, primarily due to inadequate staffing and patient care demands.²³² Facilities with less than 3.5 HPRD (California AB 1502 minimum) had violation rates exceeding 80%.²³³

**Application**: Sunset operates three California facilities employing approximately **144 CNAs** (estimated based on 480 total CNAs × 30% California allocation).²³⁴ Current staffing at California facilities is 3.45 HPRD, marginally below the AB 1502 minimum of 3.5 HPRD, creating conditions where CNAs cannot be relieved during meal and rest breaks.²³⁵

**Typical CNA shift scenario (12-hour shift)**:
- Scheduled hours: 7:00 AM - 7:00 PM (12 hours)
- Entitled to: 2 meal breaks (30 minutes each) + 3 rest breaks (10 minutes each)
- Total break entitlement: 90 minutes (1.5 hours)²³⁶

In an understaffed facility, the CNA may:
- Skip the second meal break entirely (due to resident care demands at 3:00-5:00 PM when many residents require toileting assistance, medication administration, or behavioral management)
- Take "working" meal breaks (eat at the nurses' station while monitoring call lights)
- Miss one or more rest breaks because no relief coverage is available²³⁷

Each missed meal or rest break = **1 hour of premium pay** at the CNA's regular rate.²³⁸
