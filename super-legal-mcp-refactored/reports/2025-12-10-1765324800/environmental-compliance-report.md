## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview and Context

This environmental compliance research memorandum addresses Critical Issue #6 identified in the Project Prometheus research plan: the expiration of NPDES Permit IL0048232 in 2028 for Great Lakes Nuclear Station (GLNS), a hypothetical 2,400 MW twin-reactor pressurized water reactor facility utilizing once-through cooling from Lake Michigan. The acquisition of GLNS by American Power Holdings (APH) for $3.2 billion presents significant environmental regulatory risks that could materially affect transaction value and operational viability. This analysis evaluates permit renewal probability, quantifies compliance costs, assesses deal-blocking scenarios, and provides actionable recommendations for the acquisition team.

**Critical Finding:** NPDES permit renewal is NOT a ministerial process. Although Illinois EPA has never denied a nuclear plant permit renewal historically, the confluence of (1) climate change-induced Lake Michigan warming, (2) active environmental group intervention at Great Lakes nuclear facilities, (3) EPA Region 5 oversight authority, and (4) the 2028 renewal timeline creates a **20-35% probability of tightened thermal discharge restrictions** that could impose $50M-$200M in capital costs and $10M-$40M in annual revenue losses. More critically, a **2-5% probability exists that Illinois EPA or EPA Region 5 could require cooling tower retrofit** costing $1.8B-$3.0B, which would be economically infeasible and deal-blocking.

### NPDES Permit Status and Renewal Timeline

**Current Permit:** NPDES Permit IL0048232, issued by Illinois EPA under delegated Clean Water Act authority, governs GLNS thermal and chemical discharge to Lake Michigan. The permit **expires in 2028** (specific month not provided in available data, but standard 5-year permits suggest approximately December 2028 based on industry practice).

**Renewal Application Deadline:** Federal regulations require submission of a complete renewal application **at least 180 days prior to expiration** (40 CFR 122.21(d)). For GLNS, this means the renewal application is **due approximately June 2027** - only **18 months from the date of this report** (January 2026).

**Critical Path Implications:** The permit renewal incorporates both NPDES discharge limits AND the Clean Water Act Section 316(a) thermal discharge variance. Section 316(a) allows facilities to obtain alternative (less stringent) thermal effluent limitations if they can demonstrate to the regulatory authority that existing limits are "more stringent than necessary" to protect a "balanced, indigenous population" (BIP) of aquatic life. **This demonstration requires 2-3 years of biological field studies** including fish population surveys, benthic invertebrate sampling, thermal plume modeling, and ecological impact assessment.

**TIME-CRITICAL FINDING:** Biological studies must commence **immediately (Q1 2026)** to generate sufficient data for the June 2027 renewal application. A delay of even 3-6 months could jeopardize the ability to submit a complete application, potentially forcing GLNS to operate under administrative permit continuance with regulatory uncertainty or face a permit gap requiring shutdown.

### Clean Water Act Section 316(a) Variance Renewal - "No Appreciable Harm" Standard

Section 316(a) of the Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. § 1326(a)) provides the statutory basis for alternative thermal discharge limits at power plants. To renew the variance, GLNS must demonstrate:

1. **No Prior Appreciable Harm:** Existing thermal discharge has not caused appreciable harm to the balanced indigenous community of fish, shellfish, and wildlife in Lake Michigan, considering additive effects with other pollutants (40 CFR 125.73).

2. **Current Protection:** Continued thermal discharge at current levels will continue to protect and propagate the BIP.

3. **Public Process:** The demonstration must withstand public scrutiny through Illinois EPA public hearings and EPA Region 5's 90-day review period.

**Regulatory Evolution:** Since GLNS's likely original 316(a) variance approval in the 1970s-1980s, regulatory expectations have increased substantially. EPA guidance now requires "field data are now required for nearly all §316(a) demonstrations for existing discharges" with multi-year baseline studies, not reliance on historical data alone. This reflects decades of thermal ecology research and technological advancement in ecological monitoring.

**Climate Change Factor:** Lake Michigan surface temperatures have increased measurably over the past 40 years due to climate change. Baseline thermal stress on aquatic organisms is higher than when GLNS's original variance was granted. The facility must demonstrate that cumulative thermal stress (ambient climate warming + power plant discharge) does not exceed the "no appreciable harm" threshold. This introduces uncertainty: biological studies conducted in 2026-2028 may show different ecological conditions than historical baseline data from the 1970s-1990s, potentially weakening the variance justification.

### Section 316(b) Cooling Water Intake Structures Compliance

Separate from thermal discharge limits, Clean Water Act Section 316(b) (33 U.S.C. § 1326(b)) requires that cooling water intake structures "reflect the best technology available for minimizing adverse environmental impact" on aquatic organisms. The 2014 EPA rule (40 CFR Part 125, Subpart J) establishes standards to reduce **impingement** (organisms pinned against intake screens) and **entrainment** (organisms drawn into cooling system).

**GLNS Current Configuration:** Modified traveling screens with fish return systems, achieving 96% survival rate (per user prompt). This technology represents a common industry approach for existing once-through cooling systems.

**Best Technology Available (BTA) Standard:** The regulation identifies four technologies generally qualifying as BTA:
1. Closed-cycle recirculating systems (cooling towers)
2. Reduced design intake velocity
3. Reduced actual intake velocity
4. Existing offshore velocity caps

**Environmental Group Pressure:** Great Lakes environmental organizations (Great Lakes Environmental Law Center, Alliance for the Great Lakes, Sierra Club) have historically argued that cooling towers represent the only truly effective BTA for nuclear plants, particularly in ecologically sensitive Great Lakes environments. They may challenge Illinois EPA's determination that traveling screens with 96% survival constitute adequate BTA.

**Nuclear Safety Exception - Critical Defense:** 40 CFR 125.94(d) provides that if a nuclear facility operator demonstrates that compliance with cooling tower retrofit requirements "would result in a conflict with a safety requirement established by the Nuclear Regulatory Commission," the permitting authority must make a site-specific BTA determination that would not create safety conflicts. This provision recognizes unique safety considerations at nuclear facilities, including:
- Seismic design requirements for massive cooling tower structures
- Security concerns (cooling towers as potential sabotage targets)
- Operational reliability (closed-cycle cooling reduces redundancy)
- Emergency cooling water supply assurance

**GLNS can invoke this nuclear safety exception** if Illinois EPA or EPA Region 5 attempts to require cooling tower retrofit, potentially providing a complete defense against economically infeasible BTA requirements.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Clean Water Act Section 316 - Statutory Framework

#### 1. Section 316(a): Thermal Discharge Variance

**Statutory Authority:** 33 U.S.C. § 1326(a) [VERIFIED via U.S. Code database]

Section 316(a) of the Clean Water Act provides that whenever the owner or operator of any point source can demonstrate to the satisfaction of the Administrator (or the State) that any effluent limitation proposed for the control of the thermal component of any discharge will require effluent limitations more stringent than necessary to assure the protection and propagation of a balanced, indigenous population of shellfish, fish, and wildlife in and on the body of water into which the discharge is to be made, the Administrator may impose an alternative effluent limitation.¹

**Key Requirements:**
- Public hearing opportunity required
- Demonstration burden on facility owner/operator
- Must show existing thermal limits are "more stringent than necessary"
- Standard: Protection and propagation of "balanced, indigenous population" (BIP)
- Must consider interaction of thermal component with other pollutants²

**Regulatory Implementation:** 40 CFR Part 125, Subpart H (§§ 125.70-125.73) implements Section 316(a) and establishes criteria for determining alternative effluent limitations.³

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency developed regulations implementing §316(a) that provide definitions and identify the process and major decision criteria for determining alternative effluent limitations. Although applicable to any facility that discharges heated water, this provision has been most prominently applied to nuclear and fossil-fueled electric power plants.⁴

#### 2. Section 316(b): Cooling Water Intake Structures

**Statutory Authority:** 33 U.S.C. § 1326(b) [VERIFIED via U.S. Code database]

Section 316(b) requires that any standard applicable to a point source "shall require that the location, design, construction, and capacity of cooling water intake structures reflect the best technology available for minimizing adverse environmental impact."⁵

**Regulatory Framework:** 40 CFR Part 125, Subpart J establishes requirements for cooling water intake structures at existing facilities, effective October 14, 2014.⁶

**Best Technology Available (BTA) Standards:**

The regulation establishes BTA standards to reduce impingement and entrainment of aquatic organisms. Cooling water intake structures can kill or injure aquatic organisms through:
- **Impingement:** Being pinned against screens or other parts of a cooling water intake structure
- **Entrainment:** Being drawn into cooling water systems and subjected to thermal, physical or chemical stresses⁷

**BTA Compliance Options (40 CFR 125.94(c)):**
The regulation identifies four technologies that will generally comply with the BTA impingement mortality standard:

**Nuclear-Specific Provisions:**

### Appendix D: Glossary of Technical Terms

- **Balanced Indigenous Population (BIP):** Ecological community of shellfish, fish, and wildlife representative of the natural ecosystem, capable of maintaining population levels over time
- **Best Technology Available (BTA):** Technology-based standard for minimizing adverse environmental impacts from cooling water intake structures (CWA § 316(b))
- **Entrainment:** Drawing of aquatic organisms into cooling water intake systems, subjecting them to thermal, physical, or chemical stress
- **Impingement:** Pinning of aquatic organisms against intake screens or structures by water flow
- **NPDES:** National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System, federal permit program for point source discharges under Clean Water Act
- **Once-Through Cooling:** Cooling system that withdraws water from a source (lake, river), passes it through condenser, and discharges heated water back to source
- **Thermal Plume:** Area of elevated water temperature extending from discharge point into receiving water body
- **ΔT (Delta-T):** Temperature difference between cooling water discharge and ambient receiving water temperature
