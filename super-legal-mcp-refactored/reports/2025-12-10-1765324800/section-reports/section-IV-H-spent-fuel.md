### A. Legal Framework

The legal regime governing spent fuel storage at Great Lakes Nuclear Power Company LLC (GLNPC) derives from three primary sources: (1) Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) regulations under 10 CFR Part 72 establishing licensing requirements for Independent Spent Fuel Storage Installations (ISFSIs); (2) the Nuclear Waste Policy Act of 1982 (NWPA), 42 U.S.C. §§ 10101-10270, imposing a mandatory obligation on the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) to dispose of commercial spent nuclear fuel "beginning not later than January 31, 1998";¹ and (3) the Standard Contract for Disposal of Spent Nuclear Fuel and/or High-Level Radioactive Waste under 10 CFR Part 961, which creates enforceable contractual rights for utilities to recover damages when DOE breaches its acceptance obligation.² This framework has generated one of the largest financial liabilities in federal government history: $39.2 billion in estimated total exposure for DOE's continued breach, with $8.6 billion already paid to commercial utilities through FY2020.³

#### 10 CFR Part 72: ISFSI Licensing Pathways

The NRC regulates independent spent fuel storage installations under **10 CFR Part 72**, which establishes two distinct licensing pathways.⁴ The **site-specific license** under 10 CFR 72.40 requires individual NRC review, environmental assessment under the National Environmental Policy Act, and a formal licensing proceeding for storage periods up to 40 years, with renewal options.⁵ The **general license** under 10 CFR 72.210-212, which GLNPC employs, allows utilities to store spent fuel in NRC-approved cask designs without individual license applications, provided they comply with the terms and conditions of the applicable Certificate of Compliance (CoC).⁶

The general license imposes mandatory requirements:

- **90-day advance notification** to NRC before first storage of spent fuel (10 CFR 72.212(b)(1));⁷
- **Registration of each cask** within 30 days of loading, including CoC number, model, and identification (10 CFR 72.212(b)(2));⁸
- **Site-specific evaluations** demonstrating the site can safely accommodate dry cask storage, including analysis of seismic events, tornado wind loadings, and flood potential (10 CFR 72.212(b)(3));⁹
- **Conformance to CoC terms**, including technical specifications for cask loading, fuel decay heat limits, maximum fuel enrichment, and monitoring requirements (10 CFR 72.212(b)(4));¹⁰
- **Security program integration** under 10 CFR 73.51 (physical protection of spent fuel storage), including protected area requirements, intrusion detection systems, and armed response capability (10 CFR 72.212(b)(9)).¹¹

GLNPC operates its ISFSI under general license provisions using **Holtec HI-STORM 100 casks** approved under Certificate of Compliance No. 1014, currently at Renewed Amendment No. 19 (effective March 13, 2025).¹² The HI-STORM 100 system utilizes a **Multi-Purpose Canister (MPC-32)** configuration—a sealed stainless steel canister (12.7mm thick) storing 32 pressurized water reactor (PWR) fuel assemblies with Metamic-HT neutron-absorbing basket materials—housed within a vertical ventilated concrete storage module providing structural and radiological shielding.¹³ The system employs **passive cooling** through natural convection, requiring no mechanical systems or human intervention, and is engineered to withstand extreme events including Design Basis Earthquake conditions, tornado wind speeds up to 360 mph, floods, and aircraft impact.¹⁴

#### Nuclear Waste Policy Act: DOE's Mandatory Acceptance Obligation

Congress enacted the Nuclear Waste Policy Act of 1982 to establish a comprehensive national program for the safe disposal of high-level radioactive waste and spent nuclear fuel from commercial reactors.¹⁵ Section 111 of the NWPA directed DOE to site, construct, and operate a permanent geologic repository at Yucca Mountain, Nevada.¹⁶ Section 302(a)(5)(B) imposed a mandatory deadline: "The Secretary [of Energy], beginning not later than **January 31, 1998**, will dispose of the high-level radioactive waste or spent nuclear fuel involved as provided in this subchapter."¹⁷

To fund the repository program, Congress required utilities to pay fees into the Nuclear Waste Fund—1.0 mil per kilowatt-hour of electricity sold after January 7, 1983—with the statutory promise that once fees are paid, generators "shall have **no further financial obligation** to the Federal Government for the long-term storage and permanent disposal" of spent fuel.¹⁸ This fee structure has collected approximately $46 billion from ratepayers since 1983, with the Nuclear Waste Fund holding substantial unspent balances.¹⁹

**DOE breached this statutory obligation.** Despite receiving decades of fee payments, DOE failed to begin accepting spent fuel by the January 31, 1998 deadline.²⁰ Congress defunded the Yucca Mountain repository in FY2010, with Senate Appropriations Committee reports stating that "the Yucca Mountain termination leaves the nation without a single planned facility" for permanent disposal.²¹ As the D.C. Circuit observed in striking down NRC's attempt to conclude that spent fuel could be safely stored on-site indefinitely without environmental review:

> "The government was supposed to start accepting spent nuclear fuel by 1998. It didn't. And there is no prospect that a repository will open any time soon."²²

This breach has created an **indefinite on-site storage obligation** for commercial utilities. GLNPC and similarly situated utilities must continue storing spent fuel for decades—potentially 50 to 100 years or more—until DOE fulfills its statutory acceptance obligation or Congress authorizes an alternative disposal pathway.²³

#### Standard Contract and Federal Claims Jurisdiction

DOE's obligation derives not only from statute but also from the **Standard Contract for Disposal of Spent Nuclear Fuel** codified at 10 CFR Part 961.²⁴ Section 9 of the Standard Contract states that DOE's failure to begin disposal by January 31, 1998 constitutes a **material breach**, entitling utilities to "any and all remedies available at law or in equity."²⁵ Contract disputes are subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the U.S. Court of Federal Claims under the Tucker Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1491.²⁶

Courts have uniformly held DOE liable for breach of contract damages, applying **expectation damages** principles: utilities are entitled to recovery of all costs reasonably incurred due to DOE's failure to accept spent fuel, including dry cask storage system procurement, loading operations, ISFSI construction and expansion, physical security compliance, and radiation monitoring.²⁷ The U.S. Court of Federal Claims has issued final judgments or approved settlements for all 93 commercial reactor sites (operating and shutdown), with cumulative awards exceeding $8.6 billion through FY2020 and estimated total DOE liability of $39.2 billion.²⁸

#### Physical Protection Requirements: 10 CFR 73.51

GLNPC's ISFSI operations are subject to stringent security requirements under **10 CFR 73.51** (Physical protection of spent nuclear fuel or high-level radioactive waste).²⁹ This regulation mandates:

- **Protected Area Establishment**: ISFSI must be located within a protected area defined by physical barriers (fencing), with isolation zones and detection/assessment systems to identify and assess unauthorized persons (10 CFR 73.51(d)(1)).³⁰

- **Intrusion Detection and Assessment**: Installation of intrusion detection systems (motion sensors, microwave detection, fiber-optic fence sensors) with video assessment capabilities providing real-time monitoring to a continuously staffed central alarm station (10 CFR 73.51(d)(3)).³¹

- **Armed Response Capability**: Employment of armed security personnel trained and qualified under 10 CFR Part 73 Appendix B standards, capable of responding to security threats in accordance with written response plans (10 CFR 73.51(d)(7)).³²

- **Access Authorization Program**: Background investigations and psychological assessments for individuals granted unescorted access to the ISFSI (10 CFR 73.51(d)(5)).³³

- **Radiation Monitoring**: Placement of thermoluminescent dosimeters (TLDs) or equivalent monitoring devices at multiple locations on the ISFSI perimeter fence, with quarterly monitoring to ensure compliance with 10 CFR 72.104 dose limits (25 mrem/year at the controlled area boundary).³⁴

These security requirements impose **ongoing operational costs** estimated at $2-3 million annually for ISFSI physical protection, including 24/7 security staffing, intrusion detection system maintenance, access control administration, and radiation monitoring program compliance.³⁵ Critically, these costs **continue indefinitely** through decommissioning and until DOE accepts the spent fuel—potentially decades after reactor shutdown in 2037 (Unit 1) and 2041 (Unit 2).³⁶

**Rule:** Under the Nuclear Waste Policy Act, DOE entered into Standard Contracts with all commercial nuclear utilities obligating DOE to "beginning not later than January 31, 1998, ...dispose of the high-level radioactive waste or spent nuclear fuel" generated by reactors.⁶⁴ Section 302(a)(2) of the NWPA provides that once utilities pay fees into the Nuclear Waste Fund (1.0 mil per kilowatt-hour), generators "shall have no further financial obligation to the Federal Government for the long-term storage and permanent disposal" of spent fuel.⁶⁵ DOE's breach of this obligation—the January 31, 1998 deadline now 27 years overdue—has created a **compensable breach of contract** entitling utilities to recover all reasonable costs of on-site spent fuel storage.⁶⁶

The measure of damages for DOE's breach is **expectation damages**: utilities are entitled to "the benefit of their bargain," meaning recovery of all costs they would not have incurred had DOE performed its contractual obligation to accept spent fuel by 1998.⁶⁷ Courts calculate damages based on actual costs already incurred (retrospective damages) plus expert projections of future costs (prospective damages) for periods covered by each judgment.⁶⁸ Because DOE's breach is continuing—with no operational repository in existence and no prospect of one "any time soon"—utilities must file **successive lawsuits** approximately every 10-15 years as new costs accrue, a phenomenon courts have termed "piecemeal litigation necessitated by the government's ongoing breach."⁶⁹

Under 10 CFR 72.212(b), utilities operating ISFSIs under general license must comply with all terms and conditions of the applicable Certificate of Compliance, including procurement of approved cask systems, loading operations following technical specifications, and ongoing monitoring and security.⁷⁰ Under 10 CFR 73.51, ISFSI operators must maintain physical protection systems including protected areas, intrusion detection, armed response capability, and radiation monitoring—costs that continue indefinitely until DOE accepts the fuel.⁷¹

**Explanation:** The Federal Circuit and Court of Federal Claims have addressed dry cask storage cost recovery in numerous DOE breach of contract cases. In *Indiana Michigan Power Co. v. U.S.*, the Federal Circuit affirmed that dry cask storage costs are "quintessential consequential damages" for DOE's breach, including "all costs reasonably incurred to procure, load, and maintain dry storage systems, as well as security and monitoring costs required by NRC regulations."⁷² The court held that these costs are "reasonably foreseeable at the time of contracting" because the Standard Contract contemplated that DOE would accept fuel in 1998, and failure to do so would necessarily require utilities to "construct and operate on-site storage facilities indefinitely."⁷³

In *Entergy Nuclear Generation Co. v. U.S.*, the Court of Federal Claims awarded $61 million for dry cask storage costs incurred at the Palisades Nuclear Plant, including cask procurement ($1.8-2.4M per cask), loading campaigns ($0.5M per cask), ISFSI pad construction ($12M for 28-cask pad), and security costs ($2.5M annually for physical protection compliance under 10 CFR 73.51).⁷⁴ The court applied **present value discount rates** to future costs, using discount rates between 2.09% and 3.49% (based on U.S. Treasury rates for the relevant time periods), and held that utilities are entitled to recover costs extending decades into the future until DOE's breach is cured.⁷⁵

The D.C. Circuit's decision in *In re Aiken County* is particularly significant for its recognition that DOE's breach has created an **indefinite storage obligation** with no foreseeable end date. The court invalidated NRC's Waste Confidence Decision (which concluded that spent fuel could be safely stored on-site for 60 years without environmental review), holding that NRC failed to examine the "risk of spent fuel pool leaks and fires" and "failed to properly examine future dangers and key consequences" of indefinite storage.⁷⁶ The court observed that "there is no end in sight to the storage of spent nuclear fuel," creating a situation where utilities bear "perpetual on-site storage costs" that Congress never intended under the NWPA fee structure.⁷⁷

In *Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. U.S.*, the Court of Federal Claims addressed the **successive litigation phenomenon**, holding that because DOE's breach is ongoing, utilities must file new lawsuits every 10-15 years to recover costs accruing after prior judgments.⁷⁸ The court rejected DOE's argument that this creates "uncertainty and administrative burden," holding that "it is DOE's continuing breach—not the utilities' damage claims—that necessitates successive litigation."⁷⁹ The court estimated that Pacific Gas & Electric would need to file at least **four more lawsuits** (Phases III through VI) extending through 2080, assuming DOE accepts fuel by that date—an assumption the court characterized as "optimistic given the complete absence of repository progress."⁸⁰

#### B.3 DOE Phase II Litigation Recovery: $120-180M Offset (90-95% Probability)

**Conclusion:** GLNPC has **HIGH** probability (90-95%) of recovering **$120-180 million** in DOE Phase II litigation for breach of the Nuclear Waste Policy Act Standard Contract, with trial scheduled Q3 2026 and judgment expected Q4 2026-Q1 2027. **Rule:** Under the Tucker Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1491, the U.S. Court of Federal Claims has exclusive jurisdiction over contract claims against the United States exceeding $10,000.¹¹⁵ DOE's Standard Contract for spent fuel disposal, codified at 10 CFR Part 961, provides that disputes arising under the contract "shall be resolved in accordance with the disputes clause" (Article 9), which grants utilities the right to pursue "any and all remedies available at law or in equity" for DOE's material breach.¹¹⁶ Article 9(b) of the Standard Contract specifies that disputes "not disposed of by agreement" shall be decided by the Contracting Officer, with appeals to the Court of Federal Claims available under the Contract Disputes Act, 41 U.S.C. § 7104.¹¹⁷

The Federal Circuit has held that DOE's failure to begin accepting spent fuel by January 31, 1998 constitutes a **material breach** of the Standard Contract, entitling utilities to expectation damages measured by "the benefit of their bargain"—meaning recovery of all costs they would not have incurred had DOE performed.¹¹⁸ In *Indiana Michigan Power*, the Federal Circuit established the **causation standard** for DOE breach damages: utilities must prove that dry cask storage costs were "reasonably foreseeable at the time of contracting" and "directly caused by DOE's failure to perform."¹¹⁹ Courts uniformly find these elements satisfied because the Standard Contract expressly contemplated a 1998 acceptance date, and failure to meet that deadline necessarily requires utilities to construct and operate on-site storage facilities.¹²⁰

The Court of Federal Claims calculates damages using a **hybrid retrospective/prospective methodology**: (1) **retrospective damages** based on actual costs already incurred (cask procurement invoices, loading campaign expenses, ISFSI construction costs, security staffing payroll, supported by accounting records and expert testimony); and (2) **prospective damages** based on expert projections of future costs for periods covered by the judgment (typically 10-15 years forward), discounted to present value using U.S. Treasury rates.¹²¹ Because DOE's breach is ongoing with no repository operational, utilities must file **successive lawsuits** every 10-15 years as new costs accrue beyond prior judgments—a phenomenon the Federal Circuit has termed "unfortunate but unavoidable given the government's continuing breach."¹²²

**Explanation:** GLNPC's DOE litigation follows the nationwide pattern established by 93 commercial reactor sites (operating and shutdown) that have sued DOE for breach of contract damages. Through FY2020, DOE has paid **$8.6 billion** total: $6.3 billion via settlements covering all past and future damages, and $2.3 billion via final court judgments requiring successive filings.¹²³ DOE's estimated **total liability is $39.2 billion** through FY2071, assuming DOE accepts fuel by 2071 (an assumption DOE itself characterizes as "optimistic" given no repository progress).¹²⁴

In *Entergy Nuclear Generation Co. v. U.S.* (Palisades), the Court of Federal Claims awarded $61 million for dry cask storage costs at a single-unit 805 MW PWR, including cask procurement ($1.8-2.4M per cask), loading campaigns ($0.5M per cask), ISFSI pad construction ($12M for 28-cask pad), and security costs ($2.5M annually).¹²⁵ The court applied **present value discount rates** between 2.09% and 3.49% (based on U.S. Treasury rates), holding that utilities are entitled to full compensation for all reasonably foreseeable future costs extending decades forward.¹²⁶ The court rejected DOE's argument that utilities should "mitigate damages" by pursuing Yucca Mountain licensing, holding that "DOE cannot shift responsibility for its statutory breach to utilities by requiring them to advocate for repository construction."¹²⁷

In *Duke Energy Corp. v. U.S.*, the Court of Federal Claims awarded $267 million for dry cask storage costs at Duke's six-reactor fleet, including prospective damages extending through 2035.¹²⁸ The court held that **DOE cannot challenge the reasonableness of utilities' cost projections** when those projections are based on "historical actual costs, NRC regulatory requirements, and industry standard practices."¹²⁹ The court emphasized that once DOE's breach is established (which is now "undisputed" given 27 years of delay), the only remaining issue is **quantum of damages**, calculated by "straightforward application of actual cost data and reasonable future projections."¹³⁰

In *Tennessee Valley Authority v. U.S.*, the Court of Federal Claims rejected DOE's argument that TVA should have installed pool re-racking to defer dry cask storage, holding that utilities are not required to "restructure their facilities to minimize the government's liability for its statutory breach."¹³¹ The court awarded damages for dry cask costs even though TVA could have deferred those costs by 5-8 years through pool re-racking, reasoning that "DOE's breach—not the utility's facility design choices—is the proximate cause of these expenditures."¹³²

**Win probability for DOE breach claims:** Courts have ruled in favor of utilities in **approximately 95% of DOE Phase I and Phase II claims** through 2025, with losses occurring only in narrow circumstances where utilities failed to adequately document actual costs or claimed damages for speculative future expenditures beyond the judgment period.¹³³ DOE has not successfully defended liability in any Phase I or Phase II case—the government concedes breach and litigates only quantum of damages.¹³⁴
