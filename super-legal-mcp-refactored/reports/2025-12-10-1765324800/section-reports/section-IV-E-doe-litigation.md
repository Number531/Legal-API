#### A.1. Nuclear Waste Policy Act - DOE Standard Contract Framework

Congress enacted the Nuclear Waste Policy Act (NWPA), 42 U.S.C. §§ 10101-10270, to establish federal responsibility for permanent disposal of commercial spent nuclear fuel. Section 10222 directed the Secretary of Energy to execute Standard Contracts with utilities requiring DOE to "take title to" and accept delivery of spent fuel at a permanent geological repository, with services commencing "by January 31, 1998."¹ This statutory deadline is **unconditional**—DOE cannot excuse performance based on budgetary constraints, political opposition to repository siting, or regulatory delays.²

DOE's failure to commence acceptance services by the statutory deadline constitutes **material breach of contract** as a matter of law. In *Indiana Michigan Power Co. v. United States*, 422 F.3d 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2005), the Federal Circuit established the controlling standard:

> The Government breached the contracts by failing to begin disposing of the SNF by January 31, 1998 ... The delay constitutes a partial breach, entitling the utilities to recover damages for that partial breach ... The contracts remain in effect, and the utilities may recover damages for costs incurred during any particular period of delay.³

The Court of Federal Claims has developed a **"partial breach" doctrine** specific to DOE's ongoing failure.⁴ The Standard Contract remains in effect—it is not terminated or repudiated—but DOE's delay constitutes a compensable breach. Utilities recover **incremental costs** (costs that would not have been incurred but for DOE's breach) while preserving rights to future performance.⁵ This framework enables **phased litigation**: Phase I establishes liability and damages methodology; Phase II and subsequent phases apply the same methodology to additional time periods without relitigating liability.⁶

**Recoverable Cost Categories** (uniformly approved across 78+ utility claims since 1998):⁷
- Dry cask storage system acquisition and deployment
- Independent Spent Fuel Storage Installation (ISFSI) construction, licensing, and expansion
- NRC licensing fees and regulatory compliance (10 C.F.R. Part 72)

- Radiation monitoring and environmental surveillance

- Decommissioning cost escalation attributable to extended storage

**DOE's Total Liability**: As of September 2024, DOE's estimated liability for spent fuel breach claims ranges from **$37.6 billion to $44.5 billion**, with approximately **$9 billion already paid** through the U.S. Treasury Judgment Fund.⁸ At least **78 breach claims** have been filed, and no utility has failed to recover spent fuel storage costs in final adjudication.⁹

#### A.2. Illinois Property Tort Law - Nuisance, Trespass, Inverse Condemnation

Illinois common law recognizes three theories under which neighboring landowners may seek damages from industrial facility operations:

**Nuisance**: A continuous interference with the use and enjoyment of property that is substantial and unreasonable.¹⁰ Illinois courts apply a balancing test weighing the gravity of harm to plaintiffs against the utility of defendant's conduct. For long-operating facilities, the **statute of limitations** (735 ILCS 5/13-202) imposes a 5-year bar on claims accruing when operations commence, absent a showing of "continuing nuisance" with new injuries occurring within the limitations period.¹¹

**Trespass**: A physical invasion of plaintiff's property without permission.¹² Subsurface contamination (e.g., tritium in groundwater) may constitute trespass even if contaminant levels remain below regulatory limits, provided plaintiff demonstrates actual invasion.¹³ The statute of limitations accrues when the invasion occurs or when plaintiff discovers (or should have discovered) the intrusion through reasonable diligence.¹⁴

**Inverse Condemnation**: The Illinois Constitution (Article I, § 15) provides that private property shall not be taken or damaged for public use without just compensation.¹⁵ Inverse condemnation requires: (1) government action (not private party conduct), (2) a taking or damaging of property, and (3) public use.¹⁶ Federal regulatory requirements (e.g., NRC-mandated Emergency Planning Zones) generally do not create private operator liability for inverse condemnation, as the restriction flows from government regulation rather than private party action.¹⁷

**Statute of Limitations**: 735 ILCS 5/13-202 imposes a 5-year statute of limitations for "injury done to property, real or personal."¹⁸ Claims accrue when the injury occurs, not when damages are discovered or when plaintiff chooses to file suit.¹⁹ For nuclear facilities operating 40+ years, the statute of limitations defense is typically dispositive absent evidence of new injuries occurring within the 5-year window.²⁰

#### A.3. Age Discrimination in Employment Act - Disparate Impact Standard

The Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA), 29 U.S.C. §§ 621-634, prohibits employers from discriminating against employees age 40 and older in hiring, termination, compensation, and other employment terms.²¹ ADEA plaintiffs may proceed under two theories:

**Disparate Treatment**: Plaintiff must prove age was the "but-for" cause of the adverse employment action (*Gross v. FBL Financial Services*, 557 U.S. 167 (2009)).²² This standard requires direct or circumstantial evidence that age motivated the employer's decision, such as age-related comments, replacement by a significantly younger employee, or pretext in the employer's stated reasons.²³

**Disparate Impact**: Plaintiff demonstrates that a facially neutral employment policy has a disproportionate adverse effect on older workers (*Smith v. City of Jackson*, 544 U.S. 228 (2005)).²⁴ The plaintiff's burden includes: (1) identifying the specific policy or practice, (2) proving statistically significant disparity, and (3) showing causation between policy and adverse impact.²⁵ Employer defenses include demonstrating a **Reasonable Factor Other Than Age (RFOA)**, defined as a business justification based on legitimate, age-neutral criteria (29 U.S.C. § 623(f)(1)).²⁶

**Damages**: ADEA remedies include back pay, front pay (if reinstatement impractical), lost benefits, and reasonable attorneys' fees.²⁷ If the violation is **"willful"** (employer knew or showed reckless disregard for ADEA requirements), the court may award **liquidated damages equal to compensatory damages**, effectively doubling the award (*Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins*, 507 U.S. 604, 617 (1993)).²⁸ Willfulness findings typically require evidence that the employer: (1) lacked ADEA compliance training, (2) failed to consult legal counsel, or (3) disregarded statistical evidence of age-based disparities.²⁹

**Reduction in Force (RIF) Context**: When employers eliminate positions in mass layoffs, disparate impact claims are more viable than disparate treatment claims. Courts examine RIF methodology for objective, documented criteria (skills matrices, performance ratings, position redundancy analysis) versus subjective factors susceptible to age bias (assessments of "adaptability," "energy," or "long-term potential").³⁰

If a violation is **"willful"** (employer knew or showed reckless disregard for ADEA requirements), courts may award **liquidated damages equal to compensatory damages**, effectively doubling the award. *Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins*, 507 U.S. 604, 617 (1993).⁶⁸ [VERIFIED: Supreme Court] Willfulness findings typically require evidence that the employer: (1) lacked ADEA compliance training before the RIF, (2) failed to consult legal counsel on age impact, or (3) conducted no analysis of alternative RIF scenarios to minimize age disparity.⁶⁹ [METHODOLOGY: Expert Judgment based on EEOC enforcement patterns]

**Explanation**: In *Gross v. FBL Financial Services, Inc.*, 557 U.S. 167, 177 (2009), the Supreme Court established that **disparate treatment** claims under ADEA require proof that age was the "but-for" cause of the adverse action, not merely a "motivating factor."⁷⁰ [VERIFIED: Supreme Court] This heightened causation standard makes individual disparate treatment claims difficult to prove absent direct evidence of age animus (e.g., manager comments about "old guard," "new blood," or age-related stereotypes).

However, *Gross* does not eliminate **disparate impact** claims. *Smith v. City of Jackson* permits plaintiffs to challenge facially neutral policies that statistically disadvantage older workers, even without proving intentional discrimination.⁷¹ The employer's RFOA defense is less demanding than Title VII's "business necessity" standard—RFOA requires only that the factor be "reasonable," not that it be essential to business operations.⁷²

In Reduction in Force cases, courts examine whether the employer used **objective, documented criteria** (position eliminations based on skills matrices, performance ratings, seniority systems) versus **subjective assessments** (manager evaluations of "adaptability," "energy," "potential for advancement").⁷³ [INFERRED: ADEA RIF case law patterns] Subjective criteria are susceptible to age stereotypes (assumptions that older workers lack technological skills, resist change, or have shorter remaining work tenure) and therefore increase RFOA defense vulnerability.⁷⁴

**Application**: GLNPC conducted a Reduction in Force in April 2024, eliminating **42 positions**. Of the 42 terminated employees, **35 were age 55 or older (83%)**. Peterson, age 62 with 18 years of service, was terminated as a Senior Operations Engineer and allegedly replaced by an employee age 38.

**Statistical Evidence**: Assuming GLNPC's workforce is 40% age 55+ (typical nuclear plant demographics), the expected number of age 55+ terminations in a random 42-employee RIF would be approximately 16.8 (42 × 0.40). The actual result—35 age 55+ terminations—is **208% of expected** under a race-neutral RIF. Chi-square analysis would likely show statistical significance at p < 0.01, establishing prima facie disparate impact.⁷⁵ [METHODOLOGY: Statistical analysis - chi-square test for expected vs. actual age distribution; assumes workforce is 40% age 55+ based on nuclear industry benchmarks]

**RFOA Defense Strength (Unknown)**: GLNPC must demonstrate that the April 2024 RIF was based on reasonable, age-neutral factors. Potential RFOA defenses include:

1. **Position Redundancy Analysis**: GLNPC eliminated specific positions (e.g., "Senior Operations Engineer") based on operational restructuring, consolidation of functions, or post-PPA renegotiation workload reductions. The fact that many eliminated positions happened to be held by older employees does not establish age discrimination if the position eliminations were justified by business needs.

2. **Skills Requirements**: Remaining positions require specialized skills, certifications, or NRC operator licenses that eliminated employees did not possess. For example, if GLNPC retained younger employees with critical NRC Senior Reactor Operator (SRO) licenses or specialized digital instrumentation expertise, this could constitute RFOA.

3. **Performance Ratings**: Eliminated employees had documented performance deficiencies, disciplinary records, or lower performance ratings compared to retained employees, regardless of age.
