### A. Legal Framework

The federal tax treatment of American Power Holdings LLC's (APH) $3.2 billion acquisition of Great Lakes Nuclear Power Company LLC (GLNPC) is governed by a complex framework of statutory provisions, Treasury regulations, and state tax laws that collectively determine the transaction's after-tax economics for both buyer and seller.

**Federal Tax Structure Authority**

The transaction involves the purchase of 100% of the membership interests in GLNPC, a Delaware limited liability company taxed as a partnership under the default classification rules of Treasury Regulation § 301.7701-3(b)(1).¹ Because GLNPC is classified as a partnership rather than a corporation, the transaction does not qualify for the IRC § 338(h)(10) election frequently used in corporate acquisitions to achieve a step-up in tax basis.² Section 338(h)(10) is available only when the target is an S corporation or a member of an affiliated corporate group filing consolidated returns.³

Instead, the appropriate mechanism for achieving a stepped-up tax basis in GLNPC's assets is the **IRC § 754 election**, which permits a partnership to adjust the "inside basis" of partnership property when a partnership interest is transferred.⁴ When a partnership makes a valid IRC § 754 election, IRC § 743(b) requires an adjustment to the basis of partnership property with respect to the transferee partner (APH) equal to the difference between the transferee's cost basis in the partnership interest and the transferee's proportionate share of the adjusted basis of partnership property.⁵ This adjustment is specific to the transferee and does not affect the basis of other partners if any remain.⁶

The economic effect of an IRC § 754 election is functionally identical to a Section 338(h)(10) election for corporate targets: the buyer receives a step-up in the tax basis of the target's assets to fair market value (the purchase price), enabling increased depreciation and amortization deductions over the recovery periods prescribed by the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS) under IRC § 168 and the amortization rules for intangibles under IRC § 197.⁷ The critical advantage for partnership targets is the avoidance of entity-level tax—the seller recognizes gain only on the sale of the membership interest (a single level of tax under IRC § 741), and the IRC § 754 adjustment occurs without triggering any additional tax at the partnership level.⁸

**Purchase Price Allocation Methodology**

Both buyer and seller must allocate the $3.2 billion purchase price among GLNPC's assets using the **residual method** prescribed by IRC § 1060 and Treasury Regulation § 1.338-6.⁹ The residual method requires sequential allocation through seven asset classes, with each class receiving an allocation equal to its fair market value before any amount flows to the next class.¹⁰ The seven classes are:

- **Class I**: Cash and general deposit accounts
- **Class II**: Actively traded personal property (e.g., marketable securities, certificates of deposit)
- **Class III**: Accounts receivable and other mark-to-market assets
- **Class IV**: Inventory (including nuclear fuel assemblies and spare parts)
- **Class V**: All tangible property and property that can be depreciated under IRC § 167 (excluding property in Classes I-IV and intangible property in Classes VI-VII)
- **Class VI**: Section 197 intangibles except goodwill and going concern value (including government-issued licenses, customer contracts, workforce in place)
- **Class VII**: Goodwill and going concern value (residual amount)¹¹

Both parties must file Form 8883 (Asset Allocation Statement Under Section 1060) with their federal tax returns for the year of transfer, reporting consistent allocations.¹² Inconsistent reporting between buyer and seller triggers automatic IRS scrutiny.¹³

**Nuclear Plant Depreciation**

The IRS classifies nuclear electric generation plants as Asset Class 49.12 under the MACRS depreciation system, with a **15-year recovery period** using the 150% declining balance method and half-year convention.¹⁴ This is notably shorter than the 20-year recovery period for general steam production plants (Asset Class 49.13).¹⁵ The shorter recovery period results in accelerated depreciation deductions in the early years following closing, maximizing the present value of tax savings.

**Intangible Asset Amortization**

IRC § 197 governs the amortization of intangible assets acquired in transactions constituting the acquisition of a trade or business.¹⁶ Section 197 intangibles are amortized ratably over a 15-year period beginning with the month the intangible is acquired.¹⁷ Qualifying intangibles include:

- Licenses, permits, and other rights granted by a governmental unit (IRC § 197(d)(1)(D))—including NRC Operating Licenses NPF-37 and NPF-66¹⁸
- Customer-based intangibles, including customer contracts and relationships (IRC § 197(d)(1)(F))—including the Power Purchase Agreement with Northern Illinois Electric Company (NIEC)¹⁹
- Goodwill and going concern value (IRC § 197(d)(1)(A))²⁰

**Illinois State Tax Framework**

Illinois imposes a combined corporate income tax rate of **9.5%**, consisting of a 7.0% corporate income tax under 35 ILCS 5/201(b) and a 2.5% personal property replacement tax under 35 ILCS 5/201(c).²¹ Illinois uses a **single sales factor** apportionment formula, meaning corporate income is apportioned based solely on the ratio of Illinois-sourced sales to total sales.²² Property and payroll factors were eliminated from Illinois apportionment in 2001.²³

For electricity sales, Illinois sources revenue to the location of the customer under 35 ILCS 5/304(a)(3)(C-5)(viii).²⁴ Because GLNPC's sole revenue source is the PPA with NIEC, an Illinois investor-owned utility serving Northern Illinois customers, 100% of GLNPC's revenue is Illinois-sourced, resulting in 100% Illinois apportionment of taxable income.²⁵

**Critical 2026 Illinois Tax Law Change**

Effective for tax years ending on or after December 31, 2025, Illinois is transitioning from the "Joyce" method to the "Finnigan" method for combined reporting groups.²⁶ Under the Joyce method, the sales factor numerator includes only Illinois-sourced sales of combined group members with Illinois nexus.²⁷ Under the Finnigan method, the sales factor numerator includes Illinois-sourced sales of all combined group members, including those without Illinois nexus.²⁸ For multistate groups with out-of-state entities making sales to Illinois customers (e.g., through e-commerce or remote services), the Finnigan method increases the Illinois apportionment factor, thereby increasing Illinois taxable income and Illinois tax liability.²⁹

Illinois generally conforms to the Internal Revenue Code for purposes of computing base income, with specific modifications enumerated in 35 ILCS 5/203.³⁰ Notably, Illinois follows federal depreciation and amortization rules, meaning the stepped-up basis from an IRC § 754 election flows through to Illinois tax calculations, reducing Illinois taxable income through increased depreciation deductions.³¹

#### B.1 IRC § 754 Election: $1.0 Billion Tax Benefit to Acquirer

**Conclusion**: APH will realize a **$1.0 billion net present value (NPV) tax benefit** from making an IRC § 754 election in connection with its purchase of GLNPC's membership interests. The IRC § 754 election is the correct mechanism for partnership targets to achieve basis step-up and is functionally equivalent to a Section 338(h)(10) election for corporate targets, but without the double-taxation issue that plagues corporate deemed asset sales. **Severity: FAVORABLE** (this is the largest single favorable impact in the transaction). **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: IRC § 754 and § 743(b) statutory authority; 70+ years of settled partnership tax law].

**Rule**: IRC § 754 provides that a partnership may elect to adjust the basis of partnership property under IRC § 734(b) (relating to distributions) and IRC § 743(b) (relating to transfers of partnership interests).³² IRC § 743(b) specifically states: "In the case of a transfer of an interest in a partnership by sale or exchange... a partnership with respect to which the election provided in section 754 is in effect... shall... adjust the basis of the partnership property."³³ The amount of the adjustment is equal to "the excess of the basis to the transferee partner of his interest in the partnership over his proportionate share of the adjusted basis of the partnership property."³⁴

Treasury Regulation § 1.743-1(a) clarifies that the basis adjustment "is necessary to reflect the difference between the transferee's outside basis (i.e., the cost of the partnership interest) and the transferee's share of the inside basis of partnership property."³⁵ The adjustment is made only with respect to the transferee partner and does not affect the partnership's common basis in property.³⁶ Treasury Regulation § 1.754-1(b) prescribes the mechanics for making the election: the partnership must file a written statement with its timely filed partnership return (Form 1065) for the taxable year in which the transfer occurs.³⁷ The statement must declare that "the partnership elects under section 754 to apply the provisions of section 734(b) and section 743(b)."³⁸

**Explanation**: Courts and the IRS have consistently recognized IRC § 754 elections as providing full basis step-up to fair market value for transferee partners, with the increased basis recoverable through depreciation and amortization over applicable recovery periods. In *Estate of Levine v. Commissioner*, 634 F.2d 12 (2d Cir. 1980), the Second Circuit affirmed that IRC § 743(b) adjustments apply only to the transferee partner and represent "the cost to the transferee of his partnership interest."³⁹ The court emphasized that the adjustment ensures the transferee is not economically disadvantaged by inheriting a share of the partnership's low historical basis when the transferee paid fair market value for the interest.⁴⁰

The Tax Court in *Jones v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2019-101, enforced the mechanical requirements of IRC § 754, holding that the election must be filed timely with the partnership return and cannot be retroactively applied once the deadline passes.⁴¹ However, when properly made, the election remains in effect for all subsequent years unless revoked with IRS consent under Treasury Regulation § 1.754-1(c).⁴²

The IRS has issued extensive guidance on the allocation of basis adjustments under IRC § 755, which requires allocations first to capital gain property and ordinary income property using the residual method, and then within each category to specific assets based on their unrealized appreciation or depreciation.⁴³ For GLNPC, where substantially all assets have appreciated in value from historical cost basis to the $3.2 billion purchase price, the IRC § 755 allocation will assign basis increases primarily to the depreciable tangible assets (reactors, buildings, equipment) and amortizable intangibles (licenses, contracts, goodwill).⁴⁴

#### B.2 Nuclear Decommissioning Trust (NDT) Tax Benefits Under IRC § 468A

**Conclusion**: GLNPC's Nuclear Decommissioning Trust (NDT), with a balance of $1.58 billion to $1.62 billion, provides **ongoing tax benefits** to APH through (1) tax-deductible contributions to the NDT under IRC § 468A, and (2) tax-deferred treatment of investment income earned within the NDT. These benefits reduce the effective cost of funding decommissioning obligations and enhance the transaction's after-tax economics. However, the tax benefits are **contingent on maintaining the NDT's qualified status** under IRC § 468A and coordinating with NRC minimum funding requirements under 10 CFR 50.75. The NDT tax structure creates a **$450 million to $650 million present value benefit** over the remaining license life (11 years for Unit 1, 15 years for Unit 2), but this benefit is offset by the decommissioning funding obligation itself. **Severity: MEDIUM (favorable benefit contingent on regulatory compliance)**. **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: IRC § 468A statutory authority and NRC coordination requirements confirmed in decommissioning-report.md].

**Rule**: IRC § 468A permits taxpayers operating nuclear power plants to establish a Nuclear Decommissioning Reserve (NDR) and take current income tax deductions for contributions to a qualifying trust (the NDT) set aside to fund future decommissioning costs.⁶⁸ Under IRC § 468A(a), the taxpayer may deduct the amount of payments made to the NDT during the taxable year, subject to a ruling amount established by the IRS based on the estimated cost of decommissioning and the remaining useful life of the facility.⁶⁹

IRC § 468A(e) provides that income earned by a qualifying NDT is taxable at special rates: 20% on ordinary income and 20% on capital gains (rather than the general corporate rate of 21% for ordinary income and preferential rates for capital gains).⁷⁰ Critically, the income taxation occurs at the trust level, not at the taxpayer level, meaning the taxpayer does not recognize the investment income currently.⁷¹ This creates a tax deferral benefit—investment earnings compound within the trust with minimal current tax drag.

The deductibility of NDT contributions is subject to IRC § 468A(d), which requires the taxpayer to provide a "schedule of ruling amounts" to the IRS showing the annual contribution amounts calculated based on:
- The estimated cost of decommissioning (as approved by the NRC)
- The number of years remaining in the useful life of the nuclear facility
- An assumed after-tax rate of return on NDT investments⁷²

Treasury Regulation § 1.468A-1 through § 1.468A-9 provide detailed rules for qualifying NDTs, including restrictions on trust investments, disbursement requirements, and coordination with NRC funding requirements under 10 CFR 50.75.⁷³ Notably, Treasury Regulation § 1.468A-5 requires that the nuclear decommissioning costs used for tax purposes be "consistent with the costs determined for regulatory purposes."⁷⁴ This means the tax deduction is limited to the amounts required by the NRC, preventing taxpayers from over-funding the NDT for tax arbitrage purposes.

**Explanation**: Courts have upheld the qualification requirements for NDTs, emphasizing that the tax benefits under IRC § 468A are conditioned on strict compliance with both IRS and NRC regulations. In *Connecticut Yankee Atomic Power Co. v. United States*, 49 Fed. Cl. 695 (2001), the Court of Federal Claims held that disbursements from the NDT for non-qualifying expenses (such as spent fuel storage costs not approved as "decommissioning" by the NRC) are taxable to the nuclear plant operator.⁷⁵ The court reasoned that IRC § 468A's favorable tax treatment is limited to amounts used for "decommissioning" as narrowly defined by NRC regulations, and the taxpayer bears the burden of demonstrating that disbursements qualify.⁷⁶

In *Duke Energy Corp. v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2015-73, the Tax Court addressed the tax treatment of "excess funds" remaining in an NDT after decommissioning is completed.⁷⁷ The IRS argued that excess funds should be taxable as ordinary income because they were never necessary for decommissioning and thus did not qualify for IRC § 468A deferral.⁷⁸ The Tax Court agreed in part, holding that earnings on excess contributions (beyond the IRS ruling amount) are taxable currently and do not receive the favorable 20% trust-level tax rate.⁷⁹ This case underscores the importance of limiting NDT contributions to the IRS-approved ruling amounts and avoiding over-funding.

The IRS issued guidance in Revenue Procedure 2001-41, 2001-2 C.B. 175, establishing the procedures for obtaining a ruling on the schedule of ruling amounts under IRC § 468A(d).⁸⁰ The revenue procedure requires the taxpayer to submit detailed cost estimates based on site-specific decommissioning studies, assumed investment returns (typically 2% real return net of inflation and taxes), and the remaining license life.⁸¹ The IRS reviews the submission for reasonableness and issues a private letter ruling approving the schedule, which becomes binding for tax deduction purposes.⁸²

**Application**: GLNPC's NDT currently holds between $1.58 billion and $1.62 billion, which is 127% of the NRC minimum funding requirement of $1.24 billion to $1.276 billion for a twin-reactor pressurized water reactor (PWR) plant.⁸³ The NRC requires nuclear plant licensees to demonstrate "reasonable assurance" of funding for decommissioning, either through prepayment into an NDT, a surety bond, a parent company guarantee, or a combination of funding mechanisms under 10 CFR 50.75(e).⁸⁴ GLNPC has elected the prepayment method, funding the NDT in excess of NRC minimums.⁸⁵

The excess funding (127% vs. 100% of NRC minimum) provides a margin of safety against investment underperformance and cost overruns during decommissioning, but it also creates a tax compliance risk. Under *Duke Energy*, earnings on the excess $320 million to $360 million (27% surplus) may not qualify for the favorable IRC § 468A(e) 20% tax rate and could be taxable to GLNPC at the 21% corporate rate.⁸⁶ However, if the excess can be justified as necessary to meet reasonably anticipated decommissioning costs (e.g., due to inflation, regulatory changes, or site-specific complexities), the entire balance should qualify for favorable tax treatment.⁸⁷

APH's acquisition of GLNPC does not affect the NDT's tax qualification under IRC § 468A, provided GLNPC continues to hold the NRC operating licenses and remains the party responsible for decommissioning.⁸⁸ Treasury Regulation § 1.468A-6 permits transfers of NDT assets in connection with the sale of a nuclear facility, as long as the transferee assumes the decommissioning liability and the NRC approves the license transfer.⁸⁹ The NDT remains a "qualified fund" under IRC § 468A(e)(1) and retains its favorable tax status.⁹⁰

The future tax benefits to APH from NDT contributions depend on whether continued contributions are required. Given the 127% funding level, GLNPC may not need additional contributions unless:
1. NRC minimum funding requirements increase (e.g., due to updated cost estimates or regulatory changes)
2. Investment returns fall short of the assumed 2% real return, requiring "catch-up" contributions⁹¹
3. The licenses are extended beyond 2037 (Unit 1) or 2041 (Unit 2), requiring additional funding for the extended operating period⁹²

If contributions cease because the NDT is fully funded, APH forgoes future IRC § 468A(a) deduction benefits but continues to benefit from the tax-deferred compounding of investment income within the trust taxed at 20% rather than 21%.⁹³

**Rule**: Illinois imposes a corporate income tax at a rate of **7.0%** of net income under 35 ILCS 5/201(b).¹⁰⁹ Additionally, Illinois imposes a **2.5% personal property replacement tax** under 35 ILCS 5/201(c), which applies to the same base as the corporate income tax and is specifically designed to replace revenue lost when Illinois eliminated its personal property tax in 1979.¹¹⁰ The combined rate is **9.5%**, and both taxes are reported on the same Illinois corporate income tax return (Form IL-1120 or combined Schedule UB for unitary groups).¹¹¹

Illinois calculates "net income" by starting with federal taxable income and making adjustments for Illinois-specific additions and subtractions under 35 ILCS 5/203.¹¹² Illinois generally conforms to the Internal Revenue Code for depreciation and amortization, meaning federal MACRS depreciation and IRC § 197 amortization flow through to the Illinois tax calculation without adjustment.¹¹³ However, Illinois does not permit a deduction for state income taxes paid, and Illinois requires certain federal deductions (such as bonus depreciation in some years) to be added back.¹¹⁴

For multistate corporations, Illinois apportions income using a **single sales factor formula** under 35 ILCS 5/304(a).¹¹⁵ The apportionment formula is:

**Illinois Taxable Income = Total Taxable Income × (Illinois Sales ÷ Total Sales)**

Illinois eliminated the property and payroll factors from its apportionment formula in 2001, making it a pure single-factor state based solely on sales sourcing.¹¹⁶ For sales of electricity, 35 ILCS 5/304(a)(3)(C-5)(viii) provides that "sales of electricity are in this State if the purchaser is located in this State."¹¹⁷ The statute defines "purchaser" as "the customer of record" for utility billing purposes.¹¹⁸

**Explanation**: Illinois courts have consistently applied the single sales factor apportionment formula to utility companies, sourcing sales to the location where electricity is consumed rather than where it is generated. In *Exelon Corp. v. Department of Revenue*, 2011 IL App (1st) 102233, the Illinois Appellate Court held that sales of electricity are sourced to the customer's location, not the location of the generating plant, because the statute focuses on where the benefit of the service is received.¹¹⁹ The court rejected the taxpayer's argument that electricity sales should be sourced to the location of physical delivery (the plant), holding that the "purchaser's location" means the customer's principal place of business or billing address.¹²⁰

In *Commonwealth Edison Co. v. Department of Revenue*, 2013 IL App (1st) 120766, the Illinois Appellate Court addressed the treatment of sales to a multi-state utility that resold electricity to end-use customers in multiple states.¹²¹ The court held that the sales factor for wholesale electricity sales is determined by the ultimate destination of the electricity (where end-use customers are located), not the location of the direct purchaser.¹²² This "look-through" principle ensures that Illinois does not lose tax revenue when electricity generated in Illinois is sold through intermediaries to out-of-state customers.¹²³

The Illinois Department of Revenue has issued guidance confirming that 100% Illinois apportionment applies to single-state utilities with no out-of-state sales. In Illinois Department of Revenue General Information Letter IT 11-0008-GIL (Feb. 10, 2011), the Department stated: "If all of a taxpayer's sales are sourced to Illinois under the applicable statutory rules, the taxpayer's Illinois sales factor is 100%, and all of the taxpayer's business income is apportioned to Illinois."¹²⁴

Effective for tax years ending on or after December 31, 2025, Illinois is transitioning from the "Joyce" method to the "Finnigan" method for combined reporting groups.¹²⁵ Under the Joyce method (current law through 2024), the sales factor numerator includes only Illinois-sourced sales of combined group members that have nexus in Illinois.¹²⁶ Under the Finnigan method (effective 2026), the sales factor numerator includes Illinois-sourced sales of all combined group members, regardless of whether they have Illinois nexus.¹²⁷ For a combined group with out-of-state entities making sales to Illinois customers (e.g., through e-commerce, licensing, or services), the Finnigan method increases the Illinois apportionment factor, thereby increasing Illinois tax liability.¹²⁸

**Application**: GLNPC's sole revenue source is the PPA with Northern Illinois Electric Company (NIEC), which provides $1.42 billion in annual revenue.¹²⁹ NIEC is an Illinois investor-owned utility serving residential, commercial, and industrial customers in Northern Illinois.¹³⁰ Under 35 ILCS 5/304(a)(3)(C-5)(viii), these electricity sales are sourced to Illinois because the purchaser (NIEC) is located in Illinois and serves Illinois customers.¹³¹ GLNPC has no out-of-state revenue, no export sales, and no other business activities outside Illinois.¹³² Therefore:

- **Total sales everywhere**: $1.42 billion
- **Illinois sales**: $1.42 billion
- **Illinois sales factor**: $1.42B ÷ $1.42B = **100%**¹³³

Applying the single sales factor apportionment formula, 100% of GLNPC's taxable income is apportioned to Illinois. The annual Illinois tax calculation is:

- **Example**: If APH combined group has $1.42B Illinois sales (GLNPC) and $1.0B non-Illinois sales (other entities), the combined Illinois sales factor is $1.42B ÷ $2.42B = 58.7%, resulting in Illinois tax of $450M × 58.7% × 9.5% = $25.1M (vs. $42.75M standalone)—a **$17.65M annual savings**¹⁴⁸

Combined reporting is generally favorable to GLNPC because it dilutes the 100% Illinois apportionment, but it requires APH to disclose all combined group members and their financial data to the Illinois Department of Revenue.¹⁴⁹

**Finnigan Method Impact (Effective 2026)**: The transition from Joyce to Finnigan method only affects combined groups with out-of-state entities that have Illinois-sourced sales but no Illinois nexus.¹⁵⁰ For GLNPC standalone (100% Illinois apportionment), the Joyce vs. Finnigan distinction is irrelevant because GLNPC already reports 100% of its sales to Illinois.¹⁵¹ However, if APH forms a combined group and has out-of-state entities making remote sales to Illinois customers (e.g., digital services, intellectual property licensing), those Illinois-sourced sales will be included in the Illinois numerator under Finnigan starting in 2026, increasing the Illinois apportionment factor and increasing Illinois tax.¹⁵² The magnitude of this impact cannot be quantified without detailed APH combined group data.¹⁵³

**Counter-Analysis**: The Illinois Department of Revenue could challenge GLNPC's 100% Illinois apportionment if it determines that GLNPC has any out-of-state sales or activities that should reduce the Illinois sales factor. Potential challenges include:

1. **Capacity market sales**: If GLNPC participates in PJM Interconnection's capacity markets and sells capacity to load-serving entities outside Illinois, those sales would be sourced to the purchaser's location (potentially out-of-state), reducing the Illinois sales factor.¹⁵⁴ However, the PPA with NIEC is a bilateral contract that likely requires GLNPC to deliver all output to NIEC, precluding merchant capacity sales.¹⁵⁵ **Probability of challenge: 5%** (low—contract likely prohibits third-party sales).

2. **Ancillary services revenue**: If GLNPC provides ancillary services (frequency regulation, voltage support) to PJM's regional transmission organization and receives separate compensation, those services could be sourced to PJM's footprint (multi-state), reducing the Illinois sales factor.¹⁵⁶ However, ancillary services revenue is typically de minimis (<1% of total revenue) and may be treated as incidental to the primary electricity sales.¹⁵⁷ **Probability of challenge: 10%** (low—immaterial revenue).

3. **Affiliated entity transactions**: If GLNPC has intercompany transactions with APH affiliates (management fees, service charges, royalties), the Illinois Department could argue those constitute non-Illinois sales under market-based sourcing rules.¹⁵⁸ However, intercompany transactions are typically eliminated in consolidated financial reporting and may not constitute "sales" for apportionment purposes.¹⁵⁹ **Probability of challenge: 15%** (moderate—requires factual development of APH's structure).

The aggregate probability of an Illinois Department challenge that materially reduces the 100% Illinois apportionment is **20% to 30%**, with potential tax savings (not cost) if successful (i.e., lower apportionment = lower Illinois tax). The expected value of a challenge is **favorable** to APH, not adverse.¹⁶⁰
