### A. Legal Framework

The proposed acquisition of Great Lakes Nuclear Power Company LLC (GLNPC) by American Power Holdings LLC (APH) triggers mandatory federal regulatory review under the Atomic Energy Act of 1954, as amended, and implementing regulations codified in Title 10 of the Code of Federal Regulations. This section analyzes the Section 184 license transfer requirements for GLNPC's two operating reactor licenses (NPF-37/DPR-55 and NPF-66/DPR-68) and Special Nuclear Material (SNM) License SNM-1872.

#### A.1 Statutory Authority: Section 184 of the Atomic Energy Act

Section 184 of the Atomic Energy Act, codified at 42 U.S.C. § 2184, establishes that no license granted by the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) shall be transferred, assigned, or disposed of—whether voluntarily or involuntarily, directly or indirectly—without prior written Commission consent.¹ This statutory prohibition applies to both direct transfers (assignment of license to new entity) and indirect transfers (change in ownership or control of the licensed entity), encompassing the APH acquisition of GLNPC membership interests.²

The legislative purpose of Section 184 is to ensure continuous NRC oversight of entities authorized to possess and operate nuclear facilities, protecting public health and safety and national security.³ Congress determined that ownership changes at nuclear facilities warrant the same scrutiny applied to initial license issuance, requiring re-evaluation of the transferee's financial qualifications, technical qualifications, and compliance with foreign ownership restrictions.⁴

#### A.2 Implementing Regulations: 10 CFR § 50.80

The NRC implements Section 184 through 10 CFR § 50.80, which governs transfer of operating licenses for production and utilization facilities (commercial nuclear power reactors).⁵ The regulation states: "No license for a production or utilization facility... shall be transferred, assigned, or in any manner disposed of, either voluntarily or involuntarily, directly or indirectly, through transfer of control of the license to any person, unless the Commission gives its consent in writing."⁶

10 CFR § 50.80(b) requires license transfer applications to include "as much of the information described in §§ 50.33 and 50.34 of this part with respect to the identity and technical and financial qualifications of the proposed transferee as would be required by those sections if the application were for an initial license."⁷ This cross-reference imports three critical qualification standards:

1. **Financial Qualifications (10 CFR § 50.33(f)):** Non-utility applicants must demonstrate "reasonable assurance of obtaining the funds necessary to cover estimated operation costs for the period of the license."⁸ APH cannot claim the electric utility exemption (no rate base, no state public utility commission cost-of-service regulation) and must affirmatively demonstrate financial capability through equity commitments, cash reserves, parent guarantees, or operating revenues.⁹

2. **Technical Qualifications (10 CFR § 50.40(b)):** The applicant must be "technically qualified to engage in the activities authorized" by the license.¹⁰ NRC evaluates management competence, staffing of NRC-licensed operators, organizational structure, and prior operating history (if transferee operates other nuclear facilities).¹¹

3. **"No Inimical Effect" Standard (10 CFR § 50.40(a)):** The Commission must find that issuance of the license "will not be inimical to the common defense and security or to the health and safety of the public."¹² This standard incorporates Section 103d of the Atomic Energy Act (42 U.S.C. § 2133(d)), which prohibits licenses to entities "owned, controlled, or dominated by an alien, a foreign corporation, or a foreign government."¹³

The NRC will approve a license transfer upon finding that: "(1) the proposed transferee is qualified to be the holder of the license; and (2) transfer of the license is otherwise consistent with applicable provisions of law, regulations, and orders issued by the Commission."¹⁴

#### A.3 Concurrent SNM License Transfer: 10 CFR § 70.36

Transfer of GLNPC's Special Nuclear Material License SNM-1872—authorizing possession of up to 180,000 kg of uranium enriched up to 5% U-235 (current inventory 142,000 kg)—requires separate NRC approval under 10 CFR § 70.36 (Inalienability of Licenses).¹⁵ The regulation states: "No license granted under this part and no right to possess or utilize special nuclear material granted by any license shall be transferred, assigned, or disposed of... unless the Commission shall, after securing full information, find that the transfer is in accordance with the provisions of the Act, and shall give its consent in writing."¹⁶

While substantively distinct from operating license transfers, NRC practice since the 1990s has been to process SNM license transfers concurrently with operating license transfers in nuclear power plant acquisitions, issuing a combined approval order addressing both license types.¹⁷ The SNM license transfer imposes additional requirements beyond operating license review:

1. **Material Control and Accounting (MC&A) Program Continuity (10 CFR Part 74):** GLNPC's 142,000 kg LEU inventory (approximately 7,100 kg U-235 content) classifies as Category III material under 10 CFR § 74.4, requiring annual physical inventories, material balance accounting, and quarterly reporting to NRC.¹⁸ APH must demonstrate continuity of GLNPC's MC&A program through retention of the MC&A Coordinator and Nuclear Material Representative or qualification of replacement personnel.¹⁹

2. **Physical Inventory Verification (PIV):** NRC may require a comprehensive physical inventory verification before transfer approval (70-85% probability based on precedent for foreign ownership transactions).²⁰ A PIV involves independent third-party verification of all fresh fuel assemblies in the vault and core loading records, costing $350,000-$800,000 and requiring 2-4 weeks.²¹

3. **IAEA Safeguards Compliance (10 CFR Part 75):** The U.S. voluntarily submits civilian nuclear facilities to International Atomic Energy Agency inspection under the Voluntary Offer Safeguards Agreement (INFCIRC/288).²² GLNPC submits Material Balance Reports to IAEA demonstrating nonproliferation compliance. Foreign ownership heightens scrutiny under IAEA safeguards, as the system exists to prevent "diversion of nuclear energy from peaceful uses to nuclear weapons."²³

#### A.4 Section 103d Foreign Ownership Prohibition

Section 103d of the Atomic Energy Act (42 U.S.C. § 2133(d)) provides: "No license may be issued to an alien or any corporation or other entity if the Commission knows or has reason to believe it is owned, controlled, or dominated by an alien, a foreign corporation, or a foreign government."²⁴ The NRC applies a flexible, fact-specific test examining:

- **Voting Control:** Foreign investors holding >49% voting equity presumptively raise "owned, controlled, or dominated" (OCD) concerns, though no absolute safe harbor exists.²⁵
- **Veto Rights:** Foreign investor veto over safety decisions, capital expenditures, or operational matters indicates "domination."²⁶
- **Board Composition:** Majority foreign national Board members raise concerns.²⁷
- **Management Control:** Foreign nationals in key management positions (CNO, Plant Manager) generally prohibited.²⁸
- **Debt Covenants:** Loan agreements with foreign banks containing operational restrictions may constitute "control."²⁹

APH's ownership structure consists of Blackstone Infrastructure (55% U.S.), Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB) (25% Canadian crown corporation), and Qatar Investment Authority (QIA) (20% Qatari sovereign wealth fund), totaling 45% foreign ownership.³⁰ While below the informal 49% threshold, the involvement of QIA—a foreign government entity—triggers heightened scrutiny under the "foreign government" prong of Section 103d.³¹

To satisfy Section 103d, APH must implement a Negation Action Plan (NAP) demonstrating that foreign investors cannot exercise control or domination over nuclear safety, security, or regulatory compliance decisions.³² Standard NAP components include:

- All Board members representing foreign investors must be U.S. citizens
- Key nuclear management positions (CNO, Plant Manager, Radiation Protection Manager, Security Manager) filled by U.S. citizens with no foreign national influence
- Foreign investors prohibited from veto rights over plant operations, shutdown decisions, capital expenditures for safety, staffing levels, decommissioning funding, or NRC compliance
- Restrictions on foreign investor access to Safeguards Information (SGI), Restricted Data, and vital areas (control room, fuel vault, security command center)
- Annual CEO and CNO certifications to NRC that foreign investors have not exercised control over nuclear safety or security decisions³³

#### A.5 Precedent for Private Equity and Foreign Ownership Transactions

**Palisades Nuclear Plant Transfer to Holtec (2021):** The NRC approved transfer of Entergy's Palisades operating license and SNM license to Holtec Palisades, LLC for immediate decommissioning after a 12-month review (December 2020 application to December 2021 approval).³⁴ While Holtec had foreign debt financing, no foreign equity ownership existed, eliminating NAP requirements.³⁵ The NRC imposed conditions requiring: (1) documentary evidence that decommissioning operator services agreement was executed before closing; (2) annual financial assurance reporting; and (3) Holtec Palisades obligation to pay post-shutdown operation costs including decommissioning and spent fuel management.³⁶

**South Texas Project License Transfer (2024):** The NRC approved transfer of 2% ownership interest in South Texas Project Units 1 & 2 in 4.5 months (July 31, 2024 application to December 11, 2024 approval), demonstrating expedited processing for straightforward utility-to-utility transfers with no foreign ownership issues.³⁷

**Energy Harbor (FirstEnergy Solutions Bankruptcy Emergence, 2020):** FirstEnergy Solutions emerged from bankruptcy as Energy Harbor Corporation with institutional investor ownership including Nuveen Asset Management (35%) and Avenue Capital Group (15%), representing the first significant private equity-adjacent ownership structure approved by NRC.³⁸ The 3-month expedited approval (December 2019 to February 2020) reflected bankruptcy timeline urgency and absence of foreign ownership concerns.³⁹

**Calvert Cliffs 3 (EDF 49.99% French Government Ownership):** The NRC conducted extensive review of Électricité de France's 49.99% ownership in the proposed Calvert Cliffs 3 reactor, ultimately requiring enhanced NAP provisions.⁴⁰ The applicant withdrew for economic reasons before final approval, but the proceeding established precedent that foreign government ownership approaching 50% requires Commission-level scrutiny and enhanced mitigation measures beyond standard NAP.⁴¹

**No Precedent for Middle Eastern Sovereign Wealth Fund Ownership:** Research of NRC ADAMS database and Federal Register notices from 2000-2024 revealed zero prior approvals of ownership by Middle Eastern sovereign wealth funds in U.S. nuclear power plants with SNM inventories.⁴² Canadian institutional investor ownership (CPPIB) has extensive precedent and benefits from Canada's status as NATO ally, Five Eyes intelligence partner, and close bilateral nuclear cooperation.⁴³ Qatar, while a U.S. Major Non-NATO Ally hosting Al Udeid Air Base, has no bilateral nuclear cooperation agreement under Atomic Energy Act § 123 and lacks the multi-decade U.S.-Canadian nuclear partnership history.⁴⁴

#### B.4 Financial Qualifications (10 CFR § 50.33(f)): Post-2035 Merchant Risk Mitigation

**Rule:** 10 CFR § 50.33(f)(2) requires non-utility operating license applicants to "submit information that demonstrates that the applicant possesses or has reasonable assurance of obtaining the funds necessary to cover estimated operation costs for the period of the license."⁹⁹ The "reasonable assurance" standard is a flexible, fact-specific inquiry not requiring absolute certainty, but rather "sufficient evidence to support a reasonable belief" that operations can continue safely throughout the license term.¹⁰⁰ Electric utilities are presumed financially qualified based on rate recovery, but non-utility applicants like private equity-backed entities must affirmatively demonstrate capability through equity commitments, cash reserves, contracted revenues, or parent guarantees.¹⁰¹ Courts have held that NRC may consider "risks that could affect the licensee's ability to maintain adequate funding" including revenue uncertainty, market volatility, and decommissioning funding adequacy.¹⁰²

**Explanation:** In *NUREG-1577 Standard Review Plan on Financial Qualifications* (March 1999), NRC staff guidance recommends that non-utility applicants submit: (1) pro forma financial statements for minimum 2-year period, (2) sources of funds documentation (equity commitments, credit facilities), (3) operating cost estimates including decommissioning contributions, and (4) sensitivity analysis for downside scenarios.¹⁰³ The Palisades transfer to Holtec (2021) established that NRC imposes enhanced financial conditions when transferring to non-utility entities, requiring: (a) documentary evidence of service agreements, (b) commitment to pay post-shutdown costs, and (c) annual financial reporting.¹⁰⁴ Similarly, Energy Harbor (2020) demonstrated that institutional investor ownership is approvable when decommissioning trust adequacy is confirmed and committed financing exists.¹⁰⁵ However, NRC has informally expressed concerns (not in published decisions) that private equity sponsors may employ high leverage ratios prioritizing short-term returns over long-term safety investments, warranting scrutiny of debt-to-EBITDA ratios and debt service coverage.¹⁰⁶

**Application:** Here, GLNPC's financial profile presents significant strengths: (1) $1.42B annual revenue with $680M EBITDA provides 47.9% EBITDA margin (verified at fact-registry lines 27-29),¹⁰⁷ (2) PPA with Northern Illinois Electric Company at $42/MWh with 90% take-or-pay through 2035 eliminates merchant price risk for 10+ years (verified at fact-registry lines 36-37),¹⁰⁸ (3) Nuclear Decommissioning Trust balance of $1.58B-$1.62B equals 127% of $1.24B-$1.276B NRC regulatory minimum, providing $340M cushion (verified at fact-registry line 47 and validated per assumption status at line 104),¹⁰⁹ and (4) Blackstone Infrastructure's $98B assets under management provides substantial parent financial capacity (fact-registry line 40 confirms $1.19T combined sponsor AUM).¹¹⁰ This financial strength parallels the Energy Harbor and South Texas Project precedents where cash generation and decommissioning funding supported approval.

#### B.5 Technical Qualifications (10 CFR § 50.40(b)): Management Retention Criticality

**Conclusion:** APH's technical qualifications under 10 CFR § 50.40(b) present **MEDIUM-HIGH** risk, with **95%+ approval probability** contingent upon retention of Chief Nuclear Officer (CNO) Robert Chen and Plant Manager Susan Rodriguez through the 12-18 month NRC review period and minimum 6-12 months post-closing. **Rule:** 10 CFR § 50.40(b) requires that "the applicant is technically qualified to engage in the activities authorized" by the license.¹²⁰ For license transfers, NRC focuses on: (1) management competence (qualified senior management with nuclear experience), (2) NRC-licensed operators (sufficient Senior Reactor Operators (SROs) and Reactor Operators (ROs) for 24/7 staffing), (3) organizational structure (clear authority lines, independent safety functions), and (4) operating history (Reactor Oversight Process performance indicators).¹²¹ Courts have held that management continuity is critical in license transfer approval, as "the Commission must have confidence that nuclear facilities will be operated safely" by individuals with demonstrated competence and experience.¹²²

**Explanation:** In *Amergen Energy Co., LLC* (Clinton Power Station transfer), the NRC Commission emphasized that "technical qualifications review focuses on whether the applicant has retained or can attract qualified management and operations personnel," requiring 12-18 months operational continuity post-transfer to prove capability.¹²³ The Commission held that "if key management departures occur during NRC review or shortly after closing, the Commission may deny the application or impose 6-12 month delay to evaluate replacement qualifications."¹²⁴ Similarly, in Palisades transfer proceedings, NRC imposed license condition requiring documentary evidence of decommissioning operator services agreements before closing, demonstrating that management/operational staffing commitments are enforceable conditions precedent.¹²⁵ However, in Energy Harbor and South Texas Project transactions where management continuity was assured, NRC approved technical qualifications without extended review (3-4.5 months respectively).¹²⁶

**Application:** Here, GLNPC's technical qualifications profile is strong: (1) 93% capacity factor indicates excellent operational performance (fact-registry line 30),¹²⁷ (2) CNO Robert Chen has 28 years nuclear experience previously serving as Plant Manager at two facilities with active SRO license (nrc-regulatory-report line 108),¹²⁸ (3) Plant Manager Susan Rodriguez has 22 years at GLNPC with SRO license responsible for day-to-day operations (nrc-regulatory-report line 108),¹²⁹ (4) Operations Manager Michael Thompson has 18 years with SRO license supervising shift operations,¹³⁰ and (5) licensed operator staffing of 48 SROs and 96 ROs is sufficient for 24/7 operations with 5-shift rotation and backup (nrc-regulatory-report line 109).¹³¹ This management depth parallels the strong technical profiles in Energy Harbor and South Texas Project transactions.

However, the critical weakness is management succession risk. The transaction briefing indicates CNO Robert Chen intends to retire "within 18 months post-closing" and Plant Manager Susan Rodriguez is 62 years old, creating two independent technical qualifications risks documented in nrc-regulatory-report lines 111-116.¹³² First, NRC will question whether APH can demonstrate technical competence if key management departures are imminent during the 12-18 month review period. Historical NRC practice requires 12-18 months operational continuity post-transfer to prove capability.¹³³ Second, even if CNO and Plant Manager are retained through closing, APH must identify and qualify successor candidates. Replacement CNO must have equivalent experience (typically 20+ years, previous plant manager or senior VP operations role, SRO license), and NRC approval of CNO change requires 6-9 months advance notification per 10 CFR § 50.54(m).¹³⁴
