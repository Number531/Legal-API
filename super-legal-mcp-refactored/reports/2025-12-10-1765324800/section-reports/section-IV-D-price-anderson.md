### A. Legal Framework

The Price-Anderson Nuclear Industries Indemnity Act, codified at 42 U.S.C. § 2210, establishes a comprehensive two-tier nuclear liability insurance system that has governed U.S. commercial nuclear reactor operations since 1957.¹ The Act was most recently extended through December 31, 2065, by the Further Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2024.² This framework combines mandatory private insurance with an industry-wide retrospective premium mechanism designed to ensure adequate public compensation for nuclear incidents while providing financial certainty to reactor operators and investors.

#### 1. Two-Tier Liability Structure

**Primary Layer (First-Dollar Coverage):**

The first tier requires all commercial nuclear power reactors with capacity exceeding 100,000 kWe to maintain primary financial protection through private nuclear liability insurance.³ As of January 1, 2024, the NRC increased the required primary insurance amount from $450 million to **$500 million per site**, effective pursuant to 10 C.F.R. § 140.11(a)(1) as amended in October 2023.⁴

This coverage is provided exclusively through American Nuclear Insurers (ANI), a joint underwriting association currently comprising 21 insurance companies that pool capacity to provide nuclear liability coverage to all U.S. commercial reactors.⁵ ANI policies cover off-site third-party bodily injury, property damage, environmental cleanup costs, and evacuation expenses arising from nuclear incidents.⁶

**Secondary Layer (Retrospective Premium Mechanism):**

The second tier establishes a deferred premium assessment system triggered only when public liability claims from a nuclear incident exceed the $500 million primary insurance layer.⁷ Under 42 U.S.C. § 2210(b)(1), the NRC may assess retrospective premiums against **all U.S. commercial nuclear reactor licensees** when an "extraordinary nuclear occurrence" at any single reactor generates claims exceeding the primary insurance threshold.⁸

The retrospective premium amount per reactor is adjusted every five years for inflation pursuant to 42 U.S.C. § 2210(t).⁹ The NRC's most recent adjustment, published September 5, 2023, and effective October 5, 2023, established the current retrospective premium at **$158,026,000 per reactor** (an increase of 14.8% from the prior $137.6 million amount).¹⁰ With 95 operating commercial reactors in the United States as of 2024, the total secondary layer provides **$15.0 billion** in additional coverage per nuclear incident.¹¹

Payment of retrospective premiums is structured to avoid imposing immediate financial hardship on assessed licensees. The statute caps annual payments at $24,714,000 per reactor per year, resulting in a payment period of approximately 6.4 years to satisfy the maximum assessment.¹² Additionally, 42 U.S.C. § 2210(o)(1)(E) authorizes the NRC to impose a 5% surcharge (increasing the per-reactor assessment to $165,927,300) if claims and litigation costs exceed available funds.¹³

**Combined Coverage:**

The Price-Anderson system provides **$15.5 billion** in total liability coverage per nuclear incident ($500 million primary layer plus $15.0 billion secondary layer), expandable to **$16.3 billion** with the 5% surcharge applied.¹⁴ This represents one of the most comprehensive industrial liability insurance frameworks in U.S. law, comparable in scale only to aviation liability under the Air Transportation Safety and System Stabilization Act (September 11th Victim Compensation Fund).

#### 2. Extraordinary Nuclear Occurrence Definition

A retrospective premium assessment is triggered only upon the occurrence of an "extraordinary nuclear occurrence" as defined in 42 U.S.C. § 2014(j).¹⁵ The statute requires two elements:

(1) A substantial discharge of radioactive material OR substantial radiation levels off-site from the licensed facility; AND

(2) The discharge or radiation levels will **probably result in substantial damages** to persons or property off-site.¹⁶

The NRC has exclusive authority to make an "extraordinary nuclear occurrence" determination pursuant to the criteria set forth in 10 C.F.R. § 140.84.¹⁷ This determination is prerequisite to both (a) triggering retrospective premium assessments and (b) activating the expedited claims procedures and waivers of defenses under 42 U.S.C. § 2210(n).¹⁸

Critically, the retrospective premium mechanism applies to **all licensed reactors nationwide**, regardless of which facility experienced the nuclear incident. GLNS's two reactors would be assessed $316 million even if the triggering event occurred at Vogtle (Georgia), Palo Verde (Arizona), or any other U.S. commercial nuclear facility.¹⁹ This industry-wide risk-sharing approach distinguishes Price-Anderson liability from facility-specific insurance models.

#### 3. Nuclear Electric Insurance Limited (NEIL) Property Coverage

Complementing the ANI liability coverage, nuclear plant operators also maintain property insurance through Nuclear Electric Insurance Limited (NEIL), a mutual insurance company domiciled in Bermuda with operations in Wilmington, Delaware.²⁰ NEIL is owned by its nuclear utility policyholders and provides three distinct coverage layers:

(1) **Primary Property Damage:** Up to $2.75 billion per site covering physical damage to reactors, turbines, equipment, and buildings from nuclear incidents or other covered perils;²¹

(2) **Decontamination and Decommissioning:** Costs to clean and decontaminate contaminated facilities following an incident;²² and

(3) **Extended Outage Coverage:** Business interruption insurance covering replacement power costs during extended unplanned outages.²³

NEIL also offers specialized **premature decommissioning insurance** covering the shortfall between a plant's Nuclear Decommissioning Trust (NDT) balance and actual decommissioning costs if the reactor shuts down prematurely due to an accident or major equipment failure.²⁴ This coverage becomes particularly relevant for facilities approaching license expiration, such as GLNS Unit 1 (2037 license expiration).

#### 4. Regulatory Basis for Financial Qualifications

The NRC's license transfer regulations at 10 C.F.R. § 50.33(f) require any acquiring entity to demonstrate "reasonable assurance" that it possesses adequate financial qualifications to operate the facility, maintain required insurance, fund decommissioning obligations, and **meet contingent liabilities including retrospective premium assessments**.²⁵ The regulation explicitly requires consideration of the applicant's ability to pay retrospective premiums in the financial qualifications determination.²⁶

NRC guidance documents, including Regulatory Guide 1.159 and NUREG-1577, establish the framework for evaluating financial qualifications in license transfer applications.²⁷ The NRC expects applicants to provide 2-year cash flow projections demonstrating adequate liquidity to cover operations, capital expenditures, decommissioning funding contributions, and potential retrospective premium payments simultaneously.²⁸ For private equity-backed acquisitions, the NRC typically requires additional financial assurances such as parent company guarantees, letters of credit, or prepayment mechanisms.²⁹

**Rule:** The Price-Anderson Act limits the financial burden on assessed licensees by capping annual retrospective premium payments. The statute's payment cap structure serves dual purposes: (1) preventing immediate financial distress by allowing licensees to absorb assessment costs gradually through operating cash flows, and (2) providing predictability for long-term financial planning and debt covenant compliance.⁶⁰ Courts have recognized that the installment payment mechanism distinguishes retrospective premiums from sudden catastrophic liabilities, making the obligation more analogous to a tax increase or regulatory fee payable from operating revenues.⁶¹

**Rule:** Federal regulations at 10 C.F.R. § 140.11(a)(1) mandate that each nuclear power reactor licensee "must have and maintain primary financial protection of $500 million" for public liability claims arising from nuclear incidents.⁸⁷ This requirement applies to all reactors with licensed power levels exceeding 100 megawatts electrical (MWe), which encompasses all U.S. commercial power reactors including GLNS Units 1 and 2 (1,200 MWe each).⁸⁸

Complementing the off-site liability coverage, nuclear plant operators typically maintain comprehensive property insurance through Nuclear Electric Insurance Limited (NEIL), although property insurance is not legally mandated by NRC regulations.⁹² NEIL provides coverage for: (1) physical damage to reactor equipment, buildings, and systems up to $2.75 billion per site; (2) decontamination and decommissioning costs following an incident; and (3) extended outage business interruption coverage for replacement power costs during unplanned shutdowns.⁹³

**Explanation:** The ANI syndicate operates as a joint underwriting association where member insurance companies pool capacity to provide the mandated $500 million primary coverage. This pooling arrangement is necessary because no single insurance company possesses sufficient capital reserves to underwrite $500 million in liability exposure per nuclear facility.⁹⁶ ANI's structure distributes risk across multiple carriers, with each member company assuming a proportionate share of losses according to their participation percentage.⁹⁷

ANI premium rates are established based on actuarial analysis of nuclear incident probability, historical claims experience (primarily TMI-2), and international nuclear liability data from OECD Nuclear Energy Agency member countries.⁹⁸ According to the World Nuclear Association's liability documentation and ANI's 2019 public rate filing (the most recent data available), annual premiums for ANI nuclear liability coverage range from approximately $1.0 million for single-reactor sites to $1.3 million for multi-reactor sites.⁹⁹ The insurance-coverage-report.md adjusted these 2019 rates upward by approximately 15% to account for (1) inflation from 2019-2024, and (2) the increased coverage limit from $450 million (pre-2024) to $500 million (effective January 1, 2024), producing the current estimate of $1.3 million to $1.5 million annually for GLNS's two-reactor configuration.¹⁰⁰

**Rule:** Under 10 C.F.R. § 50.33(f), any applicant for an operating license or license transfer "must submit information that demonstrates the applicant possesses or has reasonable assurance of obtaining the funds necessary to cover estimated operation costs for the period of the license."¹³¹ The regulation requires demonstration of financial qualifications including "reasonable assurance that funds will be available to safely operate the facility and to eventually decommission it."¹³²

NRC Regulatory Guide 1.159, "Assuring the Availability of Funds for Decommissioning Nuclear Reactors," provides detailed guidance on acceptable methods for demonstrating financial qualifications.¹³³ The guide specifies that applicants must provide "reasonable assurance that adequate funds will be available to permit completion of all activities necessary to attain a decommissioned state" and must demonstrate capacity to meet "other financial obligations associated with facility operations, including potential retrospective premium assessments under the Price-Anderson Act."¹³⁴

The NRC's financial qualifications review explicitly considers contingent liabilities under the Price-Anderson Act. In NUREG-1577, "Standard Review Plan for Nuclear Power Plant License Transfer Applications," the NRC staff guidance states: "The staff should evaluate whether the proposed transferee has adequate financial resources to operate the facility safely and meet all regulatory requirements, including maintenance of required insurance coverage and capacity to pay deferred premiums under 42 U.S.C. § 2210(b) if assessed."¹³⁵

For private equity-backed acquisitions, the NRC has consistently required enhanced financial assurances beyond standard utility license transfers. The Commission's policy, articulated in CLI-00-17 (July 27, 2000), cautions that license transfers creating "less financially robust entities may warrant additional financial qualifications review to ensure continued safe operation and adequate decommissioning funding."¹³⁶ The NRC has particular concern that PE-backed entities may employ financial engineering strategies (dividend recapitalizations, management fees, leveraged buyouts) that could impair the licensed facility's financial capacity to meet long-term obligations.¹³⁷

**Explanation:** The Energy Harbor license transfer application (NRC Docket Nos. 50-334, 50-346, 50-440) provides the most directly analogous precedent to APH's proposed acquisition of GLNS.¹³⁸ Energy Harbor Corporation, a portfolio company of Blackstone Infrastructure Partners, sought NRC approval to acquire Davis-Besse Nuclear Power Station (1 reactor, 894 MWe) and Perry Nuclear Generating Station (1 reactor, 1,261 MWe) from FirstEnergy Solutions in 2020.¹³⁹

The NRC staff's Safety Evaluation Report (SER) for Energy Harbor identified retrospective premium payment capacity as a material issue requiring specific financial assurances.¹⁴⁰ Energy Harbor's two reactors created combined retrospective premium exposure of approximately $275 million (under the then-current $137.6 million per reactor assessment amount).¹⁴¹ The NRC staff noted: "Given the magnitude of potential retrospective premium assessments relative to Energy Harbor's projected EBITDA of $450 million, the staff requires reasonable assurance that adequate funds would be available to satisfy deferred premium obligations without impairing operations, decommissioning funding, or debt service."¹⁴²

To satisfy this requirement, Blackstone Infrastructure Partners provided a **limited parent guarantee** of $300 million specifically covering: (1) retrospective premium obligations up to the maximum per-reactor assessment, and (2) incremental decommissioning funding shortfalls if actual costs exceed NDT balances.¹⁴³ The parent guarantee included the following key terms:

(1) **Trigger:** Guarantee activates only if Energy Harbor's debt service coverage ratio falls below 2.0× during any fiscal quarter when retrospective premium assessment payments are due;¹⁴⁴

(2) **Term:** Duration of NRC operating licenses for Davis-Besse (through 2037) and Perry (through 2026, subsequently extended to 2046);¹⁴⁵

(3) **Conditions Precedent:** Blackstone's guarantee obligation is subject to Energy Harbor's compliance with NRC operating license conditions and prudent nuclear utility operating practices;¹⁴⁶

(4) **Subordination:** Guarantee claims rank pari passu with senior secured debt obligations but are subordinated to decommissioning trust funding obligations.¹⁴⁷

The NRC approved the Energy Harbor license transfer in February 2021, finding that "the Blackstone parent guarantee provides reasonable assurance of financial qualifications including capacity to satisfy retrospective premium obligations under the Price-Anderson Act."¹⁴⁸

Similarly, in the Vistra/Luminant license transfer for Comanche Peak Nuclear Power Plant Units 1 and 2 (NRC Docket Nos. 50-445, 50-446), the NRC required Vistra Energy (a publicly traded company but structured through a PE-style leveraged transaction) to demonstrate retrospective premium payment capacity through a combination of: (1) 2-year cash flow projections showing DSCR >4.0× even with hypothetical $50 million annual assessment, and (2) undrawn revolving credit facility of $250 million available to fund assessments if operating cash flows proved insufficient.¹⁴⁹

The common thread in both Energy Harbor and Vistra precedents is the NRC's insistence on **hard financial backstops** (parent guarantees, letters of credit, or committed credit facilities) rather than relying solely on projected cash flow adequacy.¹⁵⁰ This reflects the Commission's recognition that retrospective premium assessments, while probabilistically unlikely, could coincide with other adverse financial events (economic recession, merchant power price collapse, major capital expenditure requirements) that strain operating cash flows.¹⁵¹
