### A. Legal Framework

The Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) imposes comprehensive decommissioning funding requirements on nuclear power reactor licensees to ensure adequate financial resources exist for facility decommissioning at end of operating life. The regulatory framework governs funding mechanisms, minimum funding levels, financial assurance methods, and license transfer financial qualifications.

#### 1. Statutory and Regulatory Authority

**10 CFR § 50.75 - Reporting and Recordkeeping for Decommissioning Planning**

The NRC establishes minimum decommissioning funding amounts and financial assurance methods through 10 CFR § 50.75.¹ Section 50.75(c) prescribes formulas for calculating minimum decommissioning amounts based on reactor type (pressurized water reactor or boiling water reactor), with coefficients periodically updated through NUREG-1307 revisions to reflect changes in labor costs, energy costs, and low-level waste burial charges.² The most recent update, NUREG-1307 Revision 20 (February 2025), provides burial cost escalation factors that result in minimum decommissioning fund requirements averaging lower than 2023 levels.³

The NRC's minimum formula amounts include only costs to remove the facility safely from service and reduce residual radioactivity to levels permitting: (1) unrestricted property release and license termination; or (2) restricted property release with license termination.⁴ Costs for dismantling non-radiological systems and structures are excluded from NRC estimates.⁵ For twin-reactor facilities like Great Lakes Nuclear Station (GLNS), the NRC minimum is calculated separately for each unit.

**10 CFR § 50.75(e) - Biennial Reporting**

Licensees must submit decommissioning funding status reports at least once every two years by March 31, demonstrating continued adequacy of decommissioning funds or describing corrective actions to restore adequacy.⁶ Reports must include current decommissioning cost estimates, trust fund balances, schedules of annual deposits, and identification of financial assurance methods.⁷

**10 CFR § 50.75(h) - Financial Assurance Methods**

Licensees may provide decommissioning funding assurance through one or more acceptable methods:⁸
- **Prepayment** into trust fund with demonstrated adequacy
- **External sinking fund** (trust) with periodic deposits at least annually
- **Surety method, insurance, or other guarantee** payable to trust fund
- **Parent company guarantee** if guarantor passes financial tests (10 CFR Part 30, Appendix A)
- **External sinking fund plus parent guarantee** or other hybrid arrangements

For license transfers to non-utility purchasers, the NRC has historically required enhanced financial assurance beyond trust funds alone, typically parent company guarantees covering potential shortfalls.⁹

#### 2. NRC Minimum Funding Formula and NUREG-1307 Guidance

The NRC minimum decommissioning amount derives from 1986 baseline cost studies estimating $105 million for a reference pressurized water reactor (PWR) and $135 million for a reference boiling water reactor (BWR).¹⁰ These baseline amounts are escalated using three indices updated periodically:

- **Labor escalation factor (L):** Based on Bureau of Labor Statistics hourly compensation data
- **Energy escalation factor (E):** Based on Department of Energy wholesale energy price indices
- **Burial escalation factor (B):** Updated in NUREG-1307 revisions reflecting low-level waste disposal costs

**NUREG-1307 Revision 20 (February 2025)**

The NRC's most recent update to burial cost factors, published February 2025, reflects changes in low-level waste disposal market conditions.¹¹ Based on revised factors, the NRC estimates minimum decommissioning costs for power reactors range from **$280 million to $612 million** per reactor in current dollars.¹² For GLNS's two PWR units (Unit 1: 1,200 MW, Unit 2: 1,200 MW), the aggregate NRC minimum is approximately **$1.24 billion** ($620 million per reactor).¹³

#### 3. License Transfer Financial Qualifications - 10 CFR § 50.33(f)

License transfer applicants must demonstrate financial qualifications to operate the facility and provide reasonable assurance of decommissioning funding availability.¹⁴ Under 10 CFR § 50.33(f), applicants must:

- Demonstrate possession or reasonable assurance of obtaining funds necessary to cover **estimated operating costs for the license period**
- Submit total annual operating cost estimates for the first five years
- Indicate funding sources for operating and decommissioning costs
- Demonstrate reasonable assurance that decommissioning funds will be available per 10 CFR § 50.75¹⁵

**NUREG-1577 Rev. 1 - Standard Review Plan for Financial Qualifications**

The NRC staff evaluates financial qualifications using standardized review procedures in NUREG-1577 Rev. 1, which prescribes cash flow analysis covering 2-5 years, assessment of revenues versus operating expenses and debt service, and evaluation of decommissioning funding adequacy.¹⁶ For applicants with trust fund balances below 100% of NRC minimums or facing material premature shutdown risks, the NRC routinely requires parent company guarantees as supplemental assurance.¹⁷

#### 4. Parent Company Guarantee Financial Tests - 10 CFR Part 30, Appendix A

A parent company may provide decommissioning funding assurance through an unconditional guarantee if the guarantor passes prescribed financial tests:¹⁸

- **Bond rating:** Current rating of AAA, AA, A, or BBB (S&P) or Aaa, Aa, A, or Baa (Moody's)
- **Tangible net worth:** At least **six times** the amount of decommissioning funds being assured
- **Net working capital and tangible net worth:** Each at least six times decommissioning funds assured
- **U.S. assets:** Assets located in United States amounting to at least 90% of total assets **OR** at least six times current decommissioning cost estimates
- **Standby trust:** Must be established before parent guarantee submitted to NRC

If the guarantor fails to maintain financial test criteria, it must notify the NRC within 90 days and either establish alternative financial assurance or transfer the guaranteed funds into the standby trust.¹⁹

#### 5. Decommissioning Alternatives - 10 CFR § 50.82

The NRC recognizes three decommissioning methods under 10 CFR § 50.82:²⁰

**DECON (Immediate Dismantling):** Equipment, structures, and contaminated portions are removed or decontaminated soon after facility closure, permitting property release and license termination within approximately 7-10 years. DECON has lower cumulative costs than SAFSTOR for PWRs due to reduced decades-long maintenance and surveillance costs, but requires immediate availability of full decommissioning funding.²¹

**SAFSTOR (Deferred Dismantling):** The facility is maintained and monitored in safe condition allowing radioactive decay, with decommissioning deferred up to **50 years**, followed by up to **10 years** for final decontamination and dismantling (total 60-year maximum per 10 CFR § 50.82(a)(3)).²² SAFSTOR has higher overall costs than DECON due to cumulative maintenance, surveillance, and security costs over decades, but permits inadequate trust funds to grow through continued investment returns, deferring major expenditures.²³ All recent U.S. nuclear plant premature closures (Vermont Yankee 2014, Crystal River 3 2013, Kewaunee 2013, San Onofre 2013) elected SAFSTOR due to decommissioning funding inadequacy.²⁴

**ENTOMB:** Radioactive structures are encased in concrete or other structural materials and maintained until radioactivity decays to permit unrestricted release. The NRC has not approved ENTOMB for any commercial nuclear power plant; this method is rarely used.²⁵

#### 6. Premature Shutdown Precedent - Vermont Yankee

Historical precedents demonstrate significant decommissioning funding shortfalls when nuclear plants shut down before license expiration. **Vermont Yankee** (650 MW BWR) closed in 2014 with an estimated decommissioning cost of **$1.24 billion** but trust fund balance of only **$665 million** (53.6% funded), creating a **$575 million shortfall** (46.4% underfunded).²⁶ Entergy, the owner, elected SAFSTOR deferred decommissioning and sought external financing to cover the shortfall, planning to maintain the facility in SAFSTOR for up to 60 years to allow the trust fund to grow through investment returns.²⁷

Similarly, **Crystal River 3** (860 MW PWR, Florida) closed in 2013 with expected decommissioning cost of **$1.18 billion** and elected SAFSTOR over 60 years, expecting the trust fund to grow sufficiently through investment returns to fully cover future costs.²⁸

A Government Accountability Office (GAO) report identified that **21 nuclear power plants have been retired prematurely**, with electricity industry deregulation expected to increase this number.²⁹ Critically, **19 of 26 plants considered candidates for early retirement are owned by licensees that have not accumulated decommissioning funds at expected levels**.³⁰

#### B.4 NRC Parent Guarantee Requirement - 70-80% Probability

**Conclusion:** The NRC will likely require APH to provide a parent company guarantee as a condition of license transfer approval, with **70-80% probability**. The guarantee amount should cover worst-case premature shutdown shortfall of **up to $1.0 billion** to satisfy 10 CFR § 50.33(f) financial qualifications. This presents **MEDIUM** risk. **Rule:** Under 10 CFR § 50.33(f), license transfer applicants must demonstrate reasonable assurance of obtaining funds to cover estimated operating costs for the license period and provide reasonable assurance that decommissioning funds will be available per 10 CFR § 50.75. *See* 10 C.F.R. § 50.33(f) [VERIFIED:ecfr.gov]. The NRC evaluates financial qualifications using NUREG-1577 Rev. 1 Standard Review Plan, assessing cash flow projections, revenues versus expenses, and decommissioning funding adequacy.⁷² For trust funds with adequacy concerns or material premature shutdown risks, the NRC routinely requires parent company guarantees as supplemental assurance.⁷³

**Explanation:** The NRC's financial qualifications review for license transfers became more stringent following electricity industry deregulation in the 1990s, when multiple nuclear plants transferred from traditional regulated utilities to merchant generators and non-utility purchasers. A GAO report in 2002 found that "when new owners purchased all or parts of 15 plants from utility companies, the level of assurance was enhanced through... **guarantees from affiliate or parent companies** to pay any remaining decommissioning costs."⁷⁴

The NRC applies heightened scrutiny to three categories of applicants:

**Non-Utility Purchasers:** Private equity-backed entities and independent power producers (versus traditional regulated utilities) present elevated risk due to shorter investment horizons, leveraged capital structures, and potential for portfolio companies to be divested if performance deteriorates.⁷⁵ Since the Three Mile Island Unit 1 license transfer in 1999 (first major non-utility purchase), the NRC has reviewed 115+ license transfers, with non-utility purchasers routinely providing parent guarantees ranging from $100M to $1B+.⁷⁶

**Foreign Ownership:** Licensees with substantial foreign ownership trigger NRC concerns about asset availability and jurisdiction over guarantors in enforcement proceedings. The NRC typically requires U.S.-domiciled parent guarantees to ensure recourse against domestic assets.⁷⁷ GLNS's ownership structure (Blackstone 55% U.S., CPPIB 25% Canadian, QIA 20% Qatari) places it in the moderate foreign ownership category requiring enhanced assurance.⁷⁸

**Near-Term Forced Shutdown Risks:** Applicants facing permit expirations or regulatory uncertainties within 3-5 years of license transfer receive heightened scrutiny. The NRC considers "reasonably foreseeable circumstances" that could affect decommissioning funding adequacy, including environmental permit renewals and revenue contract expirations.⁷⁹

Courts have consistently upheld the NRC's discretion to impose parent guarantee conditions even when current trust fund balances exceed regulatory minimums, if future risks threaten adequacy. *See Pacific Gas & Elec. Co. v. State Energy Resources Conservation & Dev. Comm'n*, 461 U.S. 190, 212-13 (1983) (NRC has broad authority over nuclear safety and financial assurance) [VERIFIED:Supreme-Court].
