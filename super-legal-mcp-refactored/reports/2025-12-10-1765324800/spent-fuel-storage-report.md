## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Great Lakes Nuclear Power Company (GLNPC) faces a **$400-600 million lifecycle financial obligation** for spent fuel storage extending from 2025 through at least 2075—a direct consequence of the U.S. Department of Energy's breach of the Nuclear Waste Policy Act obligation to accept commercial spent nuclear fuel by January 31, 1998. This research memorandum quantifies GLNPC's spent fuel inventory, analyzes pool capacity constraints requiring accelerated dry cask storage by 2032, assesses Independent Spent Fuel Storage Installation (ISFSI) expansion requirements, and evaluates pool re-racking as a potential cost-deferral strategy. The findings reveal **critical near-term capital requirements** ($15-25M ISFSI expansion by 2032-2035), **ongoing operational costs** ($8-12M annually for dry cask program), and **indefinite post-shutdown storage obligations** ($2-3M annually for security through 2075 or beyond until DOE accepts fuel).

### Spent Fuel Inventory and Storage Configuration

GLNPC's 46 years of dual-unit pressurized water reactor (PWR) operations have accumulated **3,356 spent fuel assemblies** as of 2025:

- **1,820 assemblies (54%)** stored in spent fuel pools (Unit 1 and Unit 2 pools combined)
- **1,536 assemblies (46%)** stored in 48 Holtec HI-STORM 100 dry casks at the ISFSI (48 casks × 32 assemblies per MPC-32 canister)

GLNPC discharges approximately **95 assemblies per year** from both units combined (typical two-unit refueling on 18-month cycles). This discharge rate will continue through operating license expiration dates: Unit 1 expires August 2037 (12 years remaining), Unit 2 expires February 2041 (16 years remaining). By 2041 shutdown, GLNPC will have accumulated approximately **4,876 total assemblies** requiring on-site storage indefinitely.

The existing ISFSI operates under NRC **general license** provisions (10 CFR 72.210-212), using Holtec HI-STORM 100 casks approved under Certificate of Compliance No. 1014 (Renewed Amendment No. 19, effective March 13, 2025). The HI-STORM 100 system employs passive cooling (natural convection, no mechanical systems), robust structural design capable of withstanding extreme events (earthquakes, tornadoes up to 360 mph, floods, aircraft impact), and effective radiation shielding maintaining dose rates below NRC limits (10 CFR 72.104: 25 mrem/year at controlled area boundary). Each MPC-32 canister stores 32 PWR fuel assemblies in a sealed stainless steel vessel with Metamic-HT neutron-absorbing basket materials.

At the current discharge rate of 95 assemblies per year, GLNPC's spent fuel pools will reach **full capacity by approximately 2032** (7-8 years from 2025). Once pools are full, **100% of discharged fuel must immediately transfer to dry cask storage**, eliminating the operational buffer that pools currently provide for fuel cooldown before cask loading (NRC requires minimum 3-year decay period before cask loading, but longer decay reduces cask thermal loading and extends cask service life).

This **2032 deadline creates three critical operational requirements**:

1. **Accelerated dry cask production**: After 2032, GLNPC must load 3 dry casks per year immediately (95 assemblies ÷ 32 per cask = 3 casks), compared to current practice of loading casks after longer pool decay periods.

2. **ISFSI expansion required**: Current 100-cask pad (48 loaded, 52 available) will be exhausted by 2032-2033. A 50-cask expansion pad must be operational by 2032-2035 to accommodate continued cask loading through 2041 shutdown.

3. **Pool re-racking consideration**: Installing high-density storage racks could gain 250-500 additional assemblies (10-20% capacity increase), deferring the 2032 deadline to 2034-2037 and providing operational flexibility.

### DOE Litigation: $120-180 Million Recovery Expected (Phase II, Q4 2026)

**Background: DOE's Contractual Breach and Nationwide Litigation**

Under the Nuclear Waste Policy Act of 1982, DOE entered into Standard Contracts with all nuclear utilities, obligating DOE to "beginning not later than January 31, 1998, ...dispose of the high-level radioactive waste or spent nuclear fuel" generated by commercial reactors (42 USC § 10222(a)(5)(B)). Utilities paid fees into the Nuclear Waste Fund—1.0 mil per kilowatt-hour of electricity sold—with the statute providing that once fees are paid, generators "shall have no further financial obligation to the Federal Government for the long-term storage and permanent disposal" of spent fuel (42 USC § 10222(a)(2)).

**DOE breached this obligation.** The Yucca Mountain repository was defunded and cancelled in 2010, and no alternative repository exists. As the D.C. Circuit observed, "The government was supposed to start accepting spent nuclear fuel by 1998. It didn't. And there is no prospect that a repository will open any time soon." *In re Aiken Cty.*, 725 F.3d 255, 259 (D.C. Cir. 2013).

**Nationwide Recovery: $8.6 Billion Paid to Date, $39.2 Billion Estimated Total Liability**

Utilities nationwide have sued DOE in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims for breach of contract damages. Through FY2020:
- **$6.3 billion** paid via settlements (covering all past and future damages)
- **$2.3 billion** paid via final court judgments (covering damages already incurred; utilities must file successive claims as costs continue to accrue)
- **Total paid to date: $8.6 billion** from the U.S. Treasury Judgment Fund (not the Nuclear Waste Fund)

DOE's estimated **total liability: $39.2 billion** (including $8.6 billion already paid), with costs continuing to grow annually as utilities incur additional dry cask storage expenses. This liability represents dry cask costs for all 93 commercial reactor sites (operating and shutdown) nationwide.

**GLNPC's DOE Litigation Status:**

- **Phase I (completed 2022)**: GLNPC was awarded **$58 million** for dry cask costs incurred 2010-2020. This judgment has been paid and is in GLNPC's cash reserves.

- **Phase II (pending trial Q3 2026)**: GLNPC claims **$120-180 million** for dry cask costs incurred 2021-2035 (15-year period × $8-12M annually). Trial scheduled Q3 2026, judgment expected **Q4 2026 or Q1 2027**. **Win probability: 90-95%** (DOE's breach is well-established by nationwide precedent; the only dispute is quantum of damages, which courts calculate based on actual costs incurred plus expert projections for future period covered by judgment).

**Impact on Cash Flow and Net Exposure:**

Phase II judgment ($120-180M expected recovery) will **offset GLNPC's dry cask costs through 2035**, effectively reimbursing $8-12M annually for 15 years (2021-2035). However, **post-2035 dry cask costs** ($8-12M annually, continuing through 2075 or beyond) remain **unrecovered** and will require successive Phase III/Phase IV claims in the Court of Federal Claims.

**Successive litigation strategy**: GLNPC must file additional claims approximately every 10-15 years as new dry cask costs accrue:
- **Phase III** (anticipated filing 2033-2035): Recovery for 2035-2050 costs (~$120-180M)
- **Phase IV** (anticipated filing 2048-2050): Recovery for 2050-2065 costs (~$120-180M)
- **Continuing phases** until DOE accepts fuel (potentially Phase V, VI, VII+ if storage extends through 2100)

**Litigation risk**: While Phase II has 90-95% win probability (based on established precedent), successive phases face uncertainty regarding future Court of Federal Claims judgments, potential Congressional action capping damages, or DOE settlement strategies. The $8.6 billion paid to date and $39.2 billion estimated total liability suggest ongoing recoveries are likely, but timing and quantum remain uncertain.

**Regulatory Requirements:**

ISFSI expansion requires NRC approval under **10 CFR 72.212(b)(7)**, which mandates evaluation under **10 CFR 72.48** (changes, tests, and experiments). If the expansion involves:
- **Unreviewed safety question** (new accident scenario, different result from previously analyzed accident, or reduction in margin of safety), OR
- **Change to technical specifications**, OR
- **Departure from FSAR as originally approved**

...then GLNPC must obtain a **site-specific license amendment** under 10 CFR 72.40, requiring full NRC review (12-18 months). If the expansion fits within the general license bounds (same cask design, no new safety questions), GLNPC may proceed under 10 CFR 72.48 with 90-day advance notification to NRC and maintain a 10 CFR 72.48 evaluation in plant records.

**Timeline for ISFSI Expansion:**

- **Planning and design** (2028-2029): 1-2 years for engineering, geotechnical investigation, seismic analysis, NRC pre-submittal meetings
- **NRC license amendment review** (2029-2030): 6-12 months if site-specific amendment required
- **Construction** (2030-2032): 6-12 months for concrete curing, security installation, readiness inspection
- **Target completion**: **2032-2033** (before existing 100-cask pad reaches capacity)

**Recommendation**: Initiate ISFSI expansion planning by **2028** to ensure regulatory approval and construction completion before existing pad capacity is exhausted (~2032-2033). Budget $15-25M capital for expansion, with funding potentially sourced from DOE Phase II judgment ($120-180M expected Q4 2026-Q1 2027).

### Pool Re-Racking: $10-30 Million Investment to Defer Dry Cask Program 3-5 Years

**Overview: High-Density Rack Installation**

Spent fuel pool **re-racking** involves replacing existing storage racks with higher-density racks, decreasing the distance between fuel assemblies to increase storage capacity. The NRC authorized many utilities in the 1980s-1990s to increase pool capacity to as much as five times original design. As the National Research Council observed, "virtually all U.S. spent fuel pools have been re-racked to hold spent fuel assemblies at densities that approach those in reactor cores."

**Capacity Gain: 250-500 Additional Assemblies (10-20% Increase)**

Industry precedents demonstrate that pool re-racking typically achieves **10-20% capacity increase**. For GLNPC's 2,500-assembly pool capacity:
- **10% gain**: 250 additional assemblies
- **20% gain**: 500 additional assemblies

**Timeline Benefit:**
- 250-500 additional assemblies ÷ 95 assemblies/year = **2.6-5.3 years** additional pool storage
- Pools reach full capacity **2034-2037** (instead of 2032), deferring 100% dry cask requirement

**Cost Estimate: $10-30 Million (Both Pools)**

| Cost Component | Estimate (Both Pools) | Basis |
|----------------|-----------------------|-------|
| **High-density rack fabrication** | $6-16M | $3-8M per pool (stainless steel racks with neutron poison panels: Boral, Metamic, Boraflex) |
| **Pool outage & installation** | $4-10M | $2-5M per pool (crane operations, underwater welding, fuel shuffling during refueling outages) |
| **NRC license amendment** | $1-3M | Safety analysis (criticality, structural, thermal-hydraulic), environmental assessment under 10 CFR 50.90 |
| **Total estimated cost** | **$10-30M** | Capital expenditure 2028-2032 |

**NRC Approval Requirements (10 CFR 50.90 License Amendment):**

Pool re-racking requires a **license amendment application** under 10 CFR 50.90, with comprehensive safety analysis demonstrating:

1. **Criticality Safety**: Neutron-absorbing materials (boron carbide panels) maintain subcriticality (k-effective < 0.95) even with high-density configuration and maximum fuel enrichment (up to 5% U-235 for GLNPC's PWR fuel).

2. **Structural Integrity**: High-density racks withstand seismic events (safe shutdown earthquake per 10 CFR 50 Appendix S) without compromising fuel integrity or pool liner.

3. **Thermal-Hydraulic Analysis**: Adequate pool cooling and heat removal capacity for denser fuel arrangement, ensuring pool temperature remains within design basis limits.

4. **Radiological Dose**: Increased spent fuel inventory does not exceed 10 CFR 50 Appendix I dose limits for normal operations or accident scenarios.

**NRC review timeline**: 12-18 months for license amendment approval. Installation must occur during refueling outages (Unit 1 and Unit 2 outages typically staggered), requiring coordination with reactor operations schedule.

**Cost-Benefit Analysis: Breakeven to Moderately Positive Net Savings**

| Scenario | Cost/Benefit | Calculation |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Re-racking investment** | -$10-30M | Upfront capital (2028-2030) |
| **Deferred dry cask costs** | +$24-60M | $8-12M annually × 3-5 years deferral |
| **Net present value (NPV)** | **$0-30M positive** | Breakeven to moderately positive savings |

**Key Decision Factors:**

**Pros of Pool Re-Racking:**
- Defers dry cask program capital and operating costs ($8-12M annually for 3-5 years)
- Extends pool capacity buffer, providing operational flexibility for fuel management
- Reduces near-term ISFSI expansion urgency (50-cask pad could be delayed to 2035-2037 instead of 2032-2035)
- Net positive cash flow if capacity gain achieves 15-20% (upper end of industry range)

**Cons of Pool Re-Racking:**
- Requires multi-month pool outages during installation, potentially affecting refueling schedule and capacity factor
- Upfront capital investment $10-30M (versus incremental dry cask program costs)
- Only **delays** (does not eliminate) need for dry cask storage—ultimate ISFSI expansion still required by 2037-2041
- Engineering feasibility uncertain without detailed structural analysis (older pool designs may have seismic margin limitations for high-density racks)

**Recommendation:**

GLNPC should conduct a **pool re-racking feasibility study** by Q2 2026, including:
1. **Structural capacity analysis**: Can Unit 1 and Unit 2 pools safely accommodate high-density racks given original design basis and current seismic requirements?
2. **Criticality safety margin**: Do current fuel enrichments (up to 5% U-235) permit adequate subcriticality margin (k-eff < 0.95) with reduced spacing?
3. **Thermal-hydraulic modeling**: Is pool cooling capacity adequate for denser fuel arrangement?
4. **Net present value calculation**: Compare $10-30M investment versus $24-60M deferred dry cask costs, discounted at GLNPC's cost of capital (likely 5-7% for regulated utility).

If feasibility study demonstrates **net positive NPV and structural feasibility**, proceed with NRC pre-application meeting Q4 2026 and file license amendment Q2 2029 for installation during 2030-2032 refueling outages. If **negative NPV or structural infeasibility**, proceed with accelerated dry cask program and ISFSI expansion on original timeline (2032-2035).

**Funding Source**: DOE Phase II judgment ($120-180M expected Q4 2026-Q1 2027) provides sufficient cash to fund pool re-racking investment ($10-30M) without affecting Nuclear Decommissioning Trust (NDT) contributions or operational cash reserves.

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. Regulatory Framework: 10 CFR Part 72 and General License Requirements

The Nuclear Regulatory Commission regulates independent spent fuel storage installations (ISFSIs) under **10 CFR Part 72**.¹ This regulation establishes two licensing pathways: (1) site-specific licenses under 10 CFR 72.40, requiring individual NRC review and environmental assessment for up to 40 years; and (2) general licenses under 10 CFR 72.210-212, allowing utilities to store spent fuel in NRC-approved cask designs without individual license applications.²

GLNPC operates its ISFSI under the **general license** provisions of 10 CFR 72.212, using Holtec HI-STORM 100 casks approved under Certificate of Compliance (CoC) No. 1014.³ The general license requires:

- **90-day advance notification** to NRC before first storage of spent fuel (10 CFR 72.212(b)(1))
- **Registration of each cask** within 30 days of loading, including CoC number, model, and identification (10 CFR 72.212(b)(2))
- **Site-specific evaluations** demonstrating the site can safely accommodate dry cask storage, including seismic and tornado analysis (10 CFR 72.212(b)(3))
- **Conformance to CoC terms and conditions**, including technical specifications for cask loading, handling, and monitoring (10 CFR 72.212(b)(4))
- **Security program integration** under 10 CFR 73.51 (physical protection of spent fuel storage) (10 CFR 72.212(b)(9))⁴

### B. DOE's Spent Fuel Disposal Obligation Under the Nuclear Waste Policy Act

The **Nuclear Waste Policy Act of 1982** (42 USC 10101 et seq.) established the framework for permanent disposal of commercial spent nuclear fuel.⁵ Section 10222 imposes a mandatory obligation on the Department of Energy:

> "The Secretary [of Energy], **beginning not later than January 31, 1998**, will dispose of the high-level radioactive waste or spent nuclear fuel involved as provided in this subchapter."⁶

Utilities pay fees into the Nuclear Waste Fund—1.0 mil per kilowatt-hour of electricity sold—to offset disposal costs.⁷ Once fees are paid, "the person delivering spent fuel...shall have **no further financial obligation** to the Federal Government for the long-term storage and permanent disposal" of that material.⁸

**DOE breached this obligation.** The Yucca Mountain repository, designated under the Act, was defunded and cancelled in 2010 with no alternative repository operational.⁹ As a result, utilities nationwide—including GLNPC—must continue storing spent fuel on-site indefinitely (decades, potentially 100+ years until a repository becomes available).¹⁰

### G. Pool Re-Racking: Alternative to Defer Dry Cask Program

**Overview:**
Spent fuel pool **re-racking** involves replacing existing storage racks with higher-density racks, decreasing the distance between fuel assemblies to increase storage capacity. The NRC authorized many power plant owners in the 1980s-1990s to increase pool capacity to as much as five times original design, and "virtually all U.S. spent fuel pools have been re-racked to hold spent fuel assemblies at densities that approach those in reactor cores."³⁶

**Re-Racking Precedents and Capacity Gains:**
- **Typical capacity increase**: 10-20% (250-500 additional assemblies for GLNPC's 2,500-assembly pools)³⁷
- **NRC approval required**: License amendment under 10 CFR 50.90, with safety analysis demonstrating:³⁸
  - **Criticality safety**: Neutron-absorbing materials (boron carbide panels: Boral, Metamic, Boraflex) maintain subcriticality (k-eff < 0.95) even with high-density configuration
  - **Structural integrity**: Racks withstand seismic events (safe shutdown earthquake) without compromising fuel integrity
  - **Thermal-hydraulic analysis**: Adequate cooling and heat removal capacity for denser fuel arrangement
  - **Radiological dose**: Increased inventory does not exceed 10 CFR 50 Appendix I dose limits

**Cost Estimate: $10-30 Million (Both Pools)**
- **Rack fabrication**: $3-8M per pool (high-density stainless steel racks with neutron poison panels)
- **Pool outage and installation**: $2-5M per pool (crane operations, underwater welding, fuel shuffling)
- **NRC license amendment**: $1-3M (safety analysis, criticality calculations, environmental assessment)
- **Total for both Unit 1 and Unit 2 pools**: $10-30M

**Benefit: Defer Dry Cask Program 3-5 Years**
- **250-500 additional assemblies** (10-20% gain) ÷ 95 assemblies/year = **2.6-5.3 years** additional pool storage
- **Pools full by 2034-2037** (instead of 2032), deferring 100% dry cask requirement
- **Cost-benefit analysis**: Deferring $8-12M annually for 3-5 years = **$24-60M avoided dry cask costs**, compared to $10-30M re-racking investment = **Net savings $0-30M** (breakeven to moderately positive)

**Recommendation:**
GLNPC should evaluate pool re-racking **cost-benefit and timing**:
- **Pros**: Defers dry cask program capital and operating costs, extends pool capacity buffer, provides operational flexibility
- **Cons**: Requires multi-month pool outages during installation (affecting refueling schedule), upfront capital investment $10-30M, only delays (does not eliminate) need for dry cask storage
- **Decision factors**: Compare net present value of re-racking investment ($10-30M) versus accelerated dry cask program costs ($8-12M annually starting 2032 vs. 2034-2037); consider whether DOE Phase II judgment ($120-180M expected Q4 2026-Q1 2027) provides sufficient cash to fund re-racking without affecting NDT contributions or operational cash reserves
