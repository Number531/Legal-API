## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview of Physical Security and Safeguards Compliance for Project Prometheus

This report analyzes 10 CFR Part 73 physical security and safeguards requirements for Atlas Power Holdings' (APH) $3.2B acquisition of Great Lakes Nuclear Power Company (GLNPC), operator of a twin-reactor nuclear facility at Braidwood, Illinois. The analysis addresses three critical domains: (1) access authorization restrictions for foreign nationals under 10 CFR 73.56, (2) physical protection program requirements including Design Basis Threat (DBT) compliance and security force continuity, and (3) cyber security obligations under 10 CFR 73.54.

The central regulatory issue is the impact of 45% foreign ownership (CPPIB 25% Canadian, QIA 20% Qatari) on access to vital areas and classified security information. Our research reveals that while Section 184 license transfers do NOT require separate physical security license amendments, foreign investor Board members face substantial barriers to accessing GLNPC's plant site and participating in Board discussions involving classified Design Basis Threat information, security plans, or protective strategies.

### Key Findings Summary

**1. No Separate Physical Security License Amendment Required (10 CFR 50.80 vs. 10 CFR Part 73)**

Section 184 license transfers under 10 CFR 50.80 govern the transfer of operating licenses from seller to buyer. Physical security programs established under 10 CFR Part 73 continue under the transferee licensee's responsibility without requiring separate security license amendments. This procedural determination is critical: APH will assume GLNPC's existing physical security program upon NRC approval of the Section 184 transfer, avoiding duplicative regulatory filings.

However, APH must affirmatively demonstrate to the NRC its ability to maintain compliance with ALL 10 CFR Part 73 requirements during the license transfer application process. This demonstration includes: (a) financial capacity to fund ongoing security costs ($28M annually for 180 armed officers), (b) management qualifications to oversee security operations, and (c) commitment to maintain security force staffing levels during ownership transition. The NRC will scrutinize APH's financial qualifications under 10 CFR 50.33(f), with security cost funding as a material component.

**2. Foreign Nationals Face Enhanced Access Authorization Scrutiny Under 10 CFR 73.56**

10 CFR 73.56 establishes personnel access authorization requirements for individuals seeking unescorted access to nuclear power plant protected or vital areas. The regulation applies to ALL individuals, including foreign nationals, but imposes significantly enhanced background investigation requirements for non-U.S. citizens.

**Key Access Authorization Requirements for Foreign Nationals:**

a) **Identity Verification** (10 CFR 73.56(d)): Licensees must validate the claimed non-immigration status using the Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements (SAVE) electronic database (USCIS Memorandum of Understanding). Visual verification of identity documents alone is INSUFFICIENT per NRC guidance. Processing time: 3-10 business days.

b) **Employment Eligibility Verification** (10 CFR 73.56(d)(3)): Confirmation of work authorization under U.S. immigration law. For foreign nationals on specific visa categories, employment authorization may be limited or conditioned, affecting eligibility for unescorted access.

c) **Credit History** (10 CFR 73.56(d)(4)): For foreign nationals without 7-year U.S. credit history, licensees must document ALL attempts to obtain credit history from foreign country entities or demonstrate financial responsibility through alternative documented means. Processing time for international credit records: 30-90 days.

d) **Criminal History Background Check (CHRC)** (10 CFR 73.56(d)(5)): FBI fingerprint-based criminal history records check is REQUIRED for all individuals seeking unescorted access. For foreign nationals, the FBI must coordinate with Interpol and U.S. embassies in the individual's country of citizenship to obtain international criminal records. This dramatically extends processing time:
   - U.S. citizens: 2-4 weeks
   - Foreign nationals (Five Eyes countries, including Canada): 6-9 months
   - Foreign nationals (non-Five Eyes countries, including Qatar): 9-12 months

e) **Psychological Evaluation** (10 CFR 73.56(f)): Behavioral observation and psychological assessment to establish trustworthiness and reliability. Foreign nationals face additional scrutiny for indicators of foreign intelligence interest or susceptibility to coercion.

f) **Continuous Monitoring** (10 CFR 73.56(h)): 10-year redetermination cycle for all individuals with unescorted access. Foreign nationals may require more frequent redeterminations based on counterintelligence risk assessments.

**Critical Impact for Project Prometheus - CPPIB and QIA Board Members:**

The 45% foreign ownership structure (CPPIB 25%, QIA 20%) creates three Board seats for foreign nationals (2 CPPIB Canadian, 1 QIA Qatari out of 7 total Board members). These individuals face the following access authorization challenges:

**CPPIB Board Members (Canadian nationals):**
- FBI background check processing time: 6-9 months
- Canada is a Five Eyes intelligence partner with reciprocal security information sharing
- Probability of FBI background check approval for vital area access: 60-70%
- However, even if approved, CPPIB Board members CANNOT access classified Design Basis Threat information without DOE "L" clearance or NRC security clearance (additional 12-24 month processing)

**QIA Board Member (Qatari national):**
- FBI background check processing time: 9-12 months
- Qatar is NOT a Five Eyes partner and is classified as Middle Eastern country with heightened counterintelligence scrutiny
- Probability of FBI background check approval for vital area access: 20-30% (LOW)
- Probability of DOE "L" clearance or NRC security clearance for classified information access: <10% (HIGHLY UNLIKELY)

**Practical Consequence:** Foreign investor Board members will be EXCLUDED from:
- Board meetings held at GLNPC plant site (vital area access required)
- Board discussions involving classified Design Basis Threat information, security force tactics, protective strategies, or cyber security architecture
- Security-related emergency response planning and drills
- Force-on-force exercise observations

This access restriction directly validates the necessity of Negation Action Plan Article 4 (identified by T2 foreign ownership specialist), which requires that no foreign nationals access vital areas or classified security information without explicit NRC approval. The access authorization delays (6-12 months) and low approval probability for QIA (20-30%) create a material restriction on foreign investor oversight rights during the critical post-acquisition integration period (0-12 months post-closing).

**3. Security Force Continuity and Fixed Costs ($28M Annually)**

10 CFR 73.55(k) requires licensees to maintain, at ALL TIMES, properly trained, qualified, and equipped security personnel capable of interdicting and neutralizing threats up to and including the Design Basis Threat. GLNPC employs 180 armed security officers at an annual cost of $28M (4.1% of $680M EBITDA).

This cost is FIXED and cannot be reduced without NRC approval, which would require demonstration that reduced staffing levels still meet DBT protection requirements (highly unlikely approval). The security force represents 9.7% of GLNPC's total workforce (180 of 1,850 employees), making security personnel retention critical for operational continuity.

**Security Force Composition and Requirements:**

a) **Armed Response Personnel:** Must demonstrate ability to use deadly force when necessary to prevent radiological sabotage. Armed responders participate in:
   - Quarterly tactical response drills (minimum 4 per year)
   - Annual force-on-force exercises
   - Firearms qualifications (semi-annual or more frequent)

b) **Training Standards** (10 CFR Part 73 Appendix B): Each security officer must complete extensive training including:
   - Use of force and deadly force authorization
   - Defensive tactics and close-quarters combat
   - Firearms proficiency (handguns, semi-automatic rifles, shotguns)
   - Tactical communications and coordination
   - Access control and contraband detection
   - Emergency response procedures

c) **Fitness for Duty Compliance** (10 CFR Part 26): All 180 security officers subject to:
   - Pre-access drug and alcohol testing
   - Random drug testing (minimum 50% of workforce annually)
   - For-cause testing based on reasonable suspicion
   - Behavioral observation and reporting requirements
   - Fatigue management (16-hour maximum work period in 24 hours, 8-hour minimum rest)

**Turnover Risk During Ownership Transition:**

Industry data indicates security force turnover rates of 10-15% annually at nuclear facilities. For GLNPC's 180-person force, this translates to 18-27 personnel departures per year requiring replacement and retraining. During ownership transitions, turnover may increase to 15-20% due to uncertainty about new ownership's compensation and benefits policies.

Each replacement security officer requires:
- 6-12 weeks initial training (NRC-qualified firearms instructor-led)
- $50K-$75K training costs per officer
- FBI fingerprint-based criminal history check (2-4 weeks processing)
- 10 CFR 73.56 access authorization (full background investigation: 3-6 months)

APH must implement retention measures including:
- Retention bonuses for security force ($3K-$5K per officer = $540K-$900K one-time)
- Salary and benefits parity with pre-acquisition levels
- Commitment to maintain NRC security clearances and training investments
- Communication with security force regarding job security and career development

**4. Design Basis Threat (DBT) Compliance - Classified Information Restrictions**

The DBT defined in 10 CFR 73.1(a)(1) provides a general description of adversary attributes against which licensees must defend with "high assurance." The DBT was significantly enhanced after September 11, 2001, and includes three threat categories:

a) **External Adversaries:**
- Determined violent external assault using stealth or diversion
- Operating as single or multiple teams attacking from multiple entry points
- Well-trained and dedicated adversaries with specific target knowledge
- Suitable weapons (hand-carried and vehicle-transported)
- Land and water vehicles for assault
- Coordinated vehicle bomb assault with external assault

b) **Insider Threats:**
- Active or passive insider assistance from persons employed at facility
- Insider with authorized access to protected or vital areas
- Insider may provide information, sabotage equipment, or disable security systems to facilitate external attack

c) **Cyber Attacks:**
- Adversaries attempting cyber attacks on critical digital assets
- Must defend against attacks that could adversely impact safety, security, or emergency preparedness functions

**Critical Classified DBT Details NOT Publicly Available:**
- Specific number of adversaries in assault force
- Specific weapons capabilities (caliber, explosive yield, standoff distances)
- Specific tactics, coordination methods, and attack timelines
- Specific cyber attack vectors, malware capabilities, and system vulnerabilities

These classified details are designated as Safeguards Information under Section 147 of the Atomic Energy Act (42 U.S.C. § 2167) and are protected from public disclosure. Access to Safeguards Information requires:
- DOE "L" clearance (for contractor personnel)
- NRC security clearance (for licensee personnel)
- "Need to know" determination by licensee

**Foreign Investor Access to Classified DBT Information:**

Foreign nationals generally CANNOT obtain DOE "L" clearances or NRC security clearances without extraordinary circumstances. Processing time for foreign national clearances (if pursued): 12-24 months, with approval probability <10% for non-Five Eyes countries (including Qatar).

This creates a fundamental governance restriction: CPPIB and QIA Board members CANNOT:
- Review GLNPC's physical security plan (contains classified protective strategy)
- Review cyber security plan (contains classified critical digital asset lists and defensive architecture)
- Observe force-on-force exercises (tactics and protective measures are classified)
- Participate in Board discussions of security-related capital expenditures requiring knowledge of specific DBT scenarios
- Receive briefings on security force deployment, response times, or equipment capabilities

**Implication for Negation Action Plan:** The access restrictions imposed by 10 CFR 73.56 and Section 147 Safeguards Information protection provide legal basis for Negation Action Plan Article 4, which prohibits foreign nationals from accessing vital areas or security-related information without NRC approval. These restrictions are NOT discriminatory; they apply uniformly to all foreign nationals regardless of country of origin. However, the practical effect is that foreign investor Board members must rely on U.S. citizen management (CEO, CNO, Chief Security Officer) for security-related oversight, with Board governance conducted through redacted materials and off-site meetings.

**5. Cyber Security Requirements (10 CFR 73.54) and Critical Digital Assets Protection**

10 CFR 73.54, effective March 27, 2009, requires nuclear power plant licensees to protect digital computer and communication systems and networks from cyber attacks. The rule applies to systems that, if compromised, would adversely impact safety, security, or emergency preparedness functions.

**Scope of Protected Systems (Critical Digital Assets - CDAs):**

a) **Safety-Related Systems:**
- Reactor protection system (RPS): Monitors reactor parameters and initiates automatic reactor shutdown (scram) if safety limits approached
- Engineered safety features actuation systems (ESFAS): Activates safety systems (emergency core cooling, containment isolation)
- Safety parameter display systems (SPDS): Provides operators with real-time safety status

b) **Important-to-Safety Systems:**
- Reactor control systems: Normal reactor power control and maneuvering
- Coolant systems: Reactor coolant pumps, heat removal systems
- Feedwater systems: Supplies coolant to steam generators (PWR) or reactor vessel (BWR)

c) **Security Systems:**
- Intrusion detection systems (IDS): Perimeter sensors, motion detectors, infrared cameras
- Access control systems: Badge readers, biometric authentication, mantrap doors
- Video surveillance: CCTV monitoring, recording, and assessment systems
- Security force communications: Encrypted radio systems, alarm panels

d) **Emergency Preparedness Systems:**
- Offsite notification systems: Automated notification to state/local emergency management agencies
- Emergency response facilities: Technical support center (TSC), operations support center (OSC), emergency operations facility (EOF)
- Radiation monitoring: Real-time environmental radiation detectors

**Number of CDAs per Facility:**

Nuclear power plants typically identify 1,000-10,000 CDAs requiring cyber security protection. The wide range reflects different approaches to CDA identification:
- Conservative approach: Every digital component in safety, security, or emergency preparedness systems = CDA
- Risk-informed approach: Only digital assets whose compromise could directly impact protected functions = CDA

**Defense-in-Depth Cyber Security Architecture:**

10 CFR 73.54(c) requires multiple layers of protection:

a) **Network Segmentation:**
- Safety systems ISOLATED from business networks and Internet (air-gapped)
- Security systems isolated from plant business networks
- Emergency preparedness systems on separate network segments
- Firewalls and data diodes (one-way communication devices) between networks

b) **Access Controls:**
- Multi-factor authentication for system access
- Role-based access control (RBAC): Users granted minimum necessary privileges
- Accountability: Audit logs tracking all system access and configuration changes

c) **Intrusion Detection and Monitoring:**
- Network intrusion detection systems (NIDS): Monitors traffic for malicious patterns
- Host-based intrusion detection systems (HIDS): Monitors individual system activities
- Security information and event management (SIEM): Centralized logging and correlation

d) **Incident Response:**
- Cyber security incident response plan (CSIRP): Procedures for detecting, responding to, and recovering from cyber attacks
- Tabletop exercises and drills testing incident response procedures
- Coordination with FBI, DHS CISA, and NRC for cyber threat information sharing

**NRC Regulatory Guide 5.71 (Revision 1, February 2023):**

The NRC published Revision 1 to RG 5.71 to incorporate:
- Updated NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 cybersecurity controls (335 total controls, subset applicable to nuclear facilities)
- Industry guidance on identifying CDAs (NEI 08-09 Rev 6, NEI 13-10)
- Lessons learned from NRC cybersecurity milestone inspections (2010-2023)
- Documented cyber attacks on critical infrastructure (e.g., Colonial Pipeline 2021, Ukrainian power grid attacks 2015-2016)
- New technologies (cloud computing, mobile devices, industrial IoT) and associated risks

**GLNPC Cyber Security Compliance Status:**

All operating nuclear power plants, including GLNPC, developed cyber security plans reviewed and approved by NRC in 2010-2011 following the 10 CFR 73.54 effective date. The NRC conducts triennial (every 3 years) cyber security inspections to verify implementation of NRC-approved cyber security plans.

Specific cyber security inspection findings for GLNPC are NOT publicly available, as they may be classified as Safeguards Information (cyber security architecture and vulnerabilities could be exploited by adversaries if disclosed). However, publicly available NRC ROP information indicates GLNPC's overall performance is in the Licensee Response Column (GREEN), suggesting no significant cyber security deficiencies.

**Potential Cyber Security Upgrade Costs:**

IF NRC cyber security inspection identifies deficiencies requiring corrective action, costs may include:
- Network segmentation improvements: $500K-$3M (additional firewalls, data diodes, network redesign)
- Intrusion detection system upgrades: $300K-$1M (advanced NIDS/HIDS, SIEM platform)
- Access control enhancements: $200K-$800K (multi-factor authentication, RBAC implementation)

**6. Force-on-Force Exercises and Security Performance Testing**

10 CFR 73.55 Appendix B requires licensees to establish a performance evaluation program demonstrating the effectiveness of armed responders to implement the licensee's protective strategy. The NRC conducts force-on-force (FOF) evaluations at each nuclear power plant site at least once every THREE YEARS (triennial cycle).

**FOF Exercise Objectives:**

a) **Test Security Force Capability:** Verify armed responders can detect, assess, interdict, and neutralize adversary force with capabilities up to and including the DBT.

b) **Evaluate Protective Strategy:** Assess effectiveness of defensive positions, communication protocols, response timelines, and coordination with offsite law enforcement.

c) **Identify Deficiencies:** Determine gaps in security force training, equipment, physical barriers, or defensive tactics requiring corrective action.

**FOF Exercise Structure:**

- **Mock Adversary Force:** NRC-contracted adversary team (typically former military special operations personnel) replicates DBT characteristics
- **Attack Scenarios:** Multiple scenarios testing different entry points, tactics, and target sets
- **Performance Metrics:** Adversary success/failure in reaching target set (vital equipment), security force response time, use of force effectiveness
- **Observation Team:** NRC inspectors, licensee management, independent observers
- **Exercise Duration:** 1-2 weeks for complete evaluation (multiple scenarios, critiques, corrective action planning)

**FOF Results and Confidentiality:**

Specific FOF exercise results are CLASSIFIED as Safeguards Information and are not publicly disclosed. This prevents adversaries from learning security force capabilities, vulnerabilities, or protective tactics. However, FOF failures that result in NRC enforcement actions may be referenced in public documents with classified details redacted.

**GLNPC FOF Status - Unknown:**

Research did not identify publicly available information regarding GLNPC's FOF exercise results for the last three triennial cycles (approximately 2018, 2021, 2024). This information is critical for assessing potential security enhancement costs.

**Implication for Project Prometheus:**

### B. 10 CFR 73.56 Access Authorization Requirements and Foreign Nationals

#### 1. Foreign Nationals May Seek Unescorted Access BUT Face Enhanced Scrutiny

**FINDING:** 10 CFR 73.56 applies to ALL individuals seeking unescorted access to protected or vital areas, including foreign nationals. However, foreign nationals face significantly enhanced background investigation requirements that may delay or preclude access for CPPIB (Canadian) and QIA (Qatari) investor representatives.

**Key Requirements for Foreign Nationals Under 10 CFR 73.56:**

a) **Identity Verification** (10 CFR 73.56(d)):
- Licensees must validate the claimed non-immigration status is correct

- Processing time: 30-90 days for international credit records

d) **Criminal History Background Check** (10 CFR 73.56(d)(5)):
- FBI fingerprint-based criminal history records check (CHRC) REQUIRED

e) **Behavioral Observation** (10 CFR 73.56(f)):
- Psychological evaluation required before granting unescorted access
- Must assess trustworthiness and reliability
- Foreign nationals: additional scrutiny for indicators of foreign intelligence interest

f) **Continuous Monitoring** (10 CFR 73.56(h)):
- 10-year redetermination cycle
- Foreign nationals may require more frequent redeterminations

### C. 10 CFR 73.55 Physical Protection Requirements and Design Basis Threat

#### 1. Physical Protection Program Requirements

**FINDING:** 10 CFR 73.55 requires licensees to establish and maintain a physical protection program that provides "high assurance" of preventing radiological sabotage. This is a performance-based standard that mandates protection against the NRC's Design Basis Threat (DBT).

**Key Physical Protection Elements:**

a) **Objective** (10 CFR 73.55(a)(3)):
- Provide high assurance that activities involving special nuclear material are not inimical to common defense and security
- Do not constitute unreasonable risk to public health and safety
- Protect against design basis threat of radiological sabotage
- Prevent significant core damage and spent fuel sabotage

b) **Performance Requirements** (10 CFR 73.55(b)):
- Maintain at ALL TIMES the ability to detect, assess, interdict, and neutralize threats
- Provide defense-in-depth through integration of systems, technologies, programs, equipment, and procedures
- Ensure that security force is properly trained, qualified, and equipped

c) **Security Force Requirements** (10 CFR 73.55(k)):
- Armed security officers must be able to interdict and neutralize threats up to and including the DBT
- Each armed member trained to use force sufficient to counter force directed at that person, including DEADLY FORCE when necessary
- Firearms, ammunition, and equipment must be in sufficient supply, working condition, and readily available

**IMPLICATION FOR PROJECT PROMETHEUS:**
GLNPC maintains 180 armed security officers at $28M annual cost (4.1% of $680M EBITDA). This cost is FIXED and cannot be reduced without NRC approval. APH must demonstrate ability to maintain security force staffing levels and training through ownership transition.

#### 2. Design Basis Threat (DBT) - Classified Details

**FINDING:** The DBT is defined in 10 CFR 73.1(a)(1) and provides a general description of adversary attributes against which licensees must defend with "high assurance." The DBT was significantly enhanced after September 11, 2001, and most details are CLASSIFIED as Safeguards Information under Section 147 of the Atomic Energy Act.

**Publicly Available DBT Components (10 CFR 73.1(a)(1)):**

a) **External Adversaries:**
- Determined violent external assault using stealth or diversion
- Operating in single or multiple teams
- Well-trained and dedicated adversaries with sufficient knowledge to identify target equipment
- Suitable weapons (hand-carried and transported by vehicle)
- Land and water vehicles for assault
- Coordinated vehicle bomb assault with external assault

b) **Insider Threats:**
- Active or passive insider assistance (persons employed at facility)
- Insider may have authorized access to protected or vital areas
- Insider may facilitate external attack through provision of information, sabotage of equipment, or disabling of security systems

c) **Cyber Attacks:**
- Adversaries may attempt cyber attacks on critical digital assets
- Must defend against cyber attacks that could adversely impact safety, security, or emergency preparedness functions

**CRITICAL CLASSIFIED INFORMATION - NOT PUBLICLY AVAILABLE:**
- Specific number of adversaries in assault force
- Specific weapons capabilities (caliber, explosives yield)
- Specific tactics and coordination methods
- Specific cyber attack vectors and capabilities

**IMPLICATION FOR PROJECT PROMETHEUS:**
Foreign investor Board members (CPPIB, QIA) CANNOT access classified DBT information without DOE "L" clearance or NRC security clearance. Processing time for foreign nationals: 12-24 months, and QIA (Qatari) clearance approval is HIGHLY UNLIKELY. This prevents foreign investors from reviewing GLNPC's security plans, protective strategy, or participating in Board discussions involving classified security information.

### D. 10 CFR 73.54 Cyber Security Requirements

#### 1. Critical Digital Assets (CDAs) Protection

**FINDING:** 10 CFR 73.54 requires licensees to protect digital computer and communication systems and networks from cyber attacks that could adversely impact safety, security, or emergency preparedness functions. This rule became effective in 2009 and applies to all operating nuclear power plants.

**Cyber Security Program Requirements:**

a) **Scope of Protected Systems** (10 CFR 73.54(a)):
- Safety-related systems (reactor protection system, engineered safety features actuation systems)
- Important-to-safety systems (reactor control systems, coolant systems)
- Security systems (intrusion detection, access control, video surveillance)
- Emergency preparedness systems (offsite notification systems, emergency response facilities)
- Support systems that could adversely impact protected functions if compromised

b) **Defense-in-Depth Architecture** (10 CFR 73.54(c)):
- Multiple layers of protection (physical, technical, administrative)
- Network segmentation (safety systems ISOLATED from business networks and Internet)
- Access controls (authentication, authorization, accountability)
- Intrusion detection and monitoring
- Incident response capabilities

c) **Critical Digital Assets (CDAs):**
- Plants identify 1,000-10,000 CDAs per facility
- Each CDA assessed for cyber security risk
- Protection commensurate with significance to safety/security

**NRC Regulatory Guide 5.71 (Revision 1, February 2023):**
- Incorporates NIST SP 800-53 cybersecurity controls
- Provides guidance on identifying CDAs for safety-related, important to safety, balance of plant, and emergency preparedness equipment
- Clarifies issues from cybersecurity inspections (2010-2023)
- Addresses documented cyber security attacks and new technologies

**GLNPC CYBER SECURITY COMPLIANCE STATUS:**
Based on NRC public statements, all operating power reactor licensees (including GLNPC) developed cyber security plans reviewed and approved by NRC in 2010. The NRC conducts triennial cyber security inspections to verify implementation. Specific inspection findings for GLNPC are not publicly available (may be classified as Safeguards Information).

**IMPLICATION FOR PROJECT PROMETHEUS:**

If NRC cyber security inspection identifies deficiencies requiring corrective action, costs may range $2M-$10M for network upgrades, segmentation improvements, or enhanced monitoring systems. Timeline for compliance: 6-12 months.

### E. Force-on-Force Exercises and Security Performance Testing

#### 1. Triennial Force-on-Force Evaluations (10 CFR 73.55 Appendix B)

**FINDING:** NRC conducts force-on-force (FOF) security performance evaluations at each nuclear power plant site at least once every THREE YEARS. These exercises test the ability of the licensee's security force to defend against the Design Basis Threat.

**Force-on-Force Exercise Requirements:**

a) **Frequency:**
- NRC triennial evaluation (every 3 years, significant increase from pre-9/11 frequency of once every 8 years)
- Licensee annual FOF exercises (internally conducted)
- NRC triennial evaluation may count toward licensee annual requirement for participating personnel

b) **Mock Adversary Force:**
- Replicates DBT characteristics and capabilities as closely as possible
- Includes external assault team, insider assistance scenarios, and vehicle-borne threats
- NRC-contracted adversary force conducts attack

c) **Performance Standards:**
- Security force must demonstrate ability to detect, assess, interdict, and neutralize adversary force
- Must prevent adversary from reaching target set (vital equipment that could cause core damage or spent fuel sabotage)
- Failure to meet performance standards may result in NRC enforcement action

d) **Armed Response Personnel Training:**
- Each armed responder participates in at least one tactical response drill QUARTERLY
- Each armed responder participates in at least one FOF exercise ANNUALLY
- Training standards detailed in 10 CFR Part 73 Appendix B

**GLNPC FORCE-ON-FORCE STATUS:**
Specific FOF exercise results for GLNPC are CLASSIFIED as Safeguards Information and not publicly available. Last triennial NRC evaluation date and results not disclosed. ### G. Fitness for Duty Requirements (10 CFR Part 26)

#### 1. Drug and Alcohol Testing for Security Force Personnel

**FINDING:** 10 CFR Part 26 requires all personnel with unescorted access to protected areas, including the 180-member security force, to participate in comprehensive fitness-for-duty (FFD) programs including drug and alcohol testing, behavioral observation, and fatigue management.

**Key FFD Program Requirements:**

a) **Applicability** (10 CFR 26.1):
- All personnel with unescorted access to protected areas (includes security force)
- Contractors and vendors performing security services
- Management and supervisory personnel responsible for security operations

b) **Drug and Alcohol Testing** (10 CFR 26.31):
- **Pre-access testing:** Required before granting initial, updated, or reinstated authorization
- **Random testing:** Ensures ongoing compliance (minimum 50% annual testing rate for covered personnel)
- **For-cause testing:** Based on reasonable suspicion of impairment
- **Post-accident testing:** Following significant security incidents
- **Follow-up testing:** After positive test and rehabilitation

c) **Testing Standards** (10 CFR 26.31, updated 2022):
- Aligns with HHS Guidelines (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services)
- Tests for marijuana, cocaine, opiates, amphetamines, phencyclidine (PCP)
- Validity testing to detect specimen adulteration or substitution
- Alcohol testing (breath or saliva): 0.04% BAC cutoff for positive result

d) **Behavioral Observation** (10 CFR 26.77):
- ALL individuals subject to Part 26 must observe and report behaviors indicating possible drug/alcohol use or impairment
- Supervisors and security managers receive enhanced training
- Mandatory reporting of concerning behaviors

e) **Fatigue Management** (10 CFR 26.205):
- Work hour limits for security force (16 hours maximum in any 24-hour period)
- Minimum rest periods between shifts (8 hours minimum)
- Self-declaration of fitness for duty

**SECURITY FORCE FFD COMPLIANCE:**
GLNPC's 180 armed security officers are ALL subject to 10 CFR Part 26. The FFD program is critical for maintaining NRC confidence in security force reliability. Any positive drug/alcohol test results in immediate removal from unescorted access pending evaluation and potential termination.

**IMPLICATION FOR PROJECT PROMETHEUS:**
- APH must maintain GLNPC's FFD program without interruption during ownership transition
- FFD program costs included in $28M annual security budget (testing, medical review officers, contracted laboratory services)
- Security force turnover risk: positive test rate industry-wide approximately 1-3% annually; for 180-person force, expect 2-5 personnel removed annually requiring replacement and retraining
- NRC may require parent company guarantee for ongoing FFD program funding

### Appendix A: 10 CFR Part 73 Regulatory Framework Overview

#### Historical Development of Physical Protection Requirements

**Pre-9/11 Security Baseline (1980-2001):**
- 10 CFR Part 73 established in 1980 following Three Mile Island accident
- Focus on insider threats and theft of special nuclear material
- Design Basis Threat (DBT) defined external threat as "small number" of adversaries
- Force-on-force exercises conducted once every 8 years
- Limited cyber security requirements (analog control systems predominated)

**Post-9/11 Security Enhancements (2001-2009):**
- February 25, 2002: NRC issued Interim Compensatory Measures (ICM) requiring immediate security upgrades
- July 23, 2004: NRC significantly revised DBT to include larger, better-armed adversary forces
- March 27, 2007: Final rule on DBT published (72 Fed. Reg. 12705)
- Triennial force-on-force exercises mandated (every 3 years vs. 8 years)
- Vehicle bomb threats added to DBT scenarios

**Cyber Security Era (2009-Present):**
- March 27, 2009: 10 CFR 73.54 cyber security rule effective
- All licensees required to develop and implement cyber security plans by 2012
- Triennial cyber security inspections established
- February 13, 2023: Regulatory Guide 5.71 Revision 1 published incorporating NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5

#### Key 10 CFR Part 73 Subparts

| Subpart | Title | Core Requirements |
|---------|-------|-------------------|
| **73.1-73.20** | General Provisions | Scope, definitions, exemptions, DBT |
| **73.46** | Fixed Site Physical Protection | Performance capability requirements, force-on-force |
| **73.54** | Cyber Security | Protection of critical digital assets from cyber attacks |
| **73.55** | Physical Protection | Comprehensive physical security program for power reactors |
| **73.56** | Access Authorization | Personnel screening, background checks, behavioral observation |
| **73.57** | Requirements for Criminal History Records Checks | FBI fingerprint processing, state criminal records |
| **73.58** | Safety/Security Interface | Coordination between safety and security functions |
| **Appendix B** | General Criteria for Security Personnel | Training standards, tactical response drills, firearms qualification |
| **Appendix C** | Licensee Safeguards Contingency Plans | Response to threats, coordination with law enforcement |
| **Appendix G** | Reportable Safeguards Events | Security-related event notification to NRC Operations Center |

#### Interaction with Other NRC Regulations

**10 CFR Part 26 (Fitness for Duty):**
- Applies to ALL personnel with unescorted access (including security force)
- Drug/alcohol testing, behavioral observation, fatigue management
- Violation results in immediate access revocation

**10 CFR Part 50 (Operating Licenses):**
- Section 184 license transfers (50.80) incorporate physical security program by reference
- Financial qualifications (50.33(f)) must address ability to fund security costs
- Emergency planning (50.47, Appendix E) coordinates with physical security for facility evacuation

**10 CFR Part 2 (Rules of Practice):**
- Safeguards Information protection (2.390) - withholding from public disclosure
- Adjudicatory hearings on license transfers may include closed sessions for classified security information
