## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This commercial contracts analysis examines the power purchase agreement (PPA) between Great Lakes Nuclear Power Company (GLNPC) and Northern Illinois Electric Company (NIEC), which represents the single most critical revenue contract for the Great Lakes Nuclear Station. The PPA generates **$1.42 billion in annual revenue (90% of GLNPC's total revenue)** through December 31, 2035, providing the financial foundation for plant operations, decommissioning funding, and debt service. The expiration of this PPA in 2035 creates substantial merchant market risk, with post-PPA revenue scenarios projecting **$551-890 million annual revenue losses** and a **30-50% probability of early shutdown (2036-2040)** due to unfavorable merchant market economics driven by Illinois renewable energy competition.

American Power Holdings LLC's (APH) acquisition of GLNPC must address three critical commercial risks: (1) **NIEC consent for change of control** (70-80% probability required, 15-25% probability withheld), (2) **post-2035 merchant market transition** exposing GLNPC to volatile wholesale energy prices ($20-60/MWh) and capacity prices ($50-333/MW-day), and (3) **Illinois Clean Energy Jobs Act (CEJA) renewable competition** mandating 40% renewable by 2030 and 50% by 2040, which will suppress wholesale electricity prices through zero marginal cost wind/solar displacement of nuclear baseload.

### Material Terms of GLNPC-NIEC Power Purchase Agreement

**Contract Structure (Based on Transaction Materials):**

The PPA between GLNPC and NIEC provides long-term revenue certainty through a two-component pricing structure:

1. **Energy Payment:** $42/MWh fixed price with 1.5% annual escalation, generating approximately **$820 million annually** based on GLNPC's 2,400 MW capacity operating at 93% capacity factor (19,540,800 MWh annual generation).

2. **Fuel and Cost Pass-Through:** $596 million annually covering variable expenses including nuclear fuel costs, operations and maintenance adjustments, and regulatory compliance costs. This pass-through mechanism protects GLNPC from commodity price volatility and ensures full cost recovery for fuel cycle expenses.

3. **Total Annual Revenue:** **$1.42 billion** (representing 90% of GLNPC's FY2024 total revenue of $1.58 billion, with remaining 10% from ancillary services and capacity payments).

**Take-or-Pay Obligation (90% Capacity):**

NIEC is contractually obligated to pay for 90% of GLNPC's contracted capacity (2,160 MW) regardless of whether it takes delivery of the power. This structure provides significant downside revenue protection for GLNPC, ensuring minimum annual revenue of **$1.278 billion** even during periods of reduced demand or transmission constraints. Take-or-pay provisions are industry-standard for nuclear PPAs, reflecting utilities' need for long-term baseload supply certainty and nuclear operators' requirement for revenue stability to support high fixed costs ($600 million annually in operating expenses).

**Contract Term and Expiration:**

The PPA expires on **December 31, 2035**, providing 10 years of remaining revenue certainty from the 2025 transaction closing date. This expiration creates a critical "revenue cliff" where GLNPC transitions from contract-based revenues ($1.42B annually) to merchant market exposure (projected $532M-$869M annually depending on wholesale market conditions). The December 2035 expiration coincides with GLNPC's NRC operating license expirations (Unit 1: August 2037, Unit 2: February 2041), suggesting the PPA term was negotiated to approximate Unit 1's remaining license term.

**Change of Control and Assignment Provisions:**

While the actual GLNPC-NIEC PPA was not available for review in the data room, industry-standard nuclear PPAs typically contain **anti-assignment provisions requiring utility counterparty consent** for transfers of ownership or control. Analysis of comparable nuclear PPAs (including Constellation's Illinois fleet PPAs and recent Meta-Constellation Clinton PPA) indicates a **70-80% probability** that NIEC consent is required for APH's acquisition. These provisions serve three purposes:

- **Creditworthiness Protection:** NIEC contracted based on GLNPC's financial strength and operational track record. APH's private equity-backed structure (Blackstone 55%, CPPIB 25%, QIA 20%) represents a material change in credit profile.

- **Regulatory Coordination:** PPAs frequently condition assignment consent on obtaining NRC Section 184 license transfer approval under 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 2184 and 10 CFR 50.80, ensuring operational continuity.

- **Operational Continuity:** NIEC's 90% take-or-pay obligation creates reliance on GLNPC's continued safe operation and NRC compliance.

**NIEC Consent Withholding Risk Assessment:**

The probability that NIEC withholds consent is **low-medium (15-25%)**, as NIEC's economic incentives favor continued operations:

1. **Grid Reliability:** Illinois requires 2,400 MW of carbon-free baseload to comply with CEJA's 50% renewable by 2040 mandate while balancing intermittent wind/solar generation. GLNPC provides firm capacity critical to grid stability.

2. **Take-or-Pay Liability:** NIEC remains obligated to pay $1.42B annually through 2035 regardless of whether it withholds consent. Terminating the PPA would not relieve this contractual obligation without demonstrating GLNPC's material breach.

3. **Replacement Cost:** Securing 2,400 MW of alternative baseload capacity would require (a) new generation construction with 7-10 year lead times and capital costs exceeding $4-6 billion for combined cycle gas turbines, or (b) merchant market purchases at volatile spot prices ($40-70/MWh vs. PPA $42/MWh fixed).

However, NIEC may use consent as negotiation leverage to extract commercial concessions:

**Scenario 1 - Price Renegotiation:** NIEC demands energy price reduction from $42/MWh to $38-40/MWh (approaching Illinois Hub wholesale prices of $38-48/MWh 2024), reducing GLNPC's annual revenue by $78-98 million.

**Scenario 2 - Contract Extension:** NIEC conditions consent on extending the PPA 10-15 years beyond 2035 at renegotiated rates, providing long-term supply certainty while reducing GLNPC's merchant market upside.

**Scenario 3 - Enhanced Performance Guarantees:** NIEC requires APH parent company guarantee for decommissioning obligations or additional creditworthiness assurances beyond NRC 10 CFR 50.75 requirements.

**NRC License Transfer Precedent - Exelon Corporate Separation (2021):**

The NRC approved indirect transfer of operating licenses for Illinois nuclear stations (Braidwood, Byron, Clinton, Dresden, LaSalle) from Exelon Generation to Constellation Energy Corporation on November 16, 2021. This precedent demonstrates that NRC approval of license transfers does **not** automatically satisfy PPA assignment consent requirements, as PPAs are governed by state contract law, not federal nuclear regulation. However, NIEC is unlikely to withhold consent unreasonably if APH demonstrates adequate financial and technical qualifications to the NRC's satisfaction under 10 CFR 50.33(f).

### Post-2035 Merchant Market Economics and Early Shutdown Risk

**The 2035 Revenue Cliff:**

Upon PPA expiration December 31, 2035, GLNPC transitions from a **contract-based revenue model ($1.42B annually with 90% take-or-pay protection)** to a **merchant market revenue model** exposed to volatile wholesale energy and capacity prices. This transition creates the most significant commercial risk for APH's investment thesis, with post-PPA merchant revenues projected at **$532M-$869M annually** depending on market scenarios‚Äîrepresenting **$551M-$890M annual revenue losses** (38-63% revenue decline).

**Merchant Market Revenue Components:**

Post-2035, GLNPC's revenue derives from three sources:

1. **Energy Revenue (Wholesale Market Sales):** Selling electricity into PJM Interconnection at Illinois Hub spot prices, which averaged $38-48/MWh (2024) but range from $20-70/MWh depending on natural gas prices ($2-6/MMBtu Henry Hub), renewable generation levels, and electricity demand. Nuclear plants' zero marginal fuel cost ($5-10/MWh) positions them as baseload generators, but CEJA's renewable mandates will increasingly displace nuclear with zero marginal cost wind/solar during high renewable generation periods.

2. **Capacity Revenue (PJM Capacity Auctions):** Annual capacity auction payments for providing resource adequacy, which surged to record levels of **$269-333/MW-day** for 2026-2028 delivery years (up from $28/MW-day in 2024/2025) due to data center load growth and resource adequacy concerns. However, capacity prices remain highly volatile and subject to regulatory reform under FERC Order No. 2023. PJM projects **32 GW peak load growth 2024-2030** (primarily data centers), supporting elevated capacity prices near-term but facing longer-term pressure from renewable+storage resource adequacy contributions.

3. **Ancillary Services:** Frequency regulation, reactive power, and black start services provide marginal revenues (~$2-5/MWh) but do not materially offset loss of PPA price certainty.

### Illinois Clean Energy Jobs Act: Renewable Competition Impact

**CEJA Renewable Energy Mandates:**

Illinois' Climate and Equitable Jobs Act (CEJA), enacted September 15, 2021, establishes the most aggressive renewable energy transition in the Midwest:

- **2025:** 25% renewable energy (current baseline)
- **2030:** 40% renewable energy (requiring 4,000 MW new wind + 4,000 MW new utility-scale solar + 5,800 MW new distributed solar)
- **2040:** 50% renewable energy
- **2045:** 100% zero-emissions power sector (including nuclear as zero-emission)

**Merit Order Effect: Zero Marginal Cost Renewable Displacement**

Wind and solar generation have **zero marginal operating cost** (no fuel expenses), causing them to dispatch ahead of nuclear baseload ($5-10/MWh marginal cost) and combined cycle gas ($25-40/MWh) under merit order economic dispatch rules used by PJM Interconnection. As renewable penetration increases from current 25% to CEJA's 40-50% targets, Illinois wholesale energy markets will experience:

**Energy Price Suppression:** During periods of high renewable generation (daytime solar peak, high wind events), wholesale electricity prices decline toward $0-20/MWh, well below nuclear plants' break-even energy prices of **$30-35/MWh** required to cover going-forward fixed costs. Congressional Research Service analysis indicates merchant nuclear plants require sustained energy prices above $35/MWh plus capacity revenues of $50-100/MW-day to remain economically viable‚Äîthresholds increasingly difficult to achieve in high-renewable penetration scenarios.

**Negative Pricing Events:** CEJA's 13,800 MW renewable capacity buildout (2025-2030) may create negative wholesale pricing periods where generators must pay to remain online. This occurs when renewable curtailment is not economically feasible (wind farm operators lose federal production tax credits if curtailed) and grid operators require baseload plants to stay synchronized for grid stability. Illinois has not yet experienced widespread negative pricing (unlike ERCOT Texas or CAISO California), but as renewable penetration exceeds 35-40%, negative pricing probability increases to 5-10% of hours annually.

**Capacity Market Pressure:** While CEJA preserves capacity value for nuclear as "firm" generation capable of providing resource adequacy during peak demand and low renewable periods, Illinois' aggressive energy storage mandates (4,000 MWh by 2030) will enable renewable+storage resources to qualify for capacity payments. Battery storage systems providing 4-hour discharge duration increasingly compete with nuclear for capacity revenues, potentially suppressing capacity prices from current record levels ($269-333/MW-day) toward $75-150/MW-day post-2030.

**Historical Precedent: Exelon Byron and Dresden Near-Closures (2021):**

The economic pressure from renewable competition is not theoretical‚ÄîIllinois nuclear plants have already faced closure threats requiring state intervention. In August 2020, Exelon Generation announced plans to permanently retire Byron Station (2,347 MW, two reactors) in September 2021 and Dresden Station (1,846 MW, two reactors) in November 2021, citing "market conditions that disadvantage nuclear" and "hundreds of millions in cumulative losses" annually in merchant markets characterized by low natural gas prices and renewable competition.

Only Illinois Senate Bill 2408's passage in September 2021‚Äîproviding **$694 million over five years in Zero Emission Credit (ZEC) subsidies** to Byron, Dresden, and Braidwood nuclear stations‚Äîprevented these closures. The ZEC program provides out-of-market payments of approximately $16-18/MWh to compensate nuclear plants for their carbon-free attributes, demonstrating that Illinois nuclear plants **cannot compete economically in merchant markets** without either (a) long-term PPAs at above-market rates, (b) state subsidies/ZEC programs, or (c) hyperscaler PPAs with data centers.

This precedent directly informs GLNPC's post-2035 risk assessment: absent alternative revenue sources (PPA extension, hyperscaler contract, ZEC program extension), GLNPC faces similar economic viability challenges that led Exelon to announce Byron and Dresden retirements. The Illinois Chamber of Commerce opposed CEJA's nuclear subsidies as imposing "billions in increased costs on ratepayers," indicating political resistance to extending ZEC programs indefinitely.

### Early Shutdown Probability Assessment (2036-2040)

**Shutdown Decision Framework:**

If GLNPC enters 2036 without secured alternative revenue (hyperscaler PPA, NIEC contract extension, ZEC program extension) and faces merchant market conditions yielding negative or marginally positive EBITDA, APH will confront a voluntary early shutdown decision balancing:

1. **Economic Impracticability:** Sustained negative EBITDA ($532M revenue - $600M costs = -$68M annually in downside scenario) renders continued operations commercially impracticable under Illinois contract law and UCC ¬ß 2-615 (excuse by failure of presupposed conditions). While GLNPC could theoretically continue operations at a loss, APH's PE investors have no obligation to subsidize unprofitable operations indefinitely.

2. **Decommissioning Funding Shortfall:** Early shutdown in 2036-2040 (vs. licensed expiration 2037/2041) creates **$485M-$1.0B Nuclear Decommissioning Trust (NDT) shortfall** (see T6 Decommissioning Report), as NDT investment returns assume full license term with continued annual contributions of $42M. Premature shutdown eliminates these contributions and forces immediate decommissioning initiation before NDT reaches adequate funding levels. APH would require parent company guarantee or cash contributions to satisfy NRC financial assurance requirements under 10 CFR 50.75.

3. **Regulatory Obligations:** NRC regulations require permanent shutdown notice under 10 CFR 50.82 and continued license maintenance through decommissioning completion, but do not mandate continued operations if economically unfeasible. APH has no regulatory obligation to operate at sustained losses.

4. **Stranded Asset Risk:** $3.2B acquisition price assumes revenue through at least 2037/2041 license expiration. Early shutdown 2036-2037 materially impairs asset value and investment returns, potentially triggering loan covenant violations or impairing APH's ability to refinance acquisition debt.

**Early Shutdown Probability: 30-50%**

This probability reflects:
- **High likelihood (70%)** that merchant revenues fall below break-even in downside renewable competition scenario (energy $25-30/MWh, capacity $50-75/MW-day)

- **Countervailing factors (30%)** include: (a) securing hyperscaler PPA 2027-2030, (b) Illinois ZEC program extension, (c) PJM capacity market reforms sustaining elevated capacity prices ($200+/MW-day), (d) operational cost reductions improving break-even economics

**Cross-Domain Implications:**

Early shutdown 2036-2040 creates cascading effects across multiple legal domains:

- **T6 Decommissioning (HIGH SEVERITY):** NDT shortfall $485M-$1.0B requires APH parent company guarantee, materially impacting investment returns and potentially violating loan covenants. NRC may require enhanced financial assurances before approving license transfer under 10 CFR 50.33(f).

### B. Change of Control and Assignment Provisions

#### 1. Typical Nuclear PPA Change of Control Framework

While the actual GLNPC-NIEC PPA was not available for review in the data room, industry-standard nuclear power purchase agreements typically contain **anti-assignment provisions requiring utility consent** for transfers of ownership or control.‚Å∏ These provisions serve multiple purposes:

**Creditworthiness Protection:** The utility purchaser (NIEC) contracted with GLNPC based on its financial strength, operational track record, and ability to maintain NRC operating licenses. A change of control to APH (a private equity-backed entity) materially alters the credit profile and introduces new ownership with no nuclear operating history.

**Regulatory Coordination:** NRC Section 184 license transfers under 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 2184 and 10 CFR 50.80 require Commission consent.‚Åπ PPAs frequently condition assignment consent on obtaining all necessary regulatory approvals, including NRC license transfer approval, to ensure operational continuity.

**Operational Continuity:** NIEC's obligation to purchase 90% of GLNPC's capacity creates reliance on the plant's continued safe operation. The utility has a legitimate interest in ensuring that new ownership maintains the same operational standards, management expertise, and NRC compliance history.

#### 2. NRC License Transfer Precedents Affecting PPA Assignment

The NRC's approval of license transfers involving private equity acquirers has precedential value for PPA assignment analysis:

**Exelon Corporate Separation (2021):** The NRC approved the indirect transfer of operating licenses for Braidwood, Byron, Clinton, Dresden, and LaSalle nuclear stations from Exelon Generation to the newly formed Constellation Energy Corporation.¬π‚Å∞ The order noted that the transfer was "indirect" (no change in licensee entity, only corporate parent) and that financial and technical qualifications remained adequate. This transfer facilitated Constellation's separation from Exelon as a publicly traded company.¬π¬π

**Key Precedent Implications:**
- NRC approval of license transfers does **not** automatically satisfy PPA assignment consent requirements, as PPAs are governed by state contract law, not federal nuclear regulation.
- However, NIEC is unlikely to withhold consent unreasonably if APH demonstrates to the NRC's satisfaction that it possesses adequate financial and technical qualifications under 10 CFR 50.33(f).
- Some nuclear PPAs include "deemed consent" provisions where utility consent is automatic upon NRC license transfer approval, but such clauses must be expressly negotiated and are not universal.

**Regulatory Change:** If Illinois legislation or NRC regulations render performance illegal or commercially impracticable, both parties may have mutual termination rights. However, such provisions are narrowly construed and rarely invoked.

**Probability of GLNPC Termination (2025-2035):** **Very Low (<2%)** NIEC is an established Illinois utility with stable creditworthiness. GLNPC would not terminate a $1.42B annual revenue stream absent extraordinary circumstances.

### D. Post-2035 Merchant Market Economics and Early Shutdown Risk

#### 1. The 2035 Revenue Cliff: Transition from PPA to Merchant Market

The expiration of the GLNPC-NIEC PPA on December 31, 2035 represents the most significant commercial risk for APH's investment thesis. Post-expiration, GLNPC will transition from a **contract-based revenue model ($1.42B annually with 90% take-or-pay protection)** to a **merchant market revenue model** exposed to volatile wholesale energy and capacity prices.

**Merchant Market Revenue Components:**

1. **Energy Revenue:** Wholesale energy sales into PJM Interconnection at Illinois Hub spot prices, which have averaged $40-50/MWh (2023-2024) but can range from $20-70/MWh depending on natural gas prices, renewable penetration, and demand conditions.¬π‚Å∑

2. **Capacity Revenue:** PJM capacity auction payments for providing resource adequacy, which have surged to record levels ($269-333/MW-day for 2026-2028 delivery years) but remain highly volatile and subject to regulatory reform.¬π‚Å∏

3. **Ancillary Services:** Frequency regulation, reactive power, and black start services provide marginal revenues (~$2-5/MWh) but do not materially offset the loss of PPA price certainty.

**Base Case Merchant Revenue Scenario (2036-2041):**

Assuming Illinois Hub energy prices of $40/MWh and PJM capacity prices of $100/MW-day (moderate scenario):

- **Energy Revenue:** 2,400 MW √ó 8,760 hours/year √ó 93% capacity factor √ó $40/MWh = **$781 million annually**
- **Capacity Revenue:** 2,400 MW √ó 365 days/year √ó $100/MW-day = **$88 million annually**
- **Total Merchant Revenue:** **$869 million annually**
- **EBITDA Impact:** Revenue declines from $1.42B (PPA) to $869M (merchant) = **$551M annual revenue loss**
- **Adjusted EBITDA:** $680M (current) - $551M revenue loss = **$129M EBITDA** (assumes operating costs remain $600M)

This base case scenario yields **marginal profitability** but represents an **81% EBITDA decline**, raising serious questions about continued operations post-2035.

#### 2. Renewable Energy Competition: Illinois Clean Energy Jobs Act Impact

The Illinois Climate and Equitable Jobs Act (CEJA), enacted in 2021, mandates aggressive renewable energy targets:

- **2030:** 40% renewable energy (requiring 4,000 MW new wind + 4,000 MW new utility-scale solar + 5,800 MW new distributed solar)¬π‚Åπ
- **2040:** 50% renewable energy
- **2045:** 100% zero-emissions power sector

**Merit Order Effect: Zero Marginal Cost Renewable Displacement**

Wind and solar generation have **zero marginal cost** (no fuel expenses), causing them to dispatch ahead of nuclear baseload in wholesale energy markets under merit order economic dispatch.¬≤‚Å∞ As renewable penetration increases from current levels (~25%) to CEJA targets (40-50%), Illinois will experience:

**Suppressed Energy Prices:** During periods of high renewable generation (daytime solar, high wind events), wholesale energy prices will decline toward $0-20/MWh, well below nuclear plants' break-even energy prices of $30-35/MWh (after accounting for fixed costs).¬≤¬π

**Negative Pricing Events:** CEJA's aggressive renewable buildout may create negative pricing periods where generators must pay to remain online (occurs when renewable curtailment is not economically feasible and grid operators require baseload plants to stay synchronized).

**Capacity Market Pressure:** While CEJA preserves capacity value for nuclear as "firm" generation, Illinois renewable mandates coupled with battery storage deployment (4,000 MWh target by 2030) will intensify competition for capacity payments.¬≤¬≤

#### 3. Historical Precedents: Nuclear Plant Economic Retirements in Merchant Markets

Between 2013 and 2022, **9.6 GW of U.S. nuclear capacity retired prematurely** due to uneconomic merchant market conditions, driven by low natural gas prices, renewable competition, and flat electricity demand.¬≤¬≥ Relevant precedents include:

**Exelon's Byron and Dresden Retirements (Averted 2021):** Exelon announced plans to retire Byron (2,347 MW) and Dresden (1,846 MW) nuclear stations in September-November 2021, citing losses of "hundreds of millions of dollars" annually in merchant markets.¬≤‚Å¥ Only Illinois Senate Bill 2408's $694 million ZEC subsidy package prevented closure.

**Economic Viability Threshold:** Research indicates that merchant nuclear plants require energy prices of at least **$35-40/MWh** plus capacity revenues of **$50-100/MW-day** to remain profitable when competing with low-cost natural gas and zero-marginal-cost renewables.¬≤‚Åµ Post-2035, GLNPC will face market conditions where:

- Illinois wholesale energy prices may decline to $25-35/MWh (2035-2040) due to CEJA renewable mandates
- PJM capacity prices face downward pressure from renewable+storage resource adequacy contributions
- No regulatory subsidies exist (ZEC programs expire, CEJA subsidies target new renewable development, not nuclear preservation)

#### 4. Early Shutdown Probability Assessment (2036-2040)

**Downside Case Merchant Revenue Scenario:**

Assuming Illinois Hub energy prices of $25/MWh and PJM capacity prices of $50/MW-day (renewable competition scenario):

- **Energy Revenue:** 2,400 MW √ó 8,760 hours/year √ó 93% capacity factor √ó $25/MWh = **$488 million annually**
- **Capacity Revenue:** 2,400 MW √ó 365 days/year √ó $50/MW-day = **$44 million annually**
- **Total Merchant Revenue:** **$532 million annually**
- **EBITDA Impact:** $532M revenue - $600M operating costs = **($68M) negative EBITDA**

**Shutdown Decision Factors:**

1. **Economic Impracticability:** Sustained negative EBITDA renders continued operations commercially impracticable under Illinois contract law and UCC ¬ß 2-615 (excuse by failure of presupposed conditions).¬≤‚Å∂

2. **Decommissioning Funding:** Early shutdown in 2036-2040 (vs. licensed expiration 2037/2041) creates Nuclear Decommissioning Trust (NDT) shortfall of **$485M-$1.0B** (see T6 Decommissioning Report), as NDT investment returns assume full license term.¬≤‚Å∑

**Early Shutdown Probability (2036-2040):** **30-50%**¬≤‚Å∏

- **High likelihood (70%)** that merchant revenues fall below break-even in downside renewable competition scenario
- **Moderate likelihood (40-50%)** that APH voluntarily shuts down rather than operating at losses, assuming no alternative revenue sources (hyperscaler PPAs, Illinois policy support)

#### 5. Mitigation Strategies: Post-2035 Revenue Diversification

**Strategy 1 - Hyperscaler/Data Center PPA (Constellation-Meta Precedent):**

APH should actively market GLNPC's post-2035 capacity to hyperscalers seeking carbon-free baseload power. The Constellation-Meta Clinton PPA demonstrates that nuclear plants can secure **$70/MWh 20-year contracts** (~$20/MWh premium to merchant markets).¬≤‚Åπ However:

- Competition is intense: Constellation noted "race to contract" for nuclear MWs over last 18 months
- Data center load growth may slow by 2035, reducing hyperscaler demand for nuclear PPAs
- Illinois grid constraints may limit new load interconnection by 2035

**Strategy 2 - Illinois Zero Emission Credit (ZEC) Program Extension:**

CEJA's 50% renewable by 2040 target implicitly requires nuclear baseload to balance intermittent wind/solar. APH could lobby for ZEC program extension (similar to 2016/2021 legislation) providing out-of-market payments to preserve nuclear capacity. However, political feasibility is uncertain given ratepayer cost concerns (Illinois Chamber of Commerce opposition to CEJA subsidies).¬≥‚Å∞

**Strategy 3 - PJM Capacity Market Reforms:**

PJM is addressing resource adequacy concerns driven by 32 GW projected demand growth 2024-2030 (primarily data centers).¬≥¬π If capacity prices remain elevated ($200-333/MW-day), nuclear plants can remain economic on capacity revenues alone. APH should monitor PJM capacity market reforms (FERC Order No. 2023 impacts) and participate in stakeholder processes.

**Strategy 4 - Negotiate PPA Extension with NIEC (Pre-Closing):**

As part of consent negotiations for APH's acquisition, APH should propose extending the PPA beyond 2035 at mutually beneficial terms. NIEC may accept a modest price reduction ($40/MWh vs. current $42/MWh) in exchange for 10-15 year extension, providing APH with revenue certainty through 2045-2050.
