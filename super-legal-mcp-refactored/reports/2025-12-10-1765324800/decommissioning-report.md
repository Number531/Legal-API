### E. NRC Parent Guarantee Requirements for License Transfer

#### 1. Regulatory Framework (10 CFR 50.33(f) Financial Qualifications)

The NRC requires license transfer applicants to demonstrate financial qualifications to operate the facility and provide decommissioning funding assurance. For non-utility purchasers (such as APH, a private equity-backed entity), the NRC has historically required **enhanced financial assurance mechanisms** beyond trust funds alone.

**10 CFR 50.33(f) Requirements:**
- Demonstrate reasonable assurance of obtaining funds to cover **estimated operation costs for license period**
- Submit estimates for total annual operating costs (first 5 years)
- For decommissioning, demonstrate **reasonable assurance funds available** per 10 CFR 50.75
- NRC Standard Review Plan (NUREG-1577 Rev. 1) evaluates based on cash flow projections

**Parent Company Guarantee Option (10 CFR Part 30, Appendix A):**
Licensees may provide decommissioning funding assurance through parent company guarantee if parent passes financial tests:
- Bond rating BBB/Baa or higher (or alternative net worth test)
- **Tangible net worth ≥ 6× decommissioning funds assured**
- U.S. assets ≥ 90% of total assets OR ≥ 6× decommissioning cost estimates
- Standby trust established before guarantee submitted

### F. SAFSTOR Deferred Decommissioning - Risk Mitigation Strategy

#### 1. Regulatory Framework (10 CFR 50.82)

The NRC permits three decommissioning methods: **DECON** (immediate dismantling), **SAFSTOR** (deferred dismantling), and **ENTOMB** (encasement). SAFSTOR is the preferred option for nuclear plants with inadequate decommissioning funding at shutdown.

**SAFSTOR Process:**
- Facility placed in safe storage and monitored, allowing radioactive decay for up to **50 years**
- Followed by up to **10 years** for final decontamination and dismantling (total 60-year maximum per 10 CFR 50.82(a)(3))
- Security force reduced from $28M annually (operating) to <$10M annually (SAFSTOR)
- Spent fuel management continues ($8M-$12M annually for dry cask storage)

**Cost Comparison (PWRs):**
- **DECON:** Lower overall cost ($893M Haddam Neck 2007 precedent); requires immediate funding; 7-10 year timeline
- **SAFSTOR:** Higher overall cost (~$1.0B-$1.2B Kewaunee 2013 precedent); defers expenditures 50+ years; allows trust growth

#### 2. Financial Rationale for Premature Shutdown

Despite higher overall costs, SAFSTOR offers compelling financial advantages when decommissioning trusts are inadequate:

**Trust Growth Opportunity:**
- Vermont Yankee closed 2014 with $665M trust against $1.24B needed ($575M shortfall)
- Elected SAFSTOR to defer decommissioning until trust grows adequately through investment returns
- $665M at 4.5% real return for 50 years = **$5.0B** (sufficient to cover SAFSTOR costs ~$1.5B in future dollars)

**APH Parent Guarantee NPV Reduction:**
- **DECON:** APH provides $480M-$1.0B immediate cash infusion
- **SAFSTOR:** APH guarantee obligation deferred 50+ years; discounted at 5% = NPV **$100M-$300M** (80-70% reduction)

**Regulatory Precedent:**
All recent premature closures elected SAFSTOR due to inadequate funding:
- Vermont Yankee (2014): 53.6% funded; elected SAFSTOR
- Crystal River 3 (2013): Elected SAFSTOR with 60-year plan; trust expected to grow to cover costs
- Kewaunee (2013): Elected SAFSTOR; completion 2073
- San Onofre Units 2 & 3 (2013): Elected SAFSTOR

**NRC Approval:** The NRC routinely approves SAFSTOR when licensees demonstrate insufficient immediate DECON funding, provided Post-Shutdown Decommissioning Activities Report (PSDAR) submitted within 2 years of shutdown per 10 CFR 50.82(a)(4).

**Recommendation:** If GLNS premature shutdown occurs, **immediately elect SAFSTOR** to eliminate APH's $480M-$1.0B immediate parent guarantee cash call obligation.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. NRC Decommissioning Funding Regulatory Framework

#### 1. Statutory and Regulatory Authority

The Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) establishes comprehensive requirements for how nuclear power reactor licensees provide reasonable assurance that funds will be available for decommissioning through 10 CFR 50.75, "Reporting and recordkeeping for decommissioning planning."¹

**10 CFR 50.75(c)** prescribes the formula for calculating the minimum decommissioning amount. The NRC's formulas include only those decommissioning costs incurred by licensees to remove a facility or site safely from service and reduce residual radioactivity to levels that permit: (1) release of the property for unrestricted use and termination of the license; or (2) release of the property under restricted conditions and termination of the license.² The cost of dismantling or demolishing non-radiological systems and structures is not included in the NRC decommissioning cost estimates.³

**Key Regulatory Components:**
- **Minimum Amount Calculation:** Based on formulas with coefficients periodically updated by NRC in NUREG-1307
- **Biennial Reporting:** Licensees must report decommissioning funding status at least once every 2 years by March 31⁴
- **Financial Assurance Methods:** Trust funds, parent guarantees, surety bonds, insurance, or prepayment⁵
- **Inflation Adjustments:** Cost estimates must account for labor costs, energy costs, and waste burial cost escalation⁶

#### 2. NUREG-1307 Cost Estimation Guidance

The NRC periodically updates NUREG-1307, "Report on Waste Burial Charges: Changes in Decommissioning Waste Disposal Costs at Low-Level Waste Burial Facilities," which provides burial cost escalation factors for calculating minimum decommissioning fund requirements.⁷

**Most Recent Update - NUREG-1307 Revision 20 (February 2025):**
- Provides burial cost escalation factors updated to year 2024 for reference PWR and BWR plants⁸
- Based on revised LLW burial factors, minimum decommissioning fund formula amounts "will likely reflect minimum decommissioning fund requirements that are lower, on average, when compared to those previously reported by licensees in 2023"⁹
- Original reference estimates developed in 1986 for pressurized water reactors (PWRs) and boiling water reactors (BWRs)¹⁰

**NRC Estimated Range:** The NRC estimates costs for decommissioning a nuclear power plant range from **$280 million to $612 million** in baseline terms.¹¹ For the transaction context, Great Lakes Nuclear Station operates two PWR units, suggesting an aggregate NRC minimum in the range of **$560 million to $1.24 billion** (doubling the per-reactor baseline).

#### 3. Premature Shutdown Precedents - Decommissioning Trust Shortfalls

Historical precedents demonstrate significant decommissioning funding shortfalls when nuclear plants shut down prematurely before accumulating adequate trust balances.

**Vermont Yankee (Closed 2014):**
- **Estimated Decommissioning Cost:** $1.24 billion
- **Trust Fund Balance at Closure:** $665 million (only 53.6% of required amount)
- **Shortfall:** $575 million (46.4% underfunded)

### C. Decommissioning Alternatives - DECON vs. SAFSTOR

#### 1. Regulatory Framework (10 CFR 50.82)

The NRC recognizes three decommissioning methods, with **DECON** and **SAFSTOR** being the primary options for power reactors:²⁶

**DECON (Immediate Dismantling):**
- Equipment, structures, and contaminated portions removed or decontaminated soon after facility closure
- Permits release of property and license termination within approximately **7-10 years**
- Lower cumulative cost than SAFSTOR for PWRs due to reduced maintenance/surveillance over decades
- Higher initial expenditure requirement

**SAFSTOR (Deferred Dismantling):**
- Facility maintained and monitored in safe condition allowing radioactivity to decay
- Decommissioning deferred up to **50 years**, followed by up to **10 years** for decontamination (total 60-year maximum per 10 CFR 50.82(a)(3))²⁷
- **Higher overall cost** than DECON for PWRs due to cumulative maintenance, surveillance, and security costs over decades²⁸
- **Lowest occupational radiation dose** for workers (radioactive decay reduces exposure)²⁹
- Allows NDT to grow through continued investment returns and contributions before major decommissioning expenditures

**ENTOMB:**
- Radioactive structures encased in concrete or other structural materials
- Rarely used for power reactors; NRC has not approved ENTOMB for any commercial nuclear plant

#### 2. Cost Comparison - DECON vs. SAFSTOR

**PWR Decommissioning Cost Hierarchy:** ENTOMB (lowest) < DECON (middle) < SAFSTOR (highest)³⁰

**Real-World Examples:**

**DECON Case Study - Haddam Neck (619 MW PWR, Connecticut):**
- Shut down: 1997
- Decommissioning method: DECON
- Completion: 2007 (10 years)
- **Total Cost: $893 million** (2007 dollars)³¹

**SAFSTOR Case Study - Kewaunee (556 MW PWR, Wisconsin):**
- Shut down: 2013
- Decommissioning method: SAFSTOR
- Expected completion: 2073 (60 years)
- **Total Cost: Nearly $1 billion** (2013 dollars)³²

**SAFSTOR Case Study - Crystal River 3 (860 MW PWR, Florida):**
- Shut down: 2013
- Expected decommissioning method: SAFSTOR over 60 years
- **Total Cost: $1.18 billion** (2013 dollars), with trust fund expected to fully cover costs through investment growth during storage period³³

**Key Cost Drivers for SAFSTOR:**
- Decades of security force maintenance ($28M annually for GLNS's 180 armed officers)
- Annual surveillance and monitoring costs
- Radiological environmental monitoring programs
- Spent fuel management during storage period
- Higher overall costs partially offset by radioactive decay (reduces disposal volumes and worker radiation exposure)

#### 3. SAFSTOR Strategic Rationale for Premature Shutdown

Despite higher overall costs, SAFSTOR offers critical advantages when decommissioning trust funds are inadequate at shutdown:³⁴

**Financial Benefits:**
- **Defers major expenditures** 40-60 years, allowing NDT to continue growing through investment returns
- **Permits continued annual contributions** if revenue source remains (GLNS's PPA continues through 2035 even if reactors shut)
- **Reduces net present value** of decommissioning costs (dollars spent decades in future worth less in today's terms)
- **Mitigates immediate funding shortfalls** (Vermont Yankee closed with $665M against $1.24B needed; elected SAFSTOR to avoid immediate $575M cash infusion)³⁵

**Regulatory Precedent:**
All recent premature closures (Vermont Yankee, Crystal River 3, Kewaunee, San Onofre) elected SAFSTOR due to decommissioning funding inadequacy.³⁶ The NRC routinely approves SAFSTOR when licensees demonstrate insufficient immediate funding for DECON.

**APH Transaction Implication:** If GLNS units shut prematurely (2030 NPDES denial scenario or 2036-2040 post-PPA merchant economics), SAFSTOR election would be financially prudent to allow NDT to grow and defer APH's parent guarantee funding obligation.
