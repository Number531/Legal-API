### Nuclear Waste Policy Act Framework and DOE Breach

The Nuclear Waste Policy Act of 1982 established a comprehensive federal program for the disposal of high-level radioactive waste and spent nuclear fuel (SNF). Under 42 U.S.C. § 10222, Congress directed DOE to execute Standard Contracts with commercial nuclear utilities requiring DOE to "take title to" and accept delivery of SNF at a permanent geological repository. The statute imposed an **unconditional obligation** on DOE to begin accepting SNF by January 31, 1998.¹

DOE's failure to meet this statutory deadline constitutes a **material breach of contract** affecting every commercial nuclear reactor in the United States. The planned Yucca Mountain repository in Nevada was never completed due to political opposition, and DOE has repeatedly failed to develop alternative disposal solutions. As of 2024, DOE has no operational repository and no near-term prospects for fulfilling its statutory obligations.²

This breach forces utilities to maintain spent fuel on-site in dry cask storage systems at Independent Spent Fuel Storage Installations (ISFSIs), incurring substantial costs that Congress intended DOE to bear through the Nuclear Waste Fund (funded by utility payments of 1 mil per kilowatt-hour). The U.S. Court of Federal Claims has consistently held DOE liable for these incremental storage costs under a "partial breach" theory—utilities can sue for damages in sequential phases while the Standard Contract remains in effect for future performance.³

**DOE's Total Liability**: As of September 2024, DOE's estimated total liability for spent fuel breach of contract claims ranges from **$37.6 billion to $44.5 billion**, representing a 10% increase from the prior year's estimate of $34.1-$41 billion.⁴ Through September 2021, DOE had already paid approximately **$9 billion** in judgments and settlements from the U.S. Treasury's Judgment Fund.⁵ At least **78 breach of contract claims** have been filed by utilities since 1998, with litigation ongoing in multiple phases across dozens of nuclear facilities.⁶

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. Nuclear Waste Policy Act of 1982: Legislative Framework

Congress enacted the Nuclear Waste Policy Act (NWPA), Pub. L. No. 97-425, 96 Stat. 2201 (1983) (codified as amended at 42 U.S.C. §§ 10101-10270), to establish a comprehensive national program for the safe disposal of high-level radioactive waste and spent nuclear fuel from commercial nuclear power plants. The Act reflected Congress's determination that the federal government, not individual utilities, should bear ultimate responsibility for permanent disposal of spent nuclear fuel.¹³

**Key Statutory Provisions:**

**42 U.S.C. § 10222 - Contracts**: This section directs the Secretary of Energy to "enter into contracts" with owners and generators of spent nuclear fuel for DOE to "take title to" and "dispose of" such fuel. The statute provides:

> The Secretary shall enter into contracts ... for the acceptance of title, transportation, and disposal of spent nuclear fuel ... Such contracts shall provide for payment to the Secretary of fees determined in accordance with paragraphs (2) and (3) ... [and] shall require the commencement of such transportation and disposal services by January 31, 1998.¹⁴

**Unconditional Obligation**: The Supreme Court and Court of Federal Claims have consistently interpreted § 10222 as imposing an **unconditional obligation** on DOE to begin accepting spent fuel by January 31, 1998. In *Indiana Michigan Power Co. v. United States*, 422 F.3d 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2005), the Federal Circuit held that the "shall require commencement" language creates a statutory deadline that DOE cannot unilaterally extend or excuse based on budgetary constraints or political opposition to repository siting.¹⁵

**Standard Contract Form**: Pursuant to § 10222(a), DOE promulgated a Standard Contract for spent fuel disposal, codified at 10 C.F.R. § 961.11 (1983). The Standard Contract obligates DOE to:
- Accept title to spent fuel at the reactor site
- Transport fuel to a DOE facility
- Store and dispose of fuel at a permanent geological repository
- Commence services by January 31, 1998

In exchange, utilities pay:
- One-time fee for fuel generated before April 7, 1983 (from reactor operating revenues)
- Ongoing fee of 1.0 mil (one-tenth of one cent) per kilowatt-hour for fuel generated after April 7, 1983

As of 2024, the Nuclear Waste Fund has collected over **$40 billion** from utilities since 1983, with no operational repository delivered.¹⁶

### B. DOE's Breach: Yucca Mountain Failure

DOE's obligation to accept spent fuel by January 31, 1998 depended on constructing a permanent geological repository. Congress designated Yucca Mountain, Nevada as the sole repository site in 1987 through amendments to the NWPA. However, DOE failed to meet the statutory deadline due to:

1. **Technical and Regulatory Delays** (1987-2008): DOE did not submit a license application to the Nuclear Regulatory Commission until June 2008—more than 10 years after the statutory deadline. The NRC licensing process was expected to take an additional 3-4 years minimum.

2. **Political Opposition** (2008-2011): The Obama Administration, responding to Nevada political concerns, effectively terminated the Yucca Mountain project:
   - Withdrew the NRC license application in March 2010
   - Eliminated funding for the project in FY 2011 budget
   - Established the Blue Ribbon Commission on America's Nuclear Future to explore alternatives

3. **Ongoing Stalemate** (2011-Present): Despite multiple federal court decisions ordering DOE to resume NRC licensing proceedings, no progress has occurred due to lack of Congressional appropriations. The Yucca Mountain site remains undeveloped, and DOE has **no alternative repository plan** with any realistic prospect of completion before 2040, if ever.¹⁷

**Result**: As of January 2026, DOE is **28 years overdue** in fulfilling its statutory obligation. Every commercial nuclear reactor in the United States continues to store spent fuel on-site, with no prospect of DOE acceptance in the foreseeable future.

### C. Court of Federal Claims Jurisdiction and "Partial Breach" Doctrine

Utilities injured by DOE's breach sue in the **U.S. Court of Federal Claims** under the Tucker Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1491, which grants jurisdiction over contract claims against the United States exceeding $10,000. The court has developed a "partial breach" doctrine specific to the unique circumstances of DOE's ongoing failure to perform under the Standard Contract.¹⁸

**Partial Breach Theory**: The Standard Contract remains in effect—it is not terminated or repudiated. DOE continues to owe performance (accepting spent fuel), and utilities continue to pay into the Nuclear Waste Fund. However, DOE's delay in commencing services constitutes a **partial breach**, entitling utilities to damages for costs incurred during the delay period while preserving their rights to future performance.¹⁹

**Phased Litigation Structure**: Because DOE's breach is ongoing and indefinite in duration, courts permit utilities to litigate damages in sequential phases:
- **Phase I**: Establishes liability (DOE breached) and damages methodology; covers initial cost period (e.g., 1998-2010)
- **Phase II**: Applies established methodology to subsequent period (e.g., 2011-2025); liability not relitigated
- **Phase III and beyond**: Continue until DOE performs or utilities cease operations

This phased approach balances judicial economy (avoiding repetitive liability trials) with the practical reality that future costs cannot be precisely calculated decades in advance (cask technology changes, NRC regulations evolve, inflation varies).²⁰

**Statute of Limitations**: Each cost incurrence creates a new cause of action accruing when the cost is paid. The six-year statute of limitations under 28 U.S.C. § 2501 runs separately for each year's costs, enabling utilities to file new phases indefinitely.²¹

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Legal Framework: Binding Precedent Under 42 U.S.C. § 10222

The legal foundation for GLNPC's Phase II claim rests on three pillars of binding authority:

**1. Statutory Text** (42 U.S.C. § 10222)

The NWPA unambiguously requires DOE to "commence" spent fuel acceptance services "by January 31, 1998." Courts have uniformly rejected DOE's attempts to read discretion, contingencies, or conditions into this deadline:

- **No Force Majeure Defense**: DOE cannot excuse performance based on political opposition to Yucca Mountain, lack of Congressional appropriations, or regulatory delays. The statute imposes an **unconditional obligation** regardless of feasibility.²⁹

- **No Substantial Performance Defense**: DOE's payment of $9 billion in judgments to date does not constitute "substantial performance" excusing the underlying breach. Each utility continues to incur costs, and each cost incurrence creates a new cause of action.³⁰

- **No Mitigation Required**: Utilities need not delay cask purchases, defer decommissioning, or take other actions to minimize damages. The incremental cost methodology presumes utilities will manage spent fuel prudently per NRC regulations; DOE pays the full cost of compliant storage.³¹

**2. Federal Circuit Precedent** (*Indiana Michigan Power Co.* and Progeny)

*Indiana Michigan Power Co. v. United States*, 422 F.3d 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2005), established the controlling legal standard for DOE breach litigation. The Federal Circuit held:

> The Government breached the contracts by failing to begin disposing of the SNF by January 31, 1998 ... The delay constitutes a partial breach, entitling the utilities to recover damages for that partial breach ... The contracts remain in effect, and the utilities may recover damages for costs incurred during any particular period of delay.³²

**Key Holdings**:
- **Liability Per Se**: DOE's failure to commence services by January 31, 1998 constitutes breach as a matter of law, requiring no further proof
- **Partial Breach Doctrine**: Utilities recover damages for delay period while preserving right to future performance
- **Incremental Cost Measure**: Damages equal costs utilities would not have incurred but for the breach
- **No Offsetting Benefits**: DOE cannot reduce damages by pointing to benefits utilities gained from delay (e.g., extended revenue operations)

The Federal Circuit's *Indiana Michigan* decision binds the Court of Federal Claims in all subsequent DOE breach cases. GLNPC's Phase I judgment applied *Indiana Michigan* principles; Phase II applies the same established law.³³

**3. Court of Federal Claims Precedent** (Uniform Application Across Utilities)

The Court of Federal Claims has decided dozens of DOE breach cases since 2005, developing a consistent body of precedent on quantum issues:

**Recoverable Cost Categories** (uniformly allowed):
- Dry cask storage system acquisition and deployment
- ISFSI design, construction, licensing, expansion
- NRC licensing fees and compliance costs
- Security personnel and systems (post-9/11 enhancements)
- Radiation monitoring and environmental surveillance
- Project management and engineering oversight
- Decommissioning cost escalation attributable to extended storage
- Insurance premium increases
- Expert witness fees for litigation

**Non-Recoverable Costs** (uniformly excluded):
- Costs that would have been incurred regardless of DOE's breach (e.g., spent fuel pool maintenance during initial cooling period)
- Costs inadequately documented or lacking expert support
- Speculative future costs not yet incurred
- General and administrative overhead not traceable to ISFSI operations

**Burden of Proof**: Utilities must demonstrate by a preponderance of evidence that each claimed cost (1) was actually incurred, (2) is incremental to the but-for baseline, and (3) is reasonable in amount. Courts accept expert testimony from utility engineers and accountants, giving substantial deference to utility cost allocation methodologies where reasonable.³⁴

GLNPC satisfied this burden in Phase I, establishing the evidentiary foundation for Phase II. DOE's challenges in Phase II will focus on specific cost items, not fundamental methodology—a much weaker litigation position.

### C. Damages Methodology: Incremental Cost Approach

The Court of Federal Claims has consistently applied the **incremental cost methodology** in DOE breach litigation, derived from standard contract damages principles articulated in *Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 347* (expectation damages):

> The injured party has a right to damages based on his expectation interest as measured by (a) the loss in the value of the other party's performance caused by its failure or deficiency, plus (b) any other loss, including incidental or consequential loss, caused by the breach, less (c) any cost or other loss that he has avoided by not having to perform.³⁵

**Application to DOE Breach**:

**Loss in Value** = Costs utilities incurred for on-site storage that DOE's timely performance would have avoided
**Other Loss** = Decommissioning cost escalation, insurance premium increases, NRC licensing fees
**Less: Avoided Costs** = None (utilities continue paying into Nuclear Waste Fund; no offsetting benefits from delay)

**"But-For" Baseline**: The methodology compares actual costs against a hypothetical baseline: What would GLNPC's spent fuel costs have been if DOE accepted fuel by January 31, 1998?

**Baseline Scenario** (DOE performs):
- Spent fuel transferred to DOE at reactor site in 1998 and subsequent years
- Minimal on-site storage (only spent fuel pool for initial cooling, typically 5-10 years)
- No dry cask storage systems required
- No ISFSI construction, licensing, or operations
- No long-term security/monitoring costs
- Decommissioning proceeds on standard timeline without extended ISFSI operations

**Actual Scenario** (DOE breaches):
- All spent fuel remains on-site indefinitely
- Dry cask storage required after spent fuel pool reaches capacity
- ISFSI construction, NRC licensing, and ongoing operations
- Extended security/monitoring through decommissioning and beyond
- Decommissioning delayed until DOE accepts fuel (or proceeds with ISFSI in place)

**Incremental Costs** = Actual Scenario minus Baseline Scenario

Courts accept this methodology because it directly measures the financial harm caused by DOE's breach, consistent with fundamental contract remedies principles. GLNPC's Phase I judgment validated this approach for its specific cost structure; Phase II applies identical methodology to a subsequent time period.³⁶

**Expert Testimony**: Utilities typically present two categories of expert witnesses:

1. **Engineering Experts**: Testify that dry cask storage and ISFSI operations are necessary, prudent, and consistent with NRC requirements; explain technical specifications and operational requirements

2. **Accounting/Damages Experts**: Allocate costs between incremental (recoverable) and baseline (non-recoverable) categories; validate that claimed costs were actually incurred and properly documented

DOE's counter-experts challenge specific cost allocations but rarely dispute the fundamental methodology. GLNPC's Phase I success demonstrates its experts' credibility and DOE's inability to undermine the incremental cost framework.³⁷
