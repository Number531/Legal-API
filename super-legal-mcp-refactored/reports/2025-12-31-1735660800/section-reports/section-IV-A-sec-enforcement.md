### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Securities Act Registration Requirements

The Securities Act of 1933 requires registration of all securities offerings unless a specific exemption applies.¹ Section 5 of the Securities Act makes it unlawful to offer or sell securities in interstate commerce without an effective registration statement filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission.² This prohibition extends to both initial offerings and secondary market transactions where the issuer or underwriter is involved.³

The definition of "security" under Section 2(a)(1) of the Securities Act includes "any note, stock, treasury stock, security future, security-based swap, bond, debenture... investment contract... or any instrument commonly known as a 'security.'"⁴ The term "investment contract" has been interpreted broadly by the Supreme Court to encompass novel financial instruments that exhibit the economic characteristics of traditional securities, regardless of their formal designation.⁵

#### 2. The Howey Test: Investment Contracts Under Federal Securities Law

In *SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.*, the Supreme Court established the definitive test for determining whether a transaction constitutes an "investment contract" subject to federal securities regulation.⁶ The Court held that an investment contract exists when there is:

> "a contract, transaction or scheme whereby a person invests his money in a common enterprise and is led to expect profits solely from the efforts of the promoter or a third party."⁷

Courts have refined this holding into a four-prong test:

**Prong 1: Investment of Money**
Satisfied when a person exchanges value—money, cryptocurrency, or other consideration—for the asset in question.⁸ The Supreme Court has clarified that "investment of money" need not be limited to cash; the commitment of assets or capital suffices.⁹

**Prong 2: Common Enterprise**
Typically demonstrated through "horizontal commonality," where investors' fortunes are pooled together and tied to the success of the overall enterprise.¹⁰ Some circuits also recognize "vertical commonality," where an investor's returns depend on the promoter's expertise and efforts.¹¹

**Prong 3: Reasonable Expectation of Profits**
Investors must have a reasonable expectation of profits, returns, or other financial benefits.¹² Profits may include capital appreciation, dividends, distributions, or other monetary returns.¹³ Marketing materials promising yields, appreciation, or investment returns constitute strong evidence of this element.¹⁴

**Prong 4: Efforts of Others**
Originally stated as "solely from the efforts of others," courts now interpret this prong as requiring that profits derive "predominantly" from the efforts of a promoter or third party, rather than from the investor's own efforts.¹⁵ The critical inquiry is whether the investor is passive or actively involved in generating returns.¹⁶

The Howey test applies a substance-over-form analysis: "form [is] disregarded for substance and the emphasis [is] on economic reality."¹⁷ Courts examine the economic realities of the transaction, not merely the labels or legal structures chosen by the parties.¹⁸

#### 3. SEC Enforcement Authority and Process

The SEC possesses broad enforcement authority under both the Securities Act and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934.¹⁹ When the SEC determines that securities laws have been violated, it may pursue enforcement through administrative proceedings (under Section 8A of the Securities Act) or federal court actions (under Section 20(b) of the Securities Act).²⁰

**Wells Notice Procedure:**
The Wells Notice process provides a procedural safeguard before the SEC files formal charges.²¹ Named after the Wells Committee's 1972 recommendations, this process requires the SEC's Division of Enforcement to notify subjects of investigations that staff intends to recommend enforcement action.²² The Wells Notice identifies the specific violations alleged and provides the recipient an opportunity to submit a written response (typically within 30 days) explaining why charges should not be brought.²³

Approximately 23% of Wells Notices result in no action, 31% result in settlement before charges are filed, and 46% result in enforcement actions.²⁴ The "golden window" for settlement negotiation occurs after submitting the Wells Response but before charges are filed; 60% of all settlements occur during this period.²⁵

**Disgorgement Authority:**
Disgorgement—the SEC's power to require defendants to surrender ill-gotten gains—is subject to significant Supreme Court limitations following *Kokesh v. SEC* and *Liu v. SEC*.

In *Kokesh v. SEC*, the Supreme Court held that SEC disgorgement constitutes a "penalty" under 28 U.S.C. § 2462, subjecting it to a five-year statute of limitations.²⁶ This means the SEC can only seek disgorgement for ill-gotten gains obtained within five years of filing the enforcement action.²⁷

In *Liu v. SEC*, the Supreme Court held (8-1) that the SEC may continue to obtain disgorgement but with critical limitations:²⁸

1. **Net Profits Requirement**: Disgorgement cannot exceed a defendant's net profits—legitimate business expenses must be deducted.²⁹
2. **Victim Return Requirement**: The SEC is "generally required" to return disgorgement to victims rather than the Treasury.³⁰
3. **Equitable Remedy**: Disgorgement must meet traditional equitable standards and cannot be punitive.³¹

The *Liu* decision fundamentally constrains the SEC's ability to seek gross revenue as disgorgement. Defendants may now demand expense deductions for operating costs, personnel, infrastructure, compliance, and other legitimate business expenses directly related to the violative conduct.³²

**Civil Penalties:**
The Securities Act authorizes civil penalties under Section 20(d), structured in three tiers:³³

| Tier | Natural Person | Entity | Criteria |
|------|----------------|--------|----------|
| **Tier 1** | $11,823/violation | $118,225/violation | Any securities law violation |
| **Tier 2** | $118,225/violation | $591,127/violation | Fraud, deceit, manipulation, or reckless disregard |
| **Tier 3** | $236,451/violation | $1,182,251/violation | Tier 2 + substantial losses or risk to others |

*Note: Amounts shown are 2025 inflation-adjusted maximums under 17 C.F.R. § 201.1001.*³⁴

Courts have rejected mechanical formulas for counting violations, refusing to multiply penalties by the number of tokens, investors, or individual transactions.³⁵ Instead, courts examine "courses of conduct"—a multi-year scheme may constitute a single violation despite numerous constituent acts.³⁶

#### 4. Staking-as-a-Service as Investment Contracts: The Kraken Precedent

On February 9, 2023, the SEC charged Payward Ventures, Inc. and Payward Trading Ltd. (collectively, "Kraken") with failing to register their crypto asset staking-as-a-service program as a securities offering.³⁷ Kraken settled for $30 million and immediately ceased offering staking services to U.S. customers.³⁸

**The SEC's Investment Contract Theory:**
The SEC applied the Howey test to Kraken's staking program and found all four prongs satisfied:³⁹

- **Prong 1 (Investment of Money)**: Customers deposited crypto assets (tokens valued at over $2.7 billion) with Kraken.⁴⁰
- **Prong 2 (Common Enterprise)**: Kraken pooled customer assets and operated validators on multiple proof-of-stake blockchains; customers received pro-rata shares of rewards (horizontal commonality).⁴¹
- **Prong 3 (Expectation of Profits)**: Kraken advertised annual percentage yields (APY) ranging from 4% to 21%, creating clear profit expectations.⁴²
- **Prong 4 (Efforts of Others)**: Kraken selected which blockchains to support, operated validator nodes, managed staking infrastructure, and determined reward distributions. Customers were entirely passive—they merely deposited assets and received returns from Kraken's efforts.⁴³

SEC Chair Gary Gensler stated in the Kraken press release:

> "Whether it's through staking-as-a-service, lending, or other means, crypto intermediaries, when offering investment contracts in exchange for investors' tokens, need to provide the proper disclosures and safeguards required by our securities laws."⁴⁴

This statement signaled the SEC's position that **any intermediary program where customers deposit assets and expect returns from the intermediary's efforts constitutes a securities offering**, regardless of whether the underlying blockchain assets are themselves securities.⁴⁵

**Operational Remedy Required:**
The Kraken settlement required **immediate cessation of U.S. staking operations**.⁴⁶ Kraken did not receive an option to register its staking services or continue operations under SEC oversight. This "cease and desist" remedy establishes the SEC's enforcement position: custodial staking-as-a-service is an unregistered securities offering that cannot be cured through registration given current SEC rules.⁴⁷

**Precedential Significance:**
The Kraken settlement provides the clearest SEC guidance to date on staking programs:⁴⁸

1. Custodial staking programs (where intermediaries operate validators) are investment contracts.
2. Non-custodial staking facilitation (where users operate their own validators) may fall outside Howey's scope.⁴⁹
3. Direct on-chain staking (where users run validators themselves) is not an investment contract because there are no "efforts of others."⁵⁰

The Kraken precedent is directly applicable to any crypto platform offering custodial staking services to U.S. customers.⁵¹

#### 5. Recent Regulatory Developments: 2025 SEC Policy Shift

On March 21, 2025, the SEC and Ripple Labs jointly requested dismissal of their appeals in *SEC v. Ripple Labs*, settling the case with a final payment of $50 million—a 60% reduction from the district court's $125 million judgment and a 97.5% reduction from the SEC's original $2 billion penalty demand.⁵² Both parties agreed to drop their respective appeals, with no operational restrictions imposed on Ripple's XRP sales.⁵³

This settlement followed the January 2025 appointment of Paul Atkins as SEC Chair, replacing Gary Gensler's enforcement-heavy approach with what industry observers have characterized as a "recalibration of the SEC's approach to crypto."⁵⁴ Additional evidence of this policy shift includes:

- **February 2025**: SEC dropped investigation into Uniswap without filing enforcement action, despite issuing a Wells Notice in April 2024.⁵⁵
- **December 2024**: SEC closed 4-year investigation into Aave without enforcement.⁵⁶
- **July 2024**: SEC withdrew requests for court rulings on securities classifications for Solana (SOL), Cardano (ADA), and Polygon (MATIC) in *SEC v. Binance*.⁵⁷
- **February 2025**: SEC dismissed lawsuit against Coinbase, dropping allegations on 13 tokens including Filecoin (FIL).⁵⁸

Enforcement statistics corroborate this shift: the SEC brought 33 crypto-related enforcement actions in 2024 (down 30% from 46 in 2023), with combined penalties of $2.6 billion (down from $4.68 billion in 2023).⁵⁹

**Implications for Settlement Negotiations:**
The 2025 policy environment creates a materially more favorable settlement landscape than existed under the Gensler SEC (2021-2024). Defendants can cite the Ripple settlement's 97.5% reduction as precedent for aggressive settlement negotiations emphasizing cooperation credit, *Liu* net profits limitations, and the absence of investor harm.⁶⁰

**SEC Settlement → Material Adverse Effect Clause (Contract Article 10.1)**

Under Delaware law, the SEC Wells Notice settlement and staking cessation constitute a "Material Adverse Effect" (MAE) triggering buyer's walk-away right only if the effect is "durationally significant."¹⁷⁶ In *Akorn, Inc. v. Fresenius Kabi AG*, the Delaware Chancery Court held that "short-term hiccups in earnings or revenue" do not constitute MAE, requiring instead "consequences persist[ing] significantly beyond the closing."¹⁷⁷

**Akorn MAE Standard Application to CTE:**

| Factor | Akorn Analysis | CTE SEC Impact | MAE Conclusion |
|--------|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Magnitude** | 86% EBITDA decline over 15 months¹⁷⁸ | 67-68% EBITDA decline (steady-state)¹⁷⁹ | Approaches MAE threshold |
| **Duration** | Persistent through closing + 5+ years¹⁸⁰ | Permanent (staking cessation perpetual; token delisting perpetual) | **Satisfies "durationally significant"** |
| **Remediability** | Not remediable without years of rebuilding¹⁸¹ | Not remediable without SEC registration (2-3 years minimum, unlikely to be granted)¹⁸² | **Not remediable = MAE** |
| **Buyer Knowledge** | Buyer knew of FDA issues but not magnitude¹⁸³ | CTE disclosed "potential SEC enforcement" but not $520M magnitude¹⁸⁴ | Generic disclosure insufficient |

**Conclusion:** Staking cessation ($55.1M annual loss, permanent) + token delisting ($46.1M annual loss, permanent) = $101.2M perpetual annual loss (30% of $185M EBITDA baseline) **exceeds MAE threshold** under *Akorn*'s "long-term earnings power" test.¹⁸⁵ The revenue loss is:¹⁸⁶

1. **Durationally significant**: Perpetual, not temporary
2. **Material in magnitude**: 67-68% EBITDA reduction
3. **Not remediable**: No regulatory path to restore staking/token revenue within transaction timeline

**Buyer's walk-away right implications:**
Unless the purchase agreement contains a **narrow MAE carve-out** for "disclosed SEC regulatory matters," buyer can terminate under the MAC clause.¹⁸⁷ However, standard MAE carve-outs exclude "disclosed" matters only if disclosed with specificity.¹⁸⁸ Generic disclosure of "potential SEC enforcement" in seller's disclosure schedules is insufficient under *Akorn*; buyer can demand carved-out MAE exception requiring:¹⁸⁹
