### 1. REGULATORY FRAMEWORK: NY BITLICENSE

#### 1.1 Overview of 23 NYCRR Part 200

The New York State Department of Financial Services (NYDFS) established the BitLicense regulatory framework under 23 NYCRR Part 200, effective June 24, 2015.⁹ The regulation applies to entities engaged in "virtual currency business activity" involving New York or New York residents, including:

- Receiving virtual currency for transmission or transmitting virtual currency
- Storing, holding, or maintaining custody of virtual currency on behalf of others
- Buying and selling virtual currency as a customer business
- Performing exchange services as a customer business
- Controlling, administering, or issuing a virtual currency¹⁰

CTE received its New York BitLicense in **October 2021** and has operated under NYDFS supervision for over three years as of this analysis date (December 2024). The BitLicense subjects CTE to comprehensive regulatory requirements including capital adequacy, surety bonds or trust accounts, cybersecurity standards, anti-money laundering programs, consumer protection measures, and ongoing reporting obligations.¹¹

#### 1.2 Capital Requirements: 23 NYCRR § 200.8(a)

**Statutory Text:**

23 NYCRR § 200.8(a) provides:

> "Each Licensee shall maintain at all times such capital in an amount and form as the superintendent determines is sufficient to ensure the financial integrity of the Licensee and its ongoing operations based on an assessment of the specific risks applicable to each Licensee."¹²

**Individualized Determination Standard:**

Unlike traditional banking regulations that prescribe formulaic capital ratios (e.g., Basel III minimum 4.5% Common Equity Tier 1 ratio), the BitLicense regulation grants the NYDFS Superintendent **discretion to determine capital requirements on a case-by-case basis**.¹³ This approach reflects the heterogeneity of virtual currency business models and the absence of standardized risk metrics in the cryptocurrency sector as of the regulation's adoption in 2015.

The Superintendent considers nine statutory factors enumerated in 23 NYCRR § 200.8(a):

1. Composition of total assets (position, size, liquidity, risk exposure, volatility)
2. Composition of total liabilities (size and repayment timing)
3. Actual and expected volume of virtual currency business activity
4. Existing licenses or regulatory status under financial services, banking, or insurance laws
5. Leverage employed by the licensee
6. Liquidity position
7. Customer financial protection via trust accounts or bonds
8. Types of entities to be serviced (retail vs. institutional)
9. Types of products or services offered¹⁴

**Form of Capital:**

Under 23 NYCRR § 200.8(b), required capital must be held in "cash, virtual currency, or high-quality, highly liquid, investment-grade assets, in such proportions as are acceptable to the superintendent."¹⁵ This flexibility allows licensees to hold capital in Bitcoin, Ethereum, or other cryptocurrencies, provided the superintendent deems such holdings sufficiently liquid and stable. In practice, NYDFS typically requires a substantial portion of capital to be held in U.S. dollar-denominated cash or U.S. Treasury securities to mitigate cryptocurrency price volatility risk.

**Supervisory Agreements:**

Upon BitLicense approval, NYDFS enters into detailed **Supervisory Agreements** with each licensee that establish individualized capital requirements, restricted activities, and reporting obligations tailored to the entity's specific business model and risk profile.¹⁶ These supervisory agreements are not publicly disclosed and constitute confidential supervisory information under New York Banking Law. NYDFS updates supervisory agreements periodically—typically annually or upon material business changes—to reflect evolving operations or market conditions.¹⁷

For CTE, the specific capital requirement would be documented in its October 2021 Supervisory Agreement (and any subsequent amendments through Q4 2024). **The $141M capital shortfall calculation provided in transaction due diligence documents indicates CTE's individualized capital requirement is $246M**, derived from a formula of **2× quarterly operating expenses**.

#### 1.3 CTE Capital Calculation

Based on transaction documents provided:

- **CTE Quarterly Operating Expenses (Q3 2024):** $123 million
- **Calculated Capital Requirement:** 2 × $123M = **$246 million**
- **CTE Current Capital (Sept 30, 2024):** $105 million
- **Capital Shortfall:** $246M - $105M = **$141 million**¹⁸

**Confidence Level:** MEDIUM. This calculation relies on transaction documents provided by deal parties rather than direct review of CTE's Supervisory Agreement with NYDFS. The 2× quarterly operating expense formula is consistent with NYDFS's stated policy of calibrating capital requirements to ensure licensees can operate for 6 months without additional funding, but individual requirements vary by licensee based on the nine statutory factors. **Recommendation:** Acquirer should request written confirmation from NYDFS of CTE's current capital requirement and compliance status as part of due diligence.

**Duration of Non-Compliance:**

If CTE's capital requirement was set at $246M when it received its BitLicense in October 2021, and CTE's quarterly operating expenses first exceeded $123M in Q2 2023 (triggering the $246M requirement), then CTE has operated with insufficient capital for approximately **18 months** (Q2 2023 through Q4 2024). This extended period of non-compliance increases regulatory risk that NYDFS may view the deficiency as willful rather than inadvertent.

Alternatively, if CTE's capital requirement was initially lower and NYDFS increased the requirement via supervisory agreement amendment in 2023-2024 as CTE's operations scaled, the non-compliance period may be shorter (6-12 months). The specific timeline depends on supervisory correspondence not available in public records.

#### 1.4 Surety Bond Requirements: 23 NYCRR § 200.9

**Statutory Requirement:**

Separate from capital requirements, 23 NYCRR § 200.9 mandates that "Each Licensee shall maintain a surety bond or trust account in United States dollars for the benefit of its customers in such form and amount as is acceptable to the superintendent."¹⁹

**Purpose:** The surety bond provides customer protection in the event of licensee insolvency, fraud, or operational failure. Unlike capital (which supports ongoing operations), the bond compensates customers for losses up to the bond amount.

**Minimum Amount:** Industry guidance indicates the minimum surety bond or trust account requirement is generally **$500,000**, though this increases based on transaction volume and customer asset holdings.²⁰ For CTE (with 8.4M customers and $15B in customer crypto assets under custody), the bond requirement would be substantially higher than the statutory minimum, likely in the range of $10M-$50M based on comparable exchanges.

**Multi-State Aggregate:** CTE operates in 47 states with active money transmitter licenses, each imposing separate surety bond requirements ranging from $10,000 to $500,000+ per state. Transaction documents indicate CTE's **aggregate surety bond requirements across all 47 states total approximately $142 million**. Annual bond premium (1-5% of bond amount) represents an ongoing compliance cost of **$1.4M-$7.1M annually**. Consolidating bonds with a single surety provider could reduce premiums to 1-2% ($1.42M-$2.84M annually).²¹

**Settlement Terms (February 23, 2021):**

### 3. CHANGE OF CONTROL APPROVAL REQUIREMENTS

#### 3.1 Regulatory Framework: 23 NYCRR § 200.11

**Statutory Prohibition:**

23 NYCRR § 200.11(a) provides: "No action shall be taken, except with the **prior written approval of the superintendent**, that may result in a change of control of a Licensee."³⁵

**Definition of Control:**

"Control" means the power to direct the management and policies of a Licensee, whether through stock ownership or otherwise. Control is **presumed** when a Person owns, directly or indirectly, **ten percent (10%) or more** of voting stock.³⁶

For the proposed acquisition of CTE by Digital Finance Ventures LLC:
- Transaction structure: Asset purchase OR stock purchase (TBD per research plan)
- If **stock purchase:** Acquirer will own 100% of CTE → Clear change of control requiring 23 NYCRR § 200.11 approval
- If **asset purchase:** May not technically trigger change of control if BitLicense remains with existing CTE entity, BUT:
  - NYDFS guidance indicates asset purchases involving transfer of substantially all virtual currency business assets constitute "change of control in substance"
  - Practical effect: BitLicense cannot be "sold" separately from the licensed entity without NYDFS approval
  - **Conclusion:** Change of control approval required regardless of transaction structure

#### 3.2 Application Timeline

**Statutory Review Period:**

23 NYCRR § 200.11(c) provides that the Superintendent shall approve or deny the application within **120 days** after the application is deemed complete, unless the Superintendent extends the period for good cause.³⁷

**Practical Timeline:**

| Phase | Duration | Cumulative |
|-------|----------|------------|
| Application preparation (legal/financial documentation) | 30-60 days | 30-60 days |
| NYDFS completeness determination | 15-30 days | 45-90 days |
| Statutory 120-day review period | 120 days | 165-210 days |
| Additional information requests (typical for large acquisitions) | 30-60 days | 195-270 days |
| **TOTAL** | **6-9 months** | **195-270 days** |

**Precedent:** NYDFS guidance for banking organization change of control applications recommends submitting applications **at least 90 days before the proposed closing date**.³⁸ For cryptocurrency exchanges—a higher-risk sector with enhanced scrutiny—the practical timeline extends to 6-9 months.

#### 3.3 Approval Criteria

**Statutory Standard:**

The Superintendent considers "public interest and needs and convenience of the public."³⁹ This broad discretionary standard allows NYDFS to deny applications based on concerns about:

- **Financial Strength:** Acquirer's capacity to meet ongoing capital requirements and support CTE's operations
- **Managerial Competence:** Relevant expertise in cryptocurrency, financial services, AML/BSA compliance
- **Regulatory Compliance History:** Track record with SEC, CFTC, FinCEN, state regulators; any enforcement actions or compliance failures
- **Business Plan Viability:** Post-acquisition operations sustainable and BitLicense-compliant
- **Customer Protection:** Asset security and segregation plan; insurance coverage; cybersecurity controls

**NYDFS De Facto Veto Power:**

The "public interest" standard grants NYDFS **de facto veto power** over cryptocurrency exchange acquisitions. The most prominent example:

**Precedent: Binance.US / Voyager (2022-2023)**

Binance.US sought to acquire Voyager Digital assets for $1.02B, but NYDFS objected because:
- Binance.US lacked BitLicense (would need to obtain post-acquisition)
- New York customers would wait 6+ months for asset return during regulatory approval
- Uncertainty about whether NYDFS would grant BitLicense to Binance.US given Binance's global regulatory issues

**Outcome:** Binance.US withdrew from acquisition in April 2023. NYDFS opposition effectively killed the transaction.⁴⁰

**Key Takeaway for CTE Acquisition:**

Unlike Binance.US (which lacked BitLicense), CTE already holds valid BitLicense, reducing NYDFS denial risk. However, the $141M capital shortfall must be addressed in the change of control application. NYDFS will scrutinize:

1. **Capital Remediation Plan:** How will Acquirer address $141M shortfall? At closing or within specified timeframe?
2. **Acquirer Financial Capacity:** Does Acquirer have liquid capital to inject $141M? What are Acquirer's other financial commitments?
3. **Ongoing Capital Compliance:** Will Acquirer maintain capital above $246M requirement going forward? What if quarterly opex increases further?
4. **Business Plan Changes:** Will Acquirer change CTE's business model (e.g., cease staking, delist tokens, exit margin trading) in ways that affect risk profile?

**Approval Denial Risk Assessment:**

- **Base Case (80% approval probability):** Acquirer submits comprehensive capital plan demonstrating $141M injection at closing plus ongoing capital adequacy. NYDFS approves with conditions (periodic capital reporting, restrictions on distributions until capital exceeds 125% of requirement).

- **Downside Case (15% conditional approval):** NYDFS identifies additional concerns (Acquirer's compliance history, business plan changes, cybersecurity issues identified in due diligence). Approval conditioned on third-party monitor, enhanced AML program, or restrictions on product offerings. Approval delayed by 3-6 months.

- **Worst Case (5% denial):** NYDFS determines Acquirer lacks financial capacity or managerial competence to operate BitLicense holder. Denial requires Acquirer to abandon transaction or restructure (e.g., divest New York operations, transfer BitLicense to separate well-capitalized subsidiary).

**Estimated Denial Risk:** 10-20% if capital plan is inadequate or Acquirer lacks demonstrated financial capacity.

### 4. CAPITAL RAISE STRATEGY OPTIONS

CTE faces a **$141 million capital shortfall**. This section analyzes three structuring options for addressing the shortfall in the context of the acquisition.

#### 4.1 Option A: Acquirer Capital Injection at Closing

**Structure:**
- Digital Finance Ventures LLC (Acquirer) injects $141M into CTE immediately at closing or within 30 days post-closing
- Injection structured as either:
  - **Subordinated Loan:** $141M loan at market rates (8-12% interest), subordinated to senior creditors, maturity 5-7 years⁴¹
  - **Equity Contribution:** $141M as additional paid-in capital, increasing Acquirer's ownership stake to 100%

**Regulatory Treatment:**

Under 23 NYCRR § 200.8(b), capital may be held in "cash, virtual currency, or high-quality, highly liquid, investment-grade assets."⁴² Both subordinated debt and equity qualify as regulatory capital, though NYDFS may prefer equity for safety-and-soundness reasons (debt creates repayment obligations that strain cash flow).

**NYDFS Approval Requirement:**

23 NYCRR § 200.10 requires prior written approval for "material changes" to a licensee's business.⁴³ A $141M capital injection would constitute a material change requiring advance NYDFS notification and approval (typically 30-60 days). This requirement is **separate from** change of control approval under 23 NYCRR § 200.11 but can be processed concurrently.

**Advantages:**
- ✅ Acquirer retains full control over capital deployment timeline
- ✅ Can be structured as debt (preserving Acquirer's equity for other uses) or equity
- ✅ Capital infusion demonstrates financial strength to NYDFS during change of control review
- ✅ No reduction in purchase price; sellers receive full $1.8B

**Disadvantages:**
- ❌ Increases Acquirer's total investment from $1.8B to **$1.941B** (7.8% premium over stated purchase price)
- ❌ If structured as subordinated debt: Annual interest cost of **$11.3M-$16.9M** (8-12% × $141M)⁴⁴
- ❌ Requires Acquirer to have $141M liquid capital available at closing (may necessitate debt financing by Acquirer)
- ❌ NYDFS may delay change of control approval pending demonstration of capital infusion capacity

**Timeline:**
- NYDFS change of control application: Filed 120 days before intended closing⁴⁵
- Capital injection: At closing or within 30 days post-closing (subject to NYDFS approval)
- Total timeline: **120-150 days (4-5 months)**

#### 4.2 Option B: Pre-Closing Capital Raise by Sellers

**Structure:**
- Target company (CTE) and current sellers raise $141M **before closing**
- Purchase price **reduced by $141M** from $1.8B to **$1.659B**
- Capital raise structured as:
  - **Seller Equity Contribution:** Current owners inject $141M as additional capital (diluting their ownership pre-sale)
  - **Third-Party Equity Investment:** Sellers bring in outside investor at discounted valuation
  - **Seller Note:** Sellers defer $141M of purchase price, converting it to subordinated debt owed by CTE pre-closing

**Regulatory Treatment:**

This structure addresses the capital shortfall **before the acquisition**, eliminating regulatory risk that NYDFS denies change of control approval due to insufficient capital. CTE would be compliant with 23 NYCRR § 200.8 at the time of acquisition closing, streamlining NYDFS review.

**Advantages:**
- ✅ Acquirer's total investment remains $1.659B (no premium over economic value)
- ✅ CTE is capital-compliant at closing, reducing NYDFS approval risk from 10-20% to <5%
- ✅ Purchase price adjustment reflects economic reality of capital deficiency (capital shortfall was undisclosed or underestimated pre-LOI)
- ✅ No post-closing integration complexity related to capital injection

**Disadvantages:**
- ❌ Sellers net $141M less in proceeds ($1.659B vs. $1.8B stated price = 7.8% reduction)
- ❌ Sellers will strongly resist unless capital deficiency constitutes breach of representations or warranties
- ❌ If third-party equity investor involved: Valuation discount (20-30% for minority investor in pre-exit situation), negotiation delays (60-90 days), possible governance rights complicating transaction
- ❌ Seller note structure creates contingent liability for Acquirer if CTE underperforms post-closing and cannot service debt

**Timeline:**
- Capital raise by sellers: **60-90 days**
- NYDFS change of control application: Filed after capital raise complete (to demonstrate compliance)
- NYDFS review: **120 days** (standard period; likely no extension needed given pre-closing compliance)
- Total timeline: **180-210 days (6-7 months)**

**Negotiation Considerations:**

Sellers will likely resist Option B unless:
- Due diligence revealed that CTE's capital deficiency was **not disclosed** in initial representations and warranties (breach of Section [X] of LOI/Purchase Agreement)
- Purchase agreement includes **specific indemnity** for regulatory compliance failures, making sellers liable for NYDFS penalties and remediation costs
- Capital shortfall constitutes a **material adverse effect** under purchase agreement, allowing Acquirer to renegotiate price or walk away

**Likely Outcome:** Sellers will argue capital shortfall was disclosed or knowable from CTE's financial statements, and propose Option A (Acquirer injection) or hybrid structure instead.

#### 4.3 Option C: Third-Party Debt Financing

**Structure:**
- CTE obtains $141M **capital loan** from third-party lender (commercial bank, private credit fund, or specialty cryptocurrency lender)
- Loan structured as **regulatory capital-qualifying subordinated debt** with maturity 5-7 years
- Loan proceeds held as liquid assets (cash or U.S. Treasury securities) to satisfy 23 NYCRR § 200.8 requirements
- Loan secured by CTE assets (accounts receivable, technology, cryptocurrency inventory) or guaranteed by Acquirer post-closing

**Regulatory Treatment:**

NYDFS would require **prior approval** for a $141M financing arrangement under 23 NYCRR § 200.10 (material change to business).⁴⁶ The Superintendent would assess whether debt service obligations ($14M-$21M annually at 10-15% interest) impair CTE's financial integrity or ability to meet ongoing capital requirements.

**Market Rates for Cryptocurrency Company Debt:**

Given cryptocurrency exchanges' higher risk profile (regulatory uncertainty, operational risks, volatile revenues), subordinated debt interest rates are significantly higher than traditional financial services:
- **Commercial banks:** Generally unwilling to lend to cryptocurrency companies without substantial collateral/guarantees
- **Private credit funds:** 10-15% interest for subordinated debt (compared to 6-8% for traditional financial services)
- **Cryptocurrency-specific lenders:** 12-18% interest (higher risk premium)

**Estimated Annual Debt Service Cost:**
- Low end (10%): **$14.1M annually**
- High end (15%): **$21.2M annually**
- Total 5-year cost (assuming 15% rate, interest-only payments): **$105.6M** in interest payments⁴⁷

**Advantages:**
- ✅ No reduction in purchase price; sellers receive full $1.8B
- ✅ No immediate cash outlay by Acquirer at closing (debt service paid from CTE operating cash flow)
- ✅ Loan amortization spreads capital requirement over 5-7 years rather than one-time $141M payment
- ✅ Interest expense is tax-deductible (unlike equity cost), providing annual tax shield of $3M-$5M (assuming 25% effective tax rate)

**Disadvantages:**
- ❌ Annual debt service cost of $14.1M-$21.2M reduces CTE's EBITDA by 8-11% (CTE's current EBITDA: $185M)
