### Key Findings

#### 1. SEC's Legal Claims Are Strong on Staking and Exchange/Broker-Dealer Violations

**Staking as Investment Contract (90-95% Probability SEC Prevails):**
The Kraken precedent (February 2023, $30M settlement) directly applies to CTE's staking model. In that case, Kraken customers deposited crypto assets, Kraken staked them on proof-of-stake blockchains, and paid customers variable yields (up to 21% advertised). The SEC determined this satisfied the Howey test: (1) investment of money (customers deposited crypto), (2) common enterprise (Kraken pooled assets), (3) expectation of profits (yields promised), and (4) derived from efforts of others (Kraken performed staking operations). CTE's staking model is identical: customers deposit $1.2B in staked assets, CTE generates $387M in annual staking rewards, and pays customers while retaining $58M (15% of rewards) as revenue.

Commissioner Hester Peirce's dissent notwithstanding, the Kraken settlement established clear precedent that staking-as-a-service requires SEC registration under Securities Act Â§ 5 or complete cessation of U.S. operations. Kraken chose the latter, immediately ceasing U.S. staking (though continuing operations for non-U.S. customers). CTE will face the same binary choice: attempt to register (no crypto firm has successfully registered staking products to date) or cease U.S. operations entirely. The revenue impact is $58M annually, representing 8.5% of CTE's total revenue and reducing EBITDA from $185M to $127M (31% reduction).

**Unregistered Exchange/Broker-Dealer (80-90% Probability SEC Prevails):**
If the 42 tokens CTE lists are deemed securities (pending T2 case-law-analyst's Howey test analysis), CTE must register as a national securities exchange under Exchange Act Â§ 6 or as a broker-dealer under Exchange Act Â§ 15. The Poloniex precedent (August 2021, $10M settlement) involved a similar fact pattern: Poloniex operated an unregistered digital asset exchange facilitating trading of tokens deemed securities from July 2017 to November 2019. CTE's operations are larger in scale ($680M revenue vs. Poloniex's smaller operations), and the SEC has demonstrated consistent enforcement against unregistered crypto exchanges (125 actions filed 2021-2024).

The defense that CTE facilitates only secondary market trading (not primary sales) has limited utility here. While SEC v. Ripple (S.D.N.Y. 2023) held that secondary sales of XRP on exchanges to retail buyers were NOT securities offerings under Howey (no reasonable expectation of profits based on Ripple's efforts), that holding applies to the securities offering analysis (Securities Act Â§ 5), not the exchange/broker-dealer registration requirement (Exchange Act Â§Â§ 6, 15). If tokens are securities, platforms trading them must register regardless of whether the platform itself is "offering" the securities.

No U.S. crypto exchange has successfully registered as a national securities exchange to date. Coinbase, the largest U.S. exchange, received a Wells Notice in March 2023 for similar allegations and chose litigation over settlement, continuing operations while challenging the SEC's jurisdiction. CTE faces a more compressed timeline (deal closing Q2-Q3 2026) and cannot afford 2-5 years of litigation uncertainty. Settlement requiring token delisting is the only viable path.

**Unregistered Securities Offerings (50-70% Probability SEC Prevails on Some Tokens):**
This claim depends on the Howey test application to each of the 42 tokens, which T2 (case-law-analyst) will analyze. However, SEC v. Ripple provides some defense: the court held that programmatic sales of XRP on secondary markets to retail buyers (those who purchased XRP on exchanges, not directly from Ripple) were NOT securities offerings because buyers had no reasonable expectation of profits based on Ripple's effortsâ€”they were purchasing on the open market at prices determined by supply and demand, not based on Ripple's promotional activities.

That said, the Ripple holding is narrow and fact-specific. The SEC successfully argued that Ripple's institutional sales (direct sales to hedge funds and other sophisticated investors) WERE securities offerings. CTE serves 2,800 institutional clients, and the SEC will likely argue those sales satisfy Howey even if retail sales do not. Additionally, the Ripple decision is not binding precedent for other circuits and remains subject to appeal (though Ripple and the SEC ultimately settled in March 2025 with both parties dropping appeals).

The token classification analysis is complex and depends on factors including: (1) degree of decentralization (Layer-1 protocols like Bitcoin and Ethereum are likely NOT securities; DeFi governance tokens like UNI, AAVE, COMP are higher risk), (2) nature of token utility (pure payment tokens vs. governance/profit-sharing tokens), and (3) manner of initial distribution (ICO/pre-sale vs. mining/proof-of-work). T2's Howey analysis will determine which specific tokens must be delisted, but a reasonable estimate is 20-30 of the 42 tokens (48-71%), representing $50-100M in annual trading revenue.

#### 2. CTE's Defenses Provide Moderate Mitigation (20-40% Penalty Reduction)

**Liu v. SEC Net Profits Requirement (60-70% Disgorgement Reduction):**
The Supreme Court's 2020 Liu decision fundamentally limits SEC disgorgement authority. The Court held that disgorgement cannot exceed a defendant's net profitsâ€”legitimate business expenses must be deductedâ€”and the SEC is generally required to return disgorgement to victims rather than the Treasury. This directly undermines the SEC's $520M disgorgement claim (gross revenue from 42 tokens + staking).

Applying CTE's FY2024 financials (72.8% operating expense ratio: $680M revenue, $185M EBITDA = $495M operating expenses), the net profits from violative conduct are approximately:
- Trading fees (42 tokens): $462M gross - $336M allocated operating expenses = $126M net
- Staking: $58M gross - $42M allocated operating expenses = $16M net
- **Total Net Profits: $142M**

However, CTE can argue for even more aggressive expense deductions. Kraken precedent provides a useful benchmark: Kraken earned $147M in staking revenue but settled for only $30M (20.4% of gross revenue), suggesting the SEC accepted significant expense deductions or negotiated down from a higher initial demand. Applying the 20% ratio to CTE's $520M gross yields $104M, though CTE's larger scale and additional violations (exchange/broker-dealer, not just staking) argue for a higher amount.

The defensible range for disgorgement, considering net profits and precedent, is **$100M-$150M (vs. $520M claimed)**. CTE should open settlement negotiations at $125M (24% of gross), justified by detailed operating expense breakdowns (technology infrastructure, personnel, compliance costs, state money transmitter licenses costing $4.5-9.3M annually, cybersecurity, customer support, marketing).

**Cooperation Credit (10-20% Penalty Reduction):**
The SEC Division of Enforcement's May 2024 guidance (former Director Gurbir Grewal's Five Principles of Cooperation) emphasizes: (1) self-policing with up-to-date compliance policies, (2) self-reporting before completing internal investigations, (3) proactive remediation, (4) "above and beyond" cooperation, and (5) early, substantive collaboration with SEC staff. In FY2024, two institutional investment managers received NO penalties due to timely self-reporting, and several credit-rating agencies received substantially lower penalties for cooperation.

CTE's cooperation record is mixed:
- **Positive:** Self-reported September 2024 hot wallet hack to SEC, filed SARs for suspicious transactions, maintained AML compliance program
- **Negative:** Did NOT self-report SEC securities law violations before receiving Wells Notice, filed 2018 no-action letter request but proceeded without response (arguable good faith)

This cooperation profile supports a 10-20% penalty reduction (vs. optimal 30-50% for full self-reporting), translating to $3-6M off civil penalties. CTE should emphasize the hot wallet self-reporting and SAR filings as evidence of compliance-oriented culture, though the SEC may counter that these were AML obligations (required by FinCEN), not voluntary cooperation on securities violations.

**Ability to Pay Defense (Limited Effect, 0-10% Reduction):**
Under 15 U.S.C. Â§ 78u-2(d), respondents may present evidence of inability to pay, and the SEC may consider such evidence in determining whether a penalty is in the public interest. The Terraform Labs precedent (June 2024, $4.47B settlement but company in bankruptcy) shows this defense can be powerful: the civil penalty ($420M) represented only 0.94% of total disgorgement ($4.47B), far below typical ratios, because Terraform was insolvent and the SEC agreed to subordinate its claims to investor compensation.

However, CTE is solvent with $185M EBITDA and is the target of a $1.8B acquisition, undermining any inability-to-pay argument. The countervailing factor is the $141M capital shortfall for NY BitLicense compliance, suggesting CTE faces significant capital constraints. The SEC may grant a modest 5-10% reduction based on CTE's capital needs, but this is unlikely to be a primary negotiation lever. The ability to pay defense works better for individuals or bankrupt entities; CTE is neither.

**2025 SEC Policy Shift (20-30% Settlement Reduction):**
The most significant mitigating factor is the change in SEC leadership and policy direction in 2025. The Ripple settlement (March 2025, final payment $50M) provides compelling precedent: the SEC originally demanded $2 billion in penalties, the court ordered $125 million in 2024, and the final settlement reduced the payment to $50 million (60% reduction from court order, 97.5% reduction from original SEC demand). Both parties agreed to drop appeals, with no operational restrictions imposed on Ripple's XRP sales.

This settlement signaled a "recalibration of the SEC's approach to crypto" under new leadership (Chair Paul Atkins, appointed under the Trump administration, replacing Gary Gensler's enforcement-heavy approach). Additionally, January 2025 off-channel communications settlements imposed "less burdensome undertakings" compared to 2024 settlements, generally not requiring independent compliance consultants or reporting of employee disciplinary actions to the SEC.

CTE should leverage this policy shift aggressively in negotiations, citing the Ripple settlement as evidence that the SEC is willing to compromise significantly on crypto enforcement actions to promote regulatory clarity and industry innovation. The SEC's willingness to reduce Ripple's penalty by 97.5% from its initial demand suggests CTE can achieve a 60-75% reduction from the $520M disgorgement claim, consistent with the $130-195M target range.

**Date:** February 9, 2023
**Parties:** SEC v. Payward Ventures, Inc. and Payward Trading Ltd. (collectively, "Kraken")
**Allegations:** Failing to register the offer and sale of crypto asset staking-as-a-service program

- **Operational Remedy:** Immediately cease offering or selling securities through crypto asset staking services or staking programs to U.S. customers
- **Revenue Context:** Kraken earned approximately $147 million in revenue from staking services; U.S. investors had crypto assets worth over $2.7 billion on Kraken's platform
- **Disclosure Violations:** SEC alleged Kraken failed to adequately disclose risks of participating in the program, which had advertised annual yields of as much as 21%

**Precedential Significance:**
- First major SEC enforcement action treating staking-as-a-service as an investment contract under Securities Act Â§ 5
- Established that platforms offering staking services must register or face enforcement
- Settlement required complete cessation of U.S. staking operations (not just registration)
- Commissioner Hester Peirce issued dissenting statement criticizing "regulation by enforcement"

#### 2. BlockFi Lending Settlement (February 2022)

**Date:** February 14, 2022

### B. SEC Disgorgement Authority and Calculation Methodology

#### 1. Supreme Court Limitations: Kokesh and Liu

**Kokesh v. SEC, 581 U.S. 455 (2017)**

The Supreme Court held that SEC disgorgement constitutes a "penalty" under 28 U.S.C. Â§ 2462, subjecting it to a five-year statute of limitations. This means the SEC can only seek disgorgement for ill-gotten gains obtained within five years of the enforcement action filing.

**Implications for CTE:** The SEC's $520M disgorgement calculation allegedly covers revenue from 2021-2024. The Wells Notice was received October 15, 2024. The five-year lookback period extends to October 15, 2019. Any revenue before October 2019 is time-barred.

**Liu v. SEC, 591 U.S. 71 (2020)**

The Supreme Court held (8-1) that the SEC may continue to obtain disgorgement but with significant limitations:

1. **Net Profits Requirement:** Disgorgement cannot exceed defendant's net profits (legitimate business expenses must be deducted)
2. **Victim Return Requirement:** SEC is "generally required" to return disgorgement to victims (not Treasury)
3. **Equitable Remedy:** Disgorgement must meet traditional equitable standards

**Implications for CTE:**
- SEC cannot claim gross revenue of $462M (trading fees) and $58M (staking) = $520M total
- SEC must deduct legitimate operating expenses (technology infrastructure, personnel, compliance, licenses)
- Settlement funds should be returned to harmed investors (if any identifiable harm exists)

**Source Citations:**
- Kokesh v. SEC, 581 U.S. 455 (2017). https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/16pdf/16-529_i426.pdf
- Liu v. SEC, 591 U.S. 71 (2020). Harvard Law School Corp Gov Blog. *An Analysis of the Supreme Court's Decision in Liu v. SEC*. https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2020/07/04/an-analysis-of-the-supreme-courts-decision-in-liu-v-sec/

The SEC continues to seek disgorgement in crypto enforcement but has adapted methodology post-Liu:

**Terraform Labs (June 2024):**
- Disgorgement: $3.59B
- Prejudgment Interest: $467M
- Civil Penalty: $420M
- **Key Point:** Despite bankruptcy, SEC pursued full disgorgement but agreed to subordinate collection to investor claims (victim return requirement from Liu)

**BlockFi (February 2022):**

- No separate disgorgement calculation disclosed
- Settlement acknowledged net profits considerations

**Kraken (February 2023):**

**Calculation Methodology Pattern:**
- SEC starts with gross revenue from violative conduct
- Deducts legitimate operating expenses to arrive at net profits (Liu requirement)
- Adds prejudgment interest (typically calculated from midpoint of violation period)
- Negotiates final amount considering ability to pay and cooperation credit

### D. Standard SEC Consent Decree Terms

#### 1. Typical Settlement Components

**Monetary Relief:**
- Disgorgement of ill-gotten gains
- Prejudgment interest (calculated from midpoint of violation period)
- Civil penalties (tiered based on violation severity)

**Injunctive Relief:**
- Permanent injunction against future violations of securities laws
- "Obey-the-law" injunction requiring compliance with specific statutory provisions
- Most potent SEC remedy due to collateral consequences

**Undertakings (Forward-Looking Remedies):**
- Cease violative conduct (e.g., delist securities tokens, cease U.S. staking operations)
- Implement compliance enhancements
- Retain independent compliance consultant (recent trend: less common in 2025 settlements)
- Periodic reporting to SEC staff
- Employee training and discipline
- Document retention (typically 6 years)

**Registration Commitments:**
- BlockFi precedent: Agree to pursue registration of products (first-ever in crypto settlement)
- Alternative: Cease offering products to U.S. customers

**Source Citations:**
- U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. (2018). *Remedies and Relief in SEC Enforcement Actions* (Speech by Steven Peikin, Co-Director, Division of Enforcement). https://www.sec.gov/news/speech/speech-peikin-100318

#### 2. Independent Compliance Consultants/Monitors

**Historical Practice (2021-2024):**
- Many settlements required independent compliance consultant
- Consultant makes recommendations to company and reports to SEC
- Typically required for:
  - Systemic compliance failures
  - Repeat violations
  - Large-scale fraud
  - AML/KYC deficiencies

**Recent Trend (2025):**
- January 2025 settlements impose "less burdensome undertakings"
- Generally do NOT require independent consultant
- No requirement to report disciplinary actions to SEC
- Shift reflects change in SEC administration/priorities

**Example Terms (2024 Regions Case):**
- Retain independent consultant within 30 days (not unacceptable to SEC staff)
- Consultant reviews compliance policies and procedures
- Company implements recommendations
- Written certification of compliance
- Maintain records for 6 years

- Global Relay. (2024). *SEC Doubles Down on Off-Channel Communications Settlements*. https://www.globalrelay.com/resources/thought-leadership/sec-doubles-down-on-off-channel-communications-settlements/

#### 3. Injunctive Relief Standards

**Purpose:**
- Designed solely to forestall future violations (preventative, not punitive)
- SEC must demonstrate "reasonable likelihood of future violations"

**"Obey-the-Law" Injunctions:**
- Defendant agrees to comply with securities laws
- Violation of injunction = contempt of court (criminal penalties possible)
- Collateral consequences: Industry bars, disqualifications, reputational harm

**Standards for Granting:**
- Second Circuit: SEC must show "reasonable likelihood of future violations"
- If SEC cannot demonstrate necessity, injunction should not be granted
- Absent proper factual support, injunction is punitive (improper)

**Consent Decree Review:**
- Courts review for reasonableness
- Must be procedurally and substantively fair
- Most defendants consent without admitting or denying allegations
