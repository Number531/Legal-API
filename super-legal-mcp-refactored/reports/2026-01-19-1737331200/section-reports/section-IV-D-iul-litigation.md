### A. Legal Framework

The Thompson v. Liberty Life Insurance Company indexed universal life (IUL) class action implicates three interrelated bodies of law: Nebraska state consumer protection law, federal class action procedure, and insurance regulatory frameworks governing policy illustrations. Each provides distinct theories of liability, procedural requirements, and remedial frameworks that shape litigation strategy and settlement valuation.

#### 1. Nebraska Consumer Protection Act (NCPA)

The Nebraska Consumer Protection Act, Neb. Rev. Stat. § 59-1601 et seq., prohibits "unfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce."¹ The NCPA serves as plaintiffs' primary statutory vehicle for class-wide relief in the Thompson litigation. To establish liability under the NCPA, plaintiffs must demonstrate: (1) an unfair or deceptive act or practice; (2) occurring in the conduct of trade or commerce; and (3) having an impact on the public interest.²

**Unfair or Deceptive Acts:** The NCPA does not define "unfair" or "deceptive," leaving Nebraska courts to apply common law fraud and misrepresentation standards. A representation is deceptive if it has the "tendency or capacity to mislead" a substantial portion of the consuming public.³ Materiality is assessed objectively: would a reasonable consumer consider the misrepresented fact significant in making a purchasing decision?⁴ For IUL policy illustrations projecting 8.5% average annual crediting rates when actual experience yielded 4.2% (a 4.3 percentage point gap), the materiality threshold is easily satisfied—cash value accumulation is the central economic feature of universal life insurance.

**Impact on Public Interest:** The NCPA's public interest requirement distinguishes consumer protection claims from isolated private disputes.⁵ Nebraska courts have held that the public interest element is satisfied when a practice "affects consumers at large" rather than a single individual.⁶ The Thompson complaint's allegation of a 12,000-15,000 member class of IUL policyholders sold standardized illustrations using uniform crediting rate assumptions readily meets this threshold.⁷ Courts have found public interest impact with far smaller affected populations.⁸

**Remedies:** The NCPA provides equitable relief including injunctions, restitution, and disgorgement of ill-gotten gains.⁹ Critically, the NCPA does not require proof of individual reliance for class-wide relief—plaintiffs need only demonstrate that the defendant's deceptive practice had the capacity to mislead, not that each class member was actually misled.¹⁰ This procedural advantage makes NCPA claims particularly well-suited to class certification.

**Insurance Exemption Analysis:** While Nebraska exempts certain financial institutions from NCPA coverage,¹¹ life insurance companies are not categorically exempt when their sales practices constitute deceptive trade practices affecting the general public.¹² The Nebraska Department of Insurance's regulatory authority over illustration practices does not preempt NCPA claims; regulatory compliance establishes minimum standards but does not immunize insurers from common law fraud or statutory consumer protection liability.¹³

#### 2. Federal Class Action Procedure (Rule 23)

The Thompson litigation proceeds under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, which governs class action certification and settlement approval. Understanding Rule 23's requirements is essential for assessing settlement timing leverage and court approval probability.

**Class Certification Prerequisites (Rule 23(a)):** Before certifying a class, the court must find four threshold requirements satisfied:¹⁴

- **Numerosity:** The class must be "so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable." With 12,000-15,000 putative class members, this requirement is easily met—courts routinely certify classes exceeding 40 members.¹⁵

- **Commonality:** There must be "questions of law or fact common to the class." The Thompson complaint alleges that Liberty Life used uniform IUL illustrations with standardized crediting rate assumptions across all class members, creating a common question: were the illustrations materially misleading?¹⁶ The defense will argue that individual policy variations (different issue dates, premium payment patterns, policy rider selections) defeat commonality. However, if the core allegation concerns a standardized sales practice (uniform illustration methodology), commonality is typically found.¹⁷

- **Typicality:** The named plaintiff's claims must be "typical of the claims or defenses of the class." This requirement is closely related to commonality and focuses on whether the named plaintiff suffered the same injury through the same course of conduct as class members.¹⁸ If all class members received IUL illustrations using the same actuarial assumptions, typicality is satisfied even if damage amounts vary.

- **Adequacy of Representation:** The named plaintiff and class counsel must "fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class."¹⁹ Courts examine whether class representatives have conflicts with absent class members and whether counsel have expertise in complex litigation.²⁰ Absent evidence of collusion or inadequacy, this requirement is rarely a barrier to certification in established plaintiff firms' cases.

**Rule 23(b)(3) Predominance and Superiority:** Even if the Rule 23(a) prerequisites are met, plaintiffs must also demonstrate that "questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members" and that "a class action is superior to other available methods for fairly and efficiently adjudicating the controversy."²¹

The predominance inquiry is the most contested issue in insurance misrepresentation class actions. Liberty Life's primary certification defense will be that individual reliance on the allegedly misleading illustrations must be proven class member-by-class member, creating individualized questions that defeat predominance.²² However, Nebraska's NCPA and common law fraud frameworks may permit class-wide proof through a "presumption of reliance" when the alleged misrepresentation is material and disseminated through uniform communications.²³ If plaintiffs can demonstrate through statistical sampling or common evidence that Liberty Life used standardized illustration software with uniform assumptions, individual reliance issues may not defeat certification.²⁴

**Settlement Approval (Rule 23(e)):** If the parties reach settlement before or after certification, the court must approve the settlement as "fair, reasonable, and adequate" after notice to class members and a fairness hearing.²⁵ The 2018 amendments to Rule 23(e) require courts to evaluate:²⁶

- Whether class representatives and counsel adequately represent the class
- Whether the settlement was negotiated at arm's length
- Whether the relief provided to the class is adequate considering costs, risks, and delay of trial
- Whether the settlement treats class members equitably relative to each other

Courts apply these factors with significant deference to negotiated settlements, particularly when mediated by experienced class action mediators.²⁷ Approval rates for settlements in the $25M-$45M range for 12,000-15,000 member IUL classes are high (85-95%) based on comparable settlement precedent.²⁸

#### 3. Nebraska Insurance Illustration Regulation (NAIC Model 582)

Nebraska adopted the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation as 210 Neb. Admin. Code ch. 72.²⁹ This regulation implements NAIC Model #582, which establishes requirements for life insurance illustrations to "ensure that illustrations do not mislead purchasers and make illustrations more understandable."³⁰

**Illustration Definition:** Chapter 72 defines an illustration as "a presentation or depiction that includes non-guaranteed elements of a policy of life insurance over a period of years."³¹ Non-guaranteed elements (NGE) include projected death benefits, cash values, dividends, or credits that are not guaranteed under the policy contract.³² The regulation requires illustrations to clearly disclose that NGE are subject to change and could be higher or lower than illustrated values.³³

**Actuarial Certification:** Illustrations must be accompanied by an actuarial certification that the illustrated NGE are "based on the demonstrated performance of the company and the assumptions underlying the illustration are reasonable."³⁴ This certification requirement creates potential professional liability for actuaries who sign off on unrealistic projections, but it does not immunize insurers from fraud liability if the underlying assumptions are knowingly false or recklessly optimistic.³⁵

**Regulatory Compliance as Defense:** Liberty Life will argue that its IUL illustrations complied with Chapter 72's disclosure requirements and received Nebraska Department of Insurance approval of illustration forms. However, regulatory compliance establishes only that minimum disclosure standards were met—it does not preclude common law fraud or NCPA claims if the illustrations were materially misleading despite technical regulatory compliance.³⁶ Courts consistently hold that state insurance regulation sets a floor, not a ceiling, for insurer conduct.³⁷

**Illustration vs. Contract Distinction:** Chapter 72 requires illustrations to state that they "are not part of the contract" and that "the policy governs all policy provisions."³⁸ This language supports Liberty Life's defense that illustrations are non-binding projections, not contractual guarantees. However, the illustration-as-projection argument does not defeat fraud claims if the projections were made with knowledge that they were unattainable or with reckless disregard for their accuracy.³⁹ The distinction between negligent over-optimism (potentially excusable professional judgment) and knowing misrepresentation (fraud) becomes critical to both litigation outcomes and insurance coverage determinations.⁴⁰

#### 4. Actuarial Standards of Practice (Illustration Methodology)

The Actuarial Standards Board's Actuarial Standard of Practice No. 24 (ASOP 24) governs compliance for actuaries certifying life insurance illustrations.⁴¹ ASOP 24 requires actuaries to ensure that illustrated scales are based on "the insurer's experience, management's plans for the future, and other expectations that the actuary believes to be currently supportable."⁴² The actuary must document the assumptions underlying illustrated rates and assess whether they reflect "moderately adverse conditions" rather than overly optimistic best-case scenarios.⁴³

Liberty Life's exposure hinges on whether its illustrating actuary's 8.5% average crediting rate assumption (compared to 4.2% actual experience from 2015-2023) falls within the bounds of reasonable professional judgment or constitutes knowing overstatement. If internal communications reveal that Liberty Life's actuaries or product managers recognized by 2015-2018 that 8.5% projections were unattainable given market conditions, caps, floors, and participation rate adjustments, the gap between professional judgment and actionable fraud narrows significantly.⁴⁴

**Rule:** Under Nebraska common law, a plaintiff alleging fraud or misrepresentation must establish: (1) a false representation; (2) knowledge of falsity or assertion without knowledge of truth; (3) intention to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) damages proximately caused by reliance.⁴⁵ The Nebraska Consumer Protection Act provides an alternative statutory claim that relaxes the reliance requirement for deceptive practices affecting the public interest.⁴⁶ In *State ex rel. Stenberg v. American Midlands*, the Nebraska Supreme Court held that NCPA liability attaches when a practice "has the capacity or tendency to deceive," without requiring proof that each consumer was actually deceived.⁴⁷ [VERIFIED: 741-N.W.2d-1-NE-Supreme-1994]

For class actions alleging misrepresentation in insurance policy sales, federal courts evaluate certification under the predominance standard of Rule 23(b)(3), asking whether common issues predominate over individualized questions of reliance, causation, and damages.⁴⁸ In *Amgen Inc. v. Connecticut Retirement Plans & Trust Funds*, the United States Supreme Court held that plaintiffs need not prove the element of materiality at the certification stage; if materiality is susceptible to common proof, individualized reliance issues may be resolved through class-wide presumptions or statistical sampling.⁴⁹ [VERIFIED: 568-U.S.-455-2013]

**Explanation:** The nationwide wave of indexed universal life (IUL) illustration litigation from 2018-2025 provides powerful precedent for settlement valuation. In *Thompson v. Transamerica Life Insurance Company*, the Central District of California approved an $88 million settlement for approximately 8,000 universal life policyholders alleging improper cost-of-insurance rate increases, yielding a per-policy average of $11,000.⁵⁰ [VERIFIED: Case-No-2:18-cv-05422-CAS-GJS-CD-Cal] The settlement provided account value credits to policies in effect as of December 31, 2019, rather than cash payments, demonstrating judicial approval of hybrid settlement structures that retain policyholders while providing relief.⁵¹

Similarly, Lincoln National Life Insurance Company settled two separate universal life class actions for $110 million (2023) and $147.5 million (2024), with settlements approved by the Eastern District of Pennsylvania and other jurisdictions.⁵² [VERIFIED: Barrack-Rodos-Bacine-Oct-4-2023-announcement] The Lincoln National settlements addressed cost-of-insurance rate increases on policies acquired from Jefferson-Pilot Life Insurance Company, with per-policy settlements in the $3,667-$3,688 range based on estimated class sizes of 30,000-40,000 policyholders.⁵³

The most instructive precedent is Equitable AXA's $307.5 million settlement (2023) covering approximately 80,000 flexible-premium universal life policies issued from 2004 forward, yielding a per-policy settlement of approximately $3,844.⁵⁴ [VERIFIED: RP-Legal-litigation-update-2023] The Equitable settlement addressed crediting rate allegations similar to the Thompson case—plaintiffs alleged that illustrated crediting rates materially overstated actual policy performance due to insurer changes to caps, floors, and participation rates.⁵⁵

Critically, **no major life insurer has proceeded to trial and obtained a defense verdict** in IUL or universal life illustration litigation in the 2018-2025 period. This universal settlement pattern indicates either: (1) plaintiff leverage is overwhelming due to evidentiary support for misrepresentation claims; or (2) defense exposure at trial (including punitive damages risk) far exceeds settlement costs, making trial economically irrational.⁵⁶ The recent *Virani v. NLV Financial Corp.* dismissal (D. Vt. Dec. 2024) represents a single district court ruling at the motion to dismiss stage, not a defense verdict following discovery and trial.⁵⁷ [VERIFIED: InsuranceNewsNet-Dec-2024] That ruling's precedential value is limited: it binds only parties to that case in Vermont federal court, and the decision may be appealed.⁵⁸

**Application:** Here, Liberty Life's IUL policyholders received illustrations projecting 8.5% average annual crediting rates based on backtested proprietary index performance, while actual crediting from 2015-2023 averaged 4.2%—a 4.3 percentage point gap.⁵⁹ [VERIFIED: case-law-analyst-report-lines-83-89] Like the Transamerica and Equitable cases, the alleged misrepresentation concerns non-guaranteed elements whose actual performance materially diverged from illustrated projections. The case-law-analyst report documents that this divergence resulted from: (1) proprietary index crediting caps (typically 10-12%) preventing participation in strong market years; (2) zero-percent floors in down markets (which appear protective but reduce long-term averaging); and (3) participation rate reductions from initial 100% to subsequent 40-60%.⁶⁰

The estimated class size of 12,000-15,000 IUL policyholders places Liberty Life's exposure within the range of settled cases. Applying the median per-policy settlement of $3,750 from comparable cases:⁶¹

Industry data demonstrates that pre-certification settlements achieve 25-40% discounts from post-certification values because defendants retain leverage from the possibility of certification denial.⁶³ Applying a 30-35% pre-certification discount to the $37.1M mid-range post-certification value yields a pre-certification settlement target of **$24M-$26M**.⁶⁴

Actuaries applying ASOP 24 standards could defensibly project higher crediting rates during the low-interest-rate environment of 2010-2015 when equity markets showed strong recovery.⁷¹ The subsequent underperformance (4.2% actual 2015-2023) resulted from unforeseeable market conditions—the prolonged low interest rate environment, equity market volatility, and insurer responses (cap reductions, participation rate adjustments) to manage risk.⁷²

There is a 25-40% probability that Liberty Life's defenses succeed, based on: (1) the strength of non-guaranteed element disclaimers in policy contracts; (2) regulatory approval of illustration forms; (3) one favorable district court ruling in *Virani*; and (4) the reasonable-at-the-time defense for actuarial assumptions made in 2010-2015.⁷³ However, the counter-analysis faces significant headwinds. **Section IV.C (Securities Regulation)** at ¶78-82 documents that Liberty Life faces a pattern of FINRA suitability violations that directly strengthen plaintiffs' Thompson class action claims.⁹⁰ The October 2023 FINRA examination identified three registered representatives who sold variable universal life (VUL) policies to customers aged 75+ with limited income, violating FINRA Rule 2111's suitability requirements.⁹¹ [VERIFIED: research-plan-line-391] FINRA imposed a $75,000 fine and required enhanced supervision procedures.⁹²

While VUL and IUL are different products (VUL contains securities, IUL does not), the FINRA findings are probative evidence of systemic sales supervision deficiencies at Liberty Life. Plaintiffs' class counsel will seek to introduce the FINRA examination findings at trial or in certification briefing to demonstrate that Liberty Life maintained inadequate training, supervision, and compliance systems for complex life insurance products generally.⁹³ Under Federal Rule of Evidence 404(b), evidence of "other acts" is admissible to prove "motive, opportunity, intent, preparation, plan, knowledge, identity, absence of mistake, or lack of accident."⁹⁴ [VERIFIED: FRE-404(b)-Cornell-LII]

The FINRA pattern also creates exposure to a FINRA "cause examination"—a comprehensive regulatory investigation triggered when member firms exhibit patterns of compliance failures.⁹⁵ Section IV.C at ¶85 estimates that a cause examination would cost $950K-$3.05M in remediation expenses and carries a 40-60% probability of occurring within 18 months if the FINRA suitability pattern continues.⁹⁶ Enhanced supervision requirements ($425,000 initial implementation + $200,000/year ongoing) are recommended as a closing condition to mitigate both the Thompson class action evidentiary issues and future FINRA examination risk.⁹⁷

**Market Conduct Examination → IV.A (RBC Capital & Insurance Regulation):**

**Section IV.A (RBC Capital & Insurance Regulation)** at ¶34-37 documents that the Nebraska Department of Insurance conducted a market conduct examination of Liberty Life in 2024, with preliminary findings expected by Q2 2026.⁹⁸ Market conduct examinations evaluate insurer compliance with state insurance laws, including illustration practices under Nebraska Regulation Chapter 72.⁹⁹ If the examination identifies IUL illustration deficiencies—such as failure to update illustrations when crediting rates declined, use of unsupportable actuarial assumptions, or inadequate agent training on illustration disclosures—these regulatory findings will be discoverable in the Thompson litigation and admissible as evidence of knowledge or reckless disregard.¹⁰⁰

Conversely, if Liberty Life implements a Corrective Action Plan addressing illustration practices **before** the Thompson mediation in Q2 2026, defense counsel can argue that the company has taken remedial measures to prevent future violations, potentially reducing settlement exposure.¹⁰¹ Federal Rule of Evidence 407 generally prohibits admission of subsequent remedial measures to prove negligence, but the rule contains an exception when remedial measures are offered to prove "ownership, control, or the feasibility of precautionary measures," which may apply in a class action settlement negotiation context.¹⁰² [VERIFIED: FRE-407-Cornell-LII]

**Contract Impact:** The Form A acquisition approval application to the Nebraska Department of Insurance should coordinate with the market conduct examination timeline. If the DOI's final market conduct report is issued before Form A approval, any required Corrective Action Plan should be implemented as a condition of approval. The Purchase Agreement should state: "Seller shall cooperate fully with the Nebraska Department of Insurance market conduct examination and implement any Corrective Action Plan required by the DOI at Seller's expense. Purchaser's obligation to close is conditioned upon the DOI not issuing a cease-and-desist order or other regulatory action that would materially impair the Business."¹⁰³

**Agent Training Deficiencies → IV.F (Employment & Agent Retention):**

**Section IV.F (Employment & Agent Retention)** at ¶56-60 identifies inadequate agent training and compensation structures that incentivize aggressive IUL illustrations as root causes of the Thompson litigation exposure.¹⁰⁴ Liberty Life's independent agent distribution model relies on 450-500 active producing agents, many of whom sell multiple carriers' products and may lack sophisticated understanding of IUL index mechanics (caps, floors, participation rates, proprietary index construction).¹⁰⁵ Agents receive higher commissions for IUL sales (80-110% of first-year premium) compared to term life (40-55%), creating financial incentives to emphasize IUL's illustrated upside potential while minimizing downside risks.¹⁰⁶

The employment-labor-analyst report recommends enhanced product training requirements and Written Supervisory Procedures (WSP) revisions as closing conditions:¹⁰⁷

1. **Product-Specific Certification:** Require all agents selling IUL products to complete 8-hour product training covering index mechanics, non-guaranteed elements, illustration assumptions, and regulatory requirements under Nebraska Chapter 72.¹⁰⁸ Certification must be renewed annually. Cost: $125,000 initial development + $50,000/year ongoing.¹⁰⁹

2. **Illustration Review:** Implement pre-sale compliance review of all IUL illustrations exceeding $500,000 face amount or for purchasers aged 65+, with approval required from licensed compliance officer. Cost: $150,000/year (0.5 FTE compliance officer).¹¹⁰

3. **Compensation Restructuring:** Reduce IUL first-year commission from 100% to 70% of premium, with 30% deferred and paid over years 2-4 contingent on policy persistency (remaining in force). This aligns agent incentives with long-term policy performance rather than initial sales. Revenue impact: Minimal (deferred commissions remain expense, just paid over longer period).¹¹¹

These training and supervision enhancements serve dual purposes: (1) they may reduce the Thompson settlement exposure by demonstrating to mediators and the court that Liberty Life has implemented systemic reforms; and (2) they reduce future litigation risk from post-acquisition IUL sales. The insurance-coverage-analyst's recommended tail coverage ($1M-$2.25M for 6 years) covers pre-acquisition claims only—post-acquisition claims require adequate going-forward compliance infrastructure.¹¹²
