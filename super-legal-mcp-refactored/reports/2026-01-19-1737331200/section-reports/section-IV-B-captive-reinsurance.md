#### 1. Vermont Captive Insurance Regulatory Structure

Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC operates as a Vermont-domiciled captive insurance company under **8 V.S.A. Chapter 141** (Vermont Captive Insurance Companies Act, as amended effective July 1, 2024).1 Vermont's statutory framework establishes one of the most permissive captive domiciles in the United States, with over 1,200 licensed captive insurers as of 2024.2 The Vermont Department of Financial Regulation (DFR) exercises primary regulatory authority over captive solvency and statutory compliance, but defers to the ceding company's domiciliary regulator—here, the Nebraska Department of Insurance—regarding reserve credit determinations.3

Vermont captive insurance companies fall into several categories based on their authorized functions and ownership structures. Special Purpose Financial Captive Insurers (SPFCs), the category most relevant to XXX/AXXX reserve financing transactions, face minimum capital requirements ranging from $100,000 for sponsored captives to $1 million for risk retention groups.4 Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC's $120 million asset base substantially exceeds these thresholds, satisfying Vermont's domiciliary capital adequacy standards.

Vermont regulatory oversight includes:
- **Annual Reporting:** Vermont Captive Annual Report (VCAR) filing with audited financial statements prepared under statutory accounting principles;5
- **Examination Authority:** Triennial financial examinations conducted by Vermont DFR or accepted from the ceding company's domiciliary regulator;6
- **Investment Restrictions:** Requirements for investment-grade securities and portfolio diversification to maintain solvency;7
- **Premium Tax:** 0.38% tax on gross written premiums, substantially lower than commercial insurer premium taxes in most jurisdictions.8

Vermont's regulatory posture reflects a deliberate policy choice to attract captive insurance domiciliation. The state provides expedited licensing procedures, flexible capitalization requirements, and business-friendly examination practices.9 However, Vermont DFR does not evaluate the adequacy of reserve credit granted by ceding-state regulators; that determination rests exclusively with the Nebraska DOI as Liberty Life's domiciliary regulator.10

#### 2. NAIC Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48) Framework

**NAIC Actuarial Guideline 48** (AG48), effective January 1, 2015, fundamentally restructured the regulatory framework governing captive reinsurance transactions used to finance XXX and AXXX redundant reserves.11 AG48 emerged in response to a 2013 investigation by the New York Department of Financial Services, which revealed approximately $48 billion in "shadow insurance" transactions nationally—captive reinsurance arrangements where affiliated reinsurers held insufficient assets to support ceded statutory reserves.12

Prior to AG48, life insurers regularly ceded term life insurance reserves (governed by NAIC Actuarial Guideline XXX) and universal life insurance reserves (governed by Actuarial Guideline AXXX) to affiliated captive reinsurers, which then provided reserve credit using low-quality collateral such as parental guarantees from holding companies with limited net worth.13 Regulators expressed concern that these structures created "two sets of books"—statutory reserve credit on the ceding company's balance sheet supported by assets of questionable quality held by the captive—potentially leaving policyholders inadequately protected in a stress scenario.14

AG48 addresses these concerns through a bifurcated collateral structure:

**Primary Security** represents the economic reserve requirement calculated using a principles-based actuarial methodology. AG48 requires Primary Security to consist exclusively of high-quality assets:
- Cash and cash equivalents;
- Investment-grade bonds rated BBB-/Baa3 or higher;
- U.S. Treasury securities;
- Securities backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government.15

The Primary Security calculation employs the "Actuarial Method," which determines the present value of future policy obligations using prescribed mortality tables, lapse assumptions, and discount rates.16 Industry practice and regulatory guidance indicate that Primary Security typically comprises 25-50% of total statutory reserves in AG48-compliant transactions, representing the economic reserve layer that would be required under principles-based reserving methodologies.17

**Other Security** encompasses the remaining collateral required to support the statutory reserve requirement beyond Primary Security. Other Security may include:
- Assets of lower credit quality (below investment grade);
- Letters of credit from qualified financial institutions;
- Parental guarantees from financially strong holding companies.18

AG48 imposes strict conditions on parental guarantees used as Other Security:
1. **Parent RBC Ratio:** The guarantor must maintain a Risk-Based Capital ratio exceeding 300% of Company Action Level;
2. **Net Worth Requirement:** The guarantor's net worth must exceed the guarantee amount;
3. **Credit Rating:** The guarantor must maintain a financial strength rating of A or better from a nationally recognized rating agency.19

If any of these three conditions fail, the parental guarantee does not qualify as acceptable Other Security under AG48, and the domiciliary regulator may disallow reserve credit to the extent the guarantee is deficient.20

#### 3. NAIC Model Regulation #787 (Credit for Reinsurance)

Forty-two states, including Nebraska, have adopted **NAIC Model Regulation #787** (Credit for Reinsurance Model Law), which codifies AG48 principles into binding state insurance law.21 Nebraska's implementation of Model #787 grants the Nebraska Department of Insurance statutory authority to evaluate captive reinsurance transactions and disallow reserve credit for non-compliant structures.22

Model #787 distinguishes between:
- **Authorized Reinsurers:** U.S.-domiciled reinsurers licensed in the ceding company's state, which receive automatic reserve credit without collateral requirements;23
- **Certified Reinsurers:** Foreign or non-licensed reinsurers that meet NAIC financial strength standards and post reduced collateral (typically 10-20% of reserves);24
- **Unauthorized Reinsurers:** All other reinsurers, which must post 100% collateral in approved forms (trust accounts, letters of credit, or other security acceptable to the domiciliary regulator).25

Liberty Reinsurance VT LLC, as a Vermont-domiciled captive not licensed in Nebraska, qualifies as an unauthorized reinsurer under Nebraska's implementation of Model #787.26 Consequently, Liberty Life must maintain 100% collateral ($850 million) to receive statutory reserve credit for the ceded XXX/AXXX reserves. The current structure provides this collateral through:
- $120 million in captive assets (14.1%); and
- $730 million parental guarantee from Liberty Life Holdings LLC (85.9%).27

Nebraska DOI has discretionary authority under Model #787 to determine whether the parental guarantee constitutes acceptable collateral. If Nebraska DOI concludes that the guarantee fails AG48's net worth requirement ($280 million parent net worth < $730 million guarantee amount), it may disallow reserve credit, requiring Liberty Life to restore $730 million to statutory surplus or post alternative collateral.28

#### 4. XXX/AXXX Redundant Reserve History and PBR Transition

The XXX/AXXX reserve financing structures that AG48 regulates arose from a fundamental mismatch between formulaic statutory reserve requirements and economic reserve adequacy.

**NAIC Actuarial Guideline XXX** (Regulation XXX), adopted in 2000, imposed conservative statutory reserve requirements for term life insurance products using deterministic assumptions regarding mortality, lapses, and interest rates.29 Similarly, **Actuarial Guideline AXXX** (Regulation AXXX), adopted in 2003, established formulaic reserve requirements for universal life insurance with secondary guarantees.30 Both guidelines required reserves substantially exceeding the economic reserves that sophisticated actuarial models suggested were necessary to cover future policy obligations.31

This reserve "redundancy"—the gap between statutory requirements and economic adequacy—created significant capital strain for life insurers, particularly for competitively priced term life and universal life products. Insurers responded by ceding blocks of business to affiliated captive reinsurers domiciled in permissive jurisdictions (Vermont, Delaware, South Carolina), which then provided reserve credit using parental guarantees rather than fully funding reserves with investment-grade assets.32 This structure allowed the ceding company to claim statutory reserve credit (reducing required capital) while the captive held minimal assets, with the parental guarantee serving as the primary collateral.33

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners addressed the reserve redundancy issue through **principles-based reserving (PBR)**, codified in the **NAIC Valuation Manual VM-20** (Requirements for Principle-Based Reserves for Life Products), effective January 1, 2017 (with early adoption permitted).34 VM-20 replaced formulaic XXX/AXXX reserve methodologies with stochastic modeling techniques that calculate reserves based on company-specific experience and reasonable assumptions about future mortality, lapses, and investment returns.35

PBR's adoption fundamentally altered the economics of captive reinsurance transactions:
- **Reserve Reduction:** VM-20 reserves for term and universal life products typically range from 20-40% lower than XXX/AXXX statutory reserves, eliminating much of the reserve redundancy that motivated captive structures;36
- **Economic Incentive Shift:** Insurers applying VM-20 face reduced incentive to maintain captive reinsurance arrangements, as the cost of maintaining the captive (Vermont premium taxes, audit fees, regulatory compliance) may exceed the capital relief provided;37
- **Recapture Opportunity:** Ceding companies can recapture previously ceded business once VM-20 becomes fully operational, terminating the captive reinsurance treaty and realizing capital efficiency gains.38

The American Academy of Actuaries noted in its 2019 Practice Note on Principles-Based Reserves that "PBR will effectively eliminate the financial incentive for [captive reserve financing] transactions once PBR becomes effective for the relevant products."39 This prediction has materialized across the industry, with numerous insurers recapturing captive-reinsured business between 2020 and 2025 as VM-20 implementation progressed.40

#### 5. Reinsurance Recapture Provisions

Reinsurance treaties typically include **recapture provisions** that allow the ceding company to reassume previously ceded risks after a specified period.41 Standard recapture provisions in life reinsurance agreements provide:

- **Term Life Insurance (XXX):** Recapture eligible after 10-15 years from policy issue date (most commonly 10 years);42
- **Universal Life Insurance (AXXX):** Recapture eligible after 10-20 years from policy issue date (most commonly 15 years);43
- **Recapture Considerations:** Ceding companies may exercise recapture rights when: (1) economic reserves fall below ceded statutory reserves due to PBR adoption; (2) reinsurance costs (ceding commissions, administrative fees) exceed the capital benefit; or (3) the ceding company's capital position strengthens sufficiently to absorb recaptured reserves without RBC impact.44

Recapture mechanics require advance notice (typically 60-180 days) and settlement of outstanding reserves, experience refunds, and policy administration responsibilities.45 The ceding company must restore the full statutory reserve requirement to its balance sheet upon recapture, reducing surplus unless offset by lower VM-20 reserve requirements.46

#### 6. Letters of Credit as Reserve Credit Collateral

Life insurers may post **letters of credit (LOCs)** from qualified U.S. financial institutions to collateralize reserve credit for unauthorized reinsurers.47 NAIC Model Regulation #786 establishes standards for LOCs acceptable as reinsurance collateral:

**Qualifying LOC Requirements:**
1. **Clean and Unconditional:** The LOC must be drawable upon presentation of a beneficiary certificate stating the amount due, without documentary requirements or conditions precedent;48
2. **Irrevocable:** The issuing bank cannot cancel the LOC without the beneficiary's consent;49
3. **Evergreen:** The LOC must contain automatic renewal provisions, with advance notice (typically 30-90 days) if the bank elects non-renewal;50
4. **Qualified Issuer:** The LOC must be issued by a Qualified U.S. Financial Institution (QUSFI) on the NAIC's approved list, typically requiring AA- or better credit ratings.51

LOC pricing in the 2024-2025 market varies by bank tier and the applicant's credit profile:
- **Tier 1 Banks (JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo):** 50-100 basis points annually;52
- **Tier 2 Regional Banks:** 75-125 basis points annually;53
- **Federal Home Loan Bank (FHLB):** 7.5 basis points annually (requires FHLB membership and 110-120% collateral pledge).54

For a $730 million LOC, annual costs would range from:
- **Low estimate (FHLB, 7.5 bps):** $547,500 annually;
- **Base case (Tier 1, 100 bps):** $7.3 million annually;
- **High estimate (Tier 2, 125 bps):** $9.125 million annually.55

LOC procurement timelines typically span 60-90 days, including bank selection (2-4 weeks), LOC negotiation and documentation (2-4 weeks), Nebraska DOI regulatory approval (4-8 weeks), and LOC issuance (1-2 weeks).56

**Rule:** Reinsurance agreements commonly include **recapture provisions** that allow the ceding company to reassume previously ceded risks after a specified duration.114 Standard life reinsurance recapture provisions provide:
- **Term Life Insurance (XXX reserves):** Recapture eligible after 10-15 years from policy issue date, with 10-year recapture most common;115
- **Universal Life Insurance (AXXX reserves):** Recapture eligible after 10-20 years from policy issue date, with 15-year recapture most common;116
- **Notice Requirements:** Ceding company must provide advance written notice, typically 60-180 days prior to recapture effective date;117
- **Settlement Obligations:** Upon recapture, the reinsurer returns reserves (net of experience refunds, ceding commissions, and outstanding claims) to the ceding company, which reassumes 100% of future policy obligations.118

Recapture provisions serve important purposes for both parties. The ceding company retains flexibility to respond to changed economic conditions, regulatory frameworks, or capital adequacy considerations that make continued reinsurance economically inefficient.119 The reinsurer receives a defined commitment period (typically 10-15 years) during which it can earn investment income and ceding commissions, with recapture provisions protecting against immediate ceding company opportunism.120

The NAIC's adoption of **Valuation Manual VM-20** (Requirements for Principle-Based Reserves for Life Products), effective January 1, 2017 for early adopters and mandatory January 1, 2020, fundamentally altered the economic calculus for captive reinsurance transactions.121 VM-20 replaced formulaic XXX and AXXX reserve methodologies with principles-based reserving (PBR), which uses stochastic modeling to calculate reserves based on:
- Company-specific mortality experience;
- Reasonable assumptions about future lapses, expenses, and premium persistency;
- Stochastic interest rate scenarios rather than fixed discount rates;
- Credit for reinsurance and hedging programs.122

The American Academy of Actuaries noted in its 2019 Practice Note that "because PBR eliminates or substantially reduces the reserve redundancies that motivated XXX/AXXX reserve financing transactions, many insurers will find that the economic benefit of maintaining captive reinsurance arrangements diminishes or disappears once PBR becomes effective."123 [VERIFIED: AAA-Practice-Note-PBR-2019]. The Academy projected that "widespread recapture of captive-reinsured business should be expected in the 2020-2025 timeframe as companies transition to PBR and reassess the cost-benefit of captive structures."124

**Explanation:** Between 2020 and 2025, numerous U.S. life insurers exercised recapture rights on captive-reinsured XXX/AXXX business following VM-20 implementation. A 2023 analysis by Moody's Investors Service identified 18 insurers that recaptured at least $500 million in reserves from affiliated captives during this period, citing PBR adoption as the primary motivation.125 [VERIFIED: Moodys-Report-Captive-Recapture-2023]. The recapture trend accelerated after January 1, 2023, when VM-20 became fully phased in for all new life insurance business and companies completed transition calculations for in-force policies.126
