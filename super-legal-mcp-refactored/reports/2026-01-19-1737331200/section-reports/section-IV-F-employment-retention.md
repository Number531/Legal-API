### A. Legal Framework

Agent retention in life insurance mergers and acquisitions implicates five distinct areas of law: post-employment restrictive covenants, FINRA broker-dealer registration transfers, state insurance producer licensing, federal and state employment notification requirements, and retention compensation structures. This section addresses the controlling authority governing Liberty Life Insurance Company's 650 captive agents and 420 FINRA-registered representatives.

#### 1. Post-Employment Restrictive Covenants - Nebraska Law

**Nebraska Common Law Standard for Non-Compete Enforceability**

Nebraska courts enforce non-compete agreements only if they meet three cumulative requirements: (1) the restriction must be reasonable in the sense that it is not injurious to the public; (2) the restriction must be no greater than reasonably necessary to protect the employer in some legitimate business interest; and (3) the restriction must not be unduly harsh and oppressive on the party against whom it is asserted.¹ [VERIFIED:Polly-v-Hilderman-225-Neb-662]

**Critical Limitation: Traditional Non-Competes Unenforceable for Employees**

Nebraska courts have adopted a strict view concluding that post-employment restrictions must be no greater than reasonably necessary to protect an employer's legitimate interest. Traditional non-compete clauses with geographic restrictions are **not allowed** in post-employment restrictions on employees.² Rather, Nebraska courts recognize only a **narrow customer non-solicitation restriction**, which must be properly limited to prevent solicitation of only those customers that the employee specifically did business with and had personal contact.³ [VERIFIED:McGrath-North-Nebraska-Non-Compete-Analysis]

In *Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co.*, 225 Neb. 662, 407 N.W.2d 751 (1987), the Nebraska Supreme Court found a covenant not to compete unreasonable and unenforceable because it attempted to restrict Polly from soliciting or working for the employer's clients **with whom Polly did not work and did not even know**.⁴ The court held that "an employer has a legitimate business interest in protection against a former employee's competition by improper and unfair means, but is **not entitled to protection against ordinary competition** from a former employee."⁵ [VERIFIED:Polly-407-NW2d-751]

**No Blue Pencil Reformation**

Unlike many states, Nebraska courts **will not modify an overbroad agreement**. Rather, they will **declare it to be void**.⁶ Nebraska is in the minority of states in that the courts will not revise an overly-broad covenant to make it enforceable; if one piece of the restriction is found unreasonable, the entire covenant will be unenforceable.⁷ [VERIFIED:McGrath-North-Blue-Pencil-Analysis]

**Recent Precedent: Sisk v. Scripps Media, Inc.**

In *Sisk v. Scripps Media, Inc.*, 2024 WL 1175140 (D. Neb. Mar. 18, 2024), the federal district court granted the plaintiff's motion for injunction against enforcement of both non-competition and non-solicitation provisions.⁸ The court found the non-solicitation clause unenforceable because it was not limited to restricting Sisk from soliciting clients with whom he actually did business and had personal contact. The language not only restricted Sisk from soliciting customers with whom he had personal contact but with whom Scripps "had an actual or prospective relationship," which the court concluded **greatly exceeded the permissible scope**.⁹ [VERIFIED:Sisk-2024-WL-1175140]

**Multi-State Choice of Law Issues**

LLIC's 650 captive agents operate across 38 states plus the District of Columbia. When an agent departs and begins competing in a different state than Nebraska, choice of law issues arise. While employment agreement choice-of-law clauses designating Nebraska law will typically be honored, states with strong anti-non-compete public policies may refuse to apply Nebraska law.¹⁰ [VERIFIED:Seyfarth-50-State-Non-Compete-Reference]

**States Categorically Prohibiting Employee Non-Competes:**

California (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600), North Dakota (N.D. Cent. Code § 9-08-06), Oklahoma (15 O.S. § 219A), and Minnesota (Minn. Stat. § 181.988, effective July 1, 2023 prospectively) void employee non-competes regardless of reasonableness.¹¹ [VERIFIED:California-BPC-16600]

**Garden Leave as Enforceable Alternative**

Garden leave provisions—whereby an employee remains employed (W-2 status) for 6-12 months post-notice of departure, employer continues paying base salary plus benefits during the restriction period, and employee is prohibited from working for competitor or soliciting customers during paid leave—are generally more enforceable than post-employment non-competes because: (1) employment relationship continues (employer pays consideration throughout restriction period), (2) no "restraint of trade" issue exists (agent is employed, not restricted post-employment), (3) limited duration is more reasonable (6-12 months vs. 12-24 month non-compete), and (4) less "oppressive" on employee (receives income during restriction period).¹² [INFERRED:Garden-Leave-Enforceability-Doctrine]

#### 2. FINRA Broker-Dealer Registration Transfer Requirements

**FINRA Rule 1017 - Continuing Membership Application**

FINRA Rule 1017 governs Applications for Approval of Change in Ownership, Control, or Business Operations. For acquisitions, the broker-dealer entity must file a **Form CMA (Continuing Membership Application)** with FINRA's Membership Application Program (MAP) Group.¹³ [VERIFIED:FINRA-Rule-1017]

**Filing Deadline and Review Timeline**

A member firm must file Form CMA for approval of a change in ownership or control **at least 30 days prior to such change**.¹⁴ This gives FINRA an opportunity to conduct a preliminary analysis. Review timeframes are 75 days if expedited review is approved by FINRA MAP or 180 days if subject to regular review.¹⁵ [VERIFIED:FINRA-CMA-Filing-Timeline]

**Critical Distinction: Advance Notice vs. Prior Approval**

FINRA Rule 1017 requires **advance notice—but not prior approval**—of changes of ownership or control. A firm may effect the change before the final written decision is issued, but FINRA MAP may impose interim restrictions on unaffected changes that would remain in effect until the application is decided or the restriction is lifted.¹⁶ [VERIFIED:FINRA-Ownership-Change-Guidance]

**Mass Transfer Program**

The Mass Transfer Program allows for the transfer of registration information in mass for qualified individuals when a firm is involved in acquisitions, asset purchases, consolidations, mergers, and successions involving **at least 50 individuals**.¹⁷ Representatives are systematically terminated with the predecessor firm and registered with the successor firm without requiring submission of Form U4 filings, Form U5 filings, and fingerprint cards.¹⁸ [VERIFIED:FINRA-Mass-Transfer-Program]

**Continuing Education Compliance**

Individuals are required to complete continuing education annually for each registration they hold. The annual Regulatory Element CE must be completed by **December 31 each year** for all covered persons registered with FINRA.¹⁹ Registered persons who fail to complete their Regulatory Element requirement by the annual deadline will be **automatically designated as CE inactive** by FINRA.²⁰ [VERIFIED:FINRA-CE-Requirements-Rule-1240]

#### 3. State Insurance Producer Licensing

**Nebraska Producer Licensing Framework**

Nebraska's insurance producer licensing is governed by the Insurance Producers Licensing Act, Neb. Rev. Stat. §§ 44-4047 to 44-4069.²¹ According to Neb. Rev. Stat. § 44-4061(2), it is the responsibility of the insurer to file a notice of appointment with the Director within **15 days from the date the agency contract is executed or the first insurance application is submitted**.²² [VERIFIED:Nebraska-Rev-Stat-44-4061]

**Multi-State Licensing Requirements**

The majority of states have adopted the NAIC Producer Licensing Model Act (Model #218), providing uniform standards for producer licensing, appointment, and termination.²³ Most states require **30-60 days notification** for appointment changes, address changes, or business entity modifications.²⁴ [VERIFIED:NAIC-Model-218-Producer-Licensing]

In a stock purchase transaction where the LLIC entity continues to exist, appointments may remain valid without re-filing. However, some states may require notification of ownership change or updated business entity information.²⁵ [INFERRED:Stock-Purchase-Appointment-Continuity]

#### 4. Federal WARN Act

**Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act**

The WARN Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, protects workers by requiring employers with 100 or more employees to provide at least **60 calendar days advance written notice** of a plant closing and mass layoff affecting 50 or more employees at a single site of employment.²⁶ [VERIFIED:WARN-Act-29-USC-2101]

**Triggering Events**

WARN is triggered when certain employment losses occur at a single site during a 30-day period: (1) **Plant Closing** - permanent or temporary shutdown resulting in employment loss for 50 or more employees, or (2) **Mass Layoff** - employment loss of at least 33% of employees and at least 50 employees, OR 500 employees.²⁷ [VERIFIED:WARN-Act-Triggering-Thresholds]

**90-Day Aggregation Rule**

WARN also applies when an employer conducts a series of smaller, but related layoffs **within 90 days** that collectively reach the WARN thresholds over the 90-day period.²⁸ When an employer makes a reduction in force, it must look forward and backward 90 days from each employment loss to determine whether WARN obligations arise.²⁹ [VERIFIED:WARN-90-Day-Aggregation-Rule]

**Nebraska State Law**

Nebraska **does not have a state "mini-WARN" law** with additional protections beyond the federal WARN Act.³⁰ Unlike states such as California, New York, or New Jersey that have enacted stricter state-level WARN laws, Nebraska relies solely on the federal WARN Act.³¹ [VERIFIED:Nebraska-No-Mini-WARN]

**Damages for Non-Compliance**

Employers who fail to comply with WARN's notice provisions are liable to employees for back pay and benefits for each day of violation up to a maximum of 60 days, plus civil penalties of up to $500 per day to the local government.³² The Third Circuit has held that employees may receive wages for **60 calendar days** (not just working days), though there is some circuit split on this issue.³³ [VERIFIED:WARN-Damages-Calculation]

#### 5. Retention Bonus Structures and Tax Treatment

**IRC § 162 Business Expense Deduction**

Retention bonuses paid to employees constitute ordinary and necessary business expenses deductible under IRC § 162(a) when paid or accrued.³⁴ For accrual-method taxpayers, retention bonuses are deductible in the year the liability becomes fixed and determinable, even if payment occurs in a subsequent year.³⁵ [VERIFIED:IRC-162-Business-Expense]

**IRC § 409A Deferred Compensation Rules**

Retention bonuses with vesting schedules must comply with IRC § 409A governing nonqualified deferred compensation. Two-tranche retention bonuses (50% at 12 months, 50% at 24 months) constitute "short-term deferrals" exempt from § 409A if payment occurs within 2½ months after the end of the employer's tax year in which vesting occurs.³⁶ [VERIFIED:IRC-409A-Short-Term-Deferral-Exception]

**Clawback Provisions**

Retention bonus agreements commonly include clawback provisions requiring repayment if the employee voluntarily terminates or is terminated for cause before the vesting date. Clawback provisions must be carefully drafted to avoid triggering § 409A penalties.³⁷ [INFERRED:Retention-Bonus-Clawback-409A-Compliance]

#### B.5 WARN Act Compliance and Integration Planning

**Conclusion:** Federal WARN Act risk is **LOW** if integration layoffs are properly structured. LLIC's 2,800 employees trigger WARN coverage, but staggered terminations beyond the 90-day aggregation window, voluntary separation incentives, and attrition/hiring freeze can achieve 15-20% headcount reduction over 12-18 months without triggering 60-day notice requirement. If >150 employees are laid off within 90 days, **liability exposure is $1.9M**. **Exposure:** $0-$1.9M (5% probability if aggressive integration pursued). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: WARN Act statutory damages formula 29 U.S.C. § 2104].

**Rule:** The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN), 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, protects workers by requiring employers with 100 or more employees to provide at least **60 calendar days advance written notice** of a plant closing and mass layoff affecting 50 or more employees at a single site of employment.⁹⁷ [VERIFIED:WARN-29-USC-2101-2109]

WARN is triggered when certain employment losses occur at a single site during a 30-day period: (1) **Plant Closing** - permanent or temporary shutdown resulting in employment loss for 50 or more employees during any 30-day period, or (2) **Mass Layoff** - employment loss at a single site during a 30-day period of at least 33% of employees (excluding part-time) and at least 50 employees, OR 500 employees (excluding part-time).⁹⁸ [VERIFIED:WARN-Triggering-Thresholds-Plant-Mass]

WARN also applies when an employer conducts a series of smaller, but related layoffs **within 90 days** that collectively reach the WARN thresholds over the 90-day period.⁹⁹ When an employer makes a reduction in force, it must look forward and backward 90 days from each employment loss to determine whether WARN obligations arise.¹⁰⁰ [VERIFIED:WARN-90-Day-Aggregation-Rule-Fisher-Phillips]

**Explanation:** Nebraska **does not have a state "mini-WARN" law** with additional protections beyond the federal WARN Act.¹⁰¹ Legislative bills (L.B. 1134, L.B. 261, and LB 472) proposing a Nebraska Workers Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act were indefinitely postponed, so these proposed state requirements never became law.¹⁰² [VERIFIED:Nebraska-WARN-Legislative-History]

Employers who fail to comply with WARN's notice provisions are liable to employees for back pay and benefits for each day of violation up to a maximum of 60 days, plus civil penalties of up to $500 per day to the local government.¹⁰³ The Third Circuit has held that employees may receive wages for **60 calendar days** (not just working days), though there is some circuit split on this issue.¹⁰⁴ [VERIFIED:WARN-60-Calendar-Days-Anderson-Kill]

**Critical Distinction:** WARN Act applies to **employment loss** (terminations, layoffs, constructive discharge), **not voluntary resignations**.¹⁰⁵ Agent attrition due to retention program failure (voluntary departures to competitors) is not a WARN event.¹⁰⁶ [VERIFIED:WARN-Voluntary-Quits-Excluded]
