### Detailed Findings Digest

#### 1. Surplus Notes: 100% Statutory Capital + Tax Deductibility

**Regulatory Treatment (NAIC SSAP No. 41):**

Surplus notes receive **100% statutory capital credit** despite being debt instruments. State insurance regulators treat surplus notes as equity because they are subordinated to all policyholders and creditors, and require Nebraska DOI approval before issuance and before each interest/principal payment. Under GAAP, surplus notes are liabilities, but under Statutory Accounting Principles (SAP), they count as surplus. The NAIC's 2024-2025 updates to SSAP No. 41 expanded treatment to include "capital notes" (hybrid instruments with deferrable interest), but traditional surplus notes remain the core instrument.

**Federal Tax Treatment (IRC ¬ß 163):**

Despite regulatory equity classification, surplus notes are treated as **debt for federal tax purposes** if they possess sufficiently debt-like features: fixed maturity (30 years), fixed interest rate (10%), unconditional payment obligations, labeling as "notes," and no management participation rights. Courts have held that subordination and regulatory approval requirements do not automatically convert surplus notes to equity. Interest payments are **fully deductible** under IRC ¬ß 163(a). The IRS has issued private letter rulings treating surplus notes as debt and excluding them from equity base calculations.

**Debt vs. Equity Classification Analysis:**

Multi-factor tests weigh toward **debt classification**. Favorable factors include: written unconditional promise to pay, fixed maturity, fixed rate, labeled as notes, and no participation in management. Unfavorable factors include subordination and regulatory payment approval. Investment-grade rating (if obtained) strengthens debt classification. Recommended mitigation: obtain tax opinion letter from national tax counsel affirming debt treatment.

**Section 163(j) Business Interest Limitation:**

IRC ¬ß 163(j) limits business interest deductions to 30% of adjusted taxable income (ATI). For $150M surplus notes at 10%:
- Annual interest: $15 million
- ATI limitation: 30% √ó $185M = $55.5 million allowable
- **Result: $15M interest fully deductible** (well within limitation)

**Nebraska Statutory Requirements (Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß¬ß 44-220, 44-221):**

- **Issuance approval:** Nebraska DOI must approve application to issue surplus notes (estimated 60-120 days)
- **Payment approval:** Each interest and principal payment requires Nebraska DOI approval
- **Approval standard:** Surplus must be at least **double** the payment amount ($30M surplus required for $15M interest payment)
- **LLIC compliance:** Current surplus $1.85B; post-injection $2.0B ‚Üí **$1.97B cushion** above $30M minimum

#### 2. Subordinated Debt: Tax Deductible but Insufficient Capital Credit

**Distinction from Surplus Notes:**

**NAIC Statutory Capital Treatment:**

Unlike surplus notes, traditional subordinated debt receives **limited or zero capital credit** (varies by state). Factors affecting treatment: degree of subordination, term to maturity (>10 years favored), and state regulator discretion. Most states provide 0-25% capital credit; best case 50%.

**Federal Tax Treatment:**

- Annual interest: $13.5M

#### 3. Contributed Capital (Equity): 100% Capital Credit but Zero Tax Benefit

**Regulatory Treatment:**

**RBC Impact:**

**Federal Tax Treatment (IRC ¬ß 162):**

Contributed capital does **not qualify as deductible business expense**. It is a capital transaction. When a parent makes a capital contribution to a subsidiary:
- **No deduction** at parent level (American Financial Holdings receives no tax benefit)
- **Basis increase:** Parent's tax basis in subsidiary stock increases by $150M
- **No income** at subsidiary level (LLIC does not recognize income)

IRS and courts have established: "Voluntary payments by a shareholder to a corporation to bolster its financial position are treated as a capital investment," not deductible expenses.

**Result:** **Zero tax benefit** from contributed capital.

### A. Surplus Notes: Tax and Regulatory Treatment

#### 1. Legal Definition and Regulatory Framework

Surplus notes are unsecured debt instruments subordinated to all claims by policyholders and creditors. Interest and principal payments on surplus notes are made only after approval has been granted by the commissioner of the state of domicile.¬π Under statutory accounting principles (SAP), surplus notes are classified as **equity**, not debt.¬≤ This treatment differs fundamentally from U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), where these instruments are treated as debt and reported as liabilities.¬≥

**Nebraska Statutory Requirements:**

Nebraska Revised Statute ¬ß 44-220 governs the issuance of surplus notes by domestic insurance companies.‚Å¥ Any domestic insurance company may borrow money for specified purposes and issue surplus notes, provided:
- Application has been made to the Nebraska Department of Insurance (DOI)
- Approval in writing is obtained from the Director of Insurance for the issuance of surplus notes in a stated maximum amount

Nebraska Revised Statute ¬ß 44-221 governs repayment requirements.‚Åµ For surplus notes issued **on or after September 9, 1995**:
- Principal and interest may be paid from available surplus funds **only when surplus over all liabilities is double the amount of principal and interest being paid**
- The company must first receive **prior approval of the Director of Insurance** for any such payments
- The director shall use standards set forth in Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-2136 relating to adequacy of surplus in determining whether to approve payments

#### 2. NAIC Statutory Accounting Treatment - 100% Capital Credit

**SSAP No. 41 (Surplus Notes):**

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) provides accounting guidance for surplus notes in Statement of Statutory Accounting Principles (SSAP) No. 41.‚Å∂ State insurance regulators treat surplus notes as statutory capital because they are unsecured and are at the bottom level of insurers' capital structure, subordinate to policyholders, claimant and beneficiary claims, and to all other classes of creditors.‚Å∑

Key provisions:
- Surplus notes receive **100% statutory capital credit** (counted as surplus, not liabilities)
- These debt-instruments are permitted to be reported as capital, not as debt, due to the subordinate nature of the notes
- They require approval by the commissioner of the state of domicile before original issuance and before interest and principal repayments can be made

**2024-2025 SSAP No. 41 Updates:**

On November 17, 2024, the Statutory Accounting Principles (E) Working Group adopted INT 24-01: Principles-Based Bond Definition Implementation Questions and Answers.‚Å∏ This guidance identifies that debt securities treated as regulatory capital by the issuer's primary regulatory authority and that do not qualify under the principles-based bond definition solely because interest can be cancelled in the event of financial stress in a non-resolution scenario without triggering an event of default are **capital notes** in scope of SSAP No. 41.‚Åπ

On December 17, 2024, and finalized February 2025, the SAPWG adopted revisions to SSAP No. 41 to incorporate changes to clarify capital notes references and guidance.¬π‚Å∞ This expands the scope of instruments receiving surplus note treatment beyond traditional surplus notes to include certain hybrid capital instruments.

#### 3. Federal Tax Treatment: Interest Deductibility Under IRC ¬ß 163

**Debt Classification for Tax Purposes:**

While surplus notes are classified as equity for regulatory purposes, they may be treated as **debt for federal tax purposes** if they possess sufficiently debt-like character under case law and IRS guidance.¬π¬π Courts and the IRS have established that the requirement of regulator approval for payments does not automatically convert surplus notes to equity for tax purposes.¬π¬≤

**IRC ¬ß 163(a) - Interest Deduction:**

IRC ¬ß 163(a) provides: "There shall be allowed as a deduction all interest paid or accrued within the taxable year on indebtedness."¬π¬≥ If surplus notes are characterized as debt for tax purposes, interest payments are **deductible as business interest expense**.

In one precedent case, a court stated: "The District concedes that the Surplus Notes are to be treated as debt for purposes of computing deductions."¬π‚Å¥ This supports the position that properly structured surplus notes receive interest deduction treatment despite regulatory approval requirements.

**Section 163(j) Business Interest Limitation:**

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 added IRC ¬ß 163(j), which limits business interest expense deductions to the sum of: (1) business interest income, (2) 30% of adjusted taxable income (ATI), and (3) floor plan financing interest.¬π‚Åµ

For tax years beginning after December 31, 2024, the One, Big, Beautiful Bill (OBBBA) amended Section 163(j) to add back deductions for depreciation, amortization, or depletion to taxable income when calculating ATI, effectively restoring the EBITDA-based calculation.¬π‚Å∂

**Small Business Exception:**

A taxpayer is exempt from IRC ¬ß 163(j) if it meets the IRC ¬ß 448(c) gross receipts test - average annual gross receipts for the prior three tax years not exceeding $30 million (2024 adjusted amount).¬π‚Å∑ LLIC's $2.1 billion in annual premiums far exceeds this threshold, so the Section 163(j) limitation **does apply**.

- LLIC statutory net income (FY2024): **$185 million**
- Adjusted taxable income (estimated): ~$185M (assuming limited add-backs)
- IRC ¬ß 163(j) limitation: 30% √ó $185M = **$55.5 million** allowable business interest deduction
- **Conclusion:** $15M surplus note interest is **well within** the $55.5M limitation; **fully deductible**

#### 4. Debt vs. Equity Classification Tests

Courts use multi-factor tests to determine whether an instrument is debt or equity for tax purposes. Key factors favoring **debt classification** for surplus notes:¬π‚Å∏

| Factor | Application to Surplus Notes | Weight |
|--------|----------------------------|--------|
| **Written unconditional promise to pay** | Surplus notes contain unconditional obligation to pay stated interest and principal | **Favors Debt** |
| **Fixed maturity date** | Typical surplus notes have stated maturity (e.g., 30 years) | **Favors Debt** |
| **Fixed interest rate** | Surplus notes specify fixed annual interest rate (e.g., 10%) | **Favors Debt** |
| **Labeled as "note"** | Instruments are expressly designated as surplus notes | **Favors Debt** |
| **Subordination to creditors** | Subordination can favor equity, BUT surplus notes subordinate to ALL creditors including policyholders | **Neutral/Favors Equity** |
| **Regulatory approval for payments** | Payments contingent on regulatory approval | **Favors Equity** |
| **Participation in management** | No participation rights for noteholders | **Favors Debt** |
| **Source of payments** | Payments from surplus, not tied exclusively to earnings | **Neutral** |

**IRS Treatment Precedent:**

The IRS has made administrative decisions that surplus notes are properly **excluded from surplus in the calculation of equity base** for purposes of certain tax provisions, suggesting recognition of their debt-like character.¬π‚Åπ In Private Letter Rulings, the IRS has applied proration rules to life insurance companies holding surplus notes, treating the interest income as investment income subject to IRC ¬ß 812 proration.¬≤‚Å∞

**Balancing Test Result:**

While subordination and regulatory approval requirements weigh toward equity classification, the preponderance of factors‚Äîfixed maturity, fixed rate, unconditional obligation, no management participation, and labeling as debt‚Äîsupport **debt classification for tax purposes**.¬≤¬π Courts have held that "hybrid notes may still be structured to be treated as debt if, as with surplus notes, they possess sufficiently debt-like features overall (an investment grade rating is particularly helpful)."¬≤¬≤

### B. Subordinated Debt: Tax and Regulatory Treatment

#### 1. Distinction from Surplus Notes

Subordinated debt issued by insurance companies differs from surplus notes in one critical respect: **interest and principal payments are contractually mandatory**, not subject to regulatory approval on a payment-by-payment basis.¬≥¬≤ However, the debt is subordinated to senior creditors and policyholders in the event of insolvency or liquidation.

Key structural differences:

| Feature | Surplus Notes | Subordinated Debt |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Payment approval** | Nebraska DOI approval required for each payment | No ongoing approval; contractually obligated |
| **Statutory capital treatment** | 100% capital credit (SSAP No. 41) | Limited or no capital credit (varies by state) |
| **Tax treatment** | Interest deductible (if debt classification maintained) | Interest deductible under IRC ¬ß 163 |
| **Default risk** | No event of default if payments deferred | Standard default provisions apply |
| **Regulatory flexibility** | High (regulator discretion) | Low (contractual obligations) |

#### 2. NAIC Statutory Capital Treatment for Subordinated Debt

Unlike surplus notes, traditional subordinated debt generally receives **limited or no statutory capital credit** under NAIC accounting principles.¬≥¬≥ The treatment varies by state insurance department interpretation and the specific terms of the subordination agreement.

**Factors affecting capital treatment:**
- **Degree of subordination:** Debt subordinated to all policyholder claims may receive partial credit
- **Term to maturity:** Longer-term debt (>10 years) may qualify for higher capital treatment
- **State regulator discretion:** Some states allow 25-50% capital credit for qualifying subordinated debt; others provide no credit

**2024 NAIC Guidance:**

The NAIC's 2024-2025 updates to capital structure treatment focused primarily on surplus notes and "capital notes" (hybrid instruments with deferrable interest).¬≥‚Å¥ Traditional subordinated debt with mandatory interest payments generally remains outside the scope of instruments receiving full statutory capital credit.¬≥‚Åµ

#### 1. Regulatory Treatment: 100% Statutory Capital Credit

#### 2. Federal Tax Treatment: No Deduction

**IRC ¬ß 162 - Business Expense Deduction:**

IRC ¬ß 162 allows deduction for "ordinary and necessary expenses paid or incurred during the taxable year in carrying on any trade or business."‚Å¥¬π Contributed capital does **not qualify** as a deductible business expense. It is a capital transaction, not an expense.

**Shareholder Tax Treatment:**

When a parent corporation makes a capital contribution to a subsidiary:‚Å¥¬≤
- **No deduction** at parent level (American Financial Holdings receives no tax benefit)
- **Basis increase:** Parent's tax basis in subsidiary stock increases by $150M contribution
- **No income** at subsidiary level (LLIC does not recognize income on capital contribution)

**Result:** **Zero tax benefit** from contributed capital structure.

### D. IRC Section 382 NOL Limitation Analysis

#### 1. Overview of Section 382 Ownership Change Rules

IRC ¬ß 382 imposes limitations on a corporation's ability to use net operating loss (NOL) carryforwards and certain built-in losses following an "ownership change."‚Å¥‚Å∑ The purpose is to prevent trafficking in NOLs‚Äîpreventing corporations from acquiring loss corporations solely to use their tax attributes.

**Ownership Change Definition:**

An ownership change occurs when there is a **more than 50 percentage point increase** in ownership by one or more 5-percent shareholders during a three-year testing period.‚Å¥‚Å∏

**Application to Project Chronos:**

The $2.9 billion acquisition of 100% of LLIC stock by American Financial Holdings constitutes a **100 percentage point increase** in ownership by a 5-percent shareholder (American Financial Holdings acquires 100% from Liberty Life Holdings LLC, going from 0% to 100%).

**Result:** This transaction **triggers an ownership change** under IRC ¬ß 382.‚Å¥‚Åπ

[VERIFIED via IRC ¬ß 382 ownership change rules: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/382]

#### 2. Section 382 Annual Limitation Formula

When an ownership change occurs, the corporation's ability to use pre-change NOLs each year is limited to:‚Åµ‚Å∞

**Section 382 Limitation = Fair Market Value of Loss Corporation √ó Long-Term Tax-Exempt Rate**

**Fair Market Value:** The value of the corporation's stock immediately before the ownership change. For Project Chronos, the purchase price of $2.9 billion represents arm's-length fair market value.

**Long-Term Tax-Exempt Rate:** Published monthly by the IRS. Recent rates (2024-2025) range from approximately 2.5% to 3.5%.‚Åµ¬π For purposes of this analysis, assume **3.0%** (mid-range estimate).

**Calculation:**

Section 382 limitation = $2,900,000,000 √ó 3.0% = **$87 million** annual NOL usage cap

This means that if LLIC has pre-acquisition NOL carryforwards, it can utilize **at most $87 million per year** to offset taxable income after the ownership change.

### E. Life Insurance Company Taxation Provisions

#### 1. IRC ¬ß¬ß 801-820: Life Insurance Company Taxable Income

Life insurance companies are subject to a special taxation regime under IRC ¬ß¬ß 801-820 (Subchapter L, Part I).‚Åµ‚Åµ Key provisions affecting LLIC:

**IRC ¬ß 801 - Tax Imposed:**

"A tax is hereby imposed for each taxable year on the life insurance company taxable income of every life insurance company. Such tax shall consist of a tax computed as provided in section 11 as though the life insurance company taxable income were the taxable income referred to in section 11."‚Åµ‚Å∂

**Result:** Life insurance companies pay tax at the standard corporate tax rate of **21%** (IRC ¬ß 11) on their life insurance company taxable income.‚Åµ‚Å∑

**Life Insurance Company Taxable Income Defined (IRC ¬ß 802):**

The calculation differs from ordinary corporate taxable income due to insurance-specific deductions and income items:

**Gross income includes:**
- Premiums and other consideration
- Investment income (dividends, interest, rents)
- Decrease in reserves
- Other amounts (per IRC ¬ß 803)

**Deductions include:**
- Death benefits and other insurance claims paid
- Increase in reserves (IRC ¬ß 807)
- Dividends to policyholders (IRC ¬ß 808)
- General business deductions (subject to proration per IRC ¬ß 805)
- Special life insurance company deductions (IRC ¬ß 804-806)

[VERIFIED via 26 U.S.C. ¬ß 801-802: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/801]

#### 2. IRC ¬ß 812: Proration for Tax-Exempt Interest and Policyholder Dividends

IRC ¬ß 812 requires life insurance companies to prorate certain deductions between the "company share" and the "policyholders' share" of investment income.‚Åµ‚Å∏ This prevents double tax benefits.

**Key Concept:**

The proration requirement reduces the amount of a life insurance company's deduction for reserve increases that are funded with tax-exempt interest, thereby denying a double benefit, but allows a full exclusion for the company's share of the tax-exempt interest.‚Åµ‚Åπ

**Application to LLIC:**

If LLIC holds municipal bonds or other tax-exempt securities in its $17.8B investment portfolio:
- Tax-exempt interest must be allocated between company share and policyholders' share
- The policyholders' share of tax-exempt interest reduces deductions for reserve increases
- This ensures LLIC does not get both (a) tax-exempt interest exclusion AND (b) reserve increase deduction for the same funds

**Impact on Capital Structure Decision:**

If LLIC invests surplus note proceeds or contributed capital in tax-exempt municipal bonds, IRC ¬ß 812 proration will reduce the net tax benefit. This favors investing in taxable securities to maximize the company's share of investment income.

[VERIFIED via IRC ¬ß 812: https://irc.bloombergtax.com/public/uscode/doc/irc/section_812]

#### 3. IRC ¬ß 805: General Deductions (Dividend Received Deduction)

If LLIC holds equity investments in its portfolio (5% equities = $890M), IRC ¬ß 805(a)(4) allows a modified dividend received deduction (DRD).‚Å∂‚Å∞ However, the DRD is subject to proration under IRC ¬ß 812 between company and policyholders' share.

**Typical DRD rates:**
- 50% deduction for dividends from <20% owned corporations
- 65% deduction for dividends from 20-80% owned corporations
- 100% deduction for dividends from >80% owned consolidated group members

After IRC ¬ß 812 proration, the effective DRD is reduced. This is **not directly relevant to the capital structure decision** but affects overall LLIC tax planning.
