**2. Non-Compete Enforceability: Nebraska Hostile Jurisdiction (60-70% Narrow Non-Solicitation Success Rate)**

Nebraska law creates significant challenges for post-employment restrictions on LLIC's 650 captive agents:

**Nebraska Common Law Standard (Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman, 225 Neb. 662 (1987)):**

- **Traditional geographic non-competes are unenforceable** for employees in Nebraska
- **Only narrow customer non-solicitation** permitted: restricted to customers with whom agent had actual personal contact
- **No "blue pencil" reformation:** Courts void overbroad agreements entirely, will not modify to reasonable scope
- **Recent precedent (Sisk v. Scripps Media, 2024 WL 1175140 (D. Neb. 2024)):** Court struck down non-solicitation covering "actual or prospective" customers as exceeding permissible scope

**Enforcement Probability:**

| Provision Type | Nebraska Enforceability | Enforcement Success Rate |
|---------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Customer non-solicitation (narrow, personal contact only) | Likely enforceable | 60-70% preliminary injunction |
| Geographic non-compete (radius restriction) | Likely unenforceable | 10-20% |
| Product-based restriction (no selling life insurance) | Likely unenforceable | 5-10% |

**Multi-State Challenges (38 Licensed States):**

- **California, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Minnesota:** Employee non-competes **categorically void** under state law
- **Estimated 80-135 agents (12-21% of force)** operate in non-compete-prohibitive jurisdictions
- **Choice of law conflicts:** Even Nebraska law designation may be rejected by states with strong anti-non-compete public policies

**Enforcement Cost-Benefit Analysis:**

- **Litigation cost per agent:** $200K-$380K through trial

### B. Non-Compete and Non-Solicitation Enforceability

#### 1. Nebraska Non-Compete Law - Hostile Jurisdiction

**Statutory Framework:**

Nebraska has no specific statute governing employee non-compete agreements. All Nebraska non-compete law derives from common law court decisions applying general contract principles and public policy analysis.â¹

**Nebraska Common Law Standard - Three-Part Reasonableness Test:**

Nebraska courts enforce non-compete agreements only if they meet three requirements:
1. The restriction must be reasonable in the sense that it is **not injurious to the public**;
2. The restriction must be reasonable in the sense that it is **no greater than reasonably necessary to protect the employer in some legitimate business interest**; and
3. The restriction must be **not unduly harsh and oppressive** on the party against whom it is asserted.Â¹â°

**Critical Limitation for Employee Non-Competes:**

Nebraska courts have adopted a strict view and concluded that **post-employment restrictions must be no greater than reasonably necessary to protect an employer's legitimate interest**. Traditional non-compete clauses with geographic restrictions are **not allowed** in post-employment restrictions on employees.Â¹Â¹ Rather, Nebraska courts recognize only a **narrow customer non-solicitation restriction**, which must be properly limited to prevent solicitation of only those customers that the employee specifically did business with and had personal contact.Â¹Â²

**No "Blue Pencil" Reformation:**

Unlike many states, Nebraska courts **will not modify an overbroad agreement**. Rather, they will **declare it to be void**.Â¹Â³ Nebraska is in the minority of states in that the courts will not revise an overly-broad covenant to make it enforceable; if one piece of the restriction is found unreasonable, the entire covenant will be unenforceable.Â¹â´

#### 2. Leading Nebraska Cases on Non-Compete Enforceability

**Polly v. Ray D. Hilderman & Co., 225 Neb. 662, 407 N.W.2d 751 (1987):**

In *Polly*, the Nebraska Supreme Court found a covenant not to compete unreasonable and unenforceable because it attempted to restrict Polly from soliciting or working for the employer's clients **with whom Polly did not work and did not even know**.Â¹âµ

The court held that "an employer has a legitimate business interest in protection against a former employee's competition by improper and unfair means, but is **not entitled to protection against ordinary competition** from a former employee."Â¹â¶ A covenant may be valid only if it restricts the former employee from working for or soliciting the former employer's clients or accounts **with whom the former employee actually did business and has personal contact**.Â¹â·

Critically, the court **declined to rewrite the covenant** so as to make it "no greater than is reasonably necessary," determining this was not a proper case for reformation of the covenant not to compete.Â¹â¸

**Sisk v. Scripps Media, Inc., 2024 WL 1175140 (D. Neb. Mar. 18, 2024):**

This 2024 federal district court case provided a recent reminder of Nebraska's strict enforcement standards for non-solicitation agreements.

**Facts:** Scripps terminated Sisk's employment on February 2, 2024. Sisk asked Scripps to release him from the non-competition and non-solicit restrictions as he was interested in working for another television station in Omaha. Scripps refused to release him and said it would enforce the agreement. Sisk filed suit asking the court to declare the non-solicit and non-competition provisions unenforceable.Â¹â¹

**Holding:** In granting Sisk's motion for an injunction, the court held that **both the non-competition and non-solicitation provisions were unenforceable** under Nebraska law.Â²â°

The court found the non-solicitation clause unenforceable because it was not limited to restricting Sisk from soliciting clients with whom he actually did business and had personal contact. The language not only restricted Sisk from soliciting customers with whom he had personal contact but with whom Scripps "had an actual or prospective relationship," which the court concluded **greatly exceeded the permissible scope**.Â²Â¹

**Takeaway:** Nebraska law is generally **not kind to noncompetes** and they are generally **unenforceable in the employee context**. By contrast, Nebraska courts will uphold employee nonsolicitation agreements if **properly and narrowly drafted**.Â²Â²

### E. WARN Act Analysis and Mass Layoff Notification Requirements

#### 1. Federal WARN Act - Statutory Requirements

**Statutory Framework:**

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN), 29 U.S.C. Â§Â§ 2101-2109, protects workers, their families, and communities by requiring employers with 100 or more employees to provide at least **60 calendar days advance written notice** of a plant closing and mass layoff affecting 50 or more employees at a single site of employment.âµâ´

**Covered Employers:**

The WARN Act applies to employers with:
- **100 or more employees** (generally not counting those who have worked less than six months in the last 12 months and those who work an average of less than 20 hours a week)âµâµ

**LLIC Workforce: 2,800 employees** â†’ WARN Act clearly applies.

**Triggering Events:**

The WARN Act is triggered when certain employment losses occur at a **single site during a 30-day period**:âµâ¶

1. **Plant Closing:** Permanent or temporary shutdown of a single site of employment that results in employment loss for **50 or more employees** during any 30-day period.âµâ·

2. **Mass Layoff:** Employment loss at a single site during a 30-day period of either:
   - At least **33% of employees** (excluding part-time) **and at least 50 employees**, OR
   - **500 employees** (excluding part-time)âµâ¸

**90-Day Aggregation Rule:**

WARN also applies when an employer conducts a series of smaller, but related layoffs **within 90 days** that collectively reach the WARN thresholds over the 90-day period.âµâ¹ When an employer makes a reduction in force, it must look forward and backward 90 days from each employment loss to determine whether WARN obligations arise.â¶â°

#### 3. WARN Act Liability Calculation

**Calendar Days vs. Working Days (Circuit Split):**

The U.S. Supreme Court declined to disturb an appeals court ruling that the remedy for a violation of the WARN Act should be measured in terms of the actual **work days** at issue during the 60-day notice window, and not the calendar days.â¶Â³ However, the Third Circuit has held that employees may receive wages for **60 calendar days**, showing there is some circuit split on this issue.â¶â´

#### 5. Integration Planning Strategies to Avoid WARN Triggers

**Strategy 1: Staggered Terminations Beyond 90-Day Window**

To avoid the 90-day aggregation rule, structure terminations such that:
- **Phase 1 (Month 1):** Terminate 40-49 employees (below 50-employee threshold)
- **Phase 2 (Month 4):** Terminate additional 40-49 employees (beyond 90-day aggregation window from Phase 1)
- **Result:** Each phase avoids 50-employee trigger, and phases are separated by >90 days, preventing aggregation

**Strategy 2: Voluntary Separation Incentives**

Offer voluntary early retirement or separation packages to reduce headcount without "employment loss" triggering WARN:
- Enhanced severance (1-2 weeks per year of service)
- Extended benefits continuation (6-12 months COBRA subsidy)
- Outplacement services

**Advantage:** Voluntary separations do not count toward WARN thresholdsâ·â°

**Strategy 3: Attrition + Hiring Freeze**

Natural attrition (retirements, voluntary departures) combined with hiring freeze can gradually reduce workforce without WARN trigger:
- LLIC's 2,800 employees, assuming 10-15% annual turnover = 280-420 voluntary departures/year
- Over 12-18 months, hiring freeze can reduce headcount 15-20% without layoffs

**Caution:** Must be genuine voluntary departures, not constructive discharge (forced resignations)

**Strategy 4: Functional Transfers (No Terminations)**

Transfer employees to other American Financial Holdings entities instead of terminating:
- Preserves employment relationship (no "employment loss")
- Avoids WARN Act entirely
- May require relocation (potential voluntary quits if employees refuse)

**Recommendation for LLIC/American Financial Holdings:**

If post-acquisition integration requires headcount reduction >150 employees:
1. **Provide full 60-day WARN notice** (safest approach, avoids litigation risk)
2. **Cost of compliance:** 60 days' advance notice (employees continue working, productivity may decline)
3. **Alternative:** Structure integration over 12-18 months with staggered reductions, voluntary incentives, and attrition to avoid WARN trigger thresholds

**Trade-Off Analysis:**

| Approach | Cost | Risk | Timeline |
|---------|------|------|---------|
| **WARN Compliance** | 60 days' productivity loss (employees work notice period) | No liability risk | 60 days minimum notice |
| **Staggered Layoffs** | Integration delayed 12-18 months | Low risk if properly structured | 12-18 months phased integration |
| **Voluntary Incentives** | Enhanced severance cost ($15K-$40K per employee) | No WARN risk (voluntary) | 6-12 months to achieve targets |
| **Non-Compliance (Pay Liability)** | $1.9M WARN damages (150 employees) | **High litigation risk**, class action exposure | Immediate integration |

**Recommended Approach:** Combination of voluntary incentives + staggered layoffs to minimize WARN exposure while achieving integration objectives within 18 months.
