### A. NAIC Risk-Based Capital Framework

#### 1. NAIC Model Act #312: Statutory Foundation

The [NAIC Risk-Based Capital (RBC) for Insurers Model Act (#312)](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/model-law-312.pdf), initially adopted in 1993 with latest revision in 2012, establishes the minimum capital standards for life and property/casualty insurers based on the riskiness of their operations.¬π The RBC framework is designed to identify weakly capitalized insurers and trigger progressively stringent regulatory interventions before insolvency occurs.¬≤

**Purpose:** Regulators use RBC requirements to determine the minimum amount of capital required for an insurer to support its operations and write coverage.¬≥ The Model Act emphasizes that "maintaining capital above minimum risk-based capital levels is desirable, as additional capital is used and useful in the insurance business to protect against inherent insurance risks."‚Å¥

**State Adoption:** The RBC Model Act has been adopted in substantially similar form by all U.S. jurisdictions, including Nebraska through [Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß¬ß 44-6008 through 44-6015](https://nebraskalegislature.gov/laws/statutes.php?statute=44-6015).‚Åµ [VERIFIED: Nebraska statutes accessed via NE Legislature website, January 20, 2026]

#### 2. RBC Risk Component Calculation Methodology (C1-C4)

The NAIC Life Risk-Based Capital formula assesses capital adequacy across four primary risk categories, applied with covariance adjustments to avoid double-counting:‚Å∂

**C1 ‚Äî Asset Risk**
- **C1o (Asset Risk - Other):** Assesses credit risk, concentration risk, and reinsurance credit risk associated with invested assets including bonds, mortgages, real estate, and policy loans‚Å∑
- **C1cs (Asset Risk - Common Stock):** Evaluates equity market volatility risk for stock holdings‚Å∏

**C2 ‚Äî Insurance Risk**
- Evaluates mortality/morbidity risk (life insurance claims experience), persistency risk (lapse/surrender rates), and adverse claims development‚Åπ

**C3 ‚Äî Interest Rate Risk**
- **C3a (C3 Phase I):** Assesses interest rate risk for policy liabilities without explicit cash flow modeling¬π‚Å∞
- **C3b (C3 Phase II):** Uses stochastic scenario testing for variable annuities with guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefits (GMWB) and other guaranteed living/death benefits¬π¬π
- **C3c:** Disintermediation risk for products with surrender options¬π¬≤

**C4 ‚Äî Business Risk**
- Addresses general business operational risks including inadequate pricing, premium growth strain, guaranty fund assessments, and administrative system failures¬π¬≥

**RBC Calculation Formula (2025):**¬π‚Å¥
```
RBC after Covariance Before Operational Risk = C0 + C4a + ‚àö[(C1o + C3a)¬≤ + (C1cs + C3c)¬≤ + (C2)¬≤ + (C3b)¬≤ + C4b]
```

The square-root covariance formula recognizes that not all risk categories will materialize simultaneously, providing a more realistic capital requirement than simple addition.¬π‚Åµ

**2025 Developments:** The NAIC Capital Adequacy (E) Task Force prioritized recommendations for longevity risk transfers, continued development of the economic scenario generator (ESG), and potential alignment of C3 Phase 1 and C3 Phase 2 interest rate risk methodologies for the 2026 RBC formula.¬π‚Å∂ [METHODOLOGY: NAIC meeting materials June 2025]

#### 3. Action Level Thresholds: Progressive Regulatory Intervention

The RBC framework establishes four progressive action levels, each calculated as a multiple of the Authorized Control Level (ACL) RBC, which trigger increasingly stringent regulatory responses:¬π‚Å∑

**Company Action Level (CAL) ‚Äî 200% of ACL RBC**
- **Threshold:** Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) between 150% and 200% of ACL RBC¬π‚Å∏
- **Insurer Obligation:** Company must prepare and submit an RBC Plan to the domiciliary insurance commissioner within 45 days of the RBC report filing date¬π‚Åπ
- **RBC Plan Requirements:** The plan must identify conditions contributing to the deficiency, propose corrective actions (capital infusion, operational changes, reinsurance, asset/liability restructuring), include financial projections demonstrating return to >200% RBC within a specified timeframe (typically 2-3 years), and be certified by a qualified actuary¬≤‚Å∞
- **Regulatory Review:** Commissioner reviews plan for adequacy, may require modifications, and monitors compliance with corrective actions¬≤¬π
- **Business Restrictions:** Dividend restrictions typically apply (Nebraska DOI requires approval for any dividend if RBC <200% CAL)¬≤¬≤

**Regulatory Action Level (RAL) ‚Äî 150% of ACL RBC**
- **Threshold:** TAC between 100% and 150% of ACL RBC¬≤¬≥
- **Regulatory Involvement:** Commissioner may issue corrective order mandating specific actions (capital contribution deadline, reinsurance procurement, asset disposition, expense reduction, business plan restrictions)¬≤‚Å¥
- **Examination Authority:** Commissioner may conduct special financial or market conduct examination to assess solvency risk¬≤‚Åµ
- **Company Obligations:** All CAL requirements continue; insurer must comply with commissioner's corrective order within specified deadlines¬≤‚Å∂

**Authorized Control Level (ACL) ‚Äî 100% of ACL RBC**
- **Threshold:** TAC between 70% and 100% of ACL RBC¬≤‚Å∑
- **Commissioner Authority:** Commissioner is **authorized** (but not required) to place insurer under regulatory control (rehabilitation or liquidation) if deemed necessary to protect policyholders¬≤‚Å∏
- **Alternative Actions:** Commissioner may permit continued operations under intensive supervision if the insurer demonstrates viable rehabilitation plan and adequate protection for policyholders exists¬≤‚Åπ
- **Receivership Risk:** At this level, the insurer faces imminent risk of seizure; most commissioners exercise control authority unless extraordinary circumstances justify continued operation¬≥‚Å∞

**Mandatory Control Level (MCL) ‚Äî 70% of ACL RBC**
- **Threshold:** TAC less than 70% of ACL RBC¬≥¬π
- **Mandatory Seizure:** Commissioner **must** place insurer under regulatory control (typically conservation or rehabilitation proceedings) unless the commissioner determines that such action would not serve policyholder interests and provides detailed written explanation to the NAIC within 30 days¬≥¬≤
- **No Discretion Exception:** The MCL represents the statutory floor; continued operation below this level is prohibited except in exceptional circumstances with NAIC notification¬≥¬≥

### B. Capital Injection Regulatory Approval Requirements

#### 1. Surplus Notes: Regulatory Treatment and Approval Process

**Statutory Authority:** Nebraska Revised Statute ¬ß 44-220 grants domestic insurance companies the power to borrow money for specific purposes, including "additional surplus funds" and "deficiency correction."¬≥‚Åµ Surplus notes are a specialized debt instrument subordinated to all policyholder claims and receive 100% statutory capital credit under [NAIC Statement of Statutory Accounting Principles (SSAP) No. 41](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/inline-files/24-28 - SSAP No. 41 - Capital Notes_0.pdf).¬≥‚Å∂

**Issuance Approval:** Under Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-220, companies must "apply to the Department of Insurance and obtain approval in writing from the Director of Insurance" before issuing surplus notes.¬≥‚Å∑ The Director specifies a "stated maximum amount" for the issuance.¬≥‚Å∏ [VERIFIED: Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-220, accessed Jan. 20, 2026]

**Payment Approval Requirement:** Neb. Rev. Stat. ¬ß 44-221 mandates that "the company must receive prior approval of the Director of Insurance for any repayments of surplus notes."¬≥‚Åπ The Director evaluates adequacy of surplus using standards from ¬ß 44-2136 (dividend restrictions) when determining whether to approve payments.‚Å¥‚Å∞

**Statutory Payment Test:** For surplus notes issued on or after September 9, 1995, "the principal of and interest on such notes may be paid from time to time, either in full or in part, from available surplus funds of the company only when the amount of the surplus of the company over all liabilities is double that of the amount of principal and interest then being paid."‚Å¥¬π This creates a 200% surplus cushion requirement for each payment.

**EXAMPLE APPLICATION TO LIBERTY LIFE:**
- Current statutory surplus: $1.85B
- Proposed surplus note issuance: $150M at 6.5% annual interest = $9.75M annual interest expense
- Post-injection surplus: $2.0B
- Annual interest payment approval test: Surplus must exceed 2√ó interest payment = 2 √ó $9.75M = $19.5M surplus requirement
- **Conclusion:** LLIC's $2.0B surplus significantly exceeds the $19.5M minimum for interest payment approval; Nebraska DOI approval highly probable for interest payments‚Å¥¬≤

**SSAP No. 41 Statutory Accounting (2025 Updates):**‚Å¥¬≥
- **Admitted Asset Treatment:** 100% statutory capital credit (included in Total Adjusted Capital for RBC calculation)
- **Balance Sheet Presentation:** Reported as liability on Page 3, Line 19 of NAIC Annual Statement, but included in surplus for regulatory capital purposes
- **Interest Expense:** Deductible for statutory accounting purposes when paid (not accrued), reducing statutory net income

#### 1. Vermont Captive Insurance Framework

#### 2. NAIC Actuarial Guideline 48 (AG48): Primary Security vs. Other Security

**Background:** AG48 was adopted by the NAIC Executive Committee and Plenary in December 2014, effective January 1, 2015, to define rules for new XXX and AXXX reserve financing transactions.¬π¬≤‚Å∏ The guideline addresses concerns about "shadow insurance" practices where captive reinsurers held insufficient high-quality assets to support ceded reserves.¬π¬≤‚Åπ

**Primary Security Layer:** AG48 divides risks under covered businesses into two layers:¬π¬≥‚Å∞
- **Primary Security:** Effectively replaces the "economic reserve layer"; must be backed by high-quality assets (cash, U.S. government securities, investment-grade bonds)
- **Required Level of Primary Security:** Determined using an actuarial valuation model specified in AG48 (economic reserve plus margin for conservatism)

**Other Security Layer:** The remainder of the statutory reserve (excess over Primary Security) may be backed by a wider variety of assets, including:
- Parental guarantees
- Letters of credit
- Less liquid assets¬π¬≥¬π

**Parental Guarantee Requirements:** AG48 permits parental guarantees as "Other Security" if:
- Parent insurer maintains RBC ratio >300%
- Parent net worth exceeds the guarantee amount
- Parent has minimum A rating from recognized rating agency¬π¬≥¬≤

#### 3. NAIC Model #787: Term and Universal Life Insurance Reserve Financing Model Regulation

**Codification of AG48 Principles:** The NAIC later codified AG48 principles in [Model Regulation #787](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/model-law-787.pdf), adopted in 2016, which harmonizes reserve financing standards and specifies how "primary security" and additional backing assets must be held even if reinsurance is assumed through a captive.¬π¬≥¬≥

**Implementation Status (as of August 2025):**¬π¬≥‚Å¥
- **42 jurisdictions have implemented Model #787:** AL, AK, AZ, AR, CA, CO, CT, DE, DC, FL, GA, HI, IN, IA, LA, ME, MD, MA, MI, MS, MO, MT, NV, NJ, NM, NC, ND, OK, OR, PA, RI, SC, SD, TX, USVI, UT, VT, VA, WA, WV, WI
