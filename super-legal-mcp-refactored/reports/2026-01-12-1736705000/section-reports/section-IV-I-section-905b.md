### A. Legal Framework

Section 905(b) of the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA) provides a limited exception to the exclusive remedy provision of federal workers' compensation law, permitting injured longshoremen to file third-party tort lawsuits against vessel owners for vessel negligence. This creates a dual-track recovery system: longshoremen receive no-fault LHWCA benefits from their stevedore employer while retaining the right to pursue full tort damages against negligent vessel owners.

#### 1. Statutory Basis: 33 U.S.C. ¬ß 905(b)

The LHWCA, enacted in 1927 and substantially amended in 1972, establishes a comprehensive federal workers' compensation scheme for maritime workers employed in traditional maritime occupations on navigable waters or adjoining areas.¬π Section 905(b) states: "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person, or anyone otherwise entitled to recover damages by reason thereof, may bring an action against such vessel as a third party."¬≤

Unlike the exclusive remedy provision under LHWCA (which limits injured workers to statutory benefits comprising two-thirds of average weekly wages plus unlimited medical care),¬≥ Section 905(b) permits full tort recovery including:
- Past and future lost wages without LHWCA's two-thirds limitation
- Pain and suffering (not compensable under LHWCA)
- Loss of consortium for spouse
- Full medical compensation without statutory caps
- Pre-judgment and post-judgment interest

The statute creates a carefully balanced structure: longshoremen injured aboard vessels receive immediate LHWCA benefits from their stevedore employer (PMSC in this transaction), but the vessel owner's duty is limited to vessel negligence under narrowly-defined standards articulated by the Supreme Court.‚Å¥ This balance protects longshoremen from the uncertainties of tort litigation while preserving accountability for vessel owners who breach specific duties.

#### 2. The Scindia Three-Duty Framework

In *Scindia Steam Navigation Co., Ltd. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), the Supreme Court rejected a general duty of care owed by vessel owners to longshoremen, instead articulating three limited duties that define the boundaries of Section 905(b) liability.‚Åµ

##### a. Turnover Duty

The **turnover duty** relates to the condition of the vessel and its equipment when cargo operations commence. The vessel owner "owes a duty to exercise ordinary care under the circumstances to turn over the ship and its equipment in such condition that an expert stevedore can carry on stevedoring operations with reasonable safety."‚Å∂

This duty encompasses two components:
1. **Physical Condition**: The vessel, cargo holds, deck, and shipboard equipment (including cargo handling gear, lashing equipment, winches, cranes, and access ways) must be in reasonably safe working condition when presented to the stevedore.
2. **Duty to Warn**: The vessel must warn of latent or hidden dangers known to the vessel owner but not obvious to or anticipated by a competent stevedore exercising reasonable care.

**Critical Limitation**: The vessel owner has no continuing duty to discover and correct dangerous conditions that develop during loading or unloading operations. Once cargo operations begin, the stevedore assumes primary responsibility for workplace safety under the "Platonic division" of responsibilities.‚Å∑ The vessel's duty is confined to the initial turnover ‚Äî not ongoing supervision.

**Container Lashing as Turnover Obligation**: Container securing equipment falls squarely within the turnover duty because lashing occurs during ocean transit under the vessel crew's control. If turnbuckles, lashing rods, twist-locks, or dunnage materials are defective, corroded, or improperly installed when the vessel arrives at the discharge port, the vessel breaches its turnover duty even if the equipment was serviceable when cargo operations in the loading port concluded.‚Å∏

##### b. Active Control Duty

The **active control duty** provides that a vessel owner must exercise reasonable care to prevent injuries to longshoremen in areas that remain under the active control of the vessel.‚Åπ This duty applies when:
- The vessel's crew actively participates in cargo operations (e.g., operating vessel cranes, attaching lashing gear, positioning cargo)
- The vessel retains control over specific equipment or spaces during stevedoring
- Vessel personnel direct or supervise stevedoring activities

The active control exception is narrowly construed. Mere presence of vessel crew does not trigger heightened liability; the vessel must actually control the specific operation that caused injury.¬π‚Å∞ For example, if a vessel's crew operates ship's gear to lift containers while longshoremen position the containers in the hold, the vessel may be liable under active control if defective crane operation causes injury. However, if the vessel's crew merely observes stevedoring operations without directing or controlling them, active control liability does not attach.

##### c. Duty to Intervene

The **duty to intervene** concerns the vessel owner's obligation with regard to cargo operations in areas under the principal control of the stevedore. This is the most difficult duty for plaintiffs to establish, requiring proof of three elements:
1. The vessel owner had **actual knowledge** (not constructive knowledge) that a condition or stevedoring operation posed an unreasonable risk to longshoremen's safety
2. The vessel owner had **actual knowledge** that it could not rely on the stevedore to protect its employees from the hazard
3. If left uncorrected, the condition posed a **substantial risk** of causing serious injury or death

The Supreme Court emphasized that "it would be inconsistent with the Act to hold that the shipowner has a continuing duty to take reasonable steps to discover and correct dangerous conditions that develop during the loading or unloading process."¬π¬π Primary responsibility for workplace safety rests with the stevedore employer, not the vessel owner. The intervention duty attaches only in the rare circumstance where the vessel has actual knowledge of an imminent hazard and actual knowledge that the stevedore is incompetent or unwilling to address it.

#### 3. Narrowing of Vessel Owner Liability: *Howlett* Warning Duty

In *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*, 512 U.S. 92 (1994), the Supreme Court further refined the turnover duty's warning component, holding that the duty to warn of latent defects in cargo stow is extremely narrow.¬π¬≤ The duty attaches only to hazards that:
- Are not known to the stevedore, AND
- Would not be obvious to or anticipated by a skilled stevedore in the competent performance of their duties

**Critical Holding**: "Absent a vessel's actual knowledge of a hazard, the turnover duty attaches only if the exercise of reasonable care would place upon the vessel an obligation to inspect for or discover the hazard's existence."¬π¬≥ The Court held that reasonable care does **not** require a vessel to:
- Supervise ongoing operations of the loading stevedore in another port
- Inspect other stevedores' work before the cargo arrives at the discharge port
- Inspect the completed stow to discover hazards in cargo that originated at another terminal

This holding substantially limits vessel exposure for defects created during loading operations at foreign ports. However, it does not absolve vessels from liability for equipment failures under their control during transit (such as container lashing gear deterioration).

#### 4. Comparative Fault and Damages Offset

##### a. Pure Comparative Fault Under California Law

California applies **pure comparative fault** under Proposition 51, codified at California Civil Code ¬ß 1431.2.¬π‚Å¥ In Section 905(b) cases filed in California federal courts (including the Northern District where Martinez v. PMSC is pending), juries apportion fault percentages between the plaintiff longshoreman and the defendant vessel owner.

**Key Rule**: The plaintiff's recovery is reduced by the percentage of fault assigned to the plaintiff, but the plaintiff can recover even if more than 50% at fault (unlike contributory negligence jurisdictions that bar recovery if plaintiff is majority at fault). For example, if a jury finds total damages of $6.5 million and assigns 40% fault to the injured longshoreman and 60% fault to the vessel owner, the plaintiff recovers $3.9 million ($6.5 million √ó 60%).

Federal courts have held that "both general maritime law and ¬ß 905(b) require adherence to a uniform standard of care in ¬ß 905(b) actions and the higher [state] standard is inapplicable to such actions."¬π‚Åµ California state courts concur that uniform federal maritime standards apply in Section 905(b) cases rather than state-specific negligence standards, ensuring consistent application across circuits and avoiding forum-shopping.

##### b. LHWCA Benefits Offset and Statutory Lien

Under 33 U.S.C. ¬ß 933, LHWCA benefits paid by the employer/insurer are **offset** from the longshoreman's Section 905(b) recovery, but the employer retains a reimbursement right.¬π‚Å∂ The mechanism works as follows:

**¬ß 933(a) Dual Recovery Permitted**: An injured longshoreman "need not elect whether to receive compensation or recover damages against such third party." The longshoreman can receive LHWCA benefits immediately (no-fault recovery) while simultaneously pursuing a Section 905(b) tort claim against the vessel owner.

**¬ß 933(f) Statutory Lien**: "If compensation is payable with respect to the disability or death of an employee, the employer shall be entitled to a lien on any judgment or settlement recovered by the employee or his survivors."¬π‚Å∑ The employer's LHWCA carrier must be reimbursed from tort recovery for all LHWCA benefits paid to date plus capitalized future benefits.

**Example Recovery Scenario** (Martinez case):
- Gross verdict after trial: $6.5 million
- Martinez comparative fault: 40% ‚Üí Reduction $2.6 million
- Net verdict: $3.9 million
- LHWCA benefits paid to date: $450K
- Future LHWCA benefits (capitalized present value): ~$800K
- LHWCA statutory lien: $1.25 million
- **Net to Martinez**: $2.65 million

This offset prevents double recovery while ensuring the longshoreman receives the excess above LHWCA benefits. From the vessel owner's perspective, the P&I club pays the full net verdict ($3.9 million in the example), but the LHWCA carrier is reimbursed $1.25 million from that recovery.

#### 5. Section 905(b)'s Prohibition on Contractual Indemnity

Section 905(b) contains a critical anti-indemnity provision: "[A]ny provision in a contract, agreement, or understanding purporting to relieve the vessel from liability for personal injury or death of a longshoreman is void as against public policy."¬π‚Å∏ Courts have interpreted this provision broadly to invalidate any agreement purporting to shift vessel negligence liability to the stevedore employer or third parties.¬π‚Åπ

**Impact on Insurance**: This prohibition means PMSC, as both vessel owner and stevedore employer, cannot contractually shift Section 905(b) liability to terminal operators, cargo owners, or other third parties through hold harmless or indemnification clauses. The vessel owner bears the full liability (subject only to comparative fault reduction). Insurance policies covering Section 905(b) exposure must directly insure the vessel owner's liability ‚Äî indemnity from third parties is void and unenforceable.
