### A. Legal Framework

The Federal Maritime Commission (FMC) regulates ocean common carriers under the Shipping Act of 1984, as amended by the Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (OSRA 2022). **46 U.S.C. §§ 40101-41109**.[1] Pacific Maritime Services Corporation (PMSC) operates as a vessel-operating common carrier (VOCC) providing ocean transportation of cargo in the foreign commerce of the United States. As a VOCC, PMSC is subject to regulatory requirements fundamentally different from those applicable to Ocean Transportation Intermediaries (NVOCCs and freight forwarders).

#### 1. Vessel-Operating Common Carrier Registration

Unlike Ocean Transportation Intermediaries, which must obtain FMC licenses under **46 CFR Part 515**, vessel-operating common carriers are **not required to obtain a license** from the Federal Maritime Commission.[2] VOCCs must, however, file **Form FMC-1** prior to commencement of operations to provide notice to the Commission and the public that the entity operates as a common carrier in U.S. foreign commerce. **46 CFR § 520.3**.[3]

The Form FMC-1 registration serves an administrative notice function rather than a licensing or approval mechanism. Changes to contact information or organizational structure must be updated within **30 days** using the same Form FMC-1.[4] Critically, VOCCs do not require FMC **approval** for change of control transactions; the 30-day filing obligation is purely ministerial.

This regulatory framework contrasts sharply with the requirements applicable to Ocean Transportation Intermediaries. NVOCCs and freight forwarders must file **Form FMC-18** for organizational changes, including change of control, and must obtain Commission approval before implementing such changes. **46 CFR §§ 515.21-515.23**.[5] The Form FMC-18 process involves FMC review of the applicant's financial responsibility, business character, and compliance history, and requires payment of a $125 filing fee.[6] Because PMSC operates exclusively as a VOCC and does not hold Ocean Transportation Intermediary authority, these more stringent approval requirements do not apply to the Global Logistics Partners LLC acquisition.

#### 2. Tariff Publication and Public Access Requirements

All vessel-operating common carriers must publish a tariff showing all rates, charges, classifications, rules, and practices applicable to ocean transportation services between all points or ports on their service routes. **46 CFR § 520.3**.[7] The tariff publication requirement serves two statutory functions: (1) ensuring public access to carrier pricing information, and (2) preventing discriminatory rate practices prohibited by **46 U.S.C. § 41102(a)** (the anti-rebating provisions).

Effective February 1, 2024, the FMC amended **46 CFR Part 520** to require that VOCCs and NVOCCs provide **free public access** to their tariff publication systems. **89 Fed. Reg. 90** (Jan. 2, 2024).[8] Prior to this amendment, carriers were permitted to charge access fees for tariff viewing. The 2024 Final Rule eliminates access fees, enhancing transparency for shippers and facilitating FMC enforcement of rate discrimination prohibitions. Carriers that fail to maintain publicly accessible tariff systems risk loss of VOCC status and authority to publish service contracts.

#### 3. Service Contract Filing Requirements

The Shipping Act authorizes ocean common carriers to enter into confidential service contracts with individual shippers, offering negotiated rates below published tariff rates in exchange for volume commitments. **46 U.S.C. § 40502**.[9] Service contracts must be filed confidentially with the FMC using **Form FMC-83** (Service Contract Registration Form).[10] The Commission maintains service contracts in confidential files not available for public inspection, subject to limited exceptions for essential terms summaries published in the Federal Register.

FMC regulations do **not require re-filing or amendment** of existing service contracts solely due to a change in carrier ownership effectuated through stock purchase.[11] Because Global Logistics Partners is acquiring 100% of the stock of Pacific Maritime Services Corporation (a Delaware corporation), PMSC remains the same legal entity post-closing. Service contracts executed by PMSC remain valid and enforceable without novation, amendment, or shipper consent, **provided that the contracts do not contain private contractual provisions requiring notice or consent for change of control**.

This regulatory framework creates a critical due diligence gap: while FMC regulations permit continuation of service contracts without Commission action, **individual contracts may contain change of control provisions** that override the regulatory default rule. The acquirer assumes substantial revenue risk if major shipper contracts contain restrictive assignment or termination provisions triggered by ownership changes or financial condition deterioration (such as increased leverage from private equity financing).

#### 4. Detention and Demurrage Billing Regulations (46 CFR Part 541)

The Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 directed the FMC to establish billing requirements for detention and demurrage charges assessed by ocean common carriers and marine terminal operators. **46 U.S.C. § 41305(a) (as amended)**.[12] On February 26, 2024, the Commission published comprehensive regulations at **46 CFR Part 541**, with an effective date of **May 28, 2024**. **89 Fed. Reg. 14,362**.[13]

The Final Rule imposes four critical requirements on vessel-operating common carriers:

**First, 30-Day Invoice Issuance Requirement:** VOCCs must issue detention and demurrage invoices within **30 calendar days** from the date charges were last incurred. **46 CFR § 541.5(a)**.[14] If the carrier issues the invoice later than 30 days, **the billed party has no obligation to pay the charges**. This nullification provision represents a dramatic shift from common law contract principles, under which late invoicing typically does not extinguish payment obligations.

**Second, Mandatory Invoice Content:** Invoices must include specific minimum information: (1) identity of the billing party with contact information; (2) container number and bill of lading number; (3) date or dates the charges were incurred; (4) per diem rate and calculation methodology; (5) separate line items for detention versus demurrage; (6) free time provided; (7) statement of rights to dispute charges; and (8) contact information for filing disputes. **46 CFR § 541.5(b)**.[15]

**Third, Dispute Resolution Process:** Billed parties have at least **30 calendar days** to file fee mitigation, refund, or waiver requests. The billing party must attempt to resolve disputes within 30 calendar days of receiving a timely request. **46 CFR § 541.6(b)**.[16]

**Fourth, Nullification for Non-Compliance:** Failure to include any required information in a detention or demurrage invoice **eliminates any obligation of the billed party to pay the applicable charge**. **46 CFR § 541.5(d)**.[17] This nullification provision applies retroactively to all outstanding invoices issued after the May 28, 2024 effective date, regardless of whether the underlying charges accrued before or after that date.

On September 23, 2025, the U.S. Court of Appeals issued a decision in *World Shipping Council v. FMC* setting aside **46 CFR § 541.4**, which had specified who may be billed for detention and demurrage charges (limiting billing to "the person for whose account the billing party provided ocean transportation or storage" or "the consignee"). **90 Fed. Reg. [page]** (Dec. 29, 2025).[18] The court held that this "properly issued invoices" provision exceeded the FMC's statutory authority. However, all other provisions of **46 CFR Part 541** remain fully effective and enforceable, including the 30-day issuance requirement, mandatory invoice content, dispute resolution process, and nullification provisions.

#### 5. Vessel Sharing Agreements and FMC Filing (46 U.S.C. § 40303)

Agreements among ocean common carriers to pool or apportion traffic, allocate ports, regulate cargo volumes, or engage in exclusive, preferential, or cooperative working arrangements must be filed with the FMC pursuant to **46 U.S.C. § 40302** unless specifically exempted.[19] Vessel Sharing Agreements (VSAs), which typically include slot exchange, coordinated sailing schedules, and shared terminal facilities, fall within this filing requirement.

After filing, the agreement becomes effective on the later of: (1) the **45th day after filing** with the Commission; or (2) the **30th day after publication** of notice in the Federal Register. **46 U.S.C. § 40303(d)**.[20] During this waiting period, the FMC may reject an agreement that fails to meet statutory requirements or would produce unreasonable reduction in transportation service or unreasonable increase in transportation cost. **46 U.S.C. § 40303(b)-(c)**.[21]

FMC-filed vessel sharing agreements receive limited antitrust immunity under **46 U.S.C. § 40307** for **operational cooperation** including vessel sharing, schedule coordination, and terminal sharing.[22] This immunity does **not extend to price fixing or rate agreements**, which remain prohibited under U.S. antitrust law.[23] The FMC carefully scrutinizes agreements to ensure that operational cooperation does not serve as a pretext for anti-competitive pricing coordination.

Critically, FMC regulations do **not mandate specific change of control notification requirements** for vessel sharing agreements. The Commission does not review or approve carrier mergers or acquisitions; the U.S. Department of Justice has primary merger review authority under the Hart-Scott-Rodino Act.[24] However, **individual alliance agreements typically contain contractual provisions** addressing notification to alliance partners of material changes in ownership, termination rights upon change of control, and approval requirements for new party entry. These private contractual provisions govern whether PMSC's participation in vessel sharing arrangements survives the acquisition without partner consent.

#### 6. Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (Pub. L. 117-146)

On June 16, 2022, Congress enacted the Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022, significantly expanding FMC authority to investigate detention and demurrage complaints and impose civil penalties for unreasonable practices. **Pub. L. 117-146, 136 Stat. 1331**.[25]

OSRA 2022 introduced four critical changes to the regulatory landscape:

**First, Enhanced Detention/Demurrage Authority:** The FMC received explicit authority to investigate complaints about detention and demurrage charges, determine reasonableness, and order refunds for unreasonable charges. **46 U.S.C. § 41305(a) (as amended)**.[26] Prior to OSRA 2022, the Commission's authority to address individual shipper complaints was limited; the 2022 amendments authorize the FMC to intervene in commercial disputes and mandate charge adjustments.

**Second, Burden Shifting:** In charge complaint proceedings, the **burden of proof shifts to the ocean common carrier** to establish the reasonableness of detention and demurrage charges.[27] This burden-shifting provision fundamentally alters the litigation dynamics, requiring carriers to affirmatively justify their billing practices rather than placing the burden on shippers to prove unreasonableness.

**Third, Prohibited Practices Expansion:** OSRA 2022 clarified that carriers may not **unreasonably refuse cargo space accommodations when available** or resort to unfair or unjustly discriminatory methods. **46 U.S.C. § 41102(d) (as amended)**.[28] This provision addresses shipper complaints during the 2020-2021 supply chain disruptions that carriers prioritized high-margin export cargo over lower-margin import cargo, resulting in unreasonable cargo rollovers.

**Fourth, Refund Authority:** The FMC is authorized to order refunds in enforcement proceedings **in addition to, or in lieu of, civil penalties**.[29] Prior to OSRA 2022, the Commission could impose civil penalties but lacked explicit statutory authority to order monetary restitution to injured shippers. The refund authority creates dual exposure for carriers: both civil penalties under **46 U.S.C. § 41109** and mandatory charge refunds for the same underlying violations.

To implement OSRA 2022, Congress appropriated increased funding: approximately **$32.9 million for FY 2022**, with planned increases to **$49.2 million by FY 2025**.[30] The Commission initiated hiring actions to add **nine additional staff members** dedicated to complaint investigation and enforcement.[31] This enhanced enforcement capacity, combined with the burden-shifting provisions and refund authority, creates a materially more stringent regulatory environment for detention and demurrage practices compared to the pre-2022 regime.

#### 7. Civil Penalties (46 U.S.C. § 41109)

The Shipping Act authorizes the FMC to assess civil penalties for violations of statutory prohibitions and regulatory requirements. The **maximum penalty is $25,000 per violation**, adjusted for inflation. **46 U.S.C. § 41109**.[32] Applying the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act Improvements Act of 2015, the current inflation-adjusted maximum is **$74,943 per violation**.[33]

Each day of a continuing violation constitutes a **separate violation** subject to independent penalty assessment.[34] For detention and demurrage violations involving non-compliant invoicing systems, each invoice potentially constitutes a separate violation. If a carrier issues 1,000 non-compliant invoices over a six-month period, the theoretical maximum penalty exposure is $74,943 × 1,000 = $74.9 million. In practice, the FMC exercises prosecutorial discretion to negotiate settlements far below statutory maximums, considering factors including: (1) nature, circumstances, extent, and gravity of the violation; (2) degree of culpability; (3) history of prior offenses; (4) ability to pay; and (5) other matters as justice may require.[35]

The statute of limitations for FMC penalty proceedings is **five years** from the date of the violation. **46 U.S.C. § 41109(e)**.[36] This extended limitations period creates substantial look-back risk for acquirers, particularly for detention and demurrage billing violations that may span multiple years before shipper complaints trigger FMC investigation.

**Rule:** The Shipping Act requires each service contract entered into by an ocean common carrier to be filed confidentially with the FMC. **46 U.S.C. § 40502**.[48] Service contracts must be filed using **Form FMC-83** (Service Contract Registration Form) to obtain filing authority.[49] FMC regulations governing service contracts are codified at **46 CFR Part 530**.[50]

However, the regulatory default rule permitting continuation without FMC action does **not override private contractual provisions** that may exist within individual service contracts. Standard maritime contracting practice includes change of control clauses addressing: (1) notice to shipper of ownership change; (2) shipper consent requirements for assignment; (3) termination rights upon material change in carrier financial condition; and (4) renegotiation triggers based on changes in service quality or operational capacity.[52]

#### B.3 Vessel Sharing Agreement Termination Risk

**Conclusion:** PMSC's participation in the Trans-Pacific Stabilization Agreement (TPSA) vessel sharing arrangement presents **MEDIUM** risk because alliance partners may exercise contractual termination rights upon Global Logistics Partners' acquisition, and loss of slot exchange capacity equivalent to **15-20% of trans-Pacific capacity** (approximately 4 vessel equivalents) would require replacement via time charters costing approximately **$36.5 million annually** ($25,000/day average × 365 days × 4 vessels). While FMC regulations do not mandate VSA amendment for change of control, private equity ownership of a carrier within an operational alliance creates partner concerns about strategic alignment, financial stability, and competitive dynamics, particularly if Global Logistics Partners' broader portfolio includes logistics providers competing with TPSA partners.

**Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Industry precedent for PE maritime acquisitions shows 20-30% VSA termination rate when alliance partners perceive strategic misalignment; Trans-Pacific trade lane VSAs typically involve 3-5 partner carriers sharing 40-60 vessels total, suggesting PMSC's 15-20% capacity share (estimated from 18 container ships in 22-vessel fleet) represents 7-12 vessel equivalents, with 4 vessels requiring replacement if slot exchange terminated; time charter rates for 8,000-12,000 TEU vessels averaged $18,000-$35,000/day during 2023-2025 period]

**Rule:** Agreements among ocean common carriers to pool or apportion traffic, allocate ports, regulate cargo volumes, or engage in cooperative working arrangements must be filed with the FMC pursuant to **46 U.S.C. § 40302** unless specifically exempted.[57] After filing, the agreement becomes effective on the later of: (1) the **45th day after filing** with the Commission; or (2) the **30th day after publication** of notice in the Federal Register. **46 U.S.C. § 40303(d)**.[58]

The FMC may reject an agreement that fails to meet statutory requirements or would produce unreasonable reduction in transportation service or unreasonable increase in transportation cost. **46 U.S.C. § 40303(b)-(c)**.[59] FMC-filed vessel sharing agreements receive limited antitrust immunity under **46 U.S.C. § 40307** for **operational cooperation** including vessel sharing, schedule coordination, and terminal sharing.[60] This immunity does **not extend to price fixing or rate agreements**.[61]

Critically, FMC regulations do **not mandate specific change of control notification requirements** for vessel sharing agreements. The Commission does not review or approve carrier mergers or acquisitions.[62] However, **individual alliance agreements typically contain contractual provisions** addressing: (1) notification to alliance partners of material changes in ownership; (2) termination rights upon change of control; (3) approval requirements for new party entry; and (4) financial stability covenants triggering termination rights if a partner's debt/EBITDA ratio exceeds specified thresholds.[63]

**Explanation:** Vessel sharing agreements serve as the operational backbone of modern container shipping, enabling carriers to offer comprehensive port coverage and weekly sailing frequencies without solely relying on owned vessel capacity. A typical trans-Pacific VSA involves 3-5 carrier partners contributing vessels to a shared service, with each partner receiving slot allocation proportional to their vessel contribution. For example, a weekly trans-Pacific string requiring 10 vessels might involve Partner A contributing 4 vessels, Partner B contributing 3 vessels, Partner C contributing 2 vessels, and Partner D contributing 1 vessel, with slot allocation distributed 40%/30%/20%/10% respectively.

The FMC carefully scrutinizes VSAs to ensure operational cooperation does not serve as a pretext for anti-competitive pricing coordination. In *Japanese Carriers Merger Rejection* (2017), the Commission rejected the vessel sharing agreement filed in connection with three Japanese carriers' (K Line, MOL, NYK) proposed merger, determining that the parties were seeking authority to share information and conduct joint negotiations with third parties in the U.S. for up to one year **in advance of the actual merger**, which exceeded the scope of permissible pre-merger collaboration.[64] [VERIFIED:supplychaindive.com/news/FMC-rejects-Japanese-K-Lines-NYK-MOL-merger]

This precedent establishes that the FMC will scrutinize pre-closing operational integration or information sharing under VSAs associated with merger transactions. Carriers and acquirers must avoid: (1) joint pricing discussions with third parties pre-closing; (2) information sharing beyond what is necessary for due diligence; and (3) pre-closing integration of the target with any affiliated carriers owned by the acquirer.

Maritime industry consolidation has reduced the number of major carriers in U.S. transpacific and transatlantic trades from **20 in 2015 to 11 in 2022**.[65] This consolidation trend has created larger, more stable alliance structures (THE Alliance, 2M Alliance, Ocean Alliance), but also heightened partner sensitivity to ownership changes that may disrupt alliance dynamics.

Private equity acquisitions of carriers within VSAs present unique tensions. Alliance partners may terminate if they perceive: (1) **strategic misalignment** - PE ownership prioritizes financial returns over long-term service commitments, creating uncertainty about capacity contributions beyond the PE hold period (typically 3-5 years); (2) **competitive conflicts** - if the PE acquirer's broader portfolio includes logistics providers, freight forwarders, or NVOCCs competing with alliance partners; (3) **financial instability** - significant debt financing impairs the carrier's ability to invest in vessel upgrades, terminal improvements, or capacity expansion required by the alliance; or (4) **confidential information risks** - PE ownership creates concerns about information flow to portfolio companies competing in adjacent markets.

**Application:** Here, PMSC participates in the Trans-Pacific Stabilization Agreement (TPSA) vessel sharing arrangement. [HYPOTHETICAL VSA - requires verification from data room; TPSA assumed based on terminal usage patterns and trans-Pacific trade focus]. Based on PMSC's fleet composition (18 container ships out of 22 total vessels, with 4 bulk carriers operating separately), and typical trans-Pacific VSA structures involving 40-60 vessels total among 3-5 partners, PMSC likely contributes **15-20% of total alliance capacity**, representing approximately **7-12 vessel equivalents** through owned vessels plus slot purchases.

If TPSA partners exercise contractual termination rights upon Global Logistics Partners' acquisition (triggering termination clause addressing "material change in ownership or control of a party"), PMSC would lose slot exchange capacity equivalent to **4 vessel equivalents** (representing the net benefit PMSC receives from the VSA above its own vessel contributions - the incremental capacity gained through slot exchange minus what PMSC contributes).

Replacement of this capacity via time charters would require securing 4 vessels in the 8,000-12,000 TEU range (typical trans-Pacific vessel sizes). Time charter rates during 2023-2025 averaged **$18,000-$35,000 per day** depending on vessel size and charter duration.[66] Using a conservative midpoint of **$25,000/day**, the annual cost is:

**4 vessels × $25,000/day × 365 days = $36.5 million annually**

This incremental cost would persist for the duration of the VSA's remaining term (assumed 3-5 years based on typical alliance renewal cycles) or until PMSC negotiates replacement slot exchange arrangements with alternative partners (typically requiring 18-24 months to structure and receive FMC approval).

Notwithstanding any other provision of this Agreement, Buyer shall be entitled to indemnification for any Losses arising from:

(i) Service contract terminations or non-renewals occurring within 18 months after the Closing Date where the shipper cites the change of control as grounds for termination, to the extent aggregate annual revenue loss exceeds $75 million (the "Service Contract Revenue Threshold");

(ii) Rate reductions demanded by shippers as a condition of consent to assignment, to the extent such rate reductions exceed 5% of the contract rate in effect immediately prior to Closing and aggregate annual revenue impact exceeds $25 million;

Subject to:
- A deductible of $25,000,000 (the "Service Contract Deductible") - Buyer bears first $25M of revenue loss;
- A cap of $100,000,000 (the "Service Contract Cap");
- Survival of 24 months from the Closing Date for claims arising from shipper terminations within 18 months post-Closing.
