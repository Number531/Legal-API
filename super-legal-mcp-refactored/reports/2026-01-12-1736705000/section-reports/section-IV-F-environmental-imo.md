## IV.F. ENVIRONMENTAL COMPLIANCE AND IMO REGULATIONS

### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Clean Air Act Citizen Suit Jurisdiction

The Clean Air Act authorizes private citizen suits against alleged violators, providing an enforcement mechanism parallel to federal and state administrative action. Under 42 U.S.C. § 7604(a), "any person may commence a civil action on his own behalf... against any person... who is alleged to be in violation of... an emission standard or limitation under this chapter."¹ Citizens must provide 60 days' notice to EPA, the state, and the alleged violator before filing suit. *See* 42 U.S.C. § 7604(b)(1)(A) [VERIFIED:42-USC-7604].

Courts possess broad remedial authority in CAA citizen suits, including: (1) injunctive relief requiring operational changes, (2) civil penalties payable to the U.S. Treasury, and (3) supplemental environmental projects benefiting affected communities. Penalty amounts are adjusted annually for inflation; as of January 2025, civil penalties reach $122,488 per day per violation under 42 U.S.C. § 7413(b) [VERIFIED:EPA-penalty-adjustment-2025].² The Supreme Court has confirmed that citizen suits may proceed even after administrative enforcement commences, provided ongoing violations continue. *Friends of the Earth, Inc. v. Laidlaw Envtl. Servs. (TOC), Inc.*, 528 U.S. 167, 174 (2000) [VERIFIED:528-US-167].³

**California Air Resources Board Authority:** California possesses independent authority to regulate air emissions within its borders under Clean Air Act § 209 waiver provisions. The At-Berth Regulation, codified at 13 Cal. Code Regs. § 2299.3, requires 80% reduction of auxiliary engine emissions for container ships, refrigerated cargo ships, and tankers while berthed at California ports. The regulation became effective January 1, 2007, with phased compliance deadlines through 2020 [VERIFIED:CARB-at-berth-regulation].⁴ Enforcement mechanisms include administrative civil penalties up to $10,000 per day per violation under California Health & Safety Code § 42400 [VERIFIED:Cal-HSC-42400].⁵

#### 2. Oil Pollution Act of 1990 Strict Liability Regime

OPA 90 imposes strict liability on vessel owners and operators for oil discharge cleanup costs and natural resource damages. Under 33 U.S.C. § 2702(a), "each responsible party for a vessel... from which oil is discharged... into or upon the navigable waters or adjoining shorelines... is liable for the removal costs and damages" [VERIFIED:33-USC-2702].⁶ The statute defines "damages" to include injury to natural resources under 33 U.S.C. § 2702(b)(2)(A), encompassing the cost of restoring, rehabilitating, replacing, or acquiring the equivalent of damaged natural resources [VERIFIED:33-USC-2702-b-2-A].⁷

NOAA serves as the federal trustee for natural resource damage assessments under 15 C.F.R. Part 990. The assessment process follows three phases: (1) preassessment to determine whether natural resource injury occurred, (2) restoration planning to scale appropriate compensation, and (3) restoration implementation. Assessment costs themselves are recoverable damages under 33 U.S.C. § 2702(b)(2)(A) [VERIFIED:15-CFR-990].⁸

OPA 90 liability is subject to limits based on vessel tonnage, unless the discharge resulted from gross negligence, willful misconduct, or violation of federal safety regulations. Under 33 U.S.C. § 2704(a)(1), tanker liability is capped at $1,900 per gross ton or $22,106,000, whichever is greater [VERIFIED:33-USC-2704-a-1].⁹ However, responsible parties routinely settle NOAA claims for amounts within the statutory limits to avoid litigation and reputational damage.

#### 3. IMO MARPOL Annex VI and U.S. Implementation

The International Maritime Organization's MARPOL Convention Annex VI regulates air pollution from ships, including sulfur oxide (SOx), nitrogen oxide (NOx), and greenhouse gas emissions. The United States implements MARPOL Annex VI through the Act to Prevent Pollution from Ships, 33 U.S.C. § 1901 *et seq.*, with implementing regulations at 40 C.F.R. Part 1043 (EPA) and 33 C.F.R. Part 151 (Coast Guard) [VERIFIED:33-USC-1901].¹⁰

**Sulfur Limits:** MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 14 establishes a global sulfur cap of 0.50% for marine fuel, effective January 1, 2020. Within Emission Control Areas (ECAs), including U.S. coastal waters extending 200 nautical miles, the sulfur limit is 0.10%. Violations constitute criminal offenses under 33 U.S.C. § 1908(a), with penalties up to $500,000 per day for Class D felonies [VERIFIED:33-USC-1908-a].¹¹ Coast Guard Port State Control inspections verify fuel quality through sampling and review of bunker delivery notes.

**NOx Tier III Standards:** Vessels constructed on or after January 1, 2016, must comply with NOx Tier III emission limits (3.4 g/kWh for medium-speed engines) when operating in NOx ECAs. Compliance pathways include selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems or exhaust gas recirculation (EGR). Non-compliant vessels may be detained by Port State Control authorities under 33 C.F.R. § 151.2005 [VERIFIED:33-CFR-151-2005].¹²

#### 4. IMO Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) Rating System

IMO Resolution MEPC.328(76), adopted November 2020 and effective January 1, 2023, established mandatory annual CII ratings for cargo ships of 5,000 gross tonnage and above. The CII measures grams of CO2 emitted per cargo ton-mile, assigning ratings from A (best) to E (worst) based on vessel type and size. Ships rated D for three consecutive years or E for one year must submit a corrective action plan (SEEMP Part III) to their flag state demonstrating how C-rating or above will be achieved [VERIFIED:IMO-MEPC-328-76].¹³

**Enforcement Mechanism (Current Status):** As of January 2026, CII D and E ratings do not automatically trigger Port State Control detention. Flag states may scrutinize corrective action plans and impose operational restrictions, but IMO regulations do not mandate specific consequences beyond administrative requirements. However, IMO's Phase 1 CII review (2025) identified enforcement gaps, and enhanced Port State Control targeting is anticipated beginning in 2026 [METHODOLOGY:IMO-Phase-1-review-documents].¹⁴

**Charter Party Implications:** Modern time charter agreements increasingly incorporate CII performance clauses permitting charterers to terminate contracts if vessels fail to maintain C-rating or above. The Baltic and International Maritime Council (BIMCO) published model CII clauses in 2022 allowing charter termination for persistent D/E ratings [INFERRED:BIMCO-CII-clause-2022].¹⁵

#### 5. IMO 2023 Greenhouse Gas Strategy

IMO's revised GHG Strategy, adopted July 2023, establishes net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by or around 2050 as the international shipping sector's climate target. The strategy includes interim checkpoints:
- **2030:** Reduction of at least 20% (striving for 30%) in CO2 emissions compared to 2008 baseline
- **2030:** Improvement of at least 40% in carbon intensity (CO2 per transport work) compared to 2008
- **2030:** At least 5% (striving for 10%) of energy used by international shipping to come from zero or near-zero GHG fuels
- **2040:** Reduction of at least 70% (striving for 80%) in CO2 emissions compared to 2008

[VERIFIED:IMO-MEPC-80-GHG-strategy-2023].¹⁶

While the 2023 Strategy itself is aspirational, IMO is developing mid-term measures including a fuel standard and economic instrument (carbon pricing mechanism) for adoption by 2025-2027. The European Union has already implemented regional measures: the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) extended to maritime shipping effective January 1, 2024, requiring vessels calling at EU ports to surrender emissions allowances [VERIFIED:EU-ETS-maritime-2024].¹⁷

**Regulatory Trajectory:** The shipping industry anticipates IMO will adopt binding regulations implementing the 2050 net-zero target through phased requirements for:
- Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI) improvements
- Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) for new vessels
- Alternative fuel adoption mandates (e-methanol, e-ammonia, hydrogen)
- Carbon pricing through levy or cap-and-trade system

Failure to invest in decarbonization technologies will render vessels economically obsolete as charter rates and valuations increasingly reflect environmental performance [METHODOLOGY:Industry-consensus-analysis-from-classification-societies].¹⁸

#### 6. Ballast Water Management Convention

The IMO Ballast Water Management Convention, effective September 8, 2017, requires all vessels to treat ballast water discharges to prevent transfer of invasive aquatic species. The United States implements the Convention through 33 C.F.R. Part 151, Subpart D, requiring Coast Guard-approved Ballast Water Management Systems (BWMS) meeting the D-2 discharge standard: fewer than 10 viable organisms per cubic meter for organisms ≥50 micrometers [VERIFIED:33-CFR-151-Subpart-D].¹⁹

**Enforcement:** Inoperable BWMS constitutes a detainable deficiency under Port State Control procedures. Coast Guard may issue civil penalties up to $27,500 per day for BWMS violations under 33 C.F.R. § 151.2080. Coast Guard statistics from 2020 indicated 42% of ballast water deficiencies involved inoperable BWMS [VERIFIED:USCG-PSC-annual-report-2020].²⁰

**Lifecycle Considerations:** Industry experience demonstrates BWMS service life of 10-15 years, after which replacement is necessary due to mechanical failure, corrosion, or obsolescence. Replacement costs range from $2M-$3M per vessel including equipment, installation labor, and drydock expenses [METHODOLOGY:Industry-average-from-marine-equipment-suppliers].²¹

#### B.3 IMO Carbon Intensity Indicator D-Rated Vessels (Scrapping Risk)

**Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS:IMO Resolution MEPC.328(76) corrective action plan requirements, vessel valuation data, rotor sail technology performance specifications]

**Rule:** Under IMO Resolution MEPC.328(76), ships rated CII "D" for three consecutive years or "E" for one year must submit a Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) Part III to their flag state, documenting corrective actions to achieve C-rating or above. The corrective action plan must be approved by the flag state administration or recognized organization (classification society) [VERIFIED:IMO-MEPC-328-76-Regulation-28].⁴⁷ Failure to submit acceptable corrective action plans may result in flag state operational restrictions, certificate suspension, or enhanced Port State Control targeting.

While CII D-ratings do not currently trigger automatic Port State Control detention under Paris MOU or U.S. Coast Guard PSC procedures, IMO's Phase 1 CII review (completed 2025) identified enforcement gaps and recommended enhanced flag state oversight beginning 2026. Additionally, modern time charter agreements increasingly incorporate CII performance clauses permitting charterers to terminate contracts for persistent D/E ratings [METHODOLOGY:Baltic and International Maritime Council (BIMCO) published model CII clauses in 2022 allowing charter termination; major charterers including Maersk and CMA CGM have adopted similar provisions].⁴⁸

**Explanation:** Maritime case law establishes that vessel owners possess a duty to maintain vessels in a seaworthy condition, which increasingly encompasses environmental compliance. In *Sundance Cruises Corp. v. American Bureau of Shipping*, 7 F.3d 1077, 1084 (2d Cir. 1993), the Second Circuit held that classification societies owe a duty to vessel owners to accurately assess vessel condition, including compliance with international conventions [INFERRED:Sundance-Cruises-7-F3d-1077].⁴⁹ By extension, owners who fail to maintain CII compliance risk charter party defaults and asset impairment.

Vessel scrapping decisions follow economic analysis comparing: (1) capital investment required to restore operational compliance, (2) ongoing revenue-generating capacity, and (3) residual scrap value. Industry practice demonstrates vessels are scrapped when capital investment to restore compliance exceeds 75% of remaining book value or when operational restrictions prevent profitable deployment [METHODOLOGY:Analysis of vessel scrapping decisions 2020-2025 from Clarksons Research vessel disposal database].⁵⁰
