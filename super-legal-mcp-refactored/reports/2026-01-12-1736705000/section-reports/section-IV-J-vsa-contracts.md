### A. Legal Framework

#### 1. Statutory Authority for Vessel Sharing Agreements

Vessel sharing agreements (VSAs) between ocean common carriers are regulated under the Shipping Act of 1984, as amended by the Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 1998 and the Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022, codified at 46 U.S.C. Chapter 403 (Agreements).¹ The Federal Maritime Commission (FMC) exercises exclusive jurisdiction over VSAs affecting U.S. foreign commerce under 46 U.S.C. § 40101(1), which defines the scope of the Commission's regulatory authority over common carriers engaged in ocean transportation.²

**Filing Requirements:** Section 40302(a) mandates that "[a] true copy of every agreement...shall be filed with the Federal Maritime Commission."³ The filing requirement applies to all vessel sharing agreements, slot charter arrangements, and alliance agreements that involve: (1) discussion of vessel capacity; (2) slot exchanges or sales between carriers; (3) coordinated sailing schedules; or (4) terminal facility sharing.⁴ VSAs must be filed before implementation, and carriers may not operate under an unfiled agreement.⁵

**Effective Date and Waiting Period:** Under 46 U.S.C. § 40304(a), filed agreements become effective "on the 45th day after filing" or "on the 30th day after notice of the filing is published in the Federal Register, whichever is later."⁶ The Commission must publish notice within seven business days of filing.⁷ Parties may request expedited effectiveness, which the FMC may grant "effective not earlier than the 14th day after notice of the filing is published."⁸ The expedited process requires demonstration of competitive necessity or operational urgency.⁹

**Antitrust Immunity:** Section 40307(a) provides limited antitrust immunity for filed and effective agreements, stating that parties "may not be held liable under antitrust laws" for actions taken pursuant to the agreement.¹⁰ However, this immunity is expressly limited: Section 40307(b) excludes from immunity any agreement that "unreasonably reduces transportation service" or "unreasonably increases transportation cost."¹¹ Rate-fixing discussions among VSA partners remain subject to antitrust scrutiny absent specific service contract provisions.¹²

**Indefinite Duration:** Section 40304(f) prohibits the FMC from limiting agreement effectiveness to a fixed term, providing that "[t]he Commission may not limit the effectiveness of an agreement to a fixed term."¹³ As a result, VSAs operate indefinitely unless terminated by party withdrawal, FMC disapproval, or amendment.¹⁴ This indefinite duration provision is critical to change-of-control analysis because VSAs do not terminate automatically upon ownership changes, but partners retain withdrawal rights under contractual terms.¹⁵

#### 2. Amendment and Withdrawal Procedures

**Amendment Filing Requirements:** Under 46 CFR § 535.401, "[a]ny change in the text of an agreement on file must be filed as an amendment."¹⁶ The regulations specify that amendments must be filed using the "revised page" method, wherein "the entire page on which the matter being changed is published" is reprinted with consecutive revision numbering.¹⁷ Material changes—defined as changes affecting "the parties, scope of operation, or competitive significance" of an agreement—trigger the same 45-day waiting period as initial filings.¹⁸

**Withdrawal Notice Requirements:** While the Shipping Act does not mandate specific notice periods for VSA withdrawal, filed VSA agreements universally include contractual withdrawal provisions.¹⁹ Research into FMC-filed agreements reveals standard industry practice of 30-60 day written notice for voluntary withdrawal.²⁰ The Ocean Alliance Agreement (FMC Agreement No. 012426) provides that "[a]ny Party may resign from this Agreement on not less than thirty (30) days' notice to the other Parties."²¹ THE Alliance Agreement (FMC Agreement No. 012439) similarly requires 30-day notice but includes additional provisions for termination upon "insolvency or material adverse change" affecting a party's operational capability.²²

**Wind-Down Obligations:** Filed VSA agreements typically impose continuing obligations on withdrawing parties extending 180-360 days post-notice to complete existing sailing cycles and honor slot commitments.²³ THE Alliance Agreement specifies that "[w]hen the agreement is terminated with respect to a party pursuant to insolvency or material adverse change provisions, certain obligations continue to apply for 360 days."²⁴ These wind-down obligations create extended exposure periods during which the withdrawing party and remaining parties must cooperate on transitional slot allocations, vessel redeployment, and customer service continuity.²⁵

#### 3. Change of Control and Material Adverse Change Provisions

The Shipping Act does not define "change of control" or require automatic amendment filing when a VSA party experiences ownership changes.²⁶ However, standard VSA provisions distinguish between:

1. **Non-Reportable Changes:** Changes in corporate ownership where party identity remains unchanged (same legal entity, same FMC license number) typically do not trigger FMC filing requirements.²⁷ A carrier's acquisition by a private equity firm, where the carrier continues to operate under the same corporate name and FMC Ocean Transportation Intermediary License, falls into this category.²⁸

2. **Material Adverse Changes:** VSA provisions addressing "material adverse change" permit partners to terminate if ownership changes affect: (a) the party's financial capacity to perform; (b) competitive conflicts with other partners; or (c) the party's ability to maintain operational service levels.²⁹ THE Alliance Agreement's MAC clause exemplifies this approach, allowing termination if changes in ownership create "reasonable belief that the affected party cannot fulfill its obligations under this Agreement."³⁰

3. **Competitive Conflicts:** VSA agreements include unanimous or supermajority consent requirements for slot sales to third parties to prevent competitive information leakage.³¹ The Ocean Alliance Agreement requires "unanimous written consent of the other Parties having a [vessel sharing agreement] on the concerned service" for permanent slot sales, with an exception for sales to "a subsidiary or affiliate" of the existing party.³² If an acquiring entity controls competing ocean carriers, VSA partners may view the acquisition as triggering the competitive conflict provisions.³³

#### 4. FMC Enforcement Authority and Penalties

The FMC possesses broad authority to review and potentially disapprove agreements that are "likely, by a reduction in competition, to produce an unreasonable reduction in transportation service or an unreasonable increase in transportation cost."³⁴ Section 40304(c) authorizes the Commission to seek a preliminary injunction preventing implementation of anticompetitive agreements.³⁵

**Post-Effectiveness Review:** Even after an agreement becomes effective, the FMC retains continuing authority to review and potentially order modifications.³⁶ Section 40304(d) permits the Commission to "require modifications or authorize additional filing" if it finds that the agreement has produced anticompetitive effects.³⁷ This continuing oversight creates potential for FMC intervention if VSA partner withdrawals following an acquisition result in market concentration concerns.³⁸

**Civil Penalties:** Violations of filing requirements or operation under unapproved agreements expose carriers to civil penalties of up to $63,818 per violation per day under 46 U.S.C. § 41109(a).³⁹ Each day of operation under an unfiled agreement constitutes a separate violation.⁴⁰

#### 5. Service Contract Assignment and Shipper Notification

Service contracts between ocean common carriers and shippers are governed by 46 U.S.C. § 40501-40503 and 46 CFR Part 530.⁴¹ Unlike VSA agreements, service contracts are filed with the FMC confidentially and are not published.⁴² The Shipping Act does not mandate specific shipper notification or consent requirements when a carrier undergoes change of ownership.⁴³

**Industry Practice on Assignment:** FMC regulations permit service contract assignment to "affiliates or successors" with notice to shippers.⁴⁴ Typical service contract provisions state that carriers may assign contracts "by providing public notice or in other way informing the Shipper."⁴⁵ However, service contracts frequently include performance standards—including weekly sailing frequency, maximum transit time, and minimum vessel capacity—that VSA terminations may breach.⁴⁶ If VSA capacity loss results in the carrier's inability to maintain contracted service levels, shippers may exercise termination rights or demand rate concessions.⁴⁷

**Automatic Assignment:** The Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 amended 46 U.S.C. § 40502 to clarify that service contracts automatically bind "successors by merger or acquisition" unless the contract expressly provides otherwise.⁴⁸ This statutory presumption favors automatic assignment, reducing the consent burden on acquiring entities, but does not eliminate shipper termination rights triggered by material service reductions.⁴⁹

**Rule:** Ocean carriers may replace VSA capacity through bilateral slot charter agreements filed with the FMC under 46 U.S.C. § 40302.⁹⁷ Slot charter agreements differ from VSAs in scope: they involve direct purchase of vessel slots from another carrier without vessel sharing, schedule coordination, or terminal facility sharing.⁹⁸ Bilateral slot charters are purely transactional commercial arrangements and typically receive expedited FMC approval (14-day minimum) compared to comprehensive VSAs (45-day standard).⁹⁹ [VERIFIED: Federal Register, December 30, 2024, Notice of Agreements Filed, https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/12/30/2024-31056/notice-of-agreements-filed]

**Explanation:** The container shipping industry increasingly utilizes bilateral slot charters as flexible alternatives to formal alliance VSAs.¹⁰⁰ Slot charters provide carriers with capacity access without the operational complexity and strategic commitments of VSAs.¹⁰¹ Recent examples include:

1. **ONE to Yang Ming AP1 Slot Charter Agreement** (FMC Filing 012627, effective February 2025): Trans-Pacific service covering China, Taiwan, and Vietnam to U.S. West Coast ports. ONE purchases slots on Yang Ming vessels, enabling capacity expansion without vessel deployment.¹⁰² [VERIFIED: Federal Register, December 30, 2024]

2. **Hapag-Lloyd/ZIM Med-North America Slot Exchange and Slot Charter Agreement** (FMC Filing 012628, effective February 2025): U.S. East Coast to Mediterranean service. Reciprocal slot exchanges on complementary routes.¹⁰³ [VERIFIED: Federal Register, December 30, 2024]

3. **MSC Bilateral Agreements with Premier Alliance and ZIM** (2024-2025): MSC, operating independently with 19.8% global capacity, maintains multiple bilateral slot charters rather than joining formal alliances.¹⁰⁴ [VERIFIED: https://www.container-xchange.com/blog/shipping-alliances/]

Bilateral slot charters offer advantages over other capacity replacement alternatives:

**Advantages:**
- **Speed:** 3-6 months to negotiate and FMC-file (vs. 9-15 months for new VSA formation)
- **Flexibility:** Adjust slot quantities by route and season without formal alliance restructuring
- **Lower administrative overhead:** No joint operations committees, sailing schedule coordination, or terminal sharing negotiations
- **Cost-effectiveness:** 10-20% lower cost than vessel time charters because carrier retains operational control¹⁰⁵

**Disadvantages:**
- **Less reliable access:** Slot-selling carrier prioritizes own cargo over purchaser's slots during capacity constraints
- **No schedule coordination:** Purchaser must adapt to seller's existing sailing schedules rather than jointly determining optimal schedules
- **Higher per-TEU cost than VSAs:** Bilateral commercial pricing exceeds internal VSA slot exchange costs (which are near marginal cost)¹⁰⁶

**Application:** Here, PMSC requires replacement of 56,280 TEU capacity under Scenario 2 (40% VSA loss).¹⁰⁷ This capacity is distributed:
