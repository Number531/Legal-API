### A. Legal Framework

Port terminal leases at PMSC's four West Coast facilities (Los Angeles Pier 300, Long Beach Pier J, Oakland Outer Harbor Terminal A, and Seattle Terminal 46) are governed by California and Washington state statutes granting port district commissions broad authority over public port property leasing. The statutory frameworks establish baseline requirements while individual lease agreements establish specific assignment restrictions, consent standards, and performance obligations.

#### 1. California Port District Leasing Authority

**California Harbors and Navigation Code § 6295** grants port districts authority to "acquire, purchase, takeover, construct, maintain, operate, develop, and regulate wharves, docks, warehouses, grain elevators, bunkering facilities, belt railroads, floating plants, lighterage, lands, towage facilities, and any and all other facilities, aids, or public personnel, incident to, or necessary for, the operation and development of ports, waterways, and the district."¹ This enabling statute provides broad discretion: ports may lease property "for such purposes and upon such terms as the port commission deems proper."²

California statutes impose **no mandatory consent requirements** for lease assignments or changes of control in port district leases.³ The public trust doctrine constrains leasing to purposes consistent with commerce, navigation, and fisheries,⁴ but assignment restrictions are contractual, not statutory. The Ports of Los Angeles, Long Beach, and Oakland operate under this statutory framework for PMSC's California terminals.

#### 2. Washington Port District Leasing Authority

**RCW 53.08.080** authorizes Washington port districts to "lease all lands, wharves, docks and real and personal property owned and controlled by it, for such purposes and upon such terms as the port commission deems proper."⁵ Maximum lease terms are 50 years for standard property, extendable up to 30 additional years (80 years total).⁶ Port commissions possess broad discretion over assignment provisions and consent standards.⁷

Like California, Washington law imposes **no statutory consent mandate** for lease assignments or changes of control; such requirements are contractual.⁸ RCW 53.08.085 requires security for rent on leases exceeding one year, typically surety bonds or letters of credit.⁹ Port of Seattle Terminal 46 operates under this statutory framework.

#### 3. Industry-Standard Change of Control Provisions

Port terminal concession agreements uniformly prohibit assignments and subleases without prior written port consent. Standard language: "Tenant shall not sublet any part of the premises or assign this Agreement without the prior written consent of the Landlord" and "An assignment or subletting without the written consent of the Landlord, or an assignment or subletting by operation of law, shall be void."¹⁰

Changes in control of corporate tenants are treated as **"deemed assignments"** requiring port consent.¹¹ The rationale: "without it, a transfer could be effected by simply transferring control of the tenant, thus circumventing the assignment restrictions contained in the lease."¹² Industry practice defines deemed assignments as "[s]ales, transfers or other dispositions aggregating a certain predefined percentage of the voting stock of a tenant (if non-public) shall be deemed to be an assignment of the lease."¹³ Common trigger: **50%+ ownership change** or **effective control transfer**, including indirect changes through parent company sales.¹⁴

#### 4. Financial Capability Standards for Port Approval

Port of Long Beach documented standards (representative of West Coast port practices) require proposed assignees to provide:¹⁵

- **Audited Financial Statements:** Most recent financial statement audited by independent CPA
- **Comparable Credit Standing:** Financial statement must disclose "credit standing and financial responsibility **comparable to Lessee's**"
- **Corporate Documentation:** Details of principal officers/directors if partnership or corporation
- **Business Experience:** Statement of proposed assignee's business experience
- **Transaction Details:** Detailed statement of business relationship and proposed financial arrangements

**Excess Rent Clawback:** If lessee receives consideration exceeding rent payable under lease (lease premium), port consent is subject to **payment of excess rent to City.**¹⁶ This prevents lessees from profiting from public port concessions without sharing economic gain with the public landowner.

**Continued Lessee Obligation:** Original lessee remains **fully obligated** under lease notwithstanding assignment—joint and several liability with assignee.¹⁷
