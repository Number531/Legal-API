#### 1. Statutory Framework

**46 U.S.C. ¬ß 8103** (commonly referred to as the "75/25 Rule") establishes mandatory citizenship requirements for documented U.S. vessels:¬π

- **Statutory Threshold:** 75% of unlicensed crew must be U.S. citizens (excluding licensed officers).‚Å¥

### B. Penalty Exposure for Crew Citizenship Violations

#### 1. Distinction Between Crew Violations and Merchandise Transportation Violations

**Critical Legal Distinction:**

The research plan references "$39.6M penalty per voyage" based on **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 55102** (Jones Act cabotage merchandise transportation statute). However, crew citizenship violations under **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 8103** are governed by **different penalty provisions**.

**46 U.S.C. ¬ß 55102** penalties apply to:
- Use of foreign-built, foreign-flagged, or non-U.S.-owned vessels for coastwise merchandise transportation
- Penalty: Forfeiture of merchandise OR recovery of greater of (a) value of merchandise or (b) cost of transportation‚Å∑

**46 U.S.C. ¬ß 8103** crew citizenship violations:
- Governed by general civil penalty provisions under 46 U.S.C. Chapter 2
- Coast Guard enforcement through administrative civil penalties
- **No statutory vessel forfeiture provision** for crew citizenship violations alone

### C. Offshore Wind Vessels ‚Äì Foreign-Built COSCO Vessels

#### 1. Jones Act Requirements for Offshore Wind Operations

**Statutory Requirements:**

For vessels to engage in U.S. offshore wind operations on the Outer Continental Shelf (OCS), they must satisfy Jones Act coastwise endorsement requirements under **46 U.S.C. ¬ß 12112**:‚Å∏

1. **U.S.-Built:** Vessel must be constructed in the United States (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 12112(a)(2)(A))
2. **U.S.-Owned:** 75% of ownership at each tier must be U.S. citizens (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 50501)
3. **U.S.-Flagged:** Documented under U.S. laws with coastwise endorsement
4. **U.S.-Crewed:** 75% of unlicensed crew must be U.S. citizens (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 8103(b))

#### 2. CBP Offshore Wind Rulings (2023-2024)

**July 17, 2023 ‚Äì Initial Ruling:**
CBP ruled that foreign vessels could load monopile foundations in U.S. ports, transport them to offshore work sites, and install them.‚Åπ

**September 14, 2023 ‚Äì Revocation:**
CBP modified its July ruling in response to a petition from the American Maritime Partnership, determining that "the use of a foreign-flagged jack-up vessel to transport monopiles between a U.S. port and an installation site on the OCS would violate the Jones Act, because a second coastwise point is established once the jack-up vessel attaches itself to the seabed for the purposes of monopile installation."¬π‚Å∞

**August 21, 2024 ‚Äì Confirmation:**
CBP confirmed its September 2023 interpretation, solidifying that Jones Act-compliant vessels are required for offshore wind foundation installation when vessels attach to the seabed.¬π¬π

**Crew Transfer Vessel Exception:**
CBP rulings indicate that foreign wind turbine installation vessels (WTIVs) can move crew members and work materials from work site to work site because crew members are not considered "passengers" and materials are not considered "merchandise" under the Jones Act.¬π¬≤

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION:** This crew transfer exception applies to **installation operations** where vessels move between fixed turbines on the OCS. It does **NOT** apply to crew transfer vessels (CTVs) that transport workers **between U.S. ports and offshore installations**, which constitutes coastwise trade requiring Jones Act compliance.

### E. Successor Liability for Pre-Closing Jones Act Violations

#### 1. Transaction Structure: Stock Purchase

**PMSC Acquisition Structure:**
- Global Logistics Partners LLC acquiring 100% stock of PMSC (Delaware corporation)
- **Stock Purchase** = Acquirer steps into seller's shoes
- PMSC corporate entity continues to exist post-closing
- All pre-closing liabilities remain with PMSC corporate entity

#### 2. Federal Common Law of Successor Liability

**General Rule (State Law):**
In asset purchases under state law, buyers generally do NOT assume seller's liabilities except:
1. Express assumption in purchase agreement
2. De facto merger
3. Mere continuation of seller's business
4. Fraudulent transfer to escape liabilities

**Federal Common Law Exception:**
Federal appellate courts (Third, Sixth, Seventh, and Ninth Circuits) have recognized **federal common law successor liability** for certain federal statutes, including:
- Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)
- Title VII (employment discrimination)
- Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA)
- Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA)

**Key Principle:** Federal common law successor liability does NOT require commonality of ownership; **substantial continuity of operations** can be sufficient to impose successor liability.¬π¬≥

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION:** PMSC transaction is a **stock purchase**, not an asset purchase. Stock purchases trigger successor liability under traditional corporate law principles, NOT federal common law exceptions.

#### 3. Jones Act Cabotage Violations: Successor Liability Analysis

**Two Distinct Jones Act Regimes:**

**A. Jones Act Seaman Injury Claims (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 30104):**
- Personal injury claims by seamen against employers
- Federal common law successor liability **HAS BEEN APPLIED** to Jones Act seaman injury claims in some circuits
- Reasoning: Jones Act extends FELA to maritime workers; FELA successor liability precedent applies

**B. Jones Act Cabotage Violations (46 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß 55102, 8103):**
- Regulatory violations for use of foreign vessels or non-compliant crew
- **NO CASE LAW FOUND** applying federal common law successor liability to Jones Act cabotage penalties
- Distinguishable from seaman injury claims (tort liability vs. regulatory penalties)

#### 4. Stock Purchase Successor Liability: Automatic

**In Stock Purchase Transactions:**
- Acquirer (Global Logistics Partners) acquires **corporate entity** PMSC
- PMSC remains liable for **ALL pre-closing debts and obligations**
- **No distinction** between pre-closing and post-closing liabilities
- Acquirer cannot avoid liability by claiming transaction occurred

**Application to PMSC Crew Citizenship Violations:**

**Scenario 1: Violations Discovered Pre-Closing**
- Coast Guard issues civil penalty assessment against PMSC for M/V Pacific Guardian and M/V Pacific Shield crew non-compliance
- Penalty accrues pre-closing but unpaid at closing
- **Result:** Global Logistics Partners (as owner of PMSC stock) **inherits liability** for unpaid penalty
- **Exposure:** $10,000 - $50,000 (full penalty amount)

**Scenario 2: Violations Discovered Post-Closing for Pre-Closing Conduct**
- Coast Guard discovers crew non-compliance that occurred **before closing date**
- Penalty assessed **after closing date**
- **Result:** PMSC (now owned by Global Logistics Partners) **remains liable** for pre-closing conduct
- **Reasoning:** Corporate entity PMSC engaged in violations; change of stock ownership does not extinguish corporate liability

**Mitigation via Purchase Agreement:**

### F. Case Law Precedent: Furie Operating Alaska LLC (2017)

#### 1. Case Background

**Furie Operating Alaska LLC v. U.S. Department of Homeland Security** (settled April 4, 2017)

**Facts:**
- Furie transported Spartan 151 jack-up drilling rig from Gulf of Mexico (Texas) to Alaska in 2011
- Used foreign-flagged vessel (Chinese heavy-lift vessel *Kang Sheng Kou*) to transport rig from Texas around South America to Vancouver, Canada
- U.S.-flagged tugs towed rig from Vancouver to Alaska
- Furie applied for Jones Act waiver from Secretary of Homeland Security; waiver **denied**
- Rationale for denial: U.S. citizen-owned vessels were available to perform transportation

**CBP Penalty Assessment:**
- CBP initially assessed **$15 million** penalty (largest Jones Act penalty in history)
- Penalty based on value of drilling rig transported in violation of 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 55102

**Settlement:**
- Furie negotiated penalty down to **$10 million** (33% reduction)
- Furie paid $10 million to settle federal claims in April 2017
- Settlement represented largest Jones Act penalty ever paid¬π‚Å¥

#### 2. Legal Significance

**Penalty Calculation Methodology:**
- CBP assessed penalty based on **fair market value of merchandise** transported (drilling rig = "merchandise")
- CBP rejected argument that drilling rig should be classified as "vessel" (which would trigger lower penalty under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 55111 towing statute: $1,100 + $60 per ton)
- Court reasoning: Jack-up drilling rigs transported aboard another vessel are "merchandise" in commercial sense, not vessels

**Mitigation Factors:**
- CBP reduced penalty from $15M to $10M (33% mitigation)
- Factors considered:
  - Furie's good faith belief it could obtain waiver
  - Lack of willful intent to violate Jones Act
  - Procedural defects in waiver application process
  - Economic impact of full penalty on company
- **Mitigation authority:** Commissioner of CBP has discretion to adjust penalties if violation incurred without willful negligence or intent to defraud

#### 3. Implications for PMSC

**Distinguishing Factors:**

**Furie Case:**
- Foreign vessel transporting merchandise (drilling rig)
- Violation of 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 55102 (merchandise transportation)
- Penalty based on value of merchandise ($15M initial assessment)
- Single voyage violation

**PMSC Crew Violations:**
- U.S.-built, U.S.-flagged vessels with non-compliant crew percentages
- Violation of 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 8103 (crew citizenship requirements)
- Penalty based on administrative civil penalty framework ($10K-$50K range)
- Multiple voyage operations over extended period

**Key Takeaway:** Furie case demonstrates CBP's willingness to assess **multi-million dollar penalties** for Jones Act merchandise transportation violations, but crew citizenship violations are governed by **different penalty regime** (civil penalties vs. merchandise forfeiture).
