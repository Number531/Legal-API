## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report analyzes the marine insurance program for Pacific Maritime Services Corporation (PMSC) in connection with the proposed $4.8 billion acquisition by Global Logistics Partners LLC, focusing on coverage adequacy for identified liabilities, insurance recovery gaps from recent casualties, and acquisition-related continuity issues. The analysis encompasses Protection & Indemnity (P&I) coverage, Hull & Machinery (H&M) insurance, Loss of Hire coverage, Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA) self-insurance, and Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90) compliance.

### Key Findings

**1. P&I Coverage Structure and Adequacy**

PMSC maintains Protection & Indemnity coverage through the American Steamship Owners Mutual Protection & Indemnity Association (The American Club), a member of the International Group of P&I Clubs. The International Group provides pooled coverage with total capacity of **$3.1 billion per occurrence** for the 2024/25 policy year, structured as follows: individual club retention $10M per claim, Pool layer $90M excess of $10M (claims pooled between Group Clubs), Group Excess of Loss (GXL) reinsurance $2B excess of $100M ($650M + $750M + $600M layers), and Collective Overspill $1B excess of GXL. PMSC's stated P&I coverage limit of $1 billion per occurrence falls within this structure.

P&I insurance covers "practically all maritime liability risks associated with the ownership and operation of a vessel," including loss of life, injury, and illness of crewmembers, passengers, and third parties; damage to cargo on board; collision liability; wreck removal; pollution cleanup costs; and defense costs. For PMSC's identified liabilities, P&I coverage provides adequate capacity: Martinez Section 905(b) litigation ($8.5M claimed, $2M-$5M expected verdict), March 2023 bunker spill cleanup costs ($850K), and pending NOAA Natural Resource Damage (NRD) assessment ($2M-$5M). Total exposure of approximately $3.7M-$11.7M is well within the $1B stated limit and far below the International Group's $3.1B total capacity.

**2. Critical Coverage Gap: Section 905(b) Vessel Negligence â€” MEL Policy Verification Required**

**DEAL-CRITICAL FINDING:** Section 905(b) vessel negligence claims by longshoremen are typically covered through **Maritime Employer's Liability (MEL) policies** or specific P&I club endorsements, NOT standard P&I club coverage. This distinction is critical for the Martinez v. PMSC litigation.

Maritime Employer's Liability (MEL) insurance provides protection when employees are working on non-owned or operated vessels. Typical MEL policies cover "sums which the employer shall become legally obligated to pay as damages under the Jones Act, the Death on the High Seas Act, or the General Maritime Law of the United States," including Section 905(b) claims for vessel negligence. However, P&I clubs provide liability coverage for personal injury or death of "any person engaged to handle the Cargo of an Entered Ship," limited to liabilities arising out of negligent acts in relation to the ship or cargo handling. Standard P&I club rules may exclude or limit coverage for longshoreman claims that are not crew members.

Section 905(b) of the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b)) allows injured maritime workers to bring third-party tort lawsuits against vessel owners for vessel negligence. The vessel owner owes three narrow duties: (1) turnover duty, (2) active control duty, and (3) duty to intervene. Notably, Section 905(b) voids agreements to indemnify between an injured longshoreman's employer and the vessel upon which he is injured, and insurance policies only apply to obligations that the insured "shall become legally obligated to pay" â€” not void indemnity agreements.

**Application to Martinez v. PMSC:** Juan Martinez, a longshoreman, was injured September 2023 when crushed by a container after lashing gear failed on M/V Pacific Horizon at Long Beach Terminal. He sustained bilateral leg fractures requiring multiple surgeries with permanent disability. Martinez has received $420K in LHWCA benefits to date and filed a Section 905(b) tort claim seeking $8.5M damages ($1.2M medical, $2.8M lost wages, $4.5M pain/suffering). Trial is scheduled Q3 2025. Expected verdict after comparative fault reduction (Martinez 30-50% at fault for safety violation): **$2M-$5M**.

**Coverage Analysis:** If PMSC has MEL coverage in addition to P&I, Section 905(b) claims are covered subject to the stated $500K deductible (PMSC retains $500K; P&I/MEL covers balance). If relying solely on P&I club coverage without MEL endorsement, there may be a **$2M-$5M uninsured exposure** plus defense costs ($300K-$500K), totaling **$2.3M-$5.5M potential gap**.

**RECOMMENDATION:** PMSC's insurance program must be confirmed to include MEL coverage or a P&I endorsement specifically covering Section 905(b) vessel negligence. This verification is **deal-critical** and should be completed in data room review. If no MEL coverage exists, Global Logistics Partners should negotiate purchase price adjustment or obtain MEL policy pre-closing (estimated $500K-$1M annual premium for $10M limits).

**3. Hull & Machinery Coverage Gaps: M/V Pacific Titan BWMS Exclusion**

M/V Pacific Titan hull corrosion incident (November 2024, Keppel Singapore drydock) resulted in **$9.85M net cost to PMSC** due to insurance recovery gaps. Total repair costs were $18.7M ($4.2M hull plate replacement, $2.8M BWMS replacement, $2.38M drydock extension, $9.3M lost revenue from 1 missed voyage). Insurance recovery totaled $8.85M ($4.2M hull net of $2M deductible, $4.65M loss of hire for 14 days after waiting period), leaving PMSC with $9.85M uninsured exposure.

**Critical BWMS Exclusion:** Insurers paid $4.2M for hull plate replacement only, excluding the $2.8M BWMS (Ballast Water Management System) replacement. The $800K shortfall (net of deductible) indicates insurers argued BWMS is not covered under standard hull policy and requires separate machinery breakdown endorsement. This creates **$2M-$4M per incident future risk** across PMSC's 22-vessel fleet, all of which require BWMS compliance under 33 CFR Part 151 Subpart D and IMO BWM Convention.

Standard Hull & Machinery insurance exclusions include: (1) wear and tear ("something that gives out because it's just old and worn out is not insurable"), (2) gradual deterioration including saltwater corrosion, (3) weathering, and (4) lack of due diligence/maintenance failure. Insurance is designed to protect against sudden and unforeseen losses, not inevitable decline in asset condition due to regular use and age. The critical question: Was the hull corrosion "sudden and accidental" (covered) or "gradual deterioration" (excluded)?

**Arguments for Coverage:** BWMS failure was the proximate cause (equipment malfunction, not wear and tear); corrosion accelerated due to BWMS failure, not normal aging; vessel was properly maintained with BWMS system defect causing damage; analogous to 2024 case precedent where expert metallurgist analysis determined corrosion resulted from design defect (not maintenance failure), leading to coverage despite insurers' maintenance exclusion argument.

**Arguments Against Coverage:** Conditional COI reinstatement (per research-plan.md) suggests corrosion pre-dated policy period (pre-existing condition); corrosion from 8mm to 6mm indicates gradual deterioration over time; ballast tank corrosion is a maintenance-related issue; insurers' partial payment ($4.2M for plates only) suggests they view BWMS as maintenance-related equipment.

**Likely Outcome:** Insurers paid for emergency hull plate replacement to restore seaworthiness but excluded BWMS replacement as either: (1) maintenance-related equipment failure (wear and tear exclusion), OR (2) separate coverage requiring machinery breakdown endorsement.

**RECOMMENDATION:** (1) Challenge BWMS exclusion with expert metallurgist report analyzing causation, (2) Obtain machinery breakdown endorsement for all 22 vessels (estimated $500K-$800K annual premium), (3) Implement enhanced BWMS maintenance program ($150K-$250K annually per research-plan.md) to avoid future disputes.

**4. Loss of Hire Waiting Period: $4.65M Uninsured Retention**

Loss of Hire insurance protects shipowner's income if a casualty prevents the vessel from earning hire. Standard deductible periods are 14 or 30 days, with typical maximum coverage of 90, 120, or 180 days. PMSC's Loss of Hire policy has a **14-day waiting period**, meaning the first 14 days of lost revenue are not covered.

**M/V Pacific Titan Calculation:** Total drydock time was 28 days (extended due to hull corrosion repairs). Daily rate: $9.3M lost revenue Ã· 28 days = **$332,143 per day**. Uncovered loss (first 14 days): **$4.65M**. Covered loss (days 15-28): $4.65M paid by insurers. PMSC bore the full 14-day waiting period loss of $4.65M, which is standard industry practice but represents significant uninsured retention.

Precedent supports coverage for drydock delays: "The 15 days it took to clean the dock was deemed a foreseeable consequence of the damage and allowed under the loss of hire insurance, even if it was not the damage to the vessel itself that caused the delay." The 28-day drydock extension for M/V Pacific Titan (beyond standard drydock time) correctly qualified for loss of hire coverage as a foreseeable consequence of hull corrosion damage. Insurers appropriately paid for days 15-28.

**Options to Reduce Waiting Period:** Some insurers offer lower deductible options: "For suitable risk profiles, options exist to lower the deductible to 7 days through specific coverage programs." PMSC should consider negotiating a 7-day waiting period for critical vessels (ULCVs like M/V Pacific Titan with daily hire rates >$300K), which would reduce uninsured exposure by **$2.3M for a 28-day casualty** (7 days Ã— $332,143 = $2.3M saved). **8. Policy Continuity and Change of Control Provisions**

Unlike D&O and professional liability policies (which are typically terminated during M&A), **marine insurance policies (H&M, P&I) are "occurrence-based" or "period of insurance" policies, NOT claims-made policies**. This distinction is critical:

**Occurrence-Based Policies (H&M, P&I):** Coverage attaches based on when the incident occurred, not when the claim was made. Change of control does NOT terminate coverage for incidents during the policy period. Policies continue until expiration date regardless of ownership change. No "retroactive date" concern (unlike claims-made policies).

**Claims-Made Policies (D&O, E&O):** Coverage attaches when claim is made to insurer. Change of control provisions can terminate coverage or force "run-off" tail coverage. Retroactive date specifies how far back retrospective cover applies. "Tail coverage" costs 75%-300% of annual premium.

**Required Actions for Acquisition:**
1. **Notify Lloyd's of London syndicates** (H&M insurers) of change of ownership per Institute Time Clauses Hulls 1995, which requires notice of change of ownership or management
2. **Notify American Steamship Owners Mutual P&I Association** of change of control
3. **Confirm policy continuity** through policy anniversary date (annual renewal)
4. **Request "no material change" letters** confirming coverage continuity through acquisition closing
5. **Request continuity commission** if renewing with same leading underwriter (calculated as percentage of net annual premium)

**Institute Time Clauses Hulls 1995 (ITC):** H&M policies typically based on ITC 1995 adopt a named perils approach with coverage for fortuitous losses, subject to English law and practice. The 1995 revision introduced widening of the Inchmaree Clause such that want of due diligence was extended to cover acts by "superintendents or onshore management." The Inchmaree Clause seeks to widen cover and includes, for example, negligence of ship's master, officers, crew, or pilots.

**Recommendation:** Maintain continuity with American Club for P&I coverage (switching P&I clubs disrupts International Group pooling benefits and $3.1B capacity). For H&M, negotiate with incumbent Lloyd's syndicates but be prepared to market to alternative syndicates if premium increases >20%.

#### 3. Section 905(b) Vessel Negligence Claims: Coverage Analysis

**Critical Issue:** Does P&I cover Section 905(b) vessel negligence claims by longshoremen?

**Finding:** Section 905(b) claims are typically covered through **Maritime Employer's Liability (MEL) policies** or endorsements, NOT standard P&I club coverage. This distinction is critical for the Martinez litigation.

**Maritime Employer's Liability (MEL) Insurance:**
- MEL coverage provides protection when employees are working on non-owned or -operated vessels
- Typical MEL policy covers "sums which the employer shall become legally obligated to pay as damages under the Jones Act, the Death on the High Seas Act, or the General Maritime Law of the United States, including transportation, unearned wages, maintenance and cure and burial expenses"
- Section 905(b) claims are specifically addressed in MEL policies

*Sources: [Maritime Employer's Liability Coverage](http://www.insuremarine.com/MC/mel.html); [What is MEL Coverage?](https://www.merrimacins.com/what-is-maritime-employers-liability-coverage/)*

**Section 905(b) Legal Framework:**
- 33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b) allows injured maritime workers to bring third-party tort lawsuits against vessel owners for vessel negligence
- Vessel owner owes three narrow duties: (1) turnover duty, (2) active control duty, and (3) duty to intervene
- **Coverage Void Provision:** Section 905(b) voids agreements to indemnify between an injured longshoreman's employer and the vessel upon which he is injured
- Insurance policies only apply to obligations that the insured "shall become legally obligated to pay" â€” not void indemnity agreements

*Sources: [Section 905(b) Claims Overview](https://blog.amequity.com/longshore-insider/article/section-905b); [Section 905(b) Indemnity Void](https://lewisbrisbois.com/newsroom/articles/section-905b-of-the-lhwca-applied-to-void-indemnity-under-contract-and-insu)*

**Application to Martinez v. PMSC:**
- **Claim:** $8.5M claimed ($1.2M medical, $2.8M lost wages, $4.5M pain/suffering)
- **Expected verdict:** $2M-$5M after comparative fault reduction (Martinez 30-50% at fault for safety violation)
- **Coverage Question:** If PMSC has MEL coverage in addition to P&I, Section 905(b) claims are covered. If relying solely on P&I club coverage, there may be a **coverage gap**.
- **P&I Club Position:** P&I clubs provide liability coverage for personal injury or death of "any person engaged to handle the Cargo of an Entered Ship," but this is limited to liabilities arising out of negligent acts in relation to the ship or cargo handling
- **Deductible:** Stated as $500K per claim â€” Martinez verdict would require PMSC to pay $500K deductible + P&I/MEL covers balance

**CRITICAL FINDING:** PMSC's insurance program must be confirmed to include MEL coverage or a P&I endorsement specifically covering Section 905(b) vessel negligence. Standard P&I rules may exclude or limit coverage for longshoreman claims that are not crew members.

#### 4. P&I Club Exclusions: Fines, Penalties, and Punitive Damages

**General Rule:** P&I insurance generally does NOT cover:

### B. Hull & Machinery (H&M) Insurance Coverage

#### 1. H&M Coverage Scope and Standard Exclusions

**General Coverage:** Hull and Machinery insurance covers physical loss or damage to the vessel, including its hull and machinery, with the damage generally needing to be accidental or out of control to be eligible for compensation.

2. **Gradual Deterioration:** Including saltwater corrosion
3. **Weathering**
4. **Insects, mold, animals, and marine life effects**
5. **Lack of Due Diligence:** Maintenance failure

*Sources: [Common Hull Insurance Exclusions](https://marlinblue.com/the-most-common-hull-insurance-exclusions/); [Hull Insurance Exclusions Guide](https://nesika.ca/hull-machinery-insurance-part-2-common-exclusions-and-requirements/)*

Expert metallurgist analysis determined corrosion resulted from design defect, not maintenance failure, leading to coverage. ### E. Change of Control Provisions and Policy Continuity

#### 1. Marine Insurance Policies and Change of Control

Unlike D&O and professional liability policies, which are typically terminated during mergers and acquisitions, **marine insurance policies (Hull & Machinery, P&I) are "occurrence-based" or "period of insurance" policies**, NOT claims-made policies. This distinction is critical:

**Occurrence-Based Policies (H&M, P&I):**
- Coverage attaches based on when the incident occurred, not when the claim was made
- Change of control does NOT terminate coverage for incidents during the policy period
- Policies continue until expiration date regardless of ownership change
- No "retroactive date" concern (unlike claims-made policies)

*Source: [Laws on Marine Insurance](https://nmmra.org/laws-on-marine-insurance/)*

**Claims-Made Policies (D&O, E&O):**
- Coverage attaches when claim is made to insurer
- Change of control provisions can terminate coverage or force "run-off" tail coverage
- Retroactive date specifies how far back in time the retrospective cover applies
- "Tail coverage" costs 75%-300% of annual premium

*Sources: [Change in Control & D&O Policy](https://thecoylegroup.com/change-in-control/); [Claims-Made Retroactive Dates](https://thecoylegroup.com/claims-made/)*
