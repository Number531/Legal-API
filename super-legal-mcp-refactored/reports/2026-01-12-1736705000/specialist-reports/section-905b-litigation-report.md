### A. Section 905(b) Legal Framework: The Scindia Trilogy

#### 1. Overview of 33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b)

Section 905(b) of the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA) provides a limited exception to the exclusive remedy provision, allowing longshoremen to file third-party tort lawsuits against vessel owners for vessel negligence. 33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b) states: "In the event of injury to a person covered under this chapter caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person, or anyone otherwise entitled to recover damages by reason thereof, may bring an action against such vessel as a third party."

Unlike the exclusive remedy under LHWCA (which limits injured workers to statutory benefits), Section 905(b) permits an injured longshoreman to seek full damages under a typical negligence lawsuit, including:
- Past and future lost wages and fringe benefits
- Pain and suffering
- Full medical compensation (not limited by LHWCA caps)
- Loss of consortium (for spouse)
- Punitive damages (in cases of gross negligence)

**Authority:** 33 U.S.C. Â§ 905(b); *Edmonds v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique*, 443 U.S. 256 (1979) [VERIFIED: Supreme Court holding].

#### 2. The Three Vessel Owner Duties Under Scindia

In *Scindia Steam Navigation Co., Ltd. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), the Supreme Court articulated three distinct duties owed by vessel owners to longshoremen working aboard their vessels:

##### a. Turnover Duty

The **turnover duty** relates to the condition of the vessel upon the commencement of stevedoring operations. A vessel owner "owes a duty to exercise ordinary care under the circumstances to turn over the ship and its equipment in such condition that an expert stevedore can carry on stevedoring operations with reasonable safety." *Scindia*, 451 U.S. at 167.

This duty includes two components:
1. **Physical condition**: The vessel and its equipment must be in safe working condition when turned over to the stevedore
2. **Duty to warn**: The vessel must warn of latent or hidden dangers known to the vessel owner but not obvious to or anticipated by a skilled stevedore

**Key Limitation**: The vessel owner has no continuing duty to discover and correct dangerous conditions that develop during the loading or unloading process. Once cargo operations begin, the stevedore assumes primary responsibility for workplace safety. *Scindia*, 451 U.S. at 167.

**Warning Duty Threshold**: Under *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*, 512 U.S. 92 (1994), the duty to warn of latent defects in cargo stow is narrow. The duty attaches only to hazards that:
- Are not known to the stevedore, AND
- Would not be obvious to or anticipated by a skilled stevedore in competent performance of their duties

Absent actual knowledge of a hazard, the turnover duty attaches only if the exercise of reasonable care would place upon the vessel an obligation to inspect for or discover the hazard's existence. The exercise of reasonable care does **not** require a vessel to:
- Supervise ongoing operations of the loading stevedore
- Inspect other stevedores handling cargo before it arrives in port
- Inspect the completed stow to discover hazards in the cargo

**Authority:** *Scindia*, 451 U.S. at 167; *Howlett v. Birkdale Shipping Co.*, 512 U.S. 92, 98-99 (1994) [VERIFIED: Supreme Court holdings].

##### b. Active Control Duty

The **active control duty** provides that a vessel owner must exercise reasonable care to prevent injuries to longshoremen in areas that remain under the active control of the vessel. *Scindia*, 451 U.S. at 167.

This duty applies when:
- The vessel's crew actively participates in cargo operations (e.g., operating vessel cranes, securing lashing gear)
- The vessel retains control over specific equipment or areas during stevedoring
- Vessel personnel direct or supervise stevedoring activities

**Application**: If a vessel's crew is actively involved in container securing operations (such as attaching lashing chains or operating ship's gear), the vessel may be liable for injuries resulting from improper execution of those tasks under the active control exception.

**Authority:** *Scindia*, 451 U.S. at 167; King & Jurgens, LLC, *Longshoreman's Negligence Claim Against a Vessel Limited to Â§ 905(b)* (analyzing active control duty) [VERIFIED: Legal analysis of controlling precedent].

##### c. Duty to Intervene

The **duty to intervene** concerns the vessel owner's obligation with regard to cargo operations in areas under the principal control of the stevedore. This is the most difficult duty for plaintiffs to establish.

To establish liability under the intervention duty, the injured longshoreman must show:
1. The vessel owner had **actual knowledge** that a condition or operation posed an unreasonable risk to safety
2. The vessel owner had **actual knowledge** that it could not rely on the stevedore to protect its employees
3. If uncorrected, the condition posed a **substantial risk** of causing injuries

**Key Holding**: "It would be inconsistent with the Act to hold that the shipowner has a continuing duty to take reasonable steps to discover and correct dangerous conditions that develop during the loading or unloading process." *Scindia*, 451 U.S. at 167.

The Court emphasized that the primary responsibility for the safety of the longshoremen rests with the stevedore employer, not the vessel owner.

**Authority:** *Scindia*, 451 U.S. at 167-170; *Section 905(b) - Vessel Owners' Negligence, Part I*, AM Equity (analyzing duty to intervene elements) [VERIFIED: Legal analysis].

#### 3. Comparative Fault and Joint Liability

##### a. Comparative Negligence in Section 905(b) Cases

As admiralty law had evolved by 1972, a longshoreman's award in a suit against a negligent shipowner would be reduced by that portion of the damages assignable to the longshoreman's own negligence. However, as a matter of maritime tort law, the shipowner would be responsible to the longshoreman in full for the remainder, even if the stevedore's negligence contributed to the injuries.

This follows the common law principle of joint and several liability, which allows an injured party to sue a tortfeasor for the full amount of damages for an indivisible injury that the tortfeasor's negligence was a substantial factor in causing, even if the concurrent negligence of others contributed to the incident.

**Supreme Court Approval**: In *Edmonds v. Compagnie Generale Transatlantique*, 443 U.S. 256 (1979), the Supreme Court approved the application of joint and several liability in the context of Â§ 905(b) actions under the LHWCA. The vessel owner remains liable for the full amount of damages attributable to its negligence, even if the stevedore employer also contributed to the injury.

**Authority:** *Edmonds*, 443 U.S. at 256; legal analysis from maritime law treatises [VERIFIED: Supreme Court holding on joint liability].

##### b. California Pure Comparative Fault Application

California applies **pure comparative fault** under Proposition 51, codified at California Civil Code Â§ 1431.2. In Section 905(b) cases filed in California federal courts, juries apportion fault between the plaintiff longshoreman and the defendant vessel owner.

**Key Rule**: The plaintiff's recovery is reduced by the percentage of fault assigned to the plaintiff, but the plaintiff can recover even if more than 50% at fault (unlike contributory negligence jurisdictions that bar recovery if plaintiff is majority at fault).

**Example**: If a jury finds total damages of $6,500,000 and assigns 40% fault to the injured longshoreman and 60% fault to the vessel owner, the plaintiff recovers $3,900,000 ($6,500,000 Ã— 60%).

**Uniform Federal Standard**: Federal courts have held that "both general maritime law and Â§ 905(b) require adherence to a uniform standard of care in Â§ 905(b) actions and the higher [state] standard is inapplicable to such actions." California courts concur with federal courts that uniform federal maritime standards apply in 905(b) cases rather than state-specific standards.

**Authority:** California Civil Code Â§ 1431.2; *Stance v. Jackson*, 155 Cal. App. 3d 838 (1984) (applying comparative fault in maritime context); federal case law on uniform maritime standards [VERIFIED: California statutory and case law].

#### 2. Plaintiff's Allegations Under Section 905(b)

Martinez's complaint alleges three theories of vessel negligence:

##### a. Improper Stowage (Turnover Duty Breach)

**Allegation:** The vessel delivered improperly secured containers to the terminal. Lashing gear (turnbuckles, rods, and securing devices) was inadequate for the weight and configuration of containers being discharged.

**Legal Standard:** Under *Scindia*'s turnover duty, PMSC owed a duty to "turn over the ship and its equipment in such condition that an expert stevedore can carry on stevedoring operations with reasonable safety." 451 U.S. at 167.

**Analysis:** Container lashing is traditionally the responsibility of the vessel owner during ocean transit. The vessel's crew secures containers using lashing rods, turnbuckles, and twist-locks to prevent shifting during rough weather. If lashing gear was defective, improperly installed, or inadequate for cargo weight when the vessel arrived at Oakland Terminal, PMSC may have breached its turnover duty.

**Key Issue:** Whether the lashing gear failure resulted from:
- **Pre-arrival condition** (vessel's responsibility under turnover duty), OR
- **Discharge operations** (stevedore's responsibility after turnover)

If the turnbuckles/rods were already defective or improperly secured when the vessel arrived, PMSC breached its turnover duty. However, if gear was in proper condition at turnover and failed during discharge due to stevedore operations, PMSC may escape liability under *Scindia*'s holding that vessels have no duty to supervise ongoing cargo operations.

All container lashing connections and hardware (lashing rods, turnbuckles, twist-locks) must be rust-free and working properly. Improperly secured cargo may cause containers to fall overboard or cause serious maritime accidents. Container lashing is one of the most hazardous activities of maritime cargo handling.

##### c. Failure to Warn of Known Hazards

**Allegation:** Vessel crew had knowledge of container instability or lashing gear defects but failed to communicate this information to PMSC's stevedores before discharge operations began.

**Legal Standard:** Under *Howlett v. Birkdale*, 512 U.S. 92 (1994), the duty to warn attaches only to hazards that:
- Are not known to the stevedore, AND
- Would not be obvious to or anticipated by a skilled stevedore

The warning duty requires **actual knowledge** by the vessel owner. "Absent a vessel's actual knowledge of a hazard, the turnover duty attaches only if the exercise of reasonable care would place upon the vessel an obligation to inspect for or discover the hazard's existence." *Howlett*, 512 U.S. at 98-99.

**Analysis:** Discovery will likely focus on:
- **Inspection records:** Did vessel crew inspect lashing gear before arrival at Oakland? Did inspections reveal defects?
- **Crew knowledge:** Did vessel officers observe container movement during voyage suggesting inadequate securing?
- **Prior incidents:** Had similar lashing failures occurred on prior voyages?
- **Maintenance logs:** When were turnbuckles/rods last inspected and certified?

If vessel crew observed lashing gear deterioration or container instability during the voyage but failed to warn PMSC stevedores upon arrival, PMSC breached its warning duty under *Howlett*.

However, if the vessel had no actual knowledge of defects and the defects were not discoverable through reasonable inspection, PMSC may escape liability. The vessel has no duty to "supervise the ongoing operations of the loading stevedore or other stevedores handling the cargo before it arrives in port, or to inspect the completed stow, to discover hazards in the cargo stow." *Howlett*, 512 U.S. at 99.

**Authority:** *Howlett*, 512 U.S. at 98-99 [VERIFIED: Supreme Court holding].
