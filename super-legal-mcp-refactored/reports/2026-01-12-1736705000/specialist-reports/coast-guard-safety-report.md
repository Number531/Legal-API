## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report analyzes U.S. Coast Guard vessel safety compliance for Pacific Maritime Services Corporation's (PMSC) 22-vessel fleet (18 container ships, 4 bulk carriers) in connection with the $4.8B acquisition by Global Logistics Partners LLC. The analysis focuses on Certificate of Inspection (COI) requirements under 46 U.S.C. Chapter 33 and 46 CFR Subchapter I, drydocking compliance under 46 CFR ¬ß 91.40-3, ballast water management system (BWMS) compliance under 33 CFR Part 151 Subpart D, Port State Control (PSC) detention risk, and civil penalty exposure under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 2110.

### Key Findings Summary

**1. M/V Pacific Titan Hull Corrosion Incident (CRITICAL ISSUE #3) - RESOLVED**

The M/V Pacific Titan (22,000 TEU ULCV, built 2019) experienced hull corrosion in cargo hold ballast tanks during November 2024 drydocking/special survey at Keppel Shipyard, Singapore. Hull thickness gauging revealed 15 shell plates corroded from 8mm original thickness to 6mm actual (vs. 7mm minimum required per 46 CFR and ABS Rules), representing 25% thickness loss over approximately 5 years of service.

**Root Cause:** Ballast water management system (BWMS) failure led to improper ballast water treatment, creating highly corrosive environment in ballast tanks. Normal corrosion rate for properly treated ballast water is 0.1-0.2mm/year; M/V Pacific Titan experienced accelerated 0.4mm/year rate (2√ó normal).

**Coast Guard Response:** Certificate of Inspection (COI) suspended May 15, 2024 (NOTE: Timeline inconsistency with November 2024 drydocking requires clarification - see Section V.B.1) under 46 CFR ¬ß 31.10-21 authority for failure to maintain structural requirements. Following emergency repairs, Coast Guard reinstated COI on June 20, 2024 with conditional monitoring requirements.

**Financial Impact:**
- **Total Repair Costs:** $18.7M
  - Hull plate replacement: $4.2M (15 shell plates replaced)
  - BWMS replacement: $2.8M (new GEA Westfalia system installed)
  - Drydock extension: $2.38M (28-day stay vs. 14-day planned)
  - Lost revenue: $9.3M (1 missed trans-Pacific voyage)
- **Insurance Recovery:** $12.05M
  - Hull & machinery coverage: $2.2M (after $2M deductible)
  - Loss of hire: $4.65M (14 days covered after 14-day waiting period)
  - **Coverage Gap:** $800K BWMS replacement cost disputed by insurers (classified as "maintenance" rather than "casualty")
- **Net Cost to PMSC:** $6.65M absorbed in FY2024

**Conditional COI Requirements:**
1. **Enhanced BWMS Maintenance:** Quarterly inspections (vs. annual standard) including UV lamp replacement, filter replacement, chemical dosing calibration, discharge water sampling for BWDS compliance verification
   - **Cost Impact:** $150K-$250K annually per vessel (4 inspections √ó $40K-$60K each)
   - **Fleet-Wide Impact:** If extended to all 22 vessels (recommended best practice), annual BWMS costs increase from $550K-$1.1M baseline to $3.3M-$5.5M enhanced program (+$2.2M-$4.4M incremental)
   - **Transaction Data:** Research plan references "$150K-$250K annually fleet-wide" which appears to represent incremental cost amortized across fleet (~$200K per vessel)

2. **Accelerated Drydocking:** Next drydock mandated September 2026 (2.0 years from June 2024 reinstatement vs. standard 2.5-year interval) for comprehensive ballast tank re-inspection and hull thickness re-gauging

3. **Quarterly Reporting:** BWMS inspection results, BWDS test results, and ballast tank condition reports submitted to Coast Guard Officer in Charge, Marine Inspection (OCMI)

**Civil Penalty Assessment:** $0 - Coast Guard exercised enforcement discretion and DID NOT assess civil penalties (potential exposure: $15K-$165K under 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3318) because:
- Deficiency discovered during proactive self-inspection (ABS special survey) rather than Coast Guard enforcement action

**Residual Risk:** **MEDIUM** - 15-25% probability that September 2026 drydock will reveal recurring ballast tank corrosion (exposure: $5M-$15M for additional repairs, drydock extension, lost revenue if corrosion mechanism not fully addressed by BWMS replacement and enhanced maintenance program).

**3. Ballast Water Management System (BWMS) Compliance**

All 22 PMSC vessels are compliant with 33 CFR Part 151 Subpart D ballast water discharge requirements and met the **September 8, 2024 IMO D-2 standard compliance deadline**. Each vessel is equipped with Coast Guard-approved BWMS (per 46 CFR Part 162 type approval) using UV treatment, electrolysis, or chemical treatment technologies from vendors including GEA Westfalia, Alfa Laval PureBallast, Hyde Marine BALPURE, and other type-approved systems.

**Ballast Water Discharge Standard (BWDS) - 33 CFR ¬ß 151.2030:**

Vessels must meet the following biological limits when discharging ballast water into U.S. waters:
- **Organisms ‚â•50 micrometers:** <10 viable organisms per cubic meter
- **Organisms 10-49 micrometers:** <10 viable organisms per milliliter
- **Indicator Microbes:**
  - Toxicogenic *Vibrio cholerae*: <1 cfu per 100 mL
  - *Escherichia coli*: <250 cfu per 100 mL
  - Intestinal enterococci: <100 cfu per 100 mL

**Commissioning Testing Completed:** All PMSC vessels completed initial commissioning testing upon BWMS installation to validate mechanical, physical, chemical, and biological performance per IMO BWM Convention and Coast Guard requirements. However, **neither IMO nor Coast Guard currently require periodic re-testing** after commissioning to verify ongoing BWDS compliance, creating compliance risk.

**M/V Pacific Titan BWMS Failure - Causation Analysis:**

The BWMS failure on M/V Pacific Titan (specific failure mode not disclosed - likely UV lamp failure, chemical dosing malfunction, or filtration bypass) created a perfect storm for accelerated corrosion:

1. **Untreated Seawater:** BWMS failed to properly treat ballast water, allowing microbial organisms and dissolved oxygen to remain in ballast tanks
2. **Residual Treatment Chemicals:** Partially functioning BWMS introduced oxidants (from electrolysis) or UV byproducts without completing biological treatment
3. **Synergistic Corrosion:** Combination of untreated seawater + residual treatment chemicals created highly corrosive environment (more corrosive than either untreated seawater OR properly treated ballast water alone)
4. **Microbial-Induced Corrosion (MIC):** Biological organisms (sulfate-reducing bacteria, iron-oxidizing bacteria) colonized ballast tank surfaces, producing acidic byproducts that accelerated localized pitting corrosion
5. **5-Year Accumulation:** Corrosion progressed undetected from 2019 (vessel commissioning) to November 2024 (special survey hull thickness gauging), resulting in 2mm thickness loss (8mm ‚Üí 6mm)

**Root Cause - Inadequate BWMS Maintenance:**

Based on repair scope and conditional COI requirements, Coast Guard/ABS investigation likely determined:
1. **Failure to replace UV lamps** per manufacturer schedule (typical 8,000-14,000 operating hours, requiring replacement every 12-24 months depending on usage)
2. **Lack of BWMS performance monitoring** - No periodic discharge water sampling to verify BWDS compliance (not required by regulation, but industry best practice)
3. **Delayed detection** - Ballast tank corrosion only detected during mandatory 5-year special survey hull thickness gauging; no interim ballast tank internal inspections conducted

**Fleet-Wide BWMS Risk Assessment:**

| **Similar BWMS failures in other vessels** (if fleet-wide maintenance deficiency) | **10-15%** | $10M-$30M (2-3 vessels require hull plate replacement) | **URGENT** - Fleet-wide BWMS audit within 30 days |
| **BWMS approaching end of service life** (systems installed 2010-2015, now 10-15 years old) | **20-30%** | $2.8M √ó [vessels requiring BWMS replacement] = $5.6M-$8.4M for 2-3 vessels | **HIGH** - Identify BWMS age profile, budget proactive replacements FY2025-2026 |

**Enhanced BWMS Maintenance Program (Conditional COI Requirement):**

Coast Guard imposed the following BWMS maintenance requirements on M/V Pacific Titan as condition of COI reinstatement; **voluntary adoption fleet-wide is strongly recommended** to prevent recurring incidents:

**Standard BWMS Maintenance (Pre-Incident):**
- **Frequency:** Annual inspection
- **Scope:** UV lamp output testing, filter cleaning, chemical tank level checks
- **Cost:** $25K-$50K per vessel annually
- **Fleet-Wide Cost:** $550K-$1.1M annually (22 vessels)

**Enhanced BWMS Maintenance (Post-Incident Requirement):**
- **Frequency:** Quarterly inspections (every 3 months)
- **Scope:**
  - **UV Lamp Replacement:** Replace UV lamps even if within rated service life (proactive replacement vs. run-to-failure)
  - **Filter Replacement:** Complete filter cartridge replacement (vs. cleaning/backwashing)
  - **Chemical Dosing Calibration:** Recalibrate chemical dosing systems, verify chemical concentrations
  - **Discharge Water Sampling:** Collect ballast water discharge samples, test for BWDS compliance (organism counts, indicator microbes), test for corrosivity (pH, dissolved oxygen, chloride content)
  - **Ballast Tank Internal Inspection:** Annual ballast tank entry inspection (vs. standard drydock interval inspection every 2.5-5 years)
- **Cost:** $150K-$250K annually per vessel (4 inspections √ó $40K-$60K each)
- **Fleet-Wide Cost (if adopted voluntarily):** $3.3M-$5.5M annually (22 vessels)
- **Incremental Cost:** +$2.2M-$4.4M above baseline annual BWMS maintenance

**Transaction Impact:** Research plan references "$150K-$250K annually fleet-wide" as incremental cost, which equates to approximately $200K per vessel if amortized across all 22 vessels. This suggests either: (1) Enhanced maintenance required only for M/V Pacific Titan with proportional allocation methodology, or (2) Voluntary fleet-wide adoption with cost efficiencies from bulk service contracts.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Certificate of Inspection (COI) Regulatory Framework

#### 1. Statutory Foundation: 46 U.S.C. Chapter 33

The U.S. Coast Guard's vessel inspection and certification authority derives from **46 U.S.C. Chapter 33 (Inspection and Certificates)**, which consolidates laws governing vessel inspection that have developed over 140+ years.¬π Chapter 33 establishes:

- **Vessels Subject to Inspection** (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3301): All 22 PMSC vessels (18 container ships, 4 bulk carriers) fall within categories requiring Coast Guard inspection and certification¬≤
- **Certificate of Inspection Requirements** (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3302): Mandates COI issuance, display, compliance, and expiration requirements¬≥
- **Classification Societies** (46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3316): Authorizes delegation of inspection authority to recognized organizations like American Bureau of Shipping (ABS)‚Å¥

**Key Provision - 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3316 Classification Societies:**

46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3316 establishes the relationship between classification societies (such as ABS) and the Federal Government for promoting maritime safety.‚Åµ The statute provides:

> "In the inspection of hulls, boilers, and machinery, the current standards established by the American Bureau of Shipping and designated Rules for Building and Classing Steel Vessels respecting material and construction of hulls, boilers, and machinery, except as otherwise provided for by law and regulations in this chapter, **shall be accepted as standard by the Coast Guard**."‚Å∂

> "The approved plans and certificate of the American Bureau of Shipping, or other recognized classification society for classed vessels, **may be accepted by the Coast Guard as evidence of the structural efficiency of the hull** and reliability of machinery of vessels."‚Å∑

**Application to M/V Pacific Titan Incident:** The M/V Pacific Titan hull corrosion incident (November 2024) directly implicates this provision. As an ABS-classed vessel, the Coast Guard relied on ABS special survey findings (15 shell plates corroded from 8mm to 6mm vs. 7mm minimum) to suspend the COI. ABS hull thickness gauging during special survey triggered Coast Guard enforcement action.

#### 2. Regulatory Implementation: 46 CFR Subchapter I

Coast Guard regulations implementing Chapter 33 are found in **46 CFR Subchapter I - Cargo and Miscellaneous Vessels**.‚Å∏ All 22 PMSC vessels fall under Subchapter I jurisdiction.

**46 CFR Part 31 - Inspection and Certification:**‚Åπ

- **¬ß 31.10-3 - Certificate of Inspection:** Every cargo vessel must have a valid COI issued by the Coast Guard. The COI specifies authorized routes, cargo limitations, manning requirements, lifesaving equipment, and structural compliance.¬π‚Å∞

- **¬ß 31.10-5 - Application for Inspection:** Vessel owners must apply for initial inspection, annual inspection, periodic drydock inspection, and renewal inspection at COI expiration (typically 5 years).¬π¬π

- **¬ß 31.10-21 - Conditions of Inspection:** The Coast Guard may refuse to issue, suspend, or revoke a COI if the vessel fails to meet statutory or regulatory requirements, or if there is a failure to maintain safety requirements.¬π¬≤

**46 CFR Part 91 - Inspection and Certification (Subchapter I Vessels):**¬π¬≥

Part 91 provides detailed inspection requirements specific to cargo vessels, including drydocking intervals (discussed in Section IV.B below).

#### 3. COI Suspension Authority and M/V Pacific Titan Incident

**Regulatory Basis for COI Suspension:**

46 CFR ¬ß 31.10-21 authorizes the Coast Guard to **suspend a Certificate of Inspection** where:

1. The vessel does not meet the requirements of law or regulations; OR
2. There is a failure to maintain the safety requirements requisite to the issuance of a certificate of inspection.¬π‚Å¥

**M/V Pacific Titan Hull Corrosion - COI Suspension Analysis:**

The M/V Pacific Titan COI suspension (May 15, 2024 per research plan, though transaction data indicates November 2024 drydocking - timeline requires clarification) was based on **structural deficiency discovered during special survey**:

**Deficiency Details:**
- **Location:** Cargo hold ballast tanks (15 shell plates)
- **Original Thickness:** 8mm (design specification)
- **Actual Thickness (measured November 2024):** 6mm
- **Minimum Required Thickness:** 7mm per 46 CFR hull structural standards and ABS Rules
- **Thickness Loss:** 25% (2mm loss from 8mm original)
- **Corrosion Rate:** Accelerated internal corrosion due to BWMS failure

**Regulatory Violations:**

The 6mm actual thickness violated:
1. **ABS Rules for Building and Classing Steel Vessels** - Hull structural strength requirements (accepted as Coast Guard standard per 46 U.S.C. ¬ß 3316)
2. **46 CFR ¬ß 176.970 (analogous provision)** - Unsafe vessel condition prohibiting voyage
3. **Classification Society Standards** - ABS special survey hull thickness requirements

**Coast Guard Enforcement Action:**

Upon receiving ABS special survey report showing sub-standard hull thickness, the Coast Guard Officer in Charge, Marine Inspection (OCMI) at Singapore (or U.S. port of next arrival) exercised authority under 46 CFR ¬ß 31.10-21 to:

1. **Suspend COI** effective immediately (May 15, 2024 or upon discovery in November 2024)
2. **Prohibit vessel from proceeding to sea** until repairs completed
3. **Require emergency plate replacement** (15 shell plates replaced at Keppel Shipyard, Singapore)
4. **Mandate BWMS replacement** (root cause of accelerated corrosion)
5. **Require re-inspection** prior to COI reinstatement

**COI Reinstatement (June 20, 2024):**

Following completion of:
- Hull plate replacement ($4.2M)
- BWMS replacement ($2.8M - new GEA Westfalia system)
- Re-gauging of repaired areas
- ABS surveyor approval
- Coast Guard OCMI re-inspection

The Coast Guard issued a **conditional COI** with the following terms:
- **Enhanced Inspection Schedule:** BWMS quarterly inspections (vs. annual standard) at cost $150K-$250K annually fleet-wide
- **Next Drydock:** September 2026 (accelerated from standard 2.5-year interval to ensure no recurrence)
- **Ongoing Monitoring:** Hull thickness re-gauging at next drydock with focus on ballast tank areas

**Compliance Assessment:** The conditional COI reinstatement indicates Coast Guard satisfaction with emergency repairs but ongoing concern about root cause (BWMS failure leading to corrosion). The enhanced BWMS inspection schedule ($150K-$250K annually fleet-wide) represents an ongoing operational cost burden imposed as condition of continued operations.

### B. Drydocking Requirements and Fleet Compliance

#### 1. Statutory and Regulatory Framework

**46 CFR ¬ß 91.40-3 - Drydock Examination Intervals:**¬π‚Åµ

46 CFR ¬ß 91.40-3 establishes mandatory drydocking intervals for cargo vessels operating in salt water. Table 91.40-3(a) specifies:

**For "Ships" (including container ships and bulk carriers):**
- **Drydock Examination Interval:** 2.5 years
- **Internal Structural Examination Interval:** 2.5 years
- **Cargo Tank Internal Examination Interval:** 2.5 years (or as specified in Part 151)

**2.5-Year Interval Definition:**¬π‚Å∂

"Where Table 91.40-3(a) indicates a 2.5 year examination interval, it means **a vessel must undergo two examinations within any five year period**. **No more than three years may elapse between any two examinations.**"

**Application to PMSC Fleet:**

All 22 PMSC vessels (18 container ships, 4 bulk carriers) operating in salt water service are subject to the **2.5-year drydocking requirement**. This translates to:

- **Mandatory drydock every 2.5 years** (with maximum 3-year gap between examinations)
- **Two drydockings per vessel within each 5-year COI cycle**
- **Annual fleet drydocking burden:** Approximately 8-9 vessels per year (22 vessels √∑ 2.5 years = 8.8 vessels/year)

#### 2. Underwater Survey Alternative

### C. Ballast Water Management System (BWMS) Compliance

#### 1. Regulatory Framework: 33 CFR Part 151 Subpart D

**Statutory Basis:**

The **National Invasive Species Act of 1996** (NISA), reauthorized and amended by the **Vessel Incidental Discharge Act of 2018** (VIDA), directs the Coast Guard to regulate ballast water discharges to prevent introduction of aquatic nuisance species into U.S. waters.¬≤¬≥

**33 CFR ¬ß 151.2025 - Ballast Water Management Requirements:**¬≤‚Å¥

All vessels operating in U.S. waters must:

1. **Install Coast Guard-approved BWMS** (per 46 CFR Part 162 type approval)
2. **Maintain BWMS per manufacturer specifications**
3. **Meet ballast water discharge standard (BWDS)** at all times of discharge

**33 CFR ¬ß 151.2030 - Ballast Water Discharge Standard (BWDS):**¬≤‚Åµ

Vessels must meet the following biological standards when discharging ballast water into U.S. waters:

| Organism Size | Discharge Limit |
|---------------|-----------------|
| **‚â•50 micrometers** | <10 viable organisms per cubic meter |
| **10-49 micrometers** | <10 viable organisms per milliliter |
| **Indicator Microbes:** | |
| - Toxicogenic *Vibrio cholerae* | <1 colony forming unit (cfu) per 100 mL |
| - *Escherichia coli* | <250 cfu per 100 mL |
| - Intestinal enterococci | <100 cfu per 100 mL |

**Compliance Deadline:**

By **September 8, 2024**, all ships were mandated to comply with the IMO D-2 standard (equivalent to U.S. BWDS above).¬≤‚Å∂

#### 3. M/V Pacific Titan BWMS Failure and Hull Corrosion Causation

**BWMS Failure Mechanism:**

The M/V Pacific Titan hull corrosion incident (November 2024) was **directly caused by BWMS failure**:

**Normal BWMS Operation:**
- Ballast water intake ‚Üí UV treatment or chemical dosing ‚Üí Kills organisms ‚Üí Treated water stored in ballast tanks
- Treated ballast water has **reduced biological activity** ‚Üí Less microbial-induced corrosion (MIC)
- Modern BWMS-treated ballast water is **more corrosive than untreated seawater** due to chemical byproducts (oxidants from electrolysis, UV byproducts)

**M/V Pacific Titan BWMS Failure:**
- BWMS **failed to treat ballast water properly** (specific failure mode not disclosed - possible UV lamp failure, chemical dosing system malfunction, or filtration bypass)
- **Untreated seawater + residual treatment chemicals** created highly corrosive environment in ballast tanks
- **Accelerated internal corrosion** of hull shell plating in ballast tank areas
- **15 shell plates corroded** from 8mm original thickness ‚Üí 6mm actual (vs. 7mm minimum required) in approximately **5 years** (vessel built 2019, incident 2024)
- **Corrosion rate:** 2mm loss over 5 years = **0.4mm/year** (vs. normal 0.1-0.2mm/year for properly treated ballast water)

**Root Cause Analysis:**

The Coast Guard and ABS investigation (details not publicly available, but inferred from repairs required) likely determined:

1. **Inadequate BWMS maintenance** - Failure to replace UV lamps, clean filters, calibrate chemical dosing per manufacturer schedule
2. **Lack of BWMS performance monitoring** - No periodic testing of discharge water quality to verify BWDS compliance
3. **Delayed detection** - Ballast tank corrosion not detected until special survey (hull thickness gauging every 5 years), allowing 5 years of accelerated corrosion
