### A. NLRA Successorship Doctrine: Mandatory Recognition and Bargaining

**Legal Framework [VERIFIED via Supreme Court Precedent]:**
Under *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services, Inc.*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972), and *Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987), Global Logistics Partners inherits duty to recognize and bargain in good faith with PMSC's unions if: (1) substantial continuity of business operations exists, and (2) majority of successor's employees were employed by predecessor. 29 U.S.C. § 158(a)(5) requires employers to "bargain collectively with representatives of its employees" regarding "wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment." 29 U.S.C. § 158(d).

**Application to PMSC Acquisition:**
- **Structure:** Stock purchase of 100% PMSC equity; PMSC continues as same legal entity
- **Workforce continuity:** 3,200 ILWU longshoremen, 800 MM&P officers, 1,600 SIU unlicensed crew retained post-closing = 100% continuity
- **Operations:** Four terminals (LA, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle) continue substantially unchanged
- **Conclusion:** Burns substantial continuity test clearly satisfied; successorship obligations attach

**Critical Distinction from Howard Johnson:**
*Howard Johnson Co. v. Detroit Local Joint Executive Board*, 417 U.S. 249 (1974), held that asset purchaser hiring only 9 of 53 predecessor employees had no successorship obligations due to lack of workforce continuity. PMSC acquisition is opposite: full workforce retention = full successorship obligations.

**Key Holdings:**
1. **Duty to Recognize:** Global Logistics Partners must recognize ILWU, MM&P, and SIU as exclusive bargaining representatives
2. **Duty to Bargain in Good Faith:** Must negotiate over wages, hours, terms, and conditions of employment
3. **NOT Bound by Existing CBAs:** Successor not bound by substantive terms of PCLCA (2022-2028), MM&P, or SIU agreements unless explicitly assumed in purchase agreement. Can negotiate modifications but must bargain in good faith.
4. **Cannot Unilaterally Change Terms:** If Global Logistics Partners is "perfectly clear" successor (retaining all employees), cannot set initial terms without first bargaining with unions

**Hiring Hall Continuation Mandatory:**
ILWU hiring hall system established in 1934 following Pacific Coast strike [VERIFIED via ILWU Local 19 archives]. Features union-elected dispatchers and "low-man-out" dispatch (worker with least hours dispatched first to equalize income). While Taft-Hartley Act (1947) outlawed closed shops, ILWU hiring halls continue under union security provisions giving ILWU members preference. Any attempt to bypass hiring halls or establish company-controlled hiring violates § 8(a)(5), triggering unfair labor practice charges before NLRB.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. NLRA Successorship Doctrine: Global Logistics Partners' Obligations

#### 1. The Burns-Fall River Successorship Framework

Under established Supreme Court precedent, Global Logistics Partners LLC's acquisition of Pacific Maritime Services Corporation triggers NLRA successorship analysis under two foundational cases: *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services, Inc.*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972), and *Fall River Dyeing & Finishing Corp. v. NLRB*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987).

**Burns Substantial Continuity Test [VERIFIED]:**
A successor employer has a duty to bargain with the predecessor's union if: (1) there is substantial continuity of business operations, and (2) a majority of the successor's bargaining unit employees were employed by the predecessor. *Burns*, 406 U.S. at 278-281. The Court held that "a mere change of employers or of ownership in the employing industry is not such an 'unusual circumstance' as to affect the force of the Board's certification within the normal operative period if a majority of employees after the change of ownership or management were employed by the preceding employer." *Id.* at 279.

**Critical Holdings from Burns:**
- Successor employer must recognize and bargain with incumbent union where bargaining unit remains unchanged and majority of employees hired by new employer were represented by recently certified bargaining agent
- Successor is NOT bound by substantive provisions of predecessor's collective bargaining agreement unless explicitly assumed
- Successor has freedom to set initial terms and conditions of employment (unless "perfectly clear" successor who plans to retain all unionized employees)

*Fall River* Clarification [VERIFIED]:
The Supreme Court in *Fall River*, 482 U.S. 27 (1987), refined the Burns test by establishing the "substantial and representative complement" rule, which fixes the moment when determination must be made whether a majority of successor's employees are former employees of predecessor. The Court emphasized that where a union certified for more than one year has rebuttable presumption of majority status, that status continues despite change in employers. *Id.* at 41-42.

#### 2. Application to PMSC Acquisition

**Stock Purchase Structure Analysis:**
Global Logistics Partners is acquiring 100% of PMSC's stock in a Delaware corporation acquisition. This creates a critical distinction from the asset purchase scenarios in *Burns* and *Fall River*. In a stock purchase:
- PMSC continues as same legal entity
- Employment relationships technically continue with same corporate employer
- NLRA successorship doctrine may not apply in traditional sense

**However, critical analysis required:**
Even in stock purchase, if Global Logistics Partners effectuates substantial operational changes, terminates employees, or refuses to honor ILWU agreements, NLRB may find de facto successor employer status triggering § 8(a)(5) bargaining obligations.

**ILWU Workforce Continuity:**
PMSC employs 3,200 ILWU longshoremen across 4 West Coast terminals (LA, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle). Post-acquisition:
- If Global Logistics Partners retains all 3,200 ILWU workers = 100% continuity = clear Burns successor status
- If Global Logistics Partners terminates any ILWU members, must analyze whether remaining workforce constitutes "substantial and representative complement"
- Terminal consolidation (e.g., walking away from Oakland) could reduce ILWU headcount by ~620 workers, but remaining 2,580 workers still constitute substantial continuity

**Duty to Bargain Under 29 U.S.C. § 158(a)(5) [VERIFIED]:**
Section 8(a)(5) of NLRA makes it unfair labor practice for employer "to refuse to bargain collectively with representatives of its employees." 29 U.S.C. § 158(a)(5). "To bargain collectively" is defined as "the performance of the mutual obligation of the employer and the representative of the employees to meet at reasonable times and confer in good faith with respect to wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment." 29 U.S.C. § 158(d).

**Practical Implication:**
Global Logistics Partners will inherit duty to recognize ILWU and bargain in good faith, but is NOT automatically bound by substantive terms of 2022-2028 PCLCA unless explicitly assumed in purchase agreement.

#### 3. Howard Johnson Exception: CBA Adoption NOT Required

*Howard Johnson Co. v. Detroit Local Joint Executive Board*, 417 U.S. 249 (1974), establishes that not all asset acquisitions bind new employer to predecessor's collective bargaining agreements, particularly when there is no significant workforce or operational continuity. *Id.* at 262-264.

**Key Holdings [VERIFIED]:**
- Howard Johnson purchased restaurant assets under agreement explicitly NOT assuming any seller obligations including CBAs
- Howard Johnson hired only 9 of seller's 53 former employees and no former supervisors = no substantial continuity
- Supreme Court held: "there was plainly no substantial continuity of identity in the workforce hired by petitioner with that of the sellers, and no express or implied assumption of the agreement to arbitrate"

**Application to PMSC:**
Unlike *Howard Johnson*, PMSC acquisition involves:
- Stock purchase (not asset purchase with explicit non-assumption)
- Retention of substantially all 3,200 ILWU workers
- Continued operation of 4 terminals in unchanged form
= *Howard Johnson* exception does NOT apply; Burns successorship obligations attach

**Conclusion:**
Global Logistics Partners has duty to recognize ILWU and bargain over wages/terms/conditions under § 8(a)(5), but can negotiate modifications to PCLCA terms and is not bound by existing agreement unless assumed.

### D. Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA) System

#### 1. LHWCA Coverage and Exclusive Remedy Doctrine

The Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act, 33 U.S.C. §§ 901–950 [VERIFIED], is federal workers' compensation law enacted in 1927 providing compensation and medical care to employees disabled from injuries occurring on navigable waters of the United States or in adjoining areas used in loading, unloading, repairing, or building vessels.

**Coverage for PMSC's 3,200 ILWU Longshoremen [VERIFIED]:**
All 3,200 ILWU longshoremen working at PMSC's four terminals are covered by LHWCA, not state workers' compensation systems. LHWCA applies primarily to longshoremen, harbor workers, shipbuilders, and ship repairers.

**Exclusive Remedy Doctrine [VERIFIED via 33 U.S.C. § 905(a)]:**
The LHWCA operates like state workers' compensation by providing covered maritime longshore workers injured at work with exclusive legal remedy against employer, regardless of fault. For employers, statute offers significant protection, providing "exclusive remedy" that limits liability to LHWCA benefits. 33 U.S.C. § 905(a) states: "The liability of an employer prescribed in section 904 of this title shall be exclusive and in place of all other liability of such employer to the employee."

**Practical Implication:**
PMSC's 3,200 ILWU longshoremen cannot sue PMSC in tort for work-related injuries. Their remedy is limited to LHWCA statutory benefits.

#### 2. LHWCA Benefits Structure Under 33 U.S.C. § 908

**Temporary Total Disability (33 U.S.C. § 908(a)) [VERIFIED]:**
"For temporary total disability, 66⅔ percent of the average weekly wages shall be paid to the employee during the continuance of the disability." All compensation is circumscribed by Minimum (MIN) and Maximum (MAX) rates, recalculated October 1st annually based on National Average Weekly Wage (NAWW) determined by DOL.

Research plan states: "LHWCA benefits: temporary total disability 66.67% AWW max $1,809/week FY2024." This represents the maximum weekly benefit cap (200% of NAWW) per federal regulations.

**Permanent Total Disability (33 U.S.C. § 908(a)):**
66⅔ percent of average weekly wage paid for life. No time limitation on benefits for permanently totally disabled workers.

**Permanent Partial Disability (33 U.S.C. § 908(c)):**
Scheduled benefits for specific body part losses:
- Loss of hand: $244,721 (research plan figure represents current scheduled award)
- Other body parts: Specific schedule in statute

**Medical Benefits (33 U.S.C. § 907):**
Unlimited medical treatment. All reasonable and necessary medical treatment covered without cap.

**Death Benefits (33 U.S.C. § 909):**
- 50% of average weekly wage to surviving spouse
- 16.67% per child
- Maximum total: 66.67% of AWW

**DOL Administration:**
United States Department of Labor's Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP) manages LHWCA through Division of Longshore Workers' Compensation.

#### 3. Section 905(b) Third-Party Vessel Negligence Exception

**Critical Exception to Exclusive Remedy [VERIFIED via 33 U.S.C. § 905(b)]:**
Despite exclusive remedy rule, statute carves out vital exception: ability to sue negligent "vessel" as third party. "In the event of injury to a person covered under this Act caused by the negligence of a vessel, then such person may bring an action against such vessel as a third party, and the employer shall not be liable to the vessel for such damages directly or indirectly."

**Vessel Negligence Suits:**
Longshore worker can file vessel negligence suit against vessel's "owner, owner pro hac vice, agent, operator, charter or bare boat charterer, master, officer, or crew member" for maritime negligence that caused injury.

**Three Categories of Vessel Owner Duties [VERIFIED]:**
1. Duty to provide ship in safe condition or warn of hidden dangers
2. Duty to prevent worker injury when owner in active control of ship
3. Duty to intervene to protect workers from known danger during operations

**Key Limitations:**
- "The liability of the vessel shall not be based upon the warranty of seaworthiness or a breach thereof at the time the injury occurred."
- Longshore worker does not need to elect between LHWCA benefits and third-party lawsuit; may pursue both
- LHWCA benefits paid immediately while tort claim litigated

**PMSC Exposure:**
When PMSC operates as both terminal operator (employing ILWU longshoremen) AND vessel owner (22-vessel fleet), potential for § 905(b) exposure:
- ILWU longshoreman injured by PMSC vessel = receives LHWCA benefits from PMSC as employer PLUS can sue PMSC as vessel owner under § 905(b)
- This creates "dual capacity" liability where PMSC is both exclusive remedy employer and third-party tortfeasor

Research plan references Martinez v. PMSC case (T9 Section 905(b) specialist analyzing in detail): "longshoreman injured September 2023, crushed by container when lashing gear failed M/V Pacific Horizon, bilateral leg fractures permanent disability, LHWCA benefits $420K paid to date ongoing, tort claim filed 905(b) alleges unseaworthy vessel defective lashing gear crew failed to secure containers properly, damages sought $8.5M."

### F. WARN Act Implications for Terminal Consolidation

#### 1. WARN Act Overview and Requirements

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109 [VERIFIED], requires qualified employers that intend to carry out plant closings or mass layoffs to provide 60 days' notice to affected employees, states, and localities.

**Employer Coverage [VERIFIED via 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(1)]:**
Employers with at least 100 employees (excluding part-time) or 100+ employees working total 4,000 hours per week (exclusive of overtime). Requirement does not count employees who have worked fewer than six of last 12 months and does not count employees working fewer than 20 hours per week.

**PMSC Clearly Covered:**
8,500 total employees (2,400 mariners, 3,200 ILWU longshoremen, 2,900 shoreside) = well above 100-employee threshold.

**Notice Requirements [VERIFIED via 29 U.S.C. § 2102]:**
Employer "shall not order a plant closing or mass layoff until the end of a 60-day period after the employer serves written notice" to:
1. Each representative of affected employees (or if no representative, to each affected employee)
2. State or entity designated to carry out rapid response activities
3. Chief elected official of unit of local government where closing/layoff occurs

#### 2. Covered Events: Mass Layoff Definition

**Mass Layoff [VERIFIED via 29 U.S.C. § 2101(a)(3)]:**
A mass layoff occurs when:
- At least 50 employees are laid off during 30-day period, if laid-off employees made up at least one-third of workforce; OR
- 500 employees are laid off during 30-day period, no matter how large workforce; OR
- Entire work site is closed down and at least 50 employees laid off during 30-day period

**Oakland Terminal Walk-Away Scenario [from research plan T7]:**
Research plan contemplates PMSC walking away from Oakland terminal if Port insists on $42M rent (vs. current $28M):
- Oakland employs ~620 ILWU longshoremen
- Walk-away = "plant closing" (entire work site closed)
- 620 employees > 50 threshold = WARN Act triggered

**Oakland Terminal Closure Scenario:**
- 620 ILWU longshoremen laid off = WARN Act notice required
- If PMSC fails to provide 60-day notice:

**Liability Calculation [VERIFIED via 29 U.S.C. § 2104]:**
"Liability shall be calculated for the period of the violation, up to a maximum of 60 days, but in no event for more than one-half the number of days the employee was employed by the employer."

**Penalty:**
- Back pay for each day of violation (up to 60 days)
- Benefits for each day of violation (up to 60 days)

**Oakland ILWU Longshoremen Calculation:**
- 620 workers × 60 days back pay = maximum exposure
- Research plan states: "LHWCA annual costs $32M, 285 claims annually, $210K longshoreman earnings annually including benefits"

- 620 workers × $575/day × 60 days = **$21.4M maximum WARN Act exposure**

**Benefits Component:**
Health and pension benefits also owed:
- Estimate ~30% of wages = $172/day per worker
- 620 workers × $172/day × 60 days = **$6.4M benefits exposure**
- **Total WARN Act exposure: $27.8M** if Oakland terminal closed without 60-day notice

#### 4. Exceptions to Notice Requirement

**Three Statutory Exceptions [VERIFIED via 29 U.S.C. § 2102(b)]:**

1. **Faltering Company:** Employer actively seeking capital or business which, if obtained, would avoid or postpone shutdown, and employer reasonably believed giving notice would preclude obtaining capital or business

2. **Unforeseeable Business Circumstances:** Closing/layoff result of business circumstances not reasonably foreseeable 60 days before notice would have been required

3. **Natural Disaster:** Closing/layoff direct result of natural disaster (flood, earthquake, drought, storm, tidal wave, tsunami)

**Oakland Walk-Away Analysis:**
- Faltering company exception: Does NOT apply (PMSC not seeking capital; electing to walk away from uneconomic lease)
- Unforeseeable circumstances: Does NOT apply (Port's $42M rent demand foreseeable; lease expires 2030, renegotiation expected)
- Natural disaster: Does NOT apply

**Conclusion:** No exception available; 60-day WARN notice required if Oakland terminal closed.

**California WARN Act (Cal. WARN) [California Labor Code §§ 1400-1408]:**
Stricter than federal:
- Applies to employers with 75+ employees (vs. federal 100)
- Requires notice for relocation of facility more than 100 miles away
- PMSC's LA, Long Beach, Oakland terminals = California locations subject to Cal. WARN

**Washington WARN (RCW 49.26):**
Similar to federal but covers smaller employers in certain circumstances. PMSC's Seattle terminal subject to Washington WARN.
