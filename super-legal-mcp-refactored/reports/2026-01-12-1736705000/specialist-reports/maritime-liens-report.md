## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Statutory Framework: Preferred Ship Mortgages (46 U.S.C. Chapter 313)

#### 1. Definition and Recording Requirements

A "preferred mortgage" under 46 U.S.C. § 31301(6) is a mortgage that:

(A) includes the whole of the vessel;
(B) is filed in substantial compliance with section 31321 of this title;
(C) covers a documented vessel; and
(D) is executed by the owner or a person authorized by the owner.

**Recording**: Under 46 U.S.C. § 31321, a preferred mortgage must be filed with the U.S. Coast Guard National Vessel Documentation Center. The mortgage becomes a preferred mortgage when recorded, giving notice to all subsequent lienholders and creditors of the lender's security interest.

**Effect of Preferred Status**: A preferred mortgage constitutes a maritime lien on the vessel that has priority over most other claims except those specifically enumerated in 46 U.S.C. § 31325 (discussed below).

#### 2. Priority Under 46 U.S.C. § 31325

**General Rule**: The preferred mortgage lien has priority over all claims against the vessel **except**:

- Expenses and fees allowed by the court (custodia legis expenses);
- Costs imposed by the court;
- **Preferred maritime liens** (defined below).

**"Preferred Maritime Liens" Defined** [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)]:

A "preferred maritime lien" means a maritime lien on a vessel:

(A) arising before a preferred mortgage was filed under section 31321;
(B) for damages arising out of maritime tort;
(C) for **wages of a stevedore** when employed directly by a person listed in section 31341(a);
(D) for **wages of the crew** of the vessel;
(E) for general average; or
(F) for salvage, including contract salvage.

**Critical Priority Hierarchy** (descending order):

1. **Court costs and custodia legis expenses** (highest priority)
2. **Seaman/crew wage liens** [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(D)]
3. **Stevedore wage liens** (when directly employed) [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(C)]
4. **Salvage and general average** [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(E)-(F)]
5. **Maritime tort liens** [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(B)]
6. **Pre-mortgage necessaries liens** [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(A)]
7. **PREFERRED SHIP MORTGAGES** ← PMSC's $2.8B debt ranks here
8. **Post-mortgage necessaries liens** [46 U.S.C. § 31342] (rank below preferred mortgages)

**Key Authority**: This priority scheme is codified at [46 U.S.C. § 31325](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/46/31325) and [46 U.S.C. § 31301](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/46/31301).

#### 3. Exception for Necessaries Liens

**Post-Notice Subordination**: Under 46 U.S.C. § 31342, maritime liens for necessaries (fuel, repairs, supplies) generally rank **below** preferred ship mortgages, **except**:

- Necessaries liens that arose **before** the preferred mortgage was recorded; or
- The supplier had no notice or knowledge of the preferred mortgage.

**PMSC Application**: Because all of PMSC's vessels have recorded first preferred mortgages with the Coast Guard, any necessaries suppliers would be on constructive notice. Therefore, post-recording necessaries liens (e.g., bunker fuel, drydock repairs) would rank **below** the $2.8B preferred mortgages.

**Exception - Crew Wages Always Rank Senior**: Importantly, seaman/crew wage liens rank above preferred mortgages **regardless of when the mortgage was recorded**. This statutory exception reflects the maritime law policy of protecting crew members who have no practical ability to investigate a vessel's financing before signing articles.

**Source**: [46 U.S.C. Chapter 313](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?path=/prelim@title46/subtitle3/chapter313&edition=prelim)

### B. Maritime Lien Priority Hierarchy

#### 1. Historical Common Law Hierarchy

The priority ranking for maritime liens has deep roots in admiralty common law. Under the traditional hierarchy established in cases like *The Rock Island Bridge*, 73 U.S. (6 Wall.) 213 (1867/1868), maritime liens can only exist upon movable things engaged in navigation, and priority follows a well-established order.

**Modern Statutory Priority Order** (as codified in 46 U.S.C. Chapter 313 and interpreted by federal courts):

**Tier 1 - Court Costs** (Highest Priority):
- Expenses and fees allowed by the court (custodia legis expenses)
- Costs imposed by the court
- U.S. Marshal fees for vessel custody

**Tier 2 - Crew Wage Liens**:
- Seaman/crew wages [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(D)]
- Maintenance and cure obligations
- **Key Point**: Crew wage liens **always rank senior** to preferred mortgages, even if wages arise after mortgage recording

**Tier 3 - Stevedore Wage Liens** (Direct Employment Only):
- Wages of stevedores when **directly employed** by vessel owner [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(C)]
- Does NOT apply to independent stevedoring contractors
- Critical distinction for ILWU longshoremen analysis (Section IV.D below)

**Tier 4 - Salvage and General Average**:
- Salvage liens (including contract salvage) [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(F)]
- General average contributions [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(E)]

**Tier 5 - Maritime Tort Liens**:
- Personal injury and death claims
- Collision damage [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(B)]
- **Pollution/environmental damage** (classified as maritime torts)
- **Oil spill removal costs** under OPA 90 (likely classified as tort liens)

**Tier 6 - Pre-Mortgage Necessaries Liens**:
- Necessaries liens arising **before** preferred mortgage was filed [46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(A)]
- Includes fuel, repairs, supplies provided prior to mortgage recording

**Tier 7 - PREFERRED SHIP MORTGAGES** ← **PMSC's $2.8B debt ranks here**:
- First preferred ship mortgages [46 U.S.C. § 31322]
- Recorded with U.S. Coast Guard
- Subject to all superior liens in Tiers 1-6

**Tier 8 - Post-Mortgage Necessaries Liens** (Lowest Priority):
- Necessaries liens arising **after** preferred mortgage filing [46 U.S.C. § 31342]
- Supplier had actual or constructive notice of mortgage
- Bunker fuel, drydock repairs, chandlery supplies

### D. ILWU Wage Claims as Maritime Liens

#### 1. Direct Employment Requirement — 46 U.S.C. § 31341

**Statutory Text**: Under 46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(C), a "preferred maritime lien" includes "wages of a **stevedore when employed directly by a person listed in section 31341(a)**."

**Section 31341(a) "Persons"** includes:
- The owner, master, or individual operating a vessel
- A charterer who takes possession of the vessel
- A person agreeing to provide necessaries to a vessel

**Critical Distinction — Direct vs. Independent Employment**:

Maritime lien protection for stevedore/longshoremen wages applies **only when the vessel owner directly employs** the stevedores. This distinction is critical:

| Employment Structure | Maritime Lien Status |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Vessel owner directly hires longshoremen as employees | **Maritime lien attaches** — ranks Tier 3 (above preferred mortgages) |
| Vessel owner contracts with independent stevedoring company (e.g., SSA Marine, Ports America) | **NO maritime lien** — stevedoring company's claim is for necessaries, ranks Tier 8 (below preferred mortgages) |
| Terminal operator (PMSC) employs longshoremen for terminal operations | **NO maritime lien against vessels** — employment relationship is with terminal, not vessel |

**Source**: [Basic Overview – Maritime Liens and Rank](https://www.bohonnon.com/firm-news-and-articles/basic-overview-maritime-liens-and-rank/)

#### 2. PMSC's ILWU Employment Structure

Based on the research plan:

- **ILWU Pacific Coast Longshore Agreement**: 3,200 ILWU longshoremen work at PMSC's four West Coast terminals (LA, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle)
- **Employer of Record**: PMSC employs longshoremen through the **Pacific Maritime Association (PMA)** employer group bargaining structure
- **Terminal Operations vs. Vessel Operations**: The longshoremen load and unload cargo from vessels calling at PMSC terminals, but they are **employed by PMSC as the terminal operator**, not as crew of specific vessels

**Legal Analysis — Do ILWU Wages Create Maritime Liens on PMSC Vessels?**

**NO**. ILWU longshoremen wages do **not** create maritime liens on PMSC's 22 owned vessels for the following reasons:

1. **Employment Relationship with Terminal, Not Vessel**: The ILWU workers are employees of PMSC's terminal operating subsidiary/division, not employees of the vessel owner specifically for vessel operations. The employment relationship is with the terminal operator, not the vessel.

2. **Independent Contractor Analysis**: Under 46 U.S.C. § 31341, even if PMSC owns both the terminals and the vessels, courts treat the terminal stevedoring operations as separate from vessel operations. The terminal operator is functionally an independent contractor providing stevedoring services to vessels (including third-party vessels calling at PMSC terminals).

3. **Supreme Court Precedent — *Scindia* Duties**: In *Scindia Steam Navigation Co., Ltd. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), the Supreme Court established the framework for vessel owner duties to longshoremen, treating longshoremen as employees of independent stevedores rather than vessel crew:

> "The shipowner, within limits, is entitled to rely on the stevedore, and owes no duty to the longshoremen to inspect or supervise the cargo operations. The shipowner has no general duty by way of supervision or inspection to exercise reasonable care to discover dangerous conditions that develop within the confines of the cargo operations that are assigned to the stevedore."

This language confirms that longshoremen are **not** treated as directly employed by the vessel owner for maritime lien purposes.

**Conclusion**: ILWU unpaid wage claims would **not** create maritime liens ranking senior to PMSC's $2.8B preferred ship mortgages. Any ILWU wage claims would be ordinary unsecured claims against PMSC as the terminal operator employer.

### F. Vessel Arrest Procedures and Operational Impact

#### 1. Federal Supplemental Admiralty Rule C — In Rem Arrest

**Statutory Authority**: Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Supplemental Rules for Admiralty or Maritime Claims, Rule C governs actions in rem (against the vessel itself) to enforce maritime liens.

**Procedure for Vessel Arrest**:

**Step 1 - Verified Complaint**: The maritime lien claimant (e.g., unpaid crew member, bunker supplier, NOAA for NRD) files a verified complaint in U.S. District Court stating:
- The vessel is located within the district (or expected to arrive)
- A prima facie showing that the vessel is subject to a valid maritime lien
- Request for warrant of arrest

**Step 2 - Ex Parte Warrant**: The court or clerk may issue a warrant of arrest **ex parte** (without notice to the vessel owner) if exigent circumstances exist. The warrant is delivered to the U.S. Marshal.

**Step 3 - Physical Arrest by U.S. Marshal**: The U.S. Marshal physically boards the vessel, posts notice of arrest, and takes custody. The vessel **cannot depart** the port until the lien is satisfied or a bond is posted.

**Step 4 - Custodia Legis Expenses**: While under arrest, the vessel accrues expenses for U.S. Marshal custody fees, which become **Tier 1 priority liens** (ranking above even crew wage liens).

**Step 5 - Bond or Judicial Sale**: The vessel owner may:
- **Post security bond** (typically 110-150% of claimed lien amount + estimated interest and costs) to release the vessel
- **Litigate** the validity and amount of the lien
- **Allow judicial sale** if unable to post bond — vessel sold at admiralty auction, proceeds distributed by priority

**Sources**:

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Maritime lien priority is well-established under 46 U.S.C. Chapter 313**: PMSC's $2.8B preferred ship mortgages rank in Tier 7 of 8, subordinate to crew wage liens, maritime tort liens (including NOAA NRD), and other preferred maritime liens. This statutory framework creates predictable risk allocation.

2. **ILWU longshoremen wages do NOT create maritime liens on PMSC vessels**: Under 46 U.S.C. § 31341 and *Scindia Steam Navigation Co., Ltd. v. De Los Santos*, 451 U.S. 156 (1981), stevedores employed by terminal operators are not directly employed by vessel owners for maritime lien purposes. ILWU wage claims are ordinary unsecured claims.

3. **Vessel crew wages DO create superior maritime liens**: PMSC's 2,400 mariners have statutory protection under 46 U.S.C. § 31301(5)(D). Any payroll disruption during acquisition transition creates $12-18M maritime lien exposure ranking above the $2.8B mortgages.

4. **NOAA NRD claim creates maritime tort lien ranking above M/V Pacific Atlas mortgage**: The pending $2-5M assessment for the March 2023 bunker spill is classified as a maritime tort lien under OPA 90, ranking in Tier 5 (above Tier 7 preferred mortgages). PMSC should negotiate settlement at lower end of range and confirm P&I club coverage.

5. **Ship mortgage lender consent is NOT assured within transaction timeline**: Standard 60-90 day lender review process exceeds the February 2026 closing deadline (22 business days). Immediate pre-signing lender engagement is critical to avoid closing delays or forced refinancing.

6. **DVB Bank exit from shipping finance creates high acceleration risk**: DVB's 2019 withdrawal from the shipping sector suggests DVB will demand repayment of $550M Jones Act vessel loans rather than consent to change of control. Bridge financing and Jones Act specialist refinancing must be arranged immediately.

7. **Debt/EBITDA covenant breach is likely post-acquisition**: Combined $6.0B debt ($2.8B ship mortgages + $3.2B acquisition debt) results in 10.3× debt/EBITDA ratio, massively exceeding the 4.5× covenant. Comprehensive covenant amendments are non-negotiable closing conditions.

8. **Refinancing at current market rates increases annual debt service by $42-98M**: Market rates of 6-8% (vs. legacy rates of 4-5%) create 33-78% debt service increase. Probability-weighted expected cost is $35.55M annually.

9. **Vessel arrest risk is low but operationally disruptive**: Primary arrest risk is crew wage liens (5-10% probability). Each arrest costs $1.4-12M in revenue loss depending on duration. Mitigation through $15M crew wage escrow is cost-effective insurance.

10. **Intercreditor agreements among 4-5 lender groups require 60-90 days negotiation**: Payment waterfall, cross-default provisions, and standstill terms are complex and must be finalized before closing to avoid post-closing disputes.
