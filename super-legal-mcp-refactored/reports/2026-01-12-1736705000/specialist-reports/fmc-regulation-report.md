## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes Federal Maritime Commission (FMC) regulatory compliance requirements and transaction risks arising from Global Logistics Partners LLC's $4.8 billion acquisition of Pacific Maritime Services Corporation (PMSC), a vessel-operating common carrier (VOCC) operating 22 vessels (18 container ships, 4 bulk carriers) with 127 confidential service contracts filed with the FMC. Research focuses on change of control notification requirements, detention and demurrage compliance, service contract assignment, vessel sharing agreement termination risks, and FMC enforcement exposure under the Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022 (OSRA).

### Key Takeaways:

1. **No FMC License Approval Required:** VOCCs are not subject to FMC license transfer or change of control approval requirements. PMSC must file an administrative Form FMC-1 update within 30 days post-closing to reflect Global Logistics Partners ownership, but no Commission approval is required. This contrasts with Ocean Transportation Intermediaries (NVOCCs/freight forwarders) who must obtain FMC approval via Form FMC-18 for organizational changes.

2. **Detention and Demurrage Compliance is Critical Transaction Risk:** PMSC faces 12 pending shipper complaints filed with the FMC alleging unreasonable detention and demurrage practices. Under OSRA 2022 and the Final Rule effective May 28, 2024 (46 CFR Part 541), carriers face substantial penalty exposure for non-compliant billing practices. Based on the Hapag-Lloyd $2 million settlement precedent (June 2022), PMSC's exposure range is **$500,000-$1,200,000 in penalties/settlement** plus **$500,000-$1,000,000 in waived charges**. The FMC's enhanced enforcement authority, increased budget ($32.9M to $49.2M), and nine additional enforcement staff create a heightened scrutiny environment.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Detention and Demurrage Compliance (46 CFR Part 541)

#### 1. Final Rule on D&D Billing Requirements

The Federal Maritime Commission issued comprehensive regulations governing demurrage and detention billing requirements pursuant to the Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022. The Final Rule was published at **89 Fed. Reg. 14,362** (Feb. 26, 2024) and codified at **46 CFR Part 541**, with an effective date of **May 28, 2024**.¬π

**Key Regulatory Requirements:**

The Final Rule mandates specific billing practices for vessel-operating common carriers (VOCCs) and marine terminal operators:

- **30-Day Invoice Issuance Requirement:** VOCCs must issue detention and demurrage invoices within 30 calendar days from when charges were last incurred. **46 CFR ¬ß 541.5(a)**. If the invoice is issued later than 30 days, the billed party has no obligation to pay the charges.¬≤

- **Mandatory Invoice Content:** Invoices must include specific minimum information:
  - Identity of the billing party
  - Container number and bill of lading number
  - Date(s) the charges were incurred
  - Per diem rate and calculation methodology
  - Separate line items for detention vs. demurrage
  - Free time provided
  - Statement of rights to dispute charges
  - Contact information for filing disputes¬≥

- **Dispute Resolution Process:** Billed parties have at least 30 calendar days to file fee mitigation, refund, or waiver requests. **46 CFR ¬ß 541.6(b)**. The billing party must attempt to resolve disputes within 30 calendar days of receiving a timely request.‚Å¥

- **Penalty for Non-Compliance:** Failure to include any required information in a detention or demurrage invoice **eliminates any obligation of the billed party to pay the applicable charge**. 46 CFR ¬ß 541.5(d). This nullification provision creates substantial exposure for carriers with non-compliant invoicing systems.‚Åµ

#### 2. Recent Court Decision - Section 541.4 Set Aside (September 2025)

On September 23, 2025, the U.S. Court of Appeals issued a decision in *World Shipping Council v. FMC* setting aside **46 CFR ¬ß 541.4**, which had specified who may be billed for detention and demurrage charges.‚Å∂ The court invalidated only this "properly issued invoices" provision, which had limited invoicing to:
- The person for whose account the billing party provided ocean transportation or storage; or
- The consignee

**Impact:** All other provisions of 46 CFR Part 541 remain fully effective and enforceable. VOCCs must continue to comply with all remaining requirements regarding invoice content, timing, and dispute resolution procedures.‚Å∑
