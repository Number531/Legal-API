### Key Findings

#### 1. **Statutory Framework — No Mandatory Consent Requirements**

**California:** Harbors and Navigation Code § 6295 grants port districts broad authority to lease property "for such purposes and upon such terms as the port commission deems proper," but does **NOT mandate statutory consent requirements** for lease assignments or changes of control. Assignment restrictions are established by individual lease agreements, not statute.¹

**Washington:** RCW 53.08.080 similarly grants port commissions discretion over lease terms "as the port commission deems proper" with maximum lease durations (50 years standard, extendable to 80 years), but contains **no statutory assignment consent mandate.** Consent requirements, if any, are contractual.²

**Implication:** Change of control consent requirements at LA, Long Beach, Oakland, and Seattle terminals depend on **specific lease agreement provisions**, not statutory law. Actual terminal lease documents must be reviewed from PMSC data room to confirm consent triggers, timelines, and approval standards.

#### 2. **Industry Standard — Change of Control as "Deemed Assignment"**

Port terminal concession agreements uniformly treat changes in control of corporate tenants as **"deemed assignments"** requiring port consent. Industry practice: "Sales, transfers or other dispositions aggregating a certain predefined percentage of the voting stock of a tenant (if non-public) shall be deemed to be an assignment of the lease."³ Common trigger: **50%+ ownership change** or **effective control transfer** (including indirect control through parent company sale).

**Application to Transaction:** Global Logistics Partners LLC (PE acquirer) purchasing **100% stock** of PMSC from family shareholders constitutes:
- **Direct change of control:** 100% ownership transfer
- **Likely consent trigger:** If leases contain industry-standard change of control provisions (anticipated based on port practices), consent required at all four terminals
- **Timeline impact:** Port consent process requires 60-120 days (30-60 days tenant notice + 30-60 days port review/commission approval)

#### 3. **Financial Capability Standards — Port of Long Beach Documented Requirements**

Port of Long Beach terminal lease standard provisions require proposed assignees to provide:⁴
- **Audited Financial Statements:** Most recent financial statement audited by independent CPA
- **Comparable Credit Standing:** Financial statement must disclose "credit standing and financial responsibility **comparable to Lessee's**"
- **Corporate Documentation:** If partnership/corporation, details of principal officers/directors
- **Business Experience:** Statement of proposed assignee's business experience
- **Transaction Details:** Detailed statement of business relationship and proposed financial arrangements

**Excess Rent Clawback:** If lessee receives consideration exceeding rent payable under lease (e.g., lease valuation premium in transaction), Port consent is subject to **payment of excess rent to City.** This prevents lessees from profiting from port concessions without sharing economic gain with public landowner.⁵

**Continued Lessee Obligation:** Original lessee remains **fully obligated** under lease notwithstanding assignment — joint and several liability with assignee.⁶

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Statutory Framework for Public Port Leases

#### 1. California Port District Leasing Authority

**California Harbors and Navigation Code § 6295** (Port Districts — District Powers)

California port districts possess broad authority to "acquire, purchase, takeover, construct, maintain, operate, develop, and regulate wharves, docks, warehouses, grain elevators, bunkering facilities, belt railroads, floating plants, lighterage, lands, towage facilities, and any and all other facilities, aids, or public personnel, incident to, or necessary for, the operation and development of ports, waterways, and the district."¹

**Key Statutory Principles:**

- **Broad Discretion:** California Harbors and Navigation Code grants port districts wide discretion in leasing port property "for such purposes and upon such terms as the port commission deems proper."²
- **No Statutory Assignment Restrictions:** California statutes do NOT mandate specific consent requirements for lease assignments or changes of control in port district leases. Such restrictions, if any, are established by individual lease agreements.³
- **Public Trust Doctrine:** California ports hold tidelands and submerged lands in public trust, which limits leasing to purposes consistent with commerce, navigation, and fisheries.⁴

**Application to LA, Long Beach, and Oakland Terminals:**

All three California terminals (LA Pier 300, Long Beach Pier J, Oakland Outer Harbor) operate under leases governed by California Harbors and Navigation Code Division 8, Part 4 (Port Districts). Assignment and change of control provisions are contract-specific, not statutory.⁵

#### 2. Washington Port District Leasing Authority

**RCW 53.08.080** (Lease of Property — Authorized — Duration)

Washington port districts may "lease all lands, wharves, docks and real and personal property owned and controlled by it, for such purposes and upon such terms as the port commission deems proper."⁶

**Maximum Lease Terms:**⁷
- **Standard Property:** 50 years (extendable up to 30 additional years = 80 years maximum)
- **Airport Property:** Up to 75 years
- **Subleased Federal/State Property:** Up to 90 years

**Key Statutory Principles:**

- **Port Commission Discretion:** RCW 53.08.080 grants port commissions broad authority to set "such terms as the port commission deems proper," including assignment and consent provisions.⁸
- **No Statutory Consent Mandate:** Washington RCW Chapter 53.08 does NOT contain statutory requirements for port approval of lease assignments or changes of control. Such terms are contractual.⁹
- **Security for Rent:** RCW 53.08.085 requires security for rent on leases exceeding one year, typically in the form of surety bonds or letters of credit.¹⁰

**Application to Seattle Terminal 46:**

Seattle Terminal 46 lease is governed by RCW 53.08.080. Change of control consent requirements, if any, are established by the lease agreement with the Port of Seattle, not by statute.¹¹

#### 1. Assignment and Sublease Restrictions

**Standard Prohibition Language:**

Port terminal leases uniformly prohibit assignments and subleases without prior written port consent. Typical language: "Tenant shall not sublet any part of the premises or assign this Agreement without the prior written consent of the Landlord" and "An assignment or subletting without the written consent of the Landlord, or an assignment or subletting by operation of law, shall be void."¹²

**Rationale:** Ports require approval rights to ensure:
1. **Financial Capability:** New tenant has sufficient creditworthiness to pay rent and perform obligations
2. **Operational Expertise:** New tenant possesses experience operating complex terminal facilities
3. **Public Trust Compliance:** New tenant will operate consistently with public trust requirements (commerce, navigation)
4. **Labor Relations:** New tenant will comply with collective bargaining agreements (ILWU on West Coast)

#### 2. Change of Control as "Deemed Assignment"

**Corporate Tenant Indirect Transfers:**

In sophisticated port terminal concession agreements, **changes in control of a corporate tenant are treated as a deemed assignment of the lease.** The rationale is that "without it, a transfer could be effected by simply transferring control of the tenant, thus circumventing the assignment restrictions contained in the lease."¹³

**Typical Change of Control Trigger:**

"Sales, transfers or other dispositions aggregating a certain predefined percentage of the voting stock of a tenant (if non-public) shall be deemed to be an assignment of the lease" requiring port consent.¹⁴ Common thresholds: **50%+ ownership change** or **effective control transfer**.

**Effective Control Definition:**

The term "effective control" commonly captures **indirect changes of control**, such as a change in control of a direct or ultimate parent company of a corporate tenant, which would necessitate landlord consent.¹⁵ This prevents circumvention through multi-tier corporate restructuring.

**Application to PMSC Transaction:**

- **Direct change of control:** 100% ownership transfer triggers deemed assignment
- **Consent requirement:** If terminal leases contain change of control provisions (industry standard), port consent required for all four terminals
- **Timeline impact:** Port consent process typically 30-90 days, may delay February 2026 closing

### C. Financial Capability Standards for Port Approval

#### 1. Port of Long Beach Requirements (Documented Standard)

**Financial Disclosure Requirements:**¹⁶

To obtain port consent for assignment, lessees must provide:
1. **Audited Financial Statements:** Most recent financial statement of proposed assignee audited by independent CPA
2. **Credit Standing Benchmark:** Financial statement must disclose "credit standing and financial responsibility **comparable to Lessee's**"
3. **Corporate Documentation:** If partnership or corporation, details of principal officers/directors (or general partners)
4. **Business Experience Statement:** Statement of business experience of proposed assignee
5. **Transaction Details:** Detailed statement of business relationship between lessee and proposed assignee, including proposed financial arrangements

**Excess Rent Payment Requirement:**

If lessee receives consideration exceeding the rent payable under the lease (e.g., premium paid by assignee), port consent is subject to **payment of such excess rent to the City.**¹⁷ This prevents lessees from profiting from port concessions without sharing economic gain with public landowner.

**Continued Lessee Obligation:**

The lessee remains **fully obligated** under the lease notwithstanding any assignment or sublease.¹⁸ This creates joint and several liability — port can pursue original tenant PMSC or new owner Global Logistics Partners for rent and performance.

### D. Consent Timeline and Approval Process

**Standard Port Consent Process:**²⁰

1. **Tenant Notice:** 30-60 days' advance written notice to port of proposed assignment/change of control
2. **Document Submission:** Financial statements, corporate documentation, business experience, transaction details
3. **Port Review:** 30-60 days for port staff analysis and port commission consideration
4. **Commission Approval:** Public hearing before port commission (required for major lease amendments)
5. **Consent Execution:** Written consent document executed by port executive director or commission

**Total Timeline:** **60-120 days** from initial notice to final consent
