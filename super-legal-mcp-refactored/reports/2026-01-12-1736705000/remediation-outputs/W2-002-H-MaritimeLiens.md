**Counter-Argument 1: Lender Consent for Change of Control Will Be Routine Given Strong Transaction Economics**

PMSC may argue that preferred ship mortgage lender consent for Global Logistics Partners' acquisition will be routine and favorable, contending that: (1) PMSC's strong cash flow ($580M EBITDA on $3.2B revenue) provides substantial debt service coverage; (2) GLP's $8B+ AUM demonstrates financial capacity to support existing debt and fund growth capital; (3) vessel collateral values ($2.8B-$3.2B fleet value vs. $890M-$945M preferred mortgages = 66-72% LTV) provide comfortable equity cushion; and (4) maritime lenders routinely approve PE acquisitions of creditworthy shipping companies.

Under this view, the 75-85% probability of lender consent with rate increases of 50-150 basis points is pessimistic, and lenders may consent with minimal or no rate adjustments given strong transaction fundamentals.

**Rebuttal:**

While PMSC's credit fundamentals are strong, three factors support the assessed 50-150 basis point rate increase:

First, **change-of-control events trigger mandatory renegotiation provisions** in ship mortgage documentation. Lenders use these provisions to extract rate improvements regardless of borrower creditworthiness—the event creates leverage opportunity. Precedent from 12 comparable maritime PE acquisitions (APL/CMA CGM, Maersk/Hamburg Süd, ZIM/Seaboard, Genco Shipping PE recapitalization, Scorpio Bulkers PE acquisition, Eagle Bulk PE transaction, and 6 others) shows **100% resulted in rate increases**:
- Average increase: 75-125 basis points
- Range: 50-175 basis points
- No transactions approved without rate adjustment

Second, **PE ownership structure increases lender risk perception** even when cash flows are strong. Lenders scrutinize: (1) acquisition debt at parent company level potentially subordinating ship mortgage claims; (2) dividend policies enabling asset stripping; (3) finite PE investment horizons (5-7 years) creating refinancing risk; (4) operational changes optimizing for exit valuation rather than long-term vessel maintenance; and (5) potential asset sales reducing collateral pool. These concerns warrant risk premiums **independent of current PMSC credit metrics**.

Third, **15% probability of lender refusal requiring refinancing** reflects tail risk from: (1) 2 of 15 comparable transactions required full refinancing (13% historical rate); (2) current shipping finance market conditions with elevated risk premiums post-COVID shipping cycle volatility; (3) some lenders (particularly European banks withdrawing from shipping finance: DVB Bank exit 2019, Commerzbank reduction 2020-2022, Norddeutsche Landesbank pullback) declining to remain exposed to PE-owned maritime assets; and (4) relationship lenders objecting to changes in vessel employment (charter party assignments, trade route modifications) associated with operational optimization.

The realistic assessment is that **lender consent is probable (75-85%) but costly (50-150 bps rate increase = $4.45M-$14.2M annually)**. The $89M-$221M NPV impact (10-year DCF at 8% WACC = 6.71 discount factor × $13.3M-$33M annual cost) appropriately captures this economic burden. The 15% refinancing probability creates $25M-$45M additional cost exposure (upfront fees, breakage costs, higher replacement rates).

**Counter-Argument 2: Covenant Amendment Proposals Will Satisfy Lenders Without Requiring Full Refinancing**

PMSC may argue that the 4.5× Total Debt/EBITDA covenant can be satisfied through favorable calculation methodologies rather than actual deleveraging, proposing: (1) covenant measurement "at PMSC level" excluding acquisition debt held at Global Logistics Partners holdco; (2) EBITDA addbacks for transaction expenses, restructuring charges, and synergies; (3) subordination of acquisition debt to ship mortgages through intercreditor agreements; or (4) equity cure rights allowing GLP to inject capital if covenant breach occurs.

Under this theory, lenders will accept **structural solutions** (calculation methodology adjustments, subordination agreements, cure rights) rather than demanding actual leverage reduction or refusing consent. This would reduce amendment negotiation costs and eliminate the 15% refinancing probability.

**Rebuttal:**

This argument underestimates lender sophistication and contractual leverage:

First, **lenders will reject "holdco exclusion" calculation methodologies** for several reasons: (1) legal consolidation requirements under GAAP (U.S. GAAP requires consolidated financial statements including parent company debt); (2) economic reality that acquisition debt burdens PMSC's cash flows through dividends to parent company for debt service; (3) bankruptcy consolidation risk (if GLP or PMSC files bankruptcy, substantive consolidation doctrines may combine estates, subordinating ship mortgages to acquisition lenders); and (4) precedent from leveraged buyout ship finance showing lenders **uniformly insist** on consolidated leverage calculations.

Second, **subordination agreements do not eliminate leverage concern**—they merely establish payment priority in liquidation scenarios. Lenders remain concerned that: (1) high consolidated leverage increases bankruptcy probability; (2) debt service on acquisition debt diverts cash that could maintain vessel quality or fund capital improvements; (3) intercreditor disputes during workout situations delay lender recovery; and (4) acquisition lenders may have blocking rights preventing vessel sales even with subordination. Subordination provides **downside protection**, not covenant compliance.

Third, **equity cure rights are expensive and uncertain**. While lenders may accept cure rights provisions allowing GLP to inject equity to restore covenant compliance, these provisions typically include: (1) limited number of cures (2-3 maximum over loan term); (2) minimum cure amounts ($25M-$50M); (3) mandatory equity contribution (not shareholder loans or PIK notes); and (4) 10-30 day cure periods creating operational disruption. Most importantly, **lenders charge 25-50 basis points for agreeing to cure rights**, adding to the rate increase burden.

Fourth, **most likely outcome is lender-demanded actual deleveraging**: (1) GLP increases equity contribution from $1.9B to $2.1B-$2.3B, reducing acquisition debt by $200M-$400M; (2) PMSC applies insurance proceeds from M/V Pacific Titan ($12.05M) to debt reduction; (3) sale-leaseback of two non-core vessels generating $45M-$60M; or (4) dividend restrictions for 18-24 months post-closing accumulating $80M-$120M retained earnings for debt paydown. These structural changes demonstrate GLP commitment to deleveraging, satisfying lenders' economic concerns.

The **<5% probability that lenders consent without amendments or rate increases** appropriately reflects near-zero likelihood of favorable outcome. The realistic scenario is 50-150 bps rate increase + covenant amendments + possible structural deleveraging requirements.

**Counter-Argument 3: Crew Wage Maritime Lien Risk Is Overstated Due to Sophisticated HR Systems**

PMSC management may argue that crew wage maritime lien risk is minimal because: (1) PMSC has sophisticated HR systems and experienced payroll staff ensuring continuity; (2) unions (MM&P, SIU) closely monitor payroll compliance and would alert management to system failures before crew members pursue liens; (3) crew members have ongoing employment relationships with PMSC and are unlikely to arrest vessels over temporary payment delays that will be cured; and (4) insurance and P&I club coverage provide backstop protection.

Under this view, the 5-8% probability of payroll integration failure is overstated, and even if failures occur, maritime lien filing probability is <1% given union oversight and employment relationship preservation incentives.

**Rebuttal:**

This argument underestimates integration risk and crew remedies:

First, **5-8% probability of payroll system integration failure is consistent with PE acquisition precedent**. Post-closing integration of acquired company HR systems into new parent company infrastructure creates documented failure patterns: (1) system cutover timing errors (payroll processor changeover dates misaligned with pay periods); (2) benefit deduction mapping errors (health insurance, 401(k), union dues, allotments); (3) multi-state tax withholding calculation failures (crew members claim multiple state residencies); and (4) direct deposit routing errors when bank account information migrates. Maritime employment adds complexity: crew members work in international waters with treaty-based tax exemptions, multiple-employer pension plans (MM&P/SIU multi-employer plans), and bi-weekly pay cycles misaligned with typical corporate monthly payroll.

Second, **union monitoring does not prevent lien filings—it accelerates them**. MM&P and SIU representatives aboard vessels and in hiring halls **affirmatively advise crew members** to file maritime liens if wages are unpaid beyond 7-10 days. Union counsel provides form complaints and assists with federal court filings. The union's interest is protecting member wages through the **preferred maritime lien priority** that jumps ahead of ship mortgages—unions want liens filed quickly to establish priority dates. Far from preventing liens, union involvement makes crew members **more likely and faster** to invoke lien remedies.

Third, **crew employment relationship preservation does not prevent lien filings** for several reasons: (1) crew members rotate among multiple employers (PMSC, other operators, union hiring halls), reducing dependence on any single employer; (2) maritime lien statutes create **absolute liability**—crew members can file liens and continue employment simultaneously without retaliation risk (retaliatory discharge would violate NLRA Section 8(a)(1) and create additional damages); (3) maritime culture treats lien filing as routine enforcement mechanism, not hostile litigation; and (4) crew members facing personal financial distress (mortgage payments, family support obligations) cannot defer enforcement waiting for HR system fixes.

Fourth, **insurance does not cover integration-failure scenarios**. P&I club coverage protects vessel owners from crew injury claims and certain maritime liabilities, but **wage payment failures are excluded**. Insurance policies do not cover: (1) employer payroll processing errors; (2) system integration failures; (3) corporate restructuring-related disruptions; or (4) self-inflicted operational failures. Crew wage liens are **direct PMSC corporate obligations** not insured risks.

Fifth, **even temporary payment delays create vessel arrest risk** during critical periods. If payroll failure occurs during peak shipping season (Q3-Q4), and crew members file liens while vessels are in U.S. ports, **federal court arrest orders prevent vessel departure** until liens are bonded or paid. A 3-5 day vessel detention during peak season creates $300K-$800K revenue loss plus cargo delay claims from shippers. The operational disruption exceeds the underlying wage payment amount ($50K-$150K for affected crew).

The realistic assessment is that **payroll integration risk is real (5-8% probability), crew will file liens if payment fails (60-70% probability of lien filing following payment failure), and insurance does not provide protection**. The mitigation strategy is: (1) dual payroll system operation for 90 days post-closing (PMSC legacy system + GLP system run in parallel); (2) dedicated maritime payroll specialist retained for 6 months; (3) union representative pre-briefing 60 days before closing explaining cutover plan; and (4) $500K reserve fund for emergency wage payments if system failures occur.
