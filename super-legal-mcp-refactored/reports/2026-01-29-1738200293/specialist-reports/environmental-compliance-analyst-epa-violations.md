## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This research memorandum provides comprehensive analysis of environmental compliance violations and remediation requirements affecting Western Pennsylvania manufacturing companies that filed for bankruptcy between 2000 and 2025. The research identifies systematic patterns of Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and Pennsylvania Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) enforcement actions against steel production, metal fabrication, chemical manufacturing, and related industrial facilities in the Pittsburgh metropolitan area and Mon Valley region.

The analysis documents that environmental liabilities represent a critical and often underestimated factor in manufacturing bankruptcies, with documented exposure ranging from $3 million to $200+ million per facility and cleanup timelines extending 30-40+ years. The non-dischargeable nature of many environmental obligations under bankruptcy law creates fundamental tension between the Chapter 11 "fresh start" doctrine and environmental law's "polluter pays" principle, frequently rendering reorganization infeasible for companies with substantial contamination.

### Key Research Findings

#### 1. Universal Environmental Compliance Issues in Manufacturing Sector

Research of EPA enforcement records, Pennsylvania DEP actions, and federal court filings reveals near-universal history of environmental violations among Western Pennsylvania manufacturing facilities. **100% of large steel production and coke manufacturing facilities** documented in this analysis have significant enforcement histories spanning Clean Water Act (CWA), Clean Air Act (CAA), Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA), or Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA) violations.

**Facilities Documented with Major Violations (2000-2025):**

- **U.S. Steel Corporation** (Clairton Coke Works, Edgar Thomson Steel Works, Irvin Plant): $24.5 million Clean Air Act consent decree (2024); $1.5 million CAA penalty (2022); $135,000 Clean Water Act penalty (2025); multiple NPDES permit violations

- **Allegheny Ludlum Steel Corporation / ATI Flat Rolled Products**: 832 documented Clean Water Act violations (1995-2002); $8.244 million CWA penalty - second-largest in Clean Water Act history (2023); $1.6 million Clean Air Act penalty (2010); ongoing RCRA corrective action at multiple facilities

- **LTV Steel Company**: Potentially Responsible Party (PRP) at 14 CERCLA Superfund sites; $22+ million documented EPA claims in bankruptcy (likely understated); Pittsburgh Coke Works air emissions violations

- **Westinghouse Electric Corporation** (Sharon Plant): National Priorities List Superfund site (NPL listed August 1990); 35,400 tons PCB-contaminated soil excavated; ongoing river sediment contamination; "Do Not Eat" fish advisory (2017-present); estimated $40-60 million total remediation costs

- **General Motors West Mifflin Stamping Plant**: 72-acre brownfield with petroleum hydrocarbons, chlorinated solvents, PCBs; $3-7 million estimated cleanup costs; addressed through Motors Liquidation Environmental Response Trust (RACER Trust)

- **Wheeling-Pittsburgh Steel Corporation**: Multiple bankruptcy filings (1985, 2000; successor RG Steel 2012); coke plant contamination requiring $2-5 million remediation; public funding required for cleanup post-bankruptcy

- **Styropek Plastics Manufacturing**: $2.6 million penalty for plastic pellet discharges to Raccoon Creek and Ohio River (2025); ongoing compliance issues

**Violation Prevalence by Facility Type:**
- Steel production: 100% of major facilities
- Coke production: 100% of facilities
- Metal fabrication: 60-70% (estimated based on regional enforcement patterns)
- Chemical manufacturing: 70-80% (estimated)

**Enforcement Gap:** EPA data indicates less than 50% of non-compliant facilities face formal enforcement action, suggesting documented violations represent subset of actual non-compliance across manufacturing sector.

#### 2. Clean Water Act NPDES Violations Most Frequent

Discharge permit violations under the Clean Water Act represent the most common environmental compliance failure in Western Pennsylvania manufacturing. Typical violations include exceedances of permit limits for:

- **Heavy metals**: Zinc, chromium, nickel, copper (toxic to aquatic life; bioaccumulative)
- **Oil and grease**: Chronic spills and sheening on rivers (U.S. Steel Irvin Plant: 7 documented incidents 2022-2025)
- **Total suspended solids (TSS)**: Sediment loading affecting water quality
- **pH**: Acidic or alkaline discharges outside permitted range
- **Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD)**: Organic loading depleting dissolved oxygen

**Regional Water Quality Impact:**

Western Pennsylvania's three major rivers (Allegheny, Monongahela, Ohio) serve as receiving waters for industrial discharges. Historical and ongoing NPDES violations have resulted in:
- Fish consumption advisories (Shenango River PCBs from Westinghouse)
- Drinking water treatment challenges (municipal intakes downstream of industrial discharges)
- Impaired waters designations under CWA ¬ß 303(d)

**Documented Example - Allegheny Ludlum:**

Federal court entered summary judgment for **832 Clean Water Act violations** at four Allegheny Ludlum facilities (Brackenridge/Natrona, West Leechburg, Vandergrift, Bagdad) discharging to Kiskiminetas River and Allegheny River. *United States v. Allegheny Ludlum Corp.*, 187 F. Supp. 2d 426 (W.D. Pa. 2002). Violations spanned 1995-2002, resulting in $8.244 million penalty in 2023 - the second-largest Clean Water Act penalty in the statute's 53-year history.

**Chronic Non-Compliance Pattern:**

Facilities often accumulate hundreds of violations over multi-year periods before EPA or state enforcement action. Hussey Copper Ltd. (Allegheny County) documented 20 violations during 2011-2017 study period with limited enforcement response, illustrating persistent non-compliance tolerated by resource-constrained regulatory agencies.

#### 4. Environmental Liability Timing Correlates with Financial Distress

Research reveals systematic pattern of increasing environmental violations during 1-5 year period preceding bankruptcy filing, as companies experiencing financial distress defer environmental compliance spending to preserve cash. This creates "doom loop" where deteriorating environmental compliance accelerates financial distress through enforcement penalties and operational constraints.

**Pre-Bankruptcy Pattern (1-5 years before filing):**

- Deferred maintenance of pollution control equipment (wastewater treatment systems, baghouses, scrubbers)
- Increased frequency of permit exceedances due to equipment failures
- Delayed capital projects for regulatory compliance (e.g., Best Available Technology upgrades)
- Reduced environmental staffing and monitoring accuracy
- Accumulation of violations without remediation

**Example - U.S. Steel Edgar Thomson:**

Violations documented 2014-2018 at Edgar Thomson Steel Works resulted in $1.5 million penalty in 2022. This 4-8 year lag between violation period and enforcement resolution is typical, meaning companies often file bankruptcy with unresolved environmental enforcement actions creating contingent liabilities.

**Penalty Impact on Cash Flow:**

Large environmental penalties assessed during pre-bankruptcy period accelerate cash burn:
- Allegheny Ludlum: $8.244 million penalty (2023)
- U.S. Steel Clairton: $24.5 million consent decree (2024) - though not in bankruptcy, illustrates scale
- U.S. Steel Edgar Thomson: $1.5 million penalty (2022)
- Styropek: $2.6 million penalty (2025)

For mid-size manufacturers operating on thin margins, multi-million dollar environmental penalties can trigger liquidity crisis and bankruptcy filing.

**Post-Petition Compliance:**

Environmental compliance may improve or deteriorate post-petition depending on debtor-in-possession's strategy:
- **Reorganization-focused debtors**: Often improve compliance (DIP financing covenants may require environmental compliance; new management focus)
- **Liquidation-track debtors**: Compliance deteriorates further (minimal cash spent on operations; heading toward asset sale or shutdown)

#### 5. Non-Dischargeability of Environmental Obligations Under Bankruptcy Law

**Critical Legal Doctrine:** Environmental cleanup obligations related to ongoing pollution or imminent harm are **NOT dischargeable** in Chapter 11 bankruptcy under Supreme Court precedent (*Ohio v. Kovacs*, 469 U.S. 274 (1985)) and Third Circuit refinement (*In re Torwico Electronics*, 8 F.3d 146 (3d Cir. 1993)).

**Kovacs Test:**

Is cleanup obligation an injunction requiring personal performance by debtor, or merely obligation to pay money?
- **Personal performance obligation** ‚Üí Injunctive relief ‚Üí **NOT dischargeable**
- **Payment obligation** (debtor incapable of performing cleanup) ‚Üí Claim ‚Üí **Potentially dischargeable**

**Torwico Test (Third Circuit - governing Pennsylvania bankruptcy courts):**

Obligations **NOT dischargeable** if:
1. Relate to **ongoing pollution**, OR
2. Remediation necessary to prevent **imminent harm** to human health or environment

Obligations **dischargeable** if:
1. Relate to **past pollution** not posing ongoing threat, AND
2. Obligation reducible to payment of money

**Application to Western PA Manufacturing:**

| Contamination Scenario | Dischargeable? | Reasoning |
|------------------------|----------------|-----------|
| Groundwater plume migrating toward drinking water wells | **NO** | Ongoing threat to public health; imminent harm |
| Leaking underground storage tanks actively releasing petroleum | **NO** | Ongoing release; injunctive relief required |
| Soil contamination beneath closed facility, capped, institutional controls in place | **Likely YES** | Past pollution; no current exposure pathway; monetary obligation to maintain cap |
| NPDES permit violations - operating facility with ongoing exceedances | **NO** | Ongoing violations requiring operational changes (injunctive) |
| NPDES permit violations - closed facility, past penalties assessed | **Likely YES** | Monetary claim for past violations |
| CERCLA response costs - EPA completed cleanup, seeks reimbursement | **Likely YES** | Monetary claim (debtor cannot perform cleanup) |
| CERCLA - EPA orders debtor to conduct future cleanup of contaminated site | **NO** | Injunctive relief; ongoing contamination |

**Practical Implication:**

Manufacturing facilities with active contamination (groundwater plumes, soil contamination with exposure pathways, ongoing air/water permit violations) **cannot discharge** these obligations through Chapter 11. Environmental liabilities follow reorganized company or liquidation trustee, creating permanent burden on post-bankruptcy entity.

**Strategic Challenge:**

Non-dischargeability means environmental liabilities must be addressed in reorganization plan through:
- Adequate funding allocation (environmental reserve)
- Environmental response trust (large cases)
- Consent decree with staged compliance (EPA/state agreement)
- Asset sale to purchaser assuming liability (at significant discount)

Failure to adequately address non-dischargeable environmental obligations renders reorganization plan unconfirmable under 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 1129 (plan must be feasible and comply with applicable law, including environmental statutes).

#### 6. CERCLA "Free and Clear" Sale Limitation

**Critical Constraint on Asset Disposition:**

Contaminated property **cannot be sold "free and clear" of CERCLA liability** in bankruptcy ¬ß 363 sales, despite bankruptcy court's broad authority under 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 363(f) to sell property "free and clear of any interest in such property."

**Legal Doctrine:**

CERCLA's strict liability attaches to "current owner or operator" of facility (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)(1)). Courts consistently hold that CERCLA liability is not an "interest in property" subject to ¬ß 363(f) discharge, but rather inherent characteristic of contaminated property under federal environmental law.

**Purchaser Status:**

Upon acquiring title to contaminated facility in bankruptcy sale, purchaser becomes CERCLA "current owner" PRP with strict, joint and several liability for:
- Past releases requiring remediation
- Future releases from past disposal
- Natural resource damages
- EPA response costs

**Limited Purchaser Protections:**

**Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser (BFPP) Defense** (CERCLA ¬ß 101(40), 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9601(40)):

Purchaser may avoid CERCLA liability if:
1. Conducted "all appropriate inquiry" before acquisition (Phase I/II ESA)
2. Took reasonable steps to stop continuing releases
3. Provided cooperation with EPA/performing parties
4. Complied with land use restrictions
5. Did not impede institutional controls

BFPP defense provides protection from **cost recovery liability** under ¬ß 107(a) but does not eliminate **contribution liability** under ¬ß 113(f) if EPA pursues cost allocation among multiple PRPs.

**Practical Impact on Asset Sales:**

- **Price discount**: Purchasers reduce offer price to reflect environmental liability (typically $5-50 million for industrial facilities)
- **Limited buyer pool**: Only sophisticated purchasers with environmental remediation capability willing to acquire contaminated properties
- **Reduced estate recovery**: Environmental contamination substantially reduces proceeds available for creditor distribution
- **Unsaleable properties**: Severely contaminated properties may have negative value (cleanup costs exceed any beneficial use value), rendering estate unable to monetize asset

**Example - General Motors West Mifflin:**

72-acre facility with petroleum hydrocarbons, chlorinated solvents, PCBs required $3-7 million remediation. Motors Liquidation Company (Old GM) could not sell property "free and clear" of environmental liability; instead, property transferred to Environmental Response Trust for cleanup and eventual disposition. This illustrates that contaminated manufacturing facilities often cannot be sold as going concerns in bankruptcy but must be remediated first or transferred to environmental trust for long-term management.

#### 7. EPA Leverage: Automatic Stay Exception and Administrative Expense Priority

**Police Power Exception to Automatic Stay:**

Bankruptcy Code ¬ß 362(b)(4) provides exception to automatic stay for governmental actions to enforce "police or regulatory power." This exception allows EPA and Pennsylvania DEP to continue environmental enforcement despite bankruptcy filing, giving agencies significant leverage over debtor-in-possession.

**EPA/DEP Can (despite automatic stay):**
‚úì Issue administrative orders requiring immediate cleanup
‚úì Conduct oversight inspections and demand access to facilities
‚úì Require environmental reporting and monitoring
‚úì Issue notices of violation and compliance orders
‚úì Perform cleanup themselves (though reimbursement may require bankruptcy court approval)

**EPA/DEP Cannot (without bankruptcy court relief):**
‚úó Enforce prepetition money judgments or penalties
‚úó Seize estate property to satisfy environmental debt
‚úó Assess post-petition penalties without bankruptcy court jurisdiction (penalties may be characterized as improper attempt to collect prepetition claim)

**Administrative Expense Elevation Strategy:**

EPA can elevate environmental claims from general unsecured status (low priority; typical recovery 10-40%) to administrative expense status (first priority; 100% payment) by:

1. **Post-petition administrative order**: Issue order requiring debtor-in-possession to conduct emergency removal or ongoing compliance, characterizing as "actual and necessary cost of preserving estate" under ¬ß 503(b)(1)(A)

2. **EPA self-help cleanup**: Perform cleanup during bankruptcy period and seek reimbursement as administrative expense under CERCLA ¬ß 107(a) cost recovery

3. **Threat of facility shutdown**: Threaten permit revocation or facility closure unless debtor addresses imminent hazards, forcing debtor to incur post-petition environmental costs qualifying for administrative priority

**Example Scenario:**

Debtor-in-possession continues operating manufacturing facility during Chapter 11 with known groundwater contamination. EPA issues post-petition administrative order under CERCLA ¬ß 106 requiring installation of groundwater extraction system to prevent off-site migration toward residential wells. Cost: $2 million capital + $200,000/year operating costs.

**Result:** Environmental obligation becomes administrative expense (first priority) rather than prepetition claim (low priority). If debtor has limited cash, $2 million administrative expense may render reorganization plan infeasible by consuming funds needed for operations or secured creditor payments.

**Strategic Dynamic:**

EPA holds extraordinary leverage in bankruptcy negotiations due to ability to:
- Continue enforcement despite automatic stay
- Convert environmental claims to administrative expenses
- Threaten facility shutdown
- Perform cleanup and invoice estate

This leverage often forces debtors to negotiate consent decrees with staged compliance obligations, allocate substantial plan funds to environmental trust, or liquidate contaminated facilities rather than attempting reorganization.

#### 8. Environmental Response Trusts Enable Reorganization for Large Cases

**RACER Trust Model (General Motors 2009 Bankruptcy):**

Largest environmental response trust in U.S. bankruptcy history (at 2011 establishment), the Motors Liquidation Company Environmental Response Trust ("RACER Trust") demonstrated mechanism for addressing extensive environmental liabilities in Chapter 11:

**Structure:**
- **Funding**: $773 million initial allocation from bankruptcy estate (later supplemented with ~$500 million from property sales proceeds)
- **Scope**: 89 contaminated sites across 14 states (former GM manufacturing facilities)
- **Governance**: Independent trustee appointed by bankruptcy court; oversight by EPA/state agencies
- **Mission**: Conduct environmental remediation; sell or donate remediated properties for productive reuse

**Operational Model:**
- Trust owns contaminated properties transferred from debtor's estate
- Conducts Phase I/II environmental assessments
- Implements remediation (soil excavation, groundwater treatment, institutional controls)
- Obtains regulatory closure (Pennsylvania Act 2 or state equivalents)
- Sells remediated properties (proceeds fund additional cleanup)
- Administrative expenses paid from dedicated funding account

**Benefits:**
- **Segregates environmental liabilities** from reorganized company (new GM), enabling "clean" reorganization
- **Dedicated funding** ensures cleanup proceeds regardless of reorganized company's financial performance
- **Professional management** by environmental trustee with expertise
- **Regulatory coordination** - EPA/states have single point of contact
- **Facilitates property disposition** - purchasers buy from trust, not debtor with uncertain liabilities

**Limitations:**
- **Requires substantial initial funding** ($500M-$773M for GM) - not feasible for smaller bankruptcies
- **Uncertain ultimate costs** - trust bears risk if remediation exceeds funding
- **Beneficiary limitations** - EPA/states/private parties have limited recourse if trust funds exhausted
- **Administrative costs** reduce funds available for actual cleanup

**Applicability to Western PA Manufacturing:**

Most regional manufacturers lack scale for trust structure:
- Small metal fabricators: $3-8M environmental liability (trust administration costs unjustified)
- Medium manufacturers: $10-35M liability (possibly feasible if adequate estate assets)
- Large steel/coke plants: $40-200M+ liability (trust potentially appropriate if viable ongoing business or substantial assets)

**Alternative: Environmental Reserve Fund** (smaller cases):

For mid-size manufacturers, less complex alternative: Bankruptcy plan establishes segregated cash reserve ($1-10M) administered by reorganized debtor or oversight committee for specified environmental purposes (ongoing monitoring, compliance, contingent cleanup). Provides dedicated funding without full trust structure.

#### 9. Pennsylvania Act 2 Land Recycling Program Provides State Cleanup Alternative

Pennsylvania's Act 2 (35 P.S. ¬ß 6026.101 et seq., enacted 1995) offers voluntary cleanup pathway particularly valuable for bankrupt manufacturers seeking to facilitate asset sales or brownfield redevelopment.

**Four Cornerstones:**
1. **Risk-based cleanup standards** (background, statewide health, site-specific)
2. **Standardized review procedures** (predictable timelines)
3. **Release from liability** (covenant not to sue from PA DEP)
4. **Financial assistance** (grants/loans for investigation and cleanup)

**Key Advantage vs. Federal Superfund:**

Act 2 provides **definitive liability closure** upon completion, whereas CERCLA/Superfund imposes **perpetual responsibility** with potential future liability if conditions change or additional contamination discovered.

**Cleanup Standard Options:**

| Standard | Cleanup Level | Typical Application |
|----------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Background** | ‚â§ Naturally occurring concentrations | Residential redevelopment; minimal contamination |
| **Statewide Health** | Generic risk-based (published values) | Commercial/industrial reuse; moderate contamination |
| **Site-Specific** | Custom risk assessment | Complex sites; cost-effective where generic standards impractical |

**Land Use-Based Approach:**

Act 2 recognizes that property designated for industrial use need not meet residential cleanup standards:
- **Residential**: Most stringent (assumes children playing in soil, consuming groundwater)
- **Commercial**: Moderate (assumes office workers, limited soil contact)
- **Industrial**: Least stringent (assumes industrial workers with PPE, no residential use)

**Strategic Value in Bankruptcy:**

**For Asset Sales (¬ß 363):**
- Seller (debtor estate) or purchaser conducts Act 2 cleanup to commercial/industrial standard
- Obtains Release of Liability from PA DEP upon completion
- Records deed notice/environmental covenant documenting institutional controls (land use restrictions)
- **Result**: Property marketable with liability closure; purchaser can obtain financing

**Cost Comparison:**

Act 2 cleanup to industrial/commercial standard typically **30-60% less expensive** than Superfund remediation to residential standard:
- Industrial standard soil cleanup: 1,000 mg/kg total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH) typical
- Residential standard: 100 mg/kg TPH typical
- Difference: 10x less soil excavation required for industrial standard

**Financial Assistance:**

Pennsylvania Industrial Sites Reuse Program provides grants/loans for:
- Phase I Environmental Site Assessment
- Phase II Environmental Site Assessment
- Phase III Remediation

**Eligibility**: "Innocent persons" conducting voluntary cleanups (not original polluters, but successor owners or municipalities acquiring through tax sale, etc.)

**Limitation**: Original polluter (bankrupt manufacturer) generally not eligible for grant funding, but purchaser from bankruptcy estate may qualify

**Example Application:**

Medium-size metal fabrication company files Chapter 11 with $5M environmental liability for soil/groundwater contamination. Debtor pursues ¬ß 363 sale:

1. Conducts Phase II ESA: $150,000
2. Purchaser conducts Act 2 remediation to industrial standard: $1.5M
3. Obtains PA DEP liability release
4. Purchase price reflects reduced environmental cost: $3.65M higher than without Act 2 (buyer's savings)

**Result**: Estate recovers $3.65M more; purchaser acquires with liability closure; property productive use vs. continued brownfield

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. Western Pennsylvania Industrial Context

Western Pennsylvania historically served as a major manufacturing center, particularly in steel production, metal fabrication, chemicals, coal mining, and heavy manufacturing. The region's industrial decline beginning in the 1980s resulted in numerous plant closures, bankruptcies, and legacy environmental contamination.

**Key Industrial Characteristics:**
- Heavy concentration along rivers (Allegheny, Monongahela, Ohio) due to water/transportation access
- Legacy steel mills, coking facilities, and integrated manufacturing operations
- Extensive use of hazardous materials (solvents, heavy metals, PCBs)
- Older facilities often lack modern pollution controls
- Significant brownfield inventory from closed facilities

### B. Regulatory Framework Overview

**Primary Federal Environmental Statutes:**

1. **Clean Air Act (CAA), 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 7401 et seq.**
   - Regulates air emissions from stationary sources
   - Title V operating permits for major sources
   - National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) compliance
   - Hazardous Air Pollutants (HAPs) regulation under ¬ß 7412

2. **Clean Water Act (CWA), 33 U.S.C. ¬ß 1251 et seq.**
   - National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permits
   - Prohibits unpermitted discharges to waters of the U.S.
   - Pretreatment standards for industrial discharges to POTWs
   - Stormwater discharge permits

3. **Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA), 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 6901 et seq.**
   - Cradle-to-grave hazardous waste management
   - Treatment, Storage, Disposal Facility (TSDF) permits
   - Corrective Action program for contaminated facilities
   - Underground Storage Tank (UST) regulation

4. **Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA), 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9601 et seq.**
   - Superfund liability for hazardous substance releases
   - Strict, joint and several, retroactive liability
   - National Priorities List (NPL) site designation
   - Potentially Responsible Party (PRP) liability scheme

### C. EPA Region 3 Enforcement Priorities (2000-2025)

EPA Region 3 (covering Pennsylvania, Delaware, Maryland, Virginia, West Virginia, DC) has focused enforcement efforts on:
- Legacy industrial contamination from steel/metal manufacturing
- Coal-related pollution (mine drainage, coal ash)
- Chemical manufacturing facility compliance
- NPDES permit violations (particularly metals and pH exceedances)
- RCRA hazardous waste violations at manufacturing facilities

### E. Environmental Liability Non-Dischargeability: Legal Framework

**Key Supreme Court Precedent: Ohio v. Kovacs (1985)**

*Ohio v. Kovacs*, 469 U.S. 274 (1985) established foundational principles for environmental obligations in bankruptcy.¬≥‚Å∑

**Case Background:**

William Kovacs was CEO and stockholder of Chem-Dyne Corp., which operated industrial and hazardous waste disposal site in Hamilton, Ohio. In 1976, Ohio sued Kovacs in state court for:
- Polluting public waters
- Maintaining nuisance
- Causing fish kills
- Violating state environmental laws

State court issued injunction ordering Kovacs to clean up hazardous waste site. After receiver appointed, Kovacs filed bankruptcy, seeking to discharge cleanup obligation.

**Supreme Court Holding:**

The Court held that with receiver in control of site, the cleanup order had been **converted into obligation to pay money**, which was **dischargeable in bankruptcy**.¬≥‚Å∏

**Critical Distinction:**

At oral argument, Ohio's counsel conceded that after receiver appointment, the only performance sought from Kovacs was payment of money (not personal performance of cleanup duties).

**Important Limitations Noted by Court:**¬≥‚Åπ

1. **No Shield from Criminal Prosecution:** Discharge does not shield Kovacs from prosecution for violating Ohio environmental laws or criminal contempt for not performing obligations prior to bankruptcy

2. **Injunctive Relief Preserved:** Injunction against bringing further toxic wastes on premises or conduct contributing to pollution **NOT dischargeable** - only affirmative cleanup duty dischargeable

3. **Successor Liability:** Anyone in possession of site (whether Kovacs or subsequent owner/vendee) must comply with Ohio environmental laws

**Kovacs Test for Dischargeability:**

Post-*Kovacs*, courts apply following analysis:
- Is debtor capable of personally executing cleanup, or can debtor only comply by paying someone else?
- If personal performance possible ‚Üí **injunctive relief, NOT dischargeable**
- If only payment of money ‚Üí **claim, potentially dischargeable**

**Third Circuit Application (Pennsylvania Bankruptcy Courts):**

In *In re Torwico Electronics, Inc.*, 8 F.3d 146 (3d Cir. 1993), the Third Circuit held:‚Å¥‚Å∞

Environmental cleanup obligations are **NOT dischargeable** when:
1. Relate to **ongoing pollution**, OR
2. Remediation necessary to **prevent imminent harm** to human health or environment

Environmental cleanup obligations **ARE dischargeable** when:
1. Relate to **past pollution** not posing ongoing threat, AND
2. Obligation reducible to payment of money

### F. CERCLA Liability in Bankruptcy: Critical Doctrines

#### 1. Strict, Joint and Several, Retroactive Liability

**Statutory Framework:** CERCLA ¬ß 107(a), 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)

**Four Categories of Potentially Responsible Parties (PRPs):**‚Å¥¬π

1. **Current owners/operators** of facility (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)(1))
2. **Past owners/operators** at time of disposal of hazardous substances (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)(2))
3. **Arrangers** - persons who arranged for disposal or treatment of hazardous substances (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)(3))
4. **Transporters** - persons who transported hazardous substances to facility (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)(4))

**Nature of Liability:**

- **Strict Liability:** No showing of negligence, intent, or fault required‚Å¥¬≤
- **Joint and Several:** Each PRP potentially liable for entire cleanup cost (unless harm divisible)‚Å¥¬≥
- **Retroactive:** Applies to disposal/releases predating CERCLA's 1980 enactment‚Å¥‚Å¥

**Burlington Northern Test for Divisibility:**

*Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. United States*, 556 U.S. 599 (2009) held: Burden on PRP to prove harm is divisible; otherwise liability is joint and several.‚Å¥‚Åµ

**Implication for Bankruptcy:**

CERCLA liability follows property and corporate entities regardless of bankruptcy discharge attempts. This creates significant obstacles for Chapter 11 reorganization when facility contamination present.

#### 2. Section 363 Sales: "Free and Clear" Limitation

**Bankruptcy Code ¬ß 363(b):** Allows sale of estate property "free and clear" of liens and interests, 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 363(f)

**CERCLA Exception:**

Courts consistently hold that contaminated property **cannot be sold "free and clear" of CERCLA liability** in bankruptcy ¬ß 363 sales.‚Å¥‚Å∂ CERCLA's strict liability for current owners means purchaser acquires PRP status upon taking title, regardless of bankruptcy court's "free and clear" order.

**Policy Rationale:**

Environmental liability is not a "claim" against debtor but rather inherent characteristic of contaminated property. Federal environmental law preempts state property law and bankruptcy provisions attempting to transfer contaminated property without liability.

**Strategic Implication:**

Bankruptcy asset sales of contaminated manufacturing facilities require:
- Comprehensive Phase II Environmental Site Assessments
- Purchaser due diligence and risk assessment
- Price discount reflecting environmental liability
- EPA/state agency notification and potential oversight
- Indemnification provisions (though limited utility given CERCLA strict liability)

#### 3. Administrative Expense Priority - Section 503(b)

**Statutory Framework:** 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 503(b) - Allowance of administrative expenses

Administrative expenses receive **first priority** distribution in bankruptcy, ahead of unsecured creditors and equity holders.‚Å¥‚Å∑

**Environmental Cleanup as Administrative Expense:**

Courts have held that environmental cleanup costs incurred **post-petition** may qualify for administrative expense priority under ¬ß 503(b)(1)(A) if:‚Å¥‚Å∏

1. Expense arose from **post-petition transaction** between creditor and debtor-in-possession, AND
2. Expense was **"actual and necessary"** to preserve estate

**Examples Qualifying for Administrative Priority:**

- Emergency removal actions required by EPA during bankruptcy to prevent imminent harm
- Ongoing groundwater treatment system operation to comply with permits
- Stormwater system maintenance to prevent unpermitted discharges
- Environmental monitoring required by consent decree

**Strategic Significance:**

EPA can elevate environmental claims to administrative expense status by:
1. Issuing administrative order requiring immediate action during bankruptcy
2. Performing cleanup itself and seeking reimbursement as administrative expense
3. Threatening facility shutdown if debtor-in-possession fails to address imminent hazards

This effectively converts environmental liability from dischargeable prepetition claim to non-dischargeable administrative priority.

## V. REMEDIATION PROCESS & TIMELINE

### A. CERCLA/Superfund Remediation Process

**Statutory Framework:** National Contingency Plan (NCP), 40 C.F.R. Part 300

The EPA Superfund remediation process follows structured phases mandated by the National Contingency Plan:‚Åµ‚Å∏

#### Phase 1: Preliminary Assessment / Site Inspection (PA/SI)

**Duration:** 6-18 months
**Purpose:** Initial site evaluation to determine if contamination warrants further investigation
**Activities:**
- Review historical records, aerial photographs, regulatory files
- Limited site visit and visual inspection
- Preliminary sampling (if necessary)
- Hazard Ranking System (HRS) scoring

**Outcome:** Decision whether to propose site for National Priorities List (NPL)

#### Phase 2: NPL Listing

**Process:** EPA proposes site for NPL, public comment period (60 days), final listing
**Legal Effect:** Triggers mandatory CERCLA remedial investigation/feasibility study
**Significance:** NPL listing creates significant property stigma and financing challenges

#### Phase 3: Remedial Investigation/Feasibility Study (RI/FS)

**Duration:** 2-4 years (complex industrial sites)
**Statutory Citation:** 40 C.F.R. ¬ß 300.430

**Remedial Investigation (RI) Components:**‚Åµ‚Åπ

1. **Project Scoping:** Define investigation objectives, data quality objectives
2. **Site Characterization:**
   - Soil sampling (surface, subsurface via borings)
   - Groundwater monitoring well installation and sampling
   - Surface water and sediment sampling
   - Indoor air/soil vapor sampling (if volatile contaminants)
   - Ecological sampling (if sensitive habitats)
3. **Risk Assessment:**
   - Human health risk assessment (exposure pathways, receptor scenarios)
   - Ecological risk assessment
   - Baseline risk quantification (cancer risk, hazard quotient)
4. **Treatability Studies:** Laboratory or pilot-scale testing of potential remediation technologies

**Feasibility Study (FS) Components:**‚Å∂‚Å∞

1. **Remedial Alternative Development:** Identify range of cleanup approaches
2. **Detailed Analysis of Alternatives:** Evaluate each alternative against nine criteria:
   - Overall protection of human health and environment (threshold)
   - Compliance with Applicable or Relevant and Appropriate Requirements (ARARs) (threshold)
   - Long-term effectiveness and permanence
   - Reduction of toxicity, mobility, or volume
   - Short-term effectiveness
   - Implementability
   - Cost
   - State acceptance
   - Community acceptance
3. **Cost Estimation:** Capital costs, operation & maintenance (O&M), present worth analysis
4. **Comparative Analysis:** Compare alternatives, identify preferred alternative

**Typical RI/FS Costs for Manufacturing Facilities:**
- Small sites (<5 acres, limited contamination): $500,000 - $1.5 million
- Medium sites (5-50 acres, multiple contaminants): $1.5 million - $5 million
- Large sites (>50 acres, complex contamination): $5 million - $20+ million

#### Phase 4: Proposed Plan & Public Comment

**Duration:** 30+ days public comment period
**Activities:**
- EPA issues Proposed Plan identifying preferred remedial alternative
- Public meeting(s) to present plan and receive comments
- EPA responds to significant comments

#### Phase 5: Record of Decision (ROD)

**Legal Document:** ROD documents EPA's final remedy selection‚Å∂¬π
**Contents:**
- Selected remedy description
- Basis for remedy selection (responsiveness summary)
- Estimated cost and timeframe
- Cleanup goals/performance standards
- ARARs to be attained

**ROD Finality:** ROD is final agency action, subject to judicial review only in limited circumstances

#### Phase 6: Remedial Design (RD)

**Duration:** 1-3 years
**Activities:**
- Detailed engineering design drawings and specifications
- Permits acquisition (state/local)
- Contracting procurement
- Design quality assurance

**Typical RD Costs:** 10-15% of remedial action construction costs

#### Phase 7: Remedial Action (RA)

**Duration:** 1-5 years (construction); 10-30+ years (long-term O&M)
**Activities:**
- Mobilization and site preparation
- Remedial system construction (e.g., excavation, treatment plants, caps)
- System startup and performance testing
- Long-term operation and maintenance (groundwater pump-and-treat, monitoring)

**Typical RA Costs for Industrial Manufacturing Sites:**

| Remedy Type | Capital Cost Range | Annual O&M Cost |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Institutional Controls Only | $50,000 - $200,000 | $10,000 - $30,000 |
| Soil Excavation (limited) | $500,000 - $3 million | Minimal (post-removal) |
| Soil Excavation (extensive) | $5 million - $30 million | Minimal |
| Groundwater Pump & Treat | $1 million - $10 million | $100,000 - $500,000/yr |
| In-Situ Treatment (PRB, bioremediation) | $2 million - $15 million | $50,000 - $200,000/yr |
| Soil Vapor Extraction | $500,000 - $5 million | $75,000 - $300,000/yr |
| Engineered Cap/Containment | $2 million - $10 million | $50,000 - $150,000/yr |
| Multi-media (combined) | $10 million - $50+ million | $200,000 - $1 million/yr |

#### Phase 8: Construction Completion

**Milestone:** All physical construction complete, systems operational
**Documentation:** Preliminary Close-Out Report
**Significance:** Site may be deleted from NPL (but ongoing O&M/monitoring continues)

#### Phase 9: Long-Term Response Actions

**Duration:** 30 years or more
**Activities:**
- Ongoing groundwater monitoring
- Treatment system operation
- Five-year remedy reviews (required by CERCLA)
- Institutional control maintenance (deed restrictions, fencing)

#### Phase 10: Site Closeout (if applicable)

**Criteria:** Cleanup goals achieved, no further action necessary
**Documentation:** Final Close-Out Report
**NPL Deletion:** Site formally removed from National Priorities List

**Reality for Manufacturing Sites:** Most industrial Superfund sites require **perpetual monitoring and institutional controls** - true "closeout" rare

## VI. BANKRUPTCY IMPACT ON REMEDIATION

### A. Automatic Stay vs. Environmental Enforcement

**Automatic Stay Provision:** 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 362(a) - bankruptcy filing automatically stays most litigation and collection actions

**Environmental Exception:** 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 362(b)(4) - "Police Power" Exception‚Å∑‚Å∞

The automatic stay does NOT apply to:
- Commencement or continuation of governmental action to enforce police or regulatory power
- Environmental enforcement actions by EPA or state agencies
- Issuance of environmental cleanup orders

**Limits on Police Power Exception:**

Stay DOES apply to:
- Collection of money judgments from debtor
- Enforcement of money judgments against estate property
- Actions to obtain property of estate (except for reasonable compensation for enforcement services)

**Practical Effect:**

EPA and state environmental agencies can:
‚úì Issue administrative orders requiring cleanup
‚úì Conduct oversight inspections
‚úì Require reporting and monitoring
‚úì Perform cleanup themselves (though may need bankruptcy court approval for reimbursement)

EPA and state agencies CANNOT (without bankruptcy court relief):
‚úó Enforce pre-petition penalties or fines
‚úó Seize estate property to satisfy pre-petition debt
‚úó Assess post-petition penalties without bankruptcy court jurisdiction

**Key Cases:**

- *Penn Terra Ltd. v. Department of Environmental Resources*, 733 F.2d 267 (3d Cir. 1984): Environmental injunctive relief falls within police power exception‚Å∑¬π
- *LTV Steel*, 269 B.R. 576 (W.D. Pa. 2001): Sixth Circuit recognizes police power exception for environmental enforcement‚Å∑¬≤

### B. Treatment of Environmental Claims in Chapter 11 Plans

Environmental liabilities in bankruptcy plans can be addressed through multiple mechanisms:

#### 1. Classification and Treatment in Plan

**Prepetition Environmental Claims:** Typically classified as general unsecured claims (absent special priority)

**Potential Classifications:**

**Class 1: Administrative Expenses (¬ß 503(b))** - First priority
- Post-petition cleanup costs
- Emergency response actions during bankruptcy
- Ongoing monitoring/compliance required for operations
- **Payment:** 100% in cash on effective date (or shortly thereafter)

**Class 2: Priority Unsecured Claims (¬ß 507)** - Second priority
- Limited environmental priority (e.g., certain penalties)
- **Payment:** 100% in cash (typically)

**Class 3: General Unsecured Claims** - Lower priority
- Prepetition cleanup costs (if not administrative expense)
- Prepetition penalties and fines
- Contribution claims from other PRPs
- **Payment:** Pro rata distribution (often 10-40% recovery in manufacturing bankruptcies)

#### 2. Environmental Response Trusts

**Structure:** Bankruptcy plan establishes trust to address environmental liabilities post-emergence

**Notable Example: General Motors (2009)**‚Å∑¬≥

General Motors bankruptcy created **Motors Liquidation Company Environmental Response Trust** (commonly known as **RACER Trust**):

- **Funding:** $773 million at establishment (later supplemented)
- **Scope:** 89 contaminated sites across 14 states
- **Largest Environmental Response Trust in U.S. bankruptcy history** (at time of creation, March 2011)‚Å∑‚Å¥

**RACER Trust Operational Model:**‚Å∑‚Åµ

- Independent trustee manages cleanup at scheduled properties
- Funded by initial bankruptcy plan allocation + proceeds from property sales/leases
- Conducts remediation with EPA/state oversight
- Day-to-day expenses paid from Administrative Funding Account
- Trust continues until all properties remediated or funds exhausted

**Tax Treatment:**

Environmental remediation trust qualifies as trust for Internal Revenue Code purposes if:‚Å∑‚Å∂
- Organized under state law
- Primary purpose: Collect and disburse amounts for environmental remediation
- Resolves environmental liability under CERCLA, RCRA, or state equivalents

**Advantages of Trust Structure:**

- Segregates environmental liabilities from reorganized debtor
- Provides dedicated funding stream
- Enables ongoing EPA/state oversight without disrupting debtor's operations
- Facilitates property sales (purchasers buy from trust, not debtor with uncertain liabilities)

**Disadvantages:**

- Requires substantial upfront funding (may not be feasible for smaller bankruptcies)
- Trust beneficiaries (EPA, states, potentially private parties) have limited recourse if funds insufficient
- Administrative costs of trust operation reduce funds available for actual cleanup

#### 3. Assumption, Rejection, or Assignment of Environmental Permits/Agreements

**Consent Decrees and Administrative Orders:**

Under 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 365, debtor-in-possession may assume or reject executory contracts, but environmental consent decrees generally deemed **non-executory government regulatory actions** not subject to ¬ß 365.‚Å∑‚Å∑

**Implication:** Debtor cannot "reject" consent decree to avoid cleanup obligations

**Environmental Permits:**

- Clean Water Act NPDES permits
- Clean Air Act Title V operating permits
- RCRA hazardous waste permits

**Treatment:** Permits generally characterized as regulatory licenses, not property subject to assumption/rejection. Permits required for ongoing operations must be maintained; failure to comply can result in permit revocation and facility shutdown.

**Asset Sales Under ¬ß 363:**

When debtor sells contaminated property under ¬ß 363, key issues:

- **CERCLA Liability Transfers:** Purchaser becomes "current owner" PRP regardless of "free and clear" language in sale order‚Å∑‚Å∏
- **Purchaser Defenses:** Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser (BFPP) defense under CERCLA ¬ß 101(40) may be available if purchaser conducts "all appropriate inquiry" and takes reasonable steps to prevent continued releases‚Å∑‚Åπ
- **Indemnification:** Debtor may provide limited indemnification (funded from estate), but CERCLA strict liability means indemnity has limited protective value
- **Price Adjustment:** Environmental contamination reflected in purchase price discount

### C. Discharge of Environmental Obligations

**Central Legal Question:** Can environmental cleanup obligations be discharged in Chapter 11?

**Answer:** **Depends on nature of obligation** (injunctive vs. monetary; ongoing threat vs. past contamination)

#### *Ohio v. Kovacs* Framework (Supreme Court 1985)‚Å∏‚Å∞

**Test:** Is cleanup obligation an injunction requiring personal performance by debtor, or merely obligation to pay money?

- **Personal Performance Obligation:** Injunctive relief, **NOT dischargeable**
- **Payment Obligation:** Claim, **potentially dischargeable**

**Kovacs Holding:** Where receiver in control of site and debtor incapable of personal performance, cleanup order converted to money obligation ‚Üí dischargeable

**Limitations Noted by Court:**‚Å∏¬π

1. Discharge does not shield from criminal prosecution for environmental violations
2. Injunctions against **future conduct** (e.g., no additional dumping) remain enforceable
3. **Successor owners/operators** must comply with environmental laws regardless of prior owner's discharge

#### Third Circuit Refinement: *In re Torwico Electronics* (1993)‚Å∏¬≤

**Test:** Obligations **NOT dischargeable** if:
1. Relate to **ongoing pollution**, OR
2. Remediation necessary to prevent **imminent harm** to human health or environment

**Obligations dischargeable** if:
1. Relate to **past pollution** not posing ongoing threat, AND
2. Obligation reducible to payment of money

**Application to Manufacturing Bankruptcies:**

| Scenario | Dischargeable? | Reasoning |
|----------|----------------|-----------|
| Groundwater plume migrating off-site toward drinking water wells | **NO** | Ongoing threat to human health |
| Soil contamination beneath closed facility, capped, no exposure pathway | **LIKELY YES** | Past pollution, no imminent harm, monetary obligation to maintain cap |
| NPDES permit violations - unpaid penalties for past exceedances | **LIKELY YES** | Monetary claim for past violations |
| NPDES permit violations - ongoing exceedances requiring operational changes | **NO** | Ongoing violations, injunctive relief |
| CERCLA response costs - EPA seeks reimbursement for past cleanup | **LIKELY YES** | Monetary claim (if debtor cannot perform cleanup) |
| CERCLA - EPA orders debtor to conduct future cleanup | **NO** | Injunctive relief for ongoing contamination |

### D. Post-Petition Environmental Obligations as Administrative Expenses

**Statutory Framework:** 11 U.S.C. ¬ß 503(b)(1)(A) - "actual, necessary costs and expenses of preserving the estate"

**Courts' Analysis:** Environmental obligations incurred post-petition qualify for administrative expense priority if:‚Å∏¬≥

1. **Post-Petition Transaction:** Obligation arises from debtor-in-possession's post-petition conduct or operations
2. **Actual and Necessary:** Expense necessary to preserve estate (e.g., compliance required to continue operations, avoid facility shutdown)

**Examples Granted Administrative Priority:**

- **Emergency removal actions:** EPA requires immediate cleanup of leaking tanks during bankruptcy to prevent imminent hazard ‚Üí administrative expense‚Å∏‚Å¥
- **Ongoing compliance costs:** Hazardous waste storage, disposal, monitoring required to continue manufacturing operations ‚Üí administrative expense‚Å∏‚Åµ
- **Permit compliance:** Modifications to wastewater treatment to maintain NPDES permit necessary for operations ‚Üí administrative expense

**Examples Denied Administrative Priority:**

- **Prepetition contamination:** Cleanup of groundwater contamination from pre-petition releases, even if discovered post-petition ‚Üí general unsecured claim
- **Elective cleanup:** Voluntary remediation not required by enforcement action or permit ‚Üí general unsecured claim

**Strategic Implications for EPA:**

EPA can elevate environmental claims to administrative priority by:
1. Issuing post-petition administrative order requiring immediate action
2. Performing cleanup itself post-petition and seeking reimbursement as administrative expense under CERCLA ¬ß 107(a)
3. Threatening to shut down facility unless debtor addresses imminent hazards

**Impact on Bankruptcy Reorganization:**

Administrative expenses paid ahead of general unsecured creditors ‚Üí substantial environmental administrative expenses can render reorganization plan infeasible by consuming available cash

## VIII. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Key Findings

#### 1. High Prevalence of Environmental Violations in Manufacturing Sector

Western Pennsylvania manufacturing facilities, particularly in steel production, metal fabrication, and chemical manufacturing, exhibit near-universal history of environmental compliance issues. Major facilities documented in this research (U.S. Steel, Allegheny Ludlum/ATI, LTV Steel, Westinghouse) all have significant EPA/state enforcement histories spanning multiple decades.

**Finding:** 100% of large steel/coke production facilities have documented Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, or RCRA violations between 2000-2025.

#### 2. Clean Water Act NPDES Violations Most Common

Discharge permit violations (heavy metals, oil & grease, total suspended solids, pH) represent the most frequent environmental compliance failure in Western PA manufacturing. These violations often persist for years before enforcement action taken.

**Examples Documented:**
- Allegheny Ludlum: 832 CWA violations (1995-2002 case)
- Hussey Copper: 20 violations documented (2011-2017 study period)
- U.S. Steel Irvin Plant: Multiple oil sheen discharges (2022-2025)

**Enforcement Gap:** EPA data indicates less than 50% of non-compliant facilities face formal enforcement, suggesting documented violations represent subset of actual non-compliance.

#### 3. Substantial Remediation Costs Threaten Reorganization Viability

Environmental cleanup costs for industrial manufacturing facilities routinely exceed $10 million and can reach $40-200+ million for large steel/coke plants. These costs, combined with long remediation timelines (30+ years), often render Chapter 11 reorganization infeasible.

**Cost Estimates by Facility Type:**
- Small metal fabrication: $3-8 million total lifecycle cost
- Medium manufacturing: $10-35 million
- Large steel/coke plant: $40-200+ million

**Documented Cases:**
- LTV Steel: $22+ million documented CERCLA liability (likely understated)
- Allegheny Ludlum: $8.2 million Clean Water Act penalty + ongoing RCRA corrective action
- Westinghouse Sharon Plant: $40-60 million estimated PCB remediation
- General Motors West Mifflin: $3-7 million site cleanup

#### 4. Timing of Violations Correlates with Financial Distress

Environmental violations increase in frequency during 1-5 year period preceding bankruptcy as companies defer environmental compliance spending to preserve cash. This creates "doom loop" where deteriorating environmental compliance accelerates financial distress.

**Pattern Observed:**
- Pre-bankruptcy: Increased permit exceedances, delayed equipment maintenance, deferred capital projects
- Financial impact: Large penalties ($1.5M - $8.2M documented) accelerate cash burn
- Outcome: Environmental liabilities compound underlying business challenges

#### 5. Environmental Liabilities Generally Non-Dischargeable

Under *Ohio v. Kovacs* (1985) and Third Circuit's *Torwico Electronics* (1993), environmental cleanup obligations related to ongoing pollution or imminent harm are NOT dischargeable in bankruptcy. Only past pollution not posing ongoing threat may be discharged as monetary claim.

**Practical Effect:** Manufacturing facilities with groundwater plumes, ongoing air emissions violations, or hazardous waste contamination **cannot** discharge these obligations in Chapter 11. Liabilities follow reorganized company or liquidation trustee.

#### 6. CERCLA "Free and Clear" Sale Exception

Contaminated property cannot be sold "free and clear" of environmental liability in bankruptcy ¬ß 363 sales. CERCLA strict liability attaches to "current owner" regardless of bankruptcy court order, creating significant obstacle to asset disposition.

**Implication:** Purchasers of contaminated manufacturing facilities in bankruptcy acquire PRP status, requiring price discount reflecting environmental liability. This substantially reduces estate recovery and may render properties unsaleable.

#### 7. EPA Can Elevate Claims to Administrative Expense Priority

By issuing post-petition administrative orders or performing cleanup during bankruptcy, EPA can elevate environmental claims from general unsecured (low recovery) to administrative expense (100% priority). This tactic can render reorganization plans infeasible by consuming available cash.

**Strategic Dynamic:** EPA holds significant leverage in bankruptcy negotiations due to ability to:
- Continue enforcement despite automatic stay (police power exception)
- Convert cleanup obligations to administrative expenses
- Threaten facility shutdown for non-compliance during bankruptcy

#### 8. Environmental Response Trusts Enable Reorganization for Large Cases

General Motors bankruptcy (2009) established RACER Trust model: segregate environmental liabilities into dedicated trust funded by bankruptcy plan, enabling "clean" reorganization of operating company. This model requires substantial capital ($500M-$773M for RACER Trust) and thus limited to largest bankruptcies.

**Applicability to Western PA Manufacturing:** Most regional manufacturers lack scale for trust structure; environmental liabilities remain with estate or revert to EPA/state for taxpayer-funded cleanup.

#### 9. Pennsylvania Act 2 Provides State Cleanup Alternative

Pennsylvania's Land Recycling Program (Act 2) offers voluntary cleanup pathway with liability relief upon completion. Act 2 enables risk-based, use-specific cleanup (industrial/commercial standards less stringent than residential) and access to state financial assistance.

**Advantage vs. Federal Superfund:** Act 2 provides definitive liability closure; Superfund requires perpetual responsibility. For bankrupt manufacturers, Act 2 may facilitate asset sales by enabling purchasers to remediate to achievable standards and obtain liability release.

#### 10. Regional Economic Impact of Environmental Legacy

Western Pennsylvania manufacturing decline (33% employment reduction 1999-2019; 97% reduction in Mon Valley steelworks employment) created extensive brownfield inventory with environmental contamination. Many sites remain unremediated decades after plant closures due to insufficient PRP resources and limited public funding.

**Long-Term Impact:**
- 95 NPL Superfund sites in Pennsylvania (3rd highest nationally)
- Extensive brownfields constrain economic redevelopment
- Ongoing fish consumption advisories (e.g., Shenango River PCBs - Westinghouse legacy)
- Taxpayer-funded cleanup when PRPs insolvent

### B. Recommendations for Addressing Environmental Obligations in Bankruptcy

#### Recommendation 1: Early Environmental Due Diligence

**Action:** Conduct comprehensive Phase II Environmental Site Assessment **before** bankruptcy filing to quantify environmental liabilities

**Rationale:**
- Enables accurate disclosure of environmental claims in bankruptcy schedules
- Informs reorganization plan feasibility analysis
- Facilitates negotiations with EPA/state agencies pre-petition
- Supports valuation of assets for potential ¬ß 363 sale

**Specific Steps:**
- Retain qualified environmental consultant to review:
  - Historical operations and waste management practices
  - Prior environmental violations and enforcement actions
  - NPDES, Title V, RCRA permits and compliance history
  - Potential CERCLA PRP status at offsite disposal facilities
- Conduct soil, groundwater, soil vapor sampling at all owned/operated facilities
- Estimate remediation costs (investigation, cleanup, long-term monitoring)
- Identify potential purchaser concerns for contaminated assets

**Estimated Cost:** $50,000 - $500,000 (depending on facility complexity)
**Benefit:** Avoids discovery of environmental "surprises" post-petition that derail reorganization

#### Recommendation 2: Proactive EPA/State Agency Engagement

**Action:** Initiate dialogue with EPA Region 3 and Pennsylvania DEP **before** bankruptcy filing

**Rationale:**
- Agencies more willing to negotiate realistic compliance schedules when debtor proactive
- May avoid post-petition administrative orders that elevate claims to administrative expense
- Can structure consent decree/administrative order to address violations while preserving reorganization viability

**Specific Steps:**
- Disclose environmental liabilities identified in Phase II ESA
- Propose compliance schedule tied to reorganization timeline
- Negotiate settlement of prepetition penalties (often reduced in bankruptcy context)
- Seek agreement on treatment of environmental claims in bankruptcy plan (e.g., general unsecured vs. administrative expense)

**Potential Outcome:** Consent decree with staged compliance obligations, avoiding facility shutdown while addressing environmental concerns

#### Recommendation 3: Utilize Pennsylvania Act 2 for Asset Sales

**Action:** For contaminated properties subject to ¬ß 363 sale, pursue Pennsylvania Act 2 cleanup with purchaser

**Rationale:**
- Act 2 provides liability release upon completion (vs. perpetual CERCLA liability)
- Risk-based, use-specific standards enable achievable remediation (industrial/commercial vs. residential)
- Pennsylvania financial assistance may be available (Industrial Sites Reuse Program)
- Enhances marketability of contaminated assets

**Specific Steps:**
- Identify intended future use (industrial, commercial, mixed-use)
- Select appropriate Act 2 cleanup standard (background, statewide health, or site-specific)
- Prepare Remedial Investigation Report and Remedial Action Plan
- Implement cleanup and obtain PA DEP approval
- Record deed notice/environmental covenant documenting institutional controls
- Obtain Release of Liability from PA DEP

**Estimated Timeline:** 18-36 months (concurrent with bankruptcy if initiated pre-petition)
**Estimated Cost:** $100,000 - $10+ million (highly site-specific; may be borne by purchaser)

#### Recommendation 4: Bankruptcy Plan Provisions for Ongoing Environmental Obligations

**Action:** Structure Chapter 11 plan to address long-term environmental compliance and monitoring

**Options:**

**Option A: Direct Assumption by Reorganized Debtor**
- Plan provides that reorganized debtor assumes ongoing environmental obligations
- Requires adequate capitalization to fund compliance
- Suitable for: Viable ongoing businesses with manageable environmental obligations

**Option B: Environmental Reserve Fund**
- Plan establishes segregated cash reserve for environmental compliance
- Funded by allocation from plan proceeds
- Trustee or reorganized debtor manages fund for specified environmental purposes
- Suitable for: Moderate environmental obligations ($1-10M) where trust structure too costly

**Option C: Environmental Response Trust** (large cases only)
- Separate legal entity established to address environmental liabilities
- Funded by significant plan allocation ($50M+) or percentage of future revenues
- Independent trustee administers cleanup with EPA/state oversight
- Suitable for: Major industrial bankruptcies with extensive contamination (GM RACER Trust model)

**Option D: Sale to Entity Assuming Environmental Obligations**
- ¬ß 363 sale to purchaser willing to assume CERCLA liability
- Purchaser must qualify for BFPP defense (all appropriate inquiry, reasonable steps)
- Purchase price discounted to reflect environmental liability
- Suitable for: Viable facilities where going-concern value exceeds environmental liability

**Plan Language Considerations:**
- Specify treatment of environmental claims (class, recovery percentage)
- Address allocation between capital costs and long-term monitoring
- Provide for contingent claims (unknown contamination discovered post-emergence)
- Coordinate with consent decrees/administrative orders

#### Recommendation 5: IP Retention Strategy to Fund Environmental Obligations

**Cross-Reference to T3 (patent-analyst):** Manufacturing companies may retain valuable intellectual property (patents, trademarks, manufacturing processes) that can be monetized to fund environmental cleanup

**Action:** Evaluate retention and licensing of IP as funding source for environmental obligations

**Rationale:**
- Bankruptcy ¬ß 365(n) protects technology licensees if debtor rejects license
- Licensing revenue can provide ongoing cash flow to fund environmental monitoring
- Sale of IP portfolio can provide lump-sum funding for environmental trust

**Specific Steps:**
- Inventory all IP assets (patents, trademarks, trade secrets, manufacturing processes)
- Obtain valuation of IP portfolio
- Identify potential licensees or purchasers
- Structure licensing agreements to provide revenue stream for environmental compliance
- Allocate percentage of licensing revenue to environmental reserve fund

**Estimated Revenue Potential:** Highly variable ($100K-$10M+ annually depending on IP portfolio value)

#### Recommendation 6: Seek Contribution from Other PRPs

**Action:** For sites with multiple Potentially Responsible Parties, pursue CERCLA ¬ß 113(f) contribution claims

**Rationale:**
- CERCLA liability is joint and several, but PRPs may seek contribution from other responsible parties
- Manufacturing facility may be one of multiple parties who disposed of waste at common site
- Contribution claims can reduce net environmental liability in bankruptcy

**Specific Steps:**
- Identify all PRPs at each CERCLA site where debtor has liability
- Assess financial viability of other PRPs
- Initiate CERCLA ¬ß 113(f) contribution action (preferably pre-petition or as asset of estate)
- Negotiate allocation agreements based on "Gore Factors" (volume, toxicity, degree of involvement)

**Potential Recovery:** 20-80% of cleanup costs (depending on number and solvency of other PRPs)

**Example:** If debtor faces $10M CERCLA liability at site with 5 other solvent PRPs, contribution may reduce debtor's share to $1-3M (10-30% allocation)

#### Recommendation 7: Challenge EPA/State Liability Determinations Where Appropriate

**Action:** Where debtor's environmental liability unclear or overstated, challenge agency determinations

**Potential Challenges:**

**A. PRP Status Challenge:**
- Argue debtor not "owner or operator" at time of disposal
- Argue debtor not "arranger" (did not arrange for disposal - see *Burlington Northern*)
- Raise innocent landowner or BFPP defenses

**B. Divisibility of Harm:**
- Per *Burlington Northern* (2009), burden on PRP to prove harm divisible, but if successful, avoids joint and several liability
- Obtain expert evidence of divisibility (volumetric analysis, toxicity analysis, geographic separation)

**C. Compliance with ARARs:**
- Challenge agency determination that cleanup must meet certain Applicable or Relevant and Appropriate Requirements (ARARs)
- Argue less stringent standards sufficient for intended land use

**D. Cost Reasonableness:**
- Challenge EPA's proposed remedy as not "cost-effective" under NCP
- Propose alternative remedy achieving similar protection at lower cost

**Estimated Cost of Challenge:** $200,000 - $2M (litigation/expert fees)
**Potential Benefit:** Substantial reduction in environmental liability if successful
**Risk:** Delays resolution; may antagonize EPA/state agencies

**Recommendation:** Pursue challenges only where strong factual/legal basis; weigh litigation costs against potential savings
