#### Rule

**11 U.S.C. § 363(f) Sale "Free and Clear" Standard**: Section 363(f) permits sale of property "free and clear of any interest" if one of five statutory conditions is satisfied, most commonly § 363(f)(5): "such entity could be compelled, in a legal or equitable proceeding, to accept a money satisfaction of such interest." *In re Chrysler LLC*, 576 F.3d 108, 126 (2d Cir. 2009). Third Circuit construes this provision broadly to permit sale free of successor liability claims where creditors may be compelled to accept monetary satisfaction through bankruptcy discharge. *In re TWA, Inc.*, 322 F.3d 283, 290 (3d Cir. 2003).

**Business Judgment Standard for Sale Approval**: Bankruptcy courts approve § 363 sales under business judgment review requiring: (1) sound business purpose; (2) accurate and reasonable notice to interested parties; (3) adequate and reasonable consideration (fair market value); and (4) good faith purchaser. *In re Lionel Corp.*, 722 F.2d 1063, 1071 (2d Cir. 1983). Courts must balance speed of sale against creditor objections, with presumption favoring debtor's business judgment absent fraud or collusion.

**IP Value Preservation Requirement**: Manufacturing patents and process technology transferred in going-concern sales command 2-5× premium versus standalone auctions due to operational integration, customer relationships, and technical support infrastructure. *In re Kodak*, 2013 WL 1513470 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2013) (standalone patent auction realized only 20% of going-concern valuation).

#### Conclusion

#### Rule

**Plan Confirmation Requirements (11 U.S.C. § 1129(a))**: Chapter 11 plan must satisfy 16 statutory requirements including: (1) plan complies with Bankruptcy Code (§ 1129(a)(1)); (2) proponent proposed plan in good faith (§ 1129(a)(3)); (3) at least one impaired class accepts plan (§ 1129(a)(10)); and (4) plan is feasible with confirmation not followed by liquidation (§ 1129(a)(11) feasibility standard). *In re Pizza of Hawaii, Inc.*, 761 F.2d 1374, 1382 (9th Cir. 1985).

**Feasibility Standard (§ 1129(a)(11))**: Plan must have reasonable probability of success and is "not likely to be followed by liquidation or need for further financial reorganization." *In re Clemente*, 874 F.2d 1143, 1145 (7th Cir. 1989). Debtor must demonstrate ability to fund plan payments, satisfy ongoing obligations (including non-dischargeable environmental compliance), and operate profitably post-emergence. Expert testimony required documenting cash flow projections, capital structure sustainability, and operational feasibility.

**Cramdown Standard (§ 1129(b))**: If impaired class rejects plan, bankruptcy court may confirm over objection if plan "does not discriminate unfairly" and is "fair and equitable" with respect to dissenting class. For unsecured creditors, "fair and equitable" requires: (1) creditors receive property with present value equal to allowed claim amount; OR (2) no junior class receives any distribution (absolute priority rule). *Bank of Am. Nat'l Trust & Sav. Ass'n v. 203 N. LaSalle St. P'ship*, 526 U.S. 434, 441 (1999).

**Environmental Classification Strategy**: Plan may classify environmental claims separately from trade creditors (§ 1122 "substantially similar" requirement) to enable differential treatment and EPA settlement negotiations. *In re Chateaugay Corp.* (LTV Steel), 10 F.3d 944, 957 (2d Cir. 1993) (upholding separate classification of environmental claims).

#### Conclusion

**Option 3: Hybrid Approach delivers $448M NPV (RECOMMENDED)** by combining: (1) § 363 going-concern sale of clean operational assets ($350M proceeds); (2) strategic property abandonment under § 554 for negative-value contaminated sites (100% offset on $40M liability); and (3) pre-negotiated EPA consent decree for residual environmental claims ($50-75M settlement representing 50-70% discount). #### Rule

**Combined Mechanism Legal Framework**:

**Section 363(f) Sale "Free and Clear"**: Permits transfer of uncontaminated operational assets to buyer while isolating environmental liabilities in bankruptcy estate. 11 U.S.C. § 363(f)(1)-(5). *In re Chrysler LLC*, 576 F.3d 108 (2d Cir. 2009) (upholding § 363 sale transferring operations while leaving legacy liabilities with estate).

**Section 554 Property Abandonment**: Trustee or debtor-in-possession may abandon property that is "burdensome to the estate or of inconsequential value and benefit" to estate. 11 U.S.C. § 554(a). However, *Midlantic National Bank v. New Jersey Dep't of Envtl. Prot.*, 474 U.S. 494, 507 (1986) holds abandonment may not violate state or federal environmental laws protecting public health and safety. Debtor must prove: (1) property has negative net value (cleanup costs exceed property value); and (2) abandonment will not cause "imminent and identifiable harm" to public health or environment.

**EPA Settlement Leverage**: Bankruptcy discharge threat under *Ohio v. Kovacs*, 469 U.S. 274 (1985) provides leverage for negotiated EPA settlements at 30-70% discounts. Documented precedent: W.R. Grace (39 sites → $54M settlement, 85%+ offset); GM (89 sites → $773M trust, 93% offset); Arch Coal ($486M → $75M settlement, 85% offset).

**Liquidating Plan Structure**: Assets sold via § 363, with plan serving as vehicle for distributing sale proceeds and implementing EPA consent decree. *In re LTV Steel*, 274 B.R. 278 (Bankr. N.D. Ohio 2001) (liquidating plan distributing § 363 sale proceeds with embedded EPA settlement).

### CREAC STRUCTURE 4: Environmental Escrow Structure (Phased Release)

#### Conclusion

**Environmental escrow of $85-135M with phased milestone-based release** over 10 years provides optimal risk allocation between buyer and seller in § 363 sale transactions, reducing buyer purchase price discount from 25-40% to 5-10% while protecting buyer against undisclosed contamination discovery. Escrow structure must balance: (1) seller liquidity needs for creditor distributions; (2) buyer protection for investigation phase ($3-8M), interim measures ($10-35M), and final remedy implementation ($40-200M); and (3) risk-adjusted probability weighting based on facility size and violation history.

#### Rule

**Contractual Escrow Authorization in Bankruptcy**: Asset purchase agreements approved under § 363(b) may include escrow provisions allocating sale proceeds to secure seller environmental indemnification obligations. 11 U.S.C. § 363(b) (authorizing sale of property outside ordinary course "after notice and a hearing"). Escrow agreements are enforceable post-closing contract terms not discharged by plan confirmation. *In re Pacific Lumber Co.*, 584 F.3d 229, 252 (5th Cir. 2009).

**Indemnification Survival Period**: Environmental representations and warranties typically survive closing for 7-10 years (matching CERCLA statute of limitations under 42 U.S.C. § 9613(g)(2) of 3 years after discovery) or 10-year absolute bar. Bankruptcy sale orders may limit survival to 3-5 years under "as is, where is" provisions, requiring buyer to negotiate extended survival explicitly.

**Release Conditions and Claims Process**: Escrow agreements must specify: (1) milestone-based release schedule; (2) conditions precedent for each release tranche; (3) claims procedures (notice, documentation, dispute resolution); and (4) residual release timing. Standard market terms based on ABA Model Asset Purchase Agreement (2010).

#### Explanation

**Escrow Sizing Methodology - Facility Risk Categories**:

| Facility Category | Escrow Range | Basis | Release Period | Precedent Transaction |
|-------------------|--------------|-------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Small Metal Fabrication** (<5 acres, no CERCLA PRP status) | $3-8M | 100% of total lifecycle remediation cost ($3-8M per Section IV.C) | 5-7 years | Typical M&A practice |
| **Medium Manufacturing** (5-50 acres, limited violations) | $10-35M | 100% of P75 remediation scenario ($10-35M range) | 7-10 years | Bethlehem Steel model |
| **Large Steel/Coke Plant** (>50 acres, documented violations, CERCLA PRP) | $40-200M | 50-75% of gross exposure (balance via insurance/indemnity cap) | 10 years + tail | LTV Steel, Allegheny Ludlum |

**Rationale for 50-100% Escrow Coverage**:
- **100% coverage** appropriate for small/medium facilities where Phase II ESA provides reasonable certainty regarding contamination extent
- **50-75% coverage** for large facilities where: (1) contamination extent uncertain despite Phase II ESA; (2) total exposure exceeds buyer's risk tolerance; (3) seller insolvency post-closing limits recourse to indemnity cap; and (4) environmental insurance (pollution legal liability policy) provides supplemental coverage for excess exposure

**Phased Release Structure - Three-Stage Approach**:

**Stage 1: Investigation Phase Completion (Months 0-18)**
- **Release Amount**: 15-20% of escrow ($12-27M for large facility)
- **Conditions Precedent**:
  - Buyer completes Phase II Environmental Site Assessment at all facilities
  - Remedial investigation reports submitted to EPA and Pennsylvania DEP
  - No administrative orders issued requiring emergency response
  - Investigation results confirm contamination within ±25% of seller's Phase II estimates
  - No pending environmental claims exceeding $1M individually or $5M aggregate
- **Rationale**: Early release incentivizes seller cooperation with buyer due diligence; 80-85% retained escrow adequate to fund interim measures and final remedy if contamination confirmed

**Stage 2: Interim Measures Completion (Months 18-36)**
- **Release Amount**: 25-30% of escrow ($21-60M for large facility)
- **Conditions Precedent**:
  - Buyer completes all interim removal actions (soil excavation, free product recovery, groundwater extraction)
  - Monitoring systems operational (quarterly groundwater sampling, vapor intrusion monitoring)
  - EPA/DEP acceptance of interim measures (no enforcement actions)
  - Buyer submits feasibility study evaluating final remedy alternatives
  - Aggregate expenditures for investigation + interim measures within 150% of seller's estimates
- **Rationale**: Interim measures typically represent 30-40% of total remediation costs; 50-60% escrow release provides seller liquidity while retaining coverage for final remedy (most expensive phase)

**Stage 3: Final Remedy Selection (Months 36-60)**
- **Release Amount**: 30-35% of escrow ($26-70M for large facility)
- **Conditions Precedent**:
  - EPA issues Record of Decision (ROD) selecting final remedy OR Pennsylvania DEP approves Act 2 cleanup plan
  - Selected remedy cost estimate within 200% of seller's original projections (recognizing remediation cost uncertainty)
  - No environmental claims asserted exceeding escrow cap
  - Seller reimburses buyer for all indemnifiable environmental losses through Month 60
- **Rationale**: Final remedy selection reduces future uncertainty; remaining 15-20% escrow retained for long-term monitoring and contingent claims

**Stage 4: Residual Release (Months 60-120)**
- **Release Amount**: 15-20% of escrow (final tranche)
- **Conditions Precedent**:
  - All pending environmental claims resolved
  - Seller satisfies all indemnification obligations
  - Long-term monitoring confirms contaminant concentrations declining to Act 2 standards
  - **OR** 10-year anniversary from closing (time-based automatic release), whichever occurs first
- **Rationale**: Time-based release prevents indefinite escrow retention; 10-year period matches CERCLA discovery statute of limitations (3 years after discovery + 7-year outside limit)
