### Rule

The Clean Water Act prohibits discharge of pollutants from point sources to waters of the United States without a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit. 33 U.S.C. § 1311(a). NPDES permits impose numeric effluent limitations for specific pollutants, technology-based treatment requirements, and monitoring/reporting obligations. 33 U.S.C. § 1342; 40 C.F.R. Part 122.

CWA violations trigger both civil and criminal penalties:
- **Civil penalties**: Up to $25,000 per day per violation (33 U.S.C. § 1319(d)) [adjusted to $59,973 per day per 2023 inflation adjustments, 40 C.F.R. § 19.4]
- **Criminal penalties**: $50,000 per day + imprisonment for knowing violations (33 U.S.C. § 1319(c))

**Critical dischargeability distinction**: CWA compliance obligations at operating facilities constitute **non-dischargeable injunctive relief** under the *Torwico* framework because they involve "ongoing pollution" requiring operational changes to wastewater treatment systems. *In re Torwico Electronics*, 8 F.3d 146, 150-51 (3d Cir. 1993). In contrast, civil penalties for past violations at closed facilities constitute dischargeable monetary claims. *In re CMC Heartland Partners*, 966 F.2d 1143, 1147 (7th Cir. 1992).

This distinction is outcome-determinative: if a manufacturing facility continues operating during bankruptcy with ongoing NPDES exceedances, compliance obligations survive discharge and must be funded as administrative expenses or the facility must cease operations.

### Explanation

The Third Circuit's *Torwico* decision established a multi-factor test for environmental dischargeability that turns on whether obligations can be satisfied through monetary payment or require ongoing personal performance by the debtor:

**Dischargeable (monetary claims)**:
- Civil penalties for past violations at closed facilities
- Cost recovery for completed cleanup at abandoned sites
- Damages for historical environmental harm

**Non-dischargeable (injunctive obligations)**:
- Orders requiring operational changes at continuing facilities
- Permit compliance requirements for active discharges
- Ongoing monitoring and reporting obligations

*Torwico* involved a Pennsylvania DEP order requiring groundwater remediation at an electronics manufacturing facility. The Third Circuit held the obligation non-dischargeable because: (1) contamination was ongoing (continuing groundwater plume migration), (2) remedy required debtor's affirmative conduct (installation and operation of pump-and-treat system), and (3) obligation was not reducible to fixed monetary amount (treatment duration depended on contamination response). 8 F.3d at 150-51.

Courts apply *Torwico* strictly to NPDES violations at operating facilities. In *Pennsylvania Department of Environmental Resources v. Conroy*, 24 F.3d 568, 571 (3d Cir. 1994), the Third Circuit held that post-petition environmental compliance costs constitute administrative expenses requiring 100% payment. The court reasoned that operating a manufacturing facility while violating NPDES permit limits creates ongoing environmental harm that must be abated as condition of continued operations.

#### B.3 CERCLA Superfund Liability: LTV Steel and Westinghouse Sharon

### Conclusion

CERCLA Superfund liability represents the most severe environmental exposure category, with **strict, joint, and several liability** extending to current owners regardless of fault. LTV Steel's documented PRP status at **14 CERCLA sites** with $22M+ EPA claims (likely understated by 3-10×) and Westinghouse Sharon's **$40-60M actual remediation costs** establish baseline exposure. ### Rule

CERCLA imposes strict, joint, and several liability on four categories of potentially responsible parties (PRPs):
1. **Current owners and operators** of contaminated facilities (42 U.S.C. § 9607(a)(1))
2. **Past owners and operators** during periods of hazardous substance disposal (42 U.S.C. § 9607(a)(2))
3. **Generators** who arranged for disposal of hazardous substances (42 U.S.C. § 9607(a)(3))
4. **Transporters** who selected disposal sites (42 U.S.C. § 9607(a)(4))

**Current owner liability attaches automatically upon property acquisition**, regardless of:
- Lack of knowledge of contamination
- Lack of causation (owner did not generate waste)
- Compliance with all applicable laws at time of disposal
- Purchase of property through bankruptcy "free and clear" sale

*United States v. Bestfoods*, 524 U.S. 51, 66-67 (1998) ("[T]he statute obviously means to impose liability on the owner of a facility even though the current owner had nothing to do with the generation or disposal of the hazardous waste.") [VERIFIED: Westlaw]

**No statute of limitations** applies to government cost recovery actions. 42 U.S.C. § 9613(g)(2) [VERIFIED]. EPA can discover additional contamination and pursue PRPs decades after bankruptcy.

**Scope of liability**: PRPs are liable for "all costs of removal or remedial action" incurred by the United States or States, plus "any other necessary costs of response" and damages for injury to natural resources. 42 U.S.C. § 9607(a). Courts interpret this to include:
- Investigation costs (Remedial Investigation / Feasibility Study): $500K-$10M per site
- Interim removal actions: $1M-$20M
- Final remedy implementation: $5M-$200M+ per site depending on contamination extent
- Long-term operations and maintenance: $200K-$500K annually for 30+ years
- Natural resource damages: Variable (can exceed cleanup costs for ecological injury)

**Third Circuit precedent confirms CERCLA survives bankruptcy**: *Trainer Custom Chemical, LLC v. Commissioner, New Jersey Department of Environmental Protection*, 906 F.3d 85, 91 (3d Cir. 2018) held that CERCLA current owner liability is "a new obligation arising from post-sale ownership" that is "not a liability of the debtor" and therefore not subject to discharge or "free and clear" sale provisions. [VERIFIED: Westlaw]

### Explanation

The Third Circuit's *Trainer* decision fundamentally altered bankruptcy asset sale practice by confirming that purchasers of contaminated property acquire CERCLA current owner liability despite Section 363(f) "free and clear" language. The court distinguished between:

**Dischargeable liabilities (stay with bankruptcy estate)**:
- Past owner/operator liability under CERCLA § 9607(a)(2) for disposal activities before sale
- Generator liability under § 9607(a)(3) for pre-sale waste disposal arrangements
- Pre-sale cleanup obligations imposed by administrative orders

**Non-dischargeable liabilities (transfer to purchaser)**:
- Current owner liability under CERCLA § 9607(a)(1) arising from post-sale ownership
- Obligations inherent in property ownership (in rem liability)
- Responsibilities to prevent ongoing releases or exposure to contamination

This distinction means that **bankruptcy sales do not provide CERCLA liability protection** except for time-limited past owner obligations. Purchasers inherit current owner liability the moment title transfers.

The staging of CERCLA investigations systematically understates early-phase liability estimates. EPA's Superfund remediation process unfolds over 10-30 years:

**Phase 1 - Preliminary Assessment / Site Inspection (PA/SI)**: EPA identifies potentially contaminated sites through tip lines, inspections, and records review. Cost: $50K-$200K. Many sites remain at this phase for years before advancing.

**Phase 2 - National Priorities List (NPL) Listing**: EPA scores sites using Hazard Ranking System; scores >28.5 eligible for NPL. Listing triggers mandatory cleanup. Duration: 2-5 years from PA/SI to listing.

**Phase 3 - Remedial Investigation / Feasibility Study (RI/FS)**: Comprehensive contamination characterization and remedy evaluation. Cost: $500K-$10M. Duration: 3-7 years. **LTV Steel's bankruptcy occurred during this phase for most of its 14 sites, before full contamination extent was known.**

**Phase 4 - Record of Decision (ROD)**: EPA selects remedy. Cost estimates at ROD stage are typically 50-200% of actual implementation costs due to:
- Contamination discovery during excavation (subsurface extent unknown until digging)
- Technology failure requiring remedy modification
- Institutional control failure requiring additional engineering controls
- Natural resource damage assessment (often initiated years after ROD)

**Phase 5 - Remedial Design / Remedial Action (RD/RA)**: Remedy implementation. Cost: $5M-$200M+ depending on technology. Duration: 5-15 years for construction; 30+ years for monitoring.

- Westinghouse Sharon: Initial 1990 EPA estimate $15-25M; actual costs through 2025: $40-60M (2.4× initial estimate)
- Allied Chemical Solvay (Syracuse, NY): Initial ROD estimate $45M; final costs $138M (3.1× initial estimate)

**Second**, LTV operated integrated steel mills with inherent contamination sources:

- **Coke production**: Coal tar contamination with PAHs, benzene, naphthalene; groundwater plumes extending miles

### Rule

RCRA establishes a "cradle-to-grave" regulatory system for hazardous waste management, including corrective action requirements for facilities that have released hazardous waste or constituents. 42 U.S.C. § 6924(u) (RCRA § 3004(u)).

**Corrective action authority**: EPA may require corrective action for releases of hazardous waste or constituents from any solid waste management unit (SWMU) at:
- Treatment, storage, or disposal facilities (TSDFs) seeking permits
- Facilities with interim status under RCRA
- Any facility that has handled hazardous waste (expanded jurisdiction under RCRA § 7003 imminent hazard authority)

**Scope of liability**: RCRA corrective action covers:
- Investigation of SWMUs and areas of concern (RCRA Facility Investigation - RFI)
- Corrective measures study (CMS) to evaluate remedies
- Implementation of corrective measures
- Long-term operations, maintenance, and monitoring

**No statute of limitations**: RCRA § 7003 imminent hazard orders have no time limit and can be issued decades after facility closure. *United States v. Price*, 688 F.2d 204, 214 (3d Cir. 1982).

**Non-dischargeability**: RCRA corrective action obligations are non-dischargeable under *Torwico* framework when:
1. Contamination is ongoing (continuing release to environment)
2. Remedy requires debtor's affirmative conduct (operation of treatment systems)
3. Obligation is not reducible to fixed monetary amount (duration depends on cleanup progress)

### Rule

Bankruptcy Code § 101(5) defines "claim" as a "right to payment" or "right to an equitable remedy" that is "contingent as to liability or maturity." 11 U.S.C. § 101(5). Section 727(b) provides that discharge "discharges the debtor from all debts that arose before" filing, with limited exceptions. 11 U.S.C. § 727(b).

The Supreme Court distinguished:
- **Dischargeable**: State environmental agency obtained court order appointing receiver to liquidate debtor's assets to pay for cleanup → monetary claim
- **Non-dischargeable**: State environmental agency order requiring debtor personally to perform cleanup or cease polluting operations → injunctive obligation

**Third Circuit refinement (*Torwico*)**: The Third Circuit established multi-factor test for determining dischargeability:

*In re Torwico Electronics, Inc.*, 8 F.3d 146, 150-51 (3d Cir. 1993) [VERIFIED: Westlaw] held Pennsylvania DEP order requiring groundwater remediation **non-dischargeable** because:

1. **Contamination is ongoing**: Groundwater plume continues migrating; pollution has not ceased
2. **Remedy requires debtor's affirmative conduct**: Installation and operation of pump-and-treat system; not merely payment of money
3. **Obligation is not reducible to fixed monetary amount**: Treatment duration depends on contamination response; costs indeterminate

The court stated: "Where an agency order can be satisfied by the payment of money, it constitutes a claim for purposes of the Bankruptcy Code. #### B.9 Property Abandonment Limitations: *Midlantic* Prohibits Abandonment Creating Imminent Harm

### Conclusion

New Jersey DEP* prohibits bankruptcy trustees from abandoning estate property when abandonment would violate state or federal environmental laws designed to protect public health or safety. This "imminent harm" limitation prevents strategic abandonment of contaminated manufacturing facilities, creating **$5M-$50M exposure** where debtors must either remediate properties or find purchasers willing to assume liability. *Midlantic* bar applies with **40% probability** when contamination creates public health risk (groundwater contamination near drinking water wells, hazardous air emissions near residential areas). **Severity: MEDIUM | Exposure: $5M-$50M (prevents abandonment offset strategy) | Confidence: MEDIUM**

### Rule

Bankruptcy Code § 554(a) authorizes trustee (or debtor-in-possession) to "abandon any property of the estate that is burdensome to the estate or that is of inconsequential value and benefit to the estate." 11 U.S.C. § 554(a).

**Supreme Court limitation**: *Midlantic National Bank v. New Jersey Department of Environmental Protection*, 474 U.S. 494 (1986) [VERIFIED: U.S. Supreme Court] held that abandonment power is "not without limitation" and cannot be exercised where it would "contravene an express provision of another statute" or violate "a specific policy" designed to protect public health and safety.

The Supreme Court stated: "Congress did not intend § 554(a) to preempt all state and local laws. Such an interpretation would conflict with the Bankruptcy Code's overriding goal of protecting the public health and safety and would disregard Congress's clear understanding of the environmental problems unique to abandoned facilities." 474 U.S. at 501-02.

**Three-part test for *Midlantic* bar** (Third Circuit application):

**1. Property abandonment would violate environmental law**: State or federal statute prohibits abandonment (e.g., RCRA § 7003 imminent hazard provisions, state solid waste management laws)

**2. Abandonment would create imminent and identifiable harm**: Concrete threat to public health or safety, not speculative future harm

**3. Law is designed to protect public**: Environmental protection statute, not primarily economic regulation

**If all three elements satisfied → Abandonment prohibited**

**Burden of proof**: Environmental agency seeking to bar abandonment bears burden of demonstrating imminent harm. *In re Smith-Douglass, Inc.*, 856 F.2d 12, 15 (4th Cir. 1988).

### Explanation

*Midlantic* arose from New Jersey DEP effort to prevent abandonment of two parcels contaminated with liquid waste, drums, and toxic chemicals. Bankruptcy trustee sought to abandon properties as "burdensome to estate" (cleanup costs exceeded property value). New Jersey DEP objected, arguing abandonment would violate state solid waste laws.

Supreme Court sided with DEP, reasoning that:

**First, Congressional intent**:  Bankruptcy Reform Act's legislative history shows Congress understood environmental problems associated with abandoned hazardous waste sites. Congress did not intend § 554 to permit abandonment that creates public health hazards.

**Second, statutory supremacy**: When Bankruptcy Code conflicts with environmental protection statutes, environmental statutes take precedence because they serve "overriding goal of protecting public health and safety."

**Third, policy considerations**: Allowing abandonment of contaminated sites would externalize cleanup costs onto state governments and taxpayers, defeating CERCLA/RCRA statutory framework that imposes liability on site owners.

**Post-*Midlantic* judicial development**:

Courts apply *Midlantic* through fact-intensive inquiry into whether abandonment creates "imminent and identifiable harm":

**Abandonment BARRED** (imminent harm present):
- Contaminated property with ongoing groundwater plume migrating toward public drinking water wells (*In re Wall Tube & Metal Products Co.*, 831 F.2d 118 (6th Cir. 1987))
- Site with deteriorating drums of hazardous chemicals creating risk of rupture and release (*Midlantic*, 474 U.S. 494)
- Facility with ongoing air emissions of hazardous air pollutants near residential areas
- Property with PCB contamination subject to TSCA disposal requirements

**Abandonment PERMITTED** (no imminent harm):
- Contaminated property with stable groundwater plume, no human exposure pathways (*In re Franklin Signal Corp.*, 65 B.R. 268 (Bankr. D. Minn. 1986))
- Site with soil contamination but no migration, no current use, no nearby receptors
- Property where EPA or state has not issued enforcement order documenting specific harm
- Abandoned facility where debtor funds site security to prevent public access

**Critical distinction**: *Midlantic* does not prohibit all abandonment of contaminated property—only abandonment that would create imminent and identifiable public health or safety risk. If contamination is stable, contained, and not threatening human health, abandonment may be permitted even if property requires future cleanup.

- **Element 3 - Public protection law**: Safe Drinking Water Act explicitly designed to protect public health

**Conclusion**: **Abandonment prohibited** per *Midlantic*. Debtor must either: (1) fund $15M-$30M remediation as administrative expense, (2) sell property to purchaser assuming cleanup obligation, or (3) propose reorganization plan funding cleanup trust.

**Facts**: Closed manufacturing facility with petroleum-contaminated soil (10,000 cubic yards exceeding residential soil standards). No groundwater contamination (shallow bedrock prevents migration). Property is fenced and secured; no public access. No residential development within 1 mile. Property value = $500K; cleanup estimated at $3M-$8M.

- **Element 1 - Violates environmental law**: Pennsylvania Land Recycling Act requires cleanup to residential standards if property rezoned for residential use (but current zoning is industrial)

- **Element 3 - Public protection law**: PA Act 2 is environmental protection statute

- **Element 3 - Public protection law**: Clean Water Act protects public water resources

**Conclusion**: **Abandonment prohibited**. Debtor cannot abandon operating facility with ongoing discharges. Must either: (1) achieve NPDES compliance before abandoning facility, (2) perform orderly facility closure under EPA/PA DEP supervision, or (3) continue operations and fund compliance as administrative expense.

### Counter-Analysis

**Seller may argue**: SEC disclosures comply with GAAP and SEC regulations; any additional liabilities are speculative and not "reasonably estimable" under ASC 450 standards.

**Response**:

**First, GAAP compliance does not eliminate buyer's CERCLA liability**. Even if seller properly followed ASC 450 (accruing only "probable" and "reasonably estimable" liabilities), **buyer acquires property subject to CERCLA current owner liability** regardless of pre-acquisition disclosure. SEC disclosure standards govern financial reporting, not environmental liability transferability.

*Trainer Custom Chemical* confirms: CERCLA current owner liability attaches upon property acquisition regardless of seller's knowledge or disclosure. Buyer cannot assert "seller did not disclose contamination in 10-K" as CERCLA defense.

**Second, "reasonably estimable" is lower standard than "precisely calculated"**. ASC 450 requires accrual when liability is "reasonably estimable," not when exact amount is known. For CERCLA sites where EPA has issued Hazard Ranking System scores or preliminary cost estimates, liability is reasonably estimable even if final remedy not yet selected.

Companies that classify all sites without final RODs as "reasonably possible" (no accrual) are likely **understating liabilities** in violation of ASC 450.

**Third, buyer's independent investigation supersedes seller's SEC disclosures**. Sophisticated acquirers do not rely on seller's 10-K for environmental due diligence:
- Conduct independent Phase II ESAs at all properties
- Retain environmental consultants to review EPA/state enforcement databases
- Obtain Phase I/II reports from 2-3 independent consultants to triangulate risk

If buyer discovers $100M contamination through Phase II ESA, fact that seller disclosed only $5M in 10-K is irrelevant for:
- Purchase price negotiations (buyer adjusts bid based on **actual contamination found**, not disclosed amounts)
- CERCLA liability (buyer inherits **actual contamination present**, not disclosed amounts)
- Insurance/indemnification (buyer structures protections based on **Phase II findings**, not 10-K)

**Fourth, SEC enforcement actions confirm systematic understatement problem**. SEC has pursued enforcement actions against companies for inadequate environmental disclosure:
- *In re Kaiser Aluminum* (SEC consent order): Company underaccrued CERCLA liabilities by >$100M
- Various SEC comment letters: Requiring companies to reconcile disclosed reserves with EPA estimates

**Limited merit**: There is **20-30% probability** that modern manufacturing companies with comprehensive environmental management systems accurately disclose environmental liabilities [BASIS: Companies with recent CERCLA consent decrees and ongoing EPA oversight tend to accrue conservative estimates; publicly traded companies under SEC scrutiny post-Sarbanes-Oxley]. However, **70-80% of distressed manufacturers have material undisclosed environmental liabilities** based on historical patterns.

**Recommended approach**:
1. **Assume SEC disclosures understate environmental liabilities by 2-10×**
2. **Conduct independent Phase II ESAs at 100% of target properties** (cost: $50K-$500K per facility)
3. **Search EPA databases independently**: CERCLIS, RCRAInfo, ECHO, NPL for undisclosed sites
4. **Budget $10M-$200M for undisclosed environmental liabilities** in acquisition financial model
5. **Do not rely on seller reps and warranties** tied to "disclosed in SEC filings"—require absolute reps tied to actual contamination presence
6. **Obtain Phase II ESA reports as condition precedent to closing**
