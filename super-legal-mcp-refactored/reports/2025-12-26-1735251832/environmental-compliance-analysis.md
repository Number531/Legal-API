Courts examine continuity of ownership, management, operations, assets, and whether seller ceases operations post-closing. If ASBC acquisition structured as asset purchase but exhibits de facto merger characteristics (ABP retains ASBC employees, operates same facilities, ASBC dissolves), buyer assumes **all pre-existing environmental liabilities** including CERCLA cleanup costs.

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. Craft Beverage Environmental Profile

#### 1. Brewery Wastewater Characteristics
Craft brewery operations generate high-strength wastewater characterized by:
- **Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD):** 1,000-5,000 mg/L (vs. domestic sewage ~200 mg/L)
- **Total Suspended Solids (TSS):** 500-2,000 mg/L
- **pH variability:** Cleaning chemicals (caustic/acid) create pH swings
- **Nutrients:** High nitrogen and phosphorus from yeast and grain

**Sources:**
- Brewing process water (mash tun, lauter tun rinses)
- Fermenter and bright tank cleaning
- Bottle/keg washing operations
- Floor drains and general facility washdown
- Cooling tower blowdown

#### 2. Distillery Wastewater Characteristics
Distillery operations generate even higher strength wastewater:
- **BOD:** 5,000-50,000 mg/L (stillage/dunder from distillation)
- **TSS:** 2,000-10,000 mg/L
- **High solids content:** Suspended grain particles
- **Temperature:** Elevated from distillation processes

#### 3. Typical Discharge Scenarios
Craft beverage facilities typically discharge via:
- **Direct discharge to surface water:** Requires NPDES permit (rare for craft scale)
- **Discharge to POTW (Publicly Owned Treatment Works):** Most common; requires industrial pretreatment permit
- **Land application:** Some facilities use spray irrigation (requires state permit)
- **Hauling to wastewater treatment:** Off-site disposal for high-strength waste

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. CLEAN WATER ACT REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

#### 1. Statutory Basis

**Clean Water Act ¬ß 301 Prohibition (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 1311):**
- Prohibits discharge of any pollutant from a point source to navigable waters except as authorized by NPDES permit
- **Point source:** Any discernible, confined conveyance (pipes, ditches, channels)
- **Navigable waters:** Waters of the United States (WOTUS), broadly defined
- **Pollutant:** Includes organic waste, pH, temperature, suspended solids

**CWA ¬ß 307 Toxic and Pretreatment Standards (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 1317):**
- Authorizes EPA to establish pretreatment standards for industrial users discharging to POTWs
- Prevents "pass-through" (pollutants pass through POTW untreated)
- Prevents "interference" (pollutants interfere with POTW treatment processes)

#### 2. Regulatory Framework

**40 C.F.R. Part 122 - NPDES Permit Program:**
- Establishes permit application requirements
- Defines permit conditions and monitoring requirements
- State authorization: EPA delegates NPDES authority to states with approved programs

**40 C.F.R. Part 403 - General Pretreatment Regulations:**
- Applies to industrial users discharging to POTWs
- Requires local limits based on POTW capacity
- Mandates Industrial User Permits for Significant Industrial Users (SIUs)
- SIU definition: >25,000 gallons/day or >5% of POTW flow/loading

**Industry-Specific Pretreatment Standards:**
- 40 C.F.R. Part 439 - Pharmaceutical Manufacturing (not directly applicable)
- No specific federal pretreatment categorical standards for breweries/distilleries
- Subject to general prohibitions and local POTW limits

#### 3. Penalty Provisions

**CWA ¬ß 309 Enforcement (33 U.S.C. ¬ß 1319):**
- **Civil penalties:** Up to $59,950 per day per violation (2024 inflation-adjusted)
- **Criminal penalties:** Knowing violations up to $50,000/day and 3 years imprisonment
- **Negligent violations:** Up to $25,000/day and 1 year imprisonment

**Administrative Orders:**
- Compliance orders (no penalties)
- Administrative penalty orders (up to $25,000 per violation, $125,000 total)

### F. CERCLA SUCCESSOR LIABILITY

#### 1. Statutory Framework - 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(a)

CERCLA ¬ß 107(a) imposes **strict, joint, and several liability** on:‚Å∂¬≥
- **Current owners and operators** of contaminated facilities
- **Past owners and operators** at time of disposal
- **Arrangers** who arranged for disposal or treatment
- **Transporters** who selected disposal sites

**Critical Issue:** Liability attaches to **"current owner"** regardless of whether contamination occurred under current ownership or was disclosed at time of acquisition.

#### 2. Asset Purchase vs. Stock Purchase

**Stock Purchase:**
- Acquiring entity becomes owner of target corporation
- Corporation remains liable for pre-existing environmental contamination
- **Buyer clearly assumes CERCLA liability** as successor owner

**Asset Purchase:**
- General rule: Asset purchaser does NOT obtain seller's liabilities unless expressly assumed‚Å∂‚Å¥
- **EXCEPTION:** Successor liability attaches under common law doctrines:‚Å∂‚Åµ
  - **Express or implied assumption** of liabilities
  - **De facto merger** (transaction structured as asset sale but effectively a merger)
  - **Mere continuation** (buyer is continuation of seller under different name)
  - **Fraudulent transfer** (sale to avoid creditors)

#### 3. De Facto Merger Doctrine

Courts find de facto merger when:‚Å∂‚Å∂
- **Continuity of ownership:** Seller's shareholders become shareholders of buyer
- **Continuity of management:** Same officers, directors, personnel
- **Continuity of operations:** Same business, assets, location
- **Seller ceases operations** and liquidates
- **Buyer assumes obligations** necessary to continue business

Once court determines asset sale was in substance a merger, **buyer assumes ALL liabilities of seller**, including CERCLA environmental liabilities.‚Å∂‚Å∑

#### 4. Mere Continuation Doctrine

"Mere continuation" theory emphasizes **identity of officers, directors, and stock** between selling and purchasing corporations.‚Å∂‚Å∏ Focus is on continuity of corporate entity rather than business operations.

#### 5. Application to ASBC Acquisition

**Transaction Structure:** User query indicates acquisition by American Beverage Partners LLC. Assuming **equity purchase** (membership interests in Delaware LLC), ASBC entity remains liable for pre-existing contamination.

**Risk if Asset Purchase:**

If transaction structured as asset purchase:
- **De facto merger risk:** HIGH if ABP retains ASBC management, employees, and operations at same facilities
- **Mere continuation risk:** MEDIUM-HIGH if ASBC entity is dissolved post-closing
- **Successor liability exposure:** Potentially unlimited cleanup costs at contaminated sites

**Relevant Case Law:**

Courts have applied successor liability doctrines in CERCLA cases to hold asset purchasers liable for cleanup costs at hazardous waste sites.‚Å∂‚Åπ Federal circuit courts are split on whether federal or state common law applies to determine successor liability scope.‚Å∑‚Å∞

#### 6. Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser (BFPP) Defense

**CERCLA ¬ß 107(r) (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 9607(r)):**

BFPP defense (added by Brownfields Amendments 2002) protects purchasers from CERCLA liability if:‚Å∑¬π
1. **All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI)** conducted before acquisition (40 CFR Part 312 - Phase I ESA)
2. **No disposal after acquisition**
3. **Appropriate care** exercised with respect to hazardous substances
4. **Cooperation** with authorities and information requests
5. **No affiliation** with potentially responsible parties (PRPs)

**Critical Requirement:** Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ASTM E1527-21) must be completed within **1 year before acquisition** (180 days for components requiring site visit).‚Å∑¬≤

#### 7. Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs)
