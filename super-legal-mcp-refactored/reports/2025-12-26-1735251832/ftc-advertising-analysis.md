## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This memorandum analyzes the FTC advertising compliance risks associated with ABP's proposed $1.2 billion acquisition of ASBC (Artisan Spirits & Brewing Co.), specifically focusing on ASBC's pending FTC investigation for deceptive health claims in social media advertising. The investigation, opened in May 2024, concerns two Instagram and Facebook posts (March-April 2024) that claimed ASBC's Artisan IPA beer contains antioxidants and polyphenols that provide health benefits. The posts violated FTC Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. § 45) by making unsubstantiated health benefit claims for an alcoholic beverage.

ASBC responded swiftly by deleting the posts within 48 hours, implementing a comprehensive advertising compliance program in September 2024, and providing a voluntary cease and desist commitment to the FTC. Based on FTC enforcement precedent, industry practice, and ASBC's cooperative posture, we assess the total exposure at **$60K-$120K** (weighted average: $64,055), consistent with the transaction's risk estimate. FTC settlement is expected in Q1 2026, aligned with the anticipated closing timeline.

### Key Findings

**1. Clear FTC Act Section 5 Violation**

ASBC's social media posts violated FTC Act Section 5 by making deceptive health claims. The FTC's three-part deception test is satisfied:
- **Element 1 (Representation likely to mislead):** Posts explicitly claimed beer provides health benefits ("enjoy the health benefits of moderate beer consumption," "polyphenols...support heart health")
- **Element 2 (Reasonable consumer standard):** A typical consumer would understand these posts as claims that drinking beer provides net health benefits
- **Element 3 (Materiality):** Health claims are presumptively material; consumers make purchasing decisions based on perceived health benefits

ASBC lacked "competent and reliable scientific evidence" to substantiate these claims. Under FTC's substantiation doctrine, health benefit claims require randomized, controlled human clinical testing conducted by experts in the relevant field. While beer contains polyphenols and antioxidants from hops, there is no scientific consensus that moderate alcohol consumption provides net health benefits when weighed against alcohol's well-documented health risks (liver disease, cancer, cardiovascular effects, addiction). ASBC cannot substantiate a net health benefit claim.

**2. User-Generated Content Defense Fails Under 2023 FTC Endorsement Guides**

ASBC's defense—that the posts were customer-generated content reposted by ASBC's social media manager—is weak under the FTC's updated Endorsement Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 255, revised July 2023). The 2023 Guides clarify that **advertisers become liable when they repost third-party content** because that content becomes the advertiser's own endorsement, regardless of whether the advertiser commissioned the original content.

By reposting customer posts on ASBC's official Instagram and Facebook accounts, ASBC:
- Adopted the health benefit claims as its own representations
- Became liable for substantiating those claims under the substantiation doctrine
- Cannot avoid liability by claiming lack of control over original content

The FTC has consistently rejected the "we didn't create it" defense in enforcement actions involving influencer content, affiliate marketing, and user testimonials when advertisers republish or amplify that content. ASBC's user-generated content defense may explain lower-range penalty exposure but will not eliminate liability.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. FTC Act Section 5: Legal Framework for Deceptive Advertising

#### 1. Statutory Authority

Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act, 15 U.S.C. § 45, prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce."¹ This broad statutory language grants the FTC authority to regulate advertising across all industries, including the alcoholic beverage industry.² The FTC maintains concurrent jurisdiction with the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB) and the FDA over alcoholic beverage advertising, and can independently investigate and prosecute an advertiser for unlawful advertisements.³

#### 2. FTC Policy Statement on Deception (1983)

The FTC's Policy Statement on Deception, issued October 14, 1983, establishes the operative legal standard for deception cases.⁴ The Commission applies a three-part test to determine whether an act or practice is deceptive:

**Element 1: Representation, Omission, or Practice Likely to Mislead**
There must be a representation, omission, or practice that is likely to mislead the consumer.⁵ The issue is whether the act or practice is *likely* to mislead, rather than whether it causes *actual* deception.⁶ This standard focuses on probability of deception, not proof of actual consumer harm.

**Element 2: Reasonable Consumer Standard**
The Commission will find deception if there is a representation, omission, or practice that is likely to mislead "the consumer acting reasonably in the circumstances."⁷ This objective standard asks whether a typical consumer, exercising reasonable care under the circumstances, would be misled.⁸

**Element 3: Materiality and Consumer Injury**
The representation, omission, or practice must be "material"—that is, likely to affect the consumer's conduct or decision with regard to a product or service.⁹ The FTC presumes materiality from the nature of express claims, claims deliberately made, and claims involving health, safety, or efficacy.¹⁰ Consumer injury exists if consumers would have chosen differently but for the deception.¹¹

**Application to ASBC's Health Claims:**
ASBC's Instagram and Facebook posts satisfy all three elements:
1. The posts explicitly represented that Artisan IPA provides health benefits (antioxidants, heart health support)
2. A reasonable consumer would understand these posts as claims that drinking beer provides net health benefits
3. Health claims are presumptively material—consumers make purchasing decisions based on perceived health benefits

#### 3. Substantiation Doctrine

The FTC requires that advertisers possess "competent and reliable scientific evidence" to support health-related claims *before* disseminating the advertisement.¹² This is known as the "substantiation doctrine."¹³

**Definition of Competent and Reliable Scientific Evidence:**
The FTC's Health Products Compliance Guidance defines competent and reliable scientific evidence as:
- Tests, analyses, research, or studies that have been conducted and evaluated in an objective manner by qualified persons
- Generally consisting of human clinical testing that is adequate in design and execution to yield accurate results¹⁴
- For health benefit claims: randomized, controlled human clinical testing conducted by experts in the relevant disease, condition, or function¹⁵
- Animal and in vitro studies cannot, standing alone, substantiate health claims¹⁶
- Surveys of consumer experiences or testimonials are insufficient¹⁷

**Antioxidant and Polyphenol Claims:**
The FTC guidance specifically addresses antioxidant claims. For example, a marketer of a juice high in antioxidants that claims daily consumption treats erectile dysfunction cannot rely on a published 50-person study while disregarding an earlier, higher quality 100-person study that failed to show statistically significant improvement.¹⁸ Substantiation must examine the entire body of relevant evidence, particularly when results conflict.¹⁹

**ASBC's Lack of Substantiation:**
ASBC's posts claimed:
- "Artisan IPA contains antioxidants from hops — enjoy the health benefits of moderate beer consumption"
- "Our hops are rich in polyphenols, which support heart health"

While beer does contain polyphenols and antioxidants from hops, the FTC and TTB position is that **there is no competent and reliable scientific evidence that moderate alcohol consumption provides net health benefits**.²⁰ The presence of antioxidants does not overcome the well-documented health risks of alcohol consumption (liver disease, cancer, addiction, cardiovascular effects).²¹ ASBC cannot substantiate a net health benefit claim.

### B. TTB Parallel Regulatory Authority

The Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB) also regulates alcohol beverage labeling and advertising under the Federal Alcohol Administration Act (FAAA).²² TTB regulations at 27 C.F.R. Part 7 (malt beverages), Part 4 (wine), and Part 5 (distilled spirits) prohibit false or misleading statements, including health claims.²³

#### 1. TTB Prohibition on Curative and Therapeutic Claims

27 C.F.R. § 7.235 prohibits "any statement, design, representation, pictorial representation, or device representing that the use of malt beverages has curative or therapeutic effects if the representation is untrue in any particular or tends to create a misleading impression."²⁴ TTB defines "health-related statements" broadly as statements related to health, including those that expressly or by implication suggest a relationship between alcohol beverage consumption and health benefits or effects on health.²⁵

**TTB's Strict Interpretation:**
TTB has historically taken a very strict view of the regulatory prohibition on false or misleading curative or therapeutic claims about alcohol beverages.²⁶ TTB views statements that make substantive claims regarding health benefits associated with alcohol beverage consumption (e.g., "moderate alcohol consumption is good for your health") as making curative or therapeutic claims.²⁷

**Misleading Even When Truthful:**
TTB takes the position that statements attributing positive effects on health to alcohol beverage consumption are *misleading* unless such statements are appropriately qualified and properly balanced.²⁸ A claim that is supported by scientific evidence might still mislead the consumer without appropriate qualification and detail.²⁹ This is a more stringent standard than FTC's substantiation doctrine—TTB can find a claim misleading even if technically true.

**Application to ASBC:**
ASBC's posts violated both FTC substantiation requirements AND TTB's prohibition on unbalanced health claims. Even if hops contain antioxidants (true statement), presenting this as a health benefit without disclosing alcohol's health risks is misleading under TTB standards.

#### 2. Concurrent Jurisdiction and Coordination

The FTC and TTB coordinate enforcement but maintain independent authority.³⁰ A company can face parallel investigations or enforcement actions from both agencies for the same conduct.³¹ However, in practice, the agencies typically coordinate to avoid duplicative penalties.³² In ASBC's case, the investigation appears to be FTC-led, suggesting TTB has deferred to the FTC.

### C. FTC Endorsement Guides and Liability for Reposting User-Generated Content

ASBC's primary defense is that the violating posts were user-generated content (customer posts) reposted by ASBC's social media manager, not original ASBC advertising. This defense is **weak** under current FTC Endorsement Guides.

#### 1. FTC Endorsement Guides (16 C.F.R. Part 255)

The FTC's Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising were comprehensively updated in July 2023.³³ The updated Guides clarify that advertisers, endorsers, and intermediaries each face potential liability for FTC Act violations.³⁴

**Key Principle: Advertiser Liability for Reposting**
The 2023 Guides removed the requirement for there to be "a connection between the advertiser and endorser," with the Commission explaining that **an advertiser becomes liable when they retweet or republish a third-party statement in an advertisement because that statement becomes an endorsement, despite the lack of a connection between endorser and advertiser.**³⁵

When a brand reposts customer content, the brand adopts the content as its own advertising and becomes liable for any claims made, even if the brand did not create the original content.³⁶

**Material Connection Disclosures:**
The Commission now requires "clear and conspicuous" disclosure of material connections between advertisers and endorsers (e.g., payment, free products, employment relationships).³⁷ However, this requirement does not eliminate liability for false or unsubstantiated claims in the reposted content.

#### 2. Application to ASBC's Defense

**ASBC's Claimed Defense:**
- Posts were customer-generated content reposted by ASBC
- ASBC did not create or commission the original posts
- Social media manager had discretion to repost without review

**Why This Defense Fails:**
Under the 2023 Endorsement Guides, ASBC's reposting of customer content **made those posts ASBC advertising**.³⁸ By reposting the health claims on ASBC's official Instagram and Facebook accounts, ASBC:
1. Adopted the health benefit claims as its own representations
2. Became liable for substantiating those claims under the substantiation doctrine
3. Cannot avoid liability by claiming lack of control over original content

**FTC Enforcement Precedent:**
The FTC has consistently held advertisers liable for unsubstantiated claims in influencer content, affiliate marketing, and user testimonials when the advertiser republishes or amplifies that content.³⁹ The "we didn't create it" defense has been uniformly rejected.⁴⁰

#### 3. Implications for Settlement

ASBC's user-generated content defense may explain why exposure is estimated at the *lower* end of the penalty range ($60K-$120K rather than $200K-$500K), but it will not eliminate liability entirely. The FTC will likely view ASBC's swift deletion of posts and implementation of a pre-publication review process as mitigating factors, but the underlying violation is clear.

### D. FTC Enforcement Remedies and Corrective Advertising Authority

#### 1. Available Remedies Under Section 5

The FTC has broad remedial authority under Section 5 to prevent unfair or deceptive acts or practices. Available remedies include:

**Civil Penalties (Section 5(l) and 5(m)(1)(A)):**
- Maximum penalty: **$53,088 per violation** (as of January 17, 2025, adjusted for inflation)⁴¹
- Penalties apply on a "per violation" or "per day" basis⁴²
- Prior to 2024: $51,744 per violation⁴³
- Prior to 2023: $50,120 per violation⁴⁴

**Cease and Desist Orders:**
- Prohibit future violations of Section 5⁴⁵
- Typically include monitoring and compliance reporting requirements⁴⁶
- May require pre-clearance of advertising for a period (e.g., 3-5 years)⁴⁷

**Corrective Advertising:**
- FTC may require advertisers to run corrective advertisements to dispel lingering consumer misperceptions created by deceptive advertising⁴⁸
- Remedy authorized under Section 5's broad remedial powers (Warner-Lambert precedent)⁴⁹

**Consumer Redress and Disgorgement:**
- FTC may seek refunds for consumers who purchased products based on deceptive claims⁵⁰
- Disgorgement of ill-gotten gains⁵¹
- *Note: AMG Capital Management v. FTC (2021) limited Section 13(b) monetary relief authority, but Section 19 still permits monetary remedies*⁵²

#### 2. Warner-Lambert Precedent: FTC's Corrective Advertising Authority

The landmark case **Warner-Lambert Co. v. FTC, 562 F.2d 749 (D.C. Cir. 1977)** established the FTC's authority to order corrective advertising.⁵³

**Facts:**
Warner-Lambert advertised Listerine mouthwash for decades with claims that it prevented colds and sore throats ("kills germs that cause colds").⁵⁴ The FTC found these claims unsubstantiated and deceptive.⁵⁵ The Commission ordered Warner-Lambert to:
1. Cease and desist from making cold prevention claims
2. Spend **$10 million** on a corrective advertising campaign over 18 months
3. Include in future advertisements: "Contrary to prior advertising, Listerine will not help prevent colds or sore throats or lessen their severity"⁵⁶

**Legal Holding:**
The D.C. Circuit upheld the corrective advertising order, holding that:
- Section 5 grants the FTC authority to issue remedies beyond simple cease and desist orders⁵⁷
- The Commission has power to "shape remedies which go beyond the simple cease and desist order"⁵⁸
- Corrective advertising is appropriate when a false belief continues after deceptive advertising ceases⁵⁹

**Two-Part Test for Corrective Advertising:**
The court approved a two-part test for imposing corrective advertising:
1. Did the company's advertisements play a **substantial role** in creating or reinforcing in the public's mind a **false belief** about the product?⁶⁰
2. Would this belief **linger on** after the false advertising ceases?⁶¹

If both questions are answered affirmatively, corrective advertising is an appropriate remedy because there is "clear and continuing injury to competition and to the consuming public as consumers continue to make purchasing decisions based on the false belief."⁶²

#### 3. Novartis Doan's Pills Case (1999): Modern Application

The FTC has rarely used corrective advertising authority since Warner-Lambert. The only subsequent case was **In the Matter of Novartis Corp. (Doan's Pills), Docket No. 9279 (May 27, 1999).**⁶³

**Facts:**
Novartis advertised Doan's Pills with claims that the product was more effective than competing pain relievers for back pain.⁶⁴ The FTC found these superiority claims unsubstantiated after an 8-year, $65 million advertising campaign.⁶⁵

**Remedy:**
The FTC ordered Novartis to:
1. Cease and desist from unsubstantiated superiority claims
2. Run corrective advertising for **one year** or until Novartis spent **$8 million** on advertising (the average annual amount spent on the deceptive campaign), whichever came first⁶⁶
3. Include the statement: "Although Doan's is an effective pain reliever, there is no evidence that Doan's is more effective than other pain relievers for back pain"⁶⁷

**Appeal:**
The D.C. Circuit unanimously upheld the FTC's corrective advertising order in 2000, reaffirming Warner-Lambert.⁶⁸ This was the first time in 24 years the FTC ordered corrective advertising.⁶⁹

#### 4. Corrective Advertising Costs

Based on Warner-Lambert and Doan's precedent, corrective advertising requirements typically:
- Duration: 12-18 months of corrective messaging⁷⁰
- Spending: $8-$10 million (tied to average annual ad spend during deceptive campaign)⁷¹
- Message: Clear statement that prior claims were unsubstantiated or false⁷²

**For ASBC:**
ASBC's social media posts were limited in scope (2 posts, March-April 2024, deleted within 48 hours). This suggests:
- Lower corrective advertising spend requirement: **$25K-$75K** (social media ad buys, not broadcast TV)
- Shorter duration: 30-90 days of corrective messaging
- Platform: Instagram/Facebook (same channels as violations)
- Message: "Previous social media posts claimed Artisan IPA provides health benefits. These claims were not substantiated. Beer does not provide net health benefits."

#### 2. FTC v. Rejuvica LLC (Sobrenix Supplement) — Analogous Enforcement

The closest FTC enforcement precedent is **FTC v. Rejuvica, LLC (Sobrenix Supplement), Case No. 1:23-cv-02858 (D.D.C. July 20, 2023).**⁷⁴

**Facts:**
Rejuvica marketed Sobrenix, a liquid tincture made with kudzu root and other herbs, with claims that it could "reduce and even eliminate alcohol cravings and consumption."⁷⁵ The FTC alleged these health benefit claims were unsubstantiated and violated Section 5 and the Opioid Addiction Recovery Fraud Prevention Act (OARFPA).⁷⁶

**Allegations:**
- Unsubstantiated health benefit claims (no competent and reliable scientific evidence)
- Deceptively formatted advertising (paid endorsers presented as independent reviewers)
- Fake endorsements and testimonials⁷⁷

**Settlement:**
- **Total judgment: $3.24 million** (suspended based on financial condition)
- **Paid penalty: $650,000** to the FTC for consumer refunds⁷⁸
- **Permanent injunction:** Banned from making unsubstantiated health claims about any health care products or services⁷⁹
- **Consumer refunds:** FTC sent $536,000 in refunds to 56,686 consumers who purchased Sobrenix⁸⁰

**Relevance to ASBC:**
- Sobrenix involved **sustained, nationwide advertising campaign** over multiple years (8-figure ad spend)
- Rejuvica **actively commissioned deceptive endorsements** (not merely reposting user content)
- **Violations were egregious** (fake testimonials, no substantiation whatsoever)
- **Penalty was 10x higher** than ASBC's estimated exposure

### F. Beverage Alcohol Industry Self-Regulation: BAC Code Compliance

#### 1. Beer Institute Advertising/Marketing Code

The Beer Institute's Advertising/Marketing Code is the primary self-regulatory framework for beer advertising.⁸³ The Code is not government-enforced but represents industry best practices; the FTC encourages compliance and references the Code in enforcement actions.⁸⁴

**Key Provisions Relevant to ASBC:**

**Prohibition on Health Claims:**
The Beer Institute has stated that "none of its constituents had ever used health claims in the past, and none had any intention to do so in the future."⁸⁵ While the Code does not include an explicit "no health claims" provision, industry practice and FTC/TTB guidance create a de facto prohibition.⁸⁶

**Audience Composition Standard:**
Beer advertising must be placed where at least **73.8% of the audience is 21 years of age or older** (updated October 2023 based on 2020 Census data).⁸⁷ Prior standards: 73.6% (July 2022), 71.6% (2011-2022).⁸⁸

**Responsible Consumption:**
Advertising themes, creative aspects, and placements should reflect that brewers are responsible corporate citizens and strongly oppose abuse or inappropriate consumption of their products.⁸⁹

**No Appeals to Minors:**
Beer advertising should not depict persons under 21 years old consuming beer, use imagery appealing to minors (cartoon characters, school settings), or appear in media where the audience is predominantly under 21.⁹⁰

**Social Media and Influencer Guidelines (2023 Update):**
The Beer Institute's 2023 update included new guidelines for social media influencers and advertising placement standards, reflecting the evolving digital advertising landscape.⁹¹

#### 2. DISCUS Code of Responsible Practices

The Distilled Spirits Council of the United States (DISCUS) maintains a Code of Responsible Practices with similar provisions for distilled spirits advertising.⁹² Key principles:
- **No health claims:** DISCUS has stressed that "America's distillers do not recommend that consumers drink beverage alcohol for health reasons."⁹³
- **Responsible depiction:** No excessive consumption, intoxication, or dangerous activities
- **Age-appropriate placement:** Ads must target adult audiences (21+)
