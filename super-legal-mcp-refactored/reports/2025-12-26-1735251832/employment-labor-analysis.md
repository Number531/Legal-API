## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This memorandum analyzes employment and labor law issues arising from ABP's proposed $1.2 billion acquisition of Artisan Spirits & Brewing Company, LLC (ASBC). ASBC employs 1,850 workers across 5 production facilities and 18 taprooms in 5 states (Oregon, Colorado, North Carolina, Washington, California). The analysis addresses six critical areas: (1) EEOC sexual harassment claim exposure; (2) WARN Act compliance for potential post-closing restructuring; (3) responsible beverage service training requirements and dram shop liability mitigation; (4) successor employer liability; (5) non-compete enforceability and key employee retention risks; and (6) multi-state wage and hour compliance.

**Bottom Line:** All employment and labor risks are manageable and within normal parameters for a $1.2B craft beverage acquisition. Combined exposure of $645,000-$1,245,000 represents less than 0.2% of transaction value. No deal-blocking issues identified. Recommended purchase price adjustment: $150,000-$200,000 (primarily for EEOC Gonzalez settlement + contingency).

#### B. WARN Act Compliance Analysis - **ZERO CURRENT EXPOSURE; CONTINGENT FUTURE RISK**

**Current Status:** ASBC compliant (no mass layoffs or plant closings in past 90 days)

**Post-Closing Restructuring Risk:**

WARN Act requires **60 days' advance notice** for:
- **Plant closings:** Shutdown of single site affecting 50+ employees
- **Mass layoffs:** 50+ employees (if 33%+ of workforce) OR 500+ employees at single site

**ASBC's 5 Facilities Analysis:**

| Facility | Employees | Single Site Status | Closing Triggers WARN? | Penalty if No Notice |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Portland Brewery | 280 | Separate site | **YES** (280 > 50) | $3,360,000 |
| Denver Brewery | 160 | Separate site | **YES** (160 > 50) | $1,920,000 |
| Asheville Brewery | 135 | Separate site | **YES** (135 > 50) | $1,620,000 |
| Portland Distillery | 85 | Separate site | **YES** (85 > 50) | $1,020,000 |
| Hood River Meadery | 60 | Separate site | **YES** (60 > 50) | $720,000 |

**Key Determinations:**

1. **Separate Single Sites:** ASBC's 5 facilities are geographically distinct, serve different purposes (beer vs. spirits vs. mead), and do not share staff ‚Üí **5 separate sites** for WARN purposes

2. **Taproom Closures:** 18 taprooms (typically 10-20 employees each) are separate sites ‚Üí Closing 1-2 taprooms does **not** trigger WARN (below 50-employee threshold)

3. **State Mini-WARN Acts:** Oregon, Colorado, and North Carolina follow federal WARN requirements (no stricter state obligations) ‚Üí Compliance simplified

4. **90-Day Aggregation Rule:** If ASBC conducted layoffs in 90 days pre-closing AND ABP conducts layoffs in 90 days post-closing at same site, layoffs aggregate ‚Üí **Due diligence: Verify no recent layoffs**

**Successor Liability:** Equity purchase ‚Üí ABP inherits WARN obligations as successor employer. Any post-closing restructuring triggering WARN creates ABP liability.

**Restructuring Scenarios:**

*Scenario 1: Close Asheville Brewery (135 employees)*
- ‚úì WARN notice required (135 > 50 threshold)
- ‚úì 60-day notice to employees, NC dislocated worker unit, local government
- ‚úó Penalty if no notice: $1,620,000

*Scenario 2: Distributed Workforce Reduction (10% across all facilities = 185 employees)*
- ‚úó WARN notice NOT required (no single site has 50+ layoffs)
- ‚úì Layoffs distributed across separate sites

*Scenario 3: Taproom Consolidation (Close 6 taprooms, 85 employees total)*
- ‚úó WARN notice NOT required (each taproom <50 employees, separate sites)

**Best Practice Recommendations:**

*If ABP Plans Post-Closing Restructuring:*

**Option A (Delay Restructuring):**
- Wait **91+ days after closing** before mass layoffs
- Avoids 90-day aggregation with any ASBC pre-closing layoffs
- Provides "clean break" for WARN calculation

**Option B (Immediate Restructuring):**
- Issue WARN notice immediately after signing purchase agreement
- 60-day notice expires concurrent with closing
- Execute layoffs Day 1 post-closing
- **Risk:** Employee morale decline, key employee flight pre-closing

**Transaction Exposure:** Zero current exposure; $1.6M+ per facility if WARN violated post-closing (preventable with proper notice)

#### C. Responsible Beverage Service (RBS) Training & Dram Shop Liability - **LOW RISK, STRONG COMPLIANCE**

**Oregon OLCC Mandatory Training:**

*New Requirements (Effective March 31, 2025):*
- **No grace period** ‚Üí Servers must complete OLCC training + exam **before** serving alcohol
- House Bill 4138 eliminates prior 45-day grace period
- All 200+ Oregon taproom servers (8 locations) must hold active permits

*ASBC Compliance Status:*
- ‚úì All Oregon servers completed OLCC Alcohol Server Education
- ‚úì Training covers visible intoxication recognition, ORS 471.565 dram shop liability, service refusal
- ‚úì Permits valid 5 years (renewal tracking required)

**Colorado and North Carolina:**
- **Colorado:** Training not mandatory (voluntary Responsible Vendor program for liability protection)
- **North Carolina:** Training required for permit applicants (owners), recommended for servers

**Connection to Dram Shop Liability Mitigation:**

Per T4 Litigation Report (Smith v. ASBC dram shop case):
- ASBC's OLCC training compliance **strengthens defense** against negligent training claims
- Courts recognize state-mandated training as **strong evidence** of adequate training
- Insurance industry discounts liquor liability premiums **25%** for TIPS/OLCC-certified staff
- ASBC's $5M dram shop coverage likely obtained at favorable rates due to training compliance

**Negligent Training Defense:**
- **Plaintiff's theory:** ASBC training inadequate
- **ASBC's defense:** All staff completed Oregon-required OLCC training (state-mandated, covers visible intoxication)
- **Legal standard:** State-mandated training sufficient absent evidence of (1) failure to ensure completion, (2) policies contradicting training, or (3) pattern of over-service incidents
- **Assessment:** ASBC likely prevails on negligent training claim

#### D. Successor Liability Assessment - **MODERATE RISK PENDING DUE DILIGENCE**

**NLRB Successor Employer Doctrine:**

*Substantial Continuity Test (Burns Security Services):*
ABP becomes successor employer obligated to recognize/bargain with union if:
1. **Substantial continuity of business operations** (same facilities, products, customers) ‚Üí ‚úì YES
2. **Workforce continuity** (ABP hires predecessor employees as majority of post-closing workforce) ‚Üí ‚úì YES (1,850 employees expected to continue)

**Result:** ABP is **successor employer** for NLRA purposes

*Bargaining Obligations (If Unionized):*
- ABP must **recognize and bargain** with incumbent union
- ABP **not bound** by predecessor's CBA (unless voluntarily assumed)
- ABP may set initial terms but must bargain over changes
- **Successor bar:** Cannot withdraw recognition for 6 months (NLRB rule, subject to potential revision post-*Loper Bright*)

**Union Status Unknown:** User-provided facts do not disclose unionization. **Due diligence critical:**
- Determine if any ASBC facilities have union representation (breweries, distilleries likely candidates)
- If unionized, obtain copies of CBAs and assess bargaining obligations

**Title VII Claims Continuation:**

*Gonzalez EEOC Claim Survives Change of Ownership:*
- Equity purchase ‚Üí ASBC LLC remains liable (corporate entity unchanged)
- ABP as owner of ASBC inherits liability for pending EEOC investigation
- EEOC will continue investigating ASBC (now owned by ABP)
- ABP must cooperate with EEOC (documents, witnesses, conciliation)

**Mitigation:** Settle Gonzalez claim before closing (eliminates uncertainty) OR escrow $150,000-$200,000

**State Wage and Hour Claims:**

*General Principle:* Successor employers may be liable for predecessor's wage/hour violations if successor has **notice** of claims (varies by state)

*Project Bacchus:*

- No disclosed pending wage/hour claims or DOL investigations
- **Due diligence:** Review past 3 years of wage/hour complaints, misclassification audits, DOL investigations

**ERISA and Employee Benefit Plans:**

*Qualified Plan Successor Liability:*
- Equity purchase ‚Üí ABP automatically assumes sponsorship of ASBC's qualified retirement plans (401(k), pension)
- ABP must continue plan administration OR terminate and distribute assets

*Multiemployer Plan Withdrawal Liability (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 1381):*
- If ASBC participates in union-sponsored multiemployer pension plans, withdrawal triggers **proportionate share of unfunded benefits**
- Withdrawal liability can be **substantial** (millions for underfunded plans)
- **Due diligence:** Identify any multiemployer plans, obtain actuarial valuation

*Controlled Group Liability (ERISA ¬ß 4001(b)):*
- ABP acquires 100% ASBC ‚Üí Controlled group formed
- ABP and ASBC jointly liable for each other's benefit plan obligations
- **Risk:** If ABP has underfunded pension plans, ASBC becomes secondarily liable

**Transaction Impact:**

#### E. Non-Compete Enforceability & Key Employee Retention - **LIMITED ENFORCEABILITY, FLIGHT RISK**

**FTC Non-Compete Ban Rule Status:**

*Current Legal Status (December 2024):*
- FTC rule (April 2024) **not in effect** (blocked by federal courts)
- Texas District Court held rule "unlawful and set aside" (August 2024)
- FTC appealed (October 2024), pending appellate review
- **Practical impact:** FTC rule does NOT affect ASBC's non-competes ‚Üí **State law governs**

**State-by-State Enforceability Analysis:**

**Oregon (ORS 653.295 - As Amended by HB 2992):**

*Requirements (ALL must be met):*
1. **2-week advance notice** in written job offer
2. **Exempt employee** (salaried, exempt from overtime)
3. **Protectable interest** (trade secrets OR competitively sensitive confidential information)
4. **Salary threshold:** $116,427+ annually (2025, adjusted for CPI)
5. **Duration limit:** 18 months maximum
6. **30-day termination notice:** Signed copy provided within 30 days (failure = voidable)

*Application to ASBC Key Employees:*

| Position | Salary Estimate | Exempt? | Protectable Interest? | Enforceable? |
|----------|----------------|---------|----------------------|--------------|
| Brewmaster / Head Brewer | $80K-$110K | Likely NO (production) | YES (recipes) | **VOID** (below threshold) |
| Master Distiller | $120K-$150K | YES (manager) | YES (bourbon formula) | **VALID** (if procedural compliance) |
| VP Sales / Sales Executives | $130K-$180K | YES (executive) | YES (distributor networks) | **VALID** (if procedural compliance) |
| Taproom Servers | $35K-$50K | NO (hourly) | NO | **VOID** (multiple failures) |

**Colorado (HB 22-1317, Effective August 2022):**

*Requirements:*
1. **Highly compensated:** $127,091+ annually (2025 threshold)
2. **Protects trade secrets only** (narrower than Oregon)
3. **No broader than reasonably necessary** to protect trade secrets

*Application:*
- Most brewmasters below $127,091 threshold ‚Üí **VOID**
- Non-solicitation agreements: Lower threshold ($76,254.60) ‚Üí May be enforceable for some employees

**North Carolina (Common Law):**

*Requirements:*
1. **Reasonable scope** (geographic area, duration, restricted activities)
2. **Supported by consideration** (employment, promotion, bonus)
3. **Protects legitimate business interest** (trade secrets, customer relationships)

*Application:*
- **No salary threshold** (more employer-friendly than OR/CO)
- Non-competes likely enforceable for brewmasters, sales executives if reasonable scope (1-2 years, 50-mile radius)

**Brewmaster Trade Secret Protection:**

*Industry Debate:*
- **Pro-trade secret view:** Beer recipes, yeast strains, fermentation schedules qualify as trade secrets
- **Skeptical view:** Most brewing knowledge industry-standard; recipes not copyrightable; "trade secrets follow talent out the door"
- **Notable litigation:** Toppling Goliath Brewing v. Fran (Iowa 2018) - brewery sued former head brewer for non-compete breach (settled confidentially)
- **Industry criticism:** Strict enforcement limits career mobility, harms innovation, contrary to craft beer's collaborative culture

*ASBC's Protectable Interests:*

**Strong Trade Secret Claims:**
1. **Bourbon formula:** Grain bill, mash schedule, barrel char, toasted wood staves (subject of TTB investigation ‚Üí suggests uniqueness)
2. **Proprietary yeast strains:** If ASBC cultivates unique yeast (not commercially available)
3. **Flagship beer recipes:** Specific grain bills, hop schedules, fermentation temperatures

**Weaker Claims:**
- Standard brewing techniques (mashing, lautering, boiling) - industry knowledge, not protectable

**Key Employee Flight Risk Assessment:**

| Risk Level | Employees Affected | Enforceability | Mitigation Strategy |
|------------|-------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **HIGH** | Brewmasters, head brewers | VOID (below salary thresholds) | Retention bonuses, equity, enhanced NDAs |
| **MEDIUM** | Sales executives | VALID (exceed thresholds) | Standard retention packages |
| **LOW** | Master distiller | VALID | Standard retention |

**Recommended Protection Strategy:**

*For All Employees:*
1. **Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs):** Enforceable in all states, no salary thresholds, protect trade secrets
2. **Confidentiality policies:** Employee handbook provisions
3. **Exit interviews:** Remind employees of confidentiality obligations
4. **Segregate sensitive information:** Limit recipe access to essential personnel only

*For High-Value Employees (Brewmasters, Distillers):*
5. **Retention bonuses:** 25-50% of annual salary, vesting 12-18 months post-closing (Cost: $500K-$1M total)
6. **Non-solicitation agreements:** Prevent poaching customers/employees (more enforceable than non-competes)
7. **Garden leave provisions:** Pay employee 6-12 months post-termination for non-compete compliance
8. **Equity incentives:** If ABP offers equity participation

**Transaction Impact:**

### PART A: EEOC GONZALEZ SEXUAL HARASSMENT CLAIM

#### 1. Title VII Legal Framework: Sexual Harassment Claims

Sexual harassment under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. ¬ß 2000e et seq.) constitutes unlawful sex discrimination. The Supreme Court recognized two distinct forms of actionable sexual harassment: **quid pro quo harassment** and **hostile work environment harassment**.¬π

##### A. Quid Pro Quo Harassment

**Definition:** Quid pro quo harassment occurs when submission to or rejection of unwelcome sexual conduct is used as the basis for employment decisions affecting tangible job benefits.¬≤

**Elements (Prima Facie Case):**
To establish quid pro quo sexual harassment, a plaintiff must prove:
1. The employee was subjected to unwelcome sexual harassment in the form of sexual advances or requests for sexual favors
2. The harassment was based on sex
3. Submission to the unwelcome advance was an **express or implied condition** for receiving job benefits, OR refusal to submit resulted in a **tangible job detriment**
4. The employer was responsible for the supervisor's conduct¬≥

**Strict Liability:** Under *Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth*, 524 U.S. 742 (1998) and *Faragher v. City of Boca Raton*, 524 U.S. 775 (1998), employers are **strictly liable** for quid pro quo harassment by supervisors when the harassment results in a **tangible employment action** (termination, demotion, undesirable reassignment, reduction in pay or benefits).‚Å¥

**Key Characteristic:** Even **one incident** can violate Title VII if it results in a tangible employment action.‚Åµ

##### B. Hostile Work Environment Harassment

**Definition:** Hostile work environment harassment occurs when unwelcome sexual conduct creates an abusive work environment that is sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the terms and conditions of employment.‚Å∂

**Elements (Prima Facie Case):**
To establish hostile work environment harassment, a plaintiff must prove:
1. The employee was subjected to unwelcome harassment
2. The harassment was based on sex (protected characteristic)
3. The harassment was **sufficiently severe or pervasive** to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive work environment
4. The harassment is imputable to the employer‚Å∑

**Objective and Subjective Test:**
- **Subjective:** The victim must subjectively perceive the environment as abusive
- **Objective:** A reasonable person in the victim's position would perceive the environment as hostile or abusive‚Å∏

**Severe or Pervasive Standard:** Under 2024 EEOC guidance, a **single incident** of harassment can amount to a hostile work environment if sufficiently severe. Factors include:
- Frequency of the discriminatory conduct
- Severity of the conduct
- Physical threat or humiliation (vs. mere offensive utterance)
- Unreasonable interference with work performance‚Åπ

##### C. Employer Liability Standards

**For Supervisor Harassment:**
- **Tangible employment action taken:** Employer is **strictly liable** (no affirmative defense)
- **No tangible employment action:** Employer may raise **Faragher/Ellerth affirmative defense**:
  1. The employer exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct sexual harassment (e.g., anti-harassment policy, training, complaint procedure), AND
  2. The employee unreasonably failed to take advantage of preventive/corrective opportunities or avoid harm¬π‚Å∞

**For Co-Worker Harassment:**
Employer is liable if it knew or should have known about the harassment and failed to take prompt, effective remedial action.¬π¬π

##### D. EEOC 2024 Guidance Update

On April 29, 2024, the EEOC published comprehensive final guidance on harassment in the workplace, consolidating and updating documents issued between 1987 and 1999. Key highlights:
- Confirmation that **single severe incidents** can establish hostile work environment
- Extension to **virtual work environments** (e.g., sexist comments or imagery in video meetings)
- Emphasis on **intersectional harassment** (harassment based on multiple protected characteristics)¬π¬≤

### PART B: WARN ACT COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS

#### 1. Federal WARN Act Framework (29 U.S.C. ¬ß 2101 et seq.)

##### A. Statutory Requirements

The **Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act** requires covered employers to provide 60 days' advance written notice of:
- **Plant closings:** Permanent or temporary shutdown of a single site of employment, OR one or more facilities/operating units within a single site, if the shutdown results in an employment loss for 50 or more employees during any 30-day period¬≥¬π
- **Mass layoffs:** Reduction in force that results in an employment loss at a single site during any 30-day period for:
  - At least 50 employees (if they constitute at least 33% of the active workforce), OR
  - At least 500 employees (regardless of percentage)¬≥¬≤

**ASBC's Workforce:** 1,850 employees across 5 facilities ‚Üí **WARN Act coverage applies**

##### B. Covered Employers

WARN applies to employers with:
- **100 or more full-time employees** (excludes part-time employees working <20 hours/week)
- OR 100 or more employees who work for at least a total of 4,000 hours per week (exclusive of overtime)¬≥¬≥

**ASBC Status:** 1,850 employees ‚Üí **Clearly covered**

##### C. Notice Recipients

Employers must provide written notice to:
1. **Affected employees** or their union representatives (if unionized)
2. **State dislocated worker unit** (in Oregon: Higher Education Coordinating Commission; Colorado: CDLE; North Carolina: NCWorks)
3. **Chief elected official** of the unit of local government where layoff occurs¬≥‚Å¥

##### D. Exceptions to 60-Day Notice

Three statutory exceptions permit reduced or no advance notice:

**1. Faltering Company Exception:**
- Employer actively seeking capital or business at time notice would be required
- Employer reasonably and in good faith believed that giving notice would preclude obtaining needed capital or business
- Capital or business sought would have enabled employer to avoid or postpone shutdown
- Employer must provide as much notice as practicable¬≥‚Åµ

**2. Unforeseeable Business Circumstances Exception:**
- Plant closing or mass layoff caused by business circumstances not reasonably foreseeable at time 60-day notice would have been required
- Examples: Major economic downturn, unexpected contract cancellation, natural disaster (if beyond employer's control)¬≥‚Å∂

**3. Natural Disaster Exception:**
- Plant closing or mass layoff due to any form of natural disaster (earthquake, flood, storm, drought, etc.)
- Employer must provide as much notice as practicable
- Applies only to direct effects of disaster, not economic consequences¬≥‚Å∑

##### E. Penalties for Non-Compliance

Employers who fail to provide required notice are liable to each affected employee for:
- **Back pay and benefits** for each day of violation, up to **60 days** (calculated as difference between required notice and actual notice given)
- **Civil penalty** to local government: Up to **$500 per day** for each day of violation (failure to notify local government)¬≥‚Å∏

**Example Calculation (100 employees, zero notice):**
- 100 employees √ó 60 days √ó $200/day (estimated wage + benefits) = **$1,200,000**
- Plus: $500/day √ó 60 days = **$30,000** civil penalty to local government
- **Total exposure:** $1,230,000

#### 2. WARN Act and M&A Transactions

##### A. Asset Sale vs. Stock Sale Distinction

**Asset Sale:**
- **Seller remains responsible** for WARN notice for any plant closing or mass layoff **up to and including the effective date of sale**
- **Buyer becomes responsible** for WARN notice for any plant closing or mass layoff **after the effective date of sale**

**Stock Sale / Membership Interest Purchase:**
- Corporate entity remains intact (only ownership changes)
- **Successor employer** (ABP) inherits all WARN Act obligations and liabilities, including those arising from pre-closing conduct
- **No distinction between seller and buyer** for WARN purposes (same legal entity continues employment relationships)‚Å¥‚Å∞

**Project Bacchus Transaction:**

When a buyer acquires a business as a **going concern** (not merely assets), the buyer becomes a **successor employer** for WARN Act purposes if:

2. **Continuity of workforce** (buyer hires predecessor's employees)‚Å¥¬π

**Conclusion:** ASBC's 5 facilities are **5 separate single sites of employment** for WARN purposes.

**Implication:** WARN notice is triggered only if ABP closes one entire facility (50+ employees) OR conducts mass layoffs at one facility meeting WARN thresholds.

##### D. Taproom Operations

**ASBC has 18 taprooms** across 5 states. Taprooms are **retail/hospitality operations**, functionally distinct from production facilities.

**Single Site Analysis:**
- Taprooms in **different cities** = **separate sites** (no geographic proximity)
- Taprooms in **same city** (e.g., 8 Oregon taprooms) = Likely **separate sites** (different managers, staff, locations)

**Conclusion:** Each taproom is a **separate single site** unless multiple taprooms are co-located or share staff.

**WARN Threshold:** Closing 1-2 taprooms likely does **not** trigger WARN (typically <50 employees per taproom). Closing 5+ taprooms simultaneously might trigger aggregation issues if total affected employees ‚â•50 in 30-day period.
