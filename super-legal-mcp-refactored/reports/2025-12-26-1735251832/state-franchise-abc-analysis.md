## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Transaction Overview and Critical Issues

American Beverage Partners (ABP) is acquiring American Small Batch Corporation (ASBC) for $1.2 billion. ASBC operates a complex distribution network of 305 distributors (180 beer, 125 spirits) across 50 states and holds 118 state ABC licenses (50 brewery, 48 distillery, 18 retail, 2 winery). Two critical statutory and regulatory issues dominate this transaction: **(1) state beer/wine franchise laws in 25+ states that restrict distributor termination and require brand value compensation**, and **(2) 50-state ABC change of control approvals required before closing (30-180 day timelines)**.

This report provides comprehensive analysis of state franchise law termination requirements, compensation formulas, ABC licensing approval processes, brand valuation methodologies, and consolidation cost modeling across three scenarios. The research reveals that post-acquisition distributor consolidation carries $40-300M in compensation costs depending on aggressiveness, while 50-state ABC approvals drive a 90-120 day critical path timeline that must be managed carefully to avoid transaction delays.

#### 2. Brand Value Compensation Methodology: DCF Replacing Industry Multiples

Historically, the alcoholic beverage industry used **gross profit multiples** (trailing twelve-month gross profit √ó 2-7 multiplier) as the valuation standard for distributor termination compensation. However, federal courts increasingly favor **Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis** as a more rigorous, economically sound methodology.

**Landmark Case: *Tri County Wholesale Distributors v. Labatt USA Operating Co.*, 815 F.3d 383 (6th Cir. 2016):**
- Sixth Circuit endorsed DCF analysis over simple gross profit multiples
- Court used "hybrid approach": DCF for franchise contract fair market value + loss in fair market value of other tangible/intangible assets
- Distributor executives testified industry regularly uses 1-6√ó gross profit multiples, with 6-7√ó appropriate for high-performing brands
- Court found DCF analysis "repeatedly endorsed in federal system as typical method involving actual reasoned analysis"

**Case Law Trend:**
- *Ste. Michelle Wine Estates v. Tri County*: Both experts used DCF (not multiples); distributor expert valued rights at $325K vs. supplier's $86K
- Courts reject "back-of-the-envelope multiple of gross sales methodology"
- DCF can produce higher or lower valuations than multiples depending on brand growth prospects, market dynamics, competitive position

**Implication for Project Bacchus:**
- ABP should retain valuation expert experienced in DCF methodology for alcoholic beverage distribution
- DCF analysis allows ABP to argue lower valuations for declining/underperforming brands
- Distributors will counter with higher DCF valuations for strong-performing brands
- Arbitrators typically split the difference between parties' expert valuations
- Valuation disputes extend termination timeline by 6-12 months through arbitration

#### 3. 50-State ABC Change of Control Approvals: 90-120 Day Critical Path

ASBC's 118 ABC licenses across 50 states require change of control approval before closing. The approval process involves extensive background checks on ABP principals (criminal history, financial stability, tax compliance) and varies significantly by state tier.

**Tier 1 States (High Scrutiny, 90-180 Days):**
- **California (50 brewery + 48 distillery + 1 retail = 99 licenses):** 75-120 day processing; largest single-state portfolio
  - Personal Questionnaire, fingerprints, photos for all officers/directors/stockholders
  - Escrow requirement (full purchase price); Notice of Intention to Transfer; 30-day posting period
  - Criminal history (FBI), financial stability, prior ABC violations review
- **New York (12-18 distributors, multiple licenses):** 8-16 weeks; temporary 90-day permit available
  - NYC Community Board notification required 30 days before filing for "substantial corporate change" (80%+ ownership/officer change)
  - $128 application fee; comprehensive background investigation
- **Texas (Lone Star Beverages litigation):** 45-60 days
  - TABC may apply heightened scrutiny due to pending franchise law litigation against ASBC
  - Background checks, tax verification, public notice posting
- **Florida:** 90 days for transfer; temporary license available (seamless business continuity)
  - Any ownership change (direct/indirect) considered transfer requiring DBPR approval
- **Illinois:** 3-10 business days (surprisingly fast)
  - Non-transferability rule: Licenses NOT transferable; new application required for ownership change

**Tier 2 States (Moderate Scrutiny, 30-90 Days):**
- **Colorado (4 retail taprooms):** 60-90 days; temporary permit for 6 months
- **Oregon (8 taprooms + brewery/distillery/winery):** 30-75 days
  - **CRITICAL:** OLCC tied-house investigation pending (see Regulatory Specialist T1 report)
  - Investigation outcome may delay license transfer approval or impose operational conditions
  - Licenses cannot be "transferred"; new ownership must apply for NEW license when 51%+ ownership changes
  - Recent requirement (Dec. 1, 2024): Licensees must keep license status active during change processing
- **Washington (2 retail taprooms):** 30-45 days; pre-approval required before making changes
- **North Carolina (3 retail taprooms):** 60-day transition period; Ownership Transfer Request form authorizes interim operations
- **Georgia (8-10 distributors):** Up to 45 days; non-transferability rule (new applications required)

**Tier 3 States (Streamlined, 30-60 Days):**
- Remaining 40+ states: Notification within 30 days; ministerial review; local authority approval often required

**Federal TTB Coordination:**
- Change of control: Amended registration must be filed within 30 days
- Processing time: 60-90 days (can exceed 6 months for complex cases)
- **CRITICAL:** Change of proprietorship not filed within 30 days = automatic termination of predecessor's permit

**Background Check Disqualifying Factors (All States):**
- **Criminal Convictions:** First-degree felonies, capital felonies, money laundering, embezzlement, fraud, alcohol-related offenses (DUI, illegal sales, serving minors)
- **Tax Delinquency:** Outstanding state/federal taxes, liens, unpaid court fines/restitution, alcoholic beverage tax liabilities
- **Financial Unsuitability:** Disqualifying credit delinquencies, insolvency/bankruptcy, inability to demonstrate financial responsibility
- **Prior ABC Violations:** License revocations, suspensions, non-compliance history

**Rehabilitation Standard:** After disqualifying period, burden on applicant to demonstrate rehabilitation, no risk to public, fitness/trustworthiness, and qualification for licensure.

### PART C: BRAND VALUE COMPENSATION METHODOLOGY

#### C.1 Industry Valuation Methods

**Two Primary Methodologies:**

**1. Gross Profit Multiple Method (Industry Standard):**¬≤‚Å¥¬≤
- Most common industry tool: Multiple of trailing twelve-month's gross profit¬≤‚Å¥¬≥
- Typical range: 1.5√ó to 7√ó gross profit depending on market, brand strength, distributor size¬≤‚Å¥‚Å¥
- Quick and simple calculation: Easy for parties to understand and negotiate
- **Criticism:** Not economically rigorous; not typical in other industries; not endorsed by business valuators¬≤‚Å¥‚Åµ

**2. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis (Court-Preferred):**¬≤‚Å¥‚Å∂
- Measures present-day value of asset based on income expected to generate in future¬≤‚Å¥‚Å∑
- Discounts projected future cash flows to present value, accounting for time value of money and uncertainty¬≤‚Å¥‚Å∏
- Requires detailed financial projections, discount rate determination, terminal value calculation
- **Endorsement:** "Repeatedly endorsed in federal system as typical method involving actual reasoned analysis"¬≤‚Å¥‚Åπ
- **Case Law:** *Tri County Wholesale Distributors v. Labatt* (6th Cir. 2016) blessed DCF analysis¬≤‚Åµ‚Å∞

#### C.3 Key Case Law on Valuation Methodology

**Tri County Wholesale Distributors v. Labatt USA Operating Co., 815 F.3d 383 (6th Cir. 2016):**¬≤‚Åµ‚Å∑

**Facts:** Two Ohio beer distributors (Tri County and Iron City) held franchise agreements for Labatt, Genesee, Dundee, Honey Brown Lager, and Seagram's Escapes. New owner (North American Breweries) terminated franchises. Ohio statute requires compensation for "diminished value."

**Valuation Methods:**
- Both parties' experts used DCF analysis
- Top executives testified they regularly negotiate brand values based on gross profit multiples (1√ó to 6√ó)
- For subject brands, 6√ó to 7√ó gross profit appropriate given volume and profitability

**Court Holding:**
- Endorsed DCF analysis as appropriate valuation method
- Used "hybrid approach": DCF for franchise contract fair market value + loss in fair market value of other tangible/intangible components
- Supplier's expert valuation found more persuasive overall

**Significance:** Must-read case for valuators in alcoholic beverage industry; blessed DCF over simple multiples

**Ste. Michelle Wine Estates v. Tri County Wholesale Distributors:**¬≤‚Åµ‚Å∏

**Facts:** Wine brand termination; distributors sought compensation.

**Valuation Methods:**
- Each expert used DCF analysis (not gross sales multiple method)
- Wine brand's expert: $86,000 diminished value
- Distributor's expert: $325,345 diminished value

**Significance:** Courts increasingly reject "back-of-the-envelope multiple of gross sales methodology" in favor of rigorous DCF

**Florida Arbitration ‚Äì Constellation Brands:**¬≤‚Åµ‚Åπ

**Facts:** Constellation offered ~$70 million to beer wholesaler
- Stated as 7√ó multiple of plaintiff's trailing 12-month gross profits from Constellation products
- Demonstrates high-end valuation for major brands in large markets

**Washington Arbitration ‚Äì Marine View Beverage / Craig Stein Beverage:**¬≤‚Å∂‚Å∞

**Facts:** DCF methodology applied to various beer brands

**Results:**
- Marine View's Tsingtao business: 1.84√ó multiple
- Craig Stein's Olympia beer business: 8.11√ó multiple

**Significance:** DCF can produce widely varying multiples depending on brand strength, market position, growth prospects

### C. Franchise Law Negotiation Strategy

**For Unavoidable Terminations:**

**Step 1: Pre-Termination Performance Documentation (6-12 months)**
- Document all instances of distributor non-compliance with agreement terms
- Issue written notices of deficiencies and provide opportunities to cure
- Build evidentiary record for "good cause" showing
- **Goal:** Establish good cause defense to minimize compensation or enable non-compensable termination

**Step 2: Valuation Preparation (Before Notice)**
- Retain valuation expert experienced in alcoholic beverage distribution
- Prepare DCF analysis showing present value of distribution rights
- Obtain 3 years of distributor's financial statements (if available)
- Develop low-end valuation position
- **Goal:** Enter negotiations with credible valuation analysis supporting lower compensation

**Step 3: Termination Notice (State-Specific Timeline)**
- Issue termination notice complying with state statute (60-180 days depending on state)
- Specify good cause grounds in detail
- Offer distributors cure period required by statute
- **Goal:** Comply with statutory notice requirements; preserve good cause defense

**Step 4: Compensation Negotiation (30-90 days)**
- Lead with low-end DCF valuation
- Negotiate toward gross profit multiple at low end of range for state
- Emphasize distributor's underperformance and declining brand prospects
- **Target:** Settle at 1.5-2.5√ó gross profit (below typical 2.5-4√ó range)

**Step 5: Arbitration if Necessary (6-12 months)**
- File for arbitration under applicable state statute (often AAA rules)
- Present DCF expert testimony
- Challenge distributor's valuation assumptions and multiples
- **Outcome:** Arbitrator will typically split difference between parties' valuations
