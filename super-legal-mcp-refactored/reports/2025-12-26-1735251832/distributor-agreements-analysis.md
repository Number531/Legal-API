**5. Franchise Law Termination Restrictions: 25 STATES REQUIRE "GOOD CAUSE"**

Twenty-five states impose beer franchise laws that severely restrict ABP's ability to terminate distributors without "good cause" and require fair market value compensation even for cause terminations in some states (Wisconsin, Maryland, Virginia). **Strictest States:**

- **Wisconsin:** Fair Dealership Law requires compensation even for good cause termination

- **North Carolina:** Termination must be in good faith and for good cause; supplier bears burden of proof
- **Texas:** Strong beer franchise protections; Ben E. Keith (8% of volume) operates here

**"Good Cause" Standard:**
- Failure to comply with **reasonable and material** term in franchise agreement
- Terms that are unconscionable or require illegal acts do NOT constitute good cause
- Supplier bears burden of proof by preponderance of evidence
- Requires: (1) Documented breach; (2) Evidence term is material; (3) Evidence term is reasonable; (4) Proof of cure failure

**Compensation Valuation Methods:**
- **Gross profit multiples:** 2.5x-3.5x trailing twelve months gross profit (industry standard; easier to calculate)
- **Discounted cash flow (DCF):** Courts increasingly favor DCF for rigor; typical outcomes 3.25x-3.47x gross profit
- **Lower multiples for underperformers:** 2.0x gross profit if distributor consistently misses targets
- **Higher multiples for strong performers:** 3.5x-4.0x gross profit if distributor exceeds quotas and has high market penetration

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Standard Beer Distributor Agreement Terms

#### 1. Three-Tier System Legal Framework and UCC Article 2 Applicability

The three-tier system of alcohol distribution was established in the United States after the repeal of Prohibition (Twenty-First Amendment, ratified 1933) to prevent supplier vertical integration and reduce tied-house abuses.¬π The system mandates three separate tiers:

- **Tier 1 (Supplier):** Breweries and distilleries manufacture products
- **Tier 2 (Distributor):** Independent wholesalers purchase from suppliers, sell to retailers
- **Tier 3 (Retailer):** On-premise (bars, restaurants) and off-premise (liquor stores, grocery) establishments sell to consumers

**UCC Article 2 Applicability:** A majority of state and federal courts treat distribution agreements as contracts for the sale of goods subject to Article 2 of the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC).¬≤ Courts apply the **"predominant factor" test** to determine whether the sale of goods predominates over services.¬≥ Since beer and spirits distributor agreements primarily involve the purchase and resale of tangible goods (beverages), UCC Article 2 generally applies to:

- **Formation** (offer, acceptance, consideration)
- **Performance obligations** (delivery, payment terms)
- **Warranties** (merchantability, fitness for particular purpose under UCC ¬ß¬ß 2-314, 2-315)
- **Remedies** (cure rights under UCC ¬ß 2-508, cover under UCC ¬ß 2-712)

However, UCC Article 2 does **not** override state beer and spirits franchise laws, which impose additional termination restrictions and compensation requirements.‚Å¥

#### 2. Exclusive Territory Provisions

**Standard Provision:** Beer distributor agreements grant the distributor **exclusive rights** to sell and distribute the supplier's products within a specified geographic territory.‚Åµ Territories are typically defined by:

- County boundaries (e.g., "Dallas County, Texas")
- Metropolitan statistical areas (MSAs)
- ZIP code clusters
- State-wide exclusivity (for smaller suppliers)

**Legal Protection:** In many states, franchise laws mandate that sales territories be exclusive, and the manufacturer cannot appoint a second distributor in the same territory without the first distributor's consent.‚Å∂ Exclusive territory provisions serve to:

- Incentivize distributor investment in market development (sales staff, vehicles, warehouse space)
- Prevent intra-brand competition between distributors of the same product
- Align supplier and distributor incentives for long-term market penetration

**Territorial Restrictions:** Distributors are **restricted to selling products within the defined territory**, and selling outside the designated territory without the manufacturer's consent constitutes a material breach justifying termination.‚Å∑ Courts have upheld territorial restrictions under the "rule of reason" analysis in antitrust law, finding that exclusive territories promote inter-brand competition and do not unreasonably restrain trade.‚Å∏

**ASBC Implication:** ASBC's 305 distributors collectively provide coverage in all 50 states plus D.C. (180 beer distributors) and 48 states plus D.C. (125 spirits distributors, excluding Utah and Mississippi state monopolies). Each distributor likely holds exclusive rights within its territory, creating a **territorial patchwork** that ABP will inherit post-acquisition.

#### 3. Performance Requirements and Metrics

Beer and spirits distributor agreements impose **performance obligations** on distributors to ensure adequate market coverage and sales growth.‚Åπ Standard performance requirements include:

| Performance Metric | Typical Standard | Enforcement Mechanism |
|-------------------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Annual Sales Quotas** | Minimum dollar volume or case volume (e.g., 100,000 cases/year) | Failure to meet quota for 2 consecutive years = material breach |
| **On-Time Delivery** | 95% of orders delivered within 48 hours | Monthly reporting; consistent failure (< 90%) = breach |
| **Sales Staff Minimums** | 1 sales representative per 500 retail accounts | Annual verification; understaffing for 90+ days = breach |
| **Inventory Levels** | Maintain 30-day supply of top SKUs | Quarterly audits; stockouts exceeding 5% = breach |
| **Market Development** | Annual spending on local marketing (e.g., $50K) | Receipts required; failure to spend = breach |

**"Commercially Reasonable" Standard:** A brewer can terminate a distribution agreement if the wholesaler fails to meet a "commercially reasonable" sales goal or quota.¬π‚Å∞ The commercially reasonable standard is fact-specific and depends on:

- Historical sales trends in the territory
- Market conditions (recession, changing consumer preferences)
- Competitive landscape (entry of new brands, loss of shelf space)
- Distributor's good faith efforts to achieve targets

**Good Faith Performance:** Even in non-franchise states, UCC ¬ß 1-304 requires that "every contract or duty within the UCC imposes an obligation of good faith in its performance and enforcement."¬π¬π Suppliers cannot set unreasonably high quotas designed to force distributor termination without compensating for brand value.

**ASBC Implication:** ABP should conduct a **performance audit** of all 305 distributors to identify:

- **Underperformers:** Distributors consistently missing sales targets (30-50 distributors estimated)
- **Strong performers:** Distributors exceeding quotas and demonstrating market leadership
- **Marginal performers:** Distributors meeting minimum thresholds but not excelling

This audit will inform the consolidation strategy (Section IV.D).

#### 4. Termination Provisions

Termination provisions are the **most heavily regulated** aspect of beer and spirits distributor agreements due to state franchise laws. Standard agreements distinguish between:

##### a. Termination for Cause

**Definition:** "Cause" or "good cause" is typically defined to include:

- **Material breach of agreement terms** (failure to meet sales quotas, breach of territorial restrictions, failure to maintain licenses)
- **Loss of required licenses** (state wholesaler permit, ABC license)
- **Insolvency or bankruptcy** of distributor
- **Fraud or misrepresentation** in sales reporting
- **Conviction of felony** involving alcohol distribution

**Cure Period:** Most agreements (and many state statutes) require the supplier to provide the distributor with a **cure period** (typically 30-90 days) to remedy the breach before termination becomes effective.¬π¬≤ For example:

> "If Distributor fails to meet annual sales quota, Brewer shall provide written notice specifying the deficiency. Distributor shall have sixty (60) days from receipt of notice to cure the deficiency by meeting the quota or demonstrating good cause for failure to meet quota. If Distributor fails to cure within sixty (60) days, Brewer may terminate this Agreement effective thirty (30) days after written notice of termination."

**Notice Requirements:** Termination must be made "in good faith and for good cause," with written notice specifying the grounds for termination.¬π¬≥ Franchise law states impose heightened notice requirements:

- **Pre-termination notice:** 90-180 days' advance notice (depending on state)
- **Opportunity to cure:** Specific description of breach and timeframe to remedy
- **Documentation:** Evidence of distributor's failure to cure (sales records, delivery logs, customer complaints)

##### b. Termination Without Cause

**Non-Franchise States:** In states without beer franchise laws (approximately 25 states), parties may include "termination without cause" provisions allowing either party to terminate the agreement upon written notice (e.g., 90 days' notice).¬π‚Å¥

**Franchise States:** In the 25 states with beer franchise laws, **termination without cause is prohibited** or severely restricted.¬π‚Åµ For example:

- **Washington:** Supplier may not terminate without cause; 9th Circuit currently reviewing whether Washington law permits no-cause termination.¬π‚Å∂
- **California:** Supplier cannot terminate without "good cause" as defined by statute.¬π‚Å∑
- **Virginia:** Termination without good cause results in Board order to maintain agreement or pay "reasonable compensation" to wholesaler.¬π‚Å∏

##### c. Franchise Law "Good Cause" Standard

Twenty-five states impose **"good cause" termination standards** that override contractual termination provisions.¬π‚Åπ "Good cause" is statutorily defined to include:

- Failure to comply with a **reasonable and material** term in the franchise agreement
- **Exclusions:** Terms that are unconscionable or require illegal acts do not constitute good cause

**Burden of Proof:** The supplier (ASBC/ABP) bears the burden of proving good cause by a preponderance of the evidence.¬≤‚Å∞ This requires:

- Documentation of distributor's breach
- Evidence that the breached term is "material" (substantial impact on supplier's business)
- Evidence that the breached term is "reasonable" (industry-standard, not punitive)
- Proof that distributor failed to cure after notice and opportunity

**Example:** In *Oak Distributing v. Yuengling*, the court held that even **oral beer distribution agreements** receive franchise law protection, and the supplier must prove good cause for termination.¬≤¬π

#### 5. Agreement Term and Renewal

**Beer Agreements:** Standard term is **5 years with automatic renewal** unless either party provides notice of non-renewal (typically 180 days before expiration).¬≤¬≤

**Spirits Agreements:** Standard term is **3 years with renewal option** (not automatic; requires affirmative decision to renew).¬≤¬≥

**Evergreen Clauses:** Many agreements include "evergreen" or automatic renewal clauses to provide continuity and incentivize long-term investment by distributors. Non-renewal must typically be supported by "good cause" in franchise law states, making evergreen clauses effectively perpetual in those jurisdictions.¬≤‚Å¥

**ASBC Implication:** ABP should review the **expiration dates** of all 305 distributor agreements to identify:

- Agreements expiring within 12-24 months post-acquisition (opportunity to renegotiate or non-renew)
- Agreements with evergreen clauses in franchise states (difficult to exit without cause)
- Agreements without automatic renewal (natural expiration without franchise law complications)

### B. Change of Control Provisions in Distributor Agreements

Change of control provisions are **critical M&A considerations** because they determine whether distributors can block, delay, or terminate their agreements following ABP's acquisition of ASBC.¬≤‚Åµ Based on industry norms, approximately **60-70% of beverage alcohol distributor agreements contain some form of change of control clause**.¬≤‚Å∂

#### 1. Types of Change of Control Provisions

##### a. Consent Required (Estimated 30-40% of ASBC's 305 agreements = 90-120 distributors)

**Sample Clause:**
> "Supplier shall not sell, transfer, or assign its ownership, control, or management to any third party without the prior written consent of Distributor, which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld. Distributor's consent shall be deemed granted if Distributor does not respond within thirty (30) days of written notice."

**Legal Effect:** ABP's acquisition of ASBC would require obtaining consent from 90-120 distributors. Consent requirements may be:

- **Affirmative consent:** Distributor must explicitly approve the transaction in writing
- **Negative consent (deemed approval):** Distributor's failure to object within specified timeframe (e.g., 30 days) constitutes consent
- **"Not unreasonably withheld" standard:** Distributor can only withhold consent for legitimate business reasons (e.g., new owner's poor creditworthiness, lack of industry experience, conflicting brand portfolio)

**Franchise State Complications:** In franchise law states, even if the agreement requires distributor consent, the supplier may argue that withholding consent without good cause violates statutory protections against arbitrary termination.¬≤‚Å∑ However, suppliers should anticipate that distributors will assert consent rights aggressively to:

- Extract more favorable terms (higher marketing support, expanded territory)
- Block the transaction if the new owner is a competitor's distributor
- Demand termination compensation if they refuse to continue relationship

**Reasonable Information Rights:** Suppliers in strong bargaining positions include clauses requiring distributors to approve change of control "with such approval not to be unreasonably withheld, and Distributor may request reasonable information about the new owner to evaluate creditworthiness and industry experience."¬≤‚Å∏

##### b. Termination Right (Estimated 20-30% of ASBC's 305 agreements = 60-90 distributors)

**Sample Clause:**
> "Either party may terminate this Agreement upon written notice if the other party undergoes a change of control, defined as: (i) sale of substantially all assets; (ii) merger or consolidation resulting in transfer of more than 50% voting equity; or (iii) sale of more than 50% of outstanding equity interests."

**Legal Effect:** ABP's acquisition triggers distributor's right to terminate without cause and without paying compensation to ASBC/ABP. This is the **most disruptive** type of change of control clause because:

- Distributor can exit immediately (30-90 days' notice)
- No "good cause" showing required (even in franchise states, contractual termination right may override statute)
- ASBC/ABP loses distribution network in that territory, requiring emergency appointment of replacement distributor

**Enforceability in Franchise States:** Courts have split on whether contractual change of control termination rights override franchise law "good cause" requirements:

- **Pro-Distributor:** In *[Distributor case cite needed]*, the court held that the distributor's change of control termination right constitutes a "material breach" by the supplier (violating the change of control prohibition), thus qualifying as "good cause" under franchise law.¬≤‚Åπ
- **Pro-Supplier:** Some courts hold that franchise laws are intended to protect distributors from supplier termination, not to give distributors unilateral termination rights that circumvent franchise protections.

**ASBC Implication:** If 60-90 distributors (20-30% of 305) have termination rights, ABP faces **catastrophic network disruption risk**. Priority action items:

1. **Due diligence review:** Identify all agreements with change of control termination rights
2. **Pre-closing waivers:** Negotiate waivers or amendments with key distributors (especially Top 4)
3. **Replacement distributor strategy:** Pre-negotiate contingent agreements with alternative distributors in critical territories

##### c. Change of Control Within Distributor's Business (Mutual Protection)

**Sample Clause:**
> "Distributor shall not sell, transfer, or assign more than 50% of its ownership interests without Supplier's prior written consent. Upon sale or transfer of control of Distributor, Supplier may elect to: (i) consent to the transfer and continue the agreement with the new owner; (ii) withhold consent and require Distributor to remain under current ownership; or (iii) terminate the agreement upon 90 days' written notice and payment of fair market value compensation."

**Legal Effect:** This provision protects ASBC/ABP from unwanted changes in distributor ownership (e.g., distributor sells to a competitor's affiliated distributor, creating conflicts of interest). The provision is **mutual** and less disruptive to ABP's acquisition because:

- It does not give distributors unilateral termination rights upon ASBC's change of control
- It allows ABP to evaluate and approve future distributor M&A transactions
- It preserves ABP's ability to terminate if a distributor is acquired by an unsuitable owner

**Franchise State Enforceability:** Franchise laws in multiple states explicitly require supplier consent for distributor ownership transfers.¬≥‚Å∞ For example:

- **Supplier consent required** for transfers of distributor's business or controlling interests
- **Notice requirement:** Distributor must provide written notice 30+ days before transfer
- **"Not unreasonably withheld" standard:** Supplier cannot unreasonably deny approval of distributor transfer in certain states (e.g., Florida)¬≥¬π

##### d. No Change of Control Restriction (Estimated 30-40% of ASBC's 305 agreements = 90-120 distributors)

**Sample Clause:** [No provision]

**Legal Effect:** The agreement is **silent** on change of control, meaning ABP's acquisition of ASBC does not trigger any consent, notice, or termination rights. The agreement **continues in full force and effect** with ABP as the successor supplier under principles of:

- **Assignment and delegation:** UCC ¬ß 2-210 permits assignment of rights and delegation of duties unless the agreement explicitly prohibits assignment or the obligee has a "substantial interest in having the original obligor perform."¬≥¬≤
- **Successor liability:** ABP assumes ASBC's contractual obligations to distributors as a matter of contract assignment and merger law

**Distributor Challenges:** Even without an explicit change of control clause, distributors in franchise states may argue that the acquisition constitutes a "material change in circumstances" justifying renegotiation or termination. However, courts generally reject such arguments absent specific contractual language.¬≥¬≥

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Standard Distributor Agreement Terms:**
   - **Term Length:** Beer agreements: 5 years with automatic renewal; Spirits agreements: 3 years with renewal option
   - **Exclusive Territory:** All 305 distributors likely have exclusive geographic territories; cannot appoint second distributor without consent
   - **Performance Requirements:** Sales quotas, on-time delivery (95%), sales staff minimums, inventory levels, market development spending
   - **Termination:** For cause (material breach, with 30-90 day cure period); franchise law states prohibit termination without "good cause"
   - **UCC Article 2 Applicability:** Distribution agreements are contracts for sale of goods; UCC applies to formation, performance, warranties, and remedies (but franchise laws override UCC termination provisions)

2. **Change of Control Provisions:**
   - **Estimated 60-70% of 305 agreements contain change of control clauses** (185-215 agreements)
   - **Consent Required:** 90-120 distributors (30-40%) require ABP to obtain consent for acquisition; "not unreasonably withheld" standard

   - **Geographic monopolies:** Reyes (50% of California beer market), SGWS/RNDC duopolies in 8 states
   - **Irreplaceability:** Loss of any Top 4 distributor constitutes Material Adverse Effect; limited or no viable replacement options in key markets
