## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Oregon Dram Shop Insurance Coverage Framework

#### 1. General Liability Policy Structure and Liquor Liability Exclusion

**ISO CGL Policy Standard Provisions:**

The standard ISO Commercial General Liability (CGL) policy contains a **liquor liability exclusion** that eliminates coverage for dram shop claims. According to industry sources, the liquor liability exclusion applies only to insureds who are "in the business" of manufacturing, distributing, selling, serving, or furnishing alcoholic beverages.¬π

The ISO liquor liability exclusion states that coverage does not apply to bodily injury or property damage arising from:
- Causing or contributing to a person becoming intoxicated
- Furnishing alcoholic beverages to a person who is under the legal drinking age
- Furnishing alcoholic beverages to a person who is already under the influence of alcohol
- Any violation of law relating to the sale, gift, distribution, or use of alcoholic beverages¬≤

**Critical Issue for ASBC:** As a craft brewery/distillery operating 18 taprooms where alcohol is served directly to consumers, ASBC is unquestionably "in the business" of serving alcoholic beverages. This means the standard CGL liquor liability exclusion would apply, **eliminating coverage** for the Oregon Smith dram shop case under a standard ISO CGL form.

**Coverage Restoration Through Endorsements:**

Coverage for liquor liability must be restored through one of the following mechanisms:

1. **ISO CG 24 08 Endorsement (Liquor Liability Coverage Grant):** This endorsement completely removes the liquor liability exclusion from the CGL policy, providing full dram shop coverage within the policy limits.¬≥

2. **Separate Liquor Liability/Dram Shop Policy:** A standalone policy specifically designed to cover alcohol-related liability claims.

**ASBC Policy Analysis:**

The litigation report (T4) confirms that ASBC maintains **$5 million general liability policy including dram shop coverage** with a **$100,000 self-insured retention (SIR)**.‚Å¥ This language indicates one of the following scenarios:

- **Scenario A:** ASBC has a CGL policy with the ISO CG 24 08 endorsement (or equivalent) that removes the liquor liability exclusion
- **Scenario B:** ASBC has a separate liquor liability policy with $5M limits that is referenced collectively with the GL policy
- **Scenario C:** ASBC has an occurrence-based CGL policy with manuscript liquor liability coverage inclusion

**Conclusion:** Based on the confirmed "$5 million general liability policy including dram shop coverage," coverage should be available for the Smith case, subject to analysis of policy exclusions and conditions.

#### 3. Coverage Trigger Analysis: Occurrence vs. Claims-Made

**Coverage Trigger Definitions:**

The type of coverage trigger determines when a policy responds to a claim:

**Occurrence-Based Policies:**
- Coverage triggered when bodily injury or property damage **takes place**
- Policy responds to events that occur during the policy term **regardless of when the claim is made**
- Claims can be made months or years after the injury, as long as the injury occurred during the policy period‚Å∑

**Claims-Made Policies:**
- Coverage triggered when a **claim is first made** against the insured
- Bodily injury or property damage must have occurred after the policy's "retroactive date"
- Three critical dates: (1) injury date, (2) retroactive date, (3) claim date‚Å∏
- Typical for professional liability, D&O, and some liquor liability policies

**Oregon Smith Case Application:**

**Injury Date:** September 2023 (Jones' accident)
**Claim Date:** July 2024 (Smith filed lawsuit)

**If ASBC has occurrence-based coverage:**
- The policy in effect during **September 2023** (injury date) responds
- This is the most common structure for CGL/liquor liability policies
- Coverage is confirmed if ASBC maintained the $5M policy during September 2023

**If ASBC has claims-made coverage:**
- The policy in effect during **July 2024** (claim date) responds
- Must verify retroactive date is before September 2023
- Late notice issues are more critical in claims-made policies

**Conclusion:** Based on industry standards for GL/liquor liability policies serving the craft beverage industry, ASBC likely has occurrence-based coverage. The litigation report's reference to "general liability policy including dram shop coverage" suggests traditional occurrence-based CGL coverage with liquor liability endorsement.

### B. Notice Requirements and Compliance

#### 1. Oregon Statutory Notice Requirements - ORS 471.565

Oregon imposes strict statutory notice requirements for dram shop claims separate from insurance policy notice provisions:

**180-Day Notice Rule (Non-Death Cases):**

Under ORS 471.565, for dram shop claims involving injuries **other than wrongful death**, written notice must be given within **180 days** after:
- The injury occurs, OR
- The person asserting the claim discovers (or reasonably should have discovered) the existence of the claim
- **Whichever is later**‚Åπ

**One-Year Notice Rule (Wrongful Death):**

For claims arising from wrongful death, notice must be given within **one year** after the date of death or discovery of the claim (whichever is later).¬π‚Å∞

**What Constitutes Sufficient Notice:**

A licensee, permittee, or social host is considered to have received notice if:
1. They receive **formal written notice** (mailed or personally served)
2. They receive **actual notice** (any communication giving actual knowledge of the time, place, and circumstances)

### C. Duty to Defend vs. Duty to Indemnify

#### 1. Oregon's Broad Duty to Defend Standard

Oregon follows an extremely policyholder-friendly approach to the duty to defend, establishing one of the broadest duty to defend standards in the United States.

**Eight Corners Rule (Four Corners Rule):**

Oregon applies the "eight corners rule" (also called the "four corners rule") to determine whether an insurer owes a duty to defend. The court examines:
1. The **four corners of the insurance policy**
2. The **four corners of the complaint** filed against the insured

**No extrinsic evidence** is considered when determining the duty to defend (with limited exceptions for fundamental coverage issues).¬π‚Å¥

**West Hills Development Co. v. Chartis Claims, Inc., 360 Or 650 (2016):**

The Oregon Supreme Court held that an insurer owes a duty to defend if the court can **reasonably interpret the complaint's allegations** to include an incident or injury that falls within policy coverage.¬π‚Åµ The key inquiry is whether the complaint permits the presentation of evidence that would establish covered facts.

**State of Oregon v. Pacific Indemnity Company, 328 Or App 64 (2023):**

The Oregon Court of Appeals reaffirmed that an insurer has a duty to defend so long as **the complaint's allegations would permit the presentation of evidence** that would later establish the necessary facts for coverage. The court also confirmed the "complete defense" rule applies to general liability policies.¬π‚Å∂

**Key Principles:**

1. **Potential for coverage triggers defense duty:** If there is any possibility that the claim could result in covered liability, the insurer must defend.

2. **Doubt resolved in favor of insured:** Where a complaint fails to allege determinative facts, doubt must be resolved in favor of the insured.¬π‚Å∑

3. **Mixed claims require full defense:** If a complaint alleges both covered and uncovered claims, the insurer must defend the **entire action** (complete defense rule).

4. **Defense duty broader than indemnity duty:** An insurer may owe a duty to defend even if it ultimately owes no duty to indemnify.¬π‚Å∏

#### 2. Independent Nature of Defense and Indemnity Duties

**Ledford v. Gutoski, 319 Or 397, 877 P.2d 80 (1994):**

The Oregon Supreme Court held that "[t]he duty to indemnify is **independent of the duty to defend**."¬π‚Åπ Even when the allegations of the complaint do not reveal an insured claim, the duty to indemnify may arise if the evidence at trial demonstrates that the judgment was entered on a covered injury.

**Practical Implications:**

1. **Defense duty determined at outset:** The duty to defend is assessed based on the complaint's allegations at the time the claim is tendered.

2. **Indemnity duty determined at conclusion:** The duty to indemnify is determined based on the actual facts established at trial or through settlement.

3. **Insurer may defend and not indemnify:** An insurer may owe a defense duty but ultimately owe no indemnity if the actual facts establish the injury is not covered.

4. **Insurer may indemnify without defending:** Conversely, if the complaint alleges only uncovered conduct but trial evidence reveals covered conduct, the insurer may owe indemnity even if it had no duty to defend.

**Oregon Smith Case Application:**

- **Duty to Indemnify:** Will be determined based on whether ASBC is found liable for serving Jones while visibly intoxicated and whether that service proximately caused Smith's injuries
- **Likely Outcome:** Both defense and indemnity duties will apply, as the claim falls squarely within dram shop coverage

### D. Self-Insured Retention (SIR) - $100,000 Exhaustion Analysis

#### 1. SIR Structure and Operation

ASBC's policy includes a **$100,000 self-insured retention (SIR)**, which differs materially from a standard deductible.

**Key Characteristics of SIR:**

1. **Insured pays first dollar:** ASBC is responsible for the first $100,000 of covered losses (defense costs and/or indemnity).¬≤‚Å∞

2. **Insurer responsibility post-exhaustion:** Once the SIR is exhausted, the insurer assumes responsibility for additional defense and indemnity costs up to the $5M policy limit.

3. **Insured manages claims within SIR:** ASBC is typically responsible for managing and defending claims until the SIR is exhausted (unless policy provides otherwise).¬≤¬π

**SIR vs. Deductible:**

- **Deductible:** Insurer pays the full claim, then seeks reimbursement from insured for the deductible amount
- **SIR:** Insured pays claims directly until SIR is exhausted; insurer has no obligation until exhaustion¬≤¬≤

#### 2. Defense Costs and Indemnity Allocation

**Critical Question:** Does the $100,000 SIR apply to **defense costs only**, **indemnity only**, or **both combined**?

**Standard SIR Structures:**

**Option A - Combined SIR (Most Common):**
- Defense costs + indemnity are **aggregated** to exhaust the $100,000 SIR
- Example: If ASBC incurs $60K in defense costs and settles for $150K, the SIR is exhausted ($60K defense + $40K of settlement = $100K SIR), and the insurer pays the remaining $110K

**Option B - Indemnity-Only SIR:**
- SIR applies only to indemnity (settlement/judgment)
- Insurer pays defense costs outside the SIR from the first dollar
- Less common for commercial liability policies

**Option C - Defense-Inclusive SIR with Reimbursement:**
- Insured pays all defense costs until SIR is exhausted
- If settlement/judgment exceeds SIR, insurer reimburses insured for a pro-rata portion of defense costs¬≤¬≥

**ASBC Policy - Likely Structure:**

Without reviewing the actual policy language, **Option A (combined SIR)** is most likely based on industry standards for $5M GL policies with $100K SIR. This means:
- ASBC pays the first $100K of combined defense and indemnity costs
- Insurer pays all costs exceeding $100K up to $5M policy limit

### E. Reservation of Rights and Conflict of Interest Issues

#### 1. Reservation of Rights Letters - Purpose and Effect

When an insurer has concerns about coverage but chooses to provide a defense, it typically issues a **reservation of rights letter** to preserve its ability to later deny indemnity coverage.

**Purpose of Reservation of Rights:**

A reservation of rights letter informs the policyholder that:
1. The insurer will **defend the matter** (fulfilling the duty to defend)
2. The insurer is **reserving its rights** to later deny coverage based on policy exclusions or other coverage defenses
3. The insurer's provision of defense does **not waive** its right to contest coverage¬≤‚Å∂

**Legal Effect:**

- Insurer fulfills its duty to defend while preserving coverage defenses
- Insured receives defense counsel and financial protection during litigation
- Coverage determination is deferred until facts are fully developed

**Oregon Smith Case - Reservation of Rights Likelihood:**

Given the straightforward nature of the dram shop claim and the confirmed existence of liquor liability coverage, a reservation of rights letter is **unlikely unless:**
- Policy has restrictive definitions (e.g., "visible intoxication" defined narrowly)
- Insurer questions whether Jones was actually "visibly intoxicated" based on server testimony
- Insurer believes ASBC violated policy conditions (late notice, lack of cooperation)
- Policy has criminal acts or intentional misconduct exclusions the insurer seeks to preserve

**Typical Scenario:** Insurer likely accepted the defense without reservation, as dram shop claims are precisely what liquor liability coverage is designed to address. However, ABP should confirm whether a reservation of rights letter was issued.

#### 2. Conflict of Interest and Cumis Counsel Rights

**Conflict of Interest Defined:**

A conflict of interest arises when the insurance company's interests diverge from the insured's interests such that defense counsel faces competing loyalties. This typically occurs when:
- Covered and uncovered claims are alleged in the same lawsuit
- The outcome of factual issues affects both liability and coverage
- Insurer has issued a reservation of rights based on exclusions tied to specific facts¬≤‚Å∑

**Cumis Counsel (California Standard):**

Under California Civil Code ¬ß 2860, when a conflict of interest arises, the insurer must pay for **independent counsel** (commonly called "Cumis counsel") selected by the insured.¬≤‚Å∏ The landmark case *San Diego Navy Federal Credit Union v. Cumis Insurance Society, Inc.* established this principle.

**Key Requirements for Cumis Counsel:**
- **Actual conflict required:** A mere possibility of conflict is insufficient; the conflict must be "significant, not merely theoretical, actual, not merely potential"¬≤‚Åπ
- **Coverage-based conflict:** The reservation of rights must specifically identify policy provisions or exclusions that create a defense conflict¬≥‚Å∞
- **Insurer pays:** The insurer is responsible for reasonable attorney's fees for independent counsel¬≥¬π

**Oregon's Position on Cumis Counsel:**

Oregon courts have **not definitively addressed** whether insureds have a right to independent counsel when an insurer defends under a reservation of rights.¬≥¬≤ Idaho and Oregon "apparently either do not require Cumis counsel, or its courts have not squarely addressed the issue."¬≥¬≥

**Likely Oregon Approach:**

While Oregon lacks a Cumis statute, Oregon's policyholder-friendly insurance jurisprudence suggests courts would recognize independent counsel rights in appropriate circumstances, particularly where:
- Insurer's coverage position creates incentive to develop facts favorable to coverage denial
- Defense strategies that benefit the insurer's coverage position harm the insured's liability defense
- Insurer's reservation of rights is based on exclusions tied to specific factual findings

**Oregon Smith Case Application:**

**Unlikely Conflict Scenario:** The dram shop claim presents minimal conflict potential because:
- Coverage and liability determinations largely overlap (whether Jones was visibly intoxicated)
- No apparent policy exclusions tied to specific facts (intentional acts, criminal conduct)
- Insurer and insured share common interest in minimizing damages and securing favorable settlement

**Recommendation:** If a reservation of rights letter was issued, ASBC should evaluate whether the reserved coverage defenses create actual conflicts warranting independent counsel. Given Oregon's lack of clear Cumis precedent, this may require negotiation with the insurer or court intervention.

### F. Bad Faith Exposure and Extra-Contractual Liability

#### 1. Oregon's Moody Decision - Paradigm Shift in Bad Faith Law

On December 29, 2023, the Oregon Supreme Court issued a **landmark decision** in *Moody v. Oregon Community Credit Union*, 371 Or 772 (2023), fundamentally transforming Oregon insurance bad faith law.

**Pre-Moody Landscape:**

Before *Moody*, Oregon was one of the few jurisdictions that **declined to recognize first-party negligence claims** against insurers for bad faith claims handling. Insureds were limited to breach of contract remedies.¬≥‚Å¥

**Moody Holding:**

The Oregon Supreme Court held that the relationship between an insurer and its insured is sufficiently **"special"** to permit a negligence claim for violations of Oregon's Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act (ORS 746.230).¬≥‚Åµ

**Key Principles Established:**

1. **Negligence Per Se Claims:** Violations of Oregon's unfair claims practices statute can give rise to negligence per se claims against insurers.

2. **Extra-Contractual Damages Available:**
   - **Emotional distress damages** (without physical impact requirement)
   - **Economic damages** (including consequential damages, lost profits)
   - **Punitive damages** (in appropriate cases)¬≥‚Å∂

3. **Negligence Standard:** Oregon effectively created bad faith liability based on a **negligence standard** rather than the higher "unreasonable, frivolous, or unfounded" burden of proof used in most jurisdictions.¬≥‚Å∑

**2024 Expansion Beyond First-Party Claims:**

Post-*Moody* cases have extended the decision beyond its original first-party insurance context:
- *LiquidAgents Healthcare LLC v. Evanston Insurance Company* - Applied *Moody* to allow recovery of lost profits in third-party liability insurance dispute¬≥‚Å∏
- Multiple 2024 cases have invoked *Moody* in uninsured/underinsured motorist claims, PIP benefits, and other insurance disputes¬≥‚Åπ

#### 3. Settlement Authority and Hammer Clause Provisions
