## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### PART A: TEXAS LONE STAR BEVERAGES LITIGATION

#### 1. Texas Beer Distributor Franchise Law Framework

##### A. Statutory Foundation: The Beer Industry Fair Dealing Law (BIFDL)

Texas beer distributor relationships are governed by the **Beer Industry Fair Dealing Law** (BIFDL), codified in Tex. Alc. Bev. Code §§ 102.51, 102.71-102.82.¹ This statute is considered one of the strongest beer franchise protection laws in the nation, particularly given Texas's status as the country's largest beer market.²

**Key Statutory Provisions:**

**Section 102.71 - Definitions**

"Good cause" means:
> "the failure by any party to an agreement, without reasonable excuse or justification, to comply substantially with an essential, reasonable, and commercially acceptable requirement imposed by the other party under the terms of an agreement."³

This definition establishes a three-part test:
1. **Substantial failure** to comply (not minor or technical violations)
2. **Essential requirement** (not peripheral or non-material terms)
3. **Reasonable and commercially acceptable** requirement (not arbitrary or unreasonable demands)

**Section 102.73 - Notice Requirements**

Before terminating a distributor, a brewer must provide:
- **Written notice** containing (a) statement of intention to terminate, (b) specific reasons for termination, and (c) effective date
- **90-day advance notice** before the termination effective date
- The distributor receives a **90-day cure period** to eliminate the stated deficiencies⁴

**Section 102.74 - Good Cause Termination Standard**

A brewer "may not cancel, fail to renew, or otherwise terminate an agreement unless":
1. The brewer has "good cause" for termination, AND
2. The brewer provided Section 102.73 notice, AND
3. The distributor "has not eliminated the reasons specified in such notification" within 90 days⁵

**Section 102.75 - Prohibited Conduct**

Brewers are prohibited from:
- Inducing or coercing distributors to engage in illegal acts
- Requiring distributors to assent to unreasonable requirements
- Fixing or maintaining resale prices
- Failing to provide written contracts
- Requiring distributors to accept unordered deliveries⁶

**Remedies for Wrongful Termination:**

Any brewer who terminates a distributor **without good cause** must pay:
> "the fair market value of the distributor's business with relation to the affected brand or brands, with consideration given to all elements of value, including goodwill and going concern value."⁷

##### B. Case Law Interpreting "Good Cause"

While Texas beer franchise disputes are infrequently litigated to published decisions (most are settled or arbitrated), the available case law and scholarship establish key principles:

**Material Breach Standard:**

"Good cause" for termination typically requires a **significant breach of a reasonable and material term** in the distribution agreement.⁸ The burden is on the brewer to demonstrate cause for termination.⁹

**Common Valid Grounds for "Good Cause" Termination:**
- Failure to meet minimum sales requirements specified in the agreement
- Failure to maintain required staffing levels (e.g., minimum sales representatives)
- Failure to meet on-time delivery standards
- Breach of exclusive territory provisions
- Financial insolvency or failure to pay invoices¹⁰

**Invalid or Insufficient Grounds:**
- Performance decline due to market conditions beyond distributor's control
- Failure to meet goals that are "commercially unreasonable"
- Termination "in bad faith" or to circumvent distributor rights
- Technical or minor violations of non-essential terms¹¹

**Adolph Coors Co. v. Rodrigues:**

In *Adolph Coors Co. v. Rodrigues*, 780 S.W.2d 477, 480 (Tex. Ct. App. 1989), the Texas Court of Appeals held that **no cause of action for wrongful termination accrues until actual termination occurs**.¹² This means distributors cannot bring premature claims based on threatened termination.

**Cure Period Requirements:**

Texas law provides distributors with significant procedural protections. The 90-day cure period is **mandatory**, and brewers cannot terminate unless the distributor fails to cure within that window.¹³ If the distributor substantially cures the deficiencies, the brewer loses the right to terminate on those grounds.

##### C. Brand Value Compensation Methodology

When a brewer terminates a distributor without good cause, Texas law requires payment of "fair market value" with consideration for "goodwill and going concern value."¹⁴ The valuation methodology is critical to determining potential damages.

**Gross Profit Multiple Method (Traditional Approach):**

The most common valuation method in beer distributor cases is the **gross profit multiple approach**:

> Brand Value = Annual Gross Profit × Multiplier

**Gross Profit Definition:**
Gross profit = (Sale price to retailer) - (Cost of product + Freight + Taxes to distributor)¹⁵

**Typical Multiples:**
- **Range:** 2× to 10× gross profit, depending on numerous factors¹⁶
- **Common multiples:** 5× to 7× for established brands with strong performance¹⁷
- **LSB's Claimed Multiple:** 3.5× ($800K annual gross profit × 3.5 = $2.8M)

**Factors Affecting the Multiple:**
- Current and future brand growth trajectory
- Points of distribution and market penetration
- Strength and stability of brand reputation
- Competitive buyer interest

### PART B: OREGON DRAM SHOP LITIGATION (SMITH V. ASBC)

#### 1. Oregon Dram Shop Law Framework

##### A. Statutory Foundation: ORS 471.565

Oregon dram shop liability is governed by **ORS 471.565**, which creates civil liability for establishments that serve alcohol to visibly intoxicated persons who subsequently cause injuries.²⁵

**Key Statutory Provisions:**

**ORS 471.565(1) - Patron Immunity:**
> "A patron of tavern from bringing personal injury claim against tavern that overserves liquor to patron" remains restricted under this immunity framework.²⁶

This subsection **bars intoxicated patrons from suing the server** for their own injuries, even if served while visibly intoxicated.²⁷ This immunity protects establishments from claims by the drunk driver or intoxicated person.

**ORS 471.565(2) - Third-Party Liability:**

Licensed establishments and social hosts are liable to **third parties** (non-patrons) if the plaintiff proves by **clear and convincing evidence**:

1. The defendant "served or provided alcoholic beverages to the patron or guest **while the patron or guest was visibly intoxicated**," AND
2. The plaintiff "did not substantially contribute to the intoxication" through providing, furnishing, encouraging consumption of, or facilitating access to alcohol.²⁸

**"Visibly Intoxicated" Definition:**

The statute defines "visibly intoxicated" as "includ[ing] intoxication by controlled substances or alcohol or both," extending beyond mere alcohol consumption.²⁹

**Notice Requirements:**

Claims require **formal written notice** within specified timeframes:
- **180 days** for injury claims
- **One year** for wrongful death claims³⁰

This notice requirement is **much shorter** than Oregon's typical two-year statute of limitations for personal injury claims, creating a significant procedural trap for unwary plaintiffs.³¹

##### B. Key Oregon Case Law

**Chartrand v. Coos Bay Tavern, 696 P.2d 513 (Or. 1985):**

This landmark Oregon Supreme Court decision established foundational principles for dram shop liability:

1. **"Visibly intoxicated" ≠ "under the influence":**
   > "Visibly intoxicated" is not necessarily the same as being "under the influence of intoxicating liquor", and testimony of officer concerning blood alcohol standard for being under the influence of intoxicating liquor was not relevant.³²

   This means establishments cannot rely on legal intoxication standards (e.g., 0.08% BAC for driving) to determine visible intoxication. The test is **observable signs**, not blood alcohol level.

2. **Statutory liability (not negligence per se):**
   > The legislature resolved the foreseeability issue as a matter of law by holding tavern owners liable for acts of persons served while visibly intoxicated, meaning plaintiffs need not resort to concepts of negligence.³³

   ORS 471.565 creates **strict statutory liability** once the elements are proven. Plaintiffs need not establish common law negligence.

3. **Patron immunity confirmed:**
   > This section does not create claim in favor of intoxicated patrons injured off premises as result of own intoxication against OLCC licensees who serve them when visibly intoxicated (*Sager v. McClenden*, 1983).³⁴

**Bonner v. American Golf Corp.:**

This Oregon Supreme Court decision addressed constitutional challenges to patron immunity:

The court held that ORS 471.565(1) would be deemed **unconstitutional** if a server "serves alcohol to a 'visibly intoxicated' person who has reached the point at which further consumption is involuntary because the person has lost the sense of reason and volition."³⁵

The distinction hinges on whether intoxication was **voluntary or involuntary**. If the patron had lost capacity for voluntary consumption, patron immunity does not apply.

**Baker v. Tyler Gregory Smith (2016):**

The Oregon Court of Appeals clarified **social host liability**:

The phrase "served or provided," as used in ORS 471.565(2), turns on whether the social host has **"control"** over the alcohol supplied to the visibly intoxicated person.³⁶

This control requirement is equally applicable to commercial establishments.

##### C. "Visibly Intoxicated" Standard

**Observable Indicators:**

Oregon courts and practitioners recognize the following as indicators of visible intoxication:
- Slurred speech
- Unsteady movements or gait
- Bloodshot eyes
- Aggressive or unusual behavior
- Inability to focus or maintain conversation
- Fumbling with payment or personal items³⁷

These are **signs that OLCC-trained servers should reasonably recognize**.³⁸

**BAC Thresholds (Expert Testimony):**

While BAC is not the legal test for visible intoxication, toxicology experts typically testify:
- **BAC > 0.15%:** Most social drinkers will show signs of visible intoxication³⁹
- **BAC 0.08-0.15%:** Mixed presentation depending on tolerance and individual factors
- **Functional tolerance:** Experienced drinkers may not present visible signs until BAC reaches 0.20% or higher⁴⁰

**Smith Case Application:**

- **Jones' BAC at accident:** 0.12% (clearly intoxicated by legal driving standard)
- **Plaintiff's toxicologist:** BAC ~0.09% when left taproom (above legal limit, likely visibly intoxicated)
- **ASBC's toxicologist:** BAC ~0.04-0.05% when left taproom (not intoxicated, no visible signs)

The **30-minute gap** between departure and accident is **critical to the BAC back-calculation battle**.

##### D. Causation Requirements

**Proximate Cause Standard:**

Plaintiffs must prove that the establishment's service of alcohol to the visibly intoxicated patron **proximately caused** the injuries.⁴¹ This requires demonstrating:

1. **But-for causation:** If the alcohol hadn't been served, the accident would not have happened
2. **Adequate causal contribution:** The service "added to" the patron's intoxication and ultimately led to the injury⁴²

Importantly, the establishment's service **need not be the sole cause**—only that it **substantially contributed** to the intoxication level that caused the accident.⁴³

**Intervening Cause Defense:**

Establishments may argue that an **intervening cause** broke the causal chain, including:
- Post-service consumption at another location (home, another bar)
- Significant time gap allowing alcohol metabolization
- Patron's independent decision to drive (volitional intervening act)⁴⁴

However, under general tort principles, an intervening act does **not** break causation if:
- The act was **within the scope of the risk created** by the establishment's service
- The risk of the intervening act was **foreseeable**⁴⁵

**Historical Common Law Context:**

At common law, there was **no tort action** against someone who furnished alcohol to a person who voluntarily became intoxicated, on the theory that **consumption (not furnishing) was the proximate cause**.⁴⁶ Oregon's dram shop statute **overrides this common law rule** by creating statutory liability.

**Smith Case Application:**

ASBC's intervening cause defense faces significant challenges:
- **Short time gap:** 30 minutes between departure (9:00 PM) and accident (9:30 PM) provides limited opportunity for post-service consumption
- **Foreseeability:** Drunk driving after leaving a bar is the **paradigmatic foreseeable harm** dram shop statutes are designed to prevent
- **Temporal proximity:** Limited time between service and accident strengthens causation

ASBC's strongest causation argument is that Jones consumed **additional alcohol at home** during the 30-minute gap, raising his BAC from 0.04-0.05% (non-intoxicated) to 0.12% (intoxicated). This requires credible expert testimony and circumstantial evidence.

##### E. Defenses Available to Establishments

**1. Not Visibly Intoxicated When Served:**

The **primary defense** in most dram shop cases. Establishments argue:
- Patron appeared normal when served
- No observable signs of intoxication (speech clear, gait steady, eyes normal)
- Staff testimony uniformly denies visible intoxication
- Surveillance video (if available) shows patron walking normally, conversing coherently

**2. Post-Service Consumption / Intervening Cause:**

Establishments argue patron consumed additional alcohol **after leaving**, raising BAC above the level when served. Evidence includes:
- Toxicology expert testimony on BAC back-calculation
- Time gap between departure and accident
- Patron's statements or evidence of home alcohol storage
- Opportunity for consumption at other locations

**3. Adequate Training / Good Faith Effort:**

While not a complete defense, establishments may argue:
- All staff completed **Oregon-required OLCC Alcohol Server Education**⁴⁷
- Staff made "good faith effort" to assess patron sobriety
- Training emphasized recognizing visible intoxication and cutting off service
- No prior OLCC violations or disciplinary actions

**Oregon's Good Faith Effort Law (ORS 471.412):**

Under ORS 471.412, a licensee is **not in violation** if they make a **good faith effort** to remove unconsumed alcohol when observing visible intoxication.⁴⁸ This law:
- Encourages servers to promote wellbeing and safety
- Protects servers and employers from liability for damages caused by intoxicated individuals⁴⁹
- Does **not** establish a complete defense to civil liability under ORS 471.565, but provides mitigating evidence

**Important Limitation:** Courts have held that **statutory violation does not establish negligence per se**, providing additional protection for servers who make good faith efforts.⁵⁰

**4. Plaintiff Substantially Contributed to Intoxication:**

Under ORS 471.565(2), liability requires proving plaintiff "did not substantially contribute" to the patron's intoxication.⁵¹ Establishments may argue:
- Plaintiff provided alcohol to patron before or after establishment service
- Plaintiff encouraged excessive consumption
- Plaintiff facilitated patron's access to alcohol

**Smith Case Application:** This defense is **unavailable** to ASBC because Smith (injured passenger) had no connection to Jones before the accident and did not contribute to his intoxication.

### PART C: NATIONWIDE FRANCHISE LAW PRECEDENT

#### 1. Comparative State Franchise Laws

##### A. Wisconsin - Strongest Distributor Protection

**Statutory Framework:** Wisconsin's Brand Compensation Law⁶⁵

Wisconsin is widely regarded as having the **strongest beer franchise protections** in the nation, serving as a model for other states.

**Compensation Requirement:**
When distribution rights are terminated, beer wholesalers must be compensated for the fair market value of the terminated rights. Compensation typically ranges **"between three and seven times a brand's gross revenue in a sales territory."**⁶⁶

**Key Features:**
- **Exclusive territories mandatory:** Distribution rights must be exclusive to one wholesaler per territory
- **Good cause required:** Brewers cannot terminate without demonstrating material breach
- **Valuation methodology:** Statute contemplates gross revenue multipliers (not gross profit), making Wisconsin awards potentially higher than Texas

**Comparison to Texas LSB Case:**
- Wisconsin: 3-7× gross revenue
- Texas (LSB claim): 3.5× gross profit
- Gross revenue > gross profit, so Wisconsin multipliers yield higher compensation

##### B. Florida - Franchise Rights with "Good Cause" Standard

**Statutory Framework:** Fla. Stat. § 563.022⁶⁷

Florida recognizes that distributors acquire **"valuable franchise rights"** upon entering distribution agreements.⁶⁸

**Termination Requirements ("Good Cause"):**
For non-compensable termination, brewer must prove:
1. Distributor **failed to comply with material provision** of agreement
2. Distributor was given **30 days to submit corrective action plan**
3. Distributor was given **90 days to cure** the deficiency
4. Brewer provided **90 days' written notice** by certified mail⁶⁹

**Compensation for Wrongful Termination:**
> "Any manufacturer which, without good cause, cancels, terminates, or fails to renew any agreement must pay the distributor reasonable compensation for the diminished value of the distributor's business. The value includes goodwill."⁷⁰

**Comparison to Texas:**
- Similar "good cause" standard requiring material breach
- Similar cure period (90 days in both states)
- Explicit inclusion of "goodwill" in valuation (same as Texas)
- Florida requires 30-day corrective action plan (Texas does not)

##### C. Illinois - Beer Industry Fair Dealing Act

**Statutory Framework:** 815 Ill. Comp. Stat. 720/1 to 720/10⁷¹

Illinois enacted beer franchise protection legislation similar to Texas and Florida.

**Key Protections:**
- "Good cause" required for termination
- Notice and cure period requirements
- Compensation for wrongful termination

Limited published case law on Illinois brand valuation methodology.

##### D. New York - Vested Franchise Rights

**Statutory Framework:** N.Y. Alco. Bev. Cont. Law § 55-c⁷²

**Unique Feature - Covers Oral Agreements:**
> Section 55-c(4) applies to non-written agreements, with the statute's definition of "agreement" explicitly including "any contract, agreement, arrangement, course of dealing or commercial relationship."⁷³

**Vested Rights Doctrine:**
> Once appointed, the beer wholesaler has vested franchise rights that include the right to continue to distribute the brand.⁷⁴

**Termination Compensation:**
If consolidation or termination occurs, the law requires:
- Disclosed, nondiscriminatory multi-state policy
- **Ninety days' notice**
- Payment of **fair market value** of the distribution rights before termination⁷⁵

##### E. New Jersey - Strong Franchise Protections

**Statutory Framework:** N.J. Rev. Stat. §§ 33:1-93.12 to 33:1-93.20⁷⁶

New Jersey requires **exclusive territories** unless dualing existed prior to March 1, 2006.

Similar "good cause" and compensation requirements to Texas, Wisconsin, and Florida.

##### F. California - Weakest Distributor Protection

**Statutory Framework:** California Alcoholic Beverage Control Act⁷⁷

California takes a **pro-brewer approach** that **removes the "fair market value" requirement** in most circumstances:

**Key Differences:**
- **No good cause required** for termination (in most cases)
- **Only prohibits termination** due to distributor's failure to meet **commercially unreasonable** sales goals
- **No compensation requirement** for distribution rights upon termination (except in limited circumstances)⁷⁸

**Strategic Implication:**
California's weak protections explain why it remains the **#2 beer market** despite having less favorable law than Texas (#1 market). Brewers have more flexibility to change distributors, but distributors have minimal protection.

#### 2. Brand Value Calculation Methodologies

##### A. Gross Profit Multiple Method (Most Common)

**Formula:** Brand Value = Annual Gross Profit × Multiplier

**Typical Multiplier Ranges:**⁷⁹
- **Low end:** 2× - 3× (declining brands, poor distributor performance, short relationships)
- **Mid-range:** 5× - 7× (established brands, strong performance, long-term relationships)
- **High end:** 8× - 10× (premium brands, exceptional growth, competitive bidding)

**Factors Affecting Multiplier:**
1. **Brand growth trajectory:** Growing brands command higher multiples
2. **Market penetration:** Strong points of distribution increase value
3. **Brand strength:** National brands > regional > local
4. **Distributor investment:** Dedicated sales staff, trucks, warehousing add value
5. **Relationship length:** Longer relationships = higher goodwill value
6. **Territory quality:** Exclusive territories and densely populated markets increase value
7. **Competitive interest:** Multiple distributors bidding raises multiples⁸⁰

**LSB Case Application:**
- LSB claimed: 3.5× multiplier
- **Assessment:** Reasonable for a 9-year relationship with declining performance
- Comparable range: 2.5× - 4.5× depending on other factors

##### B. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis (Judicial Preference)

**Methodology:**
1. Project future cash flows from brand over remaining useful life (typically 10-20 years)
2. Apply discount rate reflecting risk and time value of money (8-15% typical)
3. Calculate present value of projected cash flows

**Judicial Treatment:**

Recent federal court decisions favor **DCF over gross profit multiples**, viewing DCF as more economically rigorous.⁸¹

In **Tri County Wholesale Distributors v. Labatt USA Operating Co.** (2016), the federal court endorsed DCF analysis over simple multiples.⁸²

However, courts may use **"collective testimony regarding gross multiples"** as a **reasonableness check** on DCF results.⁸³ This means expert witnesses present both DCF valuations and compare them to market multiples to ensure reasonableness.

**Advantages of DCF:**
- Accounts for declining brands (future cash flows decrease)
- Incorporates risk factors (higher discount rate for uncertain brands)
- More defensible in court than "rule of thumb" multiples

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires extensive financial projections (subject to manipulation)
- Highly sensitive to discount rate assumptions
- More expensive (requires sophisticated expert witnesses)

**LSB Case Application:**

If case goes to trial, expect:
- **LSB's expert:** Gross profit multiple method (3.5× = $2.8M), with DCF as support
- **ASBC's expert:** DCF showing declining brand value ($1.5M-$2M), with lower multiple (2×-2.5×) as check

##### C. Wisconsin's Gross Revenue Multiple Method

**Unique Feature:** Wisconsin uses **gross revenue** (not gross profit) as the multiplier base.⁸⁴

**Formula:** Brand Value = Annual Gross Revenue × Multiplier (3-7×)

**Example:**
- Gross revenue: $5M annually
- Multiplier: 5×
- Brand value: $25M

**Why Higher Than Gross Profit Method:**
- Gross revenue includes all sales dollars (not just margin)
- Results in **significantly higher** valuations than gross profit method
- Reflects Wisconsin's strong pro-distributor policy

**Comparison:**
- **Gross profit method (Texas):** $800K profit × 3.5 = $2.8M
- **Gross revenue method (Wisconsin):** If LSB's gross revenue = $8M, value = $8M × 5 = $40M

Wisconsin distributors receive **10-15× higher** compensation than Texas distributors for equivalent brands.
