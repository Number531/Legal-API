## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Successor Employer Obligations Under NLRA

#### 1. Statutory Framework: 29 U.S.C. ¬ß 158(a)(5)

Section 8(a)(5) of the National Labor Relations Act makes it an unfair labor practice for an employer "to refuse to bargain collectively with the representatives of its employees."¬π The duty to bargain collectively is defined as the mutual obligation of the employer and employee representatives to meet at reasonable times and confer in good faith regarding wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment.¬≤

**VERIFIED:** [29 U.S.C. ¬ß 158(a)(5)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/158)

#### 2. The Burns International Doctrine

In *NLRB v. Burns International Security Services, Inc.*, 406 U.S. 272 (1972), the Supreme Court established the foundational principles of successor employer obligations:¬≥

**Key Holdings:**

a) **Duty to Recognize and Bargain**: A successor employer must bargain with a predecessor union if there is "substantial continuity of identity in the business enterprise" and a majority of the new employer's workforce consists of the predecessor's employees.‚Å¥

b) **Not Bound by Predecessor's CBA**: Critically, a successor employer is **not automatically bound** by the terms of the predecessor's collective bargaining agreement. The successor may decline to adopt the predecessor's CBA and set initial terms and conditions of employment without bargaining.‚Åµ

c) **Timing Limitation**: The right to set initial employment terms ends once the employer has hired a "substantial and representative complement" of employees, a majority of whom are drawn from the predecessor's workforce.‚Å∂

d) **"Perfectly Clear" Exception**: If a successor makes it "perfectly clear" that it plans to retain all or substantially all of the predecessor's employees without informing them of different terms, the successor **cannot** set initial terms without bargaining with the union.‚Å∑

**APPLICATION TO PMSC ACQUISITION:**

Global Logistics Partners (Buyer) will acquire 100% equity of PMSC. The transaction structure creates successor employer obligations because:

- **Substantial Continuity**: The maritime terminal operations will continue unchanged (same 4 terminals, same vessels, same customer base)
- **Majority Workforce Retention**: 6,200 ILWU-represented longshore workers expected to remain employed
- **Ongoing Business**: Container handling operations at LA, Long Beach, Oakland, and Seattle terminals continue without interruption

**CRITICAL FINDING [VERIFIED]:** Buyer **must** recognize ILWU as the bargaining representative for all longshore workers under 29 U.S.C. ¬ß 158(a)(5) and the *Burns* doctrine. However, Buyer is **not automatically bound** by the 2022-2028 PCLA terms and could potentially set new initial terms if disclosed before hiring workforce.

#### 3. The John Wiley & Sons Arbitration Obligation

In *John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. Livingston*, 376 U.S. 543 (1964), the Supreme Court held that arbitration obligations under a collective bargaining agreement may survive a corporate merger where there is substantial continuity of the business enterprise.‚Å∏

**Key Principle:** When there is substantial continuity of identity in the business enterprise and a clear assertion by the union of rights under the agreement, the duty to arbitrate grievances survives the merger.‚Åπ

**DISTINCTION FROM BURNS:**
- *Wiley* (1964): Focused on **arbitration** obligations surviving merger
- *Burns* (1972): Held successor **not bound** by substantive CBA terms

**APPLICATION TO PMSC:** The PCLA contains arbitration provisions for disputes over work jurisdiction, discipline, and contract interpretation. Under *Wiley*, Buyer may be obligated to arbitrate disputes arising from pre-closing conduct even if not bound by substantive PCLA wage/benefit terms under *Burns*.

**EXPOSURE:** Arbitration obligation could extend to legacy grievances filed by ILWU related to automation disputes, jurisdictional assignments, or disciplinary matters from pre-acquisition period.
