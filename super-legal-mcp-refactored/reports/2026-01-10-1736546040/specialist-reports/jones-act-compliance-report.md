### B. Jones Act Legal Framework

The Jones Act, codified at 46 U.S.C. ยง 55102, is the cornerstone of U.S. maritime cabotage law. Originally enacted as Section 27 of the Merchant Marine Act of 1920, the Jones Act establishes a comprehensive regulatory regime requiring vessels engaged in coastwise trade to satisfy three core requirements: U.S.-built, U.S.-flagged (documented with coastwise endorsement), and U.S.-crewed.

**Primary Statutory Authority:**
- **46 U.S.C. ยง 55102**: Transportation of merchandise between U.S. points
- **46 U.S.C. ยง 8103**: Citizenship and crew requirements
- **46 U.S.C. ยง 12112**: Coastwise endorsement requirements
- **46 CFR Part 67**: Documentation of vessels and citizenship requirements

**Enforcement Authority:**
- **U.S. Coast Guard**: Build determinations, ownership qualifications, crew citizenship verification
- **U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP)**: Cargo transportation compliance, coastwise trade enforcement
- **Maritime Administration (MARAD)**: Policy guidance and vessel documentation oversight

CBP established the Jones Act Division of Enforcement (JADE) in New Orleans as a centralized enforcement office for all coastwise trade matters. [The Jones Act - U.S. Customs and Border Protection](https://www.cbp.gov/sites/default/files/2024-12/Jones%20Act%20ICP_Complete_04DEC24.pdf)

### C. Historical Context and Policy Rationale

The Jones Act serves multiple policy objectives:
1. **National Security**: Maintains U.S.-flag merchant fleet and trained mariners available for military sealift during national emergencies
2. **Economic Protection**: Preserves U.S. shipbuilding industrial base and maritime employment
3. **Regulatory Jurisdiction**: Ensures vessels in coastwise trade remain subject to U.S. labor, safety, and environmental laws

The statute applies to transportation of "merchandise" between "points in the United States," defined to include all U.S. states, territories, and possessions. [46 U.S. Code ยง 55102 - Transportation of merchandise](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/46/55102)

#### 3. U.S.-Crewed Requirement (46 U.S.C. ยง 8103) - **CRITICAL COMPLIANCE DEFICIENCY**

**Statutory Provisions**:

**Licensed Officers** (46 U.S.C. ยง 8103(a)):
"Only a citizen of the United States may serve as master, chief engineer, radio officer, or officer in charge of a deck watch or engineering watch on a documented vessel."

**Unlicensed Seamen** (46 U.S.C. ยง 8103(b)):
On a documented vessel, at least **75 percent of the unlicensed seamen** (excluding licensed officers) must be:
- United States citizens, OR
- Aliens lawfully admitted to the United States for permanent residence, OR
- Merchant Marine Academy students

The statute permits up to 25% of unlicensed crew to be lawful permanent residents (green card holders), but not more than 25% may be non-citizen permanent residents. [46 U.S.C. ยง 8103 - Citizenship and Navy Reserve requirements](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/46/8103)

**Exclusions from the 75% Calculation**:
- Licensed officers are excluded from the 75% calculation (but must be 100% U.S. citizens per subsection (a))
- Fishing vessels, whaling vessels, and yachts have separate standards
- Vessels operating outside the U.S. exclusive economic zone may qualify for waivers

**Secretary's Waiver Authority** (46 U.S.C. ยง 8103(c)):
The Secretary of Homeland Security "may reduce the percentage" if investigation determines that "United States seamen are not available." However, waivers are **rarely granted** and typically limited to:
- Offshore supply vessels under 1,600 gross tons operating from foreign ports
- Mobile offshore drilling units beyond the Continental Shelf
- Emergency situations where qualified U.S. mariners demonstrably unavailable

**Historical Waiver Frequency**: Federal Register notices indicate waivers are primarily granted for commercial fishing vessels experiencing seasonal crew shortages. During 2005-2009, the Coast Guard issued an average of 108 reports of violations of 46 U.S.C. ยง 8103, with 75 such reports issued in 2009 alone, demonstrating active enforcement. [Federal Register - Waiver of Citizenship Requirements for Crewmembers on Commercial Fishing Vessels](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2014/02/14/2014-03302/waiver-of-citizenship-requirements-for-crewmembers-on-commercial-fishing-vessels)

**Penalties for Crew Citizenship Violations** (46 U.S.C. ยง 8103(f)):

**Original Statutory Penalty**: $500 per individual employed in violation

**Inflation-Adjusted Penalty (2025)**: **$1,562 per individual** per violation [Coast Guard Civil Monetary Penalty Adjustments for Inflation, 33 CFR ยง 27.3](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/33/27.3)

**CRITICAL CORRECTION**: The research plan and task assignment reference a penalty of "$1,100 per ton of cargo carried + vessel forfeiture." This appears to conflate two separate penalty provisions:

1. **46 U.S.C. ยง 8103(f)**: Crew citizenship violations carry **$1,562 per individual** (inflation-adjusted 2025)

**Historic Jones Act Cargo Penalty - Furie Operating Alaska**:

**Facts**: Furie shipped the Spartan 151 jack-up drill rig from the U.S. Gulf of Mexico to Cook Inlet, Alaska via a foreign-flag heavy lift vessel in 2011, routing through Canada.

**Initial Penalty**: CBP assessed **$15 million** (later settled for $10 million)

**Legal Issue**: Furie argued the rig was a "vessel" (subject to lower towing penalties under 46 U.S.C. ยง 55111: $1,100 + $60/ton), not "merchandise" (subject to full forfeiture under ยง 55102).

**Court Holding**: U.S. District Court for the District of Alaska held that when jack-up drilling rigs are carried aboard another vessel on coastwise voyages, they are being transported in a commercial sense and lose their function as vessels, thus constituting "merchandise" subject to full Jones Act penalties.

**Significance**: The court upheld CBP's authority to assess penalties equal to the value of the "merchandise" transported, not merely a per-ton penalty. This was the **largest Jones Act penalty ever assessed** and demonstrates aggressive enforcement posture.
