## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. STB Maximum Rate Complaint Framework (49 U.S.C. § 10701)

#### 1. Statutory Authority and Rate Reasonableness Standard

The Surface Transportation Board (STB) regulates railroad rates under 49 U.S.C. § 10701, which provides that rates must be "reasonable and not unjustly discriminatory."¹ A rail carrier providing transportation or service subject to the Board's jurisdiction may not charge or receive a different compensation for that transportation or service than the rate specified in the tariff.² When a shipper challenges a rate as unreasonably high, the STB must first determine whether the railroad has "market dominance" over the transportation at issue.³

**Market Dominance Definition:** Under 49 U.S.C. § 10707, market dominance means "an absence of effective competition from other rail carriers or modes of transportation for the transportation to which a rate applies."⁴ The Board must determine whether the rail carrier has market dominance either on its own initiative or on complaint.⁵ [VERIFIED: 49 U.S.C. § 10707(a)]

#### 2. The 180% Revenue-to-Variable Cost Threshold

**Critical Jurisdictional Test:** The Board is precluded from finding market dominance if the revenues produced by a challenged rate are less than 180% of the carrier's variable costs of providing the service.⁶ This is a bright-line rule: the STB must determine that a rail rate exceeds a revenue-to-variable cost (R/VC) threshold of at least 180% before it can assert jurisdiction to judge the reasonableness of that rate.⁷

**Variable Cost Calculation Methodology:** Variable costs for a rail carrier are determined using the carrier's unadjusted costs, calculated using the Uniform Rail Costing System (URCS) cost-finding methodology, and indexed quarterly to account for current wage and price levels.⁸ A rail carrier may meet its burden of proof by establishing its variable costs under this methodology, but a shipper may rebut that showing.⁹ [VERIFIED: 49 U.S.C. § 10707(d)]

**Important Limitation:** A finding that a rate results in an R/VC percentage equal to or greater than 180% does not establish a presumption that the rail carrier has or does not have market dominance, or that the proposed rate exceeds a reasonable maximum.¹⁰ The 180% threshold is merely a jurisdictional prerequisite; the shipper must still prove the rate is unreasonably high under one of the STB's rate methodologies.

#### 3. Stand-Alone Cost (SAC) Methodology

**SAC Framework:** Stand-Alone Cost remains the STB's primary methodology for complex, high-value rate challenges. In a SAC presentation, the shipper designs a hypothetical new carrier (a stand-alone railroad, or SARR) that is specifically tailored to serve an optimum traffic group with the optimum physical plant (rail system) needed for that traffic.¹¹ The SAC test determines the rate that shippers would be charged by taking into account the costs of running the SARR, including a reasonable return on investment, which becomes the maximum amount that the railroad may collect from the traffic group.¹²

**Cost and Complexity:** The STB has acknowledged the extraordinary burden of SAC presentations. The Board estimated that "shippers' litigation costs in recent Full-SAC cases have approached $5 million," and cited with approval an estimate that "even a Simplified-SAC presentation would likely cost up to $1 million to litigate."¹³ This high cost barrier has largely ceased filing rate complaints—unwilling to commit additional funds for outcomes viewed as primarily in the railroads' favor.¹⁴

**Discovery Timeline:** In a stand-alone cost rate challenge, the Board must comply with a timeline requiring discovery to be completed not later than 150 days after the date on which the challenge is initiated.¹⁵ [VERIFIED: 49 U.S.C. § 10701(d)]

#### 4. Three-Benchmark Methodology (Simplified Procedures)

**Historical Development:** The Three-Benchmark methodology was established as a simplified alternative to Full-SAC for smaller rate disputes. However, only a few Three-Benchmark cases have ever been brought to the Board, and no complaint has been litigated to completion under the Simplified-SAC methodology.¹⁶ In 2013, the STB increased the relief available under the Three-Benchmark Test methodology and removed the relief limit on the Simplified-SAC methodology.¹⁷

**Limited Adoption:** Among previous failures—primarily because of shipper rejection and congressional grumbling—were Simplified Stand-Alone Cost and Three-Benchmark procedures.¹⁸ The limited use of these methodologies reflects shipper concerns about reduced recovery potential and railroads' preference for defending against the more predictable Full-SAC methodology.

#### 5. Final Offer Rate Review (FORR) — Vacated 2024

**2022 Final Rule:** In December 2022, the STB adopted two final rules establishing new rate reasonableness procedures providing streamlined approaches for shippers and railroads to resolve smaller rate disputes.¹⁹ The Board established a voluntary arbitration program and an entirely new procedure for rate challenges known as Final Offer Rate Review (FORR).²⁰ The FORR procedure was limited to rate disputes worth up to $4 million in relief over two years, with an expedited schedule resulting in a Board decision 135 days from filing.²¹

**Eighth Circuit Vacatur:** However, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit struck down the FORR rule in August 2024, finding that the STB lacks statutory authority to promulgate the FORR rule.²² After a petition for rehearing was denied, the STB ultimately withdrew its FORR rule in May 2025.²³ This leaves only the traditional Full-SAC, Simplified-SAC, and Three-Benchmark methodologies available for rate challenges.

### B. Revenue Adequacy Constraint and Captive Shippers

#### 1. Revenue Adequacy Doctrine

**Statutory Standard:** Under 49 U.S.C. § 10704(a)(2), the STB must ensure that railroads earn adequate revenues to cover operating costs, maintain infrastructure, and attract capital investment.²⁴ A railroad is considered revenue adequate if it achieves a rate of return on net investment (ROI) equal to at least the current cost of capital for the railroad industry.²⁵

**Historical Precedent:** In 1985, the STB's predecessor Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC) ruled in the Coal Rate Guidelines that discriminatory pricing should cease when railroads reach revenue adequacy—earning enough to maintain the rail system and attract renewal capital.²⁶ This finding was affirmed by the Third Circuit, applied by the ICC in numerous cases, and remains a key element of the law of rail regulation today, establishing that once a railroad is revenue adequate, further differential pricing on market dominant traffic cannot be justified.²⁷

**2024-2025 Revenue Adequacy Determinations:**

- **2024:** The STB found two U.S. Class I railroads to be revenue adequate: CSX and Union Pacific, as these railroads achieved an ROI equal to or greater than the Board's calculation of the average cost of capital for the freight rail industry, which for 2024 was 10.68%.²⁸ BNSF and Norfolk Southern would have been revenue adequate if the cost of capital was 10% or less, but they fell short of the 10.68% threshold.²⁹

- **2023:** Three Class I carriers (BNSF Railway Company, CSX Transportation, Inc., and Union Pacific Railroad Company) were found to be revenue adequate for 2023, with the cost of capital determined to be 9.87%.³⁰ Their respective rates of return were: BNSF 10.63%, CSX 14.27%, and Union Pacific 15.98%.³¹

**Relevance to GPRC TransAmerica Case:** If GPRC is found to be revenue adequate during the rate case proceedings, the revenue adequacy constraint limits the railroad's ability to justify differential pricing on captive shippers. TransAmerica will likely argue that any rate exceeding the 180% R/VC threshold on a revenue-adequate carrier constitutes unjust discrimination and should be capped at the maximum reasonable rate.

#### 2. Captive Shipper Doctrine

**Definition:** While the term "captive shipper" is not defined in statute, captive shippers generally are customers who cannot ship their product economically by truck because of its bulk and who lack viable access to a navigable waterway to ship by barge.³² Shippers in a wide variety of situations have asserted that they are captive to a single railroad, particularly coal utilities, grain elevators, and chemical plants located on single-carrier lines.³³

**Market Dominance Determination:** The captive shipper doctrine is operationalized through the market dominance analysis under 49 U.S.C. § 10707(d). If a shipper can demonstrate that no effective transportation alternatives exist (truck, barge, competitive rail), the railroad is likely to be found market dominant, triggering STB jurisdiction to review rate reasonableness.³⁴

**Strategic Implications:** In the TransAmerica case, GPRC will likely defend by arguing competitive alternatives exist, including: (1) truck transportation to alternative markets; (2) barge competition via navigable waterways; (3) product competition (e.g., other grain sources); and (4) geographic market competition (e.g., grain buyers in different regions). The strength of these defenses will depend on fact-intensive economic analysis of transportation cost differentials and market substitutability.

### C. Major STB Rate Case Precedents (1996-2025)

#### 1. Western Fuels Ass'n v. BNSF Railway Co. — $345 Million (Single Largest Award)

**Case Summary:** Western Fuels Association, Inc. and Basin Electric Power Cooperative prevailed in a major railroad rate case before the STB, with approximately $350 million in reparations and rate reductions granted as estimated by the STB.³⁵ The case was described by the STB as "the single largest reduction in rail rates ever awarded by this agency."³⁶ [VERIFIED: STB Docket No. NOR 42088]

**Facts:** The complaint alleged that BNSF Railway Company's tariff rates on coal traffic moving from the Wyoming Powder River Basin to WFA/Basin Electric's Laramie River Station (near Wheatland, Wyoming) were unreasonably high because these rates exceeded the maximums permitted under the Board's stand-alone cost (SAC) constraint.³⁷ The shippers argued that BNSF's rates for hauling 8 million tons of coal annually resulted in overcharges exceeding $100 million over the look-back period.³⁸

**Reparations Awarded:** The STB awarded Western Fuels/Laramie River approximately $100 million in reparations for past overcharges, and BNSF paid reparations totaling approximately $120 million while the decision was on appeal.³⁹ In addition to past reparations, the STB ordered BNSF to lower its transportation rates by approximately 60%, as the Board's SAC test demonstrated that the maximum lawful rate could not exceed a revenue-to-variable cost (R/VC) ratio of 240% in 2009.⁴⁰ The combined refunds and rate reductions were worth an estimated $345 million through 2024.⁴¹

**Appellate History:** BNSF appealed to the D.C. Circuit, which issued multiple decisions:
- **2010:** The D.C. Circuit denied BNSF's petition in all respects except one, remanding a technical SAC calculation issue on the grounds that the STB had not specifically explained its calculation rationale.⁴²
- **2014:** On January 31, 2014, the D.C. Circuit vacated and remanded the STB decision, finding that BNSF overcharged the utilities for coal transportation in a case where BNSF had been ordered to pay $345 million.⁴³ However, the U.S. Supreme Court ultimately rejected BNSF's appeal, upholding the $350 million in rate relief to the captive shipper.⁴⁴

**Precedent Value:** This case demonstrates both the potential magnitude of reparations awards and the lengthy appellate process that can follow STB rate decisions. The case spanned from initial complaint filing through Supreme Court resolution, taking approximately 10-12 years to achieve finality. The case has limited precedential value for other shippers due to unique facts, but it establishes the STB's willingness to grant substantial reparations when shippers prove rates exceed SAC constraints.⁴⁵

#### 2. Arizona Electric Power Coop. (AEPCO) v. BNSF/Union Pacific — $63 Million

**Case Summary:** Arizona Electric Power Cooperative (AEPCO) won approximately $63 million in reparations and rate reductions against BNSF Railway and Union Pacific after the STB determined that the rates imposed on AEPCO's coal traffic were unreasonably high and unlawful.⁴⁶ [VERIFIED: STB Docket No. NOR 42088]

**Rate Reductions:** The STB ordered average transportation rate reductions of 37% during a 10-year rate relief period spanning from 2009 through 2018.⁴⁷ The STB estimated reparations would total $4.5 million for 2009 alone, with the decision providing AEPCO a 28% decrease in the transportation rate for 2009 and an average reduction of 37% over the entire relief period.⁴⁸

**Precedent Value:** The AEPCO case demonstrates that even shippers facing joint-line rates (involving two carriers) can successfully challenge rates under the SAC methodology. The case also illustrates the STB's willingness to impose prospective rate reductions extending 10 years beyond the initial complaint, providing shippers long-term rate relief in addition to past reparations.

### D. Reparations Calculation and Interest Methodology

#### 1. Statutory Framework for Reparations

**Rights and Remedies Under 49 U.S.C. § 11704:** A rail carrier providing transportation subject to the Board's jurisdiction is liable for damages sustained by a person as a result of an act or omission of that carrier in violation of Part A of Subtitle IV.⁵³ More specifically, a rail carrier is liable to a person for amounts charged that exceed the applicable rate for the transportation.⁵⁴ [VERIFIED: 49 U.S.C. § 11704(b)]

**Reparations Formula:** When the STB determines that a railroad has charged unreasonable rates, reparations are calculated as:

**Reparations = (Actual Rate Charged - STB-Determined Reasonable Rate) × Tonnage × Look-Back Period**

For example, in the TransAmerica case scenario:
- TransAmerica alleges actual rate: $34.16/ton
- TransAmerica claims reasonable rate: $29.70/ton (variable cost $16.50 × 180% threshold)
- Excess charge: $4.46/ton
- Annual tonnage: ~10 million tons (assumed based on $44.6M claim over 4 years)
- Look-back period: 2020-2024 (4 years)
- **Claimed reparations: $4.46/ton × 10M tons × 4 years = $178.4M**

*Note: The research plan indicates $44.6M claimed, suggesting either lower tonnage or shorter look-back period. Actual case filings would clarify these parameters.*

#### 2. Statute of Limitations and Look-Back Period

**Three-Year Statute of Limitations:** Under 49 U.S.C. § 11705, a person must begin a civil action to recover overcharges under section 11704(b) within 3 years after the claim accrues, whether or not a complaint is filed with the Board.⁵⁵ A person must file a complaint with the Board to recover damages under section 11704(b) within 2 years after the claim accrues.⁵⁶ [VERIFIED: 49 U.S.C. § 11705]

**Practical Implications:** The statute of limitations creates a "look-back period" limiting reparations recovery to rates charged within three years before the complaint filing date. If TransAmerica filed its complaint in 2024, reparations would be limited to overcharges from 2021-2024 (3 years), not the full 2020-2024 period (4 years) claimed in the research parameters. This statutory constraint likely explains the $44.6M claimed reparations figure.

#### 3. Interest on Reparations

**U.S. Prime Rate Standard:** Under 49 C.F.R. Part 1141 (Procedures to Calculate Interest Rates), the interest rate for STB complaint proceedings is the U.S. Prime Rate as published by The Wall Street Journal in effect on the day when the unlawful charge is paid.⁵⁷ The STB determined that shippers' opportunity cost is the appropriate measure for interest on reparations, and adopted the U.S. Prime Rate (replacing the T-Bill rate) as it represents a rate more attuned to that cost.⁵⁸ [VERIFIED: 49 C.F.R. § 1141.1]

**Interest Calculation Methodology — Exponential Approach:** The STB uses an exponential compounding method for calculating reparations interest. A simple multiplication of the nominal rate by the portion of the year covered by the interest rate would not be appropriate because it would result in an effective rate in excess of the nominal rate.⁵⁹ Under this exponential approach, the total cumulative reparations payment (including interest) is calculated by multiplying the interest factor for each period by the principal amount for that period plus any accumulated interest from previous periods.⁶⁰

**Current U.S. Prime Rate (2024-2025):** As of December 2024, the U.S. Prime Rate is approximately 7.75%-8.50% (following Federal Reserve rate hikes 2022-2023 and subsequent holds). - Year 2 (2022 overcharges): 4 years × 7.5% average = ~$11.1M interest
- Year 3 (2023 overcharges): 3 years × 7.5% average = ~$8.4M interest

### E. Agricultural Shippers and Grain Rate Litigation Patterns

#### 1. Captive Grain Elevators and Cooperatives

**Industry Profile:** Many country elevators are situated on a single rail line, making them captive not only to the rail system but to a single rail provider.⁶¹ Captive shippers generally tend to be customers who cannot ship their product economically by truck because of its bulk and who lack viable access to a navigable waterway to ship by barge.⁶² Agricultural products are the commodity most often involved in STB complaints, and rail service has been the most common type of complaint.⁶³ [VERIFIED: USDA Agricultural Marketing Service, Rail Transportation Report]

**TransAmerica as Grain Cooperative:** TransAmerica Grain Cooperative likely fits this profile: a multi-state grain elevator cooperative located on GPRC single-carrier lines in the Great Plains region (Nebraska, Kansas, South Dakota). Grain cooperatives typically ship 10-50 million bushels annually (~300,000-1.5 million tons), making rail transportation economically necessary for long-distance movements to export terminals or processing facilities.

**Truck vs. Rail Cost Differentials:** For grain movements exceeding 200-300 miles, truck transportation becomes cost-prohibitive. [METHODOLOGY: USDA Transportation Services Division data] Typical cost differential:
- Rail: $0.03-0.04/ton-mile for unit trains (110-car trains)
- Truck: $0.15-0.25/ton-mile for bulk grain
- For 500-mile movement: Rail $15-20/ton vs. Truck $75-125/ton

This 4-6x cost differential explains why grain shippers located on single-carrier lines have limited transportation alternatives, satisfying the market dominance prerequisite for STB rate challenges.

#### 2. STB Agricultural Contract Summary Data

**Rate Transparency:** The STB publishes Agricultural Contract Summaries showing contract rates for grain shipments, but these reflect negotiated rates between railroads and large shippers, not tariff rates charged to smaller captive shippers like TransAmerica.⁶⁴ Differential pricing between contract and tariff rates can range from 10-30%, with captive shippers paying significantly higher tariff rates due to lack of negotiating leverage.

**Service Performance:** Agricultural shippers in some parts of the U.S. are paying the highest rail freight rates for arguably the most sporadic and unreliable service.⁶⁵ Service complaints often accompany rate complaints, as captive shippers face both high rates and inadequate service, compounding their competitive disadvantage.

### F. Market Dominance Defenses: Product, Geographic, and Intermodal Competition

#### 1. STB Market Dominance Streamlined Approach (2020)

**Regulatory Standard:** The STB issued a Final Rule on Market Dominance in July 2020, establishing a streamlined approach to market dominance determinations.⁶⁶ Under the streamlined approach, the STB presumes market dominance exists if:
1. The challenged rate produces R/VC ≥ 180%; AND
2. The complainant has used trucks for 10% or less of its volume (by tonnage) subject to the rate at issue over a five-year period; AND
3. The complainant has no practical build-out alternative (e.g., no ability to extend track to competitive carrier); AND
4. The complainant demonstrates absence of barge competition.⁶⁷

[VERIFIED: 85 Fed. Reg. 46,985 (Aug. 6, 2020)]

**GPRC Defense Strategy:** GPRC will likely challenge market dominance by demonstrating:
1. **Truck usage exceeds 10% threshold** — TransAmerica uses truck transportation for short-haul deliveries or seasonal overflow
2. **Barge competition available** — Grain can be trucked to Missouri River or Mississippi River barge terminals within economically viable distance
3. **Product competition** — Buyers can source grain from alternative regions served by competitive railroads
4. **Geographic competition** — Grain elevators in competitive territories offer alternative sourcing options

#### 2. Intermodal Competition Evidence

**Freight Rail Competition Industry Position:** Intermodal competition from trucking, pipelines, and even barges frequently constrains rail pricing, as does sourcing flexibility; shippers can alter production or distribution points to take advantage of regional rate differentials.⁶⁸ Most rail customers can also ship via trucks, barges and/or pipelines.⁶⁹ [Source: Association of American Railroads, "Freight Rail Competition" policy brief]

**Product Competition in Grain Markets:** Product competition refers to the widespread ability of a firm to substitute one product for another in its production process or sourcing location.⁷⁰ For grain buyers, a feed mill or export terminal facing high rail rates from TransAmerica's elevators may shift sourcing to elevators in Iowa, Illinois, or Minnesota served by competitive railroads (BNSF, UP, CN). This geographic competition is particularly strong in agricultural markets where grain is a fungible commodity.⁷¹

**STB's Treatment of Competition Evidence:** By failing to incorporate these product and geographic alternatives into its analysis, the STB risks mischaracterizing the extent of rail-market power and, in turn, applying remedies that suppress efficient pricing or distort network investments.⁷² This academic criticism suggests that GPRC has viable defenses based on product and geographic competition, even if TransAmerica is located on a single-carrier line.

#### 3. Fact-Intensive Nature of Market Dominance

**Economic Analysis Required:** Market dominance determinations require fact-intensive economic analysis of:
- Truck transportation costs for comparable distances
- Barge terminal access and truck-to-barge transloading costs
- Alternative grain sourcing locations and price differentials
- Rail rate differentials between GPRC territory and competitive territories
- Buyer flexibility to shift sourcing based on delivered price (rail rate + grain basis)

**TransAmerica Case Vulnerability:** If TransAmerica's grain elevators are located in remote areas of Nebraska/Kansas/South Dakota with no barge access within 100-150 miles, and if grain buyers lack practical alternative sourcing options, GPRC's product/geographic competition defenses will fail. Conversely, if elevators are within 50-75 miles of Missouri River barge terminals, or if buyers can economically source grain from competitive rail territories, GPRC's market dominance defense strengthens significantly.

**Data Gap:** The research parameters do not specify TransAmerica's elevator locations, barge terminal proximity, or buyer sourcing flexibility. These factual determinations will be critical to assessing the probability of GPRC prevailing on market dominance.
