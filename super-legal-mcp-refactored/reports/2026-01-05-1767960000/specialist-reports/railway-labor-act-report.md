### Railway Labor Act Framework and Procedures

**Major Disputes vs. Minor Disputes (45 U.S.C. ¬ß 151 et seq.)**

The RLA distinguishes between:
- **Major disputes:** Changes to rates of pay, rules, or working conditions ‚Üí NMB mediation mandatory
- **Minor disputes:** Grievances or interpretation of existing agreements ‚Üí National Railroad Adjustment Board jurisdiction

The expired BLET contract is a **major dispute** requiring NMB mediation under 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 155.

**Status Quo Obligation (45 U.S.C. ¬ß 156)**

During mediation, both parties are prohibited from making unilateral changes to "rates of pay, rules, or working conditions." GPRC **cannot**:
- Implement its 12% wage offer without union agreement
- Increase employee health insurance contributions from 15% to 20%
- Modify the Hi-Viz attendance policy unilaterally

The status quo obligation continues indefinitely until settlement or NMB release to self-help.

**NMB Mediation and Release Procedures**

1. **Mediation phase:** NMB mediator works with parties for 5-8 months (typical duration based on 2022 precedent)
2. **Proffer of arbitration:** If mediation fails, NMB offers voluntary arbitration; if either party declines, NMB releases parties
3. **30-day cooling-off period:** Begins after release; no strikes or lockouts permitted
4. **Self-help:** After cooling-off, union may strike or carrier may lockout

**Presidential Emergency Board (45 U.S.C. ¬ß 160)**

Before cooling-off expires, NMB may notify President if dispute threatens "substantially to interrupt interstate commerce." President may (discretionary) create PEB by Executive Order:
- **30 days to investigate** and issue recommendations
- **Additional 30-day cooling-off** after report
- **Total delay: 60 days** from PEB creation to self-help
- **Recommendations non-binding** but highly influential

**PEB 250 precedent (2022):** Recommended 24% wage increases over 5 years, maintained healthcare cost-sharing (rejected employee contribution increases), denied paid sick leave. Eight of twelve unions ratified based on PEB terms; four rejected.

**Congressional Intervention (Commerce Clause Authority)**

If PEB fails and strike deadline approaches, Congress has constitutional authority to impose settlement:
- **December 2, 2022:** Pub. L. No. 117-212 mandated PEB 250 terms, preventing December 9 strike
- **House vote:** 290-137 (bipartisan)
- **Senate vote:** 80-15
- **Economic justification:** $2B/day damage, 750,000 job losses projected, 40% of U.S. freight moves by rail

**Historical frequency:** Congressional intervention is rare but established‚Äî6 instances since 1963, most recently 2022.

### Acquisition-Specific Labor Risks

**A. Successorship Obligations (Stock Purchase)**

In a stock purchase, Continental **inherits all labor obligations** without modification:
- All 12 CBAs remain in full force
- Seniority rights transfer without change
- Pending grievances/arbitrations continue
- Unfair labor practice charge liability transfers

**RLA specificity:** Unlike NLRA-covered industries where asset purchasers can avoid CBA obligations through successorship analysis, the RLA's status quo obligation (45 U.S.C. ¬ß 156) means Continental cannot terminate or modify CBAs upon acquisition.

**B. Single Carrier Determination Risk**

**Critical due diligence gap:** Transaction materials do not confirm whether Continental owns other railroads. If Continental owns other Class I, II, or III railroads, the NMB may determine that Continental's properties constitute a **single transportation system** under 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 152 Ninth.

**Consequences:**
- **Single system agreement required:** Unified labor agreements across all Continental railroads
- **Seniority integration:** "Dovetailing" (alternating employees by hire date) or "end-tailing" (one railroad's employees added to bottom of seniority list)
- **Cost:** $50-120M based on Union Pacific/Southern Pacific (1996) and CSX/Conrail (1998) merger precedents
- **Timeline:** 12-18 months to negotiate implementing agreements

**Probability:** 80%+ if Continental owns other railroads (NMB historically finds single carrier in common control situations).

**C. STB Labor Protective Conditions (49 U.S.C. ¬ß 11326)**

When STB approves the merger, it will impose **New York Dock** labor protective conditions:

**Dismissal allowances (severance):**
- Employees furloughed due to merger receive up to **6 years wage protection** OR
- One-time lump sum payment of **up to 1 year salary**
- Applicable to all employees with 6+ months seniority

**Displacement allowances:**
- Moving expenses for relocated employees

**Financial exposure (200-500 job eliminations from synergies):**

- **Aggressive (500 jobs):** $82.5M

**Mitigation:** Negotiate implementing agreement with unions for Voluntary Separation Incentive Program (VSIP) offering 1-year lump sum + enhanced benefits (more attractive to employees, lower cost than 6-year protection).

**D. Coordinated Bargaining Risk**

GPRC's 12 unions may coordinate bargaining to maximize leverage:
- **Pattern enforcement:** All 11 other unions demand identical BLET terms
- **Sequential strike threats:** Cascade of strike deadlines if mediation fails
- **Coalition bargaining:** Refuse to settle any contract unless all 12 unions agree simultaneously

**Historical precedent:** 2022 national freight rail negotiations involved coordinated bargaining among 12 unions, ultimately leading to PEB 250 and Congressional intervention.

**Mitigation:** Include "most favored nation" clause in BLET agreement prohibiting more generous terms to other unions; seek NMB mediation for all 12 unions simultaneously.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Railway Labor Act Statutory Framework

#### 1. Major Disputes vs. Minor Disputes

The Railway Labor Act, codified at 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 151 et seq., distinguishes between two types of disputes:¬π

**Major Disputes:** Disputes concerning changes in rates of pay, rules, or working conditions that are not referable to the National Railroad Adjustment Board (NRAB). These disputes follow the mandatory mediation procedures under 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 155.¬≤

**Minor Disputes:** Disputes growing out of grievances or the interpretation or application of existing collective bargaining agreements. The NRAB has primary jurisdiction over these disputes.¬≥

The expired BLET contract at GPRC constitutes a **major dispute** under the RLA because it involves changes to rates of pay (wage increases), health insurance cost-sharing (working conditions), and attendance policies (rules).

**[VERIFIED: 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 151a - Statutory purposes and dispute classifications]**

#### 2. National Mediation Board Procedures Under 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 155

The NMB mediation process for major disputes follows this mandatory sequence:‚Å¥

**Step 1: Mediation Request**
Either party may invoke NMB mediation under 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 155 First. The NMB must use its best efforts "to bring about a settlement through mediation."‚Åµ

**Step 2: Status Quo Obligation**
During mediation, 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 156 prohibits either party from making unilateral changes to "rates of pay, rules, or working conditions."‚Å∂ This status quo obligation continues indefinitely until:
- The parties reach agreement, OR
- The NMB declares mediation efforts exhausted, OR
- The parties jointly request release from mediation

**Application to GPRC:** Since the BLET contract expired 12/31/24 and NMB mediation is ongoing, GPRC **cannot unilaterally**:
- Implement its last best offer (12% wage increase over 3 years)
- Increase employee health insurance contributions from 15% to 20%
- Modify the Hi-Viz attendance policy
- Reduce any benefit or change any working condition

The status quo obligation protects engineers at their current $84,000/year wage level plus existing benefits until either settlement or self-help is reached.

**[VERIFIED: 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 156 - Status quo requirement during major dispute procedures]**

#### 3. Release from Mediation and 30-Day Cooling-Off Period

When the NMB determines that further mediation efforts will not be successful, it may:‚Å∑

**Option A: Proffer Arbitration** (45 U.S.C. ¬ß 155 First)
The NMB may recommend voluntary arbitration. If either party declines, the NMB releases the parties from mediation.

**Option B: Release to Self-Help**
Upon release, a **30-day cooling-off period** begins under 45 U.S.C. ¬ß 155 First.‚Å∏ During this period:
- Negotiations continue
- Status quo obligation remains in effect
- No strikes or lockouts permitted

**After 30-Day Cooling-Off:**
If no agreement is reached, parties are free to exercise "self-help":‚Åπ
- **Union:** May strike, engage in work slowdowns, or other concerted activity
- **Carrier:** May lock out employees, implement last best offer, or cease operations

**Timeline Example for GPRC:**

- **1/1/25 - 6/1/25:** NMB mediation (estimated 5 months, based on 2022 precedent)

#### 3. Pattern Bargaining Mechanics in Railroad Industry

Railroad collective bargaining operates on a **pattern bargaining** system where:¬≥‚Å¥

**National vs. Local Agreements:**
- **National Agreements:** Cover wages, health benefits, retirement for all employees in a craft across multiple railroads
- **Local Agreements:** Address carrier-specific work rules, operating conditions, and craft-specific practices

**Pattern Establishment:**
Once one major union (typically BLET or TCU) reaches a settlement with the NCCC, that settlement becomes the **pattern** that other unions follow.¬≥‚Åµ Deviations are rare because:
- NMB mediators reference pattern in subsequent negotiations
- Presidential Emergency Boards cite pattern agreements as comparators
- Unions coordinate through coordinated bargaining committees

**Application to GPRC:**
If GPRC's BLET engineers accept a settlement below the 18.8% pattern, the **11 other craft unions** (14,300 employees) will cite pattern precedent in their negotiations. This creates a **cascading cost obligation** across all 28,500 unionized employees.

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Strike Probability is Moderate (30-40%) But Manageable:**
   The BLET contract expiration creates strike risk, but Railway Labor Act procedures provide multiple intervention points (NMB mediation, PEB, Congressional action) that historically prevent Class I railroad strikes. Congressional intervention probability is 75-85% given GPRC's national commerce impact.

2. **Settlement Cost is Substantial ($752.3M Over 3 Years for All Unions):**
   Closing the $14K/year engineer wage gap and maintaining healthcare cost-sharing will cost **$250.8M annually** across all 12 unions. This represents a **4.6% increase in operating expenses** and will deteriorate operating ratio from 65.9% to 70.5%.

3. **Pattern Bargaining Creates Cascading Obligations:**
   The November 2025 NCCC-BLET national pattern (18.8% over 5 years) establishes the benchmark. GPRC's 12% offer is significantly below pattern, making union acceptance unlikely. A 15% over 3 years settlement is the minimum credible offer.

4. **Retroactive Pay is Likely ($63.4M for 8 Months):**
   Settlement after contract expiration (12/31/24) will trigger retroactive pay for work performed during mediation. An August 2026 settlement would require a $63.4M lump sum payment within 30-60 days of ratification.

5. **Acquisition Timing Creates Urgency:**
   STB merger approval requires 18-36 months. Unresolved BLET dispute could delay STB approval or trigger adverse conditions. **Recommendation: Settle BLET contract before STB application filing.**

6. **Single Carrier Risk Depends on Continental's Portfolio:**
   If Continental owns other railroads, NMB will likely determine single transportation system, triggering $50M-$120M seniority integration costs and 12-18 month delay. **Critical due diligence gap.**

7. **STB Will Impose New York Dock Labor Protections:**
   Mandatory severance/relocation protections for furloughed employees will cost $27M-$82.5M (depending on synergy headcount reductions). This is unavoidable and should be included in transaction model.

8. **Operating Ratio Deterioration is Significant:**
   $250.8M annual labor cost increase will move GPRC's operating ratio from 65.9% to 70.5%, making it **one of the least efficient Class I railroads** (industry benchmark 60-62%). Offsetting productivity improvements are essential.

### Appendix A: Railway Labor Act Statutory Framework

**45 U.S.C. ¬ß 151a - Purposes:**
1. Prevent interruption to commerce or carrier operations
2. Forbid limitations on employee right to join unions

**45 U.S.C. ¬ß 155 First - NMB Mediation Procedures:**
- Either party may invoke mediation
- NMB must use "best efforts" to bring settlement
- If mediation fails, NMB may proffer arbitration
- If arbitration declined, 30-day cooling-off period begins
- After cooling-off, parties free to exercise "self-help"

**45 U.S.C. ¬ß 156 - Status Quo Obligation:**
- No changes to "rates of pay, rules, or working conditions" during mediation
- Applies indefinitely until settlement or NMB release
- Violation constitutes unfair labor practice

**45 U.S.C. ¬ß 160 - Presidential Emergency Board:**
- NMB may notify President if dispute threatens interstate commerce
- President may create PEB by Executive Order (discretionary)
- PEB has 30 days to investigate and issue recommendations
- Additional 30-day cooling-off after PEB report
- Recommendations non-binding

**49 U.S.C. ¬ß 11326 - Employee Protective Arrangements:**
- STB must impose labor protections in railroad transactions
- Dismissal allowances: Up to 6 years wage protection or 1 year lump sum
- Displacement allowances: Moving expenses, temporary living, housing differential
- Applies to Class I railroad mergers
