### A. Legal Framework

The Surface Transportation Board (STB) possesses **exclusive jurisdiction** over railroad mergers and acquisitions involving common carriers by rail under 49 U.S.C. § 11321(a).¹ This jurisdiction is comprehensive and preempts traditional antitrust review by the Department of Justice or Federal Trade Commission—a unique feature that distinguishes railroad consolidations from virtually all other industries subject to federal competition oversight.² The STB inherited this authority from the Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC) upon the ICC's termination in 1996 under the ICC Termination Act.³

**Statutory Framework:**

The governing statutory provisions for Continental Transportation Holdings LLC's proposed acquisition of Great Plains Railroad Company (GPRC) are:

- **49 U.S.C. § 11323**: Authorizes STB approval of railroad consolidation, merger, and acquisition of control transactions
- **49 U.S.C. § 11324(b)**: Requires STB to approve transactions "consistent with the public interest"
- **49 U.S.C. § 11321(a)**: Vests exclusive jurisdiction in STB, expressly preempting 49 U.S.C. § 11341 (expedited antitrust review)
- **49 U.S.C. § 11326**: Mandates labor protective provisions in all merger approvals
- **49 C.F.R. Part 1180**: Procedural regulations governing railroad consolidation applications and STB review

**Transaction Classification:**

Railroad consolidation transactions are classified into four categories under 49 C.F.R. § 1180.2: (1) major transactions involving two or more Class I railroads; (2) significant transactions of regional or national importance not involving two Class I carriers; (3) minor transactions; and (4) exempt transactions.⁴ The Continental-GPRC acquisition constitutes a **major transaction** because GPRC is a Class I freight railroad with annual operating revenues of $8.2 billion—substantially exceeding the $490 million Class I threshold established by the STB.⁵

Major transactions receive the **strictest level of STB scrutiny** and are subject to the "enhanced competition" standard adopted in 2001, which represents a fundamental departure from pre-2001 merger policy.⁶

**Enhanced Competition Standard (Post-2001):**

Prior to 2001, Class I railroad mergers were evaluated under a "do no harm" standard—applicants needed to demonstrate that the transaction would not materially reduce existing competition.⁷ Following service failures after the Union Pacific/Southern Pacific merger (1996), the STB substantially tightened its merger policy in 2001.⁸ Under current regulations codified at 49 C.F.R. § 1180.1, major merger applications must affirmatively demonstrate that the transaction will **enhance competition**, not merely preserve it.⁹

The STB's public interest analysis under 49 C.F.R. § 1180.1 requires applicants to address:

1. **Benefits**: Operational efficiencies, service improvements, capital investment commitments, new single-line routes, network optimization, reduced environmental impacts
2. **Harms**: Reduction of competition (captive shipper analysis, gateway closures, 2-to-1 point analysis), harm to essential services, transitional service problems, labor displacement
3. **Enhanced Competition**: How the transaction and proposed conditions would enhance competition to offset harms, including voluntary trackage rights, reciprocal switching, rate protections, gateway preservation commitments
4. **Environmental Review**: Compliance with National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) through preparation of Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)
5. **Employee Protections**: Labor protective provisions under 49 U.S.C. § 11326 (New York Dock conditions)

The enhanced competition standard applies to the Continental-GPRC transaction because GPRC is a Class I railroad.¹⁰ This standard creates substantial regulatory risk because Continental must demonstrate competitive benefits, not merely argue that the merger does not harm competition.

**NEPA Compliance:**

Major railroad mergers are "major Federal actions significantly affecting the quality of the human environment" under the National Environmental Policy Act, 42 U.S.C. § 4321 *et seq.*¹¹ The STB's Office of Environmental Analysis (OEA) prepares an Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) for major transactions, analyzing impacts on air quality, noise, safety, hazardous materials transportation, water resources, biological resources, cultural resources, land use, socioeconomics, and environmental justice.¹² The EIS process typically requires 15-18 months and includes public scoping periods, Draft EIS preparation and comment, and Final EIS preparation.¹³

**Precedent Transactions:**

The most recent major Class I railroad merger approved by the STB was Canadian Pacific Railway's acquisition of Kansas City Southern Railway, completed April 14, 2023.¹⁴ The CP-KCS merger established critical precedents for voting trust approval standards, gateway preservation conditions, and enhanced competition analysis that directly govern the Continental-GPRC transaction. Prior major mergers occurred before the 2001 enhanced competition rules took effect: Union Pacific/Southern Pacific (approved 1996) and CSX/Norfolk Southern joint acquisition of Conrail (approved 1998).¹⁵

An active major merger application is currently pending before the STB: Union Pacific Corporation's proposed acquisition of Norfolk Southern Corporation, filed in August 2025.¹⁶ The UP-NS proceeding represents the **first major Class I merger application filed under post-2001 rules** and provides real-time guidance on STB procedural requirements, completeness standards, and substantive analysis expectations.¹⁷

#### B.2 Voting Trust Approval Uncertainty: 55% Probability Creates Financial Closing Risk

**Conclusion:** If the STB approves a voting trust, Continental can complete **financial closing** 4-6 months after STB application filing, enabling Continental to acquire GPRC shares while STB merger review continues for an additional 18-24 months. **Severity: HIGH**. However, voting trust approval is **uncertain** (55% base case probability) due to CN-KCS voting trust rejection precedent and concerns about private equity ownership structures. If the voting trust is denied, Continental cannot close the transaction for 24-36 months, increasing financing costs by $120M-$180M and creating retrading risk. **Confidence: MEDIUM** [BASIS: CP-KCS voting trust approved (May 2021) versus CN-KCS voting trust rejected (August 2021); critical data gaps on Continental's railroad holdings and debt structure].

**Rule:** Under 49 U.S.C. § 11323(c), the STB may approve use of a voting trust "if consistent with the public interest."³⁶ The STB's voting trust policy is codified at 49 C.F.R. Part 1013, which requires: (1) trustee independence from the acquiring carrier, (2) irrevocability of the trust, (3) continuation of target's independent operations, and (4) financial isolation preventing acquirer's debt from threatening target's stability.³⁷ The STB evaluates voting trusts by analyzing competitive impact during the trust period, financial capacity of the acquirer, trustee independence, and whether the trust serves a legitimate business purpose beyond financial convenience.³⁸

**Explanation:** The STB's voting trust decisions in the Canadian Pacific-Kansas City Southern and Canadian National-Kansas City Southern competing bids established current precedent. In **CP-KCS (approved May 6, 2021)**, the STB found that placing KCS in a voting trust was consistent with the public interest because: (1) CP and KCS had minimal route overlap (~50 customers), minimizing competitive harm during the trust period; (2) CP demonstrated strong financial capacity with investment-grade credit ratings maintained post-acquisition; (3) CP appointed David Starling (former KCS CEO) as independent trustee with no financial ties to CP; and (4) the voting trust enabled CP to compete with CN's higher cash offer, benefiting KCS shareholders.³⁹ The approval was granted in just 31 days, reflecting the lack of controversy.⁴⁰

By contrast, in **CN-KCS (rejected August 31, 2021)**, the STB unanimously denied CN's proposed voting trust.⁴¹ The Board found that: (1) CN and KCS had substantial route overlap (~300 customers), creating competitive harm even during the trust period as shippers would anticipate future integration; (2) CN's acquisition would require $19 billion in additional debt, raising concerns about financial capacity to maintain infrastructure investment; (3) the Department of Justice filed comments objecting to the voting trust on competitive grounds, warning that single holding company control would distort shipper behavior; and (4) approval would essentially allow financial engineering before STB determined the merger was in the public interest.⁴² CN abandoned its KCS bid 12 days after the voting trust rejection.⁴³

**Application:** Here, the Continental-GPRC voting trust application presents a risk profile **between CP-KCS (approved) and CN-KCS (rejected)**:

**Factor 1: Competitive Overlap (Dispositive)**

The critical unknown is Continental's existing railroad holdings. The specialist report identifies this as a **critical data gap**: "Continental's existing railroad holdings not disclosed. If Continental owns other railroads with GPRC overlap, voting trust approval probability drops to 20-30%."⁴⁴

The fact registry does not resolve this data gap—Continental's railroad portfolio is not disclosed in the transaction materials analyzed.⁴⁵ **This uncertainty alone justifies the 55% base case approval probability.**

**Factor 2: Debt Structure and Financial Capacity**

Continental is identified as "PE-backed" (private equity) in the fact registry.⁴⁶ Private equity acquisitions typically involve substantial leverage to achieve target internal rates of return (IRRs). The CN-KCS rejection emphasized that $19 billion in debt threatened network stability.⁴⁷ If Continental's acquisition financing results in debt-to-EBITDA ratios exceeding 5.0×, the STB will scrutinize whether Continental has financial capacity to: (1) service debt without asset-stripping GPRC; (2) maintain $1.2 billion annual capital expenditure requirements for rail replacement and PTC compliance; and (3) fund the $2.88 billion deferred rail replacement backlog over 10 years.⁴⁸

The STB's concern is that private equity structures prioritize short-term financial returns over long-term infrastructure investment—precisely the concern that drove the CN-KCS rejection.⁴⁹ Continental must demonstrate debt service coverage ratios exceeding 2.0× and committed revolving credit facilities of $500 million or more to address operational flexibility concerns.

**Factor 3: Trustee Independence**

Continental must appoint a trustee with **complete independence** from Continental's management, private equity fund sponsors, and any affiliated transportation companies.⁵⁰ The CP-KCS precedent suggests that a retired Class I railroad CEO (e.g., former Union Pacific, BNSF, or Norfolk Southern executive) would satisfy independence requirements.⁵¹ An active Continental affiliate, private equity fund representative, or industry consultant with Continental ties would be **fatal** to voting trust approval.

**Factor 4: Shipper Opposition**

The pending TransAmerica rate case creates substantial voting trust opposition risk. If TransAmerica, agricultural shippers, and coal shippers file comments opposing the voting trust—arguing that placing GPRC under Continental's holding company control will chill investment, accelerate rate increases, and foreclose competition—the STB will delay or deny approval pending resolution of these concerns.⁵² The fact registry confirms 78% of rail shippers are captive to a single railroad, creating a motivated opposition constituency.⁵³

#### B.3 Rate Freeze and Captive Shipper Protections: 70% Probability, $280M-$560M NPV Revenue Impact

**Conclusion:** The STB will **very likely** (70% probability) impose rate freeze or rate moratorium conditions protecting captive shippers for 3-5 years post-merger. **Severity: HIGH**. This condition will prevent GPRC from increasing rates beyond the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for coal, agricultural, and chemical shippers lacking competitive transportation alternatives, resulting in $50M-$100M annual revenue loss and **$280M-$560M NPV impact** at 8% discount rate. **Confidence: MEDIUM** [BASIS: CP-KCS merger imposed unprecedented gateway and rate justification requirements; captive shipper statistics show 78% of rail shippers served by single railroad; TransAmerica rate case precedent risk].

**Rule:** Under the STB's enhanced competition standard, 49 C.F.R. § 1180.1(c) requires major merger applicants to propose conditions that "enhance competition" to offset potential harms, including "competitive access measures that ensure captive shippers have continued access to competitive rail alternatives."⁵⁴ The STB has authority under 49 U.S.C. § 11324(c) to impose "terms and conditions...necessary to carry out the transportation policy" when approving mergers.⁵⁵ Rate freeze conditions are a standard STB merger remedy to protect captive shippers from market power exploitation during the post-merger integration period.⁵⁶

**Explanation:** Captive shippers are those lacking economically viable alternatives to rail transportation—typically bulk commodity shippers (coal, grain, chemicals, aggregates) who cannot use trucks due to volume/distance economics and lack barge access.⁵⁷ According to Rail Customer Coalition data cited in the specialist report, **78% of rail shippers are served by only one railroad**, and **50% of rail revenue is generated from captive shippers** (up from 27% in 2004).⁵⁸ This concentration of market power is the **primary STB concern** in merger review under the enhanced competition standard.

In the **CP-KCS merger (2023)**, the STB imposed unprecedented rate protective conditions even though CP and KCS had minimal competitive overlap:⁵⁹

1. **Rate Increase Justification Requirement**: If CPKC increases rates to an affected gateway by more than CPI, CPKC must provide shippers with written justification within 15 days, enabling shippers to evaluate whether to file a maximum rate complaint under 49 U.S.C. § 10701.⁶⁰
2. **Gateway Routing Rights**: Shippers retain the right to route traffic via pre-merger gateways at pre-merger relative rates; CPKC cannot force shippers onto higher-priced single-line routes.⁶¹
3. **Seven-Year Oversight Period**: CPKC must file quarterly reports with the STB on gateway traffic volumes, rates, service performance, and shipper complaints—an unprecedented duration reflecting STB's commitment to monitoring post-merger rate behavior.⁶²

These CP-KCS conditions were imposed despite **minimal competitive overlap** and **geographic complementarity** (CP's Canada-U.S. network + KCS's U.S.-Mexico network created new single-line routes).⁶³ The STB's rationale was that enhanced competition requires **affirmative protections** for shippers, not merely absence of harm.

Prior major mergers also resulted in rate protections. The UP-SP merger (1996) included rate freezes for specified traffic lanes and coal shippers.⁶⁴ The CSX-NS acquisition of Conrail (1998) included reciprocal switching requirements and trackage rights to preserve competition.⁶⁵

**Application:** Here, GPRC's revenue composition creates **extreme captive shipper exposure** that will drive stringent STB rate protections:

**Coal Traffic (28% of revenue, $2.3 billion):** GPRC's coal shippers are predominantly Powder River Basin coal mines and Midwest/Plains coal-fired power plants.⁶⁶ These shippers are **highly captive** because: (1) coal's weight and volume make trucking uneconomical beyond 50-75 miles; (2) most coal mines lack barge access; (3) power plants receiving coal via rail have no alternative fuel supply chain without multi-year capital investments. The fact registry confirms coal traffic is declining at 5% annually due to utility plant retirements, creating stranded asset risk.⁶⁷ Remaining coal shippers will argue to the STB that Continental-GPRC merger further concentrates market power over a shrinking, captive customer base.

**Agricultural Traffic (18% of revenue, $1.48 billion):** Rural Great Plains grain elevators served by GPRC are **predominantly captive** because: (1) grain elevators are located on single railroad's tracks; (2) truck-competitive radius for grain is 100-150 miles to final destination (export terminal or processor); (3) barge access is limited to Mississippi River system, inaccessible to most Great Plains elevators. Agricultural shippers are highly organized through commodity groups and will file extensive comments opposing the merger unless Continental commits to rate protections.⁶⁸

**Chemical Traffic (12% of revenue, $984 million):** Chemical plants with rail sidings and tank car loading facilities lack truck-competitive alternatives for bulk liquid shipments.⁶⁹ Some chemical shippers have barge access via Gulf Coast or Mississippi River locations, but inland chemical plants are captive to rail.

**TransAmerica Rate Case Precedent Risk:**

The pending TransAmerica rate case (STB Docket FD 36788) alleges that GPRC charged $34.16/ton versus a maximum reasonable rate of $29.70/ton (180% of variable cost), resulting in $44.6 million in overcharges for 2021-2024.⁷⁰ If TransAmerica **prevails before the STB issues a merger decision**, it establishes that GPRC has **exercised market power** against captive shippers. This precedent will:

1. Trigger 25-30 copycat rate complaints from similarly situated coal and agricultural shippers, claiming $100M-$200M in additional overcharges⁷¹
2. Create a **presumption of competitive harm** from the Continental-GPRC merger (further consolidation of market power over captive shippers)
3. Increase rate freeze probability from 70% to **95%** and extend the freeze duration from 3-5 years to **5-7 years**
4. Support more onerous conditions such as reciprocal switching (allowing captive shippers to access competing railroads within a geographic radius) for 10-15 major shipper locations⁷²

#### B.4 Gateway Preservation Requirements: 80% Probability, $80M-$160M NPV Revenue Impact

**Conclusion:** The STB will **very likely** (80% probability) impose gateway preservation conditions requiring Continental-GPRC to maintain pre-merger gateway access and pricing for 5-7 years post-merger. **Severity: MEDIUM**. Gateway preservation prevents GPRC from forcing shippers onto higher-priced single-line routes, reducing GPRC's ability to capture the 10-15% single-line premium on $410M-$820M of affected traffic. The revenue impact is **$20M-$40M annually** with **$80M-$160M NPV** at 8% discount rate over a 5-year oversight period. **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: CP-KCS merger imposed unprecedented 7-year gateway preservation with rate justification requirements; industry data on single-line versus gateway pricing differential].

**Rule:** Under 49 U.S.C. § 10705(a), rail carriers must establish "just and reasonable" rates and cannot engage in unreasonable discrimination.⁷³ Gateway preservation conditions enforce this non-discrimination mandate by prohibiting merged railroads from discriminatory rate treatment of gateway-routed traffic (multi-railroad routing via interchange points) versus single-line traffic (origin-to-destination service by a single railroad).⁷⁴ The STB's authority to impose such conditions derives from 49 U.S.C. § 11324(c) and 49 C.F.R. § 1180.1(c) (requiring enhanced competition measures).⁷⁵

**Explanation:** A "gateway" is an interchange point where two railroads transfer traffic to enable multi-railroad routing.⁷⁶ Gateway preservation ensures shippers retain the ability to route shipments via multiple railroad combinations (e.g., GPRC to Kansas City gateway, then BNSF to final destination) rather than being forced onto a merged railroad's single-line route.⁷⁷ Gateways are critical for competition because they enable shippers to choose among multiple service options and prevent railroads from exercising market power.

In the **CP-KCS merger (March 15, 2023)**, the STB imposed unprecedented gateway protections that transformed merger conditions policy:⁷⁸

1. **Pre-Merger Gateway Rights**: Shippers retain the right to route traffic via pre-merger gateways (Kansas City, Chicago, New Orleans, etc.) at pre-merger relative rates. CPKC cannot force shippers onto single-line routes even if operationally more efficient for CPKC.⁷⁹

2. **Rate Increase Justification**: If CPKC increases rates to an affected gateway by more than CPI, CPKC must provide shippers with **written justification within 15 days**, explaining the cost basis for the increase and enabling shippers to evaluate whether the rate is challengeable under the maximum rate complaint procedures in 49 U.S.C. § 10701.⁸⁰

3. **Seven-Year Oversight Period**: CPKC must file quarterly reports with the STB on gateway traffic volumes, rates, service performance metrics (on-time delivery, car cycle times), shipper complaints, and gateway capacity investments. This **seven-year duration** is unprecedented—prior merger oversight periods were typically 3-5 years.⁸¹

4. **STB Enforcement**: The STB retained jurisdiction to modify or enhance gateway protections during the oversight period if CPKC's behavior demonstrates competitive harm.⁸²

The CP-KCS gateway conditions were imposed despite **geographic complementarity** and **minimal competitive overlap** between CP and KCS.⁸³ The STB's decision emphasized that enhanced competition requires affirmative measures to preserve shipper routing options, not merely absence of gateway closures.

**Application:** Here, GPRC operates in a network with **multiple critical gateways** that will require STB protection:

**Kansas City Gateway:** GPRC interchanges with BNSF, Union Pacific, Norfolk Southern, and CSX at Kansas City, handling agricultural traffic (Great Plains grain to Gulf Coast export terminals), intermodal traffic (West Coast to Midwest destinations), and chemical traffic (Gulf Coast to Upper Midwest).⁸⁴ Kansas City is the most important Midwest-South gateway and will receive the **highest STB scrutiny**.

**Chicago Gateway:** GPRC interchanges with all Class I railroads at Chicago, the nation's largest rail hub. Chicago handles GPRC's intermodal traffic (West Coast imports to East Coast/Midwest destinations) and is the eastern gateway for GPRC's network.⁸⁵ Shipper sensitivity to Chicago rate increases is **extreme** because intermodal traffic is truck-competitive, and even modest rate increases cause mode shift to over-the-road trucking.

**Denver Gateway:** GPRC interchanges with Union Pacific at Denver for Rocky Mountain region traffic, including coal from Western coal fields and aggregates for construction.⁸⁶

**Houston Gateway:** GPRC interchanges with Union Pacific and BNSF at Houston for Gulf Coast chemical traffic and petroleum products. Houston is also a critical export gateway for agricultural traffic.⁸⁷

**Minneapolis Gateway:** GPRC interchanges with Canadian Pacific Kansas City and Canadian National at Minneapolis for northern agricultural traffic and intermodal traffic to/from Canada.⁸⁸

Post-merger, Continental-GPRC will have **strong economic incentives** to convert gateway-routed traffic to single-line routes because:

1. **Single-line revenue retention**: On gateway-routed traffic, GPRC must split revenue with the connecting railroad. Single-line traffic generates 100% revenue retention.

2. **Single-line premium pricing**: Industry data shows that single-line rates are typically **10-15% higher** than gateway-routed rates for comparable traffic because shippers value the operational simplicity and service reliability of single-line service.⁸⁹

3. **Operational efficiency**: Single-line routes avoid interchange delays, car handoff errors, and coordination costs with other railroads.

If Continental-GPRC successfully converts gateway-routed traffic to single-line routes through discriminatory pricing (increasing gateway rates while holding single-line rates constant or reducing them modestly), GPRC could capture substantial additional revenue. #### B.5 Labor Protective Provisions: Mandatory $35M-$70M Exposure Over 6 Years

**Conclusion:** Federal law **mandates** that the STB impose labor protective conditions on the Continental-GPRC merger under 49 U.S.C. § 11326. **Severity: MEDIUM**. The standard "New York Dock" conditions will provide up to 6 years of income protection for displaced employees, relocation allowances, and seniority protections. Based on precedent major mergers (UP-SP, CSX-Conrail), GPRC will likely eliminate 300-500 positions through route rationalization, yard closures, and dispatching consolidation, resulting in **$35M-$70M in labor protective costs** (base case). If Continental owns other railroads requiring seniority integration, costs could reach $70M-$120M. **Confidence: HIGH** [BASIS: Statutory certainty of 49 U.S.C. § 11326; historical labor protective costs in UP-SP and CSX-Conrail mergers; New York Dock dismissal allowance formulas].

**Rule:** Section 11326(a) of Title 49 mandates that the STB impose labor protective conditions providing "a fair arrangement at least as protective of the interests of employees...as the terms imposed under section 5(2)(f) of the Interstate Commerce Act before February 5, 1976."⁹⁰ This statutory reference incorporates the **"New York Dock" conditions** established by the ICC in 1979, which have become the standard labor protective framework for railroad mergers.⁹¹ The New York Dock conditions provide:

| Protection | Standard Duration | Payment Calculation |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Income Protection** | Up to 6 years | 100% of prior compensation for dismissed/displaced employees |
| **Relocation Allowances** | As incurred | Full reimbursement for moving costs if required to relocate |
| **Home Sale Protection** | As incurred | Compensation for losses from forced home sale due to relocation |
| **Seniority Rights** | 4 years minimum | Employees not placed in worse position related to employment |
| **Arbitration** | Ongoing | Expedited binding arbitration for disputes (Section 4 of New York Dock) |

**Explanation:** The New York Dock conditions were adopted by the ICC in Finance Docket No. 28250 (1979) and upheld by the Second Circuit in *New York Dock Railway v. United States*, 609 F.2d 83 (2d Cir. 1979).⁹² The conditions establish comprehensive protections for railroad employees affected by consolidations, including:

**Dismissal Allowances:** Employees dismissed as a result of merger-related operational changes receive monthly displacement allowances equal to their prior monthly compensation, for up to 6 years (reduced by earnings from other employment).⁹³ The allowances are paid even if the employee finds other non-railroad employment, though offset by those earnings. This provides a financial bridge for employees who lose railroad jobs requiring specialized skills and seniority-based compensation.

**Relocation Protections:** Employees required to change residence to retain employment receive: (1) reimbursement for reasonable moving expenses; (2) 3 years of moving expense allowances for family moves related to relocation; (3) compensation for losses on home sales forced by relocation; and (4) continuation of mortgage interest differential if relocated employee must accept higher interest rate mortgage.⁹⁴

**Seniority Integration:** If the merger results in operational consolidation requiring integration of seniority rosters (e.g., engineers from GPRC and Continental's other railroads combined into a single system-wide roster), disputes are resolved through Section 3 arbitration.⁹⁵ Seniority disputes can add $15M-$50M to labor protective costs through arbitration expenses, make-whole remedies for employees disadvantaged by roster integration, and operational disruptions during transition.

**Prior Major Merger Precedents:**

- **Union Pacific/Southern Pacific (1996)**: Resulted in 200-500 job eliminations, with labor protective costs of $25M-$75M (adjusted for inflation: $45M-$135M in 2026 dollars).⁹⁶
- **CSX/Norfolk Southern Conrail acquisition (1998)**: Resulted in 300-600 job eliminations, with labor protective costs of $35M-$85M (adjusted for inflation: $60M-$145M in 2026 dollars).⁹⁷
