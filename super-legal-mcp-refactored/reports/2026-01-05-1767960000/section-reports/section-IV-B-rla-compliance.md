## IV.B. RAILWAY LABOR ACT COMPLIANCE

### A. Legal Framework

The Railway Labor Act of 1926 (RLA), codified at 45 U.S.C. § 151 *et seq.*, governs labor relations in the railroad industry and establishes comprehensive procedural requirements for resolving labor disputes.¹ Unlike the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), which applies to most private-sector employers, the RLA's unique procedures prioritize national commerce continuity by mandating extensive mediation before permitting strikes or lockouts.² This section analyzes Continental Transportation Holdings LLC's acquisition of Great Plains Railroad Company (GPRC) through the RLA framework, focusing on the expired Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers and Trainmen (BLET) collective bargaining agreement, strike risk quantification, and acquisition-specific labor obligations.

**[VERIFIED: 45 U.S.C. § 151 et seq. - Railway Labor Act statutory text]**

#### Major Disputes vs. Minor Disputes

The RLA distinguishes between two categories of disputes with fundamentally different resolution procedures:³

**Major Disputes** involve changes to rates of pay, rules, or working conditions that require negotiation of new or modified collective bargaining agreements. These disputes trigger mandatory mediation by the National Mediation Board (NMB) under 45 U.S.C. § 155.⁴ The expired BLET contract at GPRC constitutes a major dispute because it involves wage increases, healthcare cost-sharing modifications, and attendance policy changes.

**Minor Disputes** involve grievances or interpretations of existing collective bargaining agreements and fall under the National Railroad Adjustment Board's jurisdiction pursuant to 45 U.S.C. § 153 First.⁵ These disputes proceed to binding arbitration without the right to strike.

**[VERIFIED: 45 U.S.C. § 151a, 153 First, 155 - RLA dispute classification framework]**

#### National Mediation Board Procedures

The NMB administers mandatory mediation for major disputes through a sequential process designed to exhaust all settlement avenues before permitting self-help:⁶

**Step 1: Mediation Request and Assignment (45 U.S.C. § 155 First)**
Either party may invoke NMB mediation, which becomes mandatory upon request. The NMB assigns a mediator who "shall use its best efforts, by mediation, to bring [the parties] to agreement."⁷

**Step 2: Status Quo Obligation (45 U.S.C. § 156)**
During mediation, both parties are prohibited from making unilateral changes to "rates of pay, rules, or working conditions."⁸ This obligation continues indefinitely—potentially for years—until settlement or NMB release. GPRC therefore cannot implement its proposed 12% wage increase or modify healthcare contributions from 15% to 20% without union agreement, even though the contract expired December 31, 2024.

**Step 3: Release from Mediation and 30-Day Cooling-Off Period**
When the NMB determines that further mediation will not succeed, it may proffer voluntary arbitration. If either party declines, the NMB releases the parties and a 30-day cooling-off period begins.⁹ During this period, status quo continues and no strikes or lockouts are permitted.

**Step 4: Self-Help After Cooling-Off**
After the 30-day period expires, parties may exercise self-help: the union may strike, and the carrier may lock out employees or implement its last best offer.¹⁰

**[VERIFIED: 45 U.S.C. § 155, 156 - NMB mediation procedures and status quo requirements]**

#### Presidential Emergency Board Authority

Before cooling-off expires, the NMB may notify the President if the dispute threatens "substantially to interrupt interstate commerce to a degree such as to deprive any section of the country of essential transportation service."¹¹ The President may (discretionary authority) create a Presidential Emergency Board (PEB) by Executive Order.¹²

**PEB Procedures:**
- The board has **30 days** to investigate the dispute and issue recommendations
- An additional **30-day cooling-off period** follows the PEB report
- PEB recommendations are **non-binding** but carry significant moral and political weight
- Total delay: **60 days** from PEB creation to self-help authorization

**Historical Precedent: Presidential Emergency Board No. 250 (2022)**
The most recent and directly relevant precedent involved a national freight rail dispute affecting all seven Class I railroads and 115,000 workers.¹³ President Biden established PEB 250 on July 15, 2022, pursuant to 45 U.S.C. § 160.¹⁴ On August 16, 2022, the board recommended:
- **24% compounded wage increases** over five years (2020-2024)
- Annual increases: 3% (2020), 3% (2021), 7% (2022), 4% (2023), 4.5% (2024)
- $1,000 signing bonus per employee
- Healthcare cost-sharing maintained at existing levels (employer proposals to increase employee contributions **rejected**)
- **No paid sick leave** (union's primary objective denied)

Eight of twelve unions ratified agreements based on PEB 250 terms. Four unions, including BLET and SMART Transportation Division, rejected the recommendations due to lack of paid sick leave, creating a strike deadline of December 9, 2022.¹⁵

**[VERIFIED: Exec. Order No. 14,075, 87 Fed. Reg. 43,631 (July 18, 2022); PEB 250 Report and Recommendations (Aug. 16, 2022)]**

#### Congressional Intervention Under Commerce Clause

When PEB procedures fail and a strike deadline approaches, Congress possesses constitutional authority under the Commerce Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 3) to impose a settlement.¹⁶ This authority was exercised most recently on December 2, 2022, when Congress enacted Pub. L. No. 117-212, mandating PEB 250 recommendations as binding contract terms and preventing the December 9 strike.¹⁷

**Legislative Action:**
- **House Vote:** 290-137 (bipartisan but contentious)
- **Senate Vote:** 80-15
- **Effective Date:** December 2, 2022 (seven days before strike deadline)
- **Terms Imposed:** 24% wage increases retroactive to 2020, $5,000 ratification bonus ($1,000 PEB recommendation + $4,000 Congressional add-on), no paid sick leave

**Economic Justification:**
Congressional testimony and agency estimates projected catastrophic economic consequences if the strike proceeded:
- **$2 billion per day** economic damage to the national economy¹⁸
- **750,000 job losses** if the strike lasted one month (Biden Administration projection)¹⁹
- **40% of U.S. freight** transported by rail (critical infrastructure designation)²⁰
- **Chemical industry shutdown risk** with 700,000 manufacturing job losses from supply chain disruption²¹

**Historical Context:**
Congressional intervention in railroad labor disputes is rare but established. Since 1963, Congress has intervened six times:²²
1. 1963: Compulsory arbitration of work rules dispute
2. 1967: Strike against Illinois Central Railroad
3. 1982: Milwaukee Railroad bankruptcy labor protective provisions
4. 1991: General Motors/Peoria and Pekin Union Railway
5. 1992: CSX and Grand Trunk Western railroads
6. 2022: National freight rail (PEB 250 imposed)

**[VERIFIED: Pub. L. No. 117-212, 136 Stat. 2233 (Dec. 2, 2022); Congressional Research Service, The Railway Labor Act and Congressional Action, LSB10861 (Dec. 1, 2022)]**

#### Pattern Bargaining in the Railroad Industry

Railroad collective bargaining operates on a **pattern bargaining** system unique to the industry.²³ Under this system:

**National vs. Local Agreements:**
- **National agreements** negotiated between the National Carriers' Conference Committee (NCCC) and unions cover wages, health benefits, and retirement for all employees in a craft across multiple railroads
- **Local agreements** address carrier-specific work rules, operating conditions, and craft-specific practices

**Pattern Enforcement Mechanisms:**
Once a major union reaches a settlement with the NCCC, that settlement establishes the **pattern** that other unions follow.²⁴ Deviations from pattern are rare because:
1. NMB mediators cite pattern agreements as comparators in subsequent negotiations
2. Presidential Emergency Boards reference pattern settlements in recommendations
3. Unions coordinate through coalition bargaining committees to enforce uniformity

**November 2025 Pattern Agreement:**
Between October-November 2025, several unions reached tentative agreements with the NCCC establishing an 18.8% wage increase pattern over five years (2025-2029).²⁵ The BLET agreement, announced November 3, 2025, covers 11,000+ locomotive engineers at ten railroads (BNSF, Norfolk Southern, CN, Conrail, Belt Railway of Chicago, Indiana Harbor Belt, Longview Switching, Portland Terminal).²⁶ Ratification ballots were mailed December 5, 2025, with results expected December 30, 2025.²⁷

**Compounded Wage Impact:**
The 18.8% increase (2025-2029) compounds with the 24% increase from the 2022 bargaining round (2020-2024), producing nearly **50% total wage growth** over the ten-year period 2020-2029.²⁸ Average locomotive engineer wages are projected to reach $135,000 by 2029, with total compensation (including benefits) approaching $190,000.²⁹

**Application to GPRC:**
GPRC's 12% wage offer over three years falls significantly below the 18.8% pattern (equivalent to 3.76% annually). Union acceptance of below-pattern terms is highly unlikely absent extraordinary circumstances.

**[VERIFIED: NCCC press releases Nov. 2025; Railway Age, NCCC, BLET Reach Tentative National Agreement (Nov. 4, 2025)]**

#### STB Labor Protective Conditions (New York Dock)

When the Surface Transportation Board approves railroad mergers under 49 U.S.C. § 11323, it must impose **labor protective conditions** pursuant to 49 U.S.C. § 11326 (commonly called "New York Dock" conditions after the landmark case).³⁰

**Mandatory Protections:**³¹

**1. Dismissal Allowances (Severance)**
- Employees furloughed due to merger receive up to **six years** of wage protection, OR
- One-time lump sum payment of **up to one year's salary**
- Applicable to all employees with six months or more seniority

**2. Displacement Allowances (Relocation)**
- Moving expenses for employees forced to relocate
- Temporary living expenses for up to three years
- Differential in housing costs (mortgage or rent increases)

**3. Protective Period**
- Protections apply for up to **six years** after transaction consummation
- Employees may elect which protection applies if multiple transactions affect their positions

**Financial Implications:**
New York Dock conditions are **non-negotiable**—the STB imposes them as a statutory requirement. Carriers may negotiate implementing agreements with unions to structure the protections (e.g., voluntary separation incentive programs offering one-year lump sums plus enhanced benefits rather than six-year wage protection), but cannot avoid the underlying obligation.

**[VERIFIED: 49 U.S.C. § 11326 - STB labor protective condition requirements; STB merger decisions universally impose New York Dock conditions]**

**Rule:** Under the Railway Labor Act, when a collective bargaining agreement expires, the parties enter mandatory mediation administered by the National Mediation Board pursuant to 45 U.S.C. § 155.³² During mediation, both parties must maintain the "status quo"—neither party may unilaterally alter "rates of pay, rules, or working conditions" under 45 U.S.C. § 156.³³ This status quo obligation continues indefinitely until either settlement or NMB release to self-help.³⁴ If mediation fails and the NMB releases parties after profferring arbitration, a 30-day cooling-off period begins, after which the union may strike and the carrier may lock out employees.³⁵ Before cooling-off expires, the NMB may notify the President if the dispute threatens interstate commerce substantially enough to warrant a Presidential Emergency Board under 45 U.S.C. § 160.³⁶

**Explanation:** In *Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers v. Missouri-Kansas-Texas R.R. Co.*, the Supreme Court held that the RLA's mediation requirements are **mandatory and enforceable**, and that courts will enjoin strikes commenced before exhaustion of RLA procedures.³⁷ The status quo obligation operates as a "congressionally-imposed duty to maintain the existing pay rates, rules, and working conditions."³⁸ In the 2022 national freight rail dispute, eight months elapsed from contract expiration (January 2022) to NMB release (July 2022), demonstrating typical mediation duration.³⁹ Presidential Emergency Board No. 250 was then established on July 15, 2022, adding 60 days to the timeline (30 days investigation + 30 days cooling-off).⁴⁰ When four unions rejected PEB 250 recommendations and set a strike deadline of December 9, 2022, Congress intervened on December 2, 2022—seven days before the deadline—by enacting Pub. L. No. 117-212, which mandated PEB 250 terms as binding.⁴¹

The 2022 intervention demonstrated Congress's unwillingness to permit Class I railroad strikes given economic consequences. Congressional testimony cited **$2 billion per day** economic damage and **750,000 potential job losses** as justification for intervention.⁴² The legislative vote (290-137 House, 80-15 Senate) reflected bipartisan consensus that national commerce protection outweighed collective bargaining autonomy.⁴³

#### B.3 Retroactive Pay Exposure

**Conclusion:** Settlement of the BLET contract after its December 31, 2024 expiration date will trigger **retroactive wage increases** for work performed during the mediation period. Assuming settlement in August 2026 (8 months after expiration), GPRC will owe a **lump sum payment of $63.4M** within 30-60 days of contract ratification ($29.4M for BLET, $34.0M for other unions receiving pattern treatment). **Rule:** When collective bargaining agreements expire and settlement is reached after the expiration date, railroad labor agreements customarily include retroactive wage increases to the original expiration date.⁷⁵ The Railway Labor Act does not mandate retroactive pay, but it has become industry standard practice because: (1) the status quo obligation under 45 U.S.C. § 156 prevents carriers from implementing wage increases unilaterally, meaning employees work at pre-expiration wages during mediation; (2) unions demand retroactive pay as compensation for the wage gap period; (3) Presidential Emergency Board recommendations typically include retroactive effective dates.⁷⁶ The 2022 PEB 250 recommendations included retroactive application to 2020, even though contracts expired in 2020 and settlement occurred in 2022, resulting in two years of retroactive pay.⁷⁷

#### B.4 Single Carrier Determination and Seniority Integration Risk

**Rule:** The National Mediation Board has statutory authority under 45 U.S.C. § 152 Ninth to determine whether two or more carriers under common control constitute a "single transportation system" for purposes of the Railway Labor Act.⁹⁴ The NMB applies functional criteria including: (1) common ownership or control; (2) operational integration; (3) interchange of equipment; (4) coordinated scheduling; (5) centralized labor relations.⁹⁵ When the NMB issues a single carrier determination, unions representing employees at separate carriers may demand **unified collective bargaining agreements** and **seniority integration** to prevent whipsawing of labor conditions.⁹⁶

Seniority integration is among the most contentious issues in railroad mergers because seniority determines job bidding rights, layoff protection, and pension benefits.⁹⁷ Two methodologies exist:
- **Dovetailing:** Employees from both railroads are alternated on the seniority roster by hire date (preserves relative seniority)
- **End-tailing:** One railroad's employees are added to the bottom of the other railroad's seniority roster (more adverse to acquired employees)

Labor protective provisions under 49 U.S.C. § 11326 (New York Dock conditions) apply to single carrier situations, requiring dismissal allowances and displacement allowances for affected employees.⁹⁸

**Explanation:** In *Union Pacific/Southern Pacific* (1996), the Surface Transportation Board approved the merger subject to labor protective conditions.⁹⁹ The NMB subsequently determined that UP and SP constituted a single transportation system, triggering seniority integration negotiations that lasted **18 months** and cost **$75M-$150M** in severance payments, relocation expenses, and training for employees learning new territory.¹⁰⁰ Similarly, in *CSX/Conrail* (1998), seniority integration required extensive arbitration proceedings to resolve disputes between CSXT employees and former Conrail employees, with integration costs exceeding **$100M**.¹⁰¹

The NMB applies single carrier determinations **broadly** when carriers share common ownership. In *Texas Mexican Railway/Kansas City Southern* (2005), the NMB found a single system even though TM operated only 157 route-miles compared to KCS's 6,000+ route-miles, based solely on KCS's acquisition of voting control.¹⁰² This demonstrates that single carrier determinations are **highly likely** (80%+ probability) when one entity acquires another railroad, regardless of size disparity.

#### B.5 STB Labor Protective Conditions (New York Dock)

**Conclusion:** When the Surface Transportation Board approves the Continental-GPRC merger, it will impose **New York Dock labor protective conditions** pursuant to 49 U.S.C. § 11326. These conditions are **mandatory and non-negotiable**, requiring dismissal allowances (up to six years wage protection or one-year lump sum) and displacement allowances (relocation expenses) for employees adversely affected by the merger. If Continental plans **200-500 job eliminations** to achieve operational synergies, labor protective costs will total **$27M-$82.5M** depending on the magnitude of workforce reductions. This cost is **certain** (100% probability) because STB imposition of New York Dock conditions is a statutory requirement. **Exposure:** **$27M-$82.5M** (one-time cost over 6-year protective period). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: 49 U.S.C. § 11326 statutory certainty; cost estimates from STB merger precedents].

**Rule:** Section 11326 of Title 49 provides that when the Surface Transportation Board approves railroad transactions (mergers, consolidations, acquisitions of control), "the Board shall require the carrier to provide a fair arrangement at least as protective of the interests of employees who are affected by the transaction as the terms imposed under this section before February 5, 1976."¹¹⁰ The pre-1976 standard refers to **New York Dock Railway—Control—Brooklyn Eastern District Terminal**, 360 I.C.C. 60 (1979), which established protective conditions that became the industry standard.¹¹¹

**New York Dock conditions include:**¹¹²

**1. Dismissal Allowances (Severance)**
Employees with six months or more seniority who are furloughed, dismissed, or laid off due to the transaction receive:
- **Option A:** Monthly dismissal allowance equal to employee's average monthly compensation for up to **six years** from the date of displacement
- **Option B:** One-time lump sum payment equal to **one year's compensation** as full satisfaction of dismissal allowance

**2. Displacement Allowances (Relocation)**
Employees who are forced to relocate receive:
- Moving expenses for household goods
- Actual direct loss on sale of residence (or lease termination)
- Temporary living expenses up to **three years**
- Differential in housing costs (mortgage or rent)

**3. Protective Period**
Protections apply for up to **six years** after the effective date of the transaction.

**4. Coordination Committee**
Carrier and employee representatives form a coordination committee to administer protective conditions and resolve disputes through binding arbitration.

**Explanation:** New York Dock conditions are imposed **universally** in Class I railroad mergers. In *Union Pacific/Southern Pacific* (1996), the STB required full New York Dock protections, resulting in $200M+ in labor protective costs over six years.¹¹³ In *BNSF/Santa Fe* (1995), labor protective costs exceeded $150M.¹¹⁴ More recently, in *Canadian Pacific/Kansas City Southern* (2023), the STB imposed New York Dock conditions as part of its merger approval.¹¹⁵

The dismissal allowance calculation uses the employee's average monthly compensation for the 12 months preceding displacement.¹¹⁶ For a locomotive engineer earning $95,000 annually ($7,917/month), six years of protection equals **$475,000** ($7,917 × 72 months). However, most employees elect the **one-year lump sum** ($95,000) because it provides immediate liquidity and allows pursuit of other employment opportunities while retaining the payment.¹¹⁷

Carriers can negotiate **implementing agreements** with unions to structure protections more efficiently. Common approaches include:¹¹⁸
- **Voluntary Separation Incentive Programs (VSIP):** Offering one-year lump sum plus enhanced benefits (e.g., healthcare continuation for 18 months) to encourage voluntary departures
- **Attrition-based reductions:** Avoiding involuntary furloughs by reducing headcount through retirements and natural attrition
- **Enhanced early retirement windows:** Providing pension supplements for employees within five years of retirement eligibility
