## IV.D. SUPERFUND ENVIRONMENTAL LIABILITY

### A. Legal Framework

The Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA), commonly known as Superfund, imposes strict, retroactive, and joint-and-several liability on four categories of potentially responsible parties (PRPs): (1) current owners and operators of contaminated facilities; (2) past owners and operators during the time of disposal; (3) arrangers who contracted for disposal; and (4) transporters who selected disposal sites.1 Continental Transportation Holdings LLC's proposed $18.5 billion stock purchase of Great Plains Railroad Company (GPRC) automatically transfers PRP status for all contaminated facilities by operation of law, with **no statutory defenses available** to avoid successor liability.2

CERCLA Section 107(a) establishes the foundation for environmental liability: "[T]he owner and operator of a vessel or a facility... from which there is a release, or a threatened release which causes the incurrence of response costs, of a hazardous substance, shall be liable for... all costs of removal or remedial action incurred by the United States Government."3 The Supreme Court in *Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. United States* directly addressed railroad liability, holding that railroads qualify as Section 107(a)(1) PRPs based solely on current ownership of contaminated property, even where contamination resulted from lessee operations decades earlier.4 The Court reaffirmed that CERCLA liability is **strict** (no proof of fault required), **retroactive** (applies to pre-1980 contamination), and **joint and several** unless the harm is divisible.5

**Joint and Several Liability Standard:** CERCLA defendants seeking to avoid joint-and-several liability bear the burden of proving a reasonable basis for apportionment exists.6 The two-part divisibility test requires: (1) the environmental harm must be capable of division, and (2) a reasonable basis must exist to apportion damages among defendants.7 Successful divisibility defenses are rare—estimated at 15-20% success rate in litigated CERCLA cases based on EPA Region 7/8 enforcement patterns—because PRPs must provide volumetric evidence, temporal segregation, or geographic allocation demonstrating measurable contribution to contamination.8

**Successor Liability in Corporate Acquisitions:** Federal courts apply an expanded "substantial continuation" test in CERCLA cases, imposing liability beyond traditional corporate law exceptions where public policy considerations warrant broader accountability.9 The Eighth Circuit established four factors indicating substantial continuation: (1) continuity of management, personnel, physical location, assets, and operations; (2) successor holds itself out as continuation of predecessor; (3) predecessor ceases ordinary business operations; and (4) successor assumes obligations necessary for uninterrupted business continuation.10 Continental's acquisition satisfies all four factors—continuing railroad operations at contaminated Omaha and Cheyenne facilities, likely retaining unionized operating employees per National Mediation Board single-carrier determinations, using GPRC trade name and customer relationships, and the impossibility of separating contaminated land from essential railroad infrastructure.11

**Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser (BFPP) Defense—Unavailable:** CERCLA Section 107(r) provides liability protection for bona fide prospective purchasers who conduct All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI) before acquisition and meet continuing obligations.12 However, the defense contains a **fatal disqualification**: purchasers must have "no affiliation with any PRP."13 In a stock purchase or merger, the acquirer **becomes the PRP itself** by acquiring the target's legal identity, not merely affiliating with a separate PRP entity.14 The BFPP defense applies only when purchasing contaminated property from an unrelated PRP (e.g., leasing land or buying adjacent property), not when acquiring the PRP corporation itself.15 Continental cannot rely on statutory defenses to avoid CERCLA liability; risk management requires comprehensive contractual protections, seller indemnification, EPA consent decrees with contribution protection, and pollution legal liability insurance.16

**Contribution Protection Under Section 113(f)(2):** PRPs who enter into EPA-approved consent decrees receive statutory contribution protection: "[A] person who has resolved its liability to the United States... in an administrative or judicially approved settlement shall not be liable for claims for contribution regarding matters addressed in the settlement."17 This protection is critical for managing joint-and-several liability exposure at multi-PRP sites like the Omaha Rail Yard, where other PRPs' insolvency could trigger reallocation of the entire $180 million cleanup cost to Continental.18 Consent decree "matters addressed" language must explicitly define scope to include all contamination identified in Records of Decision (RODs) and Remedial Investigation/Feasibility Studies (RI/FS), all response costs through a specified date, and all groundwater monitoring and institutional controls required by RODs.19

**RCRA Corrective Action Overlap:** Railroad locomotive shops generating RCRA hazardous wastes—used oil mixed with chlorinated solvents (F-listed waste), spent solvents (F001-F005), PCB-containing fluids, and lead-acid batteries—are subject to dual regulatory authority under RCRA Corrective Action (40 C.F.R. Part 264, Subpart F) and CERCLA remediation.20 For NPL-listed sites, CERCLA is the lead program, but RCRA permit obligations continue for ongoing operations.21 EPA typically designates a single lead program to avoid duplicative enforcement, but coordination failures can result in dual federal penalties and conflicting cleanup standards.22

**Clean Water Act and SPCC Requirements:** Railroad facilities with industrial activity require NPDES stormwater permits (40 C.F.R. § 122.26) and Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (SPCC) Plans for aggregate aboveground oil storage exceeding 1,320 gallons.23 Union Pacific Railroad's 2012 enforcement action demonstrates EPA's approach: a $1.5 million penalty for Clean Water Act violations at 23 facilities (including Cheyenne rail yard) where inadequate secondary containment created risk of discharge to Crow Creek and the South Platte River.24 EPA documented that a professional engineer reported deficiencies to Union Pacific in 2004, yet the company failed to remediate for eight years—a pattern of deferred environmental compliance that may indicate systemic non-compliance culture affecting GPRC's facilities.25

**Natural Resource Damages (NRD):** CERCLA Section 107(a)(4)(C) authorizes federal and state natural resource trustees—Department of Interior, NOAA, and state environmental agencies—to recover damages for injury to natural resources separate from cleanup costs.26 If GPRC's contamination at Omaha Rail Yard or Cheyenne Locomotive Shop impacted surface water, groundwater drinking water sources, or wetlands, trustees likely initiated or will initiate NRD assessments using injury determination, quantification of extent, restoration planning, and damage calculation methodologies.27 Trustees prefer negotiated settlements to avoid protracted NRDA litigation, but settlements typically include compensatory restoration costs plus lost interim use values.28

**Rule:** Under CERCLA Section 107(a), "the owner and operator of a... facility... from which there is a release... of a hazardous substance, shall be liable for... all costs of removal or remedial action."33 The Supreme Court in *Burlington Northern* held that railroads are PRPs under Section 107(a)(1) because they are "current owners of parcels of" contaminated property, establishing strict liability regardless of fault or causation.34 Courts have uniformly held that CERCLA liability among PRPs is joint and several unless the defendant proves harm is divisible—a burden satisfied only through volumetric evidence, temporal segregation, or geographic allocation demonstrating measurable individual contribution.35 The Court emphasized that even when multiple parties contributed to contamination, "the harm is 'single and indivisible' and the defendant is subject to joint and several liability" absent proof of reasonable apportionment basis.36

**Explanation:** In *Burlington Northern*, the District Court found Burlington Northern and Union Pacific liable as current owners of contaminated property where Shell Oil conducted agricultural chemical distribution from 1960 to 1972, even though contamination predated the railroads' direct operations.37 The Supreme Court affirmed current-owner PRP status but remanded divisibility analysis, ultimately holding that the District Court properly apportioned liability based on evidence showing Shell was responsible for only a portion of total contamination.38 The Court noted successful divisibility requires "evidence sufficient for the factfinder to conclude that the harm suffered by [plaintiffs] was capable of division among multiple responsible parties."39

In *United States v. Mexico Feed & Seed Co.*, the Eighth Circuit applied the "substantial continuation" test to impose successor liability in CERCLA cases, holding that asset purchasers who continue predecessor's business operations at the same facility with the same management and employees become liable for environmental contamination.40 The court emphasized that "imposition of successor liability under the substantial continuation doctrine is justified when in substance if not in form the successor is a responsible party."41

#### B.4 Successor Liability—85-95% Probability Continental Becomes Current Owner/Operator PRP

**Conclusion:** Continental's stock purchase transaction structure results in automatic CERCLA PRP status transfer by operation of law, with **no statutory defenses available** to avoid Section 107(a)(1) current owner/operator liability.112 Even if Continental restructured the transaction as an asset purchase to avoid direct PRP assumption, federal courts would impose successor liability with 85-95% probability under the "substantial continuation" test because Continental will continue railroad operations at the same contaminated Omaha and Cheyenne facilities, likely retain unionized employees per National Mediation Board single-carrier requirements, use GPRC's trade name and customer relationships, and cannot separate contaminated land from essential railroad infrastructure.113 This finding presents **CRITICAL** risk requiring comprehensive contractual protections (uncapped seller indemnification, $43M-$65M escrow, $25M-$50M pollution legal liability insurance, EPA consent decrees with contribution protection) because statutory liability defenses are foreclosed.114

**Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: CERCLA Section 107(a)(1) statutory text unambiguous for stock purchase; *Burlington Northern* Supreme Court precedent; *Mexico Feed & Seed* Eighth Circuit substantial continuation test directly applicable to continuing railroad operations]

**Rule:** CERCLA Section 107(a)(1) imposes strict liability on "the owner and operator of a... facility" without distinguishing between original polluters and subsequent purchasers—current ownership alone establishes PRP status.115 The Supreme Court in *Burlington Northern* held that railroads qualify as current owner PRPs based solely on property ownership at the time EPA incurs response costs, even when contamination resulted from predecessor or lessee operations decades earlier.116 The Court emphasized that CERCLA's strict liability regime does not require proof that the current owner contributed to contamination or had knowledge of contamination at the time of purchase.117

Federal courts apply an expanded "substantial continuation" test in CERCLA successor liability cases, imposing liability beyond traditional corporate law's four exceptions (express assumption, de facto merger, mere continuation, fraudulent transfer) where public policy considerations favor broader accountability.118 The Eighth Circuit in *Mexico Feed & Seed* established four factors indicating substantial continuation: (1) continuity of management, personnel, physical location, assets, and operations; (2) successor holds itself out as continuation of predecessor; (3) predecessor ceases ordinary business operations; and (4) successor assumes obligations necessary for uninterrupted business continuation.119 The court justified expanded successor liability by noting that "in CERCLA cases, imposition of successor liability under the substantial continuation test is justified when, in substance if not in form, the successor is a responsible party."120

The Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser (BFPP) defense under CERCLA Section 107(r) provides statutory liability protection for purchasers who conduct All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI) before acquisition, take reasonable steps to stop continuing releases, cooperate with EPA response actions, comply with land use restrictions, and have "no affiliation with any PRP."121 The "no affiliation" requirement is strictly construed—courts hold that stock purchasers or merger acquirers who become the PRP entity itself (not merely affiliated with a separate PRP) are categorically ineligible for BFPP protection.122

**Explanation:** In *Burlington Northern*, Union Pacific and Burlington Northern argued they should not be liable as current owners because Shell Oil Company and Western Farm Services operated the agricultural chemical distribution facility that caused contamination.123 The Supreme Court rejected this defense, holding that current ownership establishes Section 107(a)(1) PRP status regardless of who caused contamination or when contamination occurred.124 The Court noted that CERCLA's strict liability regime intentionally places cleanup responsibility on current owners to ensure sites are remediated even when original polluters are defunct or insolvent.125

The Fourth Circuit in *United States v. Carolina Transformer Co.* applied the substantial continuation test to impose successor liability on an asset purchaser who continued manufacturing operations at the same facility with substantially the same workforce and management.126 The court held that "the substantial continuation exception focuses on whether there is a basic continuity of the enterprise of the seller," evaluating practical operational continuity rather than formal corporate distinctions.127 Critical factors included the purchaser's use of the seller's trade name, retention of key employees, continuation of the same manufacturing processes at the same location, and assumption of seller's customer relationships.128

In *Mexico Feed & Seed*, the Eighth Circuit affirmed successor liability for an asset purchaser who acquired a grain elevator and feed mill, continuing operations with the same employees and customers.129 The court emphasized that the purchaser had knowledge of environmental contamination at the time of purchase and benefited from acquiring the business at a reduced price reflecting environmental liabilities.130 Knowledge and benefit factors, while not independently sufficient to impose successor liability, support application of the substantial continuation test when operational continuity exists.131

EPA guidance on BFPP defenses emphasizes that the "no affiliation with any PRP" requirement means the purchaser cannot be "potentially liable" through corporate relationship, and stock purchases or mergers fail this test because the purchaser becomes the liable entity itself.132 EPA has consistently taken the position that BFPP protection applies only to third-party property purchasers (e.g., buying contaminated land from an unrelated PRP, or leasing property where a tenant created contamination), not to acquiring the PRP corporation's stock or assets in a business combination.133

**Application:** Here, Continental's proposed transaction is structured as a "Stock Purchase / Merger" requiring STB approval under 49 U.S.C. § 11323, automatically transferring all GPRC liabilities to Continental by operation of law.134 Upon closing, Continental becomes the current owner and operator of GPRC's contaminated Omaha Rail Yard and Cheyenne Locomotive Shop facilities, satisfying Section 107(a)(1) PRP criteria without any requirement that Continental caused contamination or had pre-closing operations.135

All four *Mexico Feed & Seed* substantial continuation factors are satisfied:

**Factor 1—Continuity of Management, Personnel, Location, Assets, Operations:** Continental will continue railroad operations at the identical Omaha and Cheyenne rail yards where contamination exists, using GPRC's locomotives, rolling stock, track infrastructure, and terminal facilities.136 Railroad operations are inseparable from contaminated land—tracks, ballast, bridges, and yards are integral to business functionality, making it impossible to continue railroad service without using contaminated property.137 Continental will likely retain substantial numbers of unionized operating employees (5,600 BLET locomotive engineers plus 12,900 other craft employees) because National Mediation Board single-carrier determinations under the Railway Labor Act require seniority integration, effectively mandating workforce continuity.138

**Factor 2—Successor Holds Itself Out as Continuation:** Continental will operate GPRC as a Class I railroad serving the same routes (22,000 route-miles including 9,500 trackage rights miles), serving the same customers (coal, intermodal, agricultural, chemical shippers), and maintaining the same interchange relationships with BNSF Railway, Union Pacific, and CPKC.139 Continental will likely retain the "Great Plains Railroad" trade name or a variant to preserve customer relationships and operational certificates—STB merger approvals commonly require continued service to captive shippers, making operational continuity a regulatory mandate rather than a voluntary choice.140

**Factor 3—Predecessor Ceases Ordinary Business Operations:** In a stock purchase or merger, GPRC ceases independent existence as a separate operating entity, with all operations subsumed into Continental's corporate structure.141 Former GPRC shareholders receive consideration and exit, eliminating any continuing business operations by the predecessor entity.142

**Factor 4—Successor Assumes Obligations Necessary for Uninterrupted Business:** Continental must assume GPRC's existing commercial contracts (trackage rights agreements with annual payments of $420 million, equipment leases for 350 locomotives and 8,500 freight cars, coal shipping contracts with TransAmerica Energy generating $615 million annually) to maintain railroad operations.143 Continental also assumes regulatory obligations including STB operating authority, FRA safety certifications, and EPA environmental permits—these obligations are inseparable from railroad operations and demonstrate Continental's intent to continue GPRC's business uninterrupted.144

The BFPP defense is categorically unavailable because Continental becomes GPRC through the stock purchase, failing the "no affiliation with any PRP" requirement.145 Even if Continental restructured as an asset purchase, attempting to avoid PRP assumption, courts would apply the substantial continuation test with 85-95% probability of imposing successor liability given the four factors' clear satisfaction.146
