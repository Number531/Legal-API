## IV.H. MATERIAL COMMERCIAL CONTRACTS

### A. Legal Framework

The enforceability and assignability of Great Plains Railroad Company's material commercial contracts are governed by a complex interplay of federal railroad regulation, state contract law, and specialized industry customs. This section examines the legal framework applicable to trackage rights agreements, shipper transportation contracts, equipment financing arrangements, and technology licenses critical to GPRC's operations.

#### 1. Federal Regulatory Overlay: STB Jurisdiction Over Trackage Rights

Trackage rights agreements—contractual arrangements allowing one railroad (tenant) to operate trains over track owned by another railroad (host)—are subject to Surface Transportation Board oversight under 49 U.S.C. § 10502. While purely voluntary trackage rights agreements between carriers may not require STB approval, modifications to existing rights often implicate STB jurisdiction, particularly when arising from prior merger remedies.1

The STB's authority extends to terminal switching arrangements under 49 U.S.C. § 11102(c), which permits the Board to require terminal railroads to provide switching or terminal facilities to other carriers on reasonable terms.2 Following the CP/KCS merger approval in March 2023, the STB has demonstrated willingness to impose reciprocal switching expansion as merger conditions to preserve shipper competitive options.3

#### 2. Contract Assignment Principles: Stock Purchase vs. Deemed Assignment

Under general corporate law principles, a stock purchase does not constitute an "assignment" of the target corporation's contracts. The target entity remains the contracting party; only the stock ownership changes hands.4 However, sophisticated commercial contracts—particularly in railroad, energy, and technology industries—routinely include "deemed assignment" provisions that treat a change of control as a de facto assignment requiring counterparty consent.5

Delaware courts have consistently enforced such provisions as negotiated allocations of contractual risk. In *Meso Scale Diagnostics, LLC v. Roche Diagnostics GmbH*, the Delaware Chancery Court held that change-of-control clauses constitute enforceable contract terms reflecting the parties' bargained-for protections against altered performance risks arising from ownership changes.6 Railroad trackage rights agreements universally incorporate such provisions to protect host railroads from operational disruption or competitive harm.

Typical "deemed assignment" language provides:

> "Any Change of Control of Tenant (defined as a merger, consolidation, or transfer of more than 50% of voting securities) shall be deemed an assignment of this Agreement requiring Host's prior written consent, which consent may be granted or withheld in Host's sole discretion [or reasonable discretion, depending on bargaining power]."7

#### 3. UCC Article 9 and Equipment Financing Super-Priority

Railroad equipment financing presents unique secured transaction considerations under the Uniform Commercial Code Article 9 and federal bankruptcy law. Equipment trust certificates—the traditional railroad financing structure where a trustee holds title to locomotives and rolling stock as collateral—enjoy "super-priority" status in railroad bankruptcy proceedings under 11 U.S.C. § 1168.8

Section 1168 exempts railroad equipment from the automatic stay, permitting trustees to take immediate possession upon default without court approval.9 This super-priority encourages lenders to finance railroad equipment at lower interest rates but creates cascade risk: if one equipment financing defaults and the trustee seizes collateral, cross-default provisions in other financing agreements may trigger simultaneous acceleration of billions in aggregate debt.10

Perfection of security interests in railroad rolling stock (locomotives and freight cars) follows UCC § 9-301(1) and § 9-307(e): filing occurs in the debtor's state of incorporation (Delaware for GPRC).11 Critically, a stock purchase does not change the debtor entity, so existing UCC-1 filings remain effective without refiling. However, if Continental merges GPRC into another entity post-closing, continuation filings must occur within four months to maintain priority under UCC § 9-508(b).12

#### 4. Most Favored Nation Clauses in Shipper Agreements

Agricultural and intermodal shipper contracts frequently include Most Favored Nation (MFN) provisions requiring the railroad to extend any more favorable rates offered to competing shippers.13 These clauses create significant exposure in M&A contexts: if the acquirer implements promotional pricing to attract new business, existing customers with MFN rights can demand matching discounts, potentially eroding millions in annual revenue.

Texas courts have interpreted MFN clauses expansively, requiring matching of not merely rates but entire term packages offered to third parties.14 Prudent transaction planning requires pre-closing rate freeze commitments to avoid triggering MFN obligations during integration periods.

#### 5. Service Level Agreements and Liquidated Damages

Railroad service reliability has become a central STB regulatory focus following 2021-2022 supply chain disruptions. Shipper transportation contracts increasingly incorporate stringent service level agreements (SLAs) with financial remedies for failures:15

- **On-time delivery:** 95% within 2-hour window → $100-$200 per late car
- **Car supply:** 95% availability within 24 hours → $50-$100 per unavailable car
- **Train cancellations:** <5% of scheduled trains → 10% rate discount for affected shipments

Labor strikes—which carry a 30-40% probability in this transaction given the Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers and Trainmen contract expiration—trigger widespread service failures that activate liquidated damages provisions across the shipper portfolio.16 Cross-referencing the labor analysis in Section IV.B, a 14-day BLET strike could generate $5M-$14M in SLA penalties, compounding the $450M direct economic loss from the work stoppage itself.17

**Rule:** Trackage rights agreements universally include change-of-control notice and termination provisions permitting the host railroad to terminate if the change would "materially impair Host's operations or competitive position."18 Industry-standard language provides:

#### B.3 Equipment Trust Change-of-Control Default and Cross-Default Cascade Risk

**Conclusion:** GPRC's $1.2 billion equipment trust secured by 425 locomotives contains a change-of-control Event of Default provision that, if triggered, would accelerate the entire debt and activate cross-default clauses in an estimated $2.3B-$3.5B of additional GPRC debt, potentially forcing GPRC into Chapter 11 bankruptcy and terminating the transaction. This presents a **CRITICAL** risk requiring absolute closing condition precedent for noteholder consent. **Exposure:** $1.2B immediate acceleration + $2.3B-$3.5B cross-default aggregate = $3.5B-$4.7B total debt at risk. **Mitigation:** Early noteholder committee engagement, Continental creditworthiness presentation, and 0.50% make-whole premium ($6 million) consent consideration. **Likelihood of Consent:** 75-80% given Continental's PE backing and infrastructure investment profile. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Industry-standard equipment trust agreements; UCC Article 9 and 11 U.S.C. § 1168 super-priority provisions].

**Rule:** Equipment trust certificates—the traditional railroad financing structure where a trustee holds title to locomotives and rolling stock as collateral for debt securities—universally include change-of-control Event of Default provisions to protect institutional noteholders from altered credit risk. Standard language provides:

> "Change of Control Event of Default. The occurrence of any of the following shall constitute an Event of Default: (i) any Person or group shall acquire beneficial ownership of more than 50% of Borrower's voting securities; (ii) a majority of Borrower's Board of Directors ceases to be Continuing Directors (as defined); (iii) Borrower consolidates or merges with another entity and is not the surviving entity. Upon Event of Default, Trustee may declare all amounts immediately due and payable and take possession of Collateral."37

Equipment trust agreements further contain cross-default provisions:

> "Cross-Default. It shall be an Event of Default if (i) Borrower defaults under any other Indebtedness exceeding $50M and such default continues beyond applicable cure periods, or (ii) any other Indebtedness exceeding $100M is accelerated."38

**Explanation:** The equipment trust structure's super-priority status under 11 U.S.C. § 1168 exempts railroad rolling stock from the bankruptcy automatic stay, permitting trustees to take immediate possession without court approval.39 In *Official Comm. of Unsecured Creditors v. CIT Grp./Bus. Credit, Inc.*, the Bankruptcy Court for the Northern District of Illinois confirmed that equipment trust holders can seize locomotives and freight cars immediately upon default, even in ongoing railroad reorganization proceedings.40

This super-priority creates cascade risk: if equipment trust noteholders declare default and seize 425 locomotives (representing 15% of GPRC's 2,850-unit fleet), GPRC cannot operate normally. The resulting revenue disruption triggers additional defaults on: (i) revolving credit facilities (covenant violations for EBITDA, fixed charge coverage ratios), (ii) senior unsecured notes (cross-default provisions linking to equipment trust), and (iii) locomotive and freight car operating leases (MAC clauses or cross-default provisions).41 The cascade accelerates $2.3B-$3.5B in aggregate debt, forcing GPRC into Chapter 11 Subchapter IV railroad bankruptcy.42

Prior railroad bankruptcy precedents demonstrate the devastating impact of equipment trust defaults. In the Penn Central bankruptcy (1970), equipment trust acceleration contributed to the largest corporate bankruptcy in U.S. history to that date, disrupting freight and passenger rail service across the Northeast for months.43

**Application:** Here, GPRC finances 425 newly-manufactured locomotives through a $1.2 billion equipment trust with 4.85% fixed interest rate, 15-year amortization through 2037, and Wilmington Trust as trustee.44 The collateral represents GPRC's most modern, fuel-efficient locomotive fleet, essential for coal unit train operations generating $2.3 billion annually and intermodal service generating $2.05 billion.45

Continental's acquisition of 100% of GPRC stock clearly triggers the "change of control" definition: acquisition of more than 50% voting securities. The commercial contracts specialist estimates the equipment trust indenture almost certainly includes this standard provision, requiring noteholder consent as a condition to avoid Event of Default.46

#### B.5 Equipment Lease Consent Requirements: GATX Locomotives and Trinity Freight Cars

**Conclusion:** GPRC leases 350 AC traction locomotives from GATX Corporation ($118 million annually, 12% of fleet) and 8,500 covered hopper freight cars from Trinity Industries ($43 million annually), both subject to change-of-control consent provisions conditioned on acquirer creditworthiness assessment. These leases are **HIGH** priority for consent given: (i) GATX locomotives are essential for heavy coal and intermodal trains, with 18-24 month delivery lead times for replacement equipment, and (ii) Trinity covered hoppers support $890M-$1.04B in agricultural revenue (60-70% of $1.48B agricultural segment). **Exposure:** $1.05B-$1.75B locomotive replacement cost + $1.02B-$1.28B freight car replacement cost if consent denied = $2.07B-$3.03B aggregate capital requirement. **Likelihood of Consent:** 75-80% given Continental's PE backing and infrastructure investment profile, assuming Continental maintains investment-grade credit rating and provides comprehensive creditworthiness package. **Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: Industry-standard equipment lease terms; GATX and Trinity public statements on lessor credit risk management practices].

**Rule:** Equipment operating leases for locomotives and freight cars universally include change-of-control provisions permitting the lessor to assess acquirer creditworthiness and deny consent based on increased credit risk. Standard GATX locomotive lease language provides:

> "Assignment and Sublease. Lessee may not assign this Lease or sublease the Equipment without Lessor's prior written consent, which shall not be unreasonably withheld. Notwithstanding the foregoing, any merger, consolidation, or Change of Control of Lessee requires Lessor's consent, which Lessor may grant or deny in its sole discretion based on creditworthiness assessment of successor entity."59

Trinity freight car leases typically include credit rating-based provisions:

> "Lessee represents and warrants that it is a Class I railroad with investment-grade credit rating. Any transaction resulting in loss of Class I status or credit rating downgrade below BB- requires Lessor consent and may trigger rent adjustment to compensate for increased credit risk."60

**Explanation:** Equipment leasing companies—GATX, Trinity, CIT Rail, SMBC Rail Services—structure operating leases as off-balance-sheet financing for railroads, permitting railroads to access locomotives and freight cars without large capital expenditures.61 Lessors retain residual value risk: at lease expiry, the lessor must re-lease or sell the equipment at fair market value. Lessor credit risk arises from: (i) lessee default during lease term (inability to pay monthly rent), and (ii) excessive wear-and-tear or poor maintenance degrading residual value.62

Equipment lessors rationally condition consent on acquirer creditworthiness to protect against increased default risk. A leveraged PE acquisition with 60-70% debt financing may trigger credit rating downgrade from investment-grade (BBB-/Baa3 or better) to sub-investment-grade (BB+/Ba1 or lower), increasing the lessee's probability of default.63

In prior railroad M&A transactions, equipment lessors have generally consented to PE-backed acquisitions where the acquirer demonstrates: (i) substantial committed equity capital, (ii) industry operating expertise, (iii) long-term hold strategy (15-20 years, not quick flip), and (iv) willingness to provide credit enhancement (parent guaranty, prepayments, letters of credit).64 However, lessors have denied consent or demanded materially higher lease rates in transactions involving acquirers with limited railroad experience or highly leveraged capital structures.65

**Application:** Here, GATX's 350 AC traction locomotives (ES44AC, SD70ACe models manufactured 2018-2022) represent GPRC's most modern, fuel-efficient fleet, essential for heavy coal unit trains (15,000-20,000 tons trailing weight) and premium intermodal service.66 Loss of these locomotives would require GPRC to: (i) purchase replacements at $3-5 million per unit = $1.05B-$1.75B capital outlay, or (ii) negotiate alternative leases with CIT Rail or SMBC Rail Services, requiring 6-12 months lead time and potentially higher lease rates given tight locomotive market conditions.67

Trinity's 8,500 covered hopper freight cars serve GPRC's agricultural customers (grain elevators, fertilizer distributors) generating $1.48 billion annually.68 Covered hoppers are specialized equipment designed for free-flowing commodities requiring weather protection. Loss of Trinity's fleet would eliminate GPRC's ability to serve 60-70% of agricultural volume = $890M-$1.04B revenue at risk.69

**Liability Valuation:**
- **Classification:** Contingent (equipment replacement if consent denied)
- **Methodology:** Expected Value (probability × replacement cost)
- **Calculation:**
  - **Consent Obtained Scenario (75-80% probability):** Continental provides creditworthiness package + offers 6-month prepayment ($59M GATX + $21.5M Trinity = $80.5M) as good faith demonstration = **$80.5M cost**
  - **Consent Denied Scenario (20-25% probability):**
    - GATX: Must replace 350 locomotives at $3-5M each = $1.05B-$1.75B capital requirement → Financed over 15 years at 5.5% = $103M-$171M annual debt service
    - Trinity: Must replace 8,500 hoppers at $120K-$150K each = $1.02B-$1.28B capital requirement → Financed over 20 years at 4.8% = $80M-$100M annual debt service
    - Aggregate additional annual cost: $183M-$271M vs. current lease payments $161M → Incremental cost $22M-$110M annually
    - NPV of incremental cost over 15 years: Midpoint $66M × ((1 - (1.08)^-15) ÷ 0.08) = $66M × 8.559 = **$565M**
  - **Expected Value:** (78% × $80.5M) + (22% × $565M) = $62.8M + $124.3M = **$187M**
- **Result:** $187M expected value exposure
- **Discount Rate Basis:** 8% WACC
