#### 1. STB Rate Reasonableness Jurisdiction

The Surface Transportation Board exercises exclusive federal jurisdiction over railroad rates under 49 U.S.C. § 10701(a), which provides that rates charged by rail carriers subject to the Board's jurisdiction must be "reasonable."¹ This standard applies when the Board determines, either on its own initiative or on complaint, that a rate charged by a rail carrier for transportation or service subject to its jurisdiction is unreasonable.² A rail carrier providing such transportation or service may not charge or receive a different compensation than the rate specified in its published tariff, establishing a bright-line tariff compliance requirement.³

The reasonableness inquiry proceeds in two stages. First, the Board must determine whether the railroad possesses "market dominance" over the challenged traffic movement.⁴ Market dominance exists when there is "an absence of effective competition from other rail carriers or modes of transportation for the transportation to which a rate applies."⁵ Second, if market dominance is established, the Board evaluates whether the challenged rate exceeds the maximum reasonable level using one of several methodologies, most commonly the Stand-Alone Cost (SAC) test.⁶

**Revenue-to-Variable Cost Threshold:** The statutory framework includes a critical jurisdictional prerequisite: the Board is precluded from finding market dominance if the revenues produced by a challenged rate are less than 180% of the carrier's variable costs of providing the service.⁷ This 180% revenue-to-variable cost (R/VC) ratio serves as a floor for STB jurisdiction but does not create a presumption regarding market dominance or rate reasonableness.⁸ Variable costs are calculated using the carrier's Uniform Rail Costing System (URCS), indexed quarterly to reflect current wage and price levels.⁹

#### 2. Market Dominance Determination — 2020 Streamlined Approach

In July 2020, the STB adopted a streamlined approach to market dominance determinations designed to reduce evidentiary burdens for smaller rate disputes.¹⁰ Under this framework, the Board presumes market dominance exists if the complainant demonstrates four factors:

(1) The challenged rate produces an R/VC ratio equal to or greater than 180%;
(2) The complainant has used trucks for 10% or less of its volume (by tonnage) subject to the rate at issue over a five-year period;
(3) The complainant has no practical build-out alternative (i.e., no ability to extend track to reach a competitive carrier); and
(4) The complainant demonstrates absence of barge competition.¹¹

If all four factors are satisfied, the burden shifts to the railroad to rebut the presumption by demonstrating effective product competition, geographic competition, or intermodal competition that constrains its pricing power.¹² The streamlined approach applies to both Full-SAC and Three-Benchmark rate challenges, providing shippers a procedural advantage when these threshold showings can be made.

[VERIFIED: 85-Fed-Reg-46985]

#### 3. Stand-Alone Cost Methodology

The Stand-Alone Cost (SAC) test remains the STB's primary methodology for evaluating rate reasonableness in complex, high-value rate challenges.¹³ Under SAC, the complainant designs a hypothetical efficient railroad (the "stand-alone railroad" or "SARR") specifically tailored to serve an optimum traffic group with the optimum physical plant needed for that traffic.¹⁴ The SAC test determines the rate that shippers would be charged by calculating the SARR's total costs, including a reasonable return on investment, which becomes the maximum amount that the incumbent railroad may collect from the traffic group.¹⁵

**Litigation Costs and Complexity:** The STB has acknowledged that Full-SAC cases impose extraordinary evidentiary burdens. The Board estimated in 2007 that "shippers' litigation costs in recent Full-SAC cases have approached $5 million," and that "even a Simplified-SAC presentation would likely cost up to $1 million to litigate."¹⁶ These cost barriers have materially reduced rate complaint filings, as shippers with claims below $20-30 million face unfavorable cost-benefit ratios when litigation costs consume 15-25% of potential recovery.¹⁷

**Procedural Timeline:** Stand-Alone Cost proceedings are subject to expedited handling requirements. Discovery must be completed not later than 150 days after the date on which the challenge is initiated, and the Board must issue a final decision within 485 days (approximately 16 months) from complaint filing.¹⁸ However, complex Full-SAC cases frequently exceed these targets due to discovery extensions, voluminous evidentiary submissions, and extended Board deliberation periods, with actual timelines ranging from 18 to 36 months.¹⁹

#### 4. Revenue Adequacy Constraint and Captive Shippers

Under 49 U.S.C. § 10704(a)(2), the STB must ensure that railroads earn adequate revenues to cover operating costs, maintain infrastructure, and attract capital investment.²⁰ A railroad is deemed revenue adequate if it achieves a rate of return on net investment (ROI) equal to at least the current cost of capital for the railroad industry, which the Board determines annually.²¹ For 2024, the Board calculated the industry cost of capital at 10.68%, and found two U.S. Class I railroads (CSX and Union Pacific) to be revenue adequate.²²

**Coal Rate Guidelines Doctrine:** In 1985, the Interstate Commerce Commission (the STB's predecessor agency) ruled in the Coal Rate Guidelines that discriminatory pricing should cease when railroads reach revenue adequacy—earning enough to maintain the rail system and attract renewal capital.²³ This finding, affirmed by the Third Circuit and applied by the ICC in numerous subsequent cases, establishes that once a railroad is revenue adequate, further differential pricing on market-dominant traffic cannot be justified solely by network preservation arguments.²⁴

**Relevance to GPRC:** If GPRC is found to be revenue adequate during rate case proceedings, the revenue adequacy constraint limits the railroad's ability to justify differential pricing on captive shippers. Complainants typically argue that any rate exceeding the 180% R/VC threshold imposed by a revenue-adequate carrier constitutes unjust discrimination and should be capped at the maximum reasonable rate determined under SAC methodology.²⁵ Conversely, if GPRC's coal traffic decline (annual revenue loss of $115 million at a -5% annual decline rate) pushes the railroad below revenue adequacy, GPRC may argue that high rates on captive shippers are necessary to offset losses from competitive traffic and maintain network viability.²⁶

#### 5. Reparations and Interest Calculation

When the STB determines that a railroad has charged unreasonable rates, the carrier becomes liable for damages sustained by the shipper as a result of overcharges.²⁷ Reparations are calculated as the difference between the actual rate charged and the STB-determined reasonable rate, multiplied by tonnage shipped during the look-back period.²⁸ The statute of limitations constrains recovery: a complainant must file a complaint with the Board within two years after the claim accrues to recover damages, and must commence any civil action within three years.²⁹ This creates a practical "look-back period" limiting reparations recovery to rates charged within three years before complaint filing.

**Interest on Reparations:** Under 49 C.F.R. § 1141.1, the interest rate for STB complaint proceedings is the U.S. Prime Rate as published by The Wall Street Journal in effect on the day when the unlawful charge is paid.³⁰ The Board applies an exponential compounding method, multiplying the interest factor for each period by the principal amount for that period plus any accumulated interest from previous periods.³¹ With the U.S. Prime Rate ranging from 7.75% to 8.50% during 2024-2025, interest can add 30-70% to the principal reparations amount depending on the length of the look-back period and payment timing.³²

#### 6. Major STB Rate Case Precedents

Two recent STB rate cases establish the potential magnitude of reparations awards and prospective rate reductions when shippers successfully challenge rates under SAC methodology:

**Western Fuels Association v. BNSF Railway (STB Docket No. NOR 42088):** In May 2008, the STB awarded Western Fuels Association and Basin Electric Power Cooperative approximately $350 million in reparations and prospective rate reductions, which the Board described as "the single largest reduction in rail rates ever awarded by this agency."³³ The complainants alleged that BNSF's tariff rates on coal traffic moving from the Wyoming Powder River Basin to the Laramie River Station exceeded the maximums permitted under SAC constraints.³⁴ The Board awarded approximately $100-120 million in reparations for past overcharges and ordered BNSF to lower transportation rates by approximately 60%, reducing the maximum lawful rate from an R/VC ratio exceeding 300% to 240%.³⁵ BNSF appealed to the D.C. Circuit, which issued multiple decisions from 2010 through 2014, ultimately remanding technical aspects but substantially upholding the Board's methodology and award.³⁶

**Arizona Electric Power Cooperative v. BNSF/Union Pacific (STB Docket No. NOR 42088):** In November 2011, the STB granted Arizona Electric Power Cooperative (AEPCO) approximately $63 million in reparations and prospective rate reductions against BNSF and Union Pacific, finding that rates imposed on AEPCO's coal traffic were unreasonably high.³⁷ The Board ordered average transportation rate reductions of 37% during a 10-year rate relief period from 2009 through 2018, with reparations totaling $4.5 million for 2009 alone.³⁸ The AEPCO case demonstrates the Board's willingness to impose long-term prospective rate caps extending well beyond the reparations look-back period, providing shippers with sustained rate relief.

**Precedential Value:** Both Western Fuels and AEPCO involved coal utility traffic and applied Full-SAC methodology, requiring the railroads to defend variable cost calculations and SARR design assumptions over multi-year litigation periods. Settlement rates in STB rate cases typically range from 40-60% of claimed reparations, reflecting litigation risk, cost avoidance, and confidentiality benefits.³⁹

**Rule:** Under the collateral estoppel doctrine, a final STB decision establishing material facts in a rate reasonableness proceeding may have preclusive effect in subsequent rate cases involving the same railroad and similar traffic, particularly when the prior decision definitively determines the railroad's variable costs using URCS methodology or establishes the railroad's SARR design assumptions.⁷¹ While each rate complaint requires an independent showing of market dominance and rate unreasonableness specific to the complainant's traffic, subsequent complainants benefit from reduced evidentiary burdens when they can rely on the railroad's previously adjudicated cost structure.⁷² Moreover, confidential settlements that include broad releases and non-disclosure provisions prevent subsequent complainants from accessing the settling shipper's expert reports, SARR designs, and negotiated terms, thereby forcing copycat complainants to independently fund Full-SAC litigation at the $4-5 million cost level.⁷³

**Rule:** Under 49 U.S.C. § 11324(b), when the STB approves a railroad consolidation, merger, or acquisition of control, the Board may impose "terms and conditions" necessary to protect the public interest, including conditions that preserve competition and protect captive shippers from rate increases attributable to the transaction.⁹⁸ In evaluating major rail mergers, the Board applies a two-part test: (1) whether the transaction will enhance competition, and (2) whether the transaction will maintain or improve service levels.⁹⁹ When the Board finds that a merger creates or enhances market power over captive traffic, the agency typically imposes rate freeze conditions limiting increases to inflation (CPI) for a defined period, coupled with arbitration rights for shippers disputing service quality or rate reasonableness.¹⁰⁰

The STB's 2001 Major Merger Rules elevated the competitive standard from "preserve competition" to "enhance competition," requiring applicants to demonstrate affirmative public interest benefits that outweigh any reduction in competition.¹⁰¹ In practice, major mergers involving two Class I railroads face heightened scrutiny regarding impact on captive shippers, even when the merging railroads do not directly compete in most corridors.¹⁰² The Board's conditions typically include: (1) rate freezes limiting increases to CPI for 3-5 years; (2) gateway preservation requiring continued interchange access at pre-merger locations; (3) trackage rights grants enabling competitive railroads to serve shippers losing routing alternatives; and (4) oversight reporting requiring quarterly updates on service performance and rate levels.¹⁰³

**Confidence:** MEDIUM [BASIS: GPRC's revenue adequacy status for 2024-2026 not definitively established in specialist reports; coal revenue decline rate of -5% annually is well-documented across multiple sources; 2024 STB cost of capital determination of 10.68% provides benchmark for revenue adequacy calculation; however, GPRC's actual ROI calculation requires full financial data not available in due diligence materials]

**Rule:** Under the Coal Rate Guidelines doctrine established by the Interstate Commerce Commission in 1985 and affirmed by the Third Circuit, railroads that have achieved revenue adequacy—earning a rate of return on net investment equal to or greater than the industry cost of capital—cannot justify differential pricing on market-dominant traffic solely on the basis that such pricing is necessary to maintain the rail system and attract renewal capital.¹³¹ The doctrine establishes that revenue-adequate railroads must demonstrate specific cost causation or competitive necessity to justify rates exceeding the 180% R/VC threshold on captive traffic.¹³² Conversely, railroads that fall below revenue adequacy may argue that higher rates on market-dominant traffic are necessary to offset losses from competitive traffic and maintain network-wide viability, providing a stronger defense against rate reasonableness challenges.¹³³

The STB determines revenue adequacy annually by calculating each Class I railroad's rate of return on net investment and comparing it to the Board's determination of the current cost of capital for the freight rail industry.¹³⁴ For 2024, the Board determined the industry cost of capital at 10.68%, and found that CSX and Union Pacific achieved revenue adequacy while BNSF and Norfolk Southern fell short.¹³⁵ The Board's methodology considers each railroad's operating income, net investment in railroad property, and accumulated deferred income taxes to calculate ROI, with revenue adequacy achieved when ROI ≥ cost of capital.¹³⁶

[VERIFIED: Congressional-Research-Service-2023] [VERIFIED: 49-U.S.C.-10704]

**Explanation:** In the 1985 Coal Rate Guidelines proceeding, the ICC found that once a railroad becomes revenue adequate, the justification for charging differential rates on market-dominant traffic diminishes because the railroad no longer requires supracompetitive returns to maintain infrastructure or attract capital.¹³⁷ The Commission held that revenue-adequate carriers must demonstrate that differential pricing serves purposes beyond network preservation, such as recovering fixed costs allocated to specific traffic groups or responding to competitive conditions.¹³⁸ This doctrine has been applied in subsequent rate cases to limit revenue-adequate carriers' ability to defend rates exceeding SAC constraints.¹³⁹

However, the doctrine operates differently when a railroad's financial condition deteriorates. If coal traffic decline causes GPRC's overall profitability to fall below the revenue adequacy threshold, GPRC gains stronger arguments for maintaining high rates on captive grain and chemical traffic: (1) the railroad needs captive shipper contributions to offset losses from competitive coal traffic, preventing network-wide deterioration; (2) lowering rates on captive traffic would accelerate financial distress, potentially requiring service reductions or infrastructure rationalization; and (3) the STB's statutory mandate under 49 U.S.C. § 10704(a)(2) to ensure railroads earn adequate revenues supports higher rates during periods of financial stress.¹⁴⁰
