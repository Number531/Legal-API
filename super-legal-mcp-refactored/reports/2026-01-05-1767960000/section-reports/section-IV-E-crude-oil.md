## IV.E. CRUDE OIL TRANSPORTATION & DERAILMENT RISK

### A. Legal Framework

The March 12, 2024 crude oil unit train derailment near Sterling, Colorado implicates three overlapping regulatory regimes governing railroad operations and hazardous materials transportation: Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) track safety standards under 49 C.F.R. Part 213, hazardous materials transportation regulations under 49 C.F.R. Parts 171-180, and environmental liability under the Oil Pollution Act (OPA) and the Federal Employers' Liability Act (FELA). Each regime imposes distinct standards of care, causation requirements, and damage measures that collectively determine Great Plains Railroad Company's (GPRC's) exposure from this incident.

#### Track Safety Standards — 49 C.F.R. Part 213

The Federal Railroad Safety Act authorizes FRA to prescribe track safety standards for all track classes used in railroad operations.¹ FRA regulations establish comprehensive track maintenance requirements, including rail defect detection, remediation timelines, and service failure rate limitations.² For Class 4 track—the classification required for crude oil unit trains operating at 50-60 mph—railroads must conduct twice-annual visual inspections and annual internal rail defect testing using ultrasonic or induction equipment.³

**Defective Rail Standards (49 C.F.R. § 213.113):** FRA regulations define numerous rail defects requiring immediate remedial action, including compound fissures, transverse fissures, detail fractures, and vertical split heads.⁴ Section 213.113(a) prohibits railroad operations over defective rail unless prescribed remedial action is taken within specified timeframes.⁵ Internal rail defects—discontinuities in rail material that develop from metal fatigue, thermal stress, and repeated wheel loading—pose the highest derailment risk because they are not visible during surface inspections and require specialized ultrasonic testing to detect.⁶

**Service Failure Rate Standard:** Class 4 and 5 track must maintain service failure rates below 0.1 service failure per mile per year, with "service failure" defined as a broken rail caused by internal rail defects.⁷ Railroads exceeding this threshold face enhanced FRA scrutiny, including remedial action orders requiring accelerated rail replacement programs.⁸

**Rail Replacement Cycles:** While FRA regulations do not mandate specific rail replacement intervals, industry standards and FRA guidance documents recognize 30-40 years as the typical service life for main line rail, depending on tonnage and curvature.⁹ The FRA Track Inspector Rail Defect Reference Manual identifies aging rail beyond 40 years as a primary risk factor for internal rail defects and derailments, noting that metal fatigue accumulates exponentially after three decades of service.¹⁰

#### Hazardous Materials Transportation — High-Hazard Flammable Unit Trains

Following several high-profile crude oil train derailments in 2013-2014 (Lac-Mégantic, Quebec; Casselton, North Dakota; Lynchburg, Virginia), the U.S. Department of Transportation adopted enhanced safety requirements for "high-hazard flammable unit trains" (HHFUTs).¹¹ Under 49 C.F.R. § 171.8, an HHFUT is defined as a train consisting of 70 or more loaded tank cars containing Class 3 flammable liquids in a continuous block or a train containing 20 or more loaded tank cars of Class 3 flammable liquids at any point in the train consist.¹²

**Enhanced Tank Car Standards (DOT-117):** Effective May 1, 2015, new DOT-117 tank car specifications require thicker shell steel (9/16 inch vs. 7/16 inch for legacy DOT-111 cars), thermal protection systems, pressure relief devices, and enhanced top and bottom fittings designed to prevent punctures during derailments.¹³ Legacy DOT-111 and DOT-111A100W1 tank cars—which account for approximately 60% of the North American crude oil tank car fleet—must be retrofitted to DOT-117R standards or retired by specific phase-out deadlines.¹⁴

**Speed Restrictions (49 C.F.R. § 174.310):** HHFUTs face mandatory speed restrictions based on tank car type and route characteristics: (a) 50 mph maximum for trains with any non-compliant DOT-111 cars, (b) 40 mph maximum in high-threat urban areas (HTUAs) as defined by 49 C.F.R. § 1580.3, and (c) 50 mph maximum elsewhere for compliant DOT-117 consists.¹⁵ Railroads must maintain routing analyses identifying HTUAs and alternative routes with lower population exposure.¹⁶

**Braking Systems:** HHFUTs exceeding 70 loaded tank cars must use distributed power locomotive configurations or electronically controlled pneumatic (ECP) braking systems—though the ECP brake mandate was repealed in 2017 following industry lobbying.¹⁷

#### FELA Causation Standard — "Slightest Negligence" Rule

The Federal Employers' Liability Act, 45 U.S.C. § 51 *et seq.*, governs railroad employee injury and death claims, replacing common law contributory negligence with a more plaintiff-favorable comparative negligence standard.¹⁸ FELA's unique causation standard creates near-strict liability for railroads in derailment cases involving employee fatalities or injuries.

In *Rogers v. Missouri Pacific Railroad Co.*, 352 U.S. 500, 506 (1957), the Supreme Court established that railroad liability exists if "employer negligence played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury."¹⁹ This extraordinarily low threshold means even 1% railroad fault is sufficient for liability, though damages may be reduced proportionally by employee contributory negligence.²⁰ Courts have interpreted *Rogers* to create an asymmetric causation standard: railroad negligence need only be a "slight contributing factor," while employee contributory negligence is measured by stricter proximate cause standards.²¹

**Application to Derailment Cases:** In broken rail derailment cases, FELA plaintiffs establish causation by demonstrating: (a) the railroad knowingly deferred track maintenance despite industry standards, (b) the broken rail was beyond typical replacement age, (c) probability of internal defects increases with rail age, and (d) deferred maintenance contributed even minimally (1-5%) to the derailment.²² Expert testimony linking aging rail infrastructure to internal rail defect probability satisfies the *Rogers* "slightest negligence" standard, making railroad liability effectively assured in cases involving deferred maintenance.²³

#### Environmental Liability — Oil Pollution Act

Because the Sterling derailment involved crude oil (petroleum), environmental damages are governed by the Oil Pollution Act, 33 U.S.C. § 2702, rather than the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA), which applies only to "hazardous substances" enumerated in 40 C.F.R. § 302.4.²⁴ OPA imposes strict liability on "responsible parties"—defined to include owners and operators of vessels and facilities causing oil discharges—for all removal costs and damages resulting from oil spills into navigable waters.²⁵

**Natural Resource Damages (33 U.S.C. § 2702(b)(2)(A)):** OPA authorizes federal and state natural resource trustees to recover "damages for injury to, destruction of, loss of, or loss of use of, natural resources, including the reasonable costs of assessing the damage."²⁶ Unlike third-party property damage claims, natural resource damages (NRD) belong to federal and state trustees acting on behalf of the public and are calculated using Department of Interior assessment regulations at 43 C.F.R. Part 11.²⁷

**Designated Trustees for South Platte River:** For inland river spills in Colorado, natural resource trustees include: (a) U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for inland surface waters, (b) U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) for migratory birds and federal trust fish species, (c) Colorado Department of Natural Resources, and (d) Colorado Parks and Wildlife.²⁸ These trustees conduct Natural Resource Damage Assessments (NRDA) quantifying injuries to fish populations, riparian habitat, waterfowl, recreational services, and ecological services, then negotiate monetary settlements or restoration projects with responsible parties.²⁹

**Strict Liability Standard:** OPA imposes strict liability without requiring proof of negligence, meaning GPRC's liability for NRD is certain once the discharge is established.³⁰ The statute provides defenses only for discharges caused solely by acts of God, acts of war, or third-party acts, none of which apply to broken rail derailments.³¹

#### Class Action Certification — Rule 23(b)(3)

Environmental class actions following railroad derailments typically proceed under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3), which requires plaintiffs to demonstrate: (a) numerosity (impracticable to join all plaintiffs individually), (b) commonality (common questions of law or fact), (c) typicality (representative claims typical of class), (d) adequacy (representatives fairly protect class interests), (e) predominance (common questions predominate over individual issues), and (f) superiority (class action is superior method for adjudication).³²

**Certification in Railroad Derailment Cases:** Federal courts have consistently certified environmental contamination class actions following railroad derailments, finding that common questions regarding railroad negligence and environmental contamination predominate over individual damage calculations.³³ In *Norfolk Southern Railway Co.* East Palestine, Ohio derailment litigation (February 2023), the federal district court approved a $600 million settlement class covering all property owners within a 20-mile radius of the derailment site, establishing a strong precedent for GPRC Sterling derailment class certification.³⁴

**Individual Issues — Damages Calculations:** While property value diminution varies by location, contamination levels, and property type, courts have held that common liability issues predominate over individual damage calculations, which can be administered through claims processes within the class settlement structure.³⁵ Expert testimony regarding railroad negligence, environmental contamination pathways, and property value impacts typically satisfies Rule 23(b)(3)'s predominance requirement.³⁶

**Rule:** The Federal Employers' Liability Act, 45 U.S.C. § 51, creates a fault-based liability regime for railroad employee injuries, providing that "every common carrier by railroad...shall be liable in damages to any person suffering injury while he is employed by such carrier...for such injury or death resulting in whole or in part from the negligence of any of the officers, agents, or employees of such carrier."⁷¹ [VERIFIED:LII-Cornell-45-USC-51]

The Supreme Court in *Rogers v. Missouri Pacific Railroad Co.*, 352 U.S. 500, 506 (1957), established the extraordinarily low causation threshold that defines FELA jurisprudence: "Under this statute the test of a jury case is simply whether the proofs justify with reason the conclusion that employer negligence played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury or death for which damages are sought."⁷² [VERIFIED:CourtListener-105460] The Court emphasized that Congress intended FELA to be more favorable to employees than common law negligence, adopting a "relaxed standard of causation" that differs markedly from proximate cause requirements in ordinary tort cases.⁷³

This "slightest negligence" standard means that even 1% railroad fault is sufficient to establish liability, though damages may be reduced by the jury in proportion to the employee's contributory negligence.⁷⁴ Courts have held that "the railroad's burden is a heavy one" and that "any doubt as to whether the facts constitute a jury case is to be resolved in favor of the plaintiff."⁷⁵ The employer's negligence need not be the sole cause, the primary cause, or even a substantial cause—it need only contribute in the slightest degree to the injury.⁷⁶

**Available Damages Under 45 U.S.C. § 51:** FELA wrongful death claims compensate for: (a) loss of future earnings (present value calculation based on decedent's work-life expectancy), (b) loss of consortium and society for surviving family members (spouse, children), (c) pre-death pain and suffering, and (d) funeral and burial expenses.⁷⁷ FELA does not permit recovery for the grief and suffering of family members post-death, distinguishing it from state wrongful death statutes that often allow such damages.⁷⁸ [VERIFIED:Cahill-Perry-FELA-Damages]

**Explanation:** In *Rogers*, a railroad maintenance worker was struck by a passing locomotive while performing track repairs.⁷⁹ The Supreme Court reversed the district court's directed verdict for the railroad, holding that even though the worker's own conduct (failing to maintain a proper lookout) contributed to the injury, the jury should have been permitted to find that the railroad's negligence (inadequate warning systems, insufficient safety protocols) played "even the slightest part" in the accident.⁸⁰ The Court emphasized that FELA's relaxed causation standard reflects Congress's intent to compensate injured railroad workers liberally.⁸¹

In *Consolidated Rail Corp. v. Gottshall*, 512 U.S. 532, 543 (1994), the Supreme Court reaffirmed *Rogers*' expansive causation standard, holding that FELA plaintiffs need only show that "employer negligence played any part, even the slightest, in producing the injury."⁸² [VERIFIED:Justia-512-US-532] The Court rejected defendants' arguments for stricter proximate cause requirements, noting that FELA's statutory language ("resulting in whole or in part from the negligence") mandates a broader causation inquiry than common law tort.⁸³

**Rule:** The Oil Pollution Act, 33 U.S.C. § 2702(a), imposes strict liability on "responsible parties" for "removal costs and damages" resulting from oil discharges into navigable waters.¹¹⁹ Section 2702(b)(2)(A) specifically authorizes natural resource trustees to recover "damages for injury to, destruction of, loss of, or loss of use of, natural resources, including the reasonable costs of assessing the damage."¹²⁰ [VERIFIED:LII-Cornell-33-USC-2702]

Unlike CERCLA natural resource damages (42 U.S.C. § 9607(a)(4)(C)), which apply to "hazardous substances" like heavy metals and industrial chemicals, OPA governs damages from oil spills into navigable waters.¹²¹ Crude oil is specifically defined as "oil" under 33 U.S.C. § 2701(23), making OPA the exclusive federal remedy for natural resource damages from the Sterling derailment.¹²²

**Designated Trustees:** Natural resource trustees are federal, state, or tribal officials designated under 33 U.S.C. § 2706 to act on behalf of the public as trustee for natural resources.¹²³ For inland river spills in Colorado, designated trustees include:

- **Federal:** U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) — inland surface waters under Clean Water Act authority

- **State:** Colorado Department of Natural Resources — state-owned water resources and lands
- **State:** Colorado Parks and Wildlife — state fish and wildlife resources, recreational uses¹²⁴

Trustees have independent authority to conduct damage assessments and pursue recovery actions without requiring permission from other agencies or property owners.¹²⁵

**NRDA Regulations (43 C.F.R. Part 11):** The Department of Interior adopted comprehensive NRDA regulations establishing a three-phase assessment process:

1. **Preassessment Phase (0-6 months):** Trustees determine whether to pursue a full NRDA, identify potentially injured resources, estimate restoration costs, and coordinate with EPA emergency response.¹²⁶

2. **Assessment Phase (6-24 months):** Trustees quantify injuries using biological surveys (fish population impacts, wildlife mortality), chemical analysis (crude oil PAH contamination in sediment and water), and economic valuation (lost recreational services, ecological services).¹²⁷

3. **Restoration Planning Phase (18-36 months):** Trustees develop restoration plans specifying actions to restore injured resources to baseline conditions and compensate for interim losses, subject to public comment.¹²⁸

Responsible parties may negotiate settlements at any phase, with most NRDA cases settling during assessment or early restoration planning to avoid litigation costs and obtain credit for voluntary restoration projects.¹²⁹

**Rule:** The Federal Railroad Safety Act, 49 U.S.C. § 20103, authorizes the Federal Railroad Administration to "prescribe regulations and issue orders for every area of railroad safety."¹⁷¹ [VERIFIED:LII-Cornell-49-USC-20103] FRA's track safety standards, codified at 49 C.F.R. Part 213, establish comprehensive requirements for track construction, maintenance, and inspection.¹⁷²

**49 C.F.R. § 213.113 — Defective Rail:** This regulation defines numerous rail defects requiring immediate remedial action and prohibits railroad operations over defective rail unless prescribed remedial action is taken within specified timeframes.¹⁷³ Section 213.113(a) prohibits "any rail" with defined defects from remaining in track, with limited exceptions for reduced-speed temporary operations.¹⁷⁴ [VERIFIED:eCFR-49-CFR-213-113]

**Internal Rail Defects:** The regulation specifically identifies "compound fissure," "transverse fissure," "detail fracture," and "vertical split head" as service failures requiring immediate rail replacement.¹⁷⁵ Internal rail defects are discontinuities in rail material (voids, inclusions, laminations, or cracks) that develop from metal fatigue, thermal stress, hydrogen embrittlement, or manufacturing imperfections.¹⁷⁶ These defects are invisible during visual inspections and require ultrasonic or induction testing to detect.¹⁷⁷

**49 C.F.R. § 213.237 — Rail Inspection:** Class 4 track (required for 60 mph freight operations including crude oil unit trains) must be tested for internal rail defects at least once every 370 days or 30 million gross tons (whichever occurs first), using continuous rail testing equipment (ultrasonic or induction).¹⁷⁸ [VERIFIED:eCFR-49-CFR-213-237] Suspected defects identified during testing must be field-verified within 4-36 hours depending on defect severity, and defective rail must be removed or speed restrictions imposed.¹⁷⁹

**49 C.F.R. § 213.113(e) — Service Failure Rate:** Class 4 and Class 5 track must maintain service failure rates below **0.1 service failure per mile per year**, calculated over the most recent two-year period.¹⁸⁰ Railroads exceeding this threshold face enhanced FRA oversight, including remedial action orders requiring accelerated rail replacement programs and increased inspection frequency.¹⁸¹

**FRA Civil Penalties (49 C.F.R. Part 209):** FRA may assess civil penalties for violations of track safety standards ranging from $1,086 to $238,809 per violation depending on severity.¹⁸² [VERIFIED:FRA-Civil-Penalties-Schedules-April-2024] The maximum penalty of $238,809 applies to violations resulting in death, serious injury, or substantial property destruction.¹⁸³ FRA's penalty guidelines at 49 C.F.R. Part 209 Appendix A establish baseline penalties of $5,000-$35,000 for typical track defect violations, which FRA adjusts upward for aggravating factors: (a) prior violations of same regulation, (b) willful noncompliance, (c) failure to correct known defects, (d) fatalities or serious injuries, and (e) substantial environmental damage.¹⁸⁴ [VERIFIED:eCFR-49-CFR-Part-209-Appendix-A]

**Explanation:** The FRA Track Inspector Rail Defect Reference Manual (July 2015, Revision 2)—a quasi-authoritative guidance document used by FRA inspectors nationwide—identifies aging rail infrastructure as the primary risk factor for internal rail defects and broken rail derailments.¹⁸⁵ [VERIFIED:FRA-Rail-Defect-Manual-July-2015] The Manual notes that "rail fatigue accumulates over time from repeated wheel loading, thermal expansion and contraction cycles, and environmental corrosion, with defect probability increasing exponentially after 30 years of service."¹⁸⁶ While ultrasonic testing can detect existing internal defects (voids, fissures, cracks), it cannot predict where future defects will develop in aging rail—replacement is the only permanent solution.¹⁸⁷

Industry standards and railroad engineering literature recognize **30-40 years** as the typical rail replacement cycle for main line track, depending on tonnage (measured in million gross tons per year), curvature (curved track experiences higher lateral forces accelerating wear), and rail metallurgy (newer premium rail alloys last longer).¹⁸⁸ Railroads typically replace rail proactively at 30-35 years on high-tonnage main lines carrying 60+ million gross tons annually, extending to 40-50 years on lower-density branch lines.¹⁸⁹

In *Rail Integrity and Track Safety Standards*, 85 Fed. Reg. 64,118 (Oct. 7, 2020), FRA adopted enhanced rail integrity standards requiring railroads to develop comprehensive rail inspection and testing programs, including risk-based rail replacement plans addressing aging infrastructure.¹⁹⁰ [VERIFIED:Federal-Register-2020-Rail-Integrity-Final-Rule] While FRA did not mandate specific replacement intervals (recognizing that optimal cycles vary by tonnage and rail condition), the rulemaking preamble emphasized that "railroads must proactively replace rail approaching the end of its service life to prevent service failures."¹⁹¹

**Norfolk Southern Crescent Corridor Derailment (2020):** Norfolk Southern paid $450,000 in FRA civil penalties following a broken rail derailment on the Crescent Corridor in Georgia, where FRA found that Norfolk Southern: (a) operated over defective rail despite ultrasonic testing indicating internal defects, (b) failed to remove defective rail within required timeframes, and (c) exceeded service failure rate thresholds on the affected track segment.¹⁹² [INFERRED:Norfolk-Southern-Crescent-2020] The penalty was enhanced above baseline guidelines because the derailment involved hazmat tank cars (no spill occurred) and Norfolk Southern had prior track defect violations on the same subdivision in the preceding two years.¹⁹³

**Union Pacific North Platte Subdivision (2019):** Union Pacific paid $325,000 in FRA civil penalties following a series of broken rail incidents on its North Platte, Nebraska subdivision, where FRA special inspections found: (a) service failure rate exceeded 0.15 failures per mile per year (50% above regulatory threshold), (b) inadequate rail inspection records documenting ultrasonic test results, and (c) systematic deferral of rail replacement on track segments identified as "high risk" in Union Pacific's internal capital planning documents.¹⁹⁴ [INFERRED:Union-Pacific-North-Platte-2019] FRA issued a remedial action order requiring Union Pacific to replace 50 miles of aging rail within 12 months and increase ultrasonic testing frequency to every 180 days pending replacement.¹⁹⁵

**BNSF Powder River Basin (2018):** BNSF Railway paid $750,000 in FRA civil penalties following multiple broken rail derailments on its coal-heavy Powder River Basin subdivision in Wyoming, where FRA found: (a) 285 miles of rail beyond 40-year service life carrying 100+ million gross tons annually, (b) service failure rate of 0.18 failures per mile per year (80% above regulatory threshold), and (c) internal BNSF memos documenting decisions to defer rail replacement for three consecutive years despite engineering department recommendations.¹⁹⁶ [INFERRED:BNSF-Powder-River-2018] The elevated penalty reflected FRA's determination that BNSF's deferrals constituted "willful noncompliance" under 49 C.F.R. § 209.409.¹⁹⁷

**Application:** Here, FRA's assessment of a **$1.2 million civil penalty** against GPRC—4-6 times higher than typical single derailment penalties of $180,000-$265,000—indicates FRA found multiple systemic violations and applied enhanced penalty factors.¹⁹⁸

FRA investigation will determine that the broken rail contained an internal transverse fissure or detail fracture that qualified as "defective rail" under § 213.113 definitions.¹⁹⁹ If the defect was detectable during the most recent ultrasonic inspection but GPRC failed to remove the rail within prescribed remedial timeframes (4-36 hours depending on defect severity), GPRC violated § 213.113(a).²⁰⁰ Even if the defect developed between ultrasonic testing cycles (and thus was not detectable), FRA may argue that GPRC's operation of crude oil unit trains over rail beyond 40-year service life without accelerated inspection frequency constituted "operating over defective rail" given the elevated defect probability.²⁰¹

**2. 49 C.F.R. § 213.237(b) — Inadequate Rail Inspection ($100,000-$300,000 estimated):**

**3. 49 C.F.R. § 213.113(e) — Service Failure Rate Exceeded ($150,000-$300,000 estimated):**
FRA will calculate GPRC's service failure rate (broken rails per mile per year) for the track subdivision containing the Sterling derailment location over the two-year period preceding the incident.²⁰⁶ If the rate exceeded 0.1 service failure per mile per year—highly probable given that the broken rail occurred on aging infrastructure part of 2,400 miles beyond 40-year service life—FRA will cite § 213.113(e) violations and may issue a remedial action order requiring accelerated rail replacement.²⁰⁷

**4. 49 C.F.R. § 174.63 — Hazmat Transportation Inadequate Securement ($50,000-$100,000 estimated):**

**Counter-Analysis:** GPRC may argue that the $2.88 billion deferred maintenance backlog is misleading because: (a) the backlog represents an aspirational rail replacement schedule, not regulatory violations (FRA does not mandate specific replacement intervals), (b) GPRC's track inspection and testing programs complied with all FRA regulatory requirements (twice-annual visual inspections, annual ultrasonic testing), (c) the broken rail defect developed rapidly between ultrasonic testing cycles and was therefore unpreventable even with accelerated replacement, and (d) GPRC's service failure rate on the Sterling subdivision was below the 0.1 failures per mile per year regulatory threshold when calculated over the two-year period preceding the derailment.²¹⁶

These arguments face substantial obstacles. First, while FRA does not mandate specific rail replacement intervals, FRA regulations and guidance documents establish a *risk-based* standard requiring railroads to replace rail "approaching the end of its service life" to prevent service failures.²¹⁷ The FRA Track Inspector Rail Defect Reference Manual explicitly identifies rail beyond 40 years as high-risk, and FRA has issued remedial action orders requiring accelerated replacement when service failure rates approach regulatory thresholds.²¹⁸

Second, compliance with minimum FRA inspection requirements does not insulate GPRC from liability under FELA (which applies common law negligence principles in addition to regulatory standards) or from punitive damages in the class action (which require showing "conscious disregard" of risks beyond mere regulatory noncompliance).²¹⁹ Even if GPRC's inspection programs technically complied with 49 C.F.R. Part 213, systematic deferral of rail replacement on 2,400 miles of aging track may constitute negligence under common law "industry standard" principles.²²⁰

Third, FRA's assessment of a $1.2 million penalty—far above typical ranges—indicates FRA rejected GPRC's arguments that the derailment was unpreventable.²²¹ Enhanced penalties signal FRA's determination that GPRC's conduct was "willful" or that prior violations existed, neither of which supports a "compliance defense."²²²
