### A. Legal Framework

Great Plains Railroad Company's (GPRC) safety compliance posture implicates multiple Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) regulatory regimes governing Class I freight railroad operations. As a Class I carrier operating 22,000 route-miles across 18 states with annual revenues exceeding $8.2 billion, GPRC is subject to comprehensive federal safety oversight under the Federal Railroad Safety Act of 1970, as amended.¹

#### 1. Positive Train Control (PTC) Statutory Mandate

The Rail Safety Improvement Act of 2008 established mandatory implementation of Positive Train Control systems on certain main lines.² Congress enacted the PTC mandate following high-profile passenger rail collisions to prevent four categories of train accidents: (1) train-to-train collisions, (2) over-speed derailments, (3) incursions into established work zones, and (4) movements of trains through switches left in wrong position.³

**Statutory Citation:** 49 U.S.C. § 20157 requires Class I freight railroads to implement PTC systems on all main lines over which toxic-by-inhalation hazardous materials (TIH) are regularly transported, including crude oil unit trains.⁴ The statute originally mandated December 31, 2015 compliance, but the Positive Train Control Enforcement and Implementation Act of 2015 (PTCEI Act) extended the deadline to December 31, 2018 for railroads meeting interim hardware installation and spectrum acquisition milestones, with further extension to December 31, 2020 for railroads demonstrating Revenue Service Demonstration (RSD) progress.⁵

On December 29, 2020, FRA announced industry-wide achievement of PTC implementation on all 57,536 required freight and passenger railroad route-miles prior to the statutory deadline.⁶ Any railroad operating TIH-eligible main lines without PTC after this date operates in continuing violation of federal law.

#### 2. Track Safety Standards (49 CFR Part 213)

FRA's track safety standards prescribe minimum requirements for track geometry, rail condition, and inspection protocols based on track class and maximum operating speeds.⁷ Class I railroads operating freight trains at 60-80 mph must maintain Class 4 and Class 5 track standards, which mandate:

- **Visual inspection frequency:** Twice per calendar year with minimum 120-day intervals between inspections (49 CFR § 213.233)⁸
- **Internal rail inspection:** Ultrasonic testing at intervals not exceeding 370 days or 30 million gross tons (mgt), whichever is shorter (49 CFR § 213.237)⁹
- **Service failure rate threshold:** Not exceeding 0.1 service failures per year per mile for Class 4 and 5 track; 0.09 for routes carrying regularly scheduled passenger service or hazardous materials¹⁰

**"Service failure"** is defined as broken rail occurrence caused by compound fissure, transverse fissure, detail fracture, or vertical split head—internal rail defects detectable only through ultrasonic testing.¹¹ These defects develop over time in aging rail, particularly rail beyond its 40-year design life under heavy axle loads.

**Defective Rail Standards (49 CFR § 213.113):** When a track owner learns rail contains indications of internal defects, the owner must verify the indication within 4 hours and take immediate remedial action if defects meet specified severity thresholds.¹² Broken rail violations carry civil penalties up to $102,348 per ordinary violation or $238,809 per aggravated violation (violations resulting in death, serious injury, or substantial property destruction).¹³

#### 3. Hours of Service Regulations (49 CFR Part 228)

Congress enacted hours of service limitations in 1907 to prevent fatigue-related railroad accidents.¹⁴ Current regulations limit train employees to 12 consecutive hours on duty, with mandatory 10 consecutive hours off-duty in the preceding 24 hours.¹⁵ Each employee required or permitted to exceed duty limitations constitutes a separate violation, subject to civil penalties adjusted annually for inflation.¹⁶

#### 4. Hazardous Materials Transportation (49 CFR Parts 171-180)

Following the 2013 Lac-Mégantic, Quebec derailment (47 fatalities, downtown destruction), DOT and FRA implemented enhanced safety requirements for High-Hazard Flammable Trains (HHFTs)—trains consisting of 20 or more tank cars of flammable liquids in continuous block or 35 or more cars dispersed through the train.¹⁷

**Speed Restrictions (49 CFR § 174.310):**
- 50 mph maximum in all areas for HHFTs
- 40 mph maximum in High-Threat Urban Areas (HTUAs) for trains containing non-DOT-117 compliant tank cars¹⁸

**Route Analysis (49 CFR § 172.820):** Annual route analysis considering minimum 27 safety and security factors, including track class and maintenance, population density along route, emergency response capability, and alternative routing options.¹⁹

**DOT-117 Tank Car Phase-Out:** Legacy DOT-111 tank cars (non-jacketed, thin-wall construction) must be phased out by May 1, 2029 for Class 3 flammable liquids.²⁰ Interim jacketed CPC-1232 cars carrying crude oil face May 1, 2025 deadline.²¹

#### 5. STB Merger Approval Safety Considerations (49 U.S.C. § 11323)

The Surface Transportation Board's authority to approve major railroad consolidations under 49 U.S.C. § 11323 requires consideration of "public interest" factors, including carrier safety and efficiency.²² STB's Major Rail Consolidation Procedures (effective 2001) mandate Safety Integration Plans (SIPs) formulated in coordination with FRA to ensure safe operations during post-merger integration.²³

FRA's Administrator must provide written satisfaction that the merged entity provides "reasonable assurance" of safety consistent with government regulations.²⁴ In recent major merger proceedings (CP-KCS 2023), FRA submitted filings highlighting implications for rail safety, network fluidity, and operational integration challenges.²⁵ STB has authority to impose merger conditions including enhanced safety oversight, accelerated compliance timelines, and post-merger performance monitoring.

#### B.4 Hours of Service Violations: Crew Management System Failures

**Conclusion:** GPRC's 15 hours-of-service violations in 2024 (49 CFR Part 228) resulting in $180K penalties represent **MEDIUM** severity systematic crew management and scheduling failures, likely caused by inadequate crew management software, workforce shortages, and service pressure related to BLET contract expiration (December 31, 2024). **Rule:** Under 49 CFR § 228.7, train employees may not remain on duty more than 12 consecutive hours, and must have at least 10 consecutive hours off duty in the preceding 24-hour period.⁶⁵ Each employee required or permitted to be on duty beyond prescribed limits represents separate and distinct violation, subjecting the railroad to separate civil penalty.⁶⁶ Current penalty schedule provides $850-$34,130 per violation for hours-of-service offenses, adjusted annually for inflation.⁶⁷

**Explanation:** Hours of service regulations, first enacted in 1907, address fatigue-related railroad accidents by limiting consecutive duty periods and mandating rest intervals.⁶⁸ FRA's enforcement philosophy treats hours-of-service violations as strict liability offenses—railroads are liable regardless of whether fatigue actually impaired employee performance or caused incidents.

In *Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers v. Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe Railway Co.*, the Supreme Court held that hours-of-service limitations "are not subject to waiver by agreement between carriers and employees," establishing federal preemption over state law and collective bargaining agreements that purport to allow longer duty periods.⁶⁹ [VERIFIED:BLE-v-ATSF-402-US-210] This precedent means GPRC cannot defend violations by citing operational necessity, labor agreement provisions, or employee consent.

FRA enforcement data shows hours-of-service violations cluster in three scenarios: (1) inadequate crew scheduling systems lacking real-time duty period tracking; (2) workforce shortages forcing railroads to work available crews beyond legal limits; and (3) managerial pressure to maintain service levels despite compliance constraints.⁷⁰ [ASSUMED:FRA-enforcement-analysis-hours-of-service-violation-patterns] Violations concentrated in specific terminals suggest localized management failures; violations dispersed system-wide indicate enterprise-level scheduling deficiencies.

**Application:** Here, GPRC incurred 15 hours-of-service violations in 2024, resulting in $180,000 in penalties—average of $12,000 per violation.⁷¹ For a Class I railroad with 18,500 operating craft employees (locomotive engineers, conductors, brakemen, signal maintainers), 15 violations represents systemic pattern rather than isolated incidents. Assuming GPRC operates approximately 400-500 train starts daily across its 22,000-mile network, 15 violations over 365 days yields 0.004% violation rate—seemingly low in absolute terms but sufficiently concentrated to trigger FRA enforcement action.

**Liability Valuation:**
- **Classification:** Hybrid (one-time capital expenditure + ongoing increased labor costs)
- **Methodology:** DCF combining software implementation and incremental staffing
- **Calculation:**
  - Enhanced crew management software: $5M-$10M (one-time capital)
  - Additional operating craft employees: 50-100 FTEs × $120K fully loaded annually = $6M-$12M/year
  - Additional crew schedulers: 10-15 FTEs × $150K annually = $1.5M-$2.25M/year
  - Year 1 total: $12.5M-$24.25M (capital + first-year labor)
  - Years 2-5 annual: $7.5M-$14.25M (labor only)
  - NPV over 5 years at 8%: $35M-$64M
- **Result:** $12.5M-$24.25M immediate requirement (Year 1)
- **Discount Rate Basis:** 8% WACC

**Probability Assessment:** 85% probability Continental must implement crew management system upgrades and increase staffing levels [METHODOLOGY: Hours-of-service violations reflect technology and workforce deficiencies objectively verifiable through data room inspection; remediation is operational necessity regardless of STB merger conditions].

GPRC's hours-of-service violations likely correlate with BLET labor dispute. The fact-registry confirms BLET contract expired December 31, 2024, with GPRC and BLET in National Mediation Board (NMB) mediation since January 2025.⁷² BLET represents 5,600 locomotive engineers demanding 22% wage increase over 3 years.⁷³ Workforce tensions, hiring freezes pending contract settlement, and potential crew shortages create conditions where schedulers face pressure to extend duty periods to maintain service levels.

The 15 violations' concentration in single year (2024) versus apparent absence in prior years (not mentioned in FRA enforcement summary for 2022-2023) suggests recent deterioration in compliance correlated with labor dispute timeline. If violations continue or escalate in 2025-2026 absent resolution, FRA may impose operating restrictions or enhanced monitoring requirements.

Root cause analysis requires data room access to:
- Individual violation incident reports (crew names, duty period specifics, reasons for exceedance)
- Geographic distribution (concentrated in specific divisions/terminals vs. system-wide)
- Crew management software specifications and capabilities
- Workforce staffing levels and hiring trends (2022-2024)
- Comparison of scheduled vs. actual crew starts (service reliability metrics)

**Counter-Analysis:** GPRC may argue that 15 violations across 18,500-employee operating craft workforce represents 0.08% error rate, demonstrating substantial compliance rather than systematic failure. GPRC will contend that occasional hours-of-service violations are inevitable in railroad operations involving unpredictable delays (weather, equipment failures, customer demand surges) and that FRA's penalty assessment reflects strict liability enforcement, not culpable conduct.

This defense has limited merit because: (1) hours-of-service violations are preventable through adequate crew staffing and robust scheduling systems—peer Class I railroads with similar operational complexity maintain near-zero violation rates; (2) FRA assessed penalties for all 15 violations rather than issuing warnings, suggesting violations exceeded FRA's enforcement discretion thresholds; (3) the concentration in single year indicates recent systematic failure, not acceptable baseline error rate; and (4) correlation with BLET labor dispute timeline suggests GPRC is using inadequate workforce levels to gain leverage in contract negotiations, subjecting employees to unsafe fatigue conditions to achieve cost savings—precisely the conduct hours-of-service regulations prohibit.
