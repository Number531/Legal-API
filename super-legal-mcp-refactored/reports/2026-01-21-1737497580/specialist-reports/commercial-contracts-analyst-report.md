### B. Regulatory Framework for Reinsurance Credit

State insurance regulators permit ceding insurers to take reserve credit for reinsurance ceded only when reinsurers meet specific financial security standards. This protection ensures the ceding insurer can fulfill policyholder obligations even if a reinsurer defaults.

**Authorized vs. Unauthorized Reinsurer Status:**

- **Authorized reinsurers:** Licensed in the ceding insurer's state of domicile, allowing full reserve credit without collateral requirements
- **Unauthorized reinsurers:** Not licensed in the ceding insurer's state, requiring collateral (letter of credit, trust fund, or funds withheld) equal to reserves ceded for the ceding insurer to take reserve credit

For LLIC (Nebraska-domiciled), this distinction determines whether reinsurers must post hundreds of millions in collateral to secure statutory reserve credit.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### PART A: REINSURANCE TREATY TYPES AND STANDARD TERMS

#### 1. Coinsurance (Global Re Term Life, Vermont Captive AXXX/XXX)

**Structure and Mechanics:**

Coinsurance represents a proportionate sharing arrangement where the ceding insurer (LLIC) transfers both premiums and statutory reserves to the reinsurer, which assumes a proportionate share of all policy obligations.¬π

**Key Commercial Terms:**

- **Premium Transfer:** LLIC cedes specified percentage of gross premiums to reinsurer (e.g., 90% of term life premiums to Global Re)
- **Reserve Transfer:** LLIC cedes proportionate statutory reserves to reinsurer, taking reserve credit that reduces LLIC's balance sheet liabilities
- **Risk Transfer:** Reinsurer assumes proportionate mortality/morbidity risk, paying its percentage share of death claims
- **Ceding Commission:** Reinsurer typically pays ceding commission to LLIC (80-120% of first-year premium) to offset the ceding insurer's deferred acquisition costs ("DAC"), including sales commissions, underwriting expenses, and policy issuance costs¬≤

**Statutory Reserve Relief Mechanics:**

When LLIC cedes 90% of term life reserves to Global Re under coinsurance:
- LLIC establishes 100% of statutory reserves when policies issued
- LLIC takes 90% reserve credit for amount ceded to Global Re
- LLIC's net statutory reserves = 10% (retained portion only)
- This reserve relief increases LLIC's statutory surplus and Risk-Based Capital (RBC) ratio

**Example:** $2.3 billion term life reserves ceded to Global Re
- Gross reserves if retained: $2.3B liability on LLIC's balance sheet
- Reserve credit taken: $2.3B reduction in liabilities
- Net statutory reserves: $0 (offset by reinsurance recoverables asset)
- Surplus impact: +$2.3B improvement (all else equal)

**Experience Refunds:**

Many coinsurance treaties include profit-sharing provisions. If actual mortality experience proves more favorable than pricing assumptions (fewer death claims than expected), the reinsurer refunds a percentage of profits to the ceding insurer. The Global Re treaty provides for 50% experience refunds, incentivizing LLIC to maintain high underwriting quality while sharing in favorable results.

**Recapture Provisions:**

Coinsurance treaties typically allow the ceding insurer to recapture ceded business after a specified period (commonly 10-15 years). Recapture provisions serve multiple purposes:

- Allow ceding insurer to bring profitable business back onto its own books if mortality experience favorable
- Provide reinsurer protection during early high-expense years when ceding commission and acquisition costs create losses
- Create optionality for ceding insurer to optimize capital deployment based on evolving financial position

**When LLIC exercises recapture:**
1. Reinsurance treaty terminates for recaptured policies
2. LLIC reestablishes 100% of statutory reserves on its balance sheet
3. Collateral (if any) released back to reinsurer
4. LLIC resumes paying 100% of death claims
5. LLIC retains 100% of future premiums

**Capital Impact of Recapture:**

Recapture creates significant statutory surplus pressure because reserves return to LLIC's balance sheet but collateral (if posted by unauthorized reinsurer) is released. For Global Re:

- Reserves returning to LLIC: $2.3B (estimated 90% of term life block)
- Barclays LOC collateral released: $850M
- **Net surplus reduction: $1.45B** ($2.3B reserves minus $850M collateral)

This $1.45 billion surplus reduction would severely impact LLIC's RBC ratio, as analyzed in detail in Section IV.B.3.C below.

#### 2. Modified Coinsurance / "Mod-Co" (Swiss Re IUL)

**Structure and Mechanics:**

Modified coinsurance represents a hybrid structure where:
- **LLIC retains investment assets** (continues managing portfolio backing ceded policies)
- **Swiss Re assumes mortality risk only** (receives mortality charges, pays death claims)
- **Experience adjustment mechanism** settles financial differences quarterly¬≥

This structure proves particularly advantageous for indexed universal life ("IUL") products where:
- LLIC maintains expertise in indexed account crediting strategies (S&P 500 caps, floors, participation rates)
- LLIC manages complex hedging programs for indexed account options
- Swiss Re gains mortality risk exposure without needing to manage IUL-specific investment operations

**Modified Coinsurance Reserve:**

Under statutory accounting principles:
- LLIC establishes "modified coinsurance reserve" liability equal to statutory reserves for ceded policies
- LLIC simultaneously establishes offsetting asset (retained portfolio backing the policies)
- Net statutory impact: Reserve credit taken (reduces net reserves), but asset retained on balance sheet
- **Key distinction from coinsurance:** Assets remain on LLIC's balance sheet; only net mortality risk transferred

**Experience Adjustment Quarterly True-Up:**

Each quarter, LLIC and Swiss Re perform an experience adjustment calculation:

**LLIC owes Swiss Re if:** Investment income earned on retained assets > Mortality charges credited to Swiss Re under policy terms

**Swiss Re owes LLIC if:** Mortality charges credited to Swiss Re > Investment income earned on retained assets

This quarterly settlement ensures Swiss Re receives appropriate compensation for assuming mortality risk while LLIC retains investment performance risk.

**Experience Adjustment Disputes:**

The experience adjustment calculation requires agreement on multiple assumptions:
- **Interest crediting rate:** What rate should be credited to Swiss Re for investment income? If LLIC earns 5.0% on IUL assets but treaty specifies 4.5% credited to Swiss Re, LLIC retains 0.5% spread‚Äîa common source of disputes.
- **Mortality charge accuracy:** Verification that cost of insurance ("COI") deductions from policyholders match treaty assumptions
- **Expense allocations:** Whether certain investment management or administrative expenses should adjust the experience settlement
- **Indexed account crediting:** For IUL policies with S&P 500 indexed accounts, how option costs and hedging expenses impact the settlement

Standard mod-co treaties include detailed dispute resolution procedures (typically binding arbitration) due to the complexity of quarterly calculations over multi-decade policy durations.

**No Collateral Requirement (Authorized Reinsurer):**

Swiss Re maintains authorization in Nebraska (licensed in the state), qualifying as an authorized reinsurer. LLIC takes full reserve credit for the 50% of IUL reserves ceded to Swiss Re without any collateral requirement. Swiss Re's A+ rating from A.M. Best provides additional financial strength assurance.

However, if Swiss Re were downgraded below specified thresholds (commonly BBB+ or A-), Nebraska Department of Insurance ("DOI") could require Swiss Re to post collateral retroactively even for an authorized reinsurer, or require LLIC to establish additional statutory reserves as a contingency buffer.

#### 3. Yearly Renewable Term / "YRT" (Munich Re Group Life)

**Structure and Mechanics:**

YRT reinsurance operates as excess of retention coverage, fundamentally different from quota share arrangements (coinsurance and mod-co):

- **LLIC retains first $250K per life** (retention layer)
- **Munich Re covers excess amounts** from $250K up to $5M per life
- **Annual renewable premium** paid by LLIC to Munich Re based on covered lives, ages, and amounts at risk
- **Catastrophic protection purpose:** Smooths large claim volatility from occasional high-value death benefits‚Å¥

**No Statutory Reserve Relief:**

YRT excess coverage does not provide statutory reserve relief because:
- LLIC retains 100% of policy obligations up to $250K per life
- LLIC establishes statutory reserves for entire group term life block
- YRT covers only excess amounts (rare claims exceeding $250K)
- **No reserve credit taken** for YRT reinsurance

YRT serves a risk management function (reducing surplus volatility from large individual claims) rather than a capital relief function.

**YRT Premium Calculation:**

Munich Re charges annual YRT premium based on:
- **Age distribution** of covered lives in group policies
- **Amount at risk** above $250K retention for each covered life
- **Mortality assumptions** applied to excess layer
- **Renewal annually** with premium adjustments as covered population ages or coverage amounts change

**Example Calculation:**

Group life block with 50,000 covered lives:
- 45,000 lives with coverage ‚â§$250K (no reinsurance premium; within LLIC retention)
- 4,500 lives with coverage $250K-$1M (average excess $400K; Munich Re premium ~0.15-0.25% = $600-$1,000 per life)
- 450 lives with coverage $1M-$3M (average excess $1.5M; Munich Re premium ~0.20-0.35% = $3,000-$5,250 per life)
- 50 lives with coverage $3M-$5M (average excess $3.5M; Munich Re premium ~0.25-0.40% = $8,750-$14,000 per life)

**Total estimated annual YRT premium: $5M-$8M** for catastrophic excess coverage protecting against large claim volatility.

**Retention Adequacy Analysis:**

The $250K retention level represents LLIC's assessment of the claims size it can absorb within normal operations without material surplus impact. With statutory surplus of $1.85 billion and Total Adjusted Capital ("TAC") of $1.85 billion, LLIC can readily absorb multiple $250K claims in any given year.

**Estimated annual large claims:**
- 5-10 death claims exceeding $250K per year
- Total excess losses: $2M-$5M annually (paid by Munich Re)
- LLIC retention on these large claims: $250K √ó 5-10 = $1.25M-$2.5M annually

**Retention adequacy:** $1.25M-$2.5M annual retention represents only 0.07-0.14% of LLIC's $1.85B surplus‚Äîwell within acceptable volatility tolerance. The $250K retention appears appropriately sized.

If LLIC reduced retention to $150K-$200K, YRT premium would increase significantly (Munich Re covering more of each large claim), and the incremental premium cost would exceed the value of reduced volatility on the additional $50K-$100K layer.

**Munich Re Credit Risk:**

- **A+ rating** from A.M. Best (strong financial strength)
- **Authorized reinsurer** in Nebraska (no collateral required)
- **Low absolute exposure:** Even if Munich Re defaulted, exposure limited to rare large claims exceeding $250K (5-10 claims per year = $2M-$5M annual exposure)
- **Operational risk:** LLIC could replace YRT coverage with alternative reinsurer within 30-60 days if Munich Re encountered financial difficulties

**Change of Control Provisions:**

YRT treaties traditionally include fewer restrictive change of control provisions than quota share coinsurance arrangements because:
- No reserve credit at stake (termination doesn't impact LLIC's statutory reserves)
- Annual renewable nature (either party can terminate on 90-180 days' notice)
- Limited capital impact (no recapture of reserves)

However, LLIC should review the Munich Re YRT treaty to confirm whether Munich Re consent is required for the American Financial Holdings acquisition. If consent required, Munich Re would likely grant consent given the limited financial impact and LLIC's continued operations.

#### 4. NAIC Model Law on Credit for Reinsurance

**Statutory Framework:**

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners ("NAIC") developed the Model Law on Credit for Reinsurance to establish uniform standards across states for when ceding insurers may take statutory reserve credit for reinsurance.‚Åµ Nebraska has adopted substantially similar provisions in its insurance code governing reinsurance credit.‚Å∂

**Core Principle:**

A ceding insurer may take reserve credit (reducing its statutory liabilities) for reinsurance ceded only if the reinsurer provides adequate security to protect policyholders. This ensures that if a reinsurer defaults, the ceding insurer has sufficient assets or guarantees to continue paying policyholder claims.

**Authorized Reinsurer Status ‚Äî Full Credit Without Collateral:**

Reinsurers meeting any of the following criteria qualify for "authorized" status, allowing ceding insurers to take full reserve credit without collateral:

1. **Licensed in ceding insurer's state:** Reinsurer holds certificate of authority in the ceding insurer's state of domicile (e.g., Swiss Re and Munich Re licensed in Nebraska)

2. **Accredited reinsurer:** Reinsurer licensed in another U.S. state, meets minimum capital/surplus requirements, and submits to domiciliary state jurisdiction

3. **Certified reinsurer:** Foreign (alien) reinsurers not licensed in U.S. but certified by NAIC-participating states based on jurisdiction evaluation (commonly Bermuda, UK, Switzerland, Germany, Japan qualifications)

4. **Reciprocal jurisdiction:** Reinsurer domiciled in qualified jurisdiction with substantially equivalent regulatory standards (recent NAIC initiative for EU Solvency II jurisdictions)

**Unauthorized Reinsurer ‚Äî Collateral Required:**

Reinsurers not meeting authorized status criteria are "unauthorized" reinsurers. Ceding insurers may still take reserve credit for reinsurance ceded to unauthorized reinsurers, but **only to the extent of collateral posted by the reinsurer.**‚Å∑

**Acceptable Collateral Forms:**

**1. Letter of Credit ("LOC"):**
- Issued by a qualified financial institution (typically U.S. bank or foreign bank branch with U.S. presence)
- Bank must maintain minimum credit rating (commonly A- or better from S&P/Moody's/Fitch)
- Irrevocable and unconditional commitment
- "Clean" LOC (beneficiary can draw upon certification of amount due without proving reinsurer default)
- Beneficiary: Ceding insurer (LLIC) or its statutory successor (e.g., state insurance commissioner if LLIC enters receivership)
- Term: Typically annual with evergreen automatic renewal provisions, or multi-year with mandatory renewal 60-90 days before expiration
- Amount: Equal to statutory reserves ceded, adjusted annually as reserves change

**Example:** Global Re Bermuda (unauthorized in Nebraska) posts $850M LOC from Barclays Bank to secure LLIC's reserve credit for $850M estimated reserves ceded.

**2. Trust Fund:**
- Reinsurer deposits assets in trust for benefit of ceding insurer
- Trustee: Independent qualified financial institution
- Trust assets: Typically high-quality securities (U.S. Treasury securities, investment-grade corporate bonds, cash)
- Withdrawals: Ceding insurer may withdraw trust assets to pay claims if reinsurer fails to perform
- Trust agreement: Specifies permissible investments, withdrawal procedures, valuation frequency
- Amount: Equal to statutory reserves ceded plus often a surplus margin (5-10% additional cushion)

**3. Funds Withheld:**
- Ceding insurer retains premiums otherwise payable to reinsurer
- Establishes "funds withheld" liability account on ceding insurer's balance sheet
- Reinsurer's share of premiums accumulated in funds withheld account
- Investment income credited to reinsurer's account
- Claims paid from funds withheld account
- If funds withheld exceed reserves ceded, excess paid to reinsurer; if insufficient, reinsurer must remit additional collateral

Funds withheld arrangements are common for affiliated reinsurance (captive reinsurance) but less common for third-party commercial reinsurance due to reinsurers' preference to receive cash premiums and invest them directly.

**Collateral Adjustment Mechanisms:**

Statutory reserves change over time as:
- Policies mature and reserves increase (term life policies in later years)
- Policies lapse and reserves decrease
- New policies added to reinsurance treaty
- Mortality experience differs from assumptions

Reinsurance collateral must adjust correspondingly. Standard LOC and trust fund arrangements include **annual adjustment provisions:**

- Within 60-90 days after year-end, ceding insurer certifies required collateral amount based on year-end statutory reserves
- If reserves increased, reinsurer increases LOC amount or deposits additional trust assets
- If reserves decreased, excess collateral released back to reinsurer
- Failure to adjust collateral upward when required = statutory reserve credit reduced proportionately (ceding insurer cannot take credit exceeding collateral posted)

**Example:** Global Re Barclays LOC
- 2024 year-end: $850M LOC (covering estimated $2.3B reserves ceded √ó 37% collateral ratio based on treaty terms)
- 2025: New term life policies added, reserves increase to $2.5B ceded
- Required LOC increase: $850M √ó ($2.5B / $2.3B) = $924M
- Barclays must amend LOC to $924M by March 31, 2025, or LLIC's reserve credit limited to $850M √∑ 37% = $2.3B (cannot take credit for incremental $200M reserves)

**Collateral Cost to Reinsurer:**

LOC fees represent a significant cost for unauthorized reinsurers:
- **Annual LOC fee:** 0.50-1.00% of LOC face amount (varies based on reinsurer credit rating and LOC issuer bank pricing)
- **For $850M LOC:** $4.25M-$8.5M annual fee expense
- **Over 10-year term:** $42.5M-$85M cumulative cost

This LOC cost effectively increases the reinsurer's expense ratio, reducing profitability on the reinsurance treaty. Unauthorized reinsurers must price this collateral cost into premium rates charged to ceding insurers, making unauthorized reinsurance more expensive than authorized reinsurance (all else equal).

**Bank Credit Risk ‚Äî LOC Issuer Ratings:**

The ceding insurer faces credit risk not only on the reinsurer but also on the LOC issuing bank. If the bank's credit rating declines below regulatory minimums, state insurance departments may:

- Require replacement LOC from higher-rated bank within 30-60 days
- Disallow reserve credit if replacement LOC not obtained
- Require ceding insurer to establish additional statutory reserves (eliminating reserve credit benefit)

For Global Re's $850M Barclays LOC:
- **Current Barclays rating:** A+ (S&P) ‚Äî acceptable under Nebraska standards
- **Minimum acceptable rating:** Typically A- or better
- **Downgrade risk:** If Barclays downgraded to BBB+ or below, Nebraska DOI would likely require Global Re to obtain replacement LOC from higher-rated institution (e.g., JPMorgan A+, Bank of America A, Wells Fargo A+) within 60 days

**Failure to replace LOC within required timeframe:**
- Reserve credit disallowed immediately upon expiration of grace period
- LLIC must reestablish full statutory reserves on balance sheet
- Surplus reduction = reserves ceded minus collateral previously posted
- **For Global Re example:** $2.3B reserves reestablished, but only $850M LOC remains as recoverable asset = $1.45B surplus reduction
- RBC ratio impact analyzed in Section IV.B.3.C below

**State-Specific Variations:**

While most states have adopted the NAIC Model Law substantially, variations exist:

- **New York:** More stringent requirements, historically requiring 100% trust fund collateral (full reserves ceded) for unauthorized reinsurers; recent amendments allow certified reinsurers with reduced collateral for highly-rated reinsurers
- **California:** Detailed regulations on trust fund permissible investments and valuation procedures
- **Texas:** Specific provisions for reciprocal jurisdiction reinsurers (Lloyd's of London, Bermuda commercial reinsurers)
- **Florida:** Enhanced collateral requirements for hurricane catastrophe reinsurance

For LLIC (Nebraska-domiciled), Nebraska Insurance Code provisions govern. LLIC must comply with Nebraska's authorized/unauthorized reinsurer standards and collateral requirements. However, for policies issued in other states, those states' insurance departments may conduct examinations and question reserve credit taken if collateral arrangements do not meet the examining state's standards, even if they satisfy Nebraska requirements.

##### C. Recapture Provisions ‚Äî 2030 First Cohort Eligibility

**Recapture Right:**

The Global Re treaty permits LLIC to recapture ceded policies after **10 years** from original policy issue date. This industry-standard recapture provision creates significant optionality for LLIC:

- **2020 policy issues:** Eligible for recapture beginning 2030
- **2021 policy issues:** Eligible for recapture beginning 2031
- **2022 policy issues:** Eligible for recapture beginning 2032
- **2023 policy issues:** Eligible for recapture beginning 2033
- **2024 policy issues:** Eligible for recapture beginning 2034

**Recapture Decision Factors:**

LLIC's decision whether to recapture involves multiple strategic considerations:

**1. Mortality Experience:**

*Favorable experience (actual mortality < expected):*
- Policies highly profitable
- Death claims lower than premiums less expenses = underwriting profit
- **LLIC incentive to recapture:** Bring profitable business back onto LLIC's books; stop sharing profits with Global Re (even with 50% experience refunds, LLIC forgoes 50% of profits to Global Re)
- **Economic value of recapture:** Present value of future profits on recaptured block minus recapture transition costs

*Unfavorable experience (actual mortality > expected):*
