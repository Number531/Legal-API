### B. Investment Company Act of 1940 â€” Separate Account Registration Structure

#### 1. Unit Investment Trust Registration for Insurance Separate Accounts

Insurance company separate accounts offering variable insurance products must register under the Investment Company Act of 1940, typically as unit investment trusts (UITs). The Investment Company Act defines three categories of investment companies: face-amount certificate companies, unit investment trusts, and management companies.â¸

**Unit Investment Trust Structure:** A UIT is an investment company organized under a trust indenture, contract of custodianship or agency, or similar instrument, which does not have a board of directors and does not issue redeemable securities.â¹ This structure is the predominant registration form for insurance company separate accounts offering variable life insurance and variable annuities.

**Critical Distinction from Management Companies:** Unlike management companies (mutual funds) that have boards of directors or trustees with ongoing investment management oversight, unit investment trusts do not have boards of directors, corporate officers, or investment advisers in the traditional sense.Â¹â° Instead, UITs maintain a relatively fixed portfolio or, in the case of variable insurance separate accounts, invest in underlying mutual fund subaccounts (Portfolio Companies) that are themselves registered management companies.

### C. FINRA Rule 2111 â€” Suitability Requirements for Variable Products

#### 1. Three-Part Suitability Framework

FINRA Rule 2111 imposes comprehensive suitability obligations on broker-dealers recommending securities transactions, including variable life insurance and variable annuities. The rule requires that a member or associated person have a reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy involving a security is suitable for the customer.Â¹â·

The rule establishes three distinct suitability obligations:

**a) Reasonable-Basis Suitability**

The reasonable-basis obligation requires a member or associated person to have a reasonable basis to believe, based on reasonable diligence, that the recommendation is suitable for at least some investors. This diligence must provide an understanding of the potential risks and rewards associated with the recommended security or strategy.Â¹â¸

For variable universal life insurance and variable annuities, reasonable-basis suitability requires the broker-dealer (Liberty Life Securities LLC) to conduct due diligence to understand:
- Product features: surrender charges (typically 7-10 years), mortality and expense (M&E) charges (0.90% for Liberty Life's VUL products), cost of insurance (COI) charges that increase with age, administrative fees
- Investment risks: market risk in separate account subaccounts, sequence of returns risk, volatility impact on cash value accumulation
- Insurance benefits: death benefit protection, guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefits (GMWB) for variable annuities, tax-deferral advantages
- Costs and breakeven analysis: breakeven periods (10-15 years typical for VUL given front-loaded costs and surrender charges), total cost of ownership over policy life
- Alternative products: comparison to term life insurance plus separate investment, traditional whole life, fixed annuities

**b) Customer-Specific Suitability**

The customer-specific obligation requires that a member or associated person have a reasonable basis to believe that the recommendation is suitable for a particular customer based on that customer's investment profile.Â¹â¹ FINRA Rule 2111 defines "investment profile" to include:

- **Age:** Critical for variable products with long-term surrender charges and breakeven periods. Customers age 75+ generally have shortened time horizons inconsistent with 10-15 year breakeven periods.
- **Other investments:** Portfolio diversification and concentration risk assessment.
- **Financial situation and needs:** Income sources, expenses, liquidity requirements, emergency fund adequacy.
- **Tax status:** Income tax bracket impacts tax-deferral benefits (variable products provide greater tax advantages for high-income taxpayers in elevated tax brackets).
- **Investment objectives:** Growth, income, capital preservation, estate planning, retirement income.
- **Investment experience:** Sophistication level, understanding of equity market risk, prior securities ownership.
- **Investment time horizon:** Long-term (10+ years) appropriate for VUL/VA; short-term (<5 years) inappropriate due to surrender charges.
- **Liquidity needs:** Variable products are illiquid during surrender charge period; customers must have alternative liquid assets for emergencies.
- **Risk tolerance:** Variable products with separate account market exposure require willingness to accept potential losses; unsuitable for conservative risk-averse investors who cannot tolerate market volatility.Â²â°

**c) Quantitative Suitability**

Quantitative suitability requires a member or associated person to have a reasonable basis for believing that a series of recommended transactions, even if suitable when viewed in isolation, are not excessive and unsuitable for the customer when taken together in light of the customer's investment profile. This obligation applies when the broker-dealer has actual or de facto control over the customer's account.Â²Â¹

For variable products, quantitative suitability concerns include:
- Multiple variable annuity contracts purchased within short time periods (stacking surrender charge periods)
- Excessive 1035 exchanges from existing annuities to new annuities, resetting surrender charges
- Over-concentration in variable products relative to customer's total portfolio

#### 2. FINRA Rule 2330 â€” Enhanced Requirements for Variable Annuities

FINRA adopted Rule 2330 to provide additional targeted protections specifically for deferred variable annuities. Prior to recommending the purchase or exchange of a deferred variable annuity, members must make reasonable efforts to obtain customer information including age, annual income, financial situation and needs, investment experience, investment objectives, intended use, investment time horizon, existing assets, liquidity needs, liquid net worth, risk tolerance, and tax status.Â²Â²

**Suitability Determination Requirements:**
- Member must have a reasonable basis to believe the customer has been informed of various features of deferred variable annuities, including potential surrender charges, tax penalties for early withdrawal, mortality and expense charges, investment advisory fees, potential changes to death and living benefit features, and market risk.
- Member must have a reasonable basis to believe that the customer would benefit from certain features of deferred variable annuities, such as tax-deferred growth, annuitization, or death or living benefits (e.g., GMWB riders on Liberty Life's variable annuities).Â²Â³

**Supervisory Requirements under Rule 2330:**
Rule 2330 imposes heightened supervisory obligations, requiring firms to implement surveillance procedures specifically designed to detect recommendations in violation of the rule. #### 3. Application to Liberty Life's October 2023 FINRA Violations

The assignment background describes three suitability violations identified in Liberty Life Securities LLC's October 2023 FINRA examination:

**Violation Pattern:** Three agents recommended variable universal life (VUL) insurance to customers age 75+ with limited income ($35,000-$45,000/year from Social Security and pension), no earned income, and limited liquid net worth.

**Customer-Specific Suitability Failures:**

a) **Age and Time Horizon Mismatch:**
- VUL products have breakeven periods of 10-15 years due to front-loaded costs (surrender charges, high first-year commissions embedded in M&E charges, COI charges)
- Customers age 75+ have actuarial life expectancies of approximately 11-13 years (male) or 13-15 years (female) per Social Security Administration life tables
- The probability of policy lapse or death before breakeven significantly reduces the likelihood that customers will benefit from the product
- **Suitability Violation:** Age 75+ creates time horizon incompatibility with VUL's long-term structure, violating customer-specific suitability requirements under Rule 2111

b) **Income and Affordability Concerns:**
- Limited income ($35,000-$45,000/year) from fixed sources (Social Security, pension) with no earned income growth potential
- VUL premiums must be maintained to prevent policy lapse; if premium payments cease, high policy charges (M&E 0.90%, COI, admin fees) rapidly erode cash value
- Customers with limited fixed income lack financial capacity to sustain premium payments over 10-15+ year periods, particularly if unexpected expenses arise
- **Suitability Violation:** Limited income creates affordability risk and likelihood of policy lapse, violating customer-specific suitability requirements

c) **Liquidity and Emergency Fund Deficiency:**
- VUL policies subject to surrender charges (typically 7-10 years, declining annually)
- Early surrender triggers surrender charges plus potential tax consequences (ordinary income on gains above basis)
- Customers age 75+ with limited income and limited liquid net worth lack emergency reserves; tying assets in illiquid VUL products creates financial distress risk
- **Suitability Violation:** Insufficient liquidity violates customer-specific suitability requirements under Rule 2111

d) **Cost Structure Unsuitability:**
- M&E charges: 0.90% annually
- Cost of insurance (COI): Increases exponentially with age; age 75+ COI charges typically 5-10Ã— higher than younger insureds
- Administrative fees: Fixed annual charges
- Surrender charges: 7-10 year declining schedule
- **Combined Effect:** High ongoing costs erode cash value, making it unlikely customers age 75+ with limited income will accumulate meaningful cash value or achieve positive returns before death
