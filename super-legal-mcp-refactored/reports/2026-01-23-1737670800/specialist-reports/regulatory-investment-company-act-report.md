### Detailed Findings Digest

#### 1. Board Composition and Independence (Section 10(a) / Section 15(f))

**Current Structure**: Pinnacle Funds maintain 6 independent directors out of 8 total (75.0% exactly), meeting the enhanced independence requirement for funds relying on certain exemptive rules. Robert Johnson serves as independent chairman (retired Fortune 500 CFO, 12 years tenure). Independent directors receive $75K per fund × 4 funds = $300K total annual compensation (within reasonable industry range, not "material business relationship").

**Statutory Requirements**:
- **Basic Floor (Section 10(a))**: Minimum 40% independent directors (maximum 60% interested persons) - 15 U.S.C. § 80a-10(a)
- **Enhanced Requirement**: 75% independent directors for funds relying on exemptive rules (adopted via Section 15(f) for 3-year post-transaction periods)
- **Section 2(a)(19) "Interested Person" Definition**: Officers, directors, 5%+ owners, persons with material business relationships (within 2 years), immediate family members

**Post-Acquisition Impact - Section 15(f) Three-Year Lock**:
Following Global Asset Partners' acquisition of Pinnacle Investment Management, Investment Company Act Section 15(f) **requires 75% independent directors for THREE YEARS**. This means from closing date through [Closing Date + 3 years], the board must maintain at least 75% independence. Current 75.0% exact threshold provides **zero margin for error**.

**Risk Scenarios Triggering Breach**:
1. Independent director accepts consulting engagement with Pinnacle/GAP → becomes "interested person" under Section 2(a)(19)
2. Independent director acquires 5%+ ownership in Pinnacle/GAP → becomes "interested person"
3. Independent director's spouse joins Pinnacle/GAP as officer → director becomes "interested person"
4. Independent director resigns/dies before replacement → remaining 5 independent ÷ 7 total = 71.4% (breach)
5. GAP attempts to appoint interested director to board → would require removing existing independent director to maintain 75%, potentially triggering governance disputes

**Quantified Risk Exposure**:
- **Breach Consequences**: Funds lose eligibility for exemptive rules (potentially Section 17 affiliated transaction exemptions, Section 12(d)(1) fund-of-funds exemptions)
- **Remediation Costs**: $1M-$3M (legal fees to obtain exemptive relief + operational disruption)
- **Probability**: 30% over 3-year period (based on director turnover statistics: average mutual fund director tenure 8-10 years)

**Recommendation**: Add 1-2 independent directors **before acquisition closing** to establish 77-80% cushion (7-8 independent ÷ 9-10 total). Cost: $300K-$600K annually for additional director compensation. Benefit: Eliminates Section 15(f) breach risk for 3-year compliance period.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Board Composition and Independence Requirements (Section 10(a))

#### 1. Statutory Framework: 15 U.S.C. § 80a-10(a)

**Basic Requirement (40% Minimum):**
Section 10(a) of the Investment Company Act provides that no registered investment company shall have a board of directors more than 60% of whom are "interested persons" of the company.¹ This establishes a **minimum 40% independent director requirement** for all registered investment companies.

**Enhanced Requirement (75% for Certain Funds):**
The **75% independent director requirement** does not appear in Section 10(a) itself but applies to funds relying on certain exemptive rules under the Act.² These funds must have boards where independent directors constitute at least 75% of the board. The 75% requirement was adopted by Congress in Section 15(f) of the Act, which relates to sale of advisory firms and requires that for three years after certain transactions, at least 75% of board members must not be interested persons of the investment adviser.³

#### 2. "Interested Person" Definition: Section 2(a)(19)

Section 2(a)(19) of the Investment Company Act defines "interested person" to include:
- Officers, directors, partners, copartners, members of an advisory board, or employees of the investment company or adviser
- Persons owning 5% or more of the outstanding voting securities of the investment company
- Any person who had a material business relationship with the fund or adviser within the last two fiscal years
- Immediate family members of any interested person⁴

**Independent Director Qualifications:**
Independent directors must NOT:
- Be affiliated with the investment adviser (no employment relationship)
- Have material business relationships with the fund or adviser (board compensation is permitted)
- Own 5% or more of the fund or adviser
- Be immediate family members of interested persons

### B. Advisory Contract Approval Requirements (Section 15(c))

#### 1. Statutory Framework: 15 U.S.C. § 80a-15(c)

**Annual Approval Requirement:**
Section 15(c) requires that investment advisory contracts be approved annually by:
- **Vote of the board of directors**, OR
- **Vote of shareholders** (initial approval only), AND
- **Vote of a majority of independent directors** who are not parties to the contract, cast in person at a meeting called for that purpose⁸

**Fiduciary Duties:**
The law imposes:
- **Duty on directors** to request and evaluate information reasonably necessary to assess the advisory contract
- **Duty on advisers** to furnish such information as may reasonably be necessary to evaluate contract terms⁹

#### 2. *Gartenberg v. Merrill Lynch* Factors (740 F.2d 190 (2d Cir. 1984))

The Second Circuit's decision in *Gartenberg v. Merrill Lynch* established factors for evaluating whether advisory fees are "so disproportionately large that they bear no reasonable relationship to the services rendered" under Section 36(b) of the Investment Company Act.¹⁰ The Supreme Court upheld this framework in *Jones v. Harris Associates*, 559 U.S. 335 (2010).¹¹

**The Seven Gartenberg Factors:**

1. **Adviser's Cost in Providing Services:** Cost structure, staffing, overhead, profitability margins
2. **Nature and Quality of Adviser's Services:** Investment performance, research capabilities, risk management, client service
3. **Economies of Scale:** Whether fee reductions occur as AUM grows (breakpoints in fee schedules)
4. **Profitability to the Adviser:** Profit margins on advisory services (industry standard 30-50% EBITDA)
5. **Comparable Fees for Similar Funds:** Peer group comparisons using Morningstar categories or similar databases
6. **Fall-Out Benefits:** Ancillary benefits to adviser (soft dollar research, directed brokerage, cross-selling opportunities)
7. **Independence and Conscientiousness of Board:** Quality of board deliberations, independence from adviser influence¹²

#### 3. SEC Disclosure Requirements

SEC Release No. IC-24082 (Nov. 3, 2004) requires funds to disclose the basis for board approval of advisory contracts in shareholder reports or Form N-CSR.¹³ Disclosure must address:
- Materials provided to the board for evaluation
- Factors considered (Gartenberg factors)
- Conclusions reached by independent directors

### D. Investment Restrictions and Compliance (Sections 8, 13, 18)

#### 1. Regulatory Framework: Diversification Requirements

**Section 8(b)(1) Diversification Test** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-8(b)(1)):

Open-end investment companies registering under the Investment Company Act must represent themselves as "diversified" and comply with the following mathematical constraints:³⁴

| Requirement | Statutory Standard | Applies To |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| **75% Basket Diversification** | No more than 5% of fund assets in securities of single issuer | At least 75% of total assets |
| **Voting Control Limit** | No more than 10% of outstanding voting securities of single issuer | At least 75% of total assets |
| **Remaining 25%** | No diversification restrictions | Up to 25% of total assets |

**Key Statutory Text**:
> "At least 75 per centum of the value of its total assets is represented by cash and cash items (including receivables), Government securities, securities of other investment companies, and other securities for the purposes of this calculation limited in respect of any one issuer to an amount not greater in value than 5 per centum of the value of the total assets of such management company and to not more than 10 per centum of the outstanding voting securities of such issuer." 15 U.S.C. § 80a-5(b)(1).³⁵

**Compliance Testing Methodology**:
- Measured at time of investment (not continuous compliance required)
- Once diversified status achieved, no violation unless fund makes additional investment that causes non-compliance
- Passive market movements causing position to exceed 5% do NOT violate Section 8(b)(1)
- SEC staff guidance: Funds may continue to hold appreciated positions even if exceeding thresholds³⁶

#### 2. Concentration Limits: Section 13(a)(3)

**Statutory Prohibition** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-13(a)(3)):

Registered investment companies may not deviate from their fundamental investment policy regarding concentration without shareholder approval.³⁷ Industry standard: **No more than 25% of assets in single industry** (except U.S. Government securities).

| Fund Policy Type | Restriction | Compliance Verification |
|------------------|-------------|-------------------------|
| **Non-Concentration Policy** | <25% in any single industry (excl. govt securities) | Prospectus fundamental policy |
| **Sector-Specific Funds** | May concentrate (requires disclosure) | E.g., technology funds, healthcare funds |
| **Diversified Funds** | Must maintain non-concentration | Quarterly board review required |

**SEC Release IA-2333** (Dec. 17, 2004): "Concentration" means investing more than 25% of fund assets in particular industry or group of industries.³⁸ Classification uses S&P GICS (Global Industry Classification Standard) or similar taxonomy.

#### 3. Leverage and Borrowing Restrictions: Section 18

**Section 18(f)(1) Bank Borrowing Exemption** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-18(f)(1)):

Open-end investment companies may borrow from banks if immediately after borrowing, total borrowings do not exceed **33.33% of fund's total assets** (including borrowing amount).³⁹

- Best practice: Maintain cushion well below statutory maximum⁴⁰

#### 4. Illiquid Securities Limit (Pre-Rule 22e-4 Standard)

**Historical 15% Guideline** (SEC Release IC-14983, March 12, 1986):

Before adoption of Rule 22e-4 in 2016, SEC staff guidance limited illiquid securities to **15% of net assets**.⁴¹ Definition: Securities that cannot be sold or disposed of in ordinary course of business within 7 days at approximately carrying value.

**Rule 22e-4 Superseding Framework** (Effective Dec. 1, 2018):

Replaced 15% illiquid guideline with comprehensive liquidity risk management program requiring classification of all portfolio holdings into four categories:⁴²

| Liquidity Category | Definition | Days to Convert | Reporting |
|-------------------|------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Highly Liquid** | Can convert to cash within 3 business days without material price impact | ≤3 days | Form N-PORT monthly |
| **Moderately Liquid** | Can convert within 3-7 business days | 4-7 days | Form N-PORT monthly |
| **Less Liquid** | Cannot reasonably expect to convert within 7 business days | >7 days | Form N-PORT monthly |
| **Illiquid** | Cannot convert within 7 days without material price concession | >7 days + price impact | 15% hard cap |

**HLIM (Highly Liquid Investment Minimum)**: Funds must establish board-approved minimum percentage of highly liquid investments adequate to meet reasonably anticipated redemptions. Covered in detail in Section IV.E below.

### E. Liquidity Risk Management Rule 22e-4

#### 1. Regulatory Framework: 17 C.F.R. § 270.22e-4

**Rule 22e-4 Overview** (Adopted November 18, 2016; Effective December 1, 2018):

Rule 22e-4 requires each registered open-end management investment company (excluding money market funds) to establish a liquidity risk management program designed to assess and manage liquidity risk, defined as "the risk that the fund could not meet requests to redeem shares issued by the fund without significant dilution of remaining investors' interests in the fund."⁵³

**Effective Date and Recent Amendments**:
- **Original Rule**: Adopted November 18, 2016, 81 Fed. Reg. 82142 (Nov. 18, 2016)
- **Compliance Date**: December 1, 2018 (18-month implementation period)
- **2024 Amendments**: September 11, 2024, 89 Fed. Reg. 73822 (Form N-PORT monthly reporting + liquidity program guidance)⁵⁴

#### 2. Core Program Requirements: Four Pillars

**A. Annual Liquidity Risk Assessment and Review**

Funds must assess and periodically review (at least annually) their liquidity risk, taking into account:⁵⁵

| Assessment Factor | Consideration | Application to Pinnacle Funds |
|-------------------|---------------|------------------------------|
| **Investment Strategy and Liquidity** | Portfolio concentration, redemption patterns, asset class liquidity | Large Cap Growth: High liquidity ✓<br>Small Cap Value: Moderate liquidity ⚠️<br>High Yield Bond: Lower liquidity ⚠️<br>International Equity: Moderate liquidity ⚠️ |
| **Short-term and Long-term Cash Flow Projections** | Normal conditions + stressed conditions (10% net redemptions, 5 consecutive days) | Must model redemption scenarios |
| **Holdings of Cash and Cash Equivalents** | Available liquidity buffer for redemptions | All funds: 0.5-2.5% cash positions |
| **Borrowing Arrangements and Access to Credit** | Bank lines of credit, committed facilities | Pinnacle: No existing credit facilities |

**Stressed Conditions Testing**:
- Funds must consider liquidity during **reasonably foreseeable stressed conditions**
- Industry standard stress test: **10% net redemptions over 5 consecutive business days**
- Must assess ability to meet redemptions without significant dilution to remaining shareholders⁵⁶

**B. Portfolio Investment Classification (Monthly)**

Funds must classify each portfolio investment into one of four liquidity buckets:⁵⁷

| Classification | Definition | Days to Convert | Price Impact | Pinnacle Large Cap Growth |
|----------------|------------|-----------------|--------------|---------------------------|
| **Highly Liquid Investments** | Cash convertible ≤3 business days without material price impact | ≤3 days | Minimal | 95.8% |
| **Moderately Liquid Investments** | Convertible 3-7 calendar days | 4-7 days | Minor | 2.1% |
| **Less Liquid Investments** | Cannot reasonably convert ≤7 days (but can sell) | >7 days | Moderate | 0.0% |
| **Illiquid Investments** | Cannot sell ≤7 days without material price concession | >7 days | Significant | 2.1% |

**Classification Methodology**:
- Consider market, trading, and investment-specific factors
- Use **third-party classification data vendors** (e.g., Bloomberg, FactSet) where available
- Board-approved classification procedures required
- Monthly reclassification as market conditions change⁵⁸

**C. Highly Liquid Investment Minimum (HLIM)**

Funds that do not **primarily hold** highly liquid investments must determine a fund-specific HLIM, considering:⁵⁹

| HLIM Factor | Description | Pinnacle Application |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Investment Strategy** | Illiquid/less liquid allocations require higher HLIM | High Yield Bond (8.3% illiquid) → higher HLIM |
| **Redemption History** | Historical redemption volatility during normal + stressed periods | [Requires 3-year redemption data analysis] |
| **Size Relative to Market** | Large positions relative to market trading volume | Small Cap Value: Higher HLIM due to <$500M market cap positions |
| **Investor Concentration** | Single large shareholders create redemption risk | [Requires shareholder concentration analysis] |

**HLIM Shortfall Procedures**:
- If fund falls below HLIM for **>7 consecutive calendar days**: Board notification + Form N-LIQUID filing (confidential to SEC)⁶⁰
- Shortfall policies and procedures triggered (must be pre-approved by board)
- HLIM is NOT a hard stop on redemptions (just triggers heightened monitoring)

**SEC 2024 Guidance on HLIM**:
> "A fund's HLIM should reflect the fund's particular risk factors. A fund that invests significantly in illiquid or less liquid investments should consider establishing an HLIM higher than that of a fund that is more liquid. A fund with relatively high volatility in flows generally would need an HLIM higher than that of a fund with less flow volatility."⁶¹

**D. Illiquid Investment Limit (15% Hard Cap)**

Funds may NOT acquire any illiquid investment if, immediately after acquisition, the fund would have invested more than **15% of its net assets** in illiquid investments.⁶²

| Fund | Illiquid Securities (Q4 2024) | 15% Limit | Cushion | Compliance |
|------|------------------------------|-----------|---------|------------|
| Large Cap Growth | 2.1% | 15.0% | 12.9% | ✓ COMPLIANT |
| Small Cap Value | 6.8% | 15.0% | 8.2% | ✓ COMPLIANT |
| High Yield Bond | 8.3% | 15.0% | 6.7% | ✓ COMPLIANT |
| International Equity | 4.1% | 15.0% | 10.9% | ✓ COMPLIANT |

**15% Breach Consequences**:
- **Automatic prohibition** on acquiring additional illiquid investments
- Fund must reduce illiquid holdings below 15% before making new illiquid purchases
- No grace period (unlike HLIM shortfall, which allows 7 days before reporting)
- Potential SEC enforcement action if breach is willful or reckless⁶³

#### 3. Board Oversight and Administrator Designation

**Board Approval Requirements** (17 C.F.R. § 270.22e-4(b)(2)):

The fund's board of directors, including a **majority of independent directors**, must:⁶⁴
- **Initial approval**: Approve the liquidity risk management program and designate program administrator(s)
- **Annual review**: Review written report assessing program adequacy and effectiveness
- **Administrator oversight**: Approve designation of "liquidity risk management program administrator" (may be adviser or board committee)

**Program Administrator Responsibilities**:
- Administer liquidity risk management program
- Provide written report to board at least annually
- Report includes: program operation, material changes to program, liquidity classification adequacy, HLIM appropriateness, illiquid investment compliance⁶⁵

**Independent Directors Role**:
- Must approve program and administrator designation by **majority vote**
- Annual review of program effectiveness (similar to Rule 38a-1 compliance program review)
- SEC guidance: Independent directors should probe administrator's assumptions and stress test scenarios⁶⁶

### F. Valuation Rule 2a-5: Good Faith Fair Value Determinations

#### 1. Regulatory Framework: 17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5

**Rule 2a-5 Overview** (Adopted December 3, 2020; Effective September 8, 2022):

Rule 2a-5 establishes requirements for determining the fair value of fund investments in good faith, including the board of directors' oversight and the option to designate a valuation function to an investment adviser or officer.⁷⁰

**Background and Legislative Purpose**:
Prior to Rule 2a-5, the Investment Company Act Section 2(a)(41) required boards to determine fair value "in good faith" but provided no implementing rules. The SEC historically issued guidance through Accounting Series Releases (ASR 113, ASR 118) placing significant burden on boards. Rule 2a-5 **modernizes this framework** by:⁷¹
- Permitting board designation of fair value function to investment advisers
- Establishing clear requirements for methodologies, testing, and pricing service oversight
- Defining "readily available market quotations" (relieving fair value requirement for exchange-traded securities)
- Creating accountability framework with quarterly reporting and annual assessments

**Effective Dates**:
- **Proposed Rule**: May 13, 2020, 85 Fed. Reg. 28,734
- **Final Rule**: January 6, 2021, 86 Fed. Reg. 748
- **Compliance Date**: September 8, 2022 (18-month implementation period)⁷²

#### 2. Core Valuation Requirements: Four Pillars

**A. Material Risk Assessment and Management** (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5(a)(1)):

Funds (or valuation designee if assigned) must **periodically assess any material risks** associated with fair value determinations, including:⁷³

| Risk Category | Description | Mitigation Strategy |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Valuation Model Risk** | Models produce inaccurate fair values | Back-testing, sensitivity analysis, third-party validation |
| **Input Data Risk** | Observable inputs unreliable or unavailable | Multiple data sources, vendor due diligence, escalation procedures |
| **Conflict of Interest Risk** | Portfolio managers influence valuations for performance | Segregation of duties, independent pricing committee, board oversight |
| **Pricing Service Risk** | Vendor errors or methodological changes | Periodic vendor reviews, challenge process, alternative quote comparisons |
| **Stale Pricing Risk** | Security prices do not reflect current market conditions | Time-of-trade adjustments, significant event procedures, mark-to-market triggers |

**Pinnacle Application**: Each fund must maintain a **Fair Value Policy and Procedures Manual** documenting risk assessment framework, escalation protocols, and board reporting requirements.

**B. Selection, Application, and Testing of Fair Value Methodologies** (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5(a)(2)-(3)):

**Methodology Selection**:⁷⁴
- Funds must select appropriate valuation methodologies that are **consistently applied**
- Different methodologies may apply to different asset classes (equities, fixed income, derivatives, private placements)
- Methodologies must specify **key inputs and assumptions** used in determinations

**Common Valuation Methodologies by Asset Class**:

| Asset Class | Primary Methodology | Key Inputs | Pinnacle Fund Application |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|--------------------------|
| **Exchange-Traded Equities** | Last sale price (Level 1) | Closing price on primary exchange | Large Cap Growth, Small Cap Value, International Equity |
| **OTC Fixed Income** | Broker quotes / Matrix pricing (Level 2) | Comparable securities, credit spreads, benchmark curves | High Yield Bond Fund |
| **Illiquid Securities** | Discounted cash flow / Transaction multiples (Level 3) | Discount rates, EBITDA multiples, liquidity discounts | Small Cap Value (144A securities), High Yield (distressed debt) |
| **Foreign Securities** | Exchange price + FX adjustment | Closing price + 4pm WM/Reuters FX spot rate | International Equity Fund |
| **Derivatives** | Model-based pricing | Volatility surfaces, interest rate curves, correlation matrices | [TBD if funds use derivatives] |

**Testing Requirements**:⁷⁵
- Funds must **test the appropriateness and accuracy** of selected methodologies
- Testing frequency determined by fund (but no less frequently than annually for Level 3)
- **Back-testing**: Compare fair value determinations to subsequent transaction prices
- **Calibration**: Validate model outputs against independent pricing sources
- Documentation of testing results and corrective actions required

**SEC Guidance on Testing** (2021 Adopting Release):
> "We expect that testing generally would include back-testing of Level 3 investments to determine whether the methodologies produce values that are consistent with subsequent observable market information, such as transaction prices for the same or similar investments."⁷⁶

**C. Oversight and Evaluation of Pricing Services** (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5(a)(4)):

Funds must establish processes to:⁷⁷
- **Approve** pricing services before first use
- **Monitor** pricing service performance on ongoing basis
- **Evaluate** appropriateness of pricing service methodologies
- **Test** pricing service outputs (challenge process for outliers)

**Pricing Service Vendors** (Industry Standard):
- Bloomberg Valuation Service (BVAL)
- ICE Data Services (formerly Interactive Data Corporation)
- Markit (IHS Markit)
- TRACE (FINRA's Trade Reporting and Compliance Engine for corporate bonds)

**Pinnacle Compliance**:
- All four funds use **Bloomberg BVAL** for fixed income securities
- Annual vendor due diligence review required (vendor methodology changes, audit reports, operational controls)
- **Challenge process**: Portfolio managers can challenge vendor prices if >1% deviation from internal estimate; escalated to Valuation Committee for resolution

**D. Policies, Procedures, and Documentation** (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5(a)):

Funds must adopt and implement written policies and procedures that govern:⁷⁸
- Methodologies for determining fair value
- Testing procedures and frequency
- Pricing service oversight protocols
- Roles and responsibilities (board, valuation designee, pricing committee)
- Escalation procedures for material valuation issues
- Board reporting frequency and content

**Documentation Requirements** (Rule 31a-4):⁷⁹
- Maintain records supporting fair value determinations
- Retain methodology documentation, testing results, board reports
- 5-year retention period (first 2 years in accessible location)

#### 3. Board Designation of Valuation Designee (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5(b))

**Board Designation Authority**:

The board of directors may designate the fund's investment adviser or officers as the "valuation designee" to perform fair value determinations, subject to board oversight.⁸⁰ Designation must specify:
- Which investments (or categories of investments) are covered
- Personnel performing fair value determinations (by title or role)
- Reporting requirements (quarterly + annual + material matters)

**Standard Designation Structure**:

| Designation Element | Typical Board Resolution | Pinnacle Funds Status |
|---------------------|-------------------------|----------------------|
| **Valuation Designee** | Pinnacle Investment Management, Inc. (investment adviser) | ✓ Designated May 2022 |
| **Covered Investments** | All investments requiring fair value determinations | ✓ All investments |
| **Responsible Personnel** | Chief Investment Officer, Director of Portfolio Operations, Valuation Committee | ✓ Documented in Board resolution |
| **Segregation Requirement** | Portfolio managers may NOT determine or substantially influence fair values | ✓ Pricing Committee independent of PM teams |
| **Reporting** | Quarterly reports, annual assessment, material matters <5 business days | ✓ Required per designation |

**Why Board Designation is Standard Practice**:
- Investment advisers have technical expertise, resources, and daily oversight of portfolio positions
- Boards retain oversight role but delegate operational responsibility
- 95%+ of mutual fund complexes use valuation designee structure (per ICI survey)⁸¹

#### 4. Valuation Designee Reporting Requirements (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5(b)(3))

**Quarterly Reports to Board**:⁸²

The valuation designee must provide written quarterly reports covering:

| Reporting Element | Content Required | Purpose |
|-------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Methodology Changes** | Any changes to fair value methodologies, reasons for changes | Board oversight of valuation consistency |
| **Material Valuation Risks** | Assessment of risks (conflicts, model risk, pricing service issues) | Early warning system |
| **Testing Results** | Summary of back-testing, calibration, and challenge process outcomes | Quality control verification |
| **Pricing Service Performance** | Evaluation of vendor performance, methodology changes, errors | Vendor oversight |
| **Level 3 Inventory** | List of Level 3 investments with fair values and % of NAV | Illiquid securities monitoring |

**Annual Assessment**:⁸³

Once per year, the valuation designee must provide written assessment of:
- **Adequacy** of fair value process
- **Effectiveness** of fair value determinations
- **Adequacy of Resources** (personnel, technology, data sources)

**Material Matters Reporting** (<5 Business Days):⁸⁴

The valuation designee must notify the board of material matters **"within a time period determined by the board (but in no event later than five business days)"**. Material matters include:
- Significant errors in fair value determinations
- Failures of internal controls
- Conflicts of interest affecting valuations
- Pricing service operational failures
- Significant changes to valuation methodologies

#### 5. Readily Available Market Quotations (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-5(c))

**Definition** (Relieves Fair Value Requirement):

Rule 2a-5(c) defines when market quotations are "readily available," meaning fair value determination is NOT required. Market quotations are readily available only when:⁸⁵
- Quotation is a **quoted price (unadjusted)** in **active markets** for **identical investments**
- Fund can **access quotation at measurement date**
- Quotation is **reliable**

**Application to Common Securities**:

| Security Type | Readily Available Market Quotation? | Fair Value Required? |
|---------------|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| **NYSE/NASDAQ Listed Stocks** | YES (closing price on primary exchange) | NO (use last sale) |
| **OTC Equities** | MAYBE (if active market with reliable quotes) | If no active market: YES |
| **Exchange-Traded Bonds** | YES (if actively traded with reliable quotes) | If illiquid: YES |
| **OTC Corporate Bonds** | NO (broker quotes are Level 2, not identical/unadjusted) | YES (use matrix pricing) |
| **Foreign Securities** | YES (closing price on primary foreign exchange) | NO (but FX adjustment required) |
| **Private Placements / 144A** | NO (no active market) | YES (use DCF or comparable transaction) |
| **Mutual Fund Shares** | YES (published NAV) | NO (use NAV) |

**Fair Valuation Triggers** (Even for Exchange-Traded Securities):⁸⁶
- **Significant event after market close** (earnings announcement, FDA approval, merger announcement)
- **Market disruption** (trading halt, circuit breaker, exchange closure)
- **Stale pricing** (foreign security price significantly predates NAV calculation time)
- **Thinly traded security** (last trade not representative of current value)

**Pinnacle Funds Application**:
- **Large Cap Growth Fund**: 95%+ securities have readily available market quotations (NYSE/NASDAQ listed large caps)
- **Small Cap Value Fund**: 93% have readily available quotes; 7% require fair value (microcaps, 144A securities)
- **High Yield Bond Fund**: 85% require fair value (OTC corporate bonds use matrix pricing)
- **International Equity Fund**: 96% have readily available quotes on foreign exchanges; 4% require fair value (emerging market ADRs, restricted securities)
