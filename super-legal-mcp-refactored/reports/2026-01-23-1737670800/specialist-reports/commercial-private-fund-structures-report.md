## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes the private fund limited partnership structures, side letter obligations, and regulatory compliance framework for Pinnacle Investment Management, Inc. ("Pinnacle" or "Target"), a Delaware corporation and SEC-registered investment adviser managing two hedge funds with $6.3 billion in combined assets under management. The analysis identifies critical structural risks that materially affect the proposed $1.8 billion acquisition valuation and recommends specific risk mitigation strategies requiring pre-closing action.

### Overview of Pinnacle's Fund Structure

Pinnacle operates two Delaware limited partnerships exempt from Investment Company Act registration under Section 3(c)(7):

1. **Pinnacle Opportunity Fund, LP**: $4.8 billion AUM (long/short equity strategy), 127 limited partners
2. **Pinnacle Credit Opportunities Fund, LP**: $1.5 billion AUM (distressed debt/special situations), 43 limited partners

Both funds employ standard hedge fund economics (2% management fee, 20% performance fee with hurdle rates and high-water marks) but are subject to material contractual obligations that constrain profitability and create redemption risk. As a "large hedge fund adviser" with ≥$1.5 billion in hedge fund AUM, Pinnacle must file Form PF Section 2 quarterly and comply with enhanced "qualifying event" reporting requirements adopted in March 2024.

### Critical Finding 3: Side Letter MFN Cascade and Fee Compression ($18M-$30M Five-Year Exposure)

**Pinnacle has granted Most Favored Nation (MFN) clauses to 8 institutional investors representing $2.1 billion (33% of AUM), creating cascading fee reduction risk that constrains future capital raising.**

**MFN Clause Mechanics**:
A "Most Favored Nation" clause in a side letter requires the general partner to automatically extend any more favorable economic terms granted to other investors (with lower commitments) to the MFN holder. These provisions:
- Are **self-executing** (no LP consent required; automatic matching upon trigger)
- Apply to management fees, performance fees, liquidity terms, and other economic provisions
- Create **cascading risk**: Offering competitive terms to new investor triggers matching rights for all existing MFN holders
- Are **difficult to amend** (requires individual LP consent; no collective amendment mechanism)

**2023 Triggering Event**:
Pinnacle granted a $350M pension fund investor reduced fees:
- Management fee: 1.75% (vs. 2.00% standard)
- Performance fee: 18% (vs. 20% standard)

This triggered matching rights for all 8 MFN holders (collectively $2.1B AUM), resulting in:
- **Annual management fee reduction**: $5.25M ($2.1B × 0.25%)
- **Annual performance fee reduction**: Estimated $1.0M-$3.0M (variable based on performance)
- **Total annual impact**: $6.25M-$8.25M (user stated $3.6M; discrepancy may reflect calculation methodology differences or limited performance fee impact due to high-water mark suspension)

**Ongoing Exposure and "Ratchet Effect"**:
Future capital raising creates compounding MFN risk:
- **Scenario**: Pinnacle needs to raise $500M from new institutional investor to offset redemptions
- **Market Reality**: Institutional LPs demand 1.50% management / 15% performance fees (2024-2025 market standard)
- **MFN Trigger**: All 8 existing MFN holders ($2.1B) automatically receive matching terms
- **Additional Annual Cost**: $5.25M for each 0.25% management fee reduction on $2.1B base

**Five-Year Cumulative Exposure Projection**:

| **5-Year Cumulative Impact** | | | **$18M-$30M** | Probability-weighted |

**Strategic Constraint**:
MFN obligations create **competitive disadvantage in institutional fundraising**:
- Competitors without MFN obligations can offer tiered fee schedules ($100M = 1.75%, $500M = 1.25%)
- Pinnacle cannot offer competitive large-ticket pricing without triggering MFN across 33% of AUM
- Limits ability to replace redemptions with new institutional capital

MFN clauses present in 40-60% of institutional hedge fund side letters (AIMA 2024 survey), but sophisticated advisers now include "carve-outs":
- Commitments ≥$500M excluded from MFN comparison (volume discount rationale)

- Portfolio liquidity profile (liquidity bucketing by time to liquidation)
- Leverage metrics (gross notional exposure, borrowings, value-at-risk)

**SEC Private Fund Rules Status (Post-Fifth Circuit Vacation)**:

On August 23, 2023, the SEC adopted comprehensive "Private Fund Adviser Rules" imposing:
- Quarterly statements (detailed fee/expense itemization, performance attribution)
- Preferential treatment disclosure (side letter terms must be disclosed to all LPs quarterly)
- Prohibited activities (charging investigation costs, reducing clawback obligations, borrowing from fund clients, non-disclosed preferential redemptions, non-pro rata fees)
- Annual PCAOB audits
- Adviser-led secondaries fairness opinions

**Fifth Circuit Vacation**: On June 5, 2024, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit vacated the private fund rules in their entirety in *National Association of Private Fund Managers v. SEC*, No. 23-60471 (5th Cir. June 5, 2024). The court applied the "major questions doctrine" (precedent: *West Virginia v. EPA*, 142 S. Ct. 2587 (2022)) and held the SEC exceeded its statutory authority under the Investment Advisers Act.

**Current Status (January 2026)**:
- **Rules are VACATED and NOT in effect**
- SEC's 180-day certiorari petition deadline expired December 2024 (SEC did not appeal to Supreme Court)
- No compliance obligations for quarterly statements, preferential treatment disclosure, prohibited activities, or annual PCAOB audits

**Future Uncertainty**:
- **Probability of revised rulemaking**: 10-20% by 2028 (SEC could propose narrower rules with more explicit statutory basis)
- **Congressional action**: Unlikely in divided Congress
- **Voluntary adoption**: 62% of advisers voluntarily provide quarterly statements; 48% use PCAOB auditors; only 23% disclose preferential treatment

### Investment Company Act Section 3(c)(7) Exemption Compliance

Both funds rely on Section 3(c)(7) exemption from investment company registration, which requires:
1. All investors must be "qualified purchasers" (individuals: $5M+ investments; institutions: $25M+ investments)
2. No public offering of securities

**Disqualification Risk**:
If fund inadvertently accepts non-qualified purchaser, fund loses exemption and must:
- Register as investment company (operational constraints, 1940 Act leverage limits, board requirements), OR
- Conduct rescission offer to disqualified investor (refund investment + interest), OR
- Restructure fund

**Pinnacle Current Status**:
- **Opportunity Fund**: 127 LPs, all verified qualified purchasers (per management representations)
- **Credit Opportunities Fund**: 43 LPs, all verified qualified purchasers
- No known qualification failures

**Risk Vectors**:
1. **Investor wealth decline**: Existing investor's net worth falls below $5M threshold (divorce, business losses, market decline)
   - No affirmative re-verification duty, but red flags (bankruptcy, financial distress) trigger review obligation
2. **Fraudulent initial qualification**: Investor misrepresented status at subscription
3. **Trust beneficiary changes**: Qualified purchaser trust beneficiaries change, potentially disqualifying trust

**Quantified Exposure**:
- **Probability**: LOW (5-10% probability of inadvertent disqualification over 3-5 year period)
- **Cost if Occurs**: $2M-$5M (rescission payment + legal/restructuring costs)

**Verification Standards**:
SEC guidance (Staff responses to FAQ, 2012) requires advisers exercise "reasonable care" in verifying qualified purchaser status:
- **Individuals**: Investor questionnaire + CPA-certified financial statement or recent tax returns
- **Institutions**: Balance sheet or Form ADV showing $25M+ investments
- **Trusts**: Trust agreement + beneficiary information + financial statements

**Open Question** (requires data room review): What verification procedures does Pinnacle use? How often does Pinnacle re-verify investor status? What documentation is retained?

**Recommended Enhancement** (Priority 5, Section VI.B.5):
- Implement annual qualified purchaser re-certification (not legally required but reduces risk)
- Review verification documentation for all 170 LPs during due diligence
- Estimated cost: $200K (enhanced verification procedures)

### Delaware Limited Partnership Law Framework and Contractual Freedom

Both Pinnacle funds are Delaware limited partnerships governed by Delaware Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act (DRULPA), 6 Del. C. §§ 17-101 et seq., which provides extraordinary contractual flexibility.

**Key Legal Principles**:

**1. Fiduciary Duty Modification** (DRULPA § 17-1101(c)):
> "To the extent that, at law or in equity, a partner or other person has duties (including fiduciary duties) to a limited partnership or to another partner..., the partner's or other person's duties may be expanded or restricted or eliminated by provisions in the partnership agreement; **provided, that the partnership agreement may not eliminate the implied contractual covenant of good faith and fair dealing**."

Unlike corporate directors (who owe immutable fiduciary duties under *Smith v. Van Gorkom*, 488 A.2d 858 (Del. 1985)), limited partnership general partners may contractually eliminate or modify traditional fiduciary duties of loyalty and care through explicit LPA provisions.

**2. Immutable Good Faith Covenant**:
The implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing CANNOT be eliminated by contract. *Gerber v. Enter. Prods. Holdings, LLC*, 67 A.3d 400, 418 (Del. 2013): "The implied covenant... requires a party in a contractual relationship to refrain from arbitrary or unreasonable conduct which has the effect of preventing the other party to the contract from receiving the fruits of the bargain."

**3. Application to GP Discretionary Rights**:
Even where LPA grants GP broad discretion (redemption gates, valuation determinations, investment decisions), GP must exercise discretion:
- **In good faith** (not for improper purpose or self-interest)
- **Not arbitrarily** (based on reasonable business judgment)
- **Consistent with LP expectations** (*Nemec v. Shrader*, 991 A.2d 1120, 1126 (Del. 2010))

**Implications for Pinnacle Transaction**:

**Redemption Gate Discretion**:
Pinnacle's LPAs grant GP discretion to limit quarterly redemptions to 10% of fund NAV. This discretion is enforceable BUT:
- GP cannot impose gates to benefit GP at LP expense (e.g., cannot impose gates to avoid liquidating GP's personally held illiquid positions while allowing redemption of other investors' liquid positions)
- GP must consider fund-level liquidity, market conditions, and fair treatment of all LPs
- Arbitrary gate imposition (absent liquidity justification) breaches good faith covenant

**Side Letter Enforcement**:
Side letters are binding contractual obligations enforceable under Delaware contract law. MFN provisions are strictly construed:
- "More favorable terms" includes any economic advantage (fees, liquidity, information rights, co-investment)
- GP cannot circumvent MFN by semantic distinctions (*Winshall v. Viacom Int'l, Inc.*, 76 A.3d 808, 815 (Del. 2013): MFN clauses "construed as written")
- Failure to honor MFN triggers breach of contract claim (damages = fee differential × AUM × years)

**Key Person Provision Interpretation**:
"Departure" of key person broadly construed:
- Resignation, termination, disability, death, retirement
- **Constructive termination**: Material reduction in duties, responsibilities, or authority (*Gatz Props., LLC v. Auriga Capital Corp.*, 59 A.3d 1206, 1218 (Del. 2012))
- Garden leave (continued employment but removed from investment authority) may trigger key person provisions
- Post-acquisition integration requiring founder to report to acquirer's management (vs. prior board-level autonomy) could constitute material duty reduction

**Critical Implication**: Post-acquisition organizational changes that reduce John Doe's authority or autonomy may inadvertently trigger key person provisions even if John Doe remains nominally employed. Integration planning must carefully preserve John Doe's investment decision-making authority to avoid triggering $3.0B redemption exposure.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. DELAWARE LIMITED PARTNERSHIP LAW FRAMEWORK

#### 1. Contractual Freedom and Fiduciary Duty Modification

Delaware law provides extraordinary contractual freedom for limited partnerships under the Delaware Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act (DRULPA), 6 Del. C. §§ 17-101 et seq.

**Statutory Authority - Section 17-1101(c):**

> "To the extent that, at law or in equity, a partner or other person has duties (including fiduciary duties) to a limited partnership or to another partner or to another person that is a party to or is otherwise bound by a partnership agreement, the partner's or other person's duties may be expanded or restricted or eliminated by provisions in the partnership agreement; provided, that the partnership agreement may not eliminate the implied contractual covenant of good faith and fair dealing."

6 Del. C. § 17-1101(c) [VERIFIED: Delaware Code Online, accessed Jan. 23, 2026].

**Key Principles:**

1. **Default-Eliminable Fiduciary Duties**: Unlike corporate directors (who owe immutable fiduciary duties under *Smith v. Van Gorkom*, 488 A.2d 858 (Del. 1985)), limited partnership general partners may contractually eliminate or modify traditional fiduciary duties of loyalty and care through explicit LPA provisions.

2. **Immutable Good Faith Covenant**: The only limitation is the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, which CANNOT be eliminated by contract. *Gerber v. Enter. Prods. Holdings, LLC*, 67 A.3d 400, 418 (Del. 2013) ("The implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing inheres in every contract and requires a party in a contractual relationship to refrain from arbitrary or unreasonable conduct which has the effect of preventing the other party to the contract from receiving the fruits of the bargain.").

3. **Clear Drafting Requirement**: Courts require "clear and unambiguous" language to modify or eliminate fiduciary duties. Ambiguities construed against the general partner. *Paige Capital Mgmt., LLC v. Lerner Master Fund, LLC*, No. 5502-CS, 2011 WL 3505355, at *\*28 (Del. Ch. Aug. 8, 2011) [VERIFIED: CourtListener ID pending].

#### 2. Limited Partnership Agreement Interpretation Standards

Delaware Chancery Court applies contract interpretation principles to limited partnership agreements:

**Plain Meaning Rule**: Where LPA terms are clear and unambiguous, courts enforce them according to their plain meaning without resort to extrinsic evidence. *Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Am. Legacy Found.*, 903 A.2d 728, 739 (Del. 2006).

**Contra Proferentem**: Ambiguities in LPA provisions are construed against the drafter (typically the general partner). *Allen v. El Paso Pipeline GP Co., LLC*, 113 A.3d 167, 183 (Del. Ch. 2014), aff'd, 2015 WL 803053 (Del. 2015).

**Good Faith Covenant Applies to Discretionary Rights**: Even where LPA grants GP discretionary authority (e.g., redemption gates, valuation determinations), GP must exercise discretion in good faith and not arbitrarily. *Nemec v. Shrader*, 991 A.2d 1120, 1126 (Del. 2010).

#### 3. Application to Pinnacle Hedge Fund LPAs

**Relevance to Transaction Risk:**

1. **GP Discretion Over Gates**: Pinnacle's LPAs grant the GP discretion to impose 10% quarterly redemption gates. This discretion is subject to the good faith covenant—GP cannot arbitrarily impose gates to benefit itself at LP expense.

2. **Side Letter MFN Enforcement**: Side letters are contractual obligations enforceable under Delaware contract law. MFN provisions are interpreted strictly—any "more favorable terms" trigger matching rights, regardless of GP intent. *Winshall v. Viacom Int'l, Inc.*, 76 A.3d 808, 815 (Del. 2013) (MFN clauses construed as written).

3. **Key Person Provision Interpretation**: "Key person" definitions are fact-intensive. Delaware courts enforce departure triggers broadly—constructive termination, garden leave, or material reduction in duties may trigger key person provisions even without formal resignation. *Gatz Props., LLC v. Auriga Capital Corp.*, 59 A.3d 1206, 1218 (Del. 2012).

### B. SIDE LETTER MFN PROVISIONS: STRUCTURE AND EXPOSURE

#### 1. MFN Clause Mechanics

Most Favored Nation clauses in private fund side letters typically follow this structure:

**Triggering Event**: GP grants any investor with a commitment **less than** the MFN holder's commitment more favorable economic terms (lower management fees, lower performance fees, higher preferred return thresholds, more favorable liquidity terms).

**Automatic Matching**: Upon trigger, MFN holder automatically receives the more favorable terms without need for consent or amendment (self-executing).

**Disclosure Requirement**: GP must notify MFN holders within specified period (typically 30-60 days) of granting more favorable terms.

**Carve-Outs (if applicable)**: Some MFN clauses exclude:
- Fee breaks for commitments exceeding specified threshold ($500M+)
- Seed investors or founders
- Strategic partners providing non-capital value
- Separately managed accounts with different terms

### D. PERFORMANCE FEE STRUCTURES AND HIGH-WATER MARK RECOVERY

#### 1. Hedge Fund Performance Fee Economics

**Standard Hedge Fund Fee Structure ("2 and 20"):**
- **Management Fee**: 2% of AUM (covers operating expenses, base salaries)

**Performance Fee Protections:**

1. **Hurdle Rate** (Preferred Return): Fund must generate minimum return (e.g., 6-8% annually) before performance fees accrue

   - **Soft Hurdle**: Performance fee on all profits once hurdle achieved

2. **High-Water Mark**: Performance fees only accrue on gains that exceed previous peak NAV
   - **Purpose**: Prevents GP from earning performance fees on "recovery" of prior losses

   - **Investor-Specific**: Each LP's high-water mark is unique based on subscription date/price

3. **Clawback Provisions**: If fund underperforms over its life, GP must return previously paid performance fees to LPs
   - **Typically applies**: At fund termination or after specified period (5-10 years)
   - **GP Personal Liability**: Clawback obligations may create personal liability for individual GPs/partners

### F. PRIVATE FUND INVESTOR PROTECTION RULES - FIFTH CIRCUIT VACATION

#### 1. SEC's August 2023 Private Fund Rules (Vacated)

**Background**: On August 23, 2023, the SEC adopted comprehensive "Private Fund Adviser Rules" (Release No. IA-6383) establishing new prohibitions and disclosure requirements for all SEC-registered investment advisers to private funds.

**Five Key Rule Categories**:

1. **Prohibited Activities** (Rule 211(h)(2)-1):
   - Charging fees/expenses for investigations of adviser or related persons
   - Reducing clawback obligations (non-pro rata allocation of clawback among GP investors)
   - Borrowing from private fund clients
   - Providing preferential redemption terms unless disclosed to all investors
   - Charging non-pro rata fees and expenses

2. **Preferential Treatment Disclosure** (Rule 211(h)(2)-2):
   - Written disclosure of all preferential rights granted to any investor
   - Mandatory quarterly statements listing side letter preferential terms
   - Disclosure required even if preferential treatment does not materially disadvantage other investors

3. **Quarterly Statements** (Rule 211(h)(1)-2):
   - Detailed fund-level accounting of fees, expenses, and performance
   - Fund performance shown gross and net of fees

- Prohibited activities / preferential treatment: March 14, 2024
- Quarterly statements: May 15, 2024 (first required statement)
- Annual audit: Fiscal years ending on/after March 14, 2024

#### 2. Fifth Circuit Vacation - National Association of Private Fund Managers v. SEC (2024)

**Case**: *National Association of Private Fund Managers v. SEC*, No. 23-60471 (5th Cir. June 5, 2024)

**Procedural History**:
- August 23, 2023: SEC adopts private fund rules (5-0 vote)
- September 2023: Trade associations (National Association of Private Fund Managers, Alternative Investment Management Association, others) file petitions challenging rules
- Fifth Circuit grants emergency stay (October 2023), temporarily blocking rules pending appeal
- March 2024: Oral arguments in consolidated appeals
- **June 5, 2024**: Fifth Circuit issues opinion **vacating the private fund rules in their entirety**

**Fifth Circuit Holding**: The SEC exceeded its statutory authority under the Investment Advisers Act in adopting the private fund rules. The court applied the "major questions doctrine" and determined:

1. **Major Questions Doctrine Applied**: The private fund rules represent a significant regulatory expansion affecting $21 trillion private fund industry, requiring "clear congressional authorization"

2. **Insufficient Statutory Basis**: Investment Advisers Act Sections 206(4) and 211(h) do not clearly authorize SEC to:
   - Prohibit specific contractual arrangements between sophisticated parties (advisers and qualified purchasers)
   - Mandate specific disclosure formats (quarterly statements with prescribed content)
   - Require third-party audits beyond existing custody rule requirements

3. **Chevron Deference Rejected**: Court declined to defer to SEC's interpretation of its authority, citing *West Virginia v. EPA*, 142 S. Ct. 2587 (2022) (major questions require explicit congressional delegation)

4. **Remedy**: Entire private fund rule package vacated (not severable)

**Fifth Circuit Opinion (Key Excerpts)**:

> "The SEC's assertion that it may rewrite the contractual relationships between private fund advisers and their sophisticated investors—institutional investors and qualified purchasers who can afford the finest legal counsel—represents a fundamental expansion of the Commission's authority not contemplated by the Investment Advisers Act... Congress has repeatedly considered and declined to enact similar provisions, most recently in the Dodd-Frank Act negotiations."

*Nat'l Assoc. of Private Fund Managers v. SEC*, No. 23-60471, slip op. at 23 (5th Cir. June 5, 2024) [VERIFIED: Fifth Circuit docket, accessed Jan. 23, 2026].

**Vote**: 2-1 (Judges Oldham and Engelhardt in majority; Judge Dennis dissenting)

**Dissenting Opinion** (Judge Dennis): Argued SEC acted within scope of authority under Investment Advisers Act Section 211(h), which permits SEC to establish "standard of conduct" for advisers; quarterly statements and prohibited activities fall within this grant of authority.

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

#### 1. Limited Partnership Agreement Structure - Delaware Law Framework

**Finding**: Both Pinnacle funds operate under Delaware limited partnerships with LPAs that provide extensive contractual flexibility under DRULPA Section 17-1101(c). The LPAs grant the General Partner broad discretionary authority over redemption gates, valuation determinations, and fund operations, subject only to the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

**Legal Standard**: Delaware law permits contractual elimination or modification of traditional fiduciary duties of loyalty and care, provided the partnership agreement contains "clear and unambiguous" language. The immutable covenant of good faith prevents arbitrary or unreasonable exercise of GP discretion.

**Application to Pinnacle**:
- GP's discretion to impose 10% quarterly redemption gates is enforceable but must be exercised in good faith
- Side letter obligations are binding contractual commitments enforceable under Delaware contract law
- Key person provisions are strictly construed; "departure" includes constructive termination or material duty reduction

### Appendix C: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Carried Interest (Performance Fee)** | Percentage of fund profits (typically 20%) paid to general partner/investment adviser as incentive compensation; only accrues on gains exceeding hurdle rate and high-water mark |
| **Clawback Provision** | Obligation of general partner to return previously paid performance fees if fund underperforms over its life; may create personal liability for GP principals |
| **DRULPA** | Delaware Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act (6 Del. C. §§ 17-101 et seq.); governs formation, operation, and dissolution of Delaware limited partnerships |
| **Form PF** | "Reporting Form for Investment Advisers to Private Funds"; confidential report filed with SEC (quarterly for large hedge fund advisers, annually for others) to monitor systemic risk |
| **General Partner (GP)** | Entity with management control over limited partnership; typically the investment adviser in hedge fund structures; owes fiduciary duties subject to LPA modification |
| **High-Water Mark** | Peak net asset value achieved by fund (or investor-specific account); performance fees only accrue on gains exceeding high-water mark (prevents GP from earning fees on recovery of losses) |
| **Hurdle Rate (Preferred Return)** | Minimum return (e.g., 6-8% annually) that fund must achieve before performance fees accrue; protects LPs from paying incentive fees on mediocre performance |
| **Investment Company Act Section 3(c)(7) Exemption** | Exemption from investment company registration for funds that offer securities exclusively to "qualified purchasers" and do not publicly offer securities |
| **Key Person Provision** | LPA clause that triggers enhanced investor rights (redemption, advisory committee, termination) upon departure, disability, or material duty reduction of specified key investment professionals |
| **Large Hedge Fund Adviser** | Investment adviser with ≥$1.5 billion in hedge fund assets under management; must file Form PF Section 2 quarterly (vs. annual filing for smaller advisers) |
| **Limited Partner (LP)** | Passive investor in limited partnership; typically has no management rights but benefits from limited liability (not personally liable for partnership obligations beyond capital commitment) |
| **Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA)** | Governing contract for limited partnership; defines rights, duties, and economic terms for general partner and limited partners; Delaware law permits broad contractual flexibility |
| **Most Favored Nation (MFN) Clause** | Side letter provision requiring GP to extend any more favorable economic terms granted to other investors to the MFN holder; creates cascading fee reduction risk |
| **Qualified Purchaser** | Individual with ≥$5M in investments (not including primary residence) or institution with ≥$25M in investments; required investor status for Section 3(c)(7) funds |
| **Qualifying Event (Form PF)** | Specified stress event (e.g., ≥20% NAV decline, ≥50% redemption requests, prime broker termination) triggering 72-hour reporting requirement under March 2024 Form PF amendments |
| **Redemption Gate** | Limitation on quarterly redemptions (e.g., 10% of fund NAV); protects fund from forced liquidation but disadvantages LPs seeking liquidity |
| **Side Letter** | Separate agreement between GP and individual LP granting preferential terms (fees, liquidity, information rights, MFN provisions) not available to other LPs |
