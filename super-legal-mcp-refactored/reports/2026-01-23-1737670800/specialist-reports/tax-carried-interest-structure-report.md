## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This memorandum analyzes the federal and state tax treatment of **$23 million in performance fees** earned by Pinnacle Investment Management's general partners and senior portfolio managers from two hedge funds in 2024: Opportunity Fund ($19M, 130/30 long/short equity strategy) and Credit Opp Fund ($4M, distressed debt strategy). The analysis focuses on IRC Section 1061's three-year holding period requirement (enacted in the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act 2017), partnership pass-through taxation mechanics, Massachusetts state taxation including the 4% "millionaires tax" surtax, performance fee volatility implications, and legislative risk from recurring congressional proposals to eliminate carried interest capital gains treatment entirely.

**Key Finding:** The combined federal and state tax liability on the $23M performance fees is **$10.54 million (45.8% effective rate)**, leaving $12.46M after-tax compensation for distribution among Pinnacle's founder/CIO and senior PMs. This tax burden reflects the significant impact of Section 1061's asset-level lookthrough rule, which recharacterizes $6.75M (29%) of performance fees from long-term capital gains (23.8% rate) to short-term capital gains taxed as ordinary income (40.8% rate) due to the funds' portfolio turnover patterns and holding periods averaging less than three years.

### Key Takeaways

**1. IRC Section 1061 Three-Year Holding Period Materially Increases Tax Burden on Hedge Fund Carried Interest**

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act's enactment of IRC § 1061 (effective January 1, 2018) extended the holding period requirement from one year to **three years** for "applicable partnership interests" (APIs) to qualify for long-term capital gains treatment. This provision specifically targets carried interest in private investment funds by requiring lookthrough to the underlying assets' holding periods, not the GP's holding period of the API itself.

**Structural Mismatch with Hedge Fund Strategies:**
Hedge funds employing high-turnover strategies face **structural tax inefficiency** under Section 1061:
- **Opportunity Fund (130/30 long/short equity):** Portfolio turnover **150% annually** → average holding period **8 months** → **80% of performance fees** ($15.2M of $19M) taxed as ordinary income at 40.8% rate (37% federal + 3.8% NIIT)
- **Credit Opp Fund (distressed debt):** Average holding period **24 months** (bankruptcy cycle duration) → **60% of performance fees** ($2.4M of $4M) taxed as ordinary income due to Section 1061 recharacterization

**Tax Liability Breakdown — 2024 Performance Fees $23M:**

| Holding Period Category | Amount | Tax Rate | Federal Tax |
|-------------------------|--------|----------|-------------|
| **Short-term (<1 year)** | $10.85M (47%) | 40.8% | $4.43M |
| **Mid-term (1-3 years, §1061 recharacterized)** | $6.75M (29%) | 40.8% | $2.75M |
| **Long-term (>3 years, qualified)** | $5.40M (23%) | 23.8% | $1.28M |
| **TOTAL** | $23.00M | **37.0% blended** | **$8.51M** |

**Section 1061 Cost — Pre-TCJA Comparison:**
If pre-TCJA rules applied (1-year holding period for long-term capital gains), the mid-term gains ($6.75M held 1-3 years) would qualify as long-term capital gains at 23.8% rate → Federal tax would be **$7.32M** instead of $8.51M → **Section 1061 costs an additional $1.19M annually** (5.2 percentage points higher effective rate).

Over a 10-year acquisition holding period, assuming $15M average annual performance fees, the cumulative Section 1061 cost is estimated at **$7.8 million** in additional federal taxes that would not have been owed under pre-2018 law.

**[VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 1061(a) effective January 1, 2018 per Pub. L. 115-97 § 13309(c), Treasury Regulation § 1.1061-3 asset-by-asset lookthrough rule finalized January 19, 2021 (TD 9945), accessed via Cornell LII and Federal Register]**

**Coefficient of Variation Interpretation:**
A 70% coefficient of variation (standard deviation ÷ mean) indicates performance fees are **highly unpredictable** year-to-year, ranging from $0 (2022) to $27M (2021). This volatility creates three operational challenges:

1. **Operating Expense Coverage:**
Pinnacle must maintain $180M annual operating expenses (180 investment professionals' salaries, technology infrastructure, compliance systems, office facilities) regardless of performance fees. Management fees ($52.8M annually at 1.5-1.75% on $42.5B AUM) provide base coverage, but performance fees fund discretionary investments (new PM hires, technology upgrades, marketing). Zero-carry years like 2022 force expense cuts or reduce profitability.

2. **PM Compensation Volatility:**
Senior PMs receive **5-15% of total performance fees** as individual carried interest allocation (in addition to base management fee salary $500K-$1.2M). For a senior PM receiving 10% of carry:
- 2021 peak year: 10% × $27M = **$2.7M carry comp**
- 2022 zero year: **$0 carry comp** (only base salary)
- 2024 recovery: 10% × $23M = **$2.3M carry comp**

This **$0 to $2.7M annual swing** creates retention risk if competitors offer more stable guaranteed bonus structures.

3. **Earnout Structure Risk:**
The Global Asset Partners acquisition includes **$150M maximum earnout** for Pinnacle founders, payable over years 1/2/3 post-close based on **AUM retention >$40B**. If performance fees remain suppressed due to high-water mark overhang (see below), founders earn less carry → reduced incentive to stay through earnout period → retention risk increases.

**High-Water Mark Overhang — Persistent Drag on Future Carry:**

**Current Situation (December 31, 2024):**
- Opportunity Fund current NAV: **$88.5** (per share)
- High-water mark (2021 peak): **$95.2**

- **Recovery required:** +7.6% appreciation
- **Dollar recovery:** $6.7 × $4.8B AUM = **$322 million portfolio appreciation needed**

**High-Water Mark Mechanics:**
Performance fees are payable only if NAV exceeds the **high-water mark** (prior peak NAV). The 2022 bear market loss (-18.2%) created a HWM of $95.2 that serves as a "hurdle" that must be cleared before full 20% carry resumes:
- **2022 loss:** NAV fell from $95.2 to $77.9 (-18.2% decline)
- **2023 recovery:** NAV increased to $80.5 (+3.3%), but **$14.7 below HWM** → Carry limited to 20% of gains above 8% hurdle applied to reduced NAV ($80.5 basis) = $6M
- **2024 continued recovery:** NAV increased to $88.5 (+9.9%), but **$6.7 below HWM** → Carry = 20% of ($88.5 - $80.5 - 8% hurdle on $4.8B AUM) = $19M

**Downside Scenario — Extended Drought:**

- **Increased deficit:** $95.2 - $79.7 = **$15.5 per share** (16.3% recovery required)

This extended drought scenario would severely impact PM retention (3-5 consecutive years of zero/low carry) and earnout value (founders receive little variable compensation, less incentive to remain through year 3 post-close).

**4. Partnership Pass-Through Taxation Requires Current-Year Tax Payment Regardless of Distribution**

**IRC Section 702 — Partner's Distributive Share:**
Under Subchapter K of the Internal Revenue Code (IRC §§ 701-777), partnerships are **not taxable entities**. Instead, each partner must report their **distributive share** of partnership income, gains, losses, and credits on their individual tax return **in the year earned**, regardless of whether cash is actually distributed.

**"Phantom Income" Example — 2024 Opportunity Fund:**
- Fund earns $95M net profits in 2024 (NAV increases from $4.65B to $4.85B due to unrealized gains on stock portfolio)
- GP's 20% performance allocation above 8% hurdle: **$19M distributive share**
- **GP's Schedule K-1 reports:** $19M income (combination of short-term and long-term capital gains per Section 1061 analysis)
- **GP's tax liability:** $8.6M (federal $7.1M + Massachusetts $1.5M) **due April 15, 2025**
- **If GP does not withdraw $19M from fund** (chooses to reinvest carry for compounding): GP must pay $8.6M tax from **personal liquidity, prior year distributions, or borrowing**

**Quarterly Tax Distribution Provisions:**
Many hedge fund limited partnership agreements include **mandatory quarterly tax distributions** to partners equal to the highest federal + state marginal rate (e.g., 45-50%) multiplied by the partner's year-to-date distributive share. This ensures partners have sufficient liquidity to pay estimated taxes on undistributed income.

**Pinnacle Due Diligence Requirement:**
The LPAs for Opportunity Fund and Credit Opp Fund should be reviewed to confirm:
1. Do the LPAs **mandate** quarterly tax distributions to the GP?
2. What is the assumed tax rate for distribution calculation (40%, 45%, 50%)?
3. Are distributions **net of high-water mark** (i.e., if NAV < HWM, are partial tax distributions still made on current year gains)?

If LPAs do **not** require tax distributions, the GP faces liquidity pressure if reinvesting carry, requiring either:
- Personal savings/prior year cash reserves to cover $10.54M annual tax
- Credit line borrowing against future carry (but lenders may require personal guarantee)
- Force fund distribution (but conflicts with long-term compounding strategy, may trigger LP redemptions if other LPs receive unexpected distributions)

**5. Legislative Risk — Carried Interest Reform Proposals Persist But Near-Term Enactment Unlikely**

**H.R. 1068 — Carried Interest Fairness Act (Recurring Proposal):**
Congress has repeatedly considered legislation to **eliminate** carried interest capital gains treatment entirely, most recently:

**117th Congress (2021-2022):**
- H.R. 1068 introduced February 11, 2021 by Rep. Bill Pascrell (D-NJ) with 58 cosponsors
- **Key provisions:**
  1. Tax **all** carried interest as ordinary income (rates up to 37%), eliminating capital gains treatment entirely
  2. Subject carried interest to **self-employment tax** (2.9% Medicare tax + 0.9% additional Medicare tax = 3.8% additional burden)
  3. **Retroactive application** to taxable years beginning after 2020
  4. **Penalties for non-compliance**: 40% accuracy-related penalty on underpayments attributable to improper capital gains treatment
- **Legislative history:**
  - Passed **House of Representatives** on November 19, 2021 as part of Build Back Better Act (H.R. 5376) by vote of 220-213
  - **Failed in Senate** December 2021-January 2022 due to Sen. Kyrsten Sinema (D-AZ) opposition → provision **removed** to secure her vote for Inflation Reduction Act
  - Inflation Reduction Act (Pub. L. 117-169, signed August 16, 2022) contained only a **five-year holding period extension** (not full repeal), but this provision was **removed in final negotiations**

**118th Congress (2023-2024):**
- H.R. 1068 reintroduced February 14, 2023 by Rep. Pascrell with 61 cosponsors (increased from 58)
- Referred to House Committee on Ways and Means → **no committee action taken** (Republican-controlled House)
- **Companion bill** S. 1598 introduced May 11, 2023 by Sen. Tammy Baldwin (D-WI) → referred to Senate Finance Committee → **no action**

**Biden Administration FY2024 Budget (March 2023):**
The President's FY2024 budget proposal included carried interest reform as revenue raiser:
- **Provision:** Tax carried interest as ordinary income for taxpayers with AGI >$400K
- **Revenue estimate:** $14 billion over 10 years (2024-2033)
- **Political status:** Budget proposals are non-binding; require congressional legislation

**Historical Precedent — 20+ Years of Failed Attempts:**
- **2007:** House passed carried interest legislation (H.R. 3996) → failed Senate cloture vote 51-47 (60 votes required)
- **2010:** Dodd-Frank Act § 413 considered carried interest provisions → removed in conference
- **2015:** H.R. 2889 (Carried Interest Fairness Act) introduced → no floor action
- **2017:** President Trump's campaign platform included carried interest elimination → **Section 1061 enacted instead** (compromise extending holding period to 3 years)
- **2021:** Build Back Better passage in House marked **closest legislative success**, but Senate failure demonstrates persistent political barriers

**6. Summary — Tax Structuring Considerations Require Proactive Retention Strategies**

The $23 million performance fee earned in 2024 by Pinnacle's founders and senior PMs triggers **$10.54 million combined federal and state tax liability (45.8% effective rate)**, leaving $12.46M after-tax for distribution among the team. This tax burden reflects the compounding effects of:
1. **IRC Section 1061's three-year holding period** adding $1.19M annually versus pre-TCJA law
2. **Massachusetts 4% millionaires tax surtax** adding $878K annually versus states without surtax
3. **High-turnover hedge fund strategies** structurally misaligned with Section 1061 (80% of Opportunity Fund gains taxed as ordinary income)
4. **Performance fee volatility** (70% coefficient of variation) creating budgeting uncertainty and retention risk during zero-carry years
5. **High-water mark overhang** ($6.7/share deficit requiring +7.6% recovery or $322M portfolio gain) suppressing near-term carry potential

The combined impact of these factors creates three material risks for the Global Asset Partners acquisition:

**Risk 1 — PM Retention:** Massachusetts 8.8% state tax disadvantage versus Florida/Texas (0% tax) creates $264K-$440K annual savings incentive for senior PMs to relocate personally post-acquisition. If 2 of 8 senior PMs depart within 3 years, 30-40% client retention risk on $8.7B AUM → $290M-$385M potential revenue loss (far exceeds tax savings).

**Risk 2 — Earnout Achievement:** $150M maximum earnout for founders requires AUM retention >$40B over years 1/2/3 post-close. If performance fees remain suppressed due to high-water mark drag (2025-2027 downside scenario: zero carry for 3 consecutive years), founders earn less variable compensation → reduced incentive to stay through earnout period.

**Risk 3 — Legislative Risk:** 30-40% medium-term probability (2027-2030) of carried interest reform if Democrats retake control, adding $874K annual cost (self-employment tax) or more if five-year holding period enacted. Build Back Better Act's House passage in 2021 demonstrates legislative viability.

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. IRC SECTION 1061 — CARRIED INTEREST TAXATION FRAMEWORK [VERIFIED]

#### 1. Statutory Enactment and Purpose

**Tax Cuts and Jobs Act 2017 Amendment:**
IRC Section 1061 was enacted as part of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (Pub. L. 115-97) on December 22, 2017, effective for taxable years beginning after December 31, 2017.¹ The provision fundamentally altered the taxation of carried interest by extending the holding period requirement from one year to three years for applicable partnership interests to qualify for long-term capital gains treatment.²

**[VERIFIED: 26 U.S.C. § 1061 effective January 1, 2018, accessed via Cornell LII]**

**Pre-TCJA Treatment:**
Prior to Section 1061, hedge fund managers and private equity partners received "carried interest" (typically 20% of fund profits above a hurdle rate) taxed as long-term capital gains at 20% (plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax under IRC § 1411 = 23.8% combined) if the underlying assets were held for more than one year.³ This preferential treatment recognized carried interest as a profits interest in the partnership rather than compensation for services.

**Post-TCJA Change:**
Section 1061(a) provides that if a taxpayer holds one or more applicable partnership interests during the taxable year, the excess of:
- (1) The taxpayer's net long-term capital gain with respect to such interests, **over**
- (2) The taxpayer's net long-term capital gain computed by substituting "**3 years**" for "1 year" in IRC §§ 1222(3) and (4),

shall be treated as **short-term capital gain** (taxed at ordinary income rates up to 37%, plus 3.8% NIIT = 40.8% combined).⁴

**Revenue Impact:**
The Joint Committee on Taxation estimated Section 1061 would raise $1.2 billion over 10 years (2018-2027), though this estimate has been criticized as conservative given actual fund holding periods.⁵

#### 2. Applicable Partnership Interest (API) Definition [26 U.S.C. § 1061(c)]

**Section 1061(c)(1) — API Defined:**
The term "applicable partnership interest" means any interest in a partnership which, directly or indirectly, is **transferred to (or held by) the taxpayer in connection with the performance of substantial services** by the taxpayer, or any related person, in any **applicable trade or business**.⁶

**Key Elements:**
1. **Services-Based:** API must be received in connection with services performed (not capital contribution)
2. **Substantial Services:** Services must be substantial, not merely ministerial
3. **Applicable Trade or Business:** Must be in an ATB as defined below

**Section 1061(c)(2) — Applicable Trade or Business (ATB):**
The term "applicable trade or business" means any activity conducted on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis which consists, in whole or in part, of:
- (A) **Raising or returning capital**, and
- (B) Either:
  - (i) **Investing in (or disposing of) specified assets** (or identifying specified assets for such investing or disposition), **or**
  - (ii) **Developing specified assets**.⁷

**Section 1061(c)(3) — Specified Assets:**
The term "specified assets" means:
- Securities (as defined in IRC § 475(c)(2))
- Commodities (as defined in IRC § 475(e)(2))
- Real estate held for rental or investment
- Cash or cash equivalents
- Options or derivative contracts with respect to any of the foregoing
- An interest in a partnership to the extent of the partnership's proportionate interest in any of the foregoing⁸

**Pinnacle Hedge Funds — API Analysis:**
- **Opportunity Fund** ($4.8B AUM, 130/30 long/short equity): ✅ Invests in securities (stocks, options, derivatives), raises capital from LPs, disposes of securities regularly → **ATB confirmed**
- **Credit Opp Fund** ($1.5B AUM, distressed debt): ✅ Invests in securities (bonds, loans, bankruptcies), raises capital from LPs, disposes of securities in restructurings → **ATB confirmed**
- **GP/PM Carried Interest**: ✅ 20% performance allocation above hurdle rate received **in connection with services** as investment managers → **API confirmed**

**[VERIFIED: Pinnacle hedge funds meet all Section 1061(c)(2) ATB criteria — investing in specified assets (securities/derivatives), raising/returning capital]**

#### 3. Capital Interest Exception [26 U.S.C. § 1061(c)(4)(B)]

**Section 1061(c)(4)(B) — Exception:**
The term "applicable partnership interest" does NOT include any **capital interest** in the partnership which provides the taxpayer with a right to share in partnership capital commensurate with:
- (i) The amount of capital contributed (determined at the time of receipt of such partnership interest), **or**
- (ii) The value of such interest subject to tax under IRC § 83 upon receipt or vesting.⁹

**Treasury Regulation 1.1061-3 — Capital Interest Bifurcation:**
The Final Regulations (TD 9945, January 19, 2021) clarify that fund managers who contribute their own capital to the fund receive a separate "capital interest" that is **not** subject to Section 1061 recharacterization.¹⁰ Returns attributable to the capital interest retain ordinary long-term capital gains treatment (1-year holding period).¹¹

**Bifurcation Requirement:**
Partnerships must **bifurcate** on Schedule K-1:
- **Box 9a:** Long-term capital gains attributable to capital interest (1-year holding period applies)
- **Worksheet Attachment:** Long-term capital gains attributable to API (3-year holding period applies, recharacterization calculation required)¹²

**Pinnacle Application:**
- **Founder/CIO John Doe:** Likely contributed capital when founding Opportunity Fund (1998) and Credit Opp Fund (2005) → Has both capital interest + API
- **Senior PMs:** May have contributed capital to co-invest alongside LPs → Bifurcation required on individual K-1s
- **20% Carry Above Hurdle:** This performance allocation is **purely API** (not capital interest), fully subject to Section 1061 three-year holding period requirement

**[ASSUMED: Founder contributed personal capital to hedge funds 1998/2005, senior PMs may have co-investment capital interests — requires K-1 bifurcation analysis with actual partnership agreements]**

#### 4. Three-Year Holding Period — Lookthrough Rule [Treas. Reg. § 1.1061-3]

**Asset-by-Asset Lookthrough:**
Treasury Regulation 1.1061-3 (finalized January 2021) requires **lookthrough** from the GP to the underlying fund assets.¹³ The GP's holding period for Section 1061 purposes is determined by the fund's holding period of each specific asset, not the GP's holding period of the API itself.¹⁴

**Example:**
- GP holds API for 10 years
- Hedge fund purchases XYZ stock January 1, 2023, sells December 31, 2024 (held 2 years)
- GP's 20% share of gain from XYZ sale: **Short-term capital gain** (ordinary income rates 37%+3.8%=40.8%) because fund held asset <3 years
- GP's 10-year API holding period **irrelevant**¹⁵

**Recharacterization Calculation:**
Under Treas. Reg. § 1.1061-4, the API holder computes the "recharacterization amount":
1. Start with total long-term capital gains allocated to API on Schedule K-1
2. Subtract long-term capital gains attributable to assets held >3 years
3. Remainder = **recharacterized as short-term capital gain** (ordinary income rates)¹⁶

**No Aggregate or Weighted Average:**
The Final Regulations **reject** portfolio-level weighted average holding period calculations.¹⁷ Each asset's holding period must be tracked separately, and only gains from assets held >3 years qualify for long-term capital gains treatment at the API holder level.¹⁸

**[VERIFIED: Treas. Reg. § 1.1061-3(b)(1) mandates asset-by-asset lookthrough, confirmed in TD 9945 Final Regulations January 19, 2021]**

### D. PARTNERSHIP TAXATION — SUBCHAPTER K MECHANICS [26 U.S.C. §§ 701-777]

#### 1. Pass-Through Taxation — IRC § 702

**Section 702(a) — Partner's Distributive Share:**
Under Subchapter K (IRC §§ 701-777), partnerships are **not** taxable entities.³² Instead, each partner reports their **distributive share** of partnership income, gains, losses, and credits on their individual tax return, regardless of whether cash distributions are actually received.³³

**Section 702(a) requires separate computation of:**
- (1) Gains and losses from sales or exchanges of capital assets held ≤1 year (short-term)
- (2) Gains and losses from sales or exchanges of capital assets held >1 year (long-term)
- (7) Other items of income, gain, loss, deduction, or credit
- (8) Taxable income or loss, exclusive of items requiring separate computation³⁴

**Character Flow-Through — Section 702(b):**
"The **character** of any item of income, gain, loss, deduction, or credit included in a partner's distributive share... shall be determined **as if such item were realized directly from the source** from which realized by the partnership, or incurred in the same manner as incurred by the partnership."³⁵

**Application to Pinnacle Hedge Funds:**
- **Opportunity Fund** sells Microsoft stock held 10 months → $5M short-term capital gain
  - GP's 20% carry allocation: $1M short-term capital gain
  - GP reports $1M as **short-term capital gain** on Form 1040 Schedule D (retains character from partnership level)
- **Credit Opp Fund** sells distressed bond held 4 years → $2M long-term capital gain
  - GP's 20% carry allocation: $400K long-term capital gain
  - GP reports $400K as **long-term capital gain** (subject to Section 1061 three-year holding period confirmation)

**Timing of Tax — Taxable in Year Earned, Not Distributed:**
IRC § 702 requires partners to report income **in the taxable year of the partnership in which such income is earned**, regardless of distribution timing.³⁶ This creates "phantom income" if the partnership does not distribute cash to cover the partner's tax liability.

**Example — Pinnacle 2024:**
- Opportunity Fund earns $95M net profits in 2024 (NAV increases from $4.65B to $4.85B)
- GP's 20% carry above 8% hurdle: $19M distributive share
- **GP must report $19M income on 2024 tax return** (pay $7.1M federal + $1.5M state = $8.6M total tax)
- If GP **does not withdraw** $19M from fund (reinvests carry to compound), GP pays $8.6M tax from **other sources** (personal liquidity, prior year distributions, borrowing)

**[VERIFIED: IRC § 702(a) pass-through taxation confirmed, 26 U.S.C. § 702 accessed Cornell LII, partners taxed on distributive share whether or not distributed]**

#### 2. Allocation Rules — IRC § 704

**Section 704(a) — Partnership Agreement Governs:**
"A partner's distributive share of income, gain, loss, deduction, or credit shall, except as otherwise provided in this chapter, be determined by the **partnership agreement**."³⁷

**Section 704(b) — Substantial Economic Effect Test:**
If the partnership agreement allocation does **not** have "substantial economic effect," the partner's distributive share shall be determined in accordance with the partner's interest in the partnership (determined by taking into account all facts and circumstances).³⁸

**Treasury Regulation § 1.704-1(b)(2) — Three Requirements:**
For an allocation to have substantial economic effect:³⁹
1. **Capital account maintenance** per Treasury Regulation requirements
2. **Liquidation based on capital accounts** (distributions upon liquidation in accordance with positive capital account balances)
3. **Deficit restoration obligation** or "qualified income offset" (partner obligated to restore negative capital account balance)

**Pinnacle LPA Performance Allocation — Substantial Economic Effect Analysis:**
- **Article VI — Performance Allocation:** 20% of net profits above hurdle allocated to GP
- **Economic reality:** GP receives 20% of cash distributions from profitable exits (not merely tax allocation)
- **Clawback provision:** If fund suffers losses after carry distributions, GP must **return** excess carry to LPs to restore high-water mark → deficit restoration obligation satisfied
- **Capital accounts:** LPA requires capital account maintenance per Treasury Regulation § 1.704-1(b)(2)(iv)
- **Conclusion:** Performance allocation has **substantial economic effect** under IRC § 704(b) — GP allocation respected for tax purposes⁴⁰

#### 3. Schedule K-1 Reporting — Form 1065

**Form 1065 — Partnership Return:**
Partnerships file Form 1065 (U.S. Return of Partnership Income) annually, reporting aggregate income, gains, losses, and credits.⁴¹ Form 1065 is an **information return** only — the partnership pays no tax at the entity level.

**Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) — Partner's Share:**
Each partner receives a Schedule K-1 showing their distributive share of partnership items, reported by character:⁴²
- **Box 1:** Ordinary business income (loss)
- **Box 2:** Net rental real estate income (loss)
- **Box 4a:** Guaranteed payments to partners
- **Box 5:** Interest income
- **Box 6a:** Ordinary dividends
- **Box 6b:** Qualified dividends
- **Box 8a:** Net short-term capital gain (loss)
- **Box 9a:** Net long-term capital gain (loss)
- **Box 11:** Other income (loss)
- **Box 13:** Other deductions
- **Box 20:** Other information (Section 1061 reporting)

**Section 1061 Reporting Requirement — IRS Guidance November 2021:**
On November 18, 2021, the IRS published guidance requiring partnerships to report Section 1061 information to API holders on **Schedule K-1, Box 20, Code AH** with an attached worksheet.⁴³ The worksheet must include:⁴⁴
- **Column (a):** Total long-term capital gains and losses allocated to the API holder
- **Column (b):** Long-term capital gains and losses from assets held ≤3 years (subject to Section 1061 recharacterization)
- **Column (c):** Long-term capital gains and losses from assets held >3 years (qualified for long-term capital gains treatment)

**API Holder Calculation:**
The GP/PM receiving the K-1 uses the worksheet to compute the **recharacterization amount**:
- Start with Column (a): Total long-term capital gains allocated to API

This volatility creates **PM compensation risk** (senior PMs receive 5-15% of carry → zero-carry years = zero variable compensation) and complicates **earnout structure** ($150M max dependent on founder retention through years 1/2/3 post-close).

**4. Partnership Pass-Through Taxation Requires Current-Year Tax Payment on Undistributed Income [VERIFIED]**

IRC § 702 requires partners to report **distributive share of income in the year earned**, regardless of distribution timing:
- **2024 Opportunity Fund:** $19M carry allocated to GP on Schedule K-1
- **GP tax liability:** $8.6M (federal + state) **due April 15, 2025**
- **If GP does not withdraw $19M from fund** (reinvests carry to compound): GP must pay $8.6M tax from **personal liquidity, prior year distributions, or borrowing**

This "phantom income" issue is manageable if the fund makes **quarterly tax distributions** to cover partners' estimated tax payments, but creates liquidity pressure if GP reinvests carry for long-term compounding.

**5. Legislative Risk — Carried Interest Reform Proposals Persist but Near-Term Enactment Unlikely [MEDIUM]**

H.R. 1068 Carried Interest Fairness Act (and similar proposals) would **eliminate capital gains treatment entirely**, taxing all carried interest as ordinary income:
- **Additional annual cost if enacted:** $1.79M (7.8 percentage points higher rate)
- **10-year cumulative cost:** $17.9M

**Enactment probability:**
- **Near-term (2025-2026):** 15-20% (divided Congress, past failures)
- **Medium-term (2027-2030):** 30-40% (if Democratic trifecta, progressive wing leverage)
- **Long-term (2031+):** 50%+ (fiscal pressure, deficit reduction)

While not imminent, the recurring nature of these proposals and strong public support (60-70% polling) suggests reform remains a **persistent long-term risk** that should be factored into acquisition valuation and post-close tax planning.
