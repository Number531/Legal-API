## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Overview

This report analyzes Pinnacle Investment Management's compliance with Rule 206(4)-1 under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940 (the "Marketing Rule"), which became effective November 4, 2022. Our research identifies **two material categories of violations**: (1) testimonial compensation disclosure failures involving $10.08 million annually in undisclosed fee reductions, and (2) performance composite survivorship bias inflating reported returns by 25 basis points through exclusion of three closed underperforming accounts. Combined regulatory exposure ranges from **$115,000 to $205,000** in potential SEC penalties, with **$182,000 to $305,000** in remediation costs.

The Marketing Rule replaced the 1961 Advertising Rule and 1979 Cash Solicitation Rule to modernize investment adviser marketing regulations for the digital age, establishing principles-based standards for testimonials, endorsements, and performance advertising. ### Key Takeaways

1. **Testimonial Violations — Material Undisclosed Compensation ($10.08M Annually)**

Pinnacle's corporate website features four institutional client testimonials, two of whom receive **reduced management fees of 0.35% versus standard 0.42%** (7 basis points reduction). For State Pension Plan A ($9.8B AUM) and Corporate Pension Plan C ($4.6B AUM), these fee reductions total **$10.08 million annually** and were explicitly negotiated in exchange for providing testimonials.

**Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1) Violation:** The website testimonials omit required disclosures that these clients receive material non-cash compensation. Current disclosure consists only of a generic 8-point footnote stating "testimonials reflect individual experiences," which fails the Marketing Rule's "clear and prominent" standard. The SEC's December 2025 Risk Alert identified inadequate testimonial disclosures (hyperlinked, small font, or omitted entirely) as a common deficiency, citing examples where "the affiliation between the advisers and such promoters was not readily apparent to, or was not disclosed to, clients."

**Rule 206(4)-1(b)(2) Violation:** Pinnacle has not entered into written testimonial agreements with any of the four clients, despite the rule requiring written agreements for any promoter receiving compensation exceeding $1,000 in 12 months. The $10.08 million annual compensation vastly exceeds this threshold.

**Estimated Fine**: $75,000-$125,000 based on September 2024 SEC enforcement precedent where Howard Bailey Financial paid $100,000 for testimonial disclosure failures and Integrated Advisors Network paid $125,000 for similar violations.

2. **Performance Survivorship Bias — 25 Basis Point Overstatement**

Pinnacle advertises its Large Cap Growth Separate Account Composite as achieving **+15.8% annualized returns** over the five-year period ended September 30, 2024, outperforming the Russell 1000 Growth Index (+14.8%) by 100 basis points. However, this composite excludes **three closed accounts** ($420M aggregate AUM) that terminated during 2020-2022 following periods of underperformance averaging -213 basis points versus benchmark.

**Rule 206(4)-1(b)(5) Cherry-Picking Violation:** By selectively excluding underperforming closed accounts while retaining better-performing accounts, Pinnacle's composite "include[s] or exclude[s] performance results...in a manner that is not fair and balanced." This is precisely the survivorship bias the Marketing Rule prohibits.

**Rule 206(4)-1(a)(1) Material Misstatement:** When the three closed accounts are included for the periods they were managed (as required by GIPS standards and SEC "related performance" definition), the actual 5-year return is **+15.55% annualized** — a **25 basis point overstatement** in advertised materials.

**GIPS Standards Violation:** Pinnacle's marketing materials claim "Performance calculated in accordance with Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS®)." However, GIPS Provision 3.A.3 explicitly requires "terminated portfolios must be included in the historical performance of the composite through the last full measurement period that the portfolio was under management." Excluding the three closed accounts renders the GIPS compliance claim false.

**Estimated Fine**: $30,000-$60,000 based on historical SEC enforcement for performance presentation violations and September 2023 Marketing Rule sweep where 9 advisers paid $850,000 combined for hypothetical performance deficiencies.

## III. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

### A. Investment Advisers Act Marketing Rule — Regulatory Evolution

**Old Framework (1961-2022):**

For six decades, investment adviser advertising was governed by two separate rules:
- **Rule 206(4)-1 (Advertising Rule)**: Adopted 1961, prohibited testimonials categorically, regulated performance advertising
- **Rule 206(4)-3 (Cash Solicitation Rule)**: Adopted 1979, governed third-party solicitor arrangements

These rules predated the internet, social media, and modern marketing practices, creating compliance challenges for digital communications.

**Marketing Rule Modernization (2020-2022):**

On December 22, 2020, the SEC adopted Rule 206(4)-1 (the "Marketing Rule"), merging the Advertising and Cash Solicitation Rules into a single principles-based framework. Key changes:

1. **Testimonials Permitted (with Disclosures)**: Reversed 1961 prohibition, allowing testimonials if advisers disclose compensation, conflicts, and client status

2. **Performance Advertising Modernized**: Replaced rigid prohibitions with "fair and balanced" standard addressing cherry-picking, hypothetical performance, and predecessor performance

3. **Broader "Advertisement" Definition**: Encompasses websites, social media, videos, podcasts, and any communication offering advisory services to more than one person

**Compliance Timeline:**
- **Adopted**: December 22, 2020 (SEC Release No. IA-5653)
- **Effective**: May 4, 2021
- **Compliance Date**: November 4, 2022 (18-month transition period)
- **First Enforcement Sweep**: September 11, 2023 (9 advisers, $850,000 penalties)

## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. MARKETING RULE 206(4)-1 REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

#### 1. Statutory Foundation and Regulatory Authority

The Marketing Rule codified at 17 C.F.R. § 275.206(4)-1 implements Section 206(4) of the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, which authorizes the SEC to "define, and prescribe means reasonably designed to prevent, such acts, practices, and courses of business as are fraudulent, deceptive, or manipulative."¹

**Section 206 Prohibitions** (15 U.S.C. § 80b-6):
It shall be unlawful for any investment adviser by use of the mails or any means or instrumentality of interstate commerce, directly or indirectly—
- (1) to employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud any client or prospective client;
- (2) to engage in any transaction, practice, or course of business which operates as a fraud or deceit upon any client or prospective client;
- (4) to engage in any act, practice, or course of business which is fraudulent, deceptive, or manipulative.²

The Commission adopted Rule 206(4)-1 on December 22, 2020 (SEC Release No. IA-5653), effective May 4, 2021, with a compliance date of November 4, 2022.³ The rule replaced the 1961 Advertising Rule and the 1979 Cash Solicitation Rule to reflect modern marketing practices including social media, testimonials, and digital communications.⁴

[VERIFIED: SEC Release No. IA-5653 accessed via sec.gov December 2020, effective dates confirmed in Federal Register]

#### 2. General Prohibitions — Rule 206(4)-1(a)

The Marketing Rule establishes a **principles-based framework** prohibiting advisers from disseminating advertisements that:

**(a)(1)** Include any **untrue statement of a material fact**, or are otherwise **false or misleading**;

**(a)(2)** Include material information or make any statement of material fact that is **false or misleading** by omission (considering context of the entire advertisement, including disclaimers and disclosures).⁵

**"Advertisement" Definition:**
The rule defines "advertisement" broadly to include **any direct or indirect communication** an investment adviser makes to more than one person (or to one or more persons if the communication includes hypothetical performance) that:
- Offers the adviser's investment advisory services with regard to securities; or
- Offers new investment advisory services with regard to securities, or offers to enter into, extend, or renew an investment advisory contract.⁶

This encompasses websites, social media posts, pitch books, performance composites, fact sheets, and client testimonials.

[VERIFIED: 17 C.F.R. § 275.206(4)-1 accessed via Cornell LII and sec.gov January 2026]

#### 3. Specific Prohibitions — Testimonials and Endorsements (Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1)-(3))

**Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1) — Testimonial/Endorsement Disclosure Requirements:**

If an advertisement includes a testimonial or endorsement, the adviser must:

**(i)** Clearly and prominently disclose (or reasonably believe the promoter discloses):
- **Client status**: Whether the person giving the testimonial is a **current client or investor** in a private fund advised by the adviser;
- **Compensation**: Whether **cash or non-cash compensation** was provided for the testimonial/endorsement;
- **Material conflicts of interest**: A brief statement of any material conflicts of interest on the part of the person giving the testimonial resulting from the adviser's relationship with such person or any compensation arrangement.⁷

**(ii)** Disclosure must be **clear and prominent**:
- SEC guidance: Cannot use hyperlinked disclosures or smaller font sizes that obscure material conflicts;
- Must be "reasonably designed to draw attention to the required information";
- Prominent placement near the testimonial, not buried in footnotes.⁸

[VERIFIED: SEC Risk Alert December 16, 2025 "Additional Observations Regarding Advisers' Compliance with the Advisers Act Marketing Rule" — deficiency examples include inadequate disclosure format]

**Rule 206(4)-1(b)(2) — Oversight Requirements:**

The adviser must have a **reasonable basis** to believe that any testimonial or endorsement complies with the rule's requirements, including:
- Reviewing the content of the testimonial;
- Confirming accuracy of statements;
- Obtaining written agreements from promoters.⁹

**Rule 206(4)-1(b)(3) — Disqualification:**

Advisers cannot provide compensation to **ineligible persons** with SEC or CFTC disciplinary actions within 10 years.¹⁰

**Written Agreement Requirement:**

For compensated testimonials/endorsements exceeding $1,000 in 12 months, advisers must enter into a **written agreement** with the promoter that:
- Describes the scope of agreed-upon activities;
- Discloses all material terms of the compensation arrangement;
- Includes provisions for ongoing review and updates.¹¹

[VERIFIED: 17 C.F.R. § 275.206(4)-1(b)(1)-(3) and SEC Marketing Rule FAQs 2021-2023]

### C. PERFORMANCE ADVERTISING — SURVIVORSHIP BIAS VIOLATION

#### 1. Rule 206(4)-1(b)(5) Prohibition on Cherry-Picking

**Rule 206(4)-1(b)(5)** prohibits advertisements that include:
> "Any performance results...that include or exclude performance results, or present performance time periods, in a manner that is not fair and balanced."²⁶

**Regulatory Intent:**
The SEC adopted this provision to eliminate the opportunity for advisers to "cherry-pick" performance results by **excluding underperforming accounts or unfavorable time periods** that would provide a misleading impression of the adviser's investment capabilities.²⁷

**"Related Performance" Requirements:**
Rule 206(4)-1(c)(7) defines "related performance" as performance results of one or more other portfolios with similar investment policies, objectives, and strategies. Advisers presenting related performance must:
- Include **all portfolios** (excluding private funds) that were managed or offered to be managed with similar investment policies, objectives, and strategies; and
- Cannot **selectively exclude** portfolios based on performance outcomes.²⁸

This directly addresses **survivorship bias** — the practice of excluding terminated/closed accounts from performance composites to inflate reported returns.

[VERIFIED: 17 C.F.R. § 275.206(4)-1(b)(5) and (c)(7) — cherry-picking prohibition and related performance definition accessed via Cornell LII and SEC Release IA-5653]

#### 5. GIPS 2020 Standards — Composite Construction Requirements

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Material Findings

**1. Testimonial Violations (Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1)-(3))**

Pinnacle's website features testimonials from four institutional clients, two of whom (State Pension Plan A and Corporate Pension Plan C) receive **$10.08M annually in reduced management fees** (7 basis points below standard rate). These fee reductions constitute **material non-cash compensation** under the Marketing Rule.

**Violations:**
- ❌ **Undisclosed compensation conflict**: Website testimonials omit disclosure that two clients receive $10M+ annual fee savings
- ❌ **Inadequate "clear and prominent" disclosure**: Generic 8-point footnote fails SEC standard
- ❌ **Missing written agreements**: No testimonial agreements executed with any four clients despite >$1,000 compensation threshold

**Fine Exposure**: $75,000-$125,000

**2. Performance Survivorship Bias (Rule 206(4)-1(b)(5), (a)(1))**

Pinnacle's Large Cap Growth Composite excludes **three closed underperforming accounts** ($420M aggregate AUM at termination), inflating reported 5-year returns by **+25 basis points annually**.

**Violations:**
- ❌ **Cherry-picking**: Selective exclusion of underperforming closed accounts violates "fair and balanced" requirement
- ❌ **Material misstatement**: +15.8% advertised return is factually incorrect (actual return +15.55% including closed accounts)
- ❌ **False GIPS compliance claim**: Exclusion violates GIPS Provision 3.A.3 (terminated portfolios must be included)

**Fine Exposure**: $30,000-$60,000

**3. Form ADV Disclosure Gaps**

Form ADV Part 2A Items 8 and 14 require amendments to reflect:
- Corrected composite performance (+15.55% not +15.8%)
- Testimonial compensation disclosure (Item 14)

**Total Estimated Fine Exposure**: **$115,000-$205,000**
