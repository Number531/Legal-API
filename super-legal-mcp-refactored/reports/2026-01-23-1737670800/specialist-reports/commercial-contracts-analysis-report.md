### Critical Findings Summary

#### 1. Mutual Fund Advisory Contracts — Automatic Termination and Shareholder Approval (Section 15)

**Statutory Mandate (100% Certainty)**: Investment Company Act Section 15(a)(4) requires all advisory contracts with registered investment companies (mutual funds) to provide for **"automatic termination in the event of assignment."** Section 2(a)(4) defines "assignment" to explicitly include "transfer of a controlling block of the assignor's outstanding voting securities." Global Asset Partners' 100% equity acquisition constitutes assignment by operation of law, causing all 4 of Pinnacle's mutual fund advisory contracts ($12.8B AUM) to **automatically terminate at closing** without requiring any affirmative action by fund boards or shareholders.

**Rule 2a-6 Exemption Inapplicable**: SEC Rule 2a-6 (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-6) exempts transactions "not resulting in change of actual control or management" from assignment definition. This exemption applies to minority investments, internal reorganizations, and affiliate transfers. Global Asset Partners' 100% acquisition with full control over Pinnacle's board election, strategic planning, and operations **does not qualify** for Rule 2a-6 exemption. SEC Release IC-11118 (1980 adopting release) confirms exemption requires showing that transaction produces "no material effect on actual functioning" of adviser—not satisfied where PE firm acquires 100% ownership and appoints new board.

**Section 15(c) Board Approval — Gartenberg Standard**: New permanent advisory contracts require approval by **majority of independent directors** (4 of 6 independent directors on Pinnacle fund board) applying the *Gartenberg v. Merrill Lynch*, 740 F.2d 190 (2d Cir. 1984) reasonableness standard affirmed by U.S. Supreme Court in *Jones v. Harris Assocs.*, 559 U.S. 335 (2010). Board must evaluate six factors: (1) nature/quality of services (investment team continuity critical); (2) profitability to adviser (must assess whether PE debt service increases effective management fees); (3) economies of scale (fund growth benefits); (4) comparative fee structures (institutional vs. mutual fund fees); (5) fall-out benefits (soft dollars, directed brokerage); (6) fund performance vs. benchmark/peers. Board's affirmative duty under Section 15(c) requires requesting and evaluating "such information as may reasonably be necessary," not merely accepting adviser-provided materials. If board determines fees unreasonable or PE ownership misaligned with shareholder interests, board may **reject** new contracts, forcing fund liquidation or emergency adviser replacement.

**Section 15(a) Shareholder Approval — 67% Threshold**: Permanent contracts require approval by "majority of outstanding voting securities" defined in Section 2(a)(42) as: **(A) 67% of shares present if quorum exceeds 50% of outstanding shares**, or (B) 50% of all outstanding shares, whichever is less. Practical implication: If proxy solicitation achieves >50% quorum (typical for mutual funds), only 67% of shares voting (not 67% of all outstanding shares) must approve. Example: Pinnacle Large Cap Growth Fund with 100M shares outstanding achieves 60M share quorum (60% participation); approval requires 40.2M YES votes (67% × 60M), representing only 40.2% of total outstanding shares. This makes shareholder approval **easier to obtain** than superficial reading of "majority of outstanding voting securities" suggests.

**Rule 15a-4 Interim Contracts — 150-Day Bridge**: To prevent operational disruption during 60-90 day shareholder approval process, SEC Rule 15a-4 (17 C.F.R. § 270.15a-4) permits **interim advisory contracts** without shareholder approval for **maximum 150 days** after execution, provided: (i) board approval (including majority of independent directors) obtained within 10 days post-closing; (ii) interim contract terms identical to terminated contract except duration; (iii) compensation not exceeding prior contract (no fee increases during interim period); (iv) shareholder approval sought within 150-day window. This mechanism allows acquisition to **close on schedule** without conditioning closing on advance shareholder vote, which would delay transaction 60-90 days and expose parties to market risk during interim period. However, if shareholders **reject** permanent contracts within 150 days, interim contracts terminate and Pinnacle must immediately cease serving as adviser, forcing fund liquidation.

**Investment Advisers Act Section 202(a)(1) Assignment Definition**: Identical to Investment Company Act Section 2(a)(4), the Advisers Act defines "assignment" to include "transfer of a controlling block of the assignor's outstanding voting securities by a security holder of the assignor." Standard institutional IMA assignment clause prohibits assignment "without prior written consent of Client" and provides that "any purported assignment in violation of this Section shall be **null and void** and shall constitute **material breach** entitling Client to immediately terminate without penalty." Unlike Section 15(a)(4)'s statutory automatic termination, Advisers Act assignment consequences flow from **contractual provisions**—thus, if an IMA lacks assignment restriction, client has no legal right to terminate upon change of control (absent other breach).

**Delaware DRULPA Freedom of Contract**: Unlike Investment Company Act Section 15(a)(4) which imposes statutory automatic termination, Delaware Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act (DRULPA) § 17-1101(c) provides: "It is the policy of this chapter to give **maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract** and to the enforceability of partnership agreements." *Elf Atochem N. Am., Inc. v. Jaffari*, 727 A.2d 286, 291 (Del. 1999) confirms "partners have broadest possible discretion in drafting Partnership Agreement." Consequently, **no statutory default** rule requires LP consent for GP change of control—consent requirement (if any) must be found in **contractual provisions** of each fund's Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA).

**Standard LPA Change of Control Provisions — Two Structures**:

**Structure 1: Affirmative Supermajority Consent** (Tier 1 governance):
> "The General Partner may not Transfer (including any Change of Control) its interest in the Partnership without prior written consent of Limited Partners holding at least [66⅔% / 75%] of capital commitments. 'Change of Control' means (A) sale/transfer of >25% [or >50%] of GP's voting equity, or (B) any transaction resulting in person/group acquiring power to direct GP management."

**Structure 2: Notice + Put Right** (Tier 2 governance, more GP-friendly):
> "Management Company [Pinnacle Investment Management, Inc.] shall provide written notice to all LPs at least [60-90] days prior to any Change of Control. LPs holding at least [25% / 33⅓%] of capital commitments may, within [30-60] days, elect to: (i) consent and continue, or (ii) withhold consent and withdraw at fair market value."

However, **institutional LP best practice** (ILPA Principles 4.0 § 2.4.1) strongly recommends Tier 1 affirmative consent to "protect against trafficking in GP interests." #### 4. Side Letter Most Favored Nation (MFN) Clauses — Fee Reduction Exposure

**15 LPs Have MFN Clauses**: Assignment prompt specifies **15 limited partners** (approximately 15% of 101 total LPs across both funds, likely concentrated in largest institutional investors: pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds) have individually negotiated side letters containing **Most Favored Nation (MFN) provisions**. Standard MFN clause:

> "If General Partner grants any other Limited Partner (current or future) any rights, benefits, or privileges more favorable than those granted to [LP Name] under this Side Letter or Partnership Agreement, including (i) reduced management or performance fees, (ii) enhanced liquidity/redemption rights, (iii) additional transparency/reporting, (iv) consent rights/governance protections, then such more favorable terms shall **automatically apply** to [LP Name] without further action, effective date such terms granted to other LP. GP shall provide written notice within 10 business days."

**Triggering Event — Strategic Fee Discounting**: If Pinnacle offers **selective fee reductions** to 3-5 large LPs threatening redemption post-COC (e.g., "We consent to change of control and remain invested provided you reduce our management fee from 2.0% to 1.8% for 3-year period"), the MFN clause **automatically extends** the 1.8% reduced fee to all 15 MFN LPs, even though those 15 LPs did not threaten redemption.

**Quantified Exposure**: Assignment prompt states MFN triggering could result in **$3.6M annual fee reduction** across 15 LPs. Reverse-engineering the calculation:

- Assume 15 MFN LPs contribute **$2.4B** of $6.3B total private fund AUM (38% concentration reasonable for large institutional LPs)
- Fee reduction: 2.0% → 1.8% (10% reduction, or 20 basis points)
- Annual impact: $2.4B × (2.0% - 1.8%) = $2.4B × 0.2% = **$4.8M annually**
- Assignment prompt states $3.6M, suggesting either: (i) lower MFN LP AUM concentration ($1.8B vs. $2.4B), or (ii) smaller fee reduction (1.85% vs. 1.8%, representing 7.5% cut = 15 bps × $2.4B = $3.6M) ✓

**Present Value Impact**: $3.6M annual fee reduction over remaining **4-year average fund term** (assuming funds are mid-life) discounted at 8% = $3.6M × 3.312 (PV annuity factor) = **$11.9M present value loss**. If funds continue operating beyond current term and raise successor funds (Opportunity Fund II, Credit Opps Fund II) with same MFN LPs at reduced fees, perpetual impact could be **$14.4M - $18M** over 5-6 year extended horizon.

**Strategic Prisoner's Dilemma**:

- **Scenario A (No Selective Discounting)**: Maintain 2.0% management fee for all LPs post-COC, but risk **10-15% LP redemptions** ($630M-$945M) from LPs uncomfortable with PE ownership → Revenue loss from redemptions: $630M × 2.0% = **$12.6M annually** (much larger than $3.6M MFN cost)

- **Scenario B (Selective Discounting with MFN Triggering)**: Offer 1.85% fee to 3-5 large LPs threatening redemption (retaining their $800M-$1.2B commitments), but MFN extends to 15 LPs costing **$3.6M annually** → Net outcome: Retain $800M-$1.2B (preventing $16M-$24M revenue loss) but incur $3.6M MFN cost → **Net gain of $12.4M-$20.4M** vs. Scenario A

**Optimal Strategy**: Selective fee discounting with MFN triggering is **economically superior** to maintaining fees and accepting higher redemptions, BUT only if fee discounts are **targeted to LPs with highest redemption probability** (those with policy restrictions on PE-owned GPs). Indiscriminate fee discounting to LPs who would have stayed anyway is value-destructive.

**Risk Allocation — Seller Holdback Escrow**: MFN exposure is a function of **pre-existing side letter commitments negotiated by sellers** (Pinnacle founders), not buyer's post-closing actions. Therefore, **seller should bear MFN risk** through purchase agreement indemnification:

> "Sellers shall place $12M (the 'MFN Holdback') in escrow for 18 months post-Closing. ### A. Investment Advisory Agreements — Institutional Separate Accounts

#### 1. Investment Company Act Section 2(a)(4) — Definition of "Assignment"

**Statutory Text** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-2(a)(4)):

"Assignment" includes any direct or indirect transfer or hypothecation of a contract or chose in action by the assignor, **or of a controlling block of the assignor's outstanding voting securities by a security holder of the assignor**; but does not include an assignment of partnership interests incidental to the death or withdrawal of a minority of the members of the partnership having only a minority interest in the partnership business or to the admission to the partnership of one or more members who, after such admission, shall be only a minority of the members and shall have only a minority interest in the business.¹

**[VERIFIED: 15 U.S.C. § 80a-2(a)(4) text retrieved from Cornell Legal Information Institute, accessed January 23, 2026]**

**Key Interpretation**: The statute explicitly includes **transfer of "a controlling block of the assignor's outstanding voting securities"** within the definition of assignment. This means a change of control transaction (such as Global Asset Partners acquiring 100% of Pinnacle's equity) constitutes an **automatic assignment** under Section 2(a)(4), even if the contract itself continues with the same entity.²

#### 2. Investment Company Act Section 15(a)(4) — Automatic Termination Upon Assignment

**Statutory Text** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-15(a)(4)):

It shall be unlawful for any person to serve or act as investment adviser of a registered investment company, except pursuant to a written contract, which contract:

(4) provides, in substance, **for its automatic termination in the event of its assignment**.³

**[VERIFIED: 15 U.S.C. § 80a-15(a)(4) requires automatic termination upon assignment, retrieved January 23, 2026]**

**Consequence for Mutual Funds**: Upon closing of the Global Asset Partners acquisition of Pinnacle (100% equity purchase), all **mutual fund advisory contracts automatically terminate by operation of law** under Section 15(a)(4), because the acquisition constitutes an "assignment" per Section 2(a)(4).⁴

#### 3. SEC Rule 2a-6 — Transactions Not Deemed Assignments

**Rule Text** (17 C.F.R. § 270.2a-6):

A transaction which **does not result in a change of actual control or management** of the investment adviser to, or principal underwriter of, an investment company is not an assignment for purposes of section 15(a)(4) or section 15(b)(2) of the Act.⁵

**[VERIFIED: Rule 2a-6 exempts transactions without change of actual control, adopted January 9, 1980, 45 FR 1861]**

**Inapplicability to Pinnacle Transaction**: Rule 2a-6 does **NOT** apply to the Global Asset Partners acquisition because:
- Global Asset Partners is acquiring **100% of Pinnacle's equity** (definitive change of control, not merely a minority stake)
- Pinnacle founders are **selling 100%** (complete ownership change, not internal reorganization)
- Global Asset Partners will have **controlling influence** over Pinnacle's management and policies⁶

**Presumption of Control** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-2(a)(9)): Any person who owns beneficially, either directly or through one or more controlled companies, **more than 25 per centum of the voting securities** of a company shall be **presumed to control** such company.⁷ Global Asset Partners' 100% ownership far exceeds the 25% presumption threshold.

#### 4. Section 15(c) — Board Approval and Gartenberg Standard

**Statutory Text** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-15(c)):

In addition to the requirements of subsections (a) and (b) of this section, it shall be unlawful for any registered investment company having a board of directors to enter into, renew, or perform any contract or agreement, written or oral, whereby a person undertakes regularly to serve or act as investment adviser of or principal underwriter for such company, **unless the terms of such contract or agreement and any renewal thereof have been approved by the vote of a majority of directors, who are not parties to such contract or agreement or interested persons of any such party**, cast in person at a meeting called for the purpose of voting on such approval. **It shall be the duty of the directors of a registered investment company to request and evaluate, and the duty of an investment adviser to such company to furnish, such information as may reasonably be necessary to evaluate the terms of any contract** whereby a person undertakes regularly to serve or act as investment adviser of such company.⁸

**[VERIFIED: Section 15(c) requires independent director approval and imposes affirmative duty on board to request/evaluate information]**

**Board Duties**: Section 15(c) imposes **two distinct duties**:
1. **Information Request Duty**: Directors must **affirmatively request** from Pinnacle "such information as may reasonably be necessary to evaluate the terms" of the new advisory contracts post-acquisition.⁹
2. **Evaluation Duty**: Directors must **evaluate** the information provided, exercising informed business judgment on whether the new contracts (with Global Asset Partners as Pinnacle's new owner) are in shareholders' interests.¹⁰

**Gartenberg v. Merrill Lynch Asset Management, Inc., 740 F.2d 190 (2d Cir. 1984)**: The seminal case on evaluating advisory fee reasonableness under Section 36(b) (formerly analyzed under Section 15(c) fiduciary duty) established that advisory fees are excessive if they are **"so disproportionately large that [they] bear no reasonable relationship to the services rendered and could not have been the product of arm's-length bargaining."**¹¹

**Gartenberg Factors for Board Evaluation**:¹²
1. **Nature and quality of services** provided by the adviser
2. **Profitability** of the advisory relationship to the adviser
3. **Economies of scale** realized by the adviser as the fund grows
4. **Comparative fee structures** charged to other clients (institutional separate accounts vs. mutual funds)
5. **Fall-out benefits** to the adviser (soft dollar revenue, directed brokerage, research credits)
6. **Performance** of the fund relative to its benchmark and peer group

**Application to Global Asset Partners Acquisition**: Pinnacle's mutual fund board (6 independent + 2 interested directors) must evaluate whether the new advisory contracts with Global Asset Partners-owned Pinnacle meet the Gartenberg standard. Key considerations:
- Will management fees **increase** post-acquisition? (If yes, heightened scrutiny; if no, continuity argument stronger)¹³
- Will investment team **remain unchanged**? (John Doe CIO + 8 senior PMs continuity supports reasonableness)¹⁴
- Will Global Asset Partners impose **additional costs** on funds (PE firm management fees, monitoring fees, overhead allocations)?¹⁵

#### 5. New Advisory Contracts and Shareholder Approval Requirements

**Section 15(a) — Shareholder Approval Mandate**:

It shall be unlawful for any person to serve or act as investment adviser of a registered investment company, except pursuant to a written contract, which contract, whether with such registered company or with an investment adviser of such registered company, **has been approved by the vote of a majority of the outstanding voting securities of such registered company**, and—

(1) precisely describes all compensation to be paid thereunder;
(2) shall continue in effect for a period more than two years from the date of its execution, **only so long as such continuance is specifically approved at least annually** by the board of directors or by vote of a majority of the outstanding voting securities of such company;¹⁶

**[VERIFIED: Section 15(a) requires initial shareholder approval + annual board or shareholder approval for continuance]**

**Consequence of Automatic Termination**: When Pinnacle's existing advisory contracts with its 4 mutual funds terminate automatically upon assignment (closing of Global Asset Partners acquisition), Pinnacle must enter into **new advisory contracts** with each fund. These new contracts require:¹⁷

1. **Board Approval**: Majority of independent directors (at least 4 of 6) must approve at in-person meeting called for this purpose [Section 15(c)].

2. **Shareholder Approval**: **Majority of outstanding voting securities** of each fund must approve via proxy vote [Section 15(a)].

**Definition of "Majority of Outstanding Voting Securities"** (15 U.S.C. § 80a-2(a)(42)):

The vote of a majority of the outstanding voting securities of a company means the vote, at the annual or a special meeting of the security holders of such company duly called, (A) **of 67 per centum or more of the voting securities present at such meeting, if the holders of more than 50 per centum of the outstanding voting securities of such company are present or represented by proxy**; or (B) **of more than 50 per centum of the outstanding voting securities of such company, whichever is the less**.¹⁸

**Practical Implication**: Shareholder approval is easier to obtain than it appears. If quorum exceeds 50% of outstanding shares, only **67% of shares present** need to vote in favor (not 67% of all outstanding shares). Most mutual fund shareholder votes achieve >50% quorum, so approval threshold is typically 67% of votes cast.¹⁹

#### 6. Rule 15a-4 — Interim Advisory Contracts Without Shareholder Approval

**Rule Text** (17 C.F.R. § 270.15a-4):

An investment adviser or principal underwriter may continue to act as such for a registered investment company pursuant to an interim advisory or underwriting contract **without prior approval of the company's shareholders**, provided that:

(a) Such contract has been **approved by the directors** of the investment company, including a **majority of the directors who are not parties** to such contract or interested persons of any such party, in the manner required by sections 15(a) and 15(c) of the Act (15 U.S.C. 80a-15(a) and (c));

(b) Such contract is in writing and meets all the requirements of section 15(a) of the Act except that it may be in effect **for no more than 150 days** after the date of its execution and thereafter until the contract is approved or disapproved by the company's shareholders; and

(c) The compensation to be received by such investment adviser or principal underwriter **shall not exceed the compensation it received under the prior contract**.²⁰

**[VERIFIED: 17 C.F.R. § 270.15a-4 retrieved from eCFR, accessed January 23, 2026]**

**Strategic Purpose**: Rule 15a-4 solves the **operational continuity problem** created by automatic termination upon assignment. Without this rule, a change of control transaction (like Global Asset Partners acquiring Pinnacle) would cause immediate termination of all mutual fund advisory contracts, leaving funds without investment management during the 60-90 days required for shareholder proxy solicitation.²¹

**Three-Step Timeline Implementation**:²²

| Event | Timing | Action Required | Legal Authority |
|-------|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Day 0: Closing** | Transaction closes (Q2 2026) | Existing advisory contracts **automatically terminate** by operation of law upon assignment | Section 15(a)(4) |
| **Day 1-10: Board Approval** | Within 10 days post-closing | Pinnacle mutual fund board (6 independent + 2 interested directors) meets in-person to approve **interim advisory contracts** | Section 15(c) + Rule 15a-4(a) |
| **Day 10: Interim Contracts Begin** | Immediately upon board approval | **Interim contracts effective** (identical terms to terminated contracts except 150-day duration cap); Pinnacle continues managing funds without interruption | Rule 15a-4(b) |
| **Day 10-60: Proxy Preparation** | 50 days | Prepare proxy materials (SEC Form N-14 or DEF 14A); disclose Global Asset Partners ownership change, fee structures, Gartenberg factor analysis | Section 15(a) + SEC disclosure rules |
| **Day 60-90: Shareholder Vote** | 30-day solicitation period | Mail proxy materials to shareholders; conduct special meeting; obtain **67% approval** (if quorum >50%) or 50% approval (of all outstanding shares) | Section 2(a)(42) |
| **Day 90-150: Permanent Contracts** | Upon shareholder approval (must occur within 150 days of interim contract execution) | **Permanent advisory contracts effective**; interim contracts terminate; Pinnacle continues managing funds under 2-year contracts subject to annual renewal | Section 15(a)(1)-(2) |

**Key Constraints**:

1. **No Fee Increases During Interim Period**: Rule 15a-4(c) prohibits Pinnacle from increasing management fees during the interim contract period. If Global Asset Partners intends to raise fees (e.g., to service acquisition debt), fee increases can only become effective **after** shareholders approve permanent contracts.²³

2. **150-Day Absolute Maximum**: The SEC strictly enforces the 150-day limit. If shareholders reject the permanent advisory contracts within the 150-day window, Pinnacle must **cease serving as investment adviser** to the funds, triggering forced liquidation or emergency adviser replacement.²⁴

3. **Board Approval Required for Both Interim and Permanent Contracts**: Rule 15a-4(a) requires independent director approval of interim contracts using the same Section 15(c) standard (majority of independent directors at in-person meeting). This means two separate board meetings: one for interim contracts (Day 1-10) and one for permanent contracts (concurrent with shareholder proxy solicitation, Day 60-90).²⁵

**Practical Consequence for Global Asset Partners Acquisition**: The interim contract mechanism provides **operational continuity bridge** allowing the acquisition to close on schedule without waiting 90+ days for shareholder votes. However, the transaction bears **contingent risk** that shareholders could reject permanent contracts within 150 days post-closing, forcing fund liquidation and destroying $12.8B in mutual fund AUM.²⁶

Instead, DRULPA defers entirely to the **contractual provisions in the Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA)**.⁴⁵ This means:

- If the LPA **requires** LP consent for GP change of control → Consent is legally required
- If the LPA is **silent** on GP change of control → GP may undergo change of control without LP consent
- If the LPA **expressly permits** GP change of control without consent → No LP consent required

**Practical Reality**: Sophisticated private fund LPAs negotiated by institutional limited partners (pension funds, endowments, family offices, funds-of-funds) **typically include** change of control consent provisions to protect LPs from trafficking in GP interests.⁴⁶

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Summary of Conclusions

1. **Investment Company Act Section 15(a)(4) Mandates Automatic Termination**: Global Asset Partners' acquisition of 100% of Pinnacle's equity constitutes an "assignment" under Section 2(a)(4), triggering automatic termination of all 4 mutual fund advisory contracts by operation of law. Rule 2a-6 exemption does not apply because the transaction results in change of actual control. Pinnacle must obtain board approval (Section 15(c)) and shareholder approval (Section 15(a)) for new permanent advisory contracts, but may operate under Rule 15a-4 interim contracts for maximum 150 days post-closing.

2. **Institutional Client Consent Requirements Create Material Termination Risk**: 40% of institutional clients by AUM ($9.4B across approximately 11 clients) have investment advisory agreements requiring prior written consent for change of control under Investment Advisers Act Section 202(a)(1) assignment definition. Industry precedent suggests 5-10% termination probability ($2.1M-$4.2M annual revenue loss), with heightened risk for public pension plans subject to governance constraints. Pre-closing consent solicitation with 85%-95% success rate threshold is critical closing condition.

3. **Limited Partnership Agreements Likely Require LP Consent**: Under Delaware DRULPA freedom of contract principles, LPA contractual provisions govern GP change of control consent requirements (no statutory default). Sophisticated institutional LP side letters typically require 66⅔%-75% LP consent for change of control exceeding 25%-50% ownership thresholds. Two-tier consent structure (notice + put right vs. affirmative consent) creates redemption risk of $1.2B-$1.7B if 25%-40% of LPs elect to withdraw. LP consent solicitation memorandum emphasizing Global Asset Partners' track record and investment team continuity is essential.

4. **Key Person Provisions Present Critical Post-Closing Risk**: John Doe CIO departure within 12 months post-closing (or departure of 2+ senior PMs) likely triggers Key Person Event under LP side letters, resulting in: (i) suspension of new investments; (ii) LP withdrawal rights; (iii) potential GP removal by 66⅔% LP vote. Aggregate exposure: $3.0B-$6.3B redemption risk and potential loss of $126M-$206M annual private fund revenue. Enhanced retention agreements (36-48 month term, not 12-24 months) with GP removal indemnification are necessary mitigation.

5. **Side Letter MFN Clauses Create Fee Reduction Exposure**: 15 LPs holding material capital commitments have MFN clauses requiring automatic extension of any preferential fee terms granted to other LPs. If Pinnacle offers selective fee discounts (e.g., 10-15% reduction) to 3-5 large LPs threatening redemption post-closing, MFN clauses trigger for all 15 MFN LPs, resulting in $3.6M annual revenue reduction ($14.4M present value over remaining fund term). Seller holdback escrow ($10M-$15M) should allocate this risk to sellers who negotiated pre-existing MFN side letters.

6. **Custodian and Distributor Relationships Pose Low Risk**: State Street Bank custody agreements likely continue without requiring consent (only notice required), though State Street may leverage change of control to renegotiate custody fees ($2.0M-$6.1M potential annual increase if fees rise from 2-3 bps to 3-4 bps). Pinnacle Distributors LLC must file FINRA Form BR 30+ days pre-closing (recommend 90-120 day lead time for 60-90 day FINRA review). Broker-dealer selling agreement terminations are rare (<5% probability, <$500K revenue impact) based on historical precedent of PE acquisitions of fund distributors.

7. **Proxy Solicitation Costs Are Material Transaction Expense**: Obtaining shareholder approval for 4 mutual fund permanent advisory contracts requires comprehensive proxy solicitation costing $1.7M-$2.6M (legal counsel, printing/mailing, proxy solicitor, independent counsel, tabulation agent). These costs are properly borne by Pinnacle/Global Asset Partners (not fund shareholders) under SEC guidance because change of control benefits adviser, not fund. Timeline: 60-90 days from proxy mailing to shareholder meeting.

8. **Aggregate Commercial Contracts Exposure Is Material to Deal Economics**: Combined quantified risk ranges from $10.4M-$20.7M annual revenue loss (low-high scenarios), translating to $40M-$80M present value impact and $106M-$219M potential purchase price adjustment using 10.6x revenue multiple. Mid-range exposure ($72M purchase price impact, 4% of $1.8B enterprise value) warrants earnout provisions, indemnification caps, and closing conditions tied to pre-closing consent achievement rates (85%-95% of AUM subject to consent requirements).
