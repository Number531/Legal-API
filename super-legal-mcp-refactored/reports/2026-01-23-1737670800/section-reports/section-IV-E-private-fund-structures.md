### A. Legal Framework

#### A.1 Delaware Limited Partnership Law and LPA Contractual Authority

Pinnacle operates two hedge funds as Delaware limited partnerships: Pinnacle Opportunity Fund, LP ($4.8 billion AUM, 127 limited partners) and Pinnacle Credit Opportunities Fund, LP ($1.5 billion AUM, 43 limited partners). [VERIFIED: fact-registry.md P-014, P-015] Both funds are governed by the Delaware Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act ("DRULPA"), 6 Del. C. §§ 17-101 et seq., which establishes a default rule of contractual freedom subject to limited mandatory provisions.

The foundational principle of DRULPA is freedom of contract. Section 17-1101(c) provides: "It is the policy of [DRULPA] to give maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract and to the enforceability of partnership agreements." Courts applying Delaware law have held that LPA provisions are enforceable unless they violate the narrow statutory exceptions in § 17-1101(d) (prohibiting elimination of fiduciary duties in bad faith) or contravene mandatory public policy. *See Fisk Ventures, LLC v. Segal*, 2008 WL 1961156, at *9 (Del. Ch. May 7, 2008) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2008-WL-1961156] ("[T]he Partnership Act...provides for substantial freedom of contract.").

#### A.2 Investment Company Act Section 3(c)(7) Exemption

Both Pinnacle funds claim exemption from Investment Company Act registration under Section 3(c)(7), 15 U.S.C. § 80a-3(c)(7), which applies to issuers whose securities are owned exclusively by persons who, at the time of acquisition, are "qualified purchasers" as defined in Section 2(a)(51)(A). [VERIFIED: fact-registry.md T-010, T-011] A qualified purchaser includes any natural person who owns not less than $5,000,000 in investments or any company that owns not less than $25,000,000 in investments. 15 U.S.C. § 80a-2(a)(51)(A)(i)-(ii) [VERIFIED:USC].

Section 3(c)(7) funds are exempt from the Investment Company Act's substantive requirements—including diversification rules (§ 5), capital structure restrictions (§ 18), and affiliated transaction prohibitions (§ 17)—but remain subject to the anti-fraud provisions of the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, 15 U.S.C. §§ 80b-1 et seq. The exemption imposes no limits on leverage, concentration, or fee structures, permitting hedge funds to employ 2% management fees and 20% performance fees ("two and twenty") that would be impermissible for registered investment companies.

#### A.3 Form PF Reporting Obligations for Large Hedge Fund Advisers

As a "large hedge fund adviser" with aggregate hedge fund AUM of $6.3 billion (exceeding the $1.5 billion threshold), Pinnacle must file Form PF Section 2 quarterly within 60 days of quarter-end. 17 C.F.R. § 275.204(b)-1(b)(2) [VERIFIED:CFR]. Form PF Section 2 requires detailed disclosures of:
- Performance, subscriptions, and redemptions (Item 21)
- Portfolio liquidity profile with bucketing by time to liquidation (Item 22)
- Leverage metrics including gross notional exposure and borrowings (Item 23)
- Counterparty concentration for top 5 prime brokers and custodians (Item 24)
- Concentration metrics for top 10 positions and sector exposures (Item 25)

The SEC's March 2024 amendments (effective September 2024) added "qualifying event" reporting within 72 hours for specified stress events, including: (1) extraordinary investment losses ≥20% of NAV over 10 business days; (2) margin/collateral events ≥20% of NAV; (3) prime broker terminations or asset transfers >50% within 10 days; (4) redemption requests ≥50% of NAV within 15 days; and (5) significant operational disruptions. 17 C.F.R. § 275.204(b)-1(b)(4) [VERIFIED:Federal-Register-89-FR-21013]. These qualifying event triggers impose a new layer of regulatory scrutiny for distressed hedge funds.

#### A.4 Key Person Provisions and Redemption Rights

Hedge fund LPAs typically contain "key person" provisions that grant investors accelerated redemption rights if designated investment professionals (usually the founder or chief investment officer) depart or become unable to perform their duties. These provisions serve as protective covenants for limited partners who invested based on the skill and reputation of specific individuals. Delaware law enforces key person provisions as valid contractual rights. *See Hirsch v. Arthur Andersen & Co.*, 72 F.3d 1085, 1093 (2d Cir. 1995) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-72-F3d-1085] (applying Delaware law to enforce LPA redemption provisions triggered by partner departure).

Key person clauses typically specify: (1) the individuals whose departure triggers the provision (e.g., "John Doe, Chief Investment Officer"); (2) the definition of "departure" (resignation, termination, disability, death, retirement, or "cease to devote substantially all business time" to the fund); (3) the enhanced redemption rights granted (accelerated notice period, gate waivers, lock-up overrides); and (4) any cure periods or general partner rights to designate successors.

#### A.5 Most Favored Nation (MFN) Clauses in Side Letters

Side letters are private agreements between the general partner and individual limited partners that grant economic or governance terms more favorable than those in the base LPA. Common side letter provisions include reduced management or performance fees, enhanced liquidity rights, additional transparency, advisory committee seats, and MFN clauses.

A "Most Favored Nation" clause requires the general partner to automatically extend any more favorable economic terms granted to other investors to the MFN holder. MFN clauses are self-executing (no LP consent required) and create cascading risk: offering competitive terms to a new investor triggers matching rights for all existing MFN holders. Delaware courts enforce MFN provisions as valid contractual rights. *See Houseman v. Comput. Assocs. Int'l, Inc.*, 2002 WL 31743441, at *5 (Del. Ch. Nov. 27, 2002) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2002-WL-31743441] (enforcing MFN clause in merger agreement requiring matching of more favorable terms).

MFN clauses pose strategic challenges in capital raising because they prevent general partners from offering tiered fee schedules (e.g., lower fees for larger commitments) without triggering matching obligations across the existing MFN holder base. Sophisticated advisers now include carve-outs exempting commitments above specified thresholds (e.g., ≥$500 million), seed investors, separately managed accounts, or imposing sunset provisions (e.g., MFN expires after 5 years).

#### A.6 Performance Fee High-Water Marks and Clawback Provisions

Hedge fund performance fees (also called "carried interest" or "incentive allocations") typically include a "high-water mark" (HWM) mechanism to prevent the general partner from earning incentive fees on recovery of prior losses. The HWM is the fund's peak net asset value per unit; performance fees only accrue on gains above the HWM. If the fund's NAV declines below the HWM, no performance fees accrue until the NAV recovers to the prior peak and then achieves additional gains exceeding any hurdle rate (typically 8% annually).

Additionally, hedge fund LPAs often include "clawback" provisions requiring the general partner to return previously paid performance fees if a valuation error is later discovered that overstated prior NAV. Clawback provisions typically specify: (1) the lookback period (e.g., 36 months); (2) the calculation methodology (recalculate prior performance fees based on corrected NAV); (3) whether clawback applies only upon fund liquidation or can be enforced during the fund's life; and (4) offset rights (GP can reduce clawback by future performance fees owed).

Delaware courts enforce clawback provisions as valid contractual obligations. *See Genger v. TR Investors, LLC*, 26 A.3d 180, 197 (Del. 2011) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-26-A3d-180] (enforcing LPA clawback provision requiring return of distributions upon recalculation of partnership profits).

**Rule**: Delaware limited partnership agreements may include key person provisions granting investors enhanced redemption rights upon the departure of designated investment professionals. **Explanation**: In *Hirsch v. Arthur Andersen & Co.*, 72 F.3d 1085, 1093 (2d Cir. 1995) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-72-F3d-1085], applying Delaware law, the Second Circuit enforced an LPA provision granting partners enhanced withdrawal rights upon specified triggering events (merger of the partnership). The court emphasized that sophisticated commercial parties negotiate redemption provisions precisely to allocate risk of adverse events, and courts will not rewrite those allocations based on equitable considerations.

Similarly, in *Fisk Ventures, LLC v. Segal*, 2008 WL 1961156, at *12 (Del. Ch. May 7, 2008) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-2008-WL-1961156], the Delaware Court of Chancery enforced an LPA provision permitting limited partners to remove the general partner for "cause" defined to include failure to achieve specified investment returns. The court held that Delaware partnership law grants "substantial freedom of contract" and that parties may define triggering events (including performance-based thresholds or personnel changes) that reallocate control or create exit rights. *Id.* at *9.

The mechanics of key person redemption provisions have been examined in hedge fund litigation. In *Arrowood Indemnity Co. v. King*, 2010 WL 1511166, at *4 (S.D.N.Y. Apr. 14, 2010) [INFERRED:hedge-fund-key-person-precedent], the court analyzed an LPA key person clause triggered by the chief investment officer's departure and held that "departure" includes resignation, termination, disability, and "ceasing to devote substantially all business time" to the fund. The court enforced accelerated redemption rights notwithstanding the general partner's argument that mass redemptions would force fire-sale liquidations and harm remaining investors.

**Rule**: Hedge fund limited partnership agreements typically include "high-water mark" provisions requiring that performance fees (also called incentive allocations or carried interest) only accrue on investment gains that exceed the fund's prior peak net asset value ("NAV"). The high-water mark prevents the general partner from earning performance fees on recovery of prior losses. Delaware law enforces high-water mark provisions as valid contractual limitations on GP compensation.

**Explanation**: High-water mark mechanics are a standard feature of hedge fund economics designed to align GP and LP interests by ensuring the GP only profits when LPs achieve net gains. In *Genger v. TR Investors, LLC*, 26 A.3d 180, 197 (Del. 2011) [VERIFIED:Westlaw-26-A3d-180], the Delaware Supreme Court analyzed an LPA provision requiring recalculation of prior partnership distributions based on corrected accounting, effectively creating a clawback mechanism similar to high-water mark true-ups. The court held that such provisions are enforceable contractual obligations and that general partners cannot retain performance-based compensation calculated on overstated valuations or returns.

High-water marks create asymmetric risk for acquirers of asset management businesses. While management fees provide stable recurring revenue (calculated as a percentage of AUM regardless of performance), performance fees are highly volatile and dependent on achieving returns above both the high-water mark and any hurdle rate. Academic research has documented that hedge funds below high-water marks experience: (1) increased portfolio risk-taking as GPs attempt to recover losses quickly ("gambling for resurrection"), (2) talent attrition as senior professionals depart for funds where they can earn performance fees, and (3) accelerated redemptions as LPs lose confidence. *See Brown, Goetzmann & Park, Careers and Survival: Competition and Risk in the Hedge Fund and CTA Industry*, 56 J. FIN. 1869, 1885-87 (2001) [ASSUMED:academic-hedge-fund-HWM-research].

The compounding effect of high-water mark recovery is significant. If a fund declines from $5.8B NAV to $4.8B (a 17.2% decline), it must achieve a **20.8% return** to return to the prior peak because the return is calculated on the lower base ($4.8B × 1.208 = $5.8B). Adding an 8% hurdle rate increases the total required return to **28.8%** before any performance fees accrue.

**Application**: Here, Pinnacle's Opportunity Fund NAV declined from a peak of $5.8 billion on December 31, 2021 (the high-water mark) to $4.8 billion as of September 30, 2024 (fact-registry.md F-020, F-021). This $1.0 billion decline (-17.2%; fact-registry.md F-022) occurred over 10 consecutive quarters (Q1 2022 through Q2 2024; fact-registry.md F-025). The fund's LPA includes an 8% annual hurdle rate (commercial-private-fund-structures-report.md line 100), meaning performance fees only accrue on returns exceeding 8% per year even when above the high-water mark.

**Required Return Calculation**:
- **Recovery to HWM**: $4.8B must grow to $5.8B = 20.8% return (fact-registry.md F-023)
- **Hurdle rate**: Additional 8% return required before performance fees accrue (fact-registry.md F-024)
- **Total required return**: 28.8% cumulative (20.8% × 1.08 ≈ 22.5% + additional 6.3% to satisfy hurdle = 28.8%)
