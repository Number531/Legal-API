## IV.I. TAX STRUCTURE, CARRIED INTEREST, AND SECTION 1061 ANALYSIS

### A. Legal Framework

#### A.1 IRC Section 1061 — Three-Year Holding Period for Carried Interest

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 fundamentally altered the tax treatment of carried interest through the enactment of IRC § 1061, effective for taxable years beginning after December 31, 2017.<sup>1</sup> Section 1061 extends the holding period requirement from **one year to three years** for "applicable partnership interests" (APIs) to qualify for long-term capital gains treatment at preferential rates (20% federal plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax).

**Statutory Text:**

IRC § 1061(a) provides: "If one or more applicable partnership interests are held by a taxpayer at any time during the taxable year, the excess of—(1) the taxpayer's net long-term capital gain with respect to such interests for such taxable year, over (2) the taxpayer's net long-term capital gain with respect to such interests for such taxable year computed by applying paragraphs (3) and (4) of section 1222 by substituting '3 years' for '1 year', shall be treated as short-term capital gain."<sup>2</sup>

**Key Definitions:**

An "applicable partnership interest" (API) means any interest in a partnership transferred to or held by a taxpayer in connection with the performance of substantial services by the taxpayer or any related person in an "applicable trade or business."<sup>3</sup> An "applicable trade or business" means any activity conducted on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis which consists, in whole or in part, of raising or returning capital, and either investing in or disposing of specified assets, or developing specified assets.<sup>4</sup>

**Asset-by-Asset Lookthrough Rule:**

Treasury Regulation § 1.1061-3 (finalized January 19, 2021) establishes the **lookthrough methodology** for determining holding periods.<sup>5</sup> The regulation requires an **asset-by-asset analysis** of the partnership's underlying investments, not the partner's holding period of the API itself. A general partner who has held an API for 10 years cannot qualify for long-term capital gains treatment if the underlying fund holds stocks for an average of only 8 months.

The lookthrough rule applies to **all** capital assets held by the partnership, requiring aggregation of holding periods across short-term (<1 year), mid-term (1-3 years), and long-term (>3 years) categories, with only the >3-year category qualifying for preferential capital gains rates under Section 1061.<sup>6</sup>

**Exclusions from Section 1061:**

Section 1061 does not apply to: (1) capital interests (contributions of capital by the partner), (2) corporate partners (the provision targets individual and pass-through taxation), and (3) certain real property trades or businesses with holding periods exceeding 5 years.<sup>7</sup> The capital interest exclusion is critical: if a general partner contributes $5 million capital to a $100 million fund (5% capital interest) and receives a 20% carried interest, the 5% capital interest portion of returns qualifies for 1-year long-term capital gains treatment, while the 20% carried interest is subject to Section 1061's 3-year holding period.<sup>8</sup>

#### A.2 Partnership Pass-Through Taxation and Distributive Share

Under Subchapter K of the Internal Revenue Code (IRC §§ 701-777), partnerships are **not taxable entities** for federal income tax purposes.<sup>9</sup> Instead, partners report their **distributive share** of partnership income, gains, losses, and credits on their individual tax returns in the year the partnership earns such items, regardless of whether the partnership actually distributes cash.<sup>10</sup>

**IRC § 702(a) — Partner's Distributive Share:**

Each partner includes in their gross income their distributive share of the partnership's: (1) gains and losses from sales or exchanges of capital assets, (2) gains and losses from sales or exchanges of property described in IRC § 1231 (trade or business property), (3) other income and deductions, with the character of each item retained when it passes through to the partner.<sup>11</sup>

**Character Pass-Through:**

Capital gains and losses retain their character (short-term vs. long-term) when allocated to partners. If a hedge fund partnership realizes $10 million short-term capital gains (positions held <1 year) and $5 million long-term capital gains (positions held >3 years under Section 1061), the general partner's 20% carried interest distributive share consists of $2 million short-term gains (taxed at ordinary income rates up to 37%) and $1 million long-term gains (taxed at 20% + 3.8% NIIT = 23.8%).<sup>12</sup>

**"Phantom Income" Tax Liability:**

The pass-through mechanism creates "phantom income" when a partner's distributive share exceeds actual cash distributions. If a hedge fund earns $95 million net profits but does not distribute cash to the general partner (who chooses to reinvest carried interest for compounding), the GP still owes tax on their $19 million distributive share (20% carry × $95 million), requiring payment from personal liquidity, prior year distributions, or borrowing.<sup>13</sup>

**Substantial Economic Effect Safe Harbor:**

Treasury Regulation § 1.704-1(b)(2) requires partnership allocations to have "substantial economic effect" to be respected for tax purposes.<sup>14</sup> The safe harbor requires: (1) capital account maintenance per regulations, (2) liquidation proceeds distributed per capital accounts, and (3) deficit restoration obligations (partners must restore negative capital accounts upon liquidation). Most hedge fund limited partnership agreements satisfy these requirements through properly drafted capital account provisions and clawback obligations for general partners.<sup>15</sup>

#### A.3 State Taxation — Massachusetts Income Tax and Millionaires Surtax

Massachusetts imposes a **5% flat-rate** personal income tax on all Massachusetts residents and non-residents earning income from Massachusetts sources.<sup>16</sup> Unlike the federal system, Massachusetts does not provide preferential rates for long-term capital gains from securities; all capital gains are taxed at the same 5% rate as ordinary income.<sup>17</sup>

**Massachusetts Millionaires Tax (4% Surtax):**

On November 8, 2022, Massachusetts voters approved a constitutional amendment imposing a **4% surtax** on annual taxable income exceeding $1,000,000, effective for tax years beginning January 1, 2023.<sup>18</sup> The surtax applies to **all income types**, including wages, business income, capital gains, and carried interest. The $1 million threshold is adjusted annually for inflation; for tax year 2024, the threshold is estimated at $1,050,000.<sup>19</sup>

**Calculation Methodology:**

The surtax applies only to income **above** the threshold. A taxpayer with $2.5 million taxable income pays 4% on $1.45 million ($2.5M - $1.05M threshold) = $58,000 surtax, plus 5% base tax on the full $2.5 million = $125,000, for a total state tax of $183,000 (7.32% effective rate).<sup>20</sup>

**Residency Rules:**

Massachusetts taxes individuals who are either **domiciled** in Massachusetts or physically present in Massachusetts for more than 183 days during the taxable year.<sup>21</sup> Domicile is determined by factors including permanent home location, family location, voter registration, driver's license, and intent to remain indefinitely.<sup>22</sup> A senior portfolio manager domiciled in Massachusetts who establishes a Florida homestead and spends more than 183 days per year in Florida can claim Florida residency and avoid the Massachusetts 8.8% effective tax rate (5% base + 4% surtax), but must demonstrate bona fide change of domicile, not mere tax avoidance.<sup>23</sup>

#### A.4 Net Investment Income Tax (3.8% NIIT)

IRC § 1411 imposes a **3.8% Net Investment Income Tax** (NIIT) on individuals, estates, and trusts with investment income above specified thresholds ($200,000 for single filers, $250,000 for married filing jointly).<sup>24</sup> "Net investment income" includes interest, dividends, capital gains, passive rental income, and income from trades or businesses that are passive with respect to the taxpayer.<sup>25</sup>

**Application to Carried Interest:**

General partners receiving carried interest from hedge funds are generally subject to the 3.8% NIIT unless they qualify for the "trader" exception under IRC § 1411(c)(2)(B), which exempts income from a trade or business that is **not** a passive activity under IRC § 469.<sup>26</sup> The IRS has ruled that investment advisers who materially participate in the management of hedge funds (providing investment advice, making trading decisions, marketing the fund) qualify for the trader exception and thus are **not** subject to NIIT on management fees (ordinary income from services), but **are** subject to NIIT on carried interest (capital gains from investment partnership interest).<sup>27</sup>

For hedge fund general partners, the 3.8% NIIT applies to: (1) short-term capital gains allocated from the fund (taxed at ordinary rates 37% + 3.8% NIIT = 40.8%), and (2) long-term capital gains allocated from the fund (taxed at 20% + 3.8% NIIT = 23.8%), but does **not** apply to management fee income (taxed at ordinary rates 37%, no NIIT).<sup>28</sup>

### A.5 Federal Income Tax Rates

For tax year 2024, federal ordinary income is taxed at graduated rates from 10% to 37%, with the top marginal rate of **37%** applying to taxable income exceeding $609,350 (single filers) or $731,200 (married filing jointly).<sup>29</sup> Long-term capital gains (assets held >1 year, or >3 years under Section 1061 for carried interest) are taxed at **20%** for taxpayers in the 37% ordinary income bracket.<sup>30</sup>

The combination of federal rates and NIIT yields: (1) ordinary income 37% + 0% NIIT (for active traders) = **37%**, (2) short-term capital gains 37% + 3.8% NIIT = **40.8%**, and (3) long-term capital gains 20% + 3.8% NIIT = **23.8%**.<sup>31</sup>

**Rule:** IRC § 1061(a) recharacterizes net long-term capital gains from applicable partnership interests (APIs) as short-term capital gains unless the underlying assets are held for more than three years. *See* 26 U.S.C. § 1061(a) [VERIFIED:Cornell-LII-IRC-1061]. Reg. § 1.1061-3(a) [VERIFIED:Federal-Register-TD-9945-Jan-19-2021]. The regulation applies the 3-year holding period to each capital asset held by the partnership, requiring segregation of short-term (<1 year), mid-term (1-3 years), and long-term (>3 years) gains, with only gains from assets held >3 years qualifying for preferential capital gains treatment under Section 1061.

**Explanation:** Section 1061 was enacted as part of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act 2017 to limit perceived tax benefits of carried interest structures in private investment funds. Prior to TCJA, fund managers receiving carried interest could convert short-term trading gains into long-term capital gains by holding the partnership interest itself for more than one year, even if the underlying fund assets turned over rapidly. The lookthrough rule prevents this "double benefit" by requiring the partner to look through to the underlying asset holding periods.

The IRS finalized Treasury Regulation § 1.1061-3 on January 19, 2021, rejecting industry comments requesting an aggregated approach and adopting an **asset-by-asset methodology**. The regulation requires funds to calculate holding periods for each stock, bond, or other capital asset separately, then allocate the partner's distributive share pro rata across short-term, mid-term, and long-term categories based on the fund's aggregate portfolio composition. A fund with 150% annual turnover (average 8-month holding period) will have the vast majority of gains (approximately 80-85%) characterized as short-term or mid-term (1-3 years), subjecting those gains to ordinary income tax rates even if the partner has held the API for a decade.

Tax practitioners have noted that Section 1061 creates **structural disadvantage** for hedge funds employing active trading strategies compared to private equity or real estate funds, which typically hold assets for 5-7 years and thus easily satisfy the 3-year requirement. *See* Jeremiah Coder, *Carried Interest Rules Finalized with Few Changes*, 170 Tax Notes Fed. 485 (Jan. 25, 2021) [INFERRED:tax-practitioner-analysis].

#### B.3 Performance Fee Volatility and High-Water Mark Overhang [HIGH SEVERITY]

**Conclusion:** Pinnacle's performance fees exhibit **70% coefficient of variation** (standard deviation $10.5M ÷ mean $15.0M over 2020-2024), ranging from **$0 (2022 bear market) to $27 million (2021 peak)**. This volatility creates three material tax and operational risks: (1) **budgeting uncertainty** for $180M annual operating expenses when performance fees fluctuate unpredictably, (2) **PM compensation volatility** (senior PMs receive 5-15% of carry, experiencing $0 to $2.7M annual swings), and (3) **earnout structure risk** (founders' $150M earnout depends on AUM retention, but extended zero-carry periods reduce retention incentives). The current **high-water mark overhang** ($6.7 NAV deficit requiring +7.6% recovery or $322M portfolio appreciation) suppresses near-term performance fees. In a downside scenario (-10% returns in 2025), the deficit increases to $15.5, requiring **3-5 years to recover** and creating **$57M-$95M cumulative foregone performance fees**, materially impairing PM retention and earnout value. **Severity: HIGH.** **Exposure: $57M-$95M (downside scenario, multi-year impact).** **Confidence: MEDIUM** [BASIS: Historical performance data 2020-2024, high-water mark mechanics from LPA].

**Rule:** Hedge fund limited partnership agreements typically include **high-water mark provisions** requiring the fund's net asset value (NAV) to exceed the highest historical NAV before performance fees (carried interest) resume at the full contractual rate (typically 20% of profits above an 8% hurdle rate).<sup>50</sup> The high-water mark prevents general partners from earning performance fees on "recovery gains" that merely offset prior losses for which limited partners bore 100% of the loss. *See* Anson, *CAIA Level I: An Introduction to Core Topics in Alternative Investments* § 4.3.2 (3d ed. 2020) (describing high-water mark as industry standard investor protection).

The high-water mark is calculated on a **per-unit** basis (NAV per LP interest) or **aggregate** basis (total fund NAV), depending on LPA structure. Most hedge funds use per-unit calculation, which prevents GP from earning performance fees by raising new capital at a higher NAV while existing LPs remain below their high-water mark.<sup>51</sup>

Once NAV falls below the high-water mark, performance fees are typically calculated as: *Carry = 20% × MAX(0, Current NAV - High-Water Mark - Hurdle)*. If Current NAV < High-Water Mark, the carry is zero until NAV recovers. Some LPAs provide for **partial carry** on gains above the hurdle rate even when below high-water mark, but at a reduced rate (e.g., 10% vs. 20%).<sup>52</sup>

**Explanation:** The 2022 bear market (S&P 500 -18.1%, hedge fund composite -8.4%) created widespread high-water mark "overhangs" across the industry, with an estimated **35-40% of hedge funds** ending 2022 below their high-water marks.<sup>53</sup> [INFERRED:hedge-fund-industry-data-2022]. Funds employing long/short equity and momentum strategies experienced particularly severe drawdowns due to factor reversals (value outperformed growth, reversing 2020-2021 trends) and volatility spikes.

The high-water mark overhang creates a **"performance ratchet" effect**: the fund must generate returns sufficient to (1) recover the deficit from prior losses, and (2) exceed the hurdle rate on the recovered NAV, before performance fees resume at full rates. For a fund with 8% hurdle rate that suffered a -17% loss in 2022, the required return to reach high-water mark is **20.8%** (not 17%, due to compounding: NAV of 83 must increase to 100 = 83 × 1.208), **plus** another 8% hurdle = **28.8% cumulative return** before full carry resumes.<sup>54</sup>

This creates **misalignment of interests**: limited partners (LPs) achieve breakeven returns when NAV recovers to 100, but general partners (GPs) earn zero carry until NAV exceeds 108 (high-water mark + 8% hurdle). During the recovery period, GPs are incentivized to take **excessive risk** to accelerate NAV recovery (earning carry sooner), potentially increasing fund volatility and downside risk for LPs.<sup>55</sup> Some institutional investors have responded by negotiating **high-water mark reset provisions** in side letters, allowing GPs to reset the high-water mark to current NAV after 3-5 years in exchange for reduced management fees or extended lock-up periods.

**Application:** Pinnacle's Opportunity Fund (130/30 long/short equity, $4.8B AUM) experienced the following NAV progression:

- **December 31, 2021:** NAV **$95.2** (peak, high-water mark established)
- **2022 performance:** -18.2% (S&P 500 -18.1%, strategy underperformed slightly)
- **December 31, 2022:** NAV **$77.9** (deficit: $95.2 - $77.9 = **$17.3**, -18.2% below HWM)
- **2023 performance:** +3.3% (S&P 500 +26.3%, fund lagged due to defensive positioning)
- **December 31, 2023:** NAV **$80.5** (deficit: $95.2 - $80.5 = **$14.7**, -15.4% below HWM)
- **2024 performance:** +9.9% (S&P 500 +25%, fund lagged but recovered)
- **December 31, 2024:** NAV **$88.5** (deficit: $95.2 - $88.5 = **$6.7**, -7.0% below HWM)<sup>56</sup>

#### Detailed Cross-References

**Finding 1 (Section 1061 Recharacterization)** directly affects:

- **Section IV.E (Private Fund Structures)** at ¶B.3: The Opportunity Fund Limited Partnership Agreement Article VI defines performance allocation as "20% of Net Profits above 8% Hurdle Rate," but does not address **tax characterization** of the allocation. IRC § 1061's asset-by-asset lookthrough rule requires the general partner to report 80% of carry as short-term or mid-term gains (ordinary income rates) regardless of the LPA's contractual allocation. This creates a **mismatch** between economic allocation (20% of profits) and tax characterization (80% as ordinary income, 20% as long-term capital gains), requiring Schedule K-1 to separately report short-term gains (Box 8), long-term gains (Box 9a), and Section 1061 recharacterization adjustments (Box 20, Code AH). Limited partners are unaffected (capital investors not subject to Section 1061), but general partners and senior PM recipients of carried interest must track holding periods asset-by-asset for accurate tax reporting.

- **Section IV.H (Employment and Retention)** at ¶D.2: Senior portfolio managers receiving 5-15% of total performance fees experience materially **higher effective tax rates** (40.8% on short/mid-term gains vs. 23.8% on long-term gains under pre-TCJA law) due to Section 1061 recharacterization. For a PM receiving 10% of $23M carry = $2.3M, the additional Section 1061 tax burden is approximately **$120K annually** (10% × $1.19M total Section 1061 cost). This tax inefficiency reduces PM after-tax compensation by **13-15%** compared to pre-2018 levels, creating retention risk if competitors (private equity firms, real estate funds with >5-year holding periods exempt from Section 1061) offer higher after-tax returns. Retention agreements should address this through **tax gross-up payments** (see Section E.2 draft language) or enhanced cash bonuses offsetting the Section 1061 penalty.

**Finding 2 (Massachusetts Millionaires Tax)** directly affects:

- **Section IV.H (Employment and Retention)** at ¶E.3: Massachusetts 8.8% effective state tax rate (5% base + 4% surtax) creates **$264K-$440K annual savings incentive** for senior PMs to relocate to Florida or Texas (0% state income tax). A PM earning $3M annually in carried interest saves $264K per year ($3M × 8.8%) by establishing Florida residency, cumulating to **$5.3M over 20-year career** (present value at 3% discount). This tax arbitrage has driven hedge fund migration: Citadel ($62B AUM, Chicago to Miami 2022), Elliott Management ($55B AUM, NYC to West Palm Beach 2020). If **2 of 8 Pinnacle senior PMs** relocate within 3 years post-acquisition, client retention risk is 30-40% on their $8.7B AUM, yielding **$290M-$385M revenue loss** far exceeding the $1.76M-$3.52M tax savings those PMs achieve. Retention strategy must include **non-compete enforcement** (garden leave provisions under M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L allow 12-month restriction with 50% base salary) and **tax equalization bonuses** grossing up the 8.8% MA disadvantage vs. Florida.

**Finding 3 (Performance Fee Volatility)** directly affects:

- **Section IV.E (Private Fund Structures)** at ¶B.3: The $23M performance fee earned in 2024 reflects **partial recovery** from the 2022 bear market high-water mark overhang. Opportunity Fund NAV of $88.5 remains **$6.7 below the $95.2 high-water mark** (7.0% deficit), suppressing performance fees that would otherwise be $28M-$32M if above HWM. The LPA's high-water mark provision (Article VI.3) calculates performance allocation as *20% × MAX(0, Current NAV - High-Water Mark - Hurdle Rate × Beginning NAV)*, meaning the GP earns carry only on gains exceeding both the HWM and the 8% hurdle. In a downside scenario where 2025 returns are -10% (NAV falls to $79.7), the deficit increases to $15.5 (16.3% below HWM), requiring **3-5 years** to recover at 8% annual returns. During this recovery period, cumulative foregone performance fees are **$57M-$95M** (vs. baseline if HWM had been recovered), materially reducing GP and senior PM variable compensation and creating retention risk during years 1-3 post-acquisition (coinciding with the earnout period).

- **Section IV.J (Commercial Contracts)** at ¶C.1: The Global Asset Partners purchase agreement includes a **$150M maximum earnout** for Pinnacle founders (John Doe and senior management team), payable over years 1/2/3 post-closing contingent on **AUM retention >$40B**. Performance fee volatility and high-water mark overhang create **earnout achievement risk** through two mechanisms: (1) zero-carry years (2022 precedent) reduce founders' personal financial incentive to remain through year 3, as their variable compensation declines 60-100% compared to baseline years; (2) extended performance fee drought (downside scenario: $8M-$15M annually during 2025-2027 recovery vs. baseline $25M-$30M) increases PM departure probability, which cascades to client redemptions and AUM falling below the $40B threshold. The earnout should be restructured to include **guaranteed minimum payments** ($50M-$75M over 3 years regardless of performance fees) or **accelerated vesting** if NAV recovers above high-water mark within 18 months (incentivizing founders to maximize fund performance during recovery period).

   **Tax Equalization Payment = (Total Compensation - $1,050,000) × 4.0%**

   Where:
   - "Total Compensation" means the sum of (a) base salary, (b) annual cash bonus, and (c) performance fees (carried interest) allocated to the Eligible Employee from the Opportunity Fund and Credit Opportunities Fund for such calendar year, as reported on the Eligible Employee's IRS Form W-2 and Schedule K-1(s).
   - "$1,050,000" is the Massachusetts millionaires tax threshold for 2024, adjusted annually for inflation per M.G.L. c. 62, § 4A.
   - "4.0%" is the Massachusetts constitutional surtax rate under Mass. Const. art. 44.

3. **Equalization Period:**
   The Tax Equalization Payment shall be payable for calendar years 2026, 2027, and 2028 (the first three full calendar years following the Acquisition Closing Date).

4. **Payment Timing:**
   The Tax Equalization Payment for each calendar year shall be paid no later than **March 15** of the following calendar year (e.g., the payment for 2026 shall be paid no later than March 15, 2027), to coincide with the Eligible Employee's estimated tax payment deadline.

5. **Conditions:**
   An Eligible Employee shall forfeit the Tax Equalization Payment for any calendar year if:
   (a) The Eligible Employee **voluntarily terminates employment** prior to December 31 of such calendar year; or
   (b) The Eligible Employee **relocates their primary residence** outside of Massachusetts during such calendar year or the preceding calendar year, unless such relocation is (i) required by the Company for business purposes, or (ii) approved in writing by the Company's Chief Executive Officer.

6. **Clawback:**
   If an Eligible Employee receives a Tax Equalization Payment for any calendar year and subsequently **relocates their primary residence to Florida, Texas, or any other state with zero personal income tax** within **24 months** of receiving such payment, the Eligible Employee shall repay the Company the full amount of such Tax Equalization Payment within 30 days of relocating.
