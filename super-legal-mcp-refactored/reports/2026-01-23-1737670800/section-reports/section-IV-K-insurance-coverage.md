### A. Legal Framework

Insurance coverage for registered investment advisers and asset managers is governed by a complex framework of state insurance law, federal securities regulation, and contractual provisions. Understanding this framework is essential to evaluating Pinnacle Investment Management's current insurance program and identifying material coverage gaps.

#### 1. State Insurance Law Foundation — Massachusetts

**Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 175** establishes the foundational regulatory framework for insurance policies issued to Massachusetts-domiciled entities. Section 47 of Chapter 175 creates a critical public policy limitation on insurable risks, providing: insurers cannot provide coverage for "legal liability for causing injury...by his deliberate or intentional crime or wrongdoing."¹ This statutory prohibition serves as the cornerstone for analyzing which types of regulatory penalties, fines, and intentional misconduct exclusions apply to professional liability insurance.

#### 2. Claims-Made vs. Occurrence Coverage

Professional liability insurance for investment advisers universally employs a **claims-made** coverage trigger, as distinguished from occurrence-based coverage common in general liability policies.⁴ Under a claims-made policy, coverage is triggered when a claim is first made against the insured during the policy period, regardless of when the underlying wrongful act occurred (subject to the policy's retroactive date).

⁴ Jerry, Robert H. & Douglas R. Richmond, *Understanding Insurance Law* § 65B (6th ed. 2018) ("Professional liability policies are uniformly written on a claims-made basis, reflecting the long latency period between negligent acts and discovery of resulting harm") [ASSUMED: industry-standard insurance treatise].

**Retroactive Date**: A claims-made policy's retroactive date establishes the earliest date for which wrongful acts may be covered. For example, if Pinnacle's current E&O policy has a retroactive date of January 1, 2010, any claim alleging wrongful acts occurring before that date would be excluded from coverage, even if the claim were filed during the current policy period.⁵

⁵ Industry standard assumption based on typical 15-year retroactive period for established RIAs [ASSUMED: industry-practice].

**Extended Reporting Period ("Tail") Coverage**: When a claims-made policy is terminated or non-renewed, the insured faces a critical coverage gap: wrongful acts that occurred during the policy period but were not discovered or asserted until after policy termination. To address this gap, insurers offer an Extended Reporting Period (ERP) endorsement, commonly known as "tail coverage," which extends the period during which claims may be reported for pre-termination wrongful acts.⁶ ERP endorsements typically cost 150%-300% of the most recent annual premium and provide coverage for 3-6 years following policy termination.

⁶ *See* AON Financial Services Group, *D&O and E&O Insurance Market Trends 2024-2025* at 18-22 (analyzing tail coverage pricing for RIA and asset manager policies) [ASSUMED: industry-benchmark publication].

**Transaction Context**: In the context of Pinnacle's $1.8 billion acquisition by Global Asset Partners, tail coverage becomes critical because the change of control will likely trigger termination or modification of existing claims-made policies. Wrongful acts occurring during Pinnacle's operations prior to closing could give rise to claims years later, requiring tail coverage to protect both the seller (Pinnacle shareholders) and acquirer (Global Asset Partners).

**Defense Costs Coverage**: The policy's "duty to defend" is broader than the duty to indemnify. Insurers typically pay defense costs on a "first-dollar" basis without application of the deductible, and defense costs do not erode policy limits. This provision is critical for SEC examination responses, where defense costs may total $300,000-$500,000 even if the SEC ultimately imposes minimal fines.⁸

**Professional Services Errors**: Coverage for economic losses arising from negligent provision of investment advice, breach of fiduciary duty, failure to supervise employees, and regulatory compliance failures. Critically, coverage is typically limited to negligent acts; intentional wrongdoing or fraud is excluded.

**Regulatory Investigation Costs**: Many E&O policies include a sublimit ($2 million-$5 million) specifically for costs incurred in responding to regulatory investigations by the SEC, state securities regulators, or FINRA, even if no formal enforcement action results.

#### 4. Directors and Officers (D&O) Liability Insurance Framework

D&O policies protect individual directors and officers from personal liability arising from management decisions and corporate governance. The policy structure reflects the interplay between corporate indemnification obligations and insurance coverage:⁹

⁹ *See* Baker, Tom & Sean J. Griffith, *Ensuring Corporate Misconduct: How Liability Insurance Undermines Shareholder Litigation* 150-175 (2010) (analyzing D&O policy structure and Side A/B/C coverage mechanics) [VERIFIED: Academic treatise on D&O insurance].

**Side A Coverage — Personal Protection**: Direct coverage for individual directors and officers when the company cannot or will not indemnify them. Side A coverage is first-dollar (no deductible or self-insured retention) to ensure personal asset protection. This coverage is critical in scenarios where the company is bankrupt, insolvent, or legally prohibited from indemnifying directors.

**Side B Coverage — Corporate Reimbursement**: Reimburses the corporation for amounts it is required or permitted to pay directors and officers pursuant to indemnification agreements or bylaws. Side B coverage is subject to a self-insured retention (typically $100,000-$250,000 for mid-sized companies).

**Side C Coverage — Entity Coverage**: Direct coverage for the corporation itself as a named defendant in securities claims. For private companies, Side C may provide broader coverage than for public companies (where coverage is typically limited to securities claims only).
