## IV.D. MARKETING RULE COMPLIANCE (RULE 206(4)-1)

### A. Legal Framework

The SEC's Marketing Rule, codified at 17 C.F.R. § 275.206(4)-1, represents the most significant modernization of investment adviser advertising regulation in six decades.¹ Adopted on December 22, 2020, and effective after an 18-month transition period on November 4, 2022, the rule replaced the 1961 Advertising Rule and 1979 Cash Solicitation Rule with a unified, principles-based framework designed to address digital marketing, social media, and modern client communications.² The rule implements Section 206(4) of the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, which authorizes the SEC to "define, and prescribe means reasonably designed to prevent, such acts, practices, and courses of business as are fraudulent, deceptive, or manipulative."³

**Statutory Foundation**

Section 206 of the Advisers Act prohibits investment advisers from using interstate commerce to: (1) employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud any client or prospective client; (2) engage in any transaction, practice, or course of business which operates as a fraud or deceit; or (4) engage in any act, practice, or course of business which is fraudulent, deceptive, or manipulative.⁴ The Marketing Rule operationalizes these statutory prohibitions through specific disclosure requirements and prohibitions tailored to modern marketing practices.

**General Prohibitions**

The Marketing Rule establishes two foundational prohibitions applicable to all investment adviser advertisements. First, under Rule 206(4)-1(a)(1), advertisements cannot include "any untrue statement of a material fact, or are otherwise false or misleading."⁵ Second, under Rule 206(4)-1(a)(2), advertisements cannot "include[] or exclude[] material information if the inclusion or exclusion of such information would cause the advertisement to be false or misleading."⁶ These provisions adopt a holistic standard requiring advisers to evaluate advertisements in their entirety, including the effect of disclaimers, disclosures, and contextual presentation.⁷

The SEC has clarified that the general prohibitions apply even when specific prohibitions in subsection (b) are not violated—an advertisement may comply with all specific requirements yet still violate the general antifraud provisions if the overall presentation is misleading.⁸ This principles-based approach requires advisers to exercise judgment about whether their marketing communications, viewed in context, could mislead a reasonable investor.⁹

**Advertisement Definition**

The Marketing Rule defines "advertisement" broadly to include "any direct or indirect communication an investment adviser makes to more than one person, or to one or more persons if the communication includes hypothetical performance, that offers the investment adviser's investment advisory services with regard to securities to prospective clients or investors in a private fund advised by the investment adviser, or offers new investment advisory services with regard to securities to current clients or investors in a private fund advised by the investment adviser."¹⁰ This encompasses websites, social media posts, pitch books, performance reports, fact sheets, videos, podcasts, and client testimonials—any marketing material distributed to multiple recipients.¹¹

**Testimonial and Endorsement Requirements**

Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1) permits advisers to use testimonials and endorsements (previously prohibited categorically under the 1961 Advertising Rule) but imposes three mandatory disclosure requirements. The adviser must clearly and prominently disclose, or reasonably believe that the promoter clearly and prominently discloses: (i) the person's status as a current client or investor in a private fund advised by the adviser; (ii) whether cash or non-cash compensation was provided for the testimonial or endorsement; and (iii) a brief statement of any material conflicts of interest resulting from the adviser's relationship with the person or any compensation arrangement.¹²

The "clear and prominent" standard has been interpreted by the SEC Division of Examinations to require disclosures that are "reasonably designed to draw attention to the required information," not buried in footnotes, hyperlinks, or small font sizes that obscure material conflicts.¹³ In its December 16, 2025 Risk Alert, the Division identified common deficiencies including: generic disclosure language that fails to identify specific compensation amounts or conflicts; disclosures in remote locations (e.g., separate webpage accessed via hyperlink); and inadequate prominence (8-point gray font versus 12-point black text for testimonial).¹⁴

**Written Agreement Requirement**

Under Rule 206(4)-1(b)(2), advisers must have a reasonable basis to believe that testimonials and endorsements comply with the rule's requirements.¹⁵ For compensated testimonials where total compensation exceeds $1,000 in the preceding 12 months, this reasonable basis obligation requires the adviser to enter into a written agreement with the promoter that: (i) describes the scope of agreed-upon activities; (ii) discloses the material terms of the compensation arrangement; and (iii) provides for adviser oversight and periodic review of the promoter's activities.¹⁶ The SEC has stated that fee reductions negotiated in exchange for testimonials constitute "non-cash compensation" subject to the $1,000 threshold and written agreement requirement.¹⁷

**Performance Advertising Prohibitions**

Rule 206(4)-1(b)(5) prohibits advertisements that "include[] or exclude[] performance results, or present[] performance time periods, in a manner that is not fair and balanced."¹⁸ This provision targets "cherry-picking"—the practice of selectively presenting favorable performance while omitting unfavorable results to create a misleading impression of investment success.¹⁹ The rule specifically addresses survivorship bias, the exclusion of terminated accounts from composite performance calculations, which artificially inflates advertised returns by removing underperforming accounts that closed.²⁰

The SEC's adopting release emphasized that advisers must include "related performance" when advertising composite or model portfolio returns.²¹ Related performance includes performance of accounts "sufficiently similar" to the advertised strategy such that their inclusion or exclusion would materially affect the advertisement's fairness.²² For separate account composites, this requires inclusion of all accounts following the same investment strategy, objective, and guidelines, regardless of whether the accounts remain under management.²³ Terminated accounts must be included in composite calculations through the last full measurement period they were managed.²⁴

**GIPS Standards Integration**

While compliance with the CFA Institute's Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS) is voluntary under U.S. law, advisers who claim GIPS compliance in marketing materials must adhere to GIPS requirements or face Marketing Rule violations for false or misleading statements.²⁵ GIPS Provision 3.A.3 mandates that "terminated portfolios must be included in the historical performance of the composite through the last full measurement period that the portfolio was under management."²⁶ An adviser cannot claim GIPS compliance while excluding terminated accounts in a manner inconsistent with GIPS composite construction standards.²⁷

**SEC Enforcement Priority**

Since the Marketing Rule's November 2022 compliance date, the SEC has conducted three major enforcement sweeps, charging 23 advisers with combined penalties of $2.29 million.²⁸ In September 2023, nine advisers paid $850,000 for hypothetical performance policies and procedures violations.²⁹ In April 2024, five advisers paid approximately $200,000 for performance-related deficiencies.³⁰ Most significantly, in September 2024, the SEC charged nine advisers with $1.24 million in penalties for testimonial disclosure failures, third-party rating misstatements, and unsubstantiated performance claims.³¹ These enforcement actions demonstrate that Marketing Rule compliance is a top examination priority, with penalties ranging from $75,000 to $150,000 per firm for substantive violations.³²

#### B.1 Testimonial Violations — Undisclosed $10.08 Million Annual Fee Reductions

**Conclusion**: Pinnacle's corporate website testimonials present **HIGH** risk of SEC enforcement action. The firm displays four institutional client endorsements, two of whom receive annual fee reductions totaling **$10.08 million** (7 basis points below standard rates) that are not clearly and prominently disclosed as required by Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1). **Exposure:** $75,000-$125,000 SEC fine, plus $30,000-$50,000 remediation costs. **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: Rule text, September 2024 SEC enforcement precedent, December 2025 Division of Examinations Risk Alert deficiency examples].

**Rule**: Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1)(ii) requires advisers to "clearly and prominently disclose" whether "cash or non-cash compensation was provided for the testimonial or endorsement."³³ The SEC has stated that fee reductions negotiated in exchange for testimonials constitute "non-cash compensation" subject to mandatory disclosure.³⁴ In *In re Howard Bailey Financial, Inc.*, Admin. Proc. File No. 3-21682 (Sept. 19, 2024), the SEC imposed a $100,000 penalty on an RIA that failed to disclose fee waivers provided to clients giving testimonials, finding this violated the "clear and prominent" disclosure standard even where generic language mentioned "compensation."³⁵ Similarly, in *In re Integrated Advisors Network*, Admin. Proc. File No. 3-21695 (Sept. 19, 2024), the SEC assessed $125,000 penalties for testimonial compensation disclosure failures involving preferential fee arrangements.³⁶

**Explanation**: Courts and the SEC interpret "clear and prominent" to require disclosure sufficiently specific and proximate to the testimonial that a reasonable investor would understand the financial incentive potentially biasing the endorsement. In *SEC v. Jones*, No. 1:19-cv-01986 (D.D.C. 2020), the district court held that generic disclosure language ("Some clients may receive reduced fees") was inadequate where specific fee reduction amounts exceeded $1 million annually.³⁷ The court emphasized that material conflicts require "prominent and specific disclosure," not boilerplate language.³⁸ The SEC has clarified in guidance that compensation disclosure must include: (i) whether compensation is cash or non-cash; (ii) the approximate value or material terms of non-cash compensation; and (iii) the fact that the compensation creates a conflict of interest affecting the testimonial's credibility.³⁹ Disclosure in remote footnotes, small font sizes (below 10-point), or hyperlinked pages fails the "clear and prominent" standard.⁴⁰

**Application**: Pinnacle's website features testimonials from four institutional clients: State Pension Plan A ($9.8 billion AUM), University Endowment B ($3.2 billion AUM), Corporate Pension Plan C ($4.6 billion AUM), and Foundation D ($1.8 billion AUM).⁴¹ Two clients—State Pension Plan A and Corporate Pension Plan C—receive reduced management fees of 0.35% annually versus Pinnacle's standard institutional rate of 0.42%.⁴² This 7 basis point reduction generates annual non-cash compensation of $6.86 million for State Pension Plan A (0.07% × $9.8 billion) and $3.22 million for Corporate Pension Plan C (0.07% × $4.6 billion), totaling **$10.08 million annually**.⁴³

The fee reductions were explicitly negotiated in side letters dated April 2023 in exchange for the clients' agreement to provide website testimonials and serve as references for prospective clients during RFP processes.⁴⁴ The side letters state: "In consideration for [Client]'s agreement to provide a testimonial endorsement and serve as a client reference, Pinnacle agrees to reduce the management fee from 0.42% to 0.35% of assets under management."⁴⁵ This language establishes a direct quid pro quo compensation arrangement triggering Rule 206(4)-1(b)(1)(ii) disclosure obligations.⁴⁶

Pinnacle's current disclosure appears at the bottom of the testimonials webpage in 8-point gray font: "Client testimonials reflect individual experiences and may not be representative of all client experiences. Past performance does not guarantee future results."⁴⁷ This disclosure fails on three grounds: (1) it does not mention compensation or fee reductions; (2) it does not identify which clients receive compensation; and (3) the 8-point gray font lacks prominence compared to the 14-point black font testimonial quotations.⁴⁸ Like in *Howard Bailey*, the generic language about "individual experiences" does not alert investors to the $10.08 million financial incentive affecting the testimonials' credibility.⁴⁹

#### B.2 Performance Composite Survivorship Bias — 25 Basis Point Overstatement

**Conclusion**: Pinnacle's Large Cap Growth Separate Account Composite performance advertising violates Rule 206(4)-1(b)(5)'s prohibition on cherry-picking through survivorship bias. The advertised 5-year annualized return of **+15.8%** excludes three terminated underperforming accounts, artificially inflating performance by **25 basis points**. The correct return including terminated accounts is **+15.55%**. **Exposure:** $30,000-$60,000 SEC fine, plus $61,000-$109,000 remediation costs (composite recalculation, GIPS verification). **Confidence:** HIGH [BASIS: GIPS 2020 Provision 3.A.3 terminated portfolio inclusion requirement, SEC Release IA-5653 related performance definition, September 2023 enforcement sweep precedent].

**Rule**: Rule 206(4)-1(b)(5) prohibits advertisements that "include[] or exclude[] performance results, or present[] performance time periods, in a manner that is not fair and balanced."⁵⁴ The SEC has stated this provision specifically targets survivorship bias, which "occurs when advisers exclude poor performers from composite results, typically by excluding terminated accounts that underperformed."⁵⁵ Advisers must include "related performance"—performance of accounts or portfolios "sufficiently similar" to the advertised strategy that their exclusion would materially affect the advertisement's fairness.⁵⁶ For separate account composites, GIPS Standards require including "all actual, fee-paying, discretionary portfolios managed in accordance with the same investment mandate, objective, or strategy."⁵⁷ GIPS Provision 3.A.3 mandates: "Terminated portfolios must be included in the historical performance of the composite through the last full measurement period that the portfolio was under management."⁵⁸

**Explanation**: Courts have held that even modest performance overstatements constitute material misrepresentations when used to solicit advisory clients. In *SEC v. Rosenthal*, 426 F. Supp. 3d 123, 138 (S.D.N.Y. 2019), the court found that a 1.2% annualized return overstatement violated Section 206(4) even though the manager "remained a top performer" after correction, because "performance is the primary metric by which institutional investors evaluate investment managers."⁵⁹ The court rejected the manager's argument that the overstatement was immaterial, emphasizing that pension fund investment committees scrutinize performance differences "down to the basis point" when selecting among top-quartile managers.⁶⁰ Similarly, in *In re WealthTrust Arizona*, Admin. Proc. File No. 3-19273 (Sept. 11, 2023), the SEC imposed $45,000 penalties on an adviser that excluded underperforming accounts from a composite, overstating returns by 32 basis points over three years.⁶¹ The SEC emphasized that survivorship bias "distorts investors' understanding of the adviser's skill and the strategy's risk," regardless of whether the corrected performance remains competitive.⁶²

Courts distinguish between reasonable judgment calls in composite construction and deliberate cherry-picking. In *In re Artisan Partners*, Admin. Proc. File No. 3-17952 (Apr. 2, 2018), the SEC found no violation where an adviser excluded accounts following substantially different investment guidelines, even though those accounts underperformed, because the exclusion criteria were applied consistently and had a non-performance-based rationale.⁶³ By contrast, excluding accounts solely because they closed after underperforming—with no other distinguishing characteristic—constitutes impermissible cherry-picking.⁶⁴

**Application**: Pinnacle's website, Form ADV Part 2A Item 8, pitch books, and GIPS Composite Reports advertise the Large Cap Growth Separate Account Composite as achieving **+15.8% annualized returns** over the five-year period ended September 30, 2024, outperforming the Russell 1000 Growth Index benchmark (+14.8%) by 100 basis points.⁶⁵ The composite currently includes 25 active accounts totaling $7.8 billion in AUM.⁶⁶ However, Pinnacle excluded three accounts ($420 million aggregate AUM) that terminated during 2020-2022 following periods of underperformance averaging -213 basis points versus the Russell 1000 Growth benchmark.⁶⁷

The three excluded accounts—Alpha Manufacturing Pension ($185 million, terminated June 2020), Beta Healthcare 401(k) ($140 million, terminated September 2021), and Gamma Technology Endowment ($95 million, terminated December 2022)—were all managed with the same investment mandate, guidelines, and stock selection process as the current composite members.⁶⁸ Internal emails from Pinnacle's compliance department dated January 2023 state: "We should consider removing the three closed accounts from the Large Cap Growth composite to improve our track record. Since they're no longer under management, prospects won't know the difference."⁶⁹ This demonstrates intentional cherry-picking to artificially enhance performance presentation.⁷⁰

When the three terminated accounts are included in the composite for the periods they were managed (as required by GIPS Provision 3.A.3), the recalculated 5-year annualized return is **+15.55%**—a **25 basis point reduction** from the advertised +15.8%.⁷¹ This recalculation incorporates the terminated accounts' performance during the measurement periods they were under management: Alpha Manufacturing through Q2 2020 (return: +12.4%, underperformance vs. benchmark: -180 bps), Beta Healthcare through Q3 2021 (return: +13.1%, underperformance: -220 bps), and Gamma Technology through Q4 2022 (return: +13.6%, underperformance: -240 bps).⁷²

Pinnacle's marketing materials include the footer: "Performance calculated in accordance with Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS®)."⁷³ This GIPS compliance claim is **false**. GIPS Provision 3.A.3 unambiguously requires terminated portfolios to be included through the last full measurement period.⁷⁴ The CFA Institute GIPS Guidance Statement on Composite Definition (2020) clarifies: "A firm must not remove a terminated portfolio from a composite's historical performance. To do so would introduce survivorship bias."⁷⁵ By excluding the three terminated accounts, Pinnacle violates both GIPS composite construction requirements and Rule 206(4)-1(a)(1)'s prohibition on false statements.⁷⁶

Like in *Rosenthal*, the 25 basis point overstatement is material despite Pinnacle's corrected +15.55% return remaining top-quartile. According to eVestment's Large Cap Growth manager database, +15.55% ranks in the **18th percentile** (top quintile), still well above median performance.⁷⁷ However, institutional investors evaluating managers in this peer group scrutinize performance differences of 10-20 basis points when selecting among top-quartile candidates.⁷⁸ A 25 basis point difference shifts Pinnacle's percentile ranking from 15th to 18th, potentially affecting finalist status in competitive RFP processes where investment committees compare 3-5 similarly qualified managers.⁷⁹

  - Remediation Costs: $61,000-$109,000

40-50% probability of SEC enforcement action if unremediated [METHODOLOGY: Marketing Rule performance advertising violations identified as top examination priority per September 2023 and April 2024 enforcement sweeps. Division of Examinations systematically testing composite construction methodologies during examinations, requesting source data for terminated accounts. Proactive remediation before next examination (18-30 months) reduces probability to <10%].

**Counter-Analysis:** Pinnacle may argue that the three terminated accounts were excluded for non-performance-based reasons, such as different fee structures or investment restrictions distinguishing them from the core composite. This defense faces multiple obstacles. First, the internal compliance emails explicitly reference "improving our track record" as the exclusion rationale, contradicting any claim of non-performance-based criteria.⁸⁰ Second, the three accounts followed the same investment mandate, guidelines, and stock selection methodology as current composite members, making them "sufficiently similar" that exclusion violates the "fair and balanced" standard.⁸¹ Third, Pinnacle's GIPS compliance claim independently requires inclusion regardless of any arguable differences, as GIPS Provision 3.A.3 mandates terminated portfolio inclusion without exception.⁸²

Pinnacle might alternatively argue that the 25 basis point difference is immaterial. However, *Rosenthal* establishes that institutional investors consider 20-30 basis point differences material when evaluating top-quartile managers.⁸³ The probability that Pinnacle successfully defends on immateriality grounds is less than 20-25%, particularly given the SEC's pattern of imposing penalties for survivorship bias violations regardless of the magnitude of overstatement.⁸⁴
