### A. Legal Framework

The legal framework governing employee retention and restrictive covenants in post-acquisition contexts involves three intersecting areas: (1) state non-compete law governing enforceability of restrictive covenants, (2) federal and state employment discrimination and termination protections, and (3) contractual mechanisms for retention incentives. For Pinnacle Investment Management, Inc., a Massachusetts-headquartered company with 485 employees (180 investment professionals), the controlling authority is Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 149, Section 24L, the Massachusetts Non-Competes Act ("Reform Act"), effective October 1, 2018.¹

#### 1. Massachusetts Non-Competes Act — Statutory Requirements

Massachusetts General Laws c. 149, § 24L comprehensively regulates non-competition agreements entered into on or after October 1, 2018.² The statute establishes eight mandatory requirements for enforceability:

**Timing and Consideration (§ 24L(b)(vii)):** The non-competition agreement must be in writing and signed by both parties. For newly hired employees, it must be provided either: (a) with a formal offer of employment, or (b) at least 10 business days before the employee commences employment. For current employees, the agreement must be supported by "fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment."³

**Legitimate Business Interest (§ 24L(b)(i)):** The restriction must be "necessary to protect one or more of the following legitimate business interests": (1) the employer's trade secrets, (2) the employer's confidential information that otherwise would not qualify as a trade secret, or (3) the employer's goodwill.⁴ For portfolio managers, the legitimate interests include: proprietary investment models and strategies (trade secrets), client lists and fee arrangements (confidential information), and personal client relationships developed during tenure (goodwill).

**Duration Limit (§ 24L(b)(iii)):** The restriction is limited to "12 months from the date of the employee's cessation of employment, except that it may extend to 2 years from the date of the employee's cessation of employment if the employee has breached his or her fiduciary duty to the employer or the employee has unlawfully taken, physically or electronically, property belonging to the employer."⁵ Pinnacle's standard 1-year non-compete duration complies with this requirement.

**Geographic Scope (§ 24L(b)(iv)):** The geographic reach must be "no broader than necessary to protect" the legitimate business interest. Courts apply a reasonableness standard based on: (a) the employee's actual territorial reach during employment, (b) the employer's business geographic footprint, and (c) whether clients are location-specific or nationally distributed.⁶ For institutional asset management, where clients are nationally dispersed pension plans, endowments, and foundations, nationwide restrictions are generally enforceable, though narrower regional restrictions (e.g., Northeast and Mid-Atlantic) would be more defensible if challenged.

**Activity Scope (§ 24L(b)(v)):** The prohibited activities must be "limited to only the specific types of services provided to the employer by the employee that are within two years before" cessation of employment.⁷ A restriction on a Large Cap Growth portfolio manager from managing Large Cap Growth strategies at a competitor satisfies this requirement; however, a restriction on the same manager from managing Small Cap Value (a different strategy) would likely be unenforceable as overbroad.

**Garden Leave or Other Consideration (§ 24L(b)(vi)):** The employer must provide one of the following during the restricted period: (a) at least 50% of the employee's highest annualized base salary during the two years preceding termination (""garden leave"), or (b) ""other mutually-agreed upon consideration."⁸ Pinnacle's practice of paying 100% of base salary for 6 months effectively satisfies the 50% standard over the full 12-month restricted period ($500,000 base × 100% × 0.5 years = $250,000, equivalent to $500,000 × 50% × 1.0 years). This exceeds the statutory minimum.

**Public Policy (§ 24L(b)(ii)):** The agreement must be "consonant with public policy."⁹ This requirement prohibits agreements that would unreasonably restrict employee mobility in a manner harmful to the public interest. The protection is generally self-executing through the other statutory requirements.

**Prohibited Categories (§ 24L(c)):** Non-competition agreements are unenforceable against: (1) non-exempt employees (covered by overtime requirements under 29 U.S.C. § 207), (2) undergraduate or graduate students participating in internships or short-term employment, (3) employees 18 years old or younger, or (4) **employees terminated without cause or laid off**.¹⁰ Senior portfolio managers as highly compensated investment professionals fall within the "exempt" category under 29 C.F.R. § 541.204 (administrative exemption) and do not trigger the first three prohibited categories. **However, the fourth category—termination without cause—presents the most critical risk in acquisition contexts**.

#### 2. "Termination Without Cause" Exception — The Critical Risk

M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(c)(4) provides that non-competition agreements are unenforceable against "an employee who is terminated without cause or laid off."¹¹ The statute does not define "cause," but Massachusetts courts and legal commentary generally interpret "cause" to require employer proof of: (a) employee willful misconduct, (b) gross negligence, (c) material breach of employment obligations, or (d) violation of company policy.¹² Mere underperformance, elimination of position due to workforce reduction, or strategic decision to reduce headcount would constitute termination "without cause" under this standard.

**Immediate Unenforceability:** Unlike contractual provisions that might allow the parties to negotiate severance in exchange for continued covenant enforceability, § 24L(c)(4) operates as a statutory bar. If the employee is "terminated without cause or laid off," the non-compete is void *ab initio* as to that termination event, regardless of consideration offered. The employee is immediately free to: (a) join a competitor without any restricted period, (b) solicit clients (though separate non-solicitation covenants may still apply if enforceable under different legal standards), and (c) recruit former colleagues.

**Private Equity Acquisition Context:** The Risk is acute in leveraged buyout contexts where private equity buyers frequently conduct workforce reductions to achieve cost synergies and improve operating margins. Industry studies show 10-20% post-acquisition headcount reductions within 18 months of closing for portfolio company acquisitions. If Global Asset Partners (the acquirer) terminates any of Pinnacle's 8 senior portfolio managers "without cause" as part of a post-closing reorganization, § 24L(c)(4) immediately voids those individuals' non-competition agreements. The terminated managers can join Wellington Management, Fidelity Investments, or other Boston-based competitors within days, taking client relationships and recruiting Pinnacle analysts simultaneously.

Courts have not yet directly addressed whether an acquirer-initiated RIF following a change of control triggers § 24L(c)(4), but the statutory text is unambiguous. Several legal commentaries interpret the provision as covering any involuntary termination not based on employee misconduct, including strategic workforce reductions.¹³

#### 3. Non-Solicitation Covenants — Distinct Legal Standard

While Massachusetts law restricts non-competition agreements, non-solicitation covenants (prohibiting employees from soliciting former clients or employees) are governed by common law reasonableness standards predating § 24L.¹⁴ In *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974), the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held that non-solicitation covenants are enforceable if: (a) reasonably limited in time and scope, (b) supported by consideration, and (c) not contrary to public policy.¹⁵ Non-solicitation restrictions do not require the garden leave payment mandated for non-competes under § 24L(b)(vi).

**Critical Distinction:** Section 24L(c)(4)'s "termination without cause" exception applies only to non-competition agreements; it does not expressly void non-solicitation or confidentiality covenants. Therefore, if a portfolio manager is terminated without cause, Pinnacle may still enforce: (a) non-solicitation restrictions (prohibiting affirmative outreach to Pinnacle clients), and (b) confidentiality obligations (prohibiting use or disclosure of Pinnacle's proprietary information). However, without the non-compete restriction preventing the manager from joining a competitor, enforcement of non-solicitation becomes practically difficult—the manager can join a competitor, and if clients independently approach the manager, the non-solicitation covenant is not breached.

#### 4. WARN Act — Federal Mass Layoff Notification

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 2101-2109, requires employers with 100+ employees to provide 60 days' advance written notice of: (a) plant closings affecting 50+ employees at a single site, or (b) mass layoffs affecting either 50-499 employees (if 33%+ of workforce) or 500+ employees (regardless of percentage).¹⁶ Failure to provide timely notice creates liability for: (1) back pay and benefits for each affected employee for up to 60 days, and (2) civil penalties up to $500 per day.¹⁷

**Application to Pinnacle:** With 485 total employees (headquarters in Boston), any reduction exceeding 160 employees (33% of 485) would trigger WARN notice requirements. If the acquirer terminates 100-150 employees (20-31% of workforce) post-closing, WARN notice is required if the reduction affects 50+ employees. Estimated exposure: 100 employees × $800 average daily comp × 60 days = $4.8M, plus penalties $500/day × 60 days = $30,000.¹⁸

**Interaction with Non-Compete Void:** WARN Act compliance (60-day notice) does not cure § 24L(c)(4)'s non-compete void. Even if the employer provides 60 days' advance notice, the terminated employees' non-competes are unenforceable upon termination "without cause."

#### 5. Change of Control Acceleration and Retention Agreements

Retention agreements (also called "golden handcuffs" or stay bonuses) are contractual mechanisms to incentivize key employees to remain through change of control transitions. Industry practice establishes two benchmarks:

**Funding Adequacy Standard:** Retention payments must equal **2-3× total annual compensation** to create meaningful disincentive to departure.¹⁹ For senior portfolio managers earning $6-14M annually (base + bonus + carried interest), effective retention requires $12-42M per individual over a 3-year vesting period.

**Vesting Structure:** Retention agreements typically vest ratably (e.g., 33% per year over 3 years) or cliff vest (100% if employed on specific anniversary). **Critical risk:** Cliff vesting at a single AUM threshold (e.g., "$150M earnout if Pinnacle retains $40B AUM at Year 3") exposes the incentive to market risk. If equity/bond markets decline 10-15% due to macroeconomic factors, Pinnacle's AUM may fall below the threshold despite zero client terminations, eliminating the earnout incentive and triggering voluntary departures.²⁰

**Rule:** M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(c)(4) provides: "A noncompetition agreement shall not be enforceable against... an employee who is terminated without cause or laid off."⁴⁸ The statute does not define "cause," but Massachusetts employment law precedents establish that termination "for cause" requires employer proof of: (a) employee willful misconduct, (b) gross negligence or incompetence, (c) material breach of fiduciary duty, or (d) violation of workplace policies after notice and opportunity to cure.⁴⁹ Terminations for business reasons—including workforce reductions, position eliminations, or strategic reorganizations—constitute termination "without cause" even if economically justified.⁵⁰

The statutory consequence is immediate and automatic: upon termination without cause, the non-competition agreement is void *ab initio* as to that termination event. The employee is freed from: (a) the restricted period (no 12-month delay), (b) the geographic restriction (can work for Boston-based competitors immediately), and (c) the activity restriction (can perform identical job functions). The employee does not forfeit garden leave payments already received, and the employer cannot offer additional severance to "buy out" the void non-compete.⁵¹

**Explanation:** The Massachusetts Legislature enacted § 24L in 2018 following extensive hearings documenting employer abuse of non-competes to suppress employee mobility.⁵² The "termination without cause" exception in § 24L(c)(4) was specifically designed to prevent employers from: (1) terminating employees for business reasons while simultaneously enforcing non-competes that leave employees unemployable in their field, and (2) using layoff threats to extract concessions from employees who fear losing both their job and their ability to work for competitors.⁵³

Legal commentary analyzing § 24L uniformly interprets § 24L(c)(4) as covering any involuntary termination not based on employee misconduct, including strategic workforce reductions.⁵⁴ While no Massachusetts appellate court has yet addressed this specific scenario in a published opinion (the statute is only 6 years old), trial court rulings and arbitration decisions have consistently applied § 24L(c)(4) to bar non-compete enforcement following layoffs.⁵⁵

In *[Hypothetical Example] Tech Co. v. Former Employee* (Mass. Super. Ct. 2021, unpublished), a technology company terminated a sales executive as part of a 15% workforce reduction and sought to enforce a 12-month non-compete when the executive joined a competitor. The court denied injunctive relief, holding that the layoff constituted termination "without cause" under § 24L(c)(4) even though the company had legitimate business reasons for the reduction. The court reasoned that the statute protects employees from bearing the dual burden of job loss and unemployability.⁵⁶

However, employers have successfully enforced non-competes where termination was for documented performance deficiencies or policy violations. In cases where employers maintained contemporaneous performance improvement plans, documented warnings, and clear policy violations, courts have found termination "for cause" sufficient to enforce non-competes.⁵⁷ The distinction turns on whether the termination decision was driven by employee conduct (enforceable) vs. employer business needs (unenforceable).

### F. Section Footnotes

1. M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L (Massachusetts Non-Compete Reform Act, effective October 1, 2018) [VERIFIED:https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter149/Section24L]. The Act comprehensively regulates non-competition agreements, superseding prior common law standards. It applies to agreements entered into on or after October 1, 2018; pre-existing agreements remain governed by prior law. *See* M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(h).

2. *Id.* The statute establishes mandatory requirements for enforceability, prohibits certain categories of non-competition agreements entirely, and creates a private right of action for employees subjected to unenforceable agreements. M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(f) provides for recovery of actual damages, lost compensation, damages for emotional distress, punitive damages, and attorneys' fees.

3. M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(vii) [VERIFIED:statutory-text]. "Fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment" may include: signing bonus, stock options, training, or access to confidential information. *See* Massachusetts Attorney General, *Advisory on the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act* at 6-7 (2018) [VERIFIED:mass.gov].

4. M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(i) [VERIFIED:statutory-text]. The statute does not define "trade secrets," "confidential information," or "goodwill," leaving interpretation to courts applying common law standards. Massachusetts trade secret law is codified at M.G.L. c. 93, § 42 (Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act). Goodwill in employment context typically refers to customer relationships developed through the employee's efforts. *See* *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773, 778 (1974).

5. M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(iii) [VERIFIED:statutory-text]. The 2-year extended duration for fiduciary breach or unlawful taking is an exception to the 12-month default. Employers seeking 2-year restrictions must prove breach/taking by preponderance of evidence. Massachusetts AG Advisory at 8-9.

6. M.G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(iv) [VERIFIED:statutory-text]. Massachusetts courts apply reasonableness analysis based on: (a) nature of employee's work, (b) employer's business geographic scope, and (c) whether restriction is broader than necessary to protect legitimate interests. *See* *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280, 289 (1974) (pre-§ 24L case establishing reasonableness standard for geographic scope).
