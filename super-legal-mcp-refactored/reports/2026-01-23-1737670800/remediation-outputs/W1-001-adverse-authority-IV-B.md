## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Purpose and Scope

This research memorandum identifies and analyzes adverse authority relevant to Section IV.B of the Project Argos legal memorandum, which addresses Pinnacle mutual fund board independence under Investment Company Act Section 15(f). The current memorandum concludes that Pinnacle's board composition of 9 independent directors out of 12 total (exactly 75.0%) satisfies the statutory safe harbor threshold. This counter-analysis research identifies case law, SEC enforcement actions, regulatory guidance, and industry standards that challenge or limit reliance on minimal 75% compliance.

### Research Findings: Five Categories of Adverse Authority

#### 1. Judicial Precedent: Courts Recognize Material Costs and Regulatory Uncertainty

**Chamber of Commerce v. SEC, 412 F.3d 133 (D.C. Cir. 2005)**: The D.C. Circuit vacated SEC rules requiring mutual funds to maintain 75% independent boards, finding the agency failed to adequately consider compliance costs and alternatives. While the court did not invalidate the 75% threshold substantively, it emphasized that:
- Maintaining 75% independence imposes "material costs" on funds, including director recruitment and turnover management
- The SEC must justify why 75% (versus alternatives) best serves investor protection
- Courts recognize operational challenges inherent in sustaining super-majority independence

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: Pinnacle's exactly-75% composition (9 of 12) provides zero cushion for the "material costs" and "turnover management" challenges identified by the D.C. Circuit. Any single director departure during the Section 15(f) three-year period would breach safe harbor, exposing the transaction to "unfair burden" scrutiny.

**Verkouteren v. BlackRock, 37 F. Supp. 2d 256 (S.D.N.Y. 1999)**: The Southern District of New York dismissed a shareholder challenge to director independence but held that such disputes are "a matter for Congress or the SEC," not courts applying neutral standards.

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: This holding grants the SEC broad enforcement discretion to second-guess formal independence classifications. The agency can review director questionnaires post-transaction and determine that relationships deemed "immaterial" by the fund actually create interested-person status. With zero cushion, any SEC reclassification would immediately breach safe harbor.

---

#### 2. SEC Enforcement: Independent Directors Face Individual Liability

**Northern Lights Enforcement Action (2013)**: The SEC charged five trustees—including **four independent directors**—with violations related to misleading advisory contract disclosures and inadequate compliance oversight. The agency imposed $50,000 penalties and required engagement of independent compliance consultants. The SEC's Deputy Chief specifically noted that "*turnkey mutual fund arrangements can pose significant governance concerns*."

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: This enforcement action demonstrates:
- Having 75% independent directors provides **no immunity** from SEC enforcement for governance failures
- The SEC characterizes adviser change-of-control transactions (like Project Argos) as "turnkey" arrangements requiring heightened scrutiny
- Independent directors must **actively** exercise oversight during the Section 15(f) three-year period; nominal independence is insufficient
- Governance failures during post-transaction integration trigger both monetary penalties and operational burdens (compliance consultants)

With Pinnacle's zero-cushion board, any director departure due to governance disputes or enforcement inquiries could simultaneously (a) breach safe harbor and (b) trigger further SEC scrutiny of remaining directors' oversight capabilities.

---

#### 3. SEC Regulatory Policy: 75% Designed as Sustainable Super-Majority, Not Razor-Thin Margin

**Investment Company Governance Release, IC Release No. 26520 (2004)**: The SEC articulated that the 75% independence threshold should:
- "*Strengthen the hand* of the independent directors when dealing with fund management"
- "*Assure* that independent directors *maintain control* of the board and its agenda" (emphasis added)
- Resolve "*ongoing* conflicts of interest, which can result from the sale of an advisory firm"

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: The SEC's use of "strengthen," "assure," and "maintain control" suggests 75% was intended as a **comfortable super-majority** providing structural resilience through director turnover, not a razor-thin margin. The reference to "**ongoing** conflicts" (emphasis added) indicates that 75% independence should persist throughout the three-year post-transaction period, not just at closing.

Pinnacle's 9-of-12 board (exactly 75.0%) cannot absorb even a single director departure without breaching safe harbor. This contradicts the SEC's stated policy goal of ensuring independent directors "**maintain** control" during the post-transaction period when conflicts are most acute.

The SEC also noted that "independent directors hold an overwhelming majority (75 percent) of board seats in **nearly 90 percent** of fund complexes" even before mandatory rules, indicating that 75% represents an **industry floor**, not a governance ceiling.

---

#### 4. Industry Best Practices: 75% is Baseline, Not Target

**ICI Advisory Group Report (1999)**: Recommended at least two-thirds (67%) independent directors as best practice, predating Section 15(f)'s statutory 75% threshold.

**MFDF Report (2006)**: Elevated recommendation to 75% following SEC rulemaking.

**MFDF Practical Guidance on Adviser M&A (2007)**: Warned that "*an adviser can face challenges or even fail after a reorganization because of incompatibility of cultures, software integration problems, diseconomies of scale, and the defection of key personnel, all of which could adversely impact the services provided to a fund.*"

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: Industry guidance demonstrates:
- The evolution from 67% (1999) to 75% (2006) shows a **trend toward exceeding regulatory minimums**
- 75% independence exists "in nearly 90 percent of fund complexes," indicating this percentage is **entry-level standard**, not aspirational target
- Funds undergoing adviser M&A face **elevated governance risks** (cultural incompatibility, personnel defection, service disruptions) requiring **board stability**
- Best practices suggest maintaining **cushion above 75%** to absorb M&A-related director turnover during the critical three-year Section 15(f) period

Pinnacle's exactly-75% composition places it at the **floor** of current industry practice for mature fund complexes, and **below** best practices for funds undergoing ownership transitions.

---

#### 5. Statutory Risk: Expansive "Interested Person" Definition Creates Multiple Reclassification Pathways

**Section 2(a)(19) of the Investment Company Act**: Defines "interested person" to include:
- **Immediate family members** (parents, spouses, children, siblings, step-relations) of affiliated persons
- Persons with **business relationships** during **two-year lookback period**
- Persons executing **portfolio transactions** during **six-month lookback period**
- Persons with "**material business or professional relationship**" with fund or CEO (SEC discretionary determination)

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: Due diligence must verify that all 9 of Pinnacle's purportedly independent directors satisfy these negative covenants. Common reclassification scenarios include:
- Independent director's child works for acquiring adviser or fund complex administrator
- Independent director provided consulting services to fund complex within past two fiscal years
- Independent director's affiliated brokerage firm executed trades for fund within past six months
- SEC retroactively determines that director's relationship with fund's CEO was "material," even if not initially disclosed

With Pinnacle's board at exactly 75% (9 of 12), reclassification of **even a single director** as "interested" would:
1. Drop independence to 66.7% (8 of 12), breaching Section 15(f) safe harbor
2. Disqualify fund from safe harbor protection for remainder of three-year post-closing period
3. Expose transaction to "unfair burden" scrutiny under Section 15(f)(2)(A)
4. Potentially require shareholder vote on advisory contract under Section 15(a)

### A. Cases Addressing Minimal Compliance with Section 15(f) Board Independence

#### 1. Chamber of Commerce v. SEC, 412 F.3d 133 (D.C. Cir. 2005)

**Citation**: Chamber of Commerce of the United States of America v. Securities and Exchange Commission, 412 F.3d 133 (D.C. Cir. 2005) [VERIFIED: CourtListener https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/186464/chamber-cmerc-usa-v-sec/]

**Holding**: The D.C. Circuit vacated SEC rules requiring mutual funds to have boards with no less than 75% independent directors and an independent chairman, finding that the SEC violated the Administrative Procedure Act by failing adequately to consider the costs mutual funds would incur to comply with these conditions and by failing adequately to consider proposed alternatives.

**Procedural Posture**: Petition for review of SEC rulemaking. The court granted in part the Chamber's petition for review, partially vacating the SEC's Investment Company Governance rules adopted in 2004.

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: This case demonstrates that even the SEC's attempt to mandate 75% independence as a regulatory floor (beyond Section 15(f)'s three-year safe harbor context) was found procedurally deficient. While the court did not invalidate the 75% threshold on substantive grounds, the decision shows that:

1. **Cost Concerns**: The court emphasized that achieving and maintaining 75% independence imposes material compliance costs on funds, requiring analysis of director turnover risks and replacement challenges.

2. **Regulatory Uncertainty**: After this decision, the 75% requirement exists as a Section 15(f) safe harbor requirement and as a condition for certain exemptive rule relief, but not as a universal governance mandate. This creates ambiguity about whether 75% should be viewed as an absolute floor or a context-specific threshold.

3. **Alternative Standards**: The court's requirement that SEC consider "proposed alternatives" suggests that different independence thresholds (potentially higher) may be appropriate depending on fund circumstances.

**Relevance to Project Argos Section IV.B**: The memorandum's conclusion that Pinnacle's 9-of-12 board (exactly 75.0%) provides "technical compliance" should acknowledge this case's holding that:
   - The 75% threshold was designed for Section 15(f) safe harbor purposes (post-adviser-sale scenarios)
   - Courts have recognized material costs associated with maintaining this threshold
   - The D.C. Circuit's scrutiny of the 75% requirement suggests regulatory skepticism about whether this percentage, standing alone, ensures adequate independence

**Distinguishing Factors for Pinnacle**:
- Pinnacle is relying on Section 15(f) safe harbor (where 75% is statutory), not exemptive rule relief
- The Project Argos transaction involves adviser change-of-control, precisely the scenario Section 15(f) was designed to address
- However, Pinnacle's zero-cushion board composition means any director departure or reclassification during the three-year safe harbor period would drop the fund below 75%, triggering safe harbor loss

**Integration Point in Section IV.B**: Insert after paragraph discussing "75% threshold met" to add:

> "However, the D.C. Circuit's decision in *Chamber of Commerce v. SEC*, 412 F.3d 133 (D.C. Cir. 2005), partially vacating the SEC's 75% independence mandate, underscores regulatory and judicial recognition that maintaining this threshold imposes material compliance burdens. The court's emphasis on costs associated with director turnover and replacement suggests that boards operating at exactly 75%—with zero cushion for unexpected departures or reclassifications—face heightened operational risk during the three-year Section 15(f) safe harbor period."

#### 2. Verkouteren v. BlackRock Financial Management, 37 F. Supp. 2d 256 (S.D.N.Y. 1999)

**Citation**: Verkouteren v. BlackRock Financial Management, Inc., 37 F. Supp. 2d 256 (S.D.N.Y. 1999) [VERIFIED: Justia https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/FSupp2/37/256/2415732/]

**Holding**: The Southern District of New York dismissed a shareholder derivative action alleging that BlackRock mutual fund directors were insufficiently independent, holding that without evidence of actual control by the adviser over the directors, allegations of inadequate independence are "a matter for Congress or the SEC" and courts must "apply the laws as written."

**Procedural Posture**: Motion to dismiss shareholder derivative action challenging director independence. Court granted defendant's motion to dismiss.

**Relevance to Pinnacle**: While this case superficially supports the position that technical compliance with statutory independence requirements is sufficient, it actually presents **adverse implications** for the Project Argos analysis:

1. **Deference to SEC Enforcement**: The court explicitly stated that director independence challenges are matters for "Congress or the SEC," not courts applying neutral standards. This means:
   - SEC enforcement discretion is the primary check on minimal compliance
   - SEC could challenge Pinnacle's board composition even if it technically meets 75% threshold
   - Post-transaction scrutiny may focus on substance over form

2. **"Control" Standard**: The court required evidence that adviser "controlled" directors to find independence violation. This suggests factors that could undermine Pinnacle's independence claims:
   - Do any of Pinnacle's 9 "independent" directors have historical business relationships with selling adviser?
   - Are compensation arrangements for independent directors influenced by adviser?
   - Do independent directors have family members employed by adviser or acquirer?

3. **Plaintiff's Failure**: The case was dismissed because plaintiff failed to offer evidence of control, not because 75% compliance is per se adequate. In due diligence context, Buyer could identify control relationships that plaintiff missed.

**Counter-Authority Nature**: This case is "adverse" to Pinnacle's position because:
- It suggests that formal independence classifications may not reflect substantive independence
- It places burden on challenging party (here, Buyer in due diligence) to investigate actual director relationships
- It signals that courts will defer to SEC if substance-over-form issues arise post-transaction

**Relevance to Project Argos Section IV.B**: The memorandum should note that while Section 15(f) uses a mechanical 75% threshold, case law demonstrates that:
- "Interested person" determinations under Section 2(a)(19) require factual investigation beyond board representations
- Historical business relationships, family connections, and compensation arrangements can create "interested person" status even for nominally independent directors
- With zero cushion (9 of 12), discovery of even one misclassified director would breach safe harbor

**Distinguishing Factors for Pinnacle**:
- *Verkouteren* involved post-facto shareholder challenge to existing board, not pre-transaction due diligence
- Plaintiff in *Verkouteren* had burden to prove control; in Project Argos, Buyer can condition closing on verified independence
- Transaction context triggers heightened Section 15(f) scrutiny not present in *Verkouteren* governance dispute

**Integration Point in Section IV.B**: Insert in "Risk Analysis" subsection:

> "Case law demonstrates that technical compliance with 75% independence may be insufficient if substantive 'control' relationships exist between directors and adviser. In *Verkouteren v. BlackRock Fin. Mgmt.*, 37 F. Supp. 2d 256 (S.D.N.Y. 1999), the court held that director independence challenges are 'a matter for Congress or the SEC,' indicating that regulatory enforcement—not judicial review—polices minimal compliance. With Pinnacle's board at exactly 75%, any post-closing determination that one director was misclassified as independent (due to business relationships, family connections, or compensation arrangements not disclosed in due diligence) would drop the fund below the Section 15(f) safe harbor threshold for the remainder of the three-year period."

### C. SEC Guidance on Board Independence Cushions and Best Practices

#### 1. SEC Investment Company Governance Release (2004)

**Citation**: Investment Company Governance, Investment Company Act Release No. IC-26520, 69 Fed. Reg. 46378 (Aug. 2, 2004) (codified at 17 C.F.R. pt. 270) [VERIFIED: https://www.sec.gov/rules-regulations/2005/06/investment-company-governance]

**SEC Rationale for 75% Threshold**: The SEC's 2004 rulemaking (subsequently partially vacated by *Chamber of Commerce v. SEC*) articulated the regulatory rationale for requiring 75% independent directors:

1. **"Strengthen the Hand of Independent Directors"**: The SEC stated that "*a fund board whose independent directors constitute at least 75 percent of the fund board should strengthen the hand of the independent directors when dealing with fund management, and may assure that independent directors maintain control of the board and its agenda.*"

2. **Management Dominance Concerns**: The SEC expressed concern that "*many boards continue to be dominated by their management companies*" despite previous majority-independence requirements, necessitating super-majority threshold.

#### 1. Statutory Definition Under Section 2(a)(19) – Family Member Provisions

> "any parent, spouse of a parent, child, spouse of a child, spouse, brother, or sister, and includes step and adoptive relationships"

This creates retroactive classification risk:
- If any of Pinnacle's 9 "independent" directors had business relationships with the fund, adviser, or affiliated persons during the six months before closing, they could be reclassified
- Even unwitting violations (e.g., director's family member providing services to fund complex) could trigger interested-person status
- Due diligence must verify that all 9 directors satisfy negative covenants for full six-month lookback period

**B. Immediate Family Scope**: The definition includes parents, siblings, spouses, children, and their spouses, plus step/adoptive relations. This creates exposure:

- Family employment relationships may not be obvious from board questionnaires
