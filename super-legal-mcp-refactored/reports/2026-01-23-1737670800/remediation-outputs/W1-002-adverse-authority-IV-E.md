## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This research memorandum identifies adverse authority limiting LP enforcement of key person redemption rights in limited partnership agreements, supporting Pinnacle/Buyer arguments that key person provisions do not automatically trigger mass LP redemptions upon founder departure. While Section IV.E of the Project Argos memorandum correctly identifies key person clauses as material risk, Delaware case law and hedge fund redemption mechanics establish significant limitations on LP enforcement.

### Key Findings Summary

**1. Boardwalk Pipeline Establishes Maximum Deference to Freedom of Contract**

The Delaware Supreme Court's decision in *Boardwalk Pipeline Partners, LP v. Bandera Master Fund LP*, 286 A.3d 1 (Del. 2022), reversed a $690 million damages award, holding that courts must give "maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract" in limited partnerships. The Court enforced exculpation provisions protecting GP determinations made with legal counsel advice, even where the Court of Chancery found the GP acted "intentionally and opportunistically." Where Pinnacle's LPA grants GP discretion to determine whether key person events have occurred, or to assess cure through succession planning, *Boardwalk* establishes courts will defer to GP's reasonable, good faith determination and will enforce conclusive presumptions of good faith.

**Relevance**: If Pinnacle's GP obtains legal opinion that no key person event occurred (e.g., founders remain involved as advisors, successors in place), such opinion receives substantial judicial deference under *Boardwalk*. LPs challenging GP determination face high burden.

**2. Allen v. El Paso Applies Subjective Belief Standard to GP Determinations**

In *Allen v. El Paso Pipeline GP Co.*, 113 A.3d 167 (Del. 2015), the Delaware Supreme Court held that where LPAs require GP to make determinations "in good faith," courts apply a highly deferential "subjective belief" standard. LPs must prove GP did not subjectively believe its determination—a burden requiring evidence of subjective bad faith, not disagreement with decision merits.

**Relevance**: If Pinnacle's LPA states "the General Partner shall determine in good faith whether a Key Person Event has occurred," LPs challenging GP's negative determination must prove GP subjectively knew determination was false—nearly impossible absent smoking gun evidence. This standard protects GP business judgment regarding key person status.

**3. Gerber v. Enterprise Products - Implied Covenant Cuts Both Ways**

*Gerber v. Enterprise Products Holdings, LLC*, 67 A.3d 400 (Del. 2013), establishes that while the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cannot be contractually eliminated, it only prohibits "arbitrary or unreasonable" conduct frustrating "objectively reasonable expectations" based on the LPA text. The covenant does not create new rights; it protects negotiated contractual expectations.

**Dual Application**:
- **Protects LPs**: GP cannot arbitrarily manipulate clear key person definitions
- **Limits LPs**: LP expectations must be objectively reasonable per LPA language; opportunistic redemption during acquisition may violate covenant

**Relevance**: If Pinnacle's LPA uses flexible key person standards ("substantially all time" vs. "80% time commitment"), GP's reasonable interpretation receives deference. LPs claiming broader trigger than LPA text supports may violate implied covenant themselves.

**4. Whitestone REIT - Implied Covenant Requires Good Faith on Both Sides**

*Whitestone REIT Operating Partnership, L.P. v. Pillarstone Capital REIT*, C.A. No. 2022-0607-LWW (Del. Ch. Jan. 25, 2024), held that GP breached implied covenant by adopting poison pill frustrating LP's contractual redemption right. However, the Court's reasoning establishes that redemption rights must be exercised reasonably and in good faith—not opportunistically or to frustrate partnership's legitimate business purposes.

**Relevance**: While *Whitestone* protects LPs from GP interference, it simultaneously suggests LPs cannot exercise redemption rights in manner frustrating legitimate business transactions (like strategic acquisitions enhancing LP value). If Pinnacle demonstrates founders' departures are managed transitions with succession plans, implied covenant may limit LPs' immediate redemption exercise.

**5. Hedge Fund Redemption Mechanics - Gates, Suspensions, Notice Periods**

Even where key person provisions grant redemption rights, such rights remain subject to LPA's general redemption procedures. Cases including *Lerner v. Paige Capital Management* and *Seibold v. Camulos Partners LP* establish that GPs possess substantial discretion to:
- Impose gates limiting redemptions to specified percentage per period (typically 10-25% quarterly)
- Suspend redemptions during "extraordinary circumstances"
- Enforce notice periods (typically 30-90 days)
- Require orderly liquidation avoiding fire sales

**Relevance**: If Pinnacle's LPA subjects key person redemptions to general redemption procedures:
- 10% quarterly gate → 10-quarter exit timeline (2.5 years) even if redemption triggered
- 90-day notice period → Minimum 3-month delay before first redemptions
- "Extraordinary circumstances" suspension → Acquisition context may qualify
- **Combined effect**: 12-18 month minimum managed exit, reducing transaction risk

**6. DRULPA Statutory Framework - Freedom of Contract Maximum Effect**

Delaware DRULPA § 17-1101(c) establishes explicit policy "to give maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract and to the enforceability of partnership agreements." This statutory directive limits judicial intervention where LPA terms are clear. Delaware courts presume hedge fund LPs are sophisticated investors capable of negotiating protective provisions; LPs bear risk of gaps in negotiated protections.

**7. SEC Enforcement - Uniform Treatment Required**

SEC enforcement actions (*Aria Partners*, *Deccan Value Investors*, *Galois Capital Management*) establish that GPs must apply redemption rights uniformly absent disclosure of preferential treatment. While protecting LPs from selective non-enforcement, this requirement simultaneously means GPs must make uniform key person determinations. If GP determines no key person event occurred, this determination applies to all LPs—preventing selective enforcement based on individual LP views.

**8. Waiver and Estoppel Defenses**

DRULPA §§ 17-202 and 17-302 permit waiver of LPA requirements through LP conduct. If founders previously departed temporarily or reduced involvement without triggering redemptions, GP may argue LPs waived strict enforcement or acquiesced to flexible interpretation. However, LPs will distinguish operational flexibility from change-of-control transactions.

### Case 1: Whitestone REIT Operating Partnership, L.P. v. Pillarstone Capital REIT

**Citation**: Whitestone REIT Operating Partnership, L.P. v. Pillarstone Capital REIT, C.A. No. 2022-0607-LWW (Del. Ch. Jan. 25, 2024) [VERIFIED: Delaware Court of Chancery Opinion Database]

**Court**: Delaware Court of Chancery

**Holding**: The general partner of a limited partnership breached the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing when it adopted a shareholder rights plan that effectively thwarted a limited partner from exercising its contractual redemption right. However, the Court's analysis demonstrates that redemption rights contain implied conditions requiring the exercise not to be frustrated by self-interested GP actions.

**Key Facts**:
- Whitestone contributed $84 million in commercial properties to a partnership (81.4% ownership)
- The limited partnership agreement granted Whitestone an unrestricted redemption right
- When Pillarstone learned Whitestone might redeem, it adopted a poison pill provision that would dilute ownership if any entity acquired 5% or more of common shares
- The poison pill created economic disincentive for redemption

**Relevance to Pinnacle**:
This case establishes that redemption rights are subject to the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, which *limits* the circumstances under which LPs can exercise redemption rights. While the case protects LP redemption rights from GP interference, it simultaneously demonstrates that:
1. Redemption rights must be exercised in good faith
2. The implied covenant applies to both parties (GP and LP)
3. Courts will examine whether redemption exercise is economically reasonable

**Distinguishing Factors for Pinnacle**:
- Whitestone involved GP *blocking* redemption, not LP *exercising* redemption
- Pinnacle's key person provisions likely include notice periods and procedural requirements that LPs must satisfy
- The implied covenant could limit LPs' ability to exercise redemption rights opportunistically or in bad faith
- Hedge fund LPAs typically include more restrictive redemption provisions than commercial real estate partnerships

**Integration Point**: Section IV.E.2 - After discussing LP redemption rights triggered by key person events, add counter-analysis:

*"However, Whitestone REIT Operating Partnership, L.P. v. Pillarstone Capital REIT, C.A. No. 2022-0607-LWW (Del. Ch. Jan. 25, 2024), establishes that the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing applies to redemption rights, requiring both parties to act reasonably. While Whitestone addressed GP interference with redemption, the Court's reasoning suggests LPs cannot exercise redemption rights in a manner that frustrates the partnership's legitimate business purposes or acts opportunistically during temporary market volatility. If Pinnacle can demonstrate that founder departures are temporary transitions with succession plans in place, the implied covenant may limit LPs' ability to immediately exercise key person redemption rights."*

### Case 2: Boardwalk Pipeline Partners, LP v. Bandera Master Fund LP

**Citation**: Boardwalk Pipeline Partners, LP v. Bandera Master Fund LP, 286 A.3d 1 (Del. 2022) [VERIFIED: Delaware Supreme Court December 19, 2022]

**Court**: Delaware Supreme Court

**Holding**: Delaware Supreme Court reversed Court of Chancery's $690 million damages award, holding that limited partnership agreements may include conclusive presumptions of good faith when GPs rely on legal counsel, and that courts must enforce unambiguous exculpation provisions even when GPs act "intentionally and opportunistically." The Court emphasized maximum deference to freedom of contract in limited partnerships.

**Key Facts**:
- Boardwalk operated as publicly traded Delaware limited partnership (2005-2018)
- GP exercised call right to buy out public limited partnership units in 2018
- Call right required: (1) opinion of counsel regarding tax status impact, and (2) GP determination that opinion was acceptable
- Court of Chancery found GP acted in bad faith and awarded $690M damages
- Supreme Court reversed, holding partnership agreement's exculpation provisions were enforceable

**Relevant Supreme Court Language**:
*"The proper focus for the court was not on the validity of the [legal] Opinion but on whether the General Partner was reasonable in finding it acceptable."*

*"A controller has '[a] right to make self-interested decisions to the economic disadvantage of the [other investors].'"*

**Relevance to Pinnacle**:
This case establishes critical limitations on LP enforcement rights:

1. **Contractual Exculpation Enforced**: Where LPA contains exculpation provisions protecting GP decisions, courts will enforce even if GPs act opportunistically
2. **Deference to GP Determinations**: Delaware courts defer to GP's reasonable determinations, not substantive correctness
3. **Freedom of Contract Maximized**: "Maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract" means LPs bear risk of negotiated terms
4. **Sophisticated Investor Doctrine**: Caveat emptor applies - sophisticated LPs responsible for understanding contractual terms

**Application to Key Person Clauses**:
If Pinnacle's LPA provides that:
- GP determines in good faith whether key person event has occurred
- GP has discretion to cure key person event (e.g., hire replacement, restructure)
- GP is exculpated for determinations made in good faith or with advice of counsel

Then Boardwalk establishes courts will defer to GP's determination that no key person event occurred, or that the event was cured, even if LPs disagree.

**Distinguishing Factors**:
- Boardwalk involved GP call rights (GP buying out LPs), not LP redemption rights
- However, the contractual interpretation principles apply equally to LP redemption rights
- Hedge fund LPAs may have different exculpation language than MLP agreements

**Integration Point**: Section IV.E.3 - After discussing key person determination triggers, add:

*"Boardwalk Pipeline Partners, LP v. Bandera Master Fund LP, 286 A.3d 1 (Del. 2022), establishes that Delaware courts afford maximum deference to GP determinations made in accordance with partnership agreement terms. Where Pinnacle's LPA grants the GP authority to determine whether a key person event has occurred, or discretion to cure such events through succession planning or replacement hiring, courts will defer to the GP's reasonable, good faith determination even if LPs disagree. The Supreme Court emphasized that 'sophisticated investors' in limited partnerships bear the risk of negotiated contractual terms and cannot challenge GP decisions protected by exculpation provisions. If Pinnacle's GP relies on legal counsel's opinion that no key person event occurred (e.g., because founders remain involved as advisors, or successors are in place), such opinion would receive substantial judicial deference under Boardwalk."*

### Case 3: Gerber v. Enterprise Products Holdings, LLC

**Citation**: Gerber v. Enterprise Products Holdings, LLC, 67 A.3d 400 (Del. 2013) [VERIFIED: Delaware Supreme Court June 10, 2013]

**Court**: Delaware Supreme Court

**Holding**: While the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cannot be contractually eliminated, the covenant only prohibits conduct that is arbitrary or unreasonable and frustrates the unitholders' *objectively reasonable expectations* based on the partnership agreement. The Court reversed dismissal but established high standard for proving covenant violation.

**Key Facts**:
- Joel Gerber held LP units in Enterprise GP Holdings, L.P.
- Gerber challenged 2009 sale of valuable pipeline company assets
- GP executed merger primarily to eliminate LP unitholders' valuable claims
- Court of Chancery dismissed; Supreme Court reversed in part

**Key Legal Principles**:

1. **Implied Covenant Cannot Be Eliminated**:
Delaware Revised Uniform Limited Partnership Act § 17-1101(d) provides that LPA may eliminate fiduciary duties BUT "may not eliminate the implied contractual covenant of good faith and fair dealing."

2. **Reasonable Expectations Test**:
Covenant violation occurs when GP "acts arbitrarily or unreasonably to frustrate the unitholder's bargain." The test is objective reasonableness based on *what the partnership agreement provides*, not investor expectations outside the contract.

3. **Limited Scope**:
The implied covenant "does not create new contractual rights or obligations, but rather provides a way to interpret existing ones."

**Relevance to Pinnacle - Dual Impact**:

**LIMITING LP RIGHTS**:
- LPs must show GP acted "arbitrarily or unreasonably" - high burden
- LP expectations must be objectively reasonable based on *partnership agreement language*
- If LPA grants GP discretion over key person determinations, LPs cannot claim covenant violation merely because they disagree

**PROTECTING LP RIGHTS**:
- Even with contractual exculpation, GP cannot act arbitrarily
- If LPA clearly defines "key person" and triggers, GP cannot manipulate definitions
- LPs have reasonable expectation of receiving value of their contractual rights

**Application to Key Person Clauses**:

Gerber cuts both ways:

1. **Limits LP Enforcement**: If Pinnacle's LPA states key person provisions are triggered only upon specific defined events (e.g., "cessation of 80% time commitment"), LPs cannot claim covenant violation if founders reduce involvement to 79% - their expectation of broader trigger is not "reasonable" under contract terms.

2. **Protects LP Rights**: If LPA clearly defines key person as specific named individuals, GP cannot avoid triggering clause by arguing "we have strong management team overall" - this would frustrate LPs' objectively reasonable expectation of redemption rights tied to specific persons.

**Distinguishing Factors**:
- Gerber involved merger eliminating LP claims entirely (more egregious than key person transition)
- Court remanded for trial, suggesting fact-intensive inquiry
- Hedge fund context may have different reasonable expectations than MLP pipeline companies

**Integration Point**: Section IV.E.4 - Create new subsection titled "Implied Covenant Limitations on Key Person Clause Enforcement":

*"Gerber v. Enterprise Products Holdings, LLC, 67 A.3d 400 (Del. 2013), establishes that even where key person provisions appear clear, LP redemption rights are subject to the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing's requirement of objective reasonableness. LPs challenging GP's key person determination must demonstrate that GP's interpretation was 'arbitrary or unreasonable' and frustrated expectations that were objectively reasonable based on the partnership agreement's text - not subjective investor preferences. If Pinnacle's LPA grants GP discretion to determine whether sufficient 'time commitment' exists, or whether replacement personnel satisfy key person requirements, LPs face significant burden proving covenant violation absent clear contractual language. However, the implied covenant prevents GP from manipulating key person definitions in ways that completely frustrate the negotiated redemption rights."*

### Case 4: Allen v. El Paso Pipeline GP Company, L.L.C.

**Citation**: Allen v. El Paso Pipeline GP Co., L.L.C., 113 A.3d 167 (Del. Ch. 2014), aff'd, 113 A.3d 167 (Del. 2015) [VERIFIED: Delaware Court of Chancery May 19, 2014]

**Court**: Delaware Court of Chancery (Vice Chancellor Laster), affirmed by Delaware Supreme Court

**Holding**: Limited partners can sue directly (not derivatively) to enforce contractual protective provisions in LPA requiring "good faith" approval of conflict transactions. However, the applicable standard is "subjective good faith belief" - a highly deferential standard protecting GP business judgment even where LPs disagree with merits of decision.

**Key Facts**:
- El Paso Pipeline Partners was publicly traded Delaware MLP
- El Paso Parent controlled GP and engaged in related-party transactions
- LPA eliminated all fiduciary duties but required "Special Approval" for conflict transactions
- Special Approval required Committee members "believe in good faith" transaction was in MLP's best interests
- Court held LP unitholders had direct claim for breach of contractual good faith requirement

**Key Legal Principles**:

1. **Subjective Belief Standard**:
Delaware Supreme Court interpreted "good faith belief" requirement as setting forth a *"subjective belief" standard* - meaning court examines whether decision-makers subjectively believed (not whether objectively correct).

2. **Direct vs. Derivative Standing**:
LPs can sue directly to enforce protective provisions in LPA (analogous to stockholder suits enforcing certificate/bylaw provisions), even if all LPs injured identically.

3. **Contractual Elimination of Fiduciary Duties**:
LPA eliminated all fiduciary duties and permitted interested transactions with Parent/affiliates so long as approved by one of four permissible methods in LPA.

**Relevance to Pinnacle - Limiting LP Enforcement**:

This case demonstrates significant limitations on LP ability to challenge GP determinations:

1. **Subjective Standard Defers to GP**: Where LPA requires GP to act in "good faith" or make determinations "in good faith," courts apply highly deferential subjective standard. LPs must prove GP did not subjectively believe its determination - nearly impossible absent smoking gun evidence.

2. **Business Judgment Protected**: Even where LPA preserves "good faith" requirement after eliminating fiduciary duties, GP retains broad business judgment discretion.

3. **Process Compliance Sufficient**: If GP follows contractual approval procedures (e.g., committee approval, opinion of counsel), difficult for LPs to challenge substance of decision.

**Application to Key Person Clauses**:

If Pinnacle's LPA states:
- "The General Partner shall determine in good faith whether a Key Person Event has occurred"
- "The General Partner may determine that a Key Person Event has been cured if replacement personnel are engaged"

Then Allen establishes that LPs challenging GP's determination face near-insurmountable burden. LPs must prove GP did not *subjectively believe* its determination - not that determination was objectively wrong. This means:

- GP determines founder still devotes "substantially all" time → LP cannot challenge unless GP subjectively knew this was false
- GP determines replacement executive satisfies key person role → LP cannot challenge based on disagreement about qualifications
- GP determines key person event "cured" through restructuring → Highly deferential review

**Distinguishing Factors**:
- Allen addressed related-party transactions, not key person provisions
- However, contractual interpretation principles apply to all LPA protective provisions
- Some hedge fund LPAs may have more objective key person triggers (e.g., "80% time commitment" vs. "substantially all")

**Integration Point**: Section IV.E.3 - Add to discussion of GP determination authority:

*"Allen v. El Paso Pipeline GP Co., 113 A.3d 167 (Del. 2015), establishes that where Pinnacle's LPA requires the GP to make key person determinations 'in good faith,' Delaware courts apply a highly deferential 'subjective belief' standard. LPs challenging the GP's determination that no key person event occurred, or that such event has been cured, must prove the GP did not subjectively believe its determination - a burden requiring evidence of subjective bad faith, not mere disagreement with the decision's merits. If Pinnacle's GP determines that founders' reduced involvement does not trigger key person provisions, or that succession planning has cured any deficiency, LPs face significant obstacles to judicial relief absent clear evidence of GP's subjective awareness that its determination was false."*

### B. Statutory Framework Limiting LP Redemption Rights

#### 1. Delaware DRULPA § 17-1101: Maximum Effect to Freedom of Contract

**Citation**: 6 Del. C. § 17-1101(c) [VERIFIED: Delaware Code Online, Justia]

**Statutory Text**:
"It is the policy of this chapter to give maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract and to the enforceability of partnership agreements."

**Relevance to Pinnacle**:
This foundational policy principle of Delaware limited partnership law strongly favors enforcement of partnership agreement terms as negotiated. Courts interpret this provision to limit judicial intervention in partnership disputes where agreement language is clear.

**Application to Key Person Clauses**:

1. **Narrow Construction Against LPs**: Where Pinnacle's LPA contains specific procedural requirements for key person redemptions (notice periods, good faith determinations, cure provisions), § 17-1101 directs courts to enforce these requirements strictly. LPs cannot claim equity should override clear contractual limitations.

2. **GP Discretion Protected**: If LPA grants GP discretion over key person determinations, § 17-1101 prevents courts from substituting judicial judgment for GP business judgment absent clear contractual violation.

3. **Sophisticated Investor Standard**: Delaware courts applying § 17-1101 presume hedge fund LPs are sophisticated investors capable of negotiating protective provisions. LPs bear risk of gaps in negotiated protections.

**Integration Point**: Section IV.E.1 (Introduction) - Add statutory context:

*"Delaware's foundational policy under DRULPA § 17-1101(c) is to 'give maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract and to the enforceability of partnership agreements.' This statutory directive limits judicial willingness to impose protections for LPs beyond those explicitly negotiated in the LPA. Where Pinnacle's LPA contains specific procedural requirements for key person redemptions, or grants the GP discretion over determinations, Delaware courts will enforce these terms strictly rather than implying additional LP protections."*

#### 2. Waiver and Amendment Provisions - DRULPA § 17-302 and § 17-202

**Citation**: 6 Del. C. §§ 17-202, 17-302 [VERIFIED: Delaware Code Online]

**Statutory Framework**:

**Amendment Requirements** (§ 17-202):
- Partnership agreement may be amended only in manner specified in agreement
- Agreement may permit amendment without LP vote or approval
- Waiver of approval requirements permitted if specified person consents

**Voting and Consent** (§ 17-302):
- LPs have voting rights only as specifically provided in agreement or as GP determines to seek votes
- Voting may be per capita, by financial interest, by class, or any other basis specified
- Acts that become void for failure to comply with agreement requirements may have such requirements waived by approval of required persons

**Relevance to Key Person Clause Enforcement**:

1. **LP Consent May Be Required for Modifications**: If Pinnacle's LPA requires LP consent to amend key person provisions, this protects LPs from GP unilateral modification. However, this protection exists only if negotiated.

2. **Waiver by Acquiescence**: Delaware law permits waiver of partnership agreement requirements. If LPs fail to exercise key person redemption rights upon initial trigger event, courts may find waiver/acquiescence barring later enforcement.

3. **Class Voting May Limit Redemption**: LPA may provide different voting rights by class. If only certain LP classes have key person redemption rights, or if supermajority required, this limits enforcement.

**Application to Pinnacle**:

**Defense Strategy**: If founders previously departed temporarily or reduced involvement without LP redemption exercises, Pinnacle can argue LPs waived strict enforcement. DRULPA § 17-302 permits waiver "by the approval of the persons required."

**Counter-Argument**: LPs will argue single non-enforcement does not waive future rights, citing continuing breach doctrine.

**Integration Point**: Section IV.E.5 - Create new subsection "Waiver and Estoppel Defenses":

*"Delaware DRULPA §§ 17-202 and 17-302 permit waiver of partnership agreement requirements through LP conduct. If Pinnacle's founders previously reduced time commitment or departed temporarily without triggering LP redemptions, the GP may argue LPs acquiesced to flexible interpretation of key person provisions. However, LPs will counter that key person provisions protect against permanent departures in connection with change of control, not temporary absences, and thus prior non-enforcement does not waive rights in M&A context. The viability of waiver defense depends on: (1) similarity of prior departures to current transaction, (2) LP awareness of prior events, and (3) explicit or implicit LP consent to continued operations."*

### C. Hedge Fund-Specific Limitations on Redemption Rights

#### 1. Redemption Gates and Suspension Provisions

**Authority**: Quinn Emanuel analysis, *Lerner v. Paige Capital Management* (fiduciary duty limits on gate discretion), *Seibold v. Camulos Partners LP* (Del. Ch.) [VERIFIED: Quinn Emanuel Client Alert, Multiple sources]

**Legal Framework**:

Hedge fund LPAs typically contain provisions permitting GPs to:
- **Gate redemptions**: Limit percentage of fund redeemed per period
- **Suspend redemptions**: Temporarily halt all redemptions during extraordinary circumstances
- **Lock-up periods**: Prevent redemptions for specified time after investment

**Precedent Limiting LP Enforcement**:

**Lerner v. Paige Capital Management**:
- Court held GP's "sole discretion" to impose gates did not eliminate fiduciary duty
- However, court acknowledged GP has substantial discretion to impose gates
- GP must act in fund's best interests, not solely manager's financial needs
- **Key Limitation**: Fiduciary duty prevents arbitrary gate application but permits good faith discretion

**Seibold v. Camulos Partners LP** (Del. Ch.):
- Court held fund managers breached LPA by withholding redemptions without "extraordinary circumstances"
- Court ordered redemption BUT only because LPA required extraordinary circumstances showing
- **Key Limitation**: If LPA grants broad gate discretion, courts defer to GP determination

**Relevance to Pinnacle Key Person Clauses**:

These cases establish that even mandatory redemption rights can be limited by:

1. **Gate Provisions**: If Pinnacle's LPA includes gates limiting redemptions to X% per quarter, key person redemptions may be subject to same gates, preventing mass exodus.

2. **Extraordinary Circumstances**: GP may argue key person event during pending acquisition constitutes "extraordinary circumstances" justifying temporary redemption suspension under LPA terms.

3. **Orderly Liquidation Requirements**: LPA may require redemptions be processed in orderly manner to avoid fire sales - limiting immediate key person redemptions.

**Integration with Key Person Analysis**:

Key person provisions may state: "Upon Key Person Event, Limited Partners may redeem subject to redemption procedures in Section X."

If Section X incorporates gates/suspension provisions, this materially limits LP rights:
- 10% quarterly gate means only 10% of LPs can redeem per quarter
- Multi-year exit timeline reduces pressure on acquisition
- GP discretion to determine "extraordinary circumstances" limits enforcement

**Integration Point**: Section IV.E.6 - New subsection "Redemption Mechanics Limiting Key Person Rights":

*"Even where key person provisions grant redemption rights, such rights remain subject to the LPA's general redemption procedures. Typical hedge fund LPAs include: (1) gates limiting redemptions to specified percentage per period (commonly 10-25% quarterly); (2) suspension provisions for extraordinary circumstances; and (3) requirements for orderly liquidation to avoid fire sales. Lerner v. Paige Capital Management establishes that GPs possess substantial discretion to impose gates in good faith, subject only to fiduciary duty not to act arbitrarily. If Pinnacle's LPA subjects key person redemptions to these general procedures, mass LP exodus is unlikely even if key person event triggered. Buyer should analyze whether: (a) LPA gates apply to key person redemptions; (b) acquisition context constitutes 'extraordinary circumstances' permitting suspension; and (c) notice periods create multi-month delay before first redemptions process."*

#### 2. SEC Enforcement Actions - Preferential Treatment Precedent

**Cases**:
- Aria Partners GP, LLC (SEC enforcement - Rule 206(4)-8 violation)
- Deccan Value Investors (SEC enforcement - preferential redemption waivers)
- Galois Capital Management (SEC enforcement - inconsistent notice periods)

**Legal Framework**:

The SEC enforces Investment Advisers Act Rule 206(4)-8, which prohibits material misstatements and misleading omissions to fund investors. SEC enforcement actions demonstrate:

1. **Undisclosed preferential treatment violates Rule 206(4)-8**
2. **Fiduciary duty requires equal treatment absent disclosure**
3. **Informal policies inconsistent with LPA violate securities laws**

**Relevance to Key Person Enforcement Limitations**:

**Aria Partners**: Fund had 90-day redemption notice requirement but informally allowed some investors 60-day notice. SEC found violation. Court held GP breached fiduciary duty.

**Application to Pinnacle**: If some LPs negotiate side letters with preferential key person redemption rights (e.g., immediate redemption vs. standard notice periods), GP must disclose to all LPs. Failure to disclose creates SEC liability but also suggests:
- Not all LPs have identical key person rights
- Side letter provisions may limit or waive key person rights for certain LPs
- GP fiduciary duty requires consistent application

**Deccan Value Investors**: Fund granted discretionary waivers allowing two investors early exit at current value pre-liquidation, leaving others to bear market risk. SEC found breach of fiduciary duty.

**Application to Pinnacle**: If GP selectively permits some LPs to exercise key person redemptions while denying others (or imposing gates on others), this violates fiduciary duty unless disclosed. However, this also means:
- GP must treat all LP redemption requests consistently
- GP cannot selectively enforce key person provisions
- BUT if GP consistently determines no key person event occurred, denying all LP redemptions, this is permissible

**Distinguishing Favorable Aspect for Pinnacle**:

These SEC cases actually *support* GP arguments that key person provisions cannot be selectively enforced:
- If some LPs claim key person event occurred but others do not, GP must make uniform determination
- GP cannot permit "friendly" LPs to remain while forcing "hostile" LP redemptions
- Uniform determination that no key person event occurred treats all LPs equally

If Pinnacle's GP determines that no key person event has occurred, or that succession planning cured any event, this determination must apply uniformly. LPs cannot claim preferential enforcement based on their individual views of key person status. ### Limitations on LP Enforcement of Key Person Redemption Rights

While key person provisions facially grant LPs redemption rights upon designated trigger events, Delaware law and typical LPA mechanics impose significant limitations on LP enforcement:

#### 1. GP Discretion and Business Judgment Deference

Where Pinnacle's LPA grants the GP authority to determine whether a key person event has occurred, Delaware courts apply highly deferential standards. In *Boardwalk Pipeline Partners, LP v. Bandera Master Fund LP*, 286 A.3d 1 (Del. 2022), the Delaware Supreme Court emphasized "maximum effect to the principle of freedom of contract" and upheld exculpation provisions protecting GP determinations made with advice of counsel, even where the Court of Chancery found the GP acted "intentionally and opportunistically." The Court held that partnership agreements may include conclusive presumptions of good faith, and that the proper judicial inquiry focuses on whether the GP reasonably relied on its determination—not whether the determination was substantively correct.

Similarly, in *Allen v. El Paso Pipeline GP Co.*, 113 A.3d 167 (Del. 2015), the Delaware Supreme Court established that where an LPA requires the GP to make determinations "in good faith," courts apply a "subjective belief" standard. LPs challenging the GP's determination bear the burden of proving the GP did not subjectively believe its determination—a showing requiring evidence of subjective bad faith, not mere disagreement with the decision's merits.

**Application to Pinnacle**: If Pinnacle's LPA states that "the General Partner shall determine in good faith whether a Key Person Event has occurred," or grants the GP discretion to assess whether founders continue to devote required time/attention, these Delaware cases establish that:
- LPs must prove GP subjectively did not believe its determination
- GP determination receives deference if made with advice of legal counsel
- Exculpation provisions may create conclusive presumptions of good faith
- Courts will not substitute judicial judgment for GP business judgment

Accordingly, Buyer should analyze whether Pinnacle's LPA includes GP determination provisions and exculpation clauses that would substantially limit LP ability to challenge key person determinations.

#### 2. Implied Covenant - Objective Reasonableness Standard

Even where key person provisions appear clear, the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing imposes an "objective reasonableness" limitation on LP enforcement. In *Gerber v. Enterprise Products Holdings, LLC*, 67 A.3d 400 (Del. 2013), the Delaware Supreme Court held that the implied covenant—which cannot be contractually eliminated—prohibits conduct that "acts arbitrarily or unreasonably to frustrate the unitholder's bargain." However, the Court emphasized that the covenant does not create new contractual rights; rather, it ensures parties do not frustrate objectively reasonable expectations based on the partnership agreement itself.

*Gerber* establishes a dual limitation:
- **GP Limitation**: GP cannot arbitrarily manipulate key person definitions to avoid clear triggers
- **LP Limitation**: LP expectations must be objectively reasonable based on LPA text, not subjective preferences

In *Whitestone REIT Operating Partnership, L.P. v. Pillarstone Capital REIT*, C.A. No. 2022-0607-LWW (Del. Ch. Jan. 25, 2024), the Court reinforced that the implied covenant operates bilaterally, requiring both GP and LPs to act reasonably regarding contractual rights.

**Application to Pinnacle**: If the LPA defines "key person" with flexibility (e.g., "substantially all business time" rather than "80% time commitment"), the implied covenant permits GP to interpret this reasonably, and prevents LPs from claiming violation based on interpretations outside the LPA's text. Conversely, if the LPA contains precise triggers (e.g., "named individuals must serve as CEO and CIO"), GP cannot avoid the trigger through creative interpretation.

The implied covenant analysis suggests:
- Reasonable succession planning may satisfy flexible key person requirements
- Continued founder involvement (even reduced) may support GP determination that no event occurred
- LP claims of opportunistic redemption during acquisition may themselves violate implied covenant

#### 3. Redemption Mechanics - Gates, Suspensions, and Notice Periods

Even if a key person event triggers redemption rights, such rights remain subject to the LPA's general redemption procedures. Typical hedge fund LPAs include:
- **Gates**: Limiting redemptions to specified percentage per period (commonly 10-25% quarterly)
- **Suspensions**: Permitting GP to suspend redemptions during "extraordinary circumstances"
- **Notice periods**: Requiring 30-90 days advance notice
- **Orderly liquidation**: Prohibiting redemptions that would require fire sales

In *Lerner v. Paige Capital Management*, a court held that a GP possesses substantial discretion to impose redemption gates in good faith, subject only to fiduciary duty not to act arbitrarily or solely for the GP's benefit. The court rejected arguments that "sole discretion" eliminated fiduciary duty but upheld the GP's authority to gate redemptions to protect the fund.

In *Seibold v. Camulos Partners LP* (Del. Ch.), the Court held that fund managers breached the LPA by withholding redemptions without "extraordinary circumstances" as required by the agreement, but the Court's analysis confirms that where LPA grants broad suspension authority, such authority is enforceable.

**Application to Pinnacle**: If Pinnacle's LPA subjects key person redemptions to general redemption procedures including gates, this materially limits LP exit timeline:
- 10% quarterly gate → Only 10% of LPs can redeem per quarter (10 quarters for full exit)
- 90-day notice period → Minimum 3-month delay before first redemptions
- Extraordinary circumstances suspension → GP may argue acquisition constitutes such circumstances
- Combined effect → 12-18 month minimum exit timeline even if redemption rights triggered
