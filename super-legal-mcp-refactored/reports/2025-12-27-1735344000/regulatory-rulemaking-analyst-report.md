## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. FAA Part 119 Air Carrier Certificate Change of Control Requirements

#### 1. Statutory and Regulatory Framework

**Primary Authority:** 14 C.F.R. Part 119, Subpart C governs certification requirements for air carriers and commercial operators conducting operations under 14 C.F.R. Part 121 (scheduled air carriers like SkyWest).¬π

**Certificate Application Requirements (14 C.F.R. ¬ß 119.35):**
- Applicants must submit applications to the Administrator at least **90 days before the date of intended operation**¬≤
- Applications must be in a form and manner prescribed by the Administrator
- Must include comprehensive information about proposed operations

**Change of Control Notification Requirements (14 C.F.R. ¬ß 119.36(d)):**
- Each certificate holder must notify the Administrator **within 10 days** after certain ownership or control changes occur¬≥
- This notification requirement applies to changes in:
  - Substantial ownership interests
  - Management control
  - Corporate structure affecting operational control

**Management Personnel Requirements (14 C.F.R. ¬ß 119.65):**
For Part 121 operations, certificate holders must have qualified personnel serving full-time in the following positions:‚Å¥
1. **Director of Safety** - Overall safety management
2. **Director of Operations** - Operational oversight and control
3. **Chief Pilot** - Required for each category of aircraft used (multi-category requirement added 2023)‚Åµ
4. **Director of Maintenance** - Maintenance program oversight
5. **Chief Inspector** - Quality assurance and inspection

**Management Personnel Qualifications (14 C.F.R. ¬ß 119.67):**
Each required management position must meet specific qualification requirements including:
- Appropriate experience in aviation operations
- Knowledge of relevant FAA regulations
- Demonstrated competence in their area of responsibility
- Ability to exercise operational control authority

**Notification of Management Changes:**
Certificate holders must notify the responsible Flight Standards office within 10 days of:‚Å∂
- Any change in personnel occupying required management positions
- Any vacancy in required management positions
- Changes affecting the qualifications of management personnel

#### 2. FAA Order 8900.1 - Certificate Transfer Procedures

**Governing Authority:** FAA Order 8900.1, Flight Standards Information Management System (FSIMS), provides detailed procedures for certificate transfers, mergers, and acquisitions.‚Å∑

**Key Procedural Requirements:**

**Transition Plan Submission:**‚Å∏
- Certificate holders must submit a comprehensive transition plan "as soon as possible"
- Plan submitted to Joint Transition Team (JTT) or Supervising Authority office
- Must include Schedule of Events (SOE) with detailed timeline
- Should address all aspects of operational transition

**Proving Tests and Validation Flights:**‚Åπ
- Required under FAA Order 8900.1, Volume 3, Chapter 29, Sections 1-8
- Proving tests begin only after all Phase 4 Gate requirements met
- For Part 121 operations, proving tests essentially constitute "full-scale simulation of revenue operations"
- Purpose: Demonstrate ability to operate independently, safely, and in compliance with applicable CFRs
- Minimum requirements vary based on aircraft type and operation complexity

**Amendment Applications Requiring Extended Review (14 C.F.R. ¬ß 119.49(a)):**¬π‚Å∞
Applications must be submitted at least **90 days before proposed effective date** for:
- Mergers
- Acquisitions of airline operational assets requiring additional showing of safety
- Changes in kind of operation
- Resumption of operations following bankruptcy
- Initial introduction of aircraft not previously proven for air carrier use

**2024 Certification Process Changes:**¬π¬π
Effective January 24, 2024, FAA implemented new initial certification process requiring:
- 30 calendar days for FAA review of formal application before scheduling application meeting
- Enhanced applicant readiness requirements
- More structured pre-application phase

#### 3. Operations Specifications (OpSpecs) Continuity vs. Re-Issuance

**Nature of Operations Specifications:**¬π¬≤
- OpSpecs are the "blueprint" approved by FAA for each Part 121/135 airline
- Prescribe authorizations, limitations, and procedures for each kind of operation
- No person may operate as direct air carrier without valid OpSpecs
- OpSpecs issued under 14 C.F.R. ¬ß 119.49

**OpSpecs Content Requirements (14 C.F.R. ¬ß 119.49):**¬π¬≥
Operations specifications must contain:
- Specific location and address of certificate holder's principal base of operations
- Other business names under which carrier conducts operations
- Reference to economic authority (DOT certificate)
- Type of aircraft, registration markings, and serial numbers
- Authorized kinds of operations (domestic, flag, supplemental, commuter, on-demand)
- Geographic areas of operations
- Airport authorizations and limitations
- Special authorizations (ETOPS, CAT II/III, international operations)
- Management personnel names and duties

**Change of Control Impact on OpSpecs:**
- OpSpecs are certificate-specific and tied to named certificate holder
- Change of control typically requires **amendment or re-issuance** of OpSpecs in name of new controlling entity
- All specialized authorizations (ETOPS 120, CAT II, international) must be validated for continuity
- Process may involve:
  - Validation flights for specialized operations
  - Management personnel re-qualification review
  - Updated maintenance program approval
  - Renewed airport authorizations

**Certificate Continuity Standard:**¬π‚Å¥
- Administrator may determine certificate holder's continued eligibility on any regulatory grounds
- Air Carrier Certificate issued under Part 119 remains effective until surrendered or revoked
- Certificate does not automatically transfer with asset sale

#### 4. ETOPS 120 Authorization Transfer Requirements

**ETOPS Regulatory Framework:**¬π‚Åµ
14 C.F.R. Part 121, Appendix P governs Extended-range Twin-engine Operational Performance Standards (ETOPS)

**ETOPS 120 Authorization Requirements:**¬π‚Å∂
- Airplane-engine combination must be type-design-approved for ETOPS of at least 120 minutes
- Certificate holder must operate in accordance with ETOPS authority contained in OpSpecs
- Compliance with maintenance program requirements of 14 C.F.R. ¬ß 121.374 mandatory
- Minimum Equipment List (MEL) requirements specific to 120-minute ETOPS

**Initial ETOPS Approval Prerequisites:**¬π‚Å∑

**Proving Test Requirements:**¬π‚Å∏
- Initial airplane proving tests require at least **100 hours of proving tests** acceptable to Administrator
- Proving tests required for aircraft not previously proven for use in the specific kind of operation
- ETOPS validation flights demonstrate operational readiness

#### 5. CAT II Approach Authorization Transfer

**CAT II Operations Defined:**¬≤‚Å∞
Category II (CAT II) approach operations allow aircraft to land in lower visibility conditions than standard approaches, critical for:
- All-weather operations reliability
- Major hub airport operations during inclement weather
- Schedule integrity

### B. DOT Foreign Ownership Compliance and "Actual Control" Doctrine

#### 1. Statutory Foreign Ownership Limits (49 U.S.C. ¬ß 40102(a)(15))

**"Citizen of the United States" Definition:**¬≥‚Åµ

For purposes of U.S. air carrier certification, 49 U.S.C. ¬ß 40102(a)(15) defines a "citizen of the United States" for a corporation or association as an entity that meets ALL of the following requirements:

1. **Organized under U.S. or state law**
2. **President and at least two-thirds (66.67%) of board of directors and managing officers are U.S. citizens**
3. **At least 75% of voting interest owned or controlled by U.S. citizens**
4. **Under the "actual control" of U.S. citizens**

**Critical Distinction - Voting vs. Total Equity:**¬≥‚Å∂
- **Voting Interest Limit:** Maximum **25% foreign voting** interest permitted
- **Total Equity Limit:** Maximum **49% foreign total equity** (voting + non-voting) permitted
- **Open Skies Flexibility:** 49% foreign total equity generally permitted only when foreign investors are from Open Skies agreement countries OR investments are "clearly passive and diffuse"¬≥‚Å∑

**Global Aviation Holdings (GAH) Ownership Structure - Compliance Analysis:**

| Investor | Equity % | Voting % | Citizenship | Compliant? |
|----------|----------|----------|-------------|------------|
| CPPIB (Canada Pension Plan Investment Board) | 30% | 15% | Canadian | ‚úì (individually) |
| European institutional fund | 10% | 5% | European | ‚úì (individually) |
| **Total Foreign** | **40%** | **20%** | **Non-U.S.** | **‚ö†Ô∏è SCRUTINY RISK** |
| U.S. PE sponsors/investors | 60% | 80% | U.S. | ‚úì |

**Statutory Compliance:**
- **Voting Interest:** 20% foreign voting is **COMPLIANT** (below 25% threshold) ‚úì
- **Total Equity:** 40% foreign equity is **COMPLIANT** (below 49% threshold) ‚úì

**However:** Statutory compliance **does not guarantee** DOT approval due to "actual control" doctrine.

#### 2. DOT "Actual Control" Doctrine - Beyond Percentage Ownership

**Doctrinal Evolution:**¬≥‚Å∏

The "actual control" requirement was part of CAB (Civil Aeronautics Board) and DOT administrative precedent and was **codified in 2003** following issuance of a letter from DOT's Office of Inspector General.¬≥‚Åπ

**Key Principle:**‚Å¥‚Å∞
DOT interprets "actual control" to mean determining whether foreign interests have the **ability to exercise substantial influence** over the airline **beyond the numerical requirements** of the law.

**Critical Finding from GAO-19-540R (June 2019):**‚Å¥¬π
> "DOT officials noted that based on their analysis, foreign ownership of an airline's voting stock could be **less than 25 percent but could still be found to have actual control**. Conversely, foreign ownership‚Äîincluding both voting and non-voting shares‚Äîcould be nearly 50 percent but have little influence, according to the guidance, when the foreign investors have been nationals of countries with which the United States has open skies aviation bilateral agreements or when DOT determined that the foreign investments were **clearly passive and diffuse, with no suggestion of foreign control**."

**Case-by-Case Analysis Standard:**‚Å¥¬≤

DOT determines actual control through a **totality of the circumstances** analysis examining multiple factors, not just ownership percentages.

#### 3. Factors DOT Examines in "Actual Control" Determinations

**Documented Factors from GAO Reports and DOT Precedents:**‚Å¥¬≥

DOT examines several factors when determining the issue of control, including:

1. **Voting rights** held by U.S. and non-U.S. citizens
2. **Significant contracts** between airline and business partners (especially foreign partners)
3. **Terms of debt instruments** or bankruptcy agreements
4. **Board composition** and decision-making authority
5. **Board appointment rights** - who has power to appoint directors
6. **Veto rights** over key business decisions
7. **Management appointment authority** - who controls hiring/firing of key executives
8. **Day-to-day operational control** mechanisms
9. **Special board committees** with foreign representation
10. **Consent rights** for major corporate actions
11. **Whether foreign investors are from Open Skies countries** (U.S.-Canada and U.S.-European Open Skies agreements)

**DOT Analysis Focus:**‚Å¥‚Å¥
During analysis, DOT determines whether a foreign entity's influence over any of these factors **shifts the actual, day-to-day control** of the airline from U.S. citizens to foreign citizens.

#### 4. Key Precedent: Northwest Airlines/KLM (1989)

**The Seminal "Actual Control" Case:**‚Å¥‚Åµ

In its review of the proposed transaction involving Northwest Airlines and KLM (Royal Dutch Airlines), DOT objected to the proposed deal's structure and issued a **consent order**, with Northwest and Wings Holdings agreeing to:

1. **Place KLM's interest above 25% of total equity in a voting trust**
2. **Terminate KLM's right to appoint a financial advisory committee**
3. **Disqualify KLM's board member from participating in all decisions on competitive and international aviation matters**

**DOT's Key Statement:**‚Å¥‚Å∂
> "The DOT order stated that appointment of foreign representatives to **key positions** on Wings' board, **especially the position of chairman**, would be 'cause for us to review the citizenship of the affected air carrier.'"

**Outcome After Restructuring:**‚Å¥‚Å∑
After the structural changes, DOT permitted:
- KLM to own **49% of total equity** in Wings Holdings
- **Increased representation on Wings' board** (with limitations on competitive/international matters)
- This was facilitated by U.S.-Netherlands **Open Skies bilateral aviation agreement**

**Key Takeaway:**
Even with Open Skies agreement, DOT required elimination of:
- Financial advisory committee appointment rights
- Participation in competitive decisions
- Full board voting rights on international matters

#### 5. Recent DOT Reviews (2010-2019)

**DOT Ongoing Oversight:**‚Å¥‚Å∏

According to GAO-19-540R, DOT initiated citizenship reviews for major carriers:

- **United Airlines (2010):** During merger with Continental
- **Southwest Airlines (2011):** During merger with Air Tran
- **American Airlines (2017):** Periodic review of ownership structure
- **Delta Air Lines (2019):** After reporting substantial change in ownership

**Significance:**
DOT conducts **ongoing monitoring** and can initiate review at any time upon:
- Change of control transactions
- Substantial changes in ownership reported by carrier
- Periodic compliance audits
- Concerns raised about foreign influence

#### 2. Use-or-Lose Requirement - 14 C.F.R. ¬ß 93.227

**80% Utilization Mandate:**‚Åµ‚Å¥
> "Any slot not utilized 80 percent of the time over a 2-month period shall be recalled by the FAA."

**Compliance Reporting:**‚Åµ‚Åµ

#### 4. Change of Control vs. Buy-Sell Transactions

**Critical Distinction:**

**Buy-Sell Transaction:**
