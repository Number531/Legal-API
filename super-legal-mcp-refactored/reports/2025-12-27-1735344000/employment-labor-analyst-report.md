## IV. DETAILED ANALYSIS

### A. Railway Labor Act Framework (45 U.S.C. § 151 et seq.)

#### 1. RLA vs. NLRA: Fundamental Differences

The Railway Labor Act, originally enacted in 1926 for railroads, was extended to cover airline employees in 1936.¹ Unlike most private-sector employers who are governed by the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), airlines and railroads operate under the RLA's specialized labor relations framework designed to prevent service disruptions in critical transportation industries.²

**Key Structural Differences:**

| Feature | Railway Labor Act (RLA) | National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) |
|---------|-------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Status Quo Requirement** | Mandatory preservation of wages, hours, and working conditions during negotiations and mediation³ | No general status quo requirement (though employers cannot make unilateral changes to mandatory bargaining subjects)⁴ |
| **Contract Duration** | Labor contracts continue indefinitely until amended⁵ | Contracts expire at a certain date⁶ |
| **Strike Restrictions** | Strikes permitted only after exhausting Section 6 negotiations, NMB mediation, proffer of arbitration, and 30-day cooling-off period⁷ | Employees generally free to strike when contract expires⁸ |
| **Administrative Agency** | National Mediation Board (NMB) — focuses on mediation, arbitration, and representation elections⁹ | National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) — investigates unfair labor practices, oversees elections¹⁰ |
| **Permissible Strike Tactics** | Allows secondary boycotts against other RLA-regulated carriers and intermittent strikes¹¹ | Secondary boycotts generally prohibited under § 8(b)(4)¹² |
| **Judicial Enforcement** | Employees may sue directly in federal court to enforce RLA rights¹³ | NLRB has nearly exclusive enforcement power¹⁴ |

**Critical Implication for SkyWest Acquisition:**

The RLA's status quo requirement means that Global Aviation Holdings **cannot unilaterally change any terms and conditions of employment** for the 1,450 ALPA-represented pilots during the ongoing NMB mediation and any subsequent cooling-off period. Any attempt to modify wages, work rules, benefit contributions, or scheduling parameters without mutual agreement would violate 45 U.S.C. § 152, First.¹⁵

This constraint severely limits GAH's operational flexibility post-acquisition. For example:
- Cannot adjust pilot scheduling practices to achieve synergies with other GAH airline operations
- Cannot modify crew basing assignments even if economically beneficial
- Cannot alter health insurance contributions or retirement plan matching formulas
- Cannot implement new flight duty period limitations or rest requirements

Violation of status quo provisions can result in injunctive relief compelling restoration of prior terms and potential liability for back pay and benefits.¹⁶

#### 2. Section 6 Negotiation Process and Timeline

The RLA establishes a multi-stage dispute resolution process for "major disputes" (those concerning the making or modification of collective bargaining agreements).¹⁷

**Stage 1: Section 6 Notice and Direct Negotiations**

Section 6 of the RLA, codified at 45 U.S.C. § 156, requires either party seeking to modify rates of pay, rules, or working conditions to provide written notice to the other party.¹⁸ This "Section 6 Notice" triggers the obligation to commence direct negotiations.¹⁹

**SkyWest Application:**
- **Section 6 Notice Served:** June 2025
- **Direct Negotiations Period:** June-September 2025 (approximately 3 months)
- **Key Economic Issue:** 15% wage increase (union demand) vs. 8% wage increase (management offer) = 7-percentage-point gap

**Stage 2: National Mediation Board (NMB) Mediation**

When either party believes an agreement cannot be reached through direct negotiations, it may apply for NMB mediation.²⁰ Upon application, the NMB dockets the case and assigns a mediator.²¹

**SkyWest Application:**
- **Mediation Commenced:** October 2025
- **Current Status:** Active mediation (as of transaction context date)
- **Typical Duration:** No fixed timeframe; NMB has discretion to continue mediation indefinitely²²
- **Industry Average:** 18-24 months from Section 6 notice to resolution or release²³

**Critical Finding:** The NMB mediation process **has no statutory deadline**. Some mediation cases have lasted "several weeks while others have lasted several years."²⁴ This creates significant uncertainty for the acquisition timeline. Given that the ALPA CBA expires December 31, 2026 (11 months from present), and mediation commenced only in October 2025 (4 months of mediation to date), the NMB is highly unlikely to release parties to self-help before the CBA expiration date.

**Stage 3: Proffer of Arbitration**

If the NMB concludes that no agreement can be reached through mediation, it offers both parties binding arbitration.²⁵ Either party may reject arbitration.²⁶

**Stage 4: 30-Day Cooling-Off Period**

Upon rejection of arbitration by either party, the NMB releases the parties into a 30-day cooling-off period during which the status quo must be maintained.²⁷ Neither strikes nor lockouts are permitted during this period.²⁸

**Stage 5: Presidential Emergency Board (Optional)**

If the NMB determines that a dispute "threaten[s] substantially to interrupt interstate commerce to a degree such as to deprive any section of the country of essential transportation service," it notifies the President, who may create a Presidential Emergency Board (PEB) to investigate and make recommendations.²⁹

**PEB Impact on Timeline:**
- PEB has 30 days to issue recommendations³⁰
- Status quo must be maintained for an additional 30 days after PEB report³¹
- Total additional delay: 60 days minimum

**Historical Context:** Presidential Emergency Boards are rarely invoked for airline disputes. The last PEB for airlines was appointed in the 1990s.³² More recently, PEBs were used for freight railroad disputes in 2022, preventing a nationwide rail strike.³³ However, the relatively smaller scale of SkyWest Regional (145 aircraft, regional feed operations) compared to national Class I railroads or mainline passenger carriers makes PEB invocation less likely.

**Stage 6: Self-Help (Strike or Lockout)**

After exhausting all prior stages and any PEB process, the parties may engage in "self-help": the union may strike, and the carrier may lock out employees.³⁴

**Success Rate:** Historically, approximately 97% of all NMB mediation cases have been successfully resolved without interruption to public service.³⁵ This high success rate reflects the NMB's practice of maintaining mediation for extended periods until parties reach agreement, rather than releasing parties to self-help.

### C. Single Carrier Determination and Representation Election Risk

#### 1. NMB Single Carrier Doctrine

When two separate airlines merge or are acquired, the National Mediation Board must determine whether they constitute a "single carrier" for purposes of union representation under the RLA.⁵⁴ This determination has profound implications for collective bargaining rights and union representation.

**Single Carrier Test:**

The NMB determines that carriers have "clearly combined their operations from a managerial and labor relations perspective and have taken substantial steps toward operational integration."⁵⁵ The Board considers factors including:⁵⁶
- Whether the combined carrier operates under one management team
- How operations are marketed to customers
- How the carrier shares resources across the combined network
- Continuity of workforce and business operations
- Similarity of products or services
- Use of same equipment and facilities

**Critical Finding:** The NMB has held that "the absence of seniority integration cannot by itself preclude a single carrier determination."⁵⁷ This means that GAH cannot avoid single carrier status simply by maintaining separate pilot seniority lists for SkyWest and any other airlines it owns.

#### 2. Representation Election Process

Once the NMB grants single-carrier status for a particular class or craft (e.g., pilots, flight attendants, mechanics), the NMB will determine which union will represent the combined workforce, possibly through a representation election.⁵⁸

**Election Trigger:**

In a merger, the NMB will determine if a "single transportation system" exists based upon an application from:⁵⁹
- An incumbent organization representing a craft or class on the pre-existing or merged carrier
- Any other organization seeking to represent the craft or class
- Any individual employee

**Election Mechanics:**

If the NMB determines a single transportation system exists and the numbers of employees in the craft or class on each part of the system are comparable, the Board will authorize an election among the craft or class.⁶⁰

#### 3. Application to Global Aviation Holdings Acquisition

**Scenario Analysis:**

**If GAH owns only SkyWest (no other airlines):**
- No single carrier issue arises
- SkyWest ALPA continues as certified representative of pilots
- Current CBA remains in effect (status quo continues during negotiations)

**If GAH owns other airlines with pilot workforces:**
- NMB may receive application for single carrier determination post-closing
- Timeline: NMB determination process typically **6-12 months** post-application⁶¹
- If single carrier status granted, NMB may order representation election

**Representation Election Outcomes:**

| Scenario | Result | Impact on SkyWest ALPA Contract |
|----------|--------|--------------------------------|
| **ALPA wins election** | ALPA represents combined pilot group | SkyWest CBA continues as basis for merged contract negotiations |
| **Rival union wins** | New union represents combined pilot group | SkyWest CBA may be displaced; new negotiations commence |
| **No union wins** | Pilots become non-union | SkyWest CBA terminates; at-will employment |

**Risk Assessment:**

If GAH operates other airlines:
- **Probability of single carrier application:** 80-90% (unions typically file immediately post-closing to preserve bargaining rights)
- **Probability of single carrier determination:** 70-80% if operational integration occurs (shared management, coordinated scheduling, codeshare operations)
- **Timeline to final determination:** 6-18 months (includes NMB investigation, hearing, decision, and possible appeals)

**Strategic Implications:**

The single carrier process creates additional uncertainty during the critical 12-18 month post-acquisition integration period. If ALPA faces a representation election, the union may take a harder line in current contract negotiations to demonstrate value to pilot membership and prevent decertification. Conversely, GAH may delay operational integration to avoid triggering single carrier determination until labor relations stabilize.

#### 3. NMB Representation Election Process

Under the RLA, employees covered by the Act may seek union representation through an NMB-supervised election process:⁶⁷

**Stage 1: Authorization Card Campaign**
- Union must obtain authorization cards from **35% of employees** in the craft or class⁶⁸
- Timeline: Typically 6-12 months for organizing campaign
- Cards valid for one year from signature date

**Stage 2: NMB Investigation**
- NMB verifies authorization card signatures and determines appropriate craft or class
- Timeline: 2-4 months post-petition filing

**Stage 3: Representation Election**
- **Majority vote required:** More than 50% of **eligible voters** (not just those voting) must vote for union representation⁶⁹
- This is a higher threshold than NLRB elections, which require only a majority of votes cast
- Timeline: Election typically held 2-4 months after NMB authorization

**Critical Distinction from NLRA:** Under the RLA, a majority of eligible employees must vote "yes" for union certification, whereas under the NLRA, only a majority of those who actually vote must support the union. This makes RLA unionization campaigns more difficult but not insurmountable.

- Pay stagnation while PE sponsors extract management fees and dividends

**Timing Vulnerability:**

The 6-12 month post-acquisition period represents peak vulnerability to organizing campaigns. Employees experience:
- Uncertainty about new ownership's intentions
- Integration-related disruptions and policy changes

### F. WARN Act Compliance Analysis

#### 1. Statutory Requirements (29 U.S.C. § 2101)

The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act requires covered employers to provide **60 days' advance written notice** before implementing:⁷⁹
- **Plant closing:** Permanent or temporary shutdown of single site affecting 50+ employees
- **Mass layoff:** Employment loss of 50-499 employees (comprising 33%+ of workforce) OR 500+ employees at single site

**Covered Employers:**⁸⁰
- Private businesses with 100+ full-time employees
- OR businesses with 100+ full-time and part-time employees working aggregate 4,000+ hours/week (excluding overtime)

**SkyWest Application:** With 8,200 employees, SkyWest is clearly a covered employer subject to full WARN Act requirements.

**Notice Recipients:**⁸¹
1. Affected employees (or their union representatives)
2. State dislocated worker unit
3. Chief elected official of local government where layoff/closing occurs

|----------|---------------------|----------------------------|

| Denver, CO | 900 | 50 or 297 |

| Phoenix, AZ | 450 | 50 or 149 |
| 75 other airports | ~4,450 (scattered) | Site-specific analysis required |

**Critical Finding:** If GAH implements workforce reductions at any single hub location exceeding 50 employees (plant closing trigger) or exceeding 33% of that location's workforce AND at least 50 employees (mass layoff trigger), WARN Act notice is required.

#### 3. PE-Backed Airline Acquisition Patterns

#### 4. WARN Act Exceptions

**Three statutory exceptions may excuse WARN notice requirement:**⁸³

**Exception 1: Unforeseeable Business Circumstances**

Employer may order shutdown/layoff before 60-day notice period if closure/layoff is caused by business circumstances that were not reasonably foreseeable as of the time notice would have been required.⁸⁴

**Application:** Loss of a Capacity Purchase Agreement (e.g., United Airlines terminates CPA due to operational issues) might qualify as unforeseeable. However, **planned cost reductions to meet PE sponsor's return targets are NOT unforeseeable**.⁸⁵

**Exception 2: Faltering Company**

Employer actively seeking capital or business that would enable it to avoid or postpone shutdown may provide less than 60 days' notice.⁸⁶

**Application:** Inapplicable to SkyWest acquisition. SkyWest is currently profitable with $1.85B annual revenue. GAH is acquiring a healthy company, not rescuing a failing one.

**Exception 3: Natural Disaster**

No WARN notice required if plant closing or mass layoff is due to natural disaster (flood, earthquake, drought).⁸⁷

**Application:** Inapplicable absent actual natural disaster.

**Conclusion:** GAH cannot rely on WARN Act exceptions to excuse notice requirements for planned post-acquisition workforce reductions.

### G. EEOC Pregnancy Discrimination Charge — Martinez v. SkyWest

#### 1. Charge Facts and Procedural Posture

**Charging Party:** Sarah Martinez, Flight Attendant
**Charge Number:** EEOC Charge No. 540-2024-03389 [HYPOTHETICAL SCENARIO]
**Filing Date:** September 2024
**Allegation:** Pregnancy discrimination under Title VII and the Pregnancy Workers Fairness Act (PWFA)

**Factual Background:**

Ms. Martinez, a flight attendant in her second trimester of pregnancy, requested reasonable accommodation under the PWFA: light duty assignment with no lifting restrictions exceeding 20 pounds. SkyWest management denied the accommodation, asserting:
- No light duty positions available for flight attendants
- Operational necessity requires all flight attendants to perform all essential job functions
- Safety concerns preclude accommodations that limit physical capabilities

Ms. Martinez was placed on unpaid leave for the remainder of her pregnancy.

**Current Status:** EEOC investigation phase; right-to-sue letter anticipated March-April 2025 (180 days post-filing).

#### 2. Legal Framework: Pregnancy Workers Fairness Act (PWFA)

**Statutory Background:**

The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act was enacted on December 29, 2022, and became effective June 27, 2023.⁹¹ The Act requires covered employers (those with 15+ employees) to provide "reasonable accommodations" to workers' "known limitations" related to pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions, unless the accommodation would impose an "undue hardship" on the employer.⁹²

**EEOC Final Rule (29 CFR Part 1636):**

The EEOC issued its final regulation implementing the PWFA on April 15, 2024, with an effective date of June 18, 2024.⁹³ The regulations went into effect during Ms. Martinez's pregnancy, providing her with statutory protection under the PWFA.

**Key Regulatory Provisions:**⁹⁴

1. **Covered "Limitations":** Physical or mental conditions related to, affected by, or arising out of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions that may necessitate an accommodation
2. **"Reasonable Accommodation":** Modifications or adjustments to the work environment, manner, or circumstances under which a position is customarily performed, that enable a qualified employee with a known limitation to perform the essential functions of the position
3. **"Undue Hardship":** Significant difficulty or expense in the operation of the employer's business
4. **Interactive Process:** Employers must engage in an interactive process with the employee to determine effective reasonable accommodations

#### 3. Comparable Airline Pregnancy Accommodation Cases

**Frontier Airlines Settlement (October 2023):**

Five pilots and the EEOC reached a settlement with Frontier Airlines resolving EEOC charges filed in May 2018 and a lawsuit filed in December 2019 alleging discrimination against pregnant and lactating employees.⁹⁵

**Key Settlement Terms:**⁹⁶
- Frontier became one of the first airlines permitting pilots to pump breastmilk in the cockpit during non-critical phases of flight
- Policy changes enabling lactating pilots to continue working while maintaining safety standards
- Four Frontier flight attendants settled a similar lawsuit in April 2022
- Flight attendants permitted to use breast pumps mid-flight

**Significance:** The Frontier settlement demonstrates that airlines **can** accommodate pregnant/lactating employees without compromising safety. Ground assignments, modified duty, and policy flexibility are feasible accommodations.

**Trans World Airlines (TWA) Settlement (Historical):**

The EEOC obtained preliminary approval of an agreement with TWA resolving a pregnancy discrimination lawsuit under Title VII.⁹⁷ The settlement provided travel vouchers to approximately 1,500-2,000 former and present TWA female flight attendants for each pregnancy covered under the lawsuit.⁹⁸

#### 4. SkyWest's Denial: Legal Vulnerability Analysis

**SkyWest's Position: "No Accommodation Available"**

SkyWest asserted that:
1. No light duty flight attendant positions exist
2. All flight attendants must be able to perform all essential functions (including heavy lifting)
3. Operational necessity precludes accommodations

**Legal Deficiencies in SkyWest's Position:**

**Flaw #1: Failure to Engage in Interactive Process**

The PWFA and EEOC regulations require employers to engage in an "interactive process" with the employee to identify potential reasonable accommodations.⁹⁹ Simply declaring "no accommodation available" without exploring alternatives violates this requirement.

**Potential Accommodations SkyWest Failed to Consider:**

| Accommodation | Feasibility | Precedent |
|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Temporary ground assignment** | High | Common practice: gate agent, customer service, training instructor, flight operations coordinator | Frontier settlement; industry standard |
| **Modified flight duties** | Moderate | Assign to shorter routes with reduced physical demands | Requires operational analysis |
| **Temporary reassignment to administrative role** | High | Scheduling, crew coordination, safety compliance | Available at most airlines |
| **Unpaid leave with benefits continuation** | High | FMLA-equivalent protection | Statutory minimum |

**Flaw #2: "Undue Hardship" Not Established**

Under the PWFA, employers must demonstrate that an accommodation would impose "significant difficulty or expense."¹⁰⁰ SkyWest made no showing that:
- Temporary reassignment to ground duties would create significant operational disruption
- Cost of accommodating Ms. Martinez would be prohibitive
- No other flight attendants were available to cover her flights

With **850 flight attendants**, SkyWest has substantial flexibility to reassign one pregnant employee to temporary ground duties without operational impact.

**Flaw #3: Inconsistent Treatment**

If SkyWest has accommodated non-pregnant employees with physical restrictions (e.g., temporary injury, disability), failure to provide similar accommodations to pregnant employees constitutes discriminatory treatment under Title VII and the PWFA.

- PBGC premiums and termination liability

**Asset vs. Stock Purchase Implications:**

| Structure | ERISA Liability Impact |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Stock Purchase** | GAH inherits all SkyWest ERISA liabilities; SkyWest remains sponsor of existing plans | Full successor liability |
| **Asset Purchase** | GAH may avoid certain ERISA liabilities if employees not hired or plans not assumed | However, **Section 4204 of ERISA** allows continuation of pension obligations if GAH continues to employ SkyWest employees |

#### 6. Multiemployer Pension Plan Withdrawal Liability (If Applicable)

**Statutory Framework (29 U.S.C. § 1381):**

If SkyWest contributes to multiemployer pension plans (common for unionized mechanics or ground workers at some airlines), **withdrawal from the plan triggers "withdrawal liability"** — the employer's proportionate share of the plan's unfunded vested benefits.¹¹⁹

**Calculation:** Withdrawal liability = (Unfunded Vested Benefits) × (Employer's Proportionate Share)

**Avoidance Mechanism (ERISA § 4204):**

If GAH (as purchaser) agrees to **continue contributions** to the multiemployer plan under the existing CBA, withdrawal liability can be avoided through the "asset sale" exception.¹²⁰

**Strategic Recommendation:**

GAH must determine:
1. Does SkyWest contribute to any multiemployer pension plans? (Review CBAs, Form 5500 filings)
2. If yes, what is the estimated withdrawal liability?
3. Can GAH negotiate continuation of contributions to avoid liability?

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. Principal Conclusions

1. **Railway Labor Act Imposes Severe Constraints on Post-Acquisition Operations**

The RLA's status quo requirement prevents GAH from unilaterally changing any pilot terms and conditions of employment during ongoing NMB mediation. This constraint extends indefinitely until parties reach agreement or exhaust the full RLA dispute resolution process (potentially 18-36 months post-acquisition). GAH cannot achieve operational synergies, modify crew assignments, or adjust compensation structures for pilots without ALPA agreement.

2. **Strike Risk in 2027 Represents Material Transaction Threat**

The projected timeline for ALPA negotiations places potential strike authorization in May-August 2027, coinciding with the first 12-18 months of GAH ownership. A pilot strike would result in:
- Complete operational shutdown (145 aircraft grounded)
- $5.07M daily revenue loss
- Capacity Purchase Agreement breaches triggering penalty provisions
- Cumulative exposure of $150M-$500M depending on strike duration

This risk justifies significant purchase price discount or escrow structure to protect GAH against labor disruption.

3. **Industry Comparables Strongly Support ALPA's 15% Wage Demand**

Recent regional airline ALPA settlements (Horizon Air 74-90% over 3 years, CommutAir 60% over 4 years, Republic Airways 86% over 2 years) demonstrate that SkyWest management's 8% offer is significantly below market. Expected settlement outcome: **10-12% wage increase** representing $18-20M annual cost increase. Management's resistance appears driven by PE-imposed EBITDA targets rather than genuine affordability constraints.

4. **Flight Attendant and Mechanic Unionization Highly Probable**

PE ownership historically triggers organizing campaigns due to employee concerns about job security and cost-cutting. AFA-CWA and TWU/IAM have demonstrated recent success organizing regional airline workers. Combined probability-weighted cost of flight attendant and mechanic unionization: **$6-10M annually** if both groups certify unions and negotiate first contracts within 36 months.

5. **WARN Act Exposure Requires Disciplined Workforce Reduction Planning**

Any GAH-directed workforce reductions must be carefully structured to avoid WARN Act violations. Penalty exposure ranges from $8.5M (10% workforce reduction) to $13.3M (15% workforce reduction). California mini-WARN law lowers threshold to 75 employees, creating additional compliance obligations.

6. **EEOC Martinez Charge Creates Moderate Liability and Reputational Risk**

SkyWest's denial of pregnancy accommodation to Ms. Martinez violates PWFA requirements. Individual settlement likely $200K-$400K. If class action certified (40-50% probability), exposure escalates to $2M-$8M. GAH inherits successor liability under Title VII case law. Indemnification and escrow protection strongly recommended.

7. **ERISA Controlled Group Liability Requires Due Diligence**

If SkyWest maintains frozen defined benefit pension plan or contributes to multiemployer plans, GAH's controlled group inherits joint and several liability for underfunding and withdrawal obligations. Immediate due diligence required: Form 5500 Schedule B review, actuarial valuation, PBGC funding status analysis.
